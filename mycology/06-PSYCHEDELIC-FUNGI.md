## The Psilocybin-Producing Fungi

```
TAXONOMY:
  Most psilocybin-producing species: genus Psilocybe (Basidiomycota)
  ~165 described Psilocybe species; ~116 contain psilocybin
  Also: Panaeolus (Panaeolus cyanescens, P. subbalteatus)
        Gymnopilus, Inocybe, Conocybe, Pluteus (some species)
  → Psilocybin biosynthesis evolved MULTIPLE TIMES (convergent evolution)
    and moved between genera via horizontal gene transfer (2018 genomics paper)

KEY SPECIES:
  Psilocybe cubensis: most commonly cultivated; subtropical; "shrooms" in popular culture
  Psilocybe semilanceata: "liberty cap"; temperate Europe and NW USA; wild
  Psilocybe azurescens: Pacific NW coast; highest psilocybin content of any species
  Psilocybe mexicana: Mexico; used in Mazatec ceremonies
  Psilocybe zapotecorum: Mexico; larger amounts of ceremonial use
  Panaeolus cyanescens: tropical; dung-growing; potent

IDENTIFICATION:
  Blue/green staining when bruised or cut: psilocin oxidation (not always present)
  Purple-brown spore print (Psilocybe); black (Panaeolus)
  Hygrophanous cap: color changes as it dries (distinctive but tricky)
  Habitat: Psilocybe: dung, wood chips, forest litter (species-specific)

LOOK-ALIKE DANGER:
  Galerina marginata: DEADLY (amatoxins); similar habitat and appearance
    → Brown spore print vs. purple-brown of Psilocybe
    → Does NOT bruise blue
    → Must verify species carefully; many poisonings from misidentification
```

## Pharmacology: The Molecular Mechanism

### Psilocybin to Psilocin

```
PRODRUG CONVERSION:
  Psilocybin: 4-phosphoryloxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine (molecular weight 284)
    → Inactive at receptors; phosphate group blocks receptor binding
  Alkaline phosphatase: removes phosphate group in intestinal lining
  → Psilocin: 4-hydroxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine
    → Crosses blood-brain barrier
    → Active at serotonin receptors

  Psilocybin: water-soluble; stable in storage; easier to handle
  Psilocin: less stable; oxidizes to blue quinones (hence the blue bruising)

STRUCTURAL ANALOG:
  Psilocin is structurally similar to serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine)
  Both have: indole ring + ethylamine chain
  Psilocin: 4-hydroxy-DMT (different attachment position from serotonin)
  This similarity is why psilocin binds serotonin receptors
```

### 5-HT2A Receptor Agonism

```
5-HT2A RECEPTOR:
  G-protein coupled receptor (Gq family)
  Normally activated by serotonin in thalamus, cortex, prefrontal cortex
  High density in: layer V/VI pyramidal neurons of neocortex
    prefrontal cortex, visual cortex, claustrum

  PSILOCIN BINDS 5-HT2A:
  → Activates Gq pathway → IP₃/DAG second messenger cascade
  → Increases glutamate release in cortex (indirect)
  → Increases serotonin turnover in some regions
  → Alters thalamocortical gating (how sensory information filtered)

  DOSE RELATIONSHIP:
  Psilocybin dose: 1–2 mg → threshold effects
  2–10 mg → moderate; altered perception, mood changes
  10–30 mg → high dose; potential ego dissolution
  30+ mg → deep psychedelic experience; rarely used clinically
  Duration: 4–6 hours
  (Compare LSD: lasts 8–12 hours; similar 5-HT2A mechanism but longer half-life)
```

## Default Mode Network (DMN) and Psychedelics

```
DEFAULT MODE NETWORK (DMN):
  Brain regions active during: self-referential thought, mind-wandering,
    introspection, rumination about the past/future
  Regions: medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex (PCC),
    precuneus, angular gyrus, temporal-parietal junction
  Active during REST; SUPPRESSED during focused task performance

  CLINICAL RELEVANCE:
  Depression: associated with HYPERACTIVATION of DMN
    → Excessive self-referential negative rumination
  PTSD: DMN abnormally active → intrusive memories, hypervigilance
  Addiction: DMN active → craving-related rumination

PSILOCYBIN EFFECT ON DMN:
  fMRI studies (Carhart-Harris et al., 2012, Imperial College London):
  Psilocybin → DECREASED DMN activity (within-network connectivity drops)
  → Increased communication between previously disconnected brain regions
  → "Entropy" of brain network increases: more diverse, less structured patterns

  "EGO DISSOLUTION":
  DMN = neural substrate of the narrative "self"
  DMN suppression → dissolution of self-referential processing
  → Subjective: "losing sense of self"; "oceanic boundlessness"
  → Inverse correlation: degree of DMN suppression = degree of ego dissolution

  ANTIDEPRESSANT HYPOTHESIS:
  One psychedelic session → DMN "reset":
  → Breaks rumination loops (immediate effect)
  → Facilitates neuroplasticity (BDNF upregulation; new connections formed)
  → Sustained effect: changes in personality (openness) persist 1 year+
```

## Clinical Evidence

### Major Trials (as of 2024)

```
DEPRESSION:
  COMPASS Pathways Phase 2B (2022, NEJM Medicine):
    N=233; treatment-resistant depression (TRD)
    Single dose (1 mg / 10 mg / 25 mg) psilocybin + therapy
    25 mg dose: significant response at 3 weeks; primary endpoint met
    Adverse events: transient anxiety, headache; no serious drug-related SAEs
    Limitation: single dose; 3-week endpoint; psychiatric setting required

  Johns Hopkins Phase 2 (Davis et al., 2021, JAMA Psychiatry):
    N=24; Major Depressive Disorder (not treatment-resistant)
    2 sessions (20 mg/70 kg then 30 mg/70 kg), 2 weeks apart
    71% response rate (>50% symptom reduction); 54% remission at 4 weeks
    Effect persisted at 12 months in follow-up (not blinded)

  USONA/NIH Phase 3 (ongoing as of 2024):
    First Phase 3 RCT for psilocybin for MDD
    If successful: regulatory pathway opens for MDMA parallel

PTSD:
  Multiple Phase 2 studies ongoing; less data than MDD

ADDICTION:
  Smoking cessation: Johns Hopkins pilot (80% 6-month abstinence; n=15; unblinded)
  Alcohol use disorder: NYU pilot (positive, small sample)
  → All pilot-scale; Phase 3 data not yet available

EXISTENTIAL ANXIETY (end of life):
  Two parallel RCTs (NYU, Johns Hopkins): psilocybin for cancer patients
  Dramatic reductions in death anxiety; persisted at 6-month follow-up
  Quality of evidence: Phase 2; relatively small; but striking effect sizes
```

## Historical and Cultural Context

```
MESOAMERICAN CEREMONIAL USE:
  "Teonanácatl" (Nahuatl: "flesh of the gods" or "sacred mushroom"):
  Evidence: Aztec codices, Stone mushroom effigies from Oaxaca (1000+ BCE)
  Spanish conquest: missionaries suppressed use; drove underground

  Mazatec tradition (Oaxaca, Mexico):
  María Sabina: curandera; conducted veladas (all-night healing ceremonies)
  1957: R. Gordon Wasson (JP Morgan banker, amateur mycologist) participated
    → Published in Life magazine (1957) → first Western popular account
    → Opened the door to Albert Hofmann (LSD discoverer) identifying psilocybin
    → Hofmann extracted and identified psilocybin (1958, Sandoz Labs)

  ETHICAL NOTE: Wasson's Life article led to massive tourism and disruption
  of Mazatec ceremonial culture. Intellectual property of traditional knowledge
  and appropriate attribution remain contested.

WESTERN RESEARCH WAVE 1 (1960s):
  Sandoz marketed psilocybin ("Indocybin") and LSD for research
  Timothy Leary: Harvard Psilocybin Project (Concord Prison, Good Friday experiments)
  → Promising early results BUT: methodological flaws, political controversy
  → Nixon's "War on Drugs" (1971): Controlled Substances Act → Schedule I
  → All research halted for ~20 years

WESTERN RESEARCH WAVE 2 (2000–present):
  Roland Griffiths, Matthew Johnson (Johns Hopkins)
  Robin Carhart-Harris (Imperial College London → UCSF)
  Rigorous double-blind designs; standardized therapy protocols
  → Psilocybin renaissance with proper clinical methodology
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Psilocybin ≠ psilocin**: Psilocybin is the prodrug (inactive); psilocin is the active compound. This matters pharmacologically (bioavailability, metabolism) and legally (both are Schedule I in the US, but some jurisdictions regulate differently).

**Psilocybin mushrooms are not the same as Amanita muscaria**: Both are called "magic mushrooms" colloquially, but they have entirely different active compounds and mechanisms. Psilocybin → 5-HT2A agonist (serotonergic). A. muscaria → muscimol is a GABA-A agonist + ibotenic acid is NMDA agonist. Different subjective effects, different safety profiles, completely different pharmacology.

**Clinical trials use specific protocols**: The positive clinical results are from carefully controlled settings: screened patients, preparation sessions with therapists, supervised dosing in a specific physical environment, integration sessions afterward. The "therapy" is as important as the drug. Street use without this context has different risk profile and different outcomes.

**DMN suppression ≠ "brain damage"**: The reduction in DMN connectivity is transient and reverses. The therapeutic hypothesis is that chronic depression involves pathologically hyperactive DMN rumination, and a temporary disruption breaks this pattern and allows psychological change during therapy.

**Effect on "healthy" people vs. patients**: Trials show dramatic effects in depressed patients. Effects in "healthy" individuals are different — significant psychedelic experience, possible lasting personality change (increased openness), but not the same clinical benefit as in people with active depression or PTSD.
