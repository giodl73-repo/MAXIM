## Chytridiomycota

```
CHYTRIDS: the most basal (ancestral-branching) fungi
  Aquatic and soil-dwelling; unicellular or simple thallus
  Unique feature: FLAGELLATED ZOOSPORES (the only fungi with flagella)
    → Single posterior flagellum (undulipodium)
    → Ancestral link: LUCA probably had flagella; lost in most fungi
    → This is evidence for the evolutionary link: fungi evolved from
       flagellated opisthokont ancestors

  EXAMPLES:
  Allomyces: freshwater; alternation of generations
  Rhizophydium: parasite of algae and pollen
  Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (Bd): FROG KILLER
    → Causes chytridiomycosis: kills amphibians globally
    → Probably spread by global trade in amphibians (Xenopus used for
       pregnancy tests → shipped worldwide carrying Bd)
    → 90 amphibian species extinct; 500 declining
    → Most devastating infectious disease in vertebrate history (by species loss)

  Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans (Bsal):
    → Salamander-specific; currently devastating European salamanders
    → Not yet in North America; under biosecurity watch
```

## Microsporidia

```
MICROSPORIDIA: former uncertainty now resolved
  Highly reduced obligate intracellular parasites
  Tiny (1–40 µm)
  Originally classified as primitive eukaryotes (no mitochondria visible)
  → Phylogenomics: are fungi; have reduced/transformed mitochondria (mitosomes)

  INFECTION MECHANISM: polar tube injection
  Spore approaches host cell → polar tube (coiled inside spore) fires out
  → Injects sporoplasm directly into host cytoplasm
  → Like a biological injection needle

  DISEASE:
  Nosema apis + N. ceranae: honeybee pathogens → colony collapse contributor
  Nosema bombycis: silkworm pébrine disease (Pasteur's first germ theory work)
  Enterocytozoon bieneusi: human AIDS patients → severe chronic diarrhea
  Encephalitozoon: brain/eye infections in immunocompromised

  EVOLUTIONARY INTEREST:
  Microsporidia have the smallest known eukaryotic genomes (2.3 Mb in some)
  → Extreme genome reduction through obligate parasitism
  → Horizontal gene transfer from host is common
```

## Mucoromycota

```
MUCOROMYCOTA (formerly Zygomycota + allies):
  Terrestrial, mostly saprotrophic or mycorrhizal
  ASEPTATE (coenocytic) hyphae — characteristic
  Sexual reproduction via ZYGOSPORES (thick-walled, resistant)

KEY GROUPS:
  MUCOROMYCOTINA (bread molds):
    Rhizopus stolonifer: common black bread mold; fast-growing
    Mucor: round sporangia; cheese molds, fermentation
    Rhizomucor: thermophilic; compost; mucormycosis cause
    Sporangia: spherical, on sporangiophores

  MORTIERELLOMYCOTINA:
    Soil fungi; important in decomposition
    Mortierella: produce arachidonic acid (omega-6 FA)

  GLOMEROMYCOTINA (arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi):
    OBLIGATE MYCORRHIZAL SYMBIONTS: cannot survive without a host plant
    Form arbuscules: branching tree-like structures inside root cells
    ~80% of all land plants depend on these fungi for phosphorus
    ~250 million year old symbiosis (fossil evidence in Devonian roots)
    Cannot be cultured without a host
    → The most agronomically important fungi in existence

MUCORMYCOSIS (medical):
  Caused by Rhizopus, Mucor, Rhizomucor, Cunninghamella
  Rare but very high mortality (~50%)
  Affects: severely immunocompromised, uncontrolled diabetes, COVID-19 survivors
  "Black fungus" epidemic in India during COVID-19 Delta wave (2021):
    COVID-19 treatment with steroids + hyperglycemia → immune suppression → Mucor
  Angioinvasive: grows into blood vessels → vascular occlusion → necrosis
```

## Ascomycota (Sac Fungi)

**The largest phylum: ~64,000 described species; includes most economically important fungi.**

```
DEFINING FEATURE: ASCUS (plural asci)
  Sac-like cell containing typically 8 ascospores
  Spores produced by meiosis + one mitosis → 8 ascospores
  Forcibly discharged in many species (turgor pressure)

SUBPHYLA:
  SACCHAROMYCOTINA: true yeasts
    Unicellular; reproduce by budding
    Saccharomyces cerevisiae: baker's/brewer's yeast
    Candida: some commensal; some pathogenic
    Yarrowia: lipid production for industrial use

  TAPHRINOMYCOTINA: unusual; early branching
    Schizosaccharomyces pombe: fission yeast (model organism for cell cycle)

  PEZIZOMYCOTINA: largest group
    Truffles (Tuber): underground, ectomycorrhizal; most valuable food fungi
    Cup fungi (Peziza, Morchella): "morels"
    Powdery mildews: plant pathogens
    Aspergillus: industrial + pathogen; citric acid, aflatoxin
    Penicillium: cheese ripening + antibiotic production
    Neurospora: genetics model organism (Beadle & Tatum: one gene-one enzyme)
    Trichoderma: biocontrol agent + cellulase producer

IMPORTANT ASCOMYCETES:
  Saccharomyces cerevisiae:
    Genome sequenced 1996 (first eukaryote genome)
    ~6,000 genes; ~1,000 are essential
    Still the primary eukaryotic genetics model
    Baking, brewing, biofuel production

  Neurospora crassa:
    Circadian rhythm biology (1970s–present)
    DNA methylation research
    Horizontal gene transfer studies

  Aspergillus niger:
    Citric acid production (Pfizer process, 1917): 99% of world's citric acid
    Glucoamylase for glucose syrup production
    Aflatoxin precursor pathways (in other Aspergillus spp.)

  Aspergillus oryzae / A. sojae:
    Koji molds: produce amylases, proteases, lipases
    Used in: sake, soy sauce, miso, shochu production
```

## Basidiomycota (Club Fungi)

```
DEFINING FEATURE: BASIDIUM (plural basidia)
  Club-shaped cell bearing 4 basidiospores on sterigmata (prongs)
  Basidiospores forcibly discharged
  DIKARYOTIC STAGE (n+n): extended; often years or decades
  → Two genetically distinct nuclei coexist in same cell
  → Fusion (karyogamy) only occurs in basidium before meiosis

SUBPHYLA:
  AGARICOMYCOTINA:
    Mushrooms (Agaricales): most familiar fungi
    Bracket fungi (Polyporales): wood decay; Ganoderma, Trametes
    Chanterelles (Cantharellales): ectomycorrhizal; prized edible
    Boletes (Boletales): porcini (Boletus edulis) — finest edible
    Puffballs (Calvatiales): when mature: dry spore masses
    Stinkhorns (Phallales): attract flies with rotting smell
    Jelly fungi (Tremellales): Cryptococcus (pathogen) in this group

  USTILAGINOMYCOTINA: SMUTS
    Ustilago maydis: corn smut → transforms kernels into masses of spores
      → "Huitlacoche" in Mexican cuisine; considered a delicacy
    Ustilago tritici: wheat smut
    ~1,400 species; all plant pathogens

  PUCCINIOMYCOTINA: RUSTS
    Puccinia graminis: wheat stem rust
    Hemileia vastatrix: coffee leaf rust (devastated Ceylon, 1869)
    Melampsora lini: flax rust (classic genetics model: Flor's gene-for-gene)
    ~8,000 species; complex life cycles with multiple spore stages
    Often require two different host species to complete life cycle

SECONDARY METABOLISM IN BASIDIOMYCETES:
  Mushrooms produce: terpenes (flavors), polyketides, alkaloids
  Amanita phalloides: amatoxins (cyclic peptides, RNAP II inhibitors)
  Psilocybe: psilocybin + psilocin (indole alkaloids)
  Ganoderma: polysaccharides, triterpenes (immunomodulatory claims)
```

## "False Fungi" — Common Confusions

```
NOT FUNGI BUT STUDIED BY MYCOLOGISTS:
  OOMYCETES (Water molds):
    Phytophthora, Pythium, Peronospora, Plasmopara
    Kingdom Stramenopila; close to brown algae
    CELL WALL: cellulose (not chitin)
    → Azole antifungals DO NOT work
    → Phenylamide fungicides (metalaxyl) are oomycete-specific
    Historical accident: Phytophthora infestans (potato blight)
      studied by the same people who studied fungi → landed in mycology

  MYXOMYCETES (Slime molds):
    Physarum polycephalum: studied in network optimization research
    Kingdom Amoebozoa
    Move like amoebae; occasionally aggregate
    Not fungi; not plants; not animals

  LICHENS:
    ARE partly fungal (the fungal partner, the mycobiont, is usually Ascomycete)
    The photosynthetic partner = photobiont (alga or cyanobacterium)
    → Lichens are traditionally in mycology but are not purely fungi
    ~20,000 species of lichenized fungi

IMPACT ON DRUG DISCOVERY:
  Antifungal agents that target chitin or ergosterol work on TRUE FUNGI
  Oomycetes require completely different control agents
  → Getting the taxonomy right matters for control strategy
```

## Phylogenomics and Classification

```
THE REVISION ONGOING:
  Traditional (pre-1990): classification by morphology
  → Zygomycota (aseptate); Ascomycota (asci); Basidiomycota (basidia);
    Deuteromycota ("imperfect fungi" lacking known sexual stage)

  Phylogenomics (1990s–present): molecular phylogeny
  → Deuteromycota abandoned: all had asexual stages of Ascomycetes or Basidiomycetes
  → Chytridiomycota paraphyletic: split into multiple phyla
  → Zygomycota polyphyletic: reorganized as Mucoromycota + others
  → Microsporidia placed within Fungi (formerly thought to be primitive non-fungi)

  CURRENT MAJOR PHYLA (consensus 2020):
    Chytridiomycota, Neocallimastigomycota, Blastocladiomycota
    Microsporidia, Cryptomycota (Rozellomycota)
    Mucoromycota (includes Glomeromycota/AM fungi)
    Mortierellomycota, Calcarisporiellomycota
    Basidiomycota, Ascomycota (the two "dikarya" = largest phyla)

  NOTE: new phyla being described regularly as environmental sequencing expands
  The tree is still being resolved at the base
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Imperfect fungi" (Deuteromycota) is abolished**: Historically, fungi without known sexual stages were placed in Deuteromycota. Molecular phylogenetics showed all of them were simply asexual forms of Ascomycetes or Basidiomycetes. The category no longer exists in formal classification — though you may encounter it in older literature.

**Glomus/Rhizophagus is not in Basidiomycota or Ascomycota**: Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (formerly Glomus, now often Rhizophagus) are in Glomeromycota/Mucoromycota — a separate lineage. They're not the same as ectomycorrhizal fungi (which are Basidiomycetes and some Ascomycetes like truffle).

**Psilocybe is a Basidiomycete**: Psilocybin-containing mushrooms (Psilocybe, Panaeolus, etc.) are in Basidiomycota (mushrooms). Their basidiospore-producing gills are where spores form — the same structural plan as edible mushrooms.

**Most fungi cannot be cultured in pure culture**: Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (Glomeromycota) cannot be grown without a plant root. Many other fungi are nutritionally obligate (rusts, smuts, powdery mildews). The cultivable fungi studied in labs are not representative of the kingdom's diversity.
