## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|           FUNGAL AND OOMYCETE PLANT PATHOGENS: SCOPE            |
|                                                                  |
|  MAJOR GROUPS:                                                  |
|  Rust fungi (Pucciniales): obligate biotrophs; cereal crops    |
|  Smut fungi (Ustilaginales): grain crops; corn                 |
```

## Rust Fungi (Pucciniales)

Rusts are obligate biotrophs — they must infect living plant tissue; they cannot be grown on laboratory media.

### Wheat Stem Rust — Puccinia graminis f.sp. tritici

```
LIFE CYCLE: complex, with 5 spore stages and 2 hosts
  ALTERNATE HOST: Berberis (barberry shrub)
    Pycnia stage (spring): sexual fusion on barberry leaf (+ and - mating types)
    Aecia stage: aeciospores form; wind-dispersed to wheat

  PRIMARY HOST: wheat and other cereals
    Uredinia stage: urediniospores (repeating season stage)
      → "Summer rust" — causes epidemic spread
      → Orange-red pustules on wheat stems; epidemics within weeks
    Telia stage: teliospores (overwintering stage)
      → Thick-walled, black; germinate in spring → basidiospores
    Basidiospores → infect barberry → restart sexual cycle

  INFECTION:
    Urediniospores land on wheat → germinate in humidity → appressoria form
    → Direct penetration through stoma → haustoria inside cells
    → HAUSTORIUM: specialized structure that penetrates host cell
      wall but NOT membrane → nutrient absorption
    → Obligate biotroph: takes nutrients from living cells; doesn't kill first

THE Ug99 THREAT:
  1999: New race of P. graminis detected in Uganda
  Named TTKS → Ug99 (Uganda, 1999)
  Why dangerous:
    → Virulent against Sr31: the main resistance gene deployed in >80% of
      global wheat varieties at the time
    → "Super race": overcomes multiple resistance genes
  Spread: Africa (Ethiopia, Kenya, Sudan, Zimbabwe) → Middle East (Yemen,
    Iran) → potential for South Asia; estimated to potentially affect
    80%+ of currently grown wheat varieties
  Potential crop loss: ~20% of global wheat (wheat is ~20% of human calories)
  Response:
    → International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center (CIMMYT): emergency breeding
    → Deploying pyramided resistance (multiple resistance genes, no single target)
    → "Durable resistance" breeding programs
    → Sr35, Sr22, Sr57, Lr34 (multisite resistance genes)
  Current status: spread slower than worst-case; resistant varieties being deployed
    → Not an emergency yet; but biosecurity threat maintained
<!-- @editor[content/P2]: Ug99 status may be outdated — check current spread status and whether CIMMYT deployment of pyramided resistance varieties has changed the threat assessment since original writing -->
```

### Flax Rust — The Gene-for-Gene Model

```
MELAMPSORA LINI (flax rust) and Linum usitatissimum (flax):
  H.H. Flor (1940s–1960s): classic experimental system
  Showed: for each rust virulence gene, there is a corresponding
    plant resistance (R) gene
  "Gene-for-gene hypothesis":
    Plant R gene → recognizes specific pathogen Avr (avirulence) gene product
    → Triggers hypersensitive response (HR): local programmed cell death
    → Stops pathogen spread

  This is the foundational model of plant immunity:
    R protein (plant) = NLR (nucleotide-binding, leucine-rich repeat)
    Avr protein (pathogen) = effector
    R recognizes Avr → signal cascade → HR → disease resistance

  APPLICATION:
  Understanding gene-for-gene allows:
    → Identification of resistance genes (R gene cloning)
    → Breeding: stack multiple R genes (pyramiding)
    → Molecular markers for resistance gene presence
    → BUT: races that mutate Avr → overcome R gene protection
    → Arms race between pathogen diversification and plant breeding
```

### Coffee Leaf Rust — Hemileia vastatrix

```
COFFEE LEAF RUST: changed history of agriculture

1869: Coffee leaf rust destroyed Ceylon's (Sri Lanka) coffee industry
  → British Ceylon was the world's #1 coffee producer
  → Within 20 years: complete collapse of coffee agriculture
  → Ceylon pivoted to tea → British "tea drinking culture" = result of rust
  → Central America: massive economic disruption 2012–2016
    ("La Roya" epidemic; affected 50-70% of plantings in some countries)
  → Estimated $3+ billion in losses over 2012–2017

HEMILEIA VASTATRIX:
  Infects only coffee (Coffea arabica most susceptible)
  Complex races; resistance breaks down
  Some C. canephora (robusta) varieties more resistant → blend economics
  Fungicide (copper hydroxide, triazoles): expensive; used on plantations
  Breeding: requires C. arabica × resistant wild relatives
```

## Smut Fungi (Ustilaginales)

```
SMUTS: basidiomycetes that cause smut diseases of grasses and cereals

CORN SMUT (Ustilago maydis):
  Infects corn kernels → swollen, tumor-like galls filled with black spores
  Cosmopolitan pathogen; reduces yield
  BUT: in Mexico ("huitlacoche" / "cuitlacoche"):
    → Considered a delicacy ("Mexican truffle")
    → Earthy, mushroomy, smoky flavor
    → Active market; restaurants specifically cultivate infected corn
  RESEARCH MODEL:
    Genetics and cell biology model organism for Basidiomycetes
    Dikaryotic growth, cell biology of infection well-studied

WHEAT STINKING SMUT (Tilletia tritici / T. laevis):
  "Stinking smut" or "bunt": replaces grain with black spore balls
  Spores have fish-like smell (trimethylamine derivative)
  Contaminated threshed grain: entire lot unusable + contaminates equipment
  Seed treatment with fungicides (thiram, carboxin) controls effectively
  Organic wheat farming: loose smut challenge → certified disease-free seed

COVERED SMUTS (Ustilago hordei): barley and oat smut
  Spore masses protected by seed coat until threshing
  Seed treatment essential
```

## Powdery Mildews (Erysiphales)

```
POWDERY MILDEW CHARACTERISTICS:
  White powdery growth ON surface of leaves (not inside)
  Obligate biotrophs: must infect living tissue
  Ascomycete (Erysiphales); their own order
  Haustoria: penetrate epidermal cells; external hyphae spread across leaf surface

  WIDE HOST RANGE (genus-specific):
  Blumeria graminis f.sp. tritici: wheat
  Erysiphe necator (Uncinula necator): grapevine — major wine production problem
  Oidium tuckeri: powdery mildew of grape (same as above)
  Podosphaera leucotricha: apple
  Erysiphe pisi: peas; Erysiphe cichoracearum: cucurbits

  GRAPE POWDERY MILDEW HISTORY:
  1845: Oidium tuckeri discovered in England; spread to France within a year
  Devastated French vineyards before sulfur treatments developed
  → Sulfur dusting (19th c.) remains the primary organic control
  → Modern alternatives: copper, bicarbonates, sterol biosynthesis inhibitors

  RESISTANCE MECHANISMS:
  Pm genes in wheat: over 50 known resistance genes against Blumeria graminis
  MLO (mlo) mutants: loss of function gives broad resistance (barley, wheat)
    → Pleiotropic cost: accelerated senescence, but useful in practice
    → mlo-based varieties deployed commercially
```

## Botrytis cinerea — Noble Rot and Gray Mold

```
BOTRYTIS CINEREA: The Jekyll and Hyde pathogen

AS GRAY MOLD (devastating):
  Necrotroph: kills tissue first, then feeds (opposite of biotroph)
  Host range: >200 plant species including strawberry, grapes, tomatoes
  Infection: under humid conditions; enters through wounds, old petals
  Postharvest: major problem in stored fruit; strawberry, grapes, lettuce
  Control: fungicides (fenhexamid, boscalid, fluazinam); resistant varieties
  Resistance: Botrytis develops multidrug resistance rapidly
    → B. cinerea has extensive natural genetic diversity → fast adaptation

AS "NOBLE ROT" (beneficial in winemaking):
  When grape is infected in very specific conditions:
    Dry warm days alternating with misty cool nights
    → Botrytis removes water from berry → concentrates sugars + flavors
    → Adds specific compounds: gluconic acid, glycerol, botrytis-specific
       volatile compounds (rose oxide, sotolon, geraniol modified)
  Result: intensely sweet, complex dessert wine
  → Sauternes (Château d'Yquem): Botrytis essential
  → German Trockenbeerenauslese (TBA): same effect
  → Hungarian Tokaji Aszú: 500+ year tradition
  → Australian botrytis semillon, Austrian Ruster Ausbruch
  The line between gray mold disaster and noble rot: luck + terroir + timing
```

## Vascular Wilts

```
FUSARIUM WILT (Fusarium oxysporum formae speciales):
  Infection: through roots; colonizes vascular tissue (xylem)
  Mechanism: blocks water transport; produces fusaric acid (phytotoxic)
  Symptoms: wilting (water stress), yellowing, brown vascular staining
  Host-specific: each f.sp. attacks specific host
    F.o. f.sp. cubense: banana wilt (Panama disease)
    F.o. f.sp. lycopersici: tomato wilt
    F.o. f.sp. vasinfectum: cotton wilt

  BANANA PANAMA DISEASE:
  Race 1: destroyed Gros Michel banana (the commercially dominant 1950s variety)
    → The pre-1960 banana tasted different from today's
    → Gros Michel: extinct from commercial cultivation
    → Cavendish replaced it: resistant to Race 1
  Race 4 (Tropical Race 4 = TR4): now threatens Cavendish
    → First detected 1990s; spreading globally
    → No resistant commercial replacement for Cavendish yet
    → Global $10 billion banana industry at risk
    → No effective chemical treatment; soil stays infected for decades
    → Biocontrol, resistant varieties in development

VERTICILLIUM WILT (Verticillium dahliae, V. albo-atrum):
  Similar mechanism to Fusarium; affects different hosts
  Strawberry, potato, cotton, olive, many ornamentals
  V. dahliae: soil persists as microsclerotia for 10+ years
    → Resistant to rotation; limits management options
```

## Phytophthora — The Critical Oomycete

**Taxonomic note**: Phytophthora is NOT a true fungus. It is in Kingdom Stramenopila (same as brown algae, diatoms). Cell wall = cellulose (not chitin). Phylogenetically closer to algae than to fungi. Studied in mycology by historical tradition — because it behaves like a fungus in many ways and was classified with fungi before molecular phylogenetics.

```
PHYTOPHTHORA INFESTANS — LATE BLIGHT OF POTATO:
  The Irish Potato Famine (1845–1852):
    Ireland: 30–40% of population dependent on potato as primary food
    Potato = calorie-dense, space-efficient, cold-climate crop
    1845–1849: Phytophthora infestans destroys potato crop 3+ years running
    Result: ~1 million dead (starvation, disease), ~2 million emigrated
    Ireland's population: 8 million (1845) → 6.5 million by 1851
    Ongoing emigration: Ireland's population never recovered to pre-famine levels
    Political consequences: Irish diaspora, anti-British sentiment, home rule movement

    POLITICAL-ECONOMIC CONTEXT:
    Food was being exported from Ireland to Britain throughout the famine
    (grain, livestock) — Famine is now understood as political economy failure
    as much as fungal (oomycete) disaster

  P. INFESTANS BIOLOGY:
    Heterothallic: requires two mating types (A1 + A2) for sexual reproduction
    A2 mating type was absent from Europe until the 1970s
    → All epidemic spread in Europe until 1970s: clonal A1 strains only
    → Introduction of A2: now sexual reproduction → more genetic diversity
    → New recombinant strains → harder to control
    Zoospores: flagellated (oomycete = still has flagella, unlike true fungi)
    → Water-dispersed; rain splash spreads infection
    → Warm (but not hot) + wet = epidemic conditions

  PHYTOPHTHORA RAMORUM (Sudden Oak Death):
    California/Oregon coast: kills coast live oak, tanoak
    UK: killing larch (different host; P. ramorum has wide host range)
    Nursery trade vector: spread via infected Rhododendron/Viburnum shipments

  PHYTOPHTHORA CINNAMOMI (Cinnamon fungus / Jarrah dieback):
    Western Australia: devastating to native Banksia, Xanthorrhoea, Kunzea
    Invasive to Australian native ecosystem; no evolved resistance
    Worst plant disease in Australia by species affected
    Spreads via soil movement, vehicle tyres, water movement

OOMYCETE CONTROL (why it differs from fungi):
  Fungicides targeting ergosterol (azoles, polyenes) DO NOT WORK
  → Oomycetes have NO ergosterol
  Oomycete-specific options:
    Metalaxyl (phenylamide): inhibits oomycete RNAP I (specific to oomycetes)
    Phosphonate fungicides (phosphorous acid): unclear; possibly inhibit host
    Copper hydroxide: broad spectrum; still used in organic farming
    Resistance to metalaxyl: now widespread → rotating chemistry required
```

## Dutch Elm Disease and Chestnut Blight

### Dutch Elm Disease

```
DUTCH ELM DISEASE: Ophiostoma ulmi + O. novo-ulmi (Ascomycetes)
  Vector: bark beetles (Scolytus spp., Hylurgopinus rufipes)
  Mechanism: beetles breed in diseased/dead elm → carry spores on body
    → Fly to healthy elm → feed at branch axils → introduce fungus
    → Fungus grows in xylem → produces toxic compounds (ophiostatins)
    → Elm produces tyloses (blockages) to wall off infection → kills itself
  Devastating: elms were dominant street tree in N. American and European cities
    → 20 million elms killed in UK alone (1970s–2000s outbreak)
    → American elm: nearly eliminated from urban landscapes
  Resistant varieties exist (Princeton elm, Jefferson elm): slowly being replanted
```

### Chestnut Blight

```
CHESTNUT BLIGHT: Cryphonectria parasitica (Ascomycete)
  Origins: East Asia (China); Asian chestnuts (Castanea mollissima) evolved resistance
  Arrived in New York: ~1904 (accidental introduction on ornamental chestnut from Asia)
  Spread: across entire range of American chestnut (Castanea dentata) within 40 years
  Effect: killed virtually every mature American chestnut
    → ~4 billion trees; dominant canopy tree in eastern N. American forests
    → Trees still root-sprout but cannot reach maturity before reinfection

  MECHANISM:
    Infects through wounds in bark → grows in cambium → produces oxalic acid
    → pH drop kills cambium tissue → canker expands → girdling
    → Roots remain alive; sprouts reinfected each time they grow to bark-thin stage

  RESTORATION EFFORTS:
    Backcross breeding: American × Chinese chestnut × backcross
    → American Chestnut Foundation: ~15/16 American genome + blight resistance gene
    Transgenic approach: wheat oxalate oxidase gene introduced into American chestnut
    → Neutralizes oxalic acid → allows tree to survive canker
    → SUNY ESF research: field trials; regulatory approvals pending (2024)
<!-- @editor[content/P2]: Transgenic chestnut regulatory status dated 2024 — check whether USDA deregulation decision has landed -->
    Hypovirulence biocontrol: CHV1 virus infects Cryphonectria → attenuates virulence
    → Works in Europe; American strains more genetically diverse → less effective
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Phytophthora is not a fungus**: This cannot be overstated. Phytophthora infestans caused the Irish Famine. It is not a fungus. It is an oomycete (water mold) in the Stramenopila kingdom. This matters because fungicides that target ergosterol (the standard approach) have NO effect on oomycetes. The biology, control strategies, and evolutionary history are completely different.

**Biotrophs vs. necrotrophs**: Biotrophs (rusts, powdery mildews, smuts) require living tissue. Necrotrophs (Botrytis, Sclerotinia) kill tissue and then feed on it. This distinction fundamentally affects control: killing the plant cell (as some defenses do) stops a biotroph but has no effect on a necrotroph, which is happy to feed on dead tissue.

<!-- @editor[bridge/P3]: The gene pyramiding → HAART analogy is excellent; could add a one-liner bridge to defense-in-depth / layered security architecture for the CS-minded reader -->
**Wheat rust resistance gene pyramiding**: No single R gene is durable long-term — the pathogen evolves. The strategy now is pyramiding multiple R genes with different recognition specificities. Breaking all of them simultaneously requires multiple mutations = much lower probability. This is analogous to combination antiretroviral therapy (HAART) for HIV — targeting multiple points simultaneously reduces resistance evolution.

**The Cavendish banana crisis**: The banana in your supermarket is a specific cultivar (Cavendish). It replaced the Gros Michel (which tasted different — more banana-flavored by historical accounts) after Panama disease Race 1 devastated that cultivar. Tropical Race 4 (TR4) now threatens the Cavendish worldwide. This is an exact parallel — monoculture + globally distributed trade = global vulnerability.
