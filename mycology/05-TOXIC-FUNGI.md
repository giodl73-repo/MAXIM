```

|                                                                  |

|                                                                  |
```

## Amatoxins — The Lethal Mechanism

### Amanita phalloides: Death Cap

```
AMANITA PHALLOIDES ("Death Cap"):
  Appearance: pale green to yellow-green cap; white gills; skirt; volva (cup)
  Habitat: ectomycorrhizal with oak and other trees; European origin
  INTRODUCED to North America with oak plantings; expanding in California, PNW
  Taste: reported mild, pleasant → no warning

AMATOXIN CONTENT:
  α-amanitin: principal lethal toxin
  β-amanitin, γ-amanitin: additional congeners
  Phalloidins: phallotoxins (actin polymerization inhibitors)
    → More toxic in vitro; not absorbed orally → less important in poisoning
  Amanitins: cyclic octapeptides; heat-stable; not inactivated by cooking
    LD₅₀ in mice: ~0.4 mg/kg (extremely potent)
    One medium Death Cap (50 g): ~5–7 mg α-amanitin
    Lethal human dose: estimated ~0.1 mg/kg → 7–8 mg for 70 kg adult
    → Half a Death Cap can kill an adult

MECHANISM: RNA POLYMERASE II INHIBITION
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  α-amanitin → enters cells → binds RNA polymerase II (RNAP II) |
|                                                                  |
|  RNAP II = the enzyme that transcribes all protein-coding genes |
|  (mRNA synthesis)                                               |
|                                                                  |
|  α-amanitin binds in the RNAP II "clamp" region →              |
|  Inhibits translocation of RNA-DNA hybrid during elongation     |
|  → Transcription arrested → no new mRNA → no new protein       |
|                                                                  |
|  WHO DIES FIRST:                                                |
|  Highest-turnover cells (most protein synthesis needed):       |
|  → Hepatocytes (liver) — first organ damaged                   |
|  → Intestinal epithelial cells — GI symptoms                  |
|  → Kidney tubular cells — secondary damage                     |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

MOLECULAR BIOLOGY CONNECTION:
  α-amanitin is used as a RESEARCH TOOL:
  → At 1 µg/mL: inhibits RNAP II (eukaryotic mRNA synthesis)
  → At 1 mg/mL: inhibits RNAP III (5S rRNA, tRNA synthesis)
  → Does NOT inhibit RNAP I (rRNA) or prokaryotic RNAP
  → Used to distinguish transcription by RNAP I vs II vs III in vitro
  → Every molecular biology lab has α-amanitin in the freezer
```

### Clinical Course of Amatoxin Poisoning

```
THE FOUR PHASES (without treatment):
  Phase 1: Latent period (6–24 hours)
    → No symptoms despite toxin absorption
    → Amatoxin absorbed in small intestine; distributed to liver
    → DANGEROUS: people may not seek help

  Phase 2: GI phase (24–72 hours)
    → Violent abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, profuse watery diarrhea
    → Severe dehydration; electrolyte disturbance
    → Patient feels acutely ill

  Phase 3: Apparent recovery (72–96 hours)
    → Symptoms improve → patient feels better
    → ACTUALLY: massive hepatocyte death occurring; transaminases rising
    → FALSE RECOVERY — the most dangerous misconception
    → Many patients sent home during this phase; or refuse hospital

  Phase 4: Hepatic/renal failure (4–9 days)
    → Liver failure: jaundice, coagulopathy, hepatic encephalopathy
    → Renal failure: secondary to hepatotoxicity
    → Death from multi-organ failure within 7–10 days if untreated

TREATMENT:
  No specific antidote to α-amanitin
  Supportive: aggressive fluid replacement; activated charcoal (early)
  Silibinin (milk thistle extract): blocks hepatic uptake of amanitin via OATP
    → IV silibinin: reduces liver damage if given early; available in Europe
  Liver transplantation: last resort; indicated when liver failure established
  Outcome: if treatment early (<24 hr of ingestion) → good recovery possible
           if treatment late or liver transplant required → high mortality
```

## Orellanine — The Invisible Kidney Toxin

```
CORTINARIUS spp. (Webcaps):
  One of the largest mushroom genera (~2,000 species)
  Many edible; several groups produce orellanine

ORELLANINE TOXIN:
  Chemical: a bipyridine dione; water-soluble; heat-stable
  Mechanism: NOT fully elucidated
    → Accumulates in kidney proximal tubule cells
    → Oxidative stress → tubular necrosis → renal failure
    → Also chelates copper → interferes with antioxidant enzymes
  Dose: ~20 mg orellanine = ~20–40 g fresh mushroom
  LD₅₀ (rat): ~10–20 mg/kg

THE EXTREME DELAY:
  Onset of symptoms: 2–3 weeks after ingestion
  → All memory of mushroom consumption may be lost
  → Even the mushroom discarded/forgotten
  Symptoms: initially nonspecific (fatigue, thirst, lower back pain)
  → Gradually: oliguria (decreased urine) → renal failure
  → Diagnosis: often discovered when patient presents with
    renal failure of unknown cause; dietary history crucial

OUTCOME:
  Chronic interstitial nephritis → dialysis dependency
  Up to 30% require permanent kidney transplantation
  If identified early (still rare due to delay) → supportive care only
  No antidote, no specific treatment

SPECIES:
  Cortinarius rubellus (C. speciosissimus): Orange webcap — Europe
  Cortinarius orellanus: Fool's webcap — Europe
  Cortinarius gentilis: Deadly cort — North America, Europe
  → All have rusty-brown spore print (Cortinarius characteristic)
  → Young Cortinarius: cortina (cobweb-like veil) → disappears with age
```

## Muscimol and Ibotenic Acid — The Fly Agaric

```
AMANITA MUSCARIA ("Fly Agaric"):
  Iconic red-cap-with-white-spots mushroom
  Ectomycorrhizal with birch, pine, spruce
  Found across northern hemisphere
  Also: Amanita pantherina (Panther cap) — more dangerous by mg equivalent

TOXINS:
  Ibotenic acid: excitatory amino acid; structural analog of glutamate
    → NMDA receptor agonist (excitotoxic)
  Muscimol: decarboxylation product of ibotenic acid (drying converts IA → muscimol)
    → GABA-A receptor agonist (inhibitory; sedating/dissociative)
    → 5–10× more potent than ibotenic acid
    → Main psychoactive agent in the dried mushroom

CLINICAL EFFECTS:
  30 min – 2 hr onset
  Confusion, delirium, ataxia, visual distortions
  "Entheogenic" effects: religious/mystical use in Siberian shamanism
    (Siberian reindeer herders; "urine drinking" tradition to recycle muscimol)
  Anticholinergic effects also present (salivation, lacrimation, urination — SLUDE)
<!-- @editor[content/P2]: SLUDE symptoms (salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, emesis) are cholinergic, not anticholinergic — the sentence above says "anticholinergic" but then lists cholinergic symptoms. Clarify: muscimol is GABAergic; the cholinergic/muscarinic confusion is the historical naming accident -->
    → Actually from muscarinic receptors? Debate ongoing
  Deaths: rare; require very large doses
  Treatment: supportive; benzodiazepines for seizures if severe
    → ATROPINE IS CONTRAINDICATED (does not treat muscimol toxicity;
       worsens anticholinergic effects if those are present)

INTERESTING PHARMACOLOGY:
  Muscimol produces different subjective effects from psilocybin
  → Psilocybin: 5-HT2A agonist (serotonergic)
  → Muscimol: GABA-A agonist (GABAergic) + ibotenic acid NMDA component
  → Different receptor targets → qualitatively different experiences
  Muscimol analogs: THIP (gaboxadol) was in Phase III clinical trials for insomnia
    → Discontinued after safety concerns but legitimate pharmacology
```

## Other Toxin Categories

### GI Irritants

```
MANY SPECIES CAUSE GI IRRITATION:
  Mechanism: often unknown; some toxic peptides, some irritant terpenes
  Species: Chlorophyllum molybdites (false parasol) — most common mushroom
    poisoning in North America; causes severe GI illness
    → Green spore print (unique in large edible-looking mushrooms)
  Omphalotus spp. (jack-o'-lantern): illudin S (sesquiterpene) → vomiting
  Paxillus involutus: immune-mediated hemolysis (worse with repeated exposure)
  Scleroderma (earthball): very bitter; peppery; GI irritant

ONSET: 30 min – 4 hours
TREATMENT: supportive; most self-limiting within 24 hours
LETHALITY: very low but severe discomfort
```

### Disulfiram-Like Reaction (Coprinus, Coprinopsis)

```
INKY CAP MUSHROOMS: Coprinopsis atramentaria
  Safe to eat alone
  BUT: combined with alcohol → disulfiram reaction

MECHANISM:
  Coprine in mushroom → metabolized to 1-aminocyclopropanol
  → Inhibits acetaldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH)
  → Acetaldehyde accumulates after alcohol ingestion
  → Flushing, palpitations, nausea, hypotension

  Same mechanism as disulfiram (Antabuse) used in alcoholism treatment
  Reaction window: 5 days after mushroom ingestion + alcohol = reaction
  → Patients don't connect the symptoms to a meal from days ago
```

### Gyromitrin (False Morels)

```
GYROMITRA ESCULENTA ("False morel"):
  Looks somewhat like a morel (wrinkled cap) but smaller; reddish-brown

TOXIN: gyromitrin → hydrolyzed to N-methyl-N-formylhydrazine → monomethylhydrazine (MMH)
  MMH = rocket fuel component (same chemical!)
  Mechanism: inhibits pyridoxal phosphate (B6) dependent enzymes
    → GABA synthesis inhibited → seizures
    → MetHb formation, hemolysis
  Volatile: MMH volatilizes during cooking
    → Boiling or prolonged drying removes most MMH
    → "Edible with proper preparation" in some traditions
    → But: vapor during cooking also hazardous (inhalation)
  GEOGRAPHIC VARIATION: some populations of G. esculenta have low gyromitrin
    Others have high; unpredictable
  Deaths: recorded in central Europe (Germany, Scandinavia)
  True morels (Morchella): DO NOT contain gyromitrin; safe when cooked
```

## Identification Principles to Avoid Poisoning

```
THE MULTIPLE-CHARACTER RULE:
  Never identify for eating based on one feature

FEATURES TO CHECK:
  Cap color AND texture (dry? slimy? scaly?)
  Gill attachment (free? adnexed? decurrent? pores instead of gills?)
  Gill color AND spore print color (white/cream/pink/brown/black/green/rust)
  Stem features: hollow? solid? ring? volva (cup at base)?
  Flesh: color change when cut? smell?
  Substrate: soil? wood? dung?
  Habitat: forest type? associated trees?
  Season

SPORE PRINT (critical tool):
  Place cap gill-side down on paper overnight
  White spore print: Amanita (DANGER), Marasmius, Pleurotus, Cantharellus
  Pink: Entoloma, Clitopilus (some toxic)
  Brown/rusty: Cortinarius (orellanine risk!), Galerina (amatoxin risk!)
  Black: Coprinus group, Panaeolus (some psilocybin)
  Purple-brown/dark: Psilocybe, Stropharia
  Green: Chlorophyllum molybdites (GI toxin)

THE AMANITA RULE:
  ALL Amanita with: white gills + white spore print + ring + volva = SUSPECT
  → Even if it looks like something edible
  → Death cap is the main exception to "looks edible"
```

| Toxin | Symptom onset | Main organ affected | Treatment |
|-------|--------------|--------------------|-----------|
| Amatoxin | 6–24 hr (then false recovery) | Liver (then kidney) | Silibinin IV; liver transplant |
| Orellanine | 2–3 weeks | Kidney | Supportive only; dialysis/transplant |
| Muscimol/ibotenic acid | 30 min – 2 hr | CNS | Supportive; NO atropine |
| Gyromitrin | 6–12 hr | Liver, blood | Pyridoxine; methylene blue |

## Common Confusion Points

**The false recovery is the killer**: Amatoxin poisoning victims who feel better at 72 hours are NOT recovering — their liver is in the process of massive failure. The apparent recovery is because GI cells have died (no more GI symptoms) but hepatocyte death has not yet caused measurable organ failure. This is the window for intervention; missed, it leads to death.

**Cooking does not inactivate amatoxins**: Amatoxins are heat-stable. Frying, boiling, or drying a Death Cap does not make it safe. This is in contrast to some toxins (gyromitrin, muscimol) which are volatile or hydrolyzed by cooking.

**Amanita muscaria is not always red**: Color varies with geography, weather, and age. Young caps may be white or yellow. Old caps may be faded. White Amanita muscaria = Amanita muscaria var. guessowii (pale yellow) or formosa (yellow). The "classic" red is one variety.

**Atropine is contraindicated in A. muscaria poisoning in many cases**: The old teaching was to use atropine for muscarinic symptoms (SLUDE — salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, emesis). Current guidance: atropine may worsen outcomes because it adds anticholinergic toxicity. Supportive care without atropine is now preferred except when SLUDE symptoms are very severe.

**Galerina marginata is widely distributed and dangerous**: This small brown mushroom grows on rotting wood across the northern hemisphere. It contains the same amatoxins as Amanita phalloides but is often overlooked because it doesn't look "dangerous." Many poisonings occur when foragers confuse it with Pholiota (scalecap) or Hypholoma (brick cap). It has a brown spore print (which Amanita does not).
