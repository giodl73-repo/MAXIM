## Candida — From Commensal to Pathogen

### Candida albicans

```
CANDIDA ALBICANS:
  Commensal: lives in gut, oral cavity, vagina of ~50% of healthy adults
  Pathogen: when host immunity weakened → opportunistic
  Major virulence factor: DIMORPHIC SWITCH
    Yeast form: commensal; colonizes mucosa
    Hyphal form: invasive; tissue penetration; biofilm
    Switch trigger: body temperature (37°C), serum, CO₂, neutral pH

  DISEASE SPECTRUM:
  Superficial mucosal (oropharyngeal, vaginal):
    "Thrush" (oral): common in infants, denture users, antibiotics, HIV
    Vulvovaginal candidiasis: ~75% of women experience at least once
  Deep organ / invasive:
    Risk factors: central venous catheter, broad-spectrum antibiotics,
      immunosuppression (neutropenia, steroids), GI surgery
    Candidemia (bloodstream): mortality 30–45% despite treatment
    → Biofilm on catheters → source of bloodstream infection
    → Biofilm: resistant to fluconazole at high concentrations

  TREATMENT:
  Fluconazole: first-line for most Candida; oral availability
  Echinocandins (caspofungin, micafungin, anidulafungin): for invasive disease
    → β-glucan synthase inhibitors; low toxicity; no azole resistance cross
  Amphotericin B: reserved for severe/refractory disease (nephrotoxic)
  Resistance: C. albicans relatively azole-sensitive; C. glabrata more resistant
```

### Candida auris — The Emerging Threat

```
CANDIDA AURIS: globally emerging multidrug-resistant pathogen
  First described: Japan 2009 (ear isolate — hence "auris")
  Simultaneously emerged in multiple continents (2012–2016)
  → Simultaneous independent emergence from same clade → likely thermal adaptation
    linked to climate change (C. auris growth at 40–42°C suggests
    warming selected for this trait — hypothesis by Casadevall 2019)

RESISTANCE PROFILE:
  Azole resistant: >90% of isolates
  Echinocandin resistant: ~5–10% (increasing)
  Polyene resistant: ~10–30%
  PAN-RESISTANT: increasing frequency; limited to last-resort options

EPIDEMIOLOGY:
  Hospital outbreaks: difficult to eradicate from surfaces
    → Resistant to standard hospital disinfectants (chlorhexidine, quats)
    → Requires sodium hypochlorite (bleach) or specialized agents
  Colonizes: skin (including axilla, groin); can persist for months
  High-risk patients: ICU, immunocompromised, indwelling devices
  Mortality: invasive C. auris bloodstream infection: 30–60%

  CDC: classified as an "Urgent Threat" (highest category)
  WHO: added to Fungal Priority Pathogen List (2022)

DETECTION:
  Traditional lab: often misidentified as other Candida species
  → Requires MALDI-TOF or molecular identification
  → Many early cases went undetected (thought to be C. haemulonii)
```

## Aspergillus fumigatus — Invasive Aspergillosis

```
ASPERGILLUS FUMIGATUS:
  Ubiquitous mold; conidia in every breath (100–300 conidia/m³ typical outdoor air)
  Thermotolerant: grows at 37–50°C → adapted to mammalian body temperature
  Small conidia (2–3 µm): reach deep alveoli when inhaled
  In immunocompetent: conidia cleared by alveolar macrophages → no disease

INVASIVE ASPERGILLOSIS (IA):
  Requires: neutropenia (absolute neutrophil count <500/µL) or
    impaired macrophage function (corticosteroids, transplant immunosuppression)
  Pathogenesis:
    Conidia germinate in lung → hyphae → invade blood vessels (angioinvasion)
    → Disseminate hematogenously to brain, liver, kidney
    → Thrombosis, hemorrhage → infarction
  Mortality: 30–90% depending on host immune status and treatment timing

  RISK POPULATIONS:
  Hematological malignancy (AML/ALL): most at risk
  Stem cell transplant (HSCT): especially during engraftment
  Solid organ transplant: lung transplant highest risk
  COVID-19 severe cases: COVID-19-associated pulmonary aspergillosis (CAPA)
    → Recognized as major complication of ICU COVID-19

  TREATMENT:
  Voriconazole: first-line (azole; Aspergillus-specific spectrum)
  Isavuconazole: alternative first-line
  Liposomal amphotericin B: alternative or combination
  Echinocandins: for salvage or combination

ALLERGIC BRONCHOPULMONARY ASPERGILLOSIS (ABPA):
  Not invasive; allergic reaction to colonizing Aspergillus in airways
  Complication of asthma or cystic fibrosis
  Treatment: corticosteroids + itraconazole
```

## Cryptococcus neoformans — Meningitis

```
CRYPTOCOCCUS NEOFORMANS (and C. gattii):
  Basidiomycete yeast; unique polysaccharide capsule
  ENCAPSULATED: polysaccharide capsule = major virulence factor
    → Inhibits phagocytosis by macrophages
    → Inhibits opsonization
    → Causes immune evasion and chronic persistent infection

  HABITAT: soil enriched with bird (especially pigeon) guano
  EXPOSURE: ubiquitous; most people encounter it; cleared asymptomatically
    unless immunocompromised

  PATHOGENESIS:
  Inhalation → lung (primary) → usually contained
  In immunocompromised: disseminates via bloodstream → crosses blood-brain barrier
  Cryptococcal meningitis: severe, chronic; 6+ month treatment
    → Most common cause of meningitis in HIV/AIDS patients (without ART)
    → Global: ~180,000 deaths/year (primarily in sub-Saharan Africa)
    → Most preventable with ART adherence

  TREATMENT:
  Induction: amphotericin B + flucytosine (2 weeks)
  Consolidation: fluconazole (8 weeks)
  Maintenance: fluconazole (1 year+)
  Flucytosine: pyrimidine analog; selectively taken up by fungi (cytosine permease)
    → Converted to 5-fluorouracil inside fungal cell → inhibits RNA and DNA synthesis
    → Very narrow antifungal spectrum: mainly Candida, Cryptococcus

CRYPTOCOCCUS GATTII:
  Different host range: primarily infects immunocompetent individuals
  Vancouver Island outbreak (1999): C. gattii in Pacific NW
    → Previously thought restricted to tropical/subtropical regions
    → Climate range expansion? Still debated
  Higher mortality than C. neoformans (less expected, less treated aggressively)
```

## Dimorphic Systemic Fungi

These fungi switch between a mold form (environmental, infective spores) and a yeast form (in mammalian tissue at 37°C) — see 01-FUNGAL-BIOLOGY for dimorphism mechanism.

```
HISTOPLASMA CAPSULATUM (Histoplasmosis):
  Geographic: Ohio/Mississippi River valley (USA), Mexico, Central/South America
  Habitat: bird and bat guano; soil → disruption releases spores
  Exposure: common in endemic areas (80%+ of residents exposed by adulthood)
  Most infections: asymptomatic or mild flu-like illness
  Severe disease: in immunocompromised → disseminated histoplasmosis

  CLINICAL NOTE: cave explorers, spelunkers, construction workers in endemic areas
  at elevated risk from high-dose exposures

COCCIDIOIDES immitis / C. posadasii (Coccidioidomycosis, "Valley Fever"):
  Geographic: San Joaquin Valley (CA), Sonoran Desert (AZ/NM/TX)
  Expanding range: moving northward as climate warms
<!-- @editor[content/P2]: Valley Fever case counts and range expansion data may be outdated — check current CDC surveillance data for updated case numbers and northward range shift -->
  ~100,000 diagnosed cases/year in USA; possibly 150,000+ unrecognized
  Arthrospores: dangerous — inhaled → convert to spherules in lung
    → Each spherule bursts → 100s endospores → expand infection
  Severity: range from flu-like to chronic pneumonia to disseminated disease
  Risk for disseminated: immunocompromised, males, African Americans, Filipinos
    (genetic factors in susceptibility identified)
  Military significance: multiple cases in US soldiers training in endemic areas
  Treatment: fluconazole (mild-moderate); amphotericin B (severe)

PARACOCCIDIOIDES (Paracoccidioidomycosis):
  Geographic: Brazil and Latin America; major cause of systemic mycosis there
  Often unrecognized in North American/European clinicians (low awareness)

BLASTOMYCES dermatitidis (Blastomycosis):
  Ohio/Mississippi valley and Great Lakes region
  Soil saprophyte; dimorphic
  Misdiagnosed as bacterial pneumonia commonly
```

## Dermatophytes (Tinea / Ringworm)

```
DERMATOPHYTES: specialized fungi that infect keratinized tissue
  Three genera: Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton
  Feed on keratin: hair, nails, stratum corneum of skin
  NOT invasive (unless severely immunocompromised)

DISEASE NAMES (all "tinea" + location):
  Tinea pedis: athlete's foot (Trichophyton rubrum most common)
  Tinea cruris: jock itch (T. rubrum, E. floccosum)
  Tinea capitis: scalp ringworm (Microsporum canis, T. tonsurans)
    → In children; can cause permanent hair loss if not treated
  Tinea corporis: body ringworm (classic ring-shaped lesion)
  Tinea unguium (onychomycosis): nail infection
    → Very common in elderly; difficult to treat (nails have poor blood supply)
    → Oral terbinafine 12 weeks + topical; ~70% cure rate for toenails

TERBINAFINE:
  Allylamine antifungal; inhibits squalene epoxidase (ergosterol synthesis)
  Oral: systemic; reaches nails via nail matrix
  Drug interactions: CYP2D6 inhibitor; important in polypharmacy patients

TRICHOPHYTON TONSURANS:
  Major cause of tinea capitis in USA (especially urban African American populations)
  Epidemic in schools; spread by direct contact and shared combs
  Requires oral treatment (topicals don't penetrate hair shaft)
```

## Wildlife Fungal Crises

### Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (Bd) — Amphibian Apocalypse

```
BD: CHYTRIDIOMYCOTA; aquatic; infects skin of amphibians

MECHANISM:
  Infects epidermal cells; disrupts sodium/potassium transport across skin
  Frogs breathe through skin (cutaneous respiration) AND osmoregulate via skin
  → Chytridiomycosis: impaired skin function → cardiac arrest (electrolyte failure)
  → Death by electrolyte imbalance; not respiratory failure

SCALE OF DISASTER:
  ~500 amphibian species affected globally
  ~90 species extinctions attributed to Bd (or Bd + habitat loss)
  Most devastating infectious disease impact on vertebrate biodiversity in history
  1/3 of all amphibian species now threatened; Bd is a major driver

ORIGINS:
  Bd originated in Asia (Korean peninsula); Asian amphibians have co-evolved resistance
  Global spread: trade in amphibians; African clawed frog (Xenopus laevis) used for
    pregnancy tests (1930s–1960s) → exported globally carrying Bd as asymptomatic carrier
  → International trade moved Bd to every continent (except Antarctica)

STATUS: Pandemic in native amphibian populations; ongoing; no solution

BATRACHOCHYTRIUM SALAMANDRIVORANS (BSAL):
  Discovered 2013; originated in Asia
  Currently devastating European fire salamanders
  Not yet in North America; biosecurity protocols established
  If Bsal enters North America: 200+ salamander species at risk
```

### White-Nose Syndrome in Bats

```
PSEUDOGYMNOASCUS DESTRUCTANS (formerly Geomyces):
  European origin; introduced to North America
  European bats: co-evolved; carry the fungus without disease
  North American bats: no prior exposure → highly susceptible

  MECHANISM:
    Grows on hibernating bat skin (cold-adapted; grows at 5–15°C)
    Disrupts torpor: wakes bats during winter hibernation
    → Energy depletion: burn fat reserves → starve before spring
    Infects wing membranes → compromises flight, water balance

  SCALE:
    First detected: New York (2006)
    By 2020: spread to 38 US states + 7 Canadian provinces
    Some cave populations: 90–99% mortality
    Little brown bat (Myotis lucifugus): was most common bat in NE USA
    → Now listed as endangered candidate in some states
    Some species showing resistance in some cave populations (genetic variation?)

  ECONOMIC IMPACT:
    Bats are primary nighttime insectivores: consume billions of insects
    Estimated $23 billion/year in agricultural pest suppression (bats eat crop pests)
    White-nose syndrome → fewer bats → more pesticides needed → indirect economic harm

  TREATMENT:
    UV light treatment: P. destructans sensitive to UV-C
    → Applying UV-C inside caves being trialed
    Probiotics: beneficial bacteria on bat skin that inhibit fungal growth
    Antifungal treatment: volatile antifungals (being developed)
    None fully deployed at scale yet
```

```
ANTIFUNGAL TARGETS AND DRUGS
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  TARGET            | DRUG CLASS      | Examples                 |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  Ergosterol        | Polyenes        | Amphotericin B, nystatin |
|  (membrane binding)|                 |                          |
|                                                                  |
|  Ergosterol synth  | Azoles          | Fluconazole, voriconazole|
|  (CYP51 inhibit)   | (triazoles)     | itraconazole, isavuconazole|
|                                                                  |
|  Ergosterol synth  | Allylamines     | Terbinafine, naftifine  |
|  (squalene epoxid.)|                 |                          |
|                                                                  |
|  β-1,3-glucan synth| Echinocandins   | Caspofungin, micafungin |
|  (cell wall)       |                 | anidulafungin            |
|                                                                  |
|  DNA/RNA synth     | Pyrimidine      | Flucytosine (5-FC)       |
|  (via 5-FU)        | analog          |                          |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

RESISTANCE MECHANISMS:
  Azoles: CYP51 mutations; efflux pumps (CDR1/2, MDR1 upregulation)
  Echinocandins: FKS1/FKS2 mutations (β-glucan synthase)
  Polyenes: membrane sterol changes (rare)
  Flucytosine: transporter mutations; target enzyme mutations → rapid resistance alone

  COMBINATION THERAPY:
  Cryptococcus meningitis: AmB + flucytosine (synergistic; prevents 5-FC resistance)
  Severe invasive: sometimes azole + echinocandin
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Pathogen | Target organ | Main risk population | First-line treatment |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Candida albicans | Mucosa, bloodstream | IV catheters, neutropenia | Fluconazole (mucosa); echinocandin (invasive) |
| Candida auris | Bloodstream, skin | ICU, immunocompromised | Echinocandin (if susceptible); extremely limited options |
| Aspergillus fumigatus | Lung (invasive) | Neutropenia, transplant | Voriconazole |

## Common Confusion Points

**Candida auris is not "just another Candida"**: C. albicans is generally susceptible to fluconazole and manageable. C. auris is typically resistant to fluconazole, increasingly resistant to echinocandins, and extremely difficult to eliminate from hospital environments. The CDC's "Urgent Threat" classification reflects a qualitatively different problem.

**Aspergillus flavus vs. A. fumigatus**: Both are important Aspergillus species but very different. A. fumigatus: primary invasive pulmonary pathogen in immunocompromised. A. flavus: less common invasive pathogen but important in sinusitis + produces aflatoxins (potent carcinogens in stored grain). Don't conflate.

**Bd vs. Bsal**: Two different chytrid species causing different diseases. Bd affects amphibians broadly. Bsal is specifically devastating to salamanders. Bsal is not yet in North America; biosecurity to keep it out is active concern.

**Ringworm is not a worm**: "Ringworm" is a fungal infection (tinea corporis, caused by dermatophytes). The name comes from the ring-shaped lesion. No worms involved. This is one of those lay-term/scientific-reality disconnects that comes up clinically.

**Pneumocystis jirovecii**: This is a fungus that was previously classified as a protozoan. It causes Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP) in immunocompromised patients (classic AIDS-defining illness). Treated with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (not a conventional antifungal) because Pneumocystis has unusual biochemistry (no ergosterol). It cannot be cultured in the lab outside a living host.
