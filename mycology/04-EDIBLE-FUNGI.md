## Major Cultivated Species

### Agaricus bisporus (Button/Cremini/Portobello)

```
THREE NAMES, ONE SPECIES:
  Button mushroom: harvested young (closed veil)
  Cremini (crimini, baby bella): brown strain, harvested young
  Portobello: same brown strain, harvested mature (full cap expansion)

CULTIVATION:
  Substrate: composted straw + horse manure (Phase I composting)
    → Phase II: pasteurization at 60°C, 8 hours (kills competitors)
    → Spawn run: inoculate with mycelium; colonize substrate; ~14 days
  Casing layer: peat + lime on surface → triggers fruiting
  Fruiting: requires: high CO₂ during spawn run → CO₂ reduction → pin formation
  Flush cycle: 1st flush (largest) → 2nd → 3rd; typically 4–6 flushes per crop
  Growing environment: 16–18°C; 85–90% RH; CO₂-controlled

COMMERCIAL SCALE:
  Trays or shelf systems (Pascal's Law equivalent)
  4–6 week growing cycle from spawn to harvest
  Mechanized farms: Pennsylvania mushroom capital
  Yields: 20–30 kg/m² per crop cycle

WHITE vs. BROWN STRAINS:
  White (commercial "button"): developed 1926 in Pennsylvania
  Brown (cremini/portobello): likely the original wild type color
  Same species, different maturity stage = different market products
```

### Lentinula edodes (Shiitake)

```
SHIITAKE: second largest commercial mushroom globally
  Native to East Asia; Japan dominant producer
  Substrate: oak wood chips + wheat bran + water (log cultivation or bags)
  Spawn: inoculate → colonize → fruiting after ~3 months (bags)
         or 1–2 years (natural log cultivation)
  Logs: traditional method; multiple years of production

  FLAVOR CHEMISTRY:
  Lentinan: β-glucan polysaccharide; immunomodulatory claims
  Lenthionine: volatile sulfide (1,2,3,5,6-pentathiepane)
    → Generated from lentinic acid by enzyme action during cooking
    → KEY flavor component; increases dramatically with drying
  Ergosterol: provitamin D₂; UV exposure on dried shiitake → vitamin D₂
    → Dried shiitake placed gill-up in sun = excellent vitamin D source

  HEALTH CLAIMS:
  Lentinan: used as cancer immunotherapy adjuvant (IV) in Japan
  AHCC (Active Hexose Correlated Compound): proprietary extract; studied
  Eritadenine: inhibits plasma cholesterol (animal studies)
  → Many claims; clinical evidence mixed; no FDA-approved claims in US
```

### Pleurotus ostreatus (Oyster Mushroom)

```
OYSTER MUSHROOM:
  White rot fungus → grows on lignocellulosic substrates
  Very fast growth; one of easiest to cultivate
  Substrate: straw, cardboard, coffee grounds, wood chips

CULTIVATION:
  Substrate pasteurization (82°C for 1–2 hours in water bath)
  → Cool → inoculate with oyster mushroom spawn (grain spawn)
  → Incubate in bags/buckets: 21–25°C, 10–14 days
  → Fruiting: cut holes in bag → expose to fresh air, lower temperature
  → Pin formation: CO₂ gradient + fresh air + 18–20°C
  → Harvest: before veil tears; multiple flushes

SPECIES COMPLEX:
  P. ostreatus: grey oyster (most common)
  P. eryngii: king oyster (dense, meaty texture)
  P. citrinopileatus: golden oyster (delicate)
  P. djamor: pink oyster (tropical; short shelf life)
  P. cornucopiae: yellow oyster

BIOREMEDIATION:
  Oyster mushrooms are white rot fungi → degrade petroleum hydrocarbons,
    TNT residues, dioxins (limited but demonstrated)
  "Mycoremediation" (Paul Stamets' term): using fungi to clean polluted sites
  Research ongoing; applied cases limited; promising for certain compounds
```

### Grifola frondosa (Maitake / Hen of the Woods)

```
MAITAKE (Japanese: "dancing mushroom"):
  Large bracket fungus; grows at base of oak trees
  Wild-foraged in Japan: up to 5 kg clusters; highly valued
  Commercial cultivation in substrate bags; labor-intensive

  β-glucan content: "D-fraction" extract studied for immune effects
  Maitake D-fraction: some clinical evidence for blood glucose modulation
  → Not FDA-approved; food supplement in US
```

## Truffle — The Extreme Case

```
TRUFFLES (Tuber spp.):
  Hypogeous (underground) fruiting bodies
  Ectomycorrhizal with oak, hazel, beech
  Cannot be fully cultivated without host tree
  Truffle orchards (truffières): inoculated oak/hazel seedlings planted
    → Takes 7–15 years before first truffle production
    → Yield: 5–50 kg/ha/year (very low compared to cultivated mushrooms)

  THE AROMA CHEMISTRY:
  Black Périgord (T. melanosporum): 2,4-dithiapentane, dimethyl disulfide,
    methyl thiomethyl sulfide; also more subtle alcohols, aldehydes
  White Alba (T. magnatum pico): 2,4-dithiapentane + bis(methylthio)methane
    + complex mixture; "garlic-y honey" with earthy undertones
    → Chemical profile destroyed by cooking → add raw at service
  → Volatile compounds diffuse up through soil → animals find truffle
    → Pigs, dogs, flies (Suillia) all attracted

  ECONOMICS:
  White truffle (T. magnatum): ~€3,000–5,000/kg record prices
    Almost exclusively from Piedmont, Italy (Alba region)
    Cannot be reliably cultivated → entirely wild
  Black Périgord (T. melanosporum): ~€800–1,500/kg in season
    70% of world production: France, Spain, Italy, Australia
    Cultivated in truffières (hazel/oak inoculated orchards)
    Australia: winter in N. hemisphere = summer in S. → year-round supply
  Chinese truffle (T. sinense/indicum): ~€15–50/kg
    Physically similar to Périgord; aroma dramatically weaker
    Often sold as Périgord in fraud cases; GC-MS detects adulteration
```

## Cultivation Technology

Commercial mushroom cultivation is a controlled manufacturing process: substrate = feedstock, sterilization = upstream processing, inoculation = process initiation, incubation = batch reactor phase, fruiting = product formation phase, harvest = downstream processing. The environmental control parameters (CO₂ ppm, relative humidity %, temperature, photoperiod) map directly to industrial process control — PID loops on ventilation, humidification, and heat exchange. Contamination control is analogous to sterile manufacturing: cleanroom-equivalent protocols for inoculation, HACCP-style monitoring for competitor mold (Trichoderma, Penicillium). The biggest commercial mushroom operations (China, Netherlands) are fully automated sensor-actuator loops with continuous environmental monitoring.

```
SUBSTRATE TYPES AND PREPARATION:
  Lignocellulosic:
    Wood chips, straw, cottonseed hulls, bran
    Sterilization (121°C, 30–60 min) or pasteurization (82°C, 1–2 hr)
    → Kills competing molds and bacteria

  Composted:
    Horse manure + straw or coco coir
    Selective fermentation selects for Agaricus-compatible microbiome

  Semi-synthetic:
    Hardwood sawdust + wheat bran + gypsum (for wood-loving species)
    Grain + gypsum (spawn grain)

SPAWN TYPES:
  Grain spawn: fungal mycelium colonizing sterilized grain (rye, millet, wheat)
    → Most common; fast colonization
  Sawdust spawn: for wood-lovers; slower
  Plug spawn: wooden dowels for log inoculation

FRUITING TRIGGERS:
  Reduced CO₂: high CO₂ during colonization → reduce for fruiting
  Temperature drop: simulate seasonal transition
  Light: phototropism in some species (guides development)
  Humidity: typically 85–95% RH during fruiting
  Fresh air exchange: critical; stale air → aborted pins

CONTAMINATION CONTROL:
  Primary competitors: Trichoderma (green mold), Cobweb mold, Bacillus
  Prevention: sterilization + clean inoculation + monitoring
  → Same contamination thinking as fermentation control in brewing
```

## Flavor Chemistry

```
MUSHROOM UMAMI:
  Glutamate: free glutamic acid → umami taste
    Dried mushrooms: high glutamate (protein degradation during drying)
    Shiitake: ~1,060 mg/100g dry weight
    Porcini: very high glutamate; classic "forest" umami
  5'-Nucleotides: 5'-GMP, 5'-AMP, 5'-IMP (Adenosine, Guanosine, Inosine)
    → Synergize with glutamate: 5'-GMP increases perceived umami 7×
    → Explains "umami bomb" effect of dried mushrooms

VOLATILE COMPOUNDS:
  1-Octen-3-ol: "mushroom alcohol"; earth + mushroom aroma
    → From linoleic acid by lipoxygenase pathway
    → Released when mushroom is damaged/cut
    → Universal mushroom aroma compound
  Linalool, geraniol: floral notes in some species
  Sulfur compounds: truffle, shiitake (lenthionine)

FLAVOR DEVELOPMENT WITH DRYING:
  Drying concentrates volatiles + enzymatic reactions
  Porcini: much more intensely flavored dried than fresh
  Shiitake: lentinic acid → lenthionine only forms with enzyme action
    → Heat inactivates enzyme; dry at low T first → then heat
  Morel: must be cooked (raw morel contains monomethylhydrazine → toxic)
```

## Look-Alike Safety

```
COMMON DANGEROUS LOOK-ALIKES:
  Edible → Dangerous look-alike → Key distinguishing feature

  Button mushroom → Amanita phalloides (Death cap)
  → Distinguishing: Amanita has volva (cup at base), skirt-like ring
  → Death cap can look like a small button mushroom when young

  Chanterelle → Omphalotus olearius (Jack-o'-lantern)
  → Distinguishing: Jack-o'-lantern has true gills (not forked ridges);
    bioluminescent; orange gills; grows on wood/roots

  Porcini → Rubroboletus satanas (Satan's bolete)
  → Distinguishing: red pore surface (not white/yellow); blue staining

  Oyster → Crepidotus, some Pleurocybella
  → Distinguishing: wrong substrate; wrong cap color; fragile flesh

GENERAL PRINCIPLE:
  Every edible mushroom has at least one dangerous look-alike
  Never eat based on one feature
  Use multiple characters: cap, gills/pores, stem, base (volva?), spore print,
    smell, substrate, color change when cut/bruised
  When in doubt: do not eat
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Button/cremini/portobello are the same species**: All Agaricus bisporus. Button = young white strain (closed veil). Cremini = young brown strain. Portobello = mature brown strain with open cap. The different names are marketing, not biology.

**Cultivated vs. mycorrhizal fungi**: Button, shiitake, oyster, enoki — all saprotrophic; can be grown on dead substrate. Truffle, porcini, chanterelle, morel (largely) — mycorrhizal or at least partially; cannot be grown on dead substrate alone. This is why the high-value gourmet mushrooms are mostly wild-foraged.

**Drying increases umami**: Fresh porcini is good; dried porcini is extraordinary. The drying concentrates glutamate and 5'-GMP and allows enzymatic reactions that produce the characteristic flavors. This is not just concentration — drying triggers flavor development reactions.

**Morels must be cooked**: Raw morels contain gyromitrin (and the derivative monomethylhydrazine). Cooking destroys the toxin. Do not eat raw morels. Also: some people have sensitivity even to cooked morel in large quantities.
