```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Saprotrophic Decomposition

Fungi are the primary decomposers of plant litter in terrestrial ecosystems. Without them, dead wood would not decay.

### Lignocellulose Degradation

```
PLANT CELL WALL COMPOSITION:
  Cellulose: 40–50% of dry weight; crystalline glucose polymer
  Hemicellulose: 25–35%; branched polysaccharides (xylan, mannan)
  Lignin: 20–30%; complex aromatic polymer; extremely resistant
    → Lignin: three-dimensional network of phenylpropanoid units
    → NO animal can digest lignin (no animals produce ligninases)
    → Only white rot fungi and some bacteria can break it down

WHITE ROT FUNGI (Basidiomycetes primarily):
  Degrade BOTH cellulose AND lignin
  Leave white/pale fibrous residue (cellulose after lignin removed)
  Enzymes: lignin peroxidase, manganese peroxidase, laccase
    → All oxidative, peroxide-dependent enzymes
    → Radicals attack the phenolic bonds of lignin
  Examples: Trametes versicolor (turkey tail), Pleurotus (oyster mushroom),
    Phanerochaete chrysosporium (research model)

BROWN ROT FUNGI (Basidiomycetes):
  Degrade cellulose + hemicellulose; leave LIGNIN intact
  Residue: brown, cubic-cracking (lignin skeleton remains)
  Mechanism: H₂O₂-driven Fenton chemistry (non-enzymatic cellulose attack)
  Examples: Poria, Postia, Serpula lacrymans (dry rot)

DRY ROT (Serpula lacrymans):
  Can transport water through hyphae for many meters
  → Attack dry timber that couldn't otherwise sustain fungal growth
  → Major building deterioration problem; extremely hard to eradicate
  → Mycelial strands penetrate through masonry
```

### Carbon Cycle Role

```
FUNGAL CARBON CYCLE CONTRIBUTION:
  1. Fungi mine recalcitrant carbon (lignin, cellulose)
     → Returns C to atmosphere as CO₂
  2. Mycorrhizal fungi: plants allocate 10–30% of photosynthate to mycorrhizae
     → Substantial C transfer from atmosphere to soil
  3. Fungal necromass: dead fungal biomass is major input to soil organic matter
     → Chitin decomposes slowly → soil carbon storage
  4. Glomalin: glycoprotein from AM fungi; ~27% of soil organic carbon
     → Soil aggregation; important for soil structure

WITHOUT FUNGI:
  Permafrost carbon: ancient plant carbon locked in permafrost
  As permafrost thaws → brown rot fungi could decompose it
  → Positive feedback loop: warming → decomposition → more CO₂ → more warming
  This is a significant climate tipping point concern
```

## Mycorrhizal Symbiosis

### Ectomycorrhizae (ECM)

```
ECTOMYCORRHIZAL (ECM) FUNGI:
  Form a sheath (mantle) around the root tip
  Hyphae grow between root cortex cells (Hartig net) but NOT inside cells
  Hyphal network extends into soil → enormous surface area

  PARTNERS:
  Fungi: ~20,000 ECM species; Basidiomycetes dominant (Amanita, Boletus,
    Cantharellus, Suillus, Lactarius, Cortinarius, Russula)
    + some Ascomycetes (Tuber/truffle, Elaphomyces)
  Plants: ~8,000 species; mostly trees: Pinus, Quercus, Betula, Fagus,
    Eucalyptus, Larix, Populus
  → ECM trees dominate most temperate and boreal forests

  EXCHANGE:
  Plant → Fungus: sucrose (sugar, the photosynthate)
  Fungus → Plant: Pi (inorganic phosphorus), N (as amino acids), water
  Rate: plant allocates 10–20% of net primary production to ECM
  Fungus provides: what the plant can't reach with just roots
    → Fungal hyphae: 1–10 µm; root hairs: 10–20 µm; roots: much larger
    → Hyphae access micropores in soil that roots cannot penetrate

  SPECIFICITY:
  Not random: specific plant-fungal pairings, though not absolute
  Suillus: specific to Pinus; Rhizopogon: specific to some Pinus/Pseudotsuga
  Amanita muscaria: wide host range (many tree genera)
  Truffle (Tuber melanosporum): specific to Quercus, Corylus in wild
```

### Arbuscular Mycorrhizae (AM)

```
ARBUSCULAR MYCORRHIZAL (AM) FUNGI (Glomeromycota):
  Most common mycorrhizal type: ~80% of land plant species
  Hyphae penetrate INTO root cortex cells → form arbuscules
  Arbuscule: tree-like branching structure inside cell
  → Huge surface area for carbon/phosphorus exchange at cell-fungal interface
  Periarbuscular membrane: plant plasma membrane invaginates around arbuscule
  → Not inside cytoplasm; surrounded by membrane "apoplast"

  OBLIGATE: cannot live without a plant host; cannot be cultured alone
  ANCIENT: fossil evidence in Devonian roots (410 Ma)
    → Among the oldest terrestrial symbioses known
    → May have enabled land colonization by early plants

  PARTNERS: wide range of plants (grasses, crops, most non-woody plants)
    Wheat, corn, rice, tomato, clover: all AM-dependent
  FUNGI: ~250 species (Glomus/Rhizophagus, Funneliformis, Diversispora, etc.)

  AGRICULTURAL IMPORTANCE:
  AM fungi are the primary route of phosphorus uptake for most crops
  Conventional agriculture: heavy phosphate fertilization → suppresses AM
  → Crops become dependent on fertilizer, not mycorrhizae
  Organic agriculture: preserve AM communities → better P use efficiency
  Inoculant products: Glomus inoculants sold for revegetation, agriculture
```

### The Wood Wide Web

Suzanne Simard's work (1997, Nature) demonstrated that carbon moves between trees through mycorrhizal networks — "Mother Trees" send carbon to seedlings.

```
THE MYCORRHIZAL NETWORK:
  Individual tree roots → connected to multiple fungal partners
  Same fungal individual → connected to multiple tree roots
  → Network of carbon/nutrient exchange at ecosystem scale

SIMARD'S FINDINGS (1997):
  Radiolabeled CO₂ fed to birch → ¹⁴C appeared in Douglas fir
  → Carbon moved from birch to fir via mycorrhizal network
  Mechanism: net carbon flow from source (high photosynthate, light)
    to sink (shaded, low photosynthate)
  → "Altruism" in the popular press; biology of resource allocation

THE REAL BIOLOGY:
  NOT "trees talking" or "forest internet"
  It IS: fungal hyphae connecting root systems, allowing bidirectional
    flow of carbon and nutrients between trees
  Carbon moves DOWN gradients (source-sink):
    → Sun-lit canopy trees → shaded seedlings (Simard's main finding)
    → Dying trees → release carbon to network? (less well established)
  Phosphorus and water also move through networks
  Defense signals? Tentative evidence; far less certain than C/P exchange

SCALE:
  A single Armillaria mycelium: 2,385 ha (Oregon)
  A single forest stand: multiple fungal genets interconnecting trees
  → Community-level nutrient cycling, not just individual plant-fungus pairs
```

## Lichens

```
LICHEN = FUNGUS (mycobiont) + PHOTOBIONT (alga or cyanobacterium)

STRUCTURE:
  Cortex (upper): organized fungal hyphae; UV protection
  Algal layer: photobiont cells embedded in hyphae
  Medulla: loose fungal hyphae; gas exchange
  Cortex (lower): attachment, often rhizines (root-like structures)

PHOTOBIONTS:
  Most commonly: Trebouxia (green alga) — ~50% of lichenized fungi
  Also: Trentepohlia (orange alga, orange pigment from iron), cyanobacteria
  Cyanobacterial lichens: fix nitrogen (contribute N to ecosystem)
  Some lichens have BOTH alga and cyanobacterium (tripartite)

FUNGAL PARTNER:
  Almost always Ascomycete (Lecanoromycetes mainly)
  ~20% of all ascomycetes are lichenized
  The fungus provides: protection, water retention, mineral access
  The alga/cyanobacterium provides: fixed carbon (photosynthate)
  → Mutualism, but fungus is dominant (controls structure)
  → Lichen "identity" is based on fungal partner (nomenclature rule)

WHERE LICHENS LIVE:
  Pioneer colonizers of bare rock, tree bark, soil
  Extremophiles: Antarctica, tundra, deserts, volcanic rock
  Most sensitive to air pollution (SO₂): bioindicators of air quality
  Caloplaca on tombstones: dates of initial colonization = dating tool

LICHEN PRODUCTS:
  Usnic acid: antibacterial, used in some cosmetics
  Lichen acids: etching rock → soil formation (bioweathering)
  Litmus: from Roccella and Ochrolechia (pH indicator history)
  Vulpinic acid: from wolf lichen Letharia vulpina; toxic to dogs/wolves
  Parmotrema perlatum: perfumery ("oak moss" substitute)
```

## Endophytic Fungi

```
ENDOPHYTES: live inside healthy plant tissue without causing disease
  ALL terrestrial plants harbor endophytic fungi
  Average grass: >100 endophytic fungal species
  Tropical trees: >1,000 endophytic species (different species in each leaf)

CLASSES (Rodriguez et al. 2009):
  Class 1: Clavicipitaceous endophytes (cool-season grasses)
    → Epichloë/Neotyphodium: systemic; transmitted via seed
    → Produce ALKALOIDS that deter herbivory (ergot alkaloids, lolines)
    → Drought tolerance conferred to host grass
    → Fescue toxicosis: cattle eating endophyte-infected tall fescue
       develop ergot alkaloid toxicity (vasoconstriction → gangrene)

  Classes 2–4: all other endophytes; various life strategies

PHARMACEUTICAL POTENTIAL:
  Taxol (paclitaxel): cancer drug; originally from Pacific yew bark
  → Later found in endophytic fungi from the same yew tree
  → Taxomyces andreanae: first report; subsequent debate about purity
  → Many endophytes from medicinal plants produce plant-like compounds

ECOLOGICAL FUNCTIONS:
  Drought tolerance: some evidence (poorly understood)
  Herbivore deterrence: alkaloids (Class 1 well established)
  Pathogen resistance: may prime plant immune responses
  → Microbiome concept applies to plants, not just animals
```

## Common Confusion Points

**The Wood Wide Web media hype vs. actual biology**: The popular coverage often claims trees "communicate" and "share resources altruistically." The actual biology is more mundane but still interesting: fungal networks move carbon and phosphorus along gradients, and some carbon from canopy trees ends up in shaded seedlings. This is mycorrhizal network dynamics, not intentional communication. The "Mother Tree" framing is Simard's narrative; the biochemistry is osmotic/concentration gradient-driven transport.

**White rot vs. brown rot is not about color**: White rot leaves white fibrous residue because lignin is removed; brown rot leaves brown cubic material because cellulose is removed and lignin remains. Both cause wood decay; only white rot can fully mineralize the carbon.

**Lichen is not a single organism**: A lichen is an ecosystem in miniature. The fungal partner (the mycobiont) and the photosynthetic partner (photobiont) are different organisms. They can sometimes be separated and cultured independently. The "lichen" identity is defined by the fungal partner.

**AM fungi cannot be cultured alone**: This is a practical constraint with major agricultural implications. You cannot grow AM fungal inoculants the same way you grow bacteria. Commercial products use roots with attached spores (trap cultures) or spore collections. This limits production scale and quality control.

**Dry rot is misnamed**: Serpula lacrymans (dry rot) doesn't require less water than other fungi — it just has unusually long mycelial water-transport systems that let it colonize timber that is drier than most fungi can tolerate. It's "dry" relative to wet rot conditions, but the fungus still needs water.
