# Sociolinguistics — Language and Society

## The Big Picture

Sociolinguistics studies the relationship between language and social factors — who says what to whom, in which variety, and why.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    SOCIOLINGUISTICS LANDSCAPE                     |
|                                                                  |
|  LANGUAGE VARIATION           LANGUAGE AND IDENTITY             |
|  (dialect, sociolect,         (ethnicity, gender, age,          |
|   register, style)            class signal through speech)      |
|                                                                  |
|  QUANTITATIVE                 DIGLOSSIA AND                     |
|  VARIATIONIST                 MULTILINGUALISM                   |
|  SOCIOLINGUISTICS             (two-language communities,        |
|  (Labov: correlate variants   code choice, language policy)     |
|   with social variables)                                        |
|                                                                  |
|  LANGUAGE CHANGE              ENDANGERMENT AND                  |
|  (social factors drive        REVITALIZATION                    |
|   spread/retraction)          (Welsh, Hawaiian, Hebrew)         |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

The core claim: **Language use is not random — it is patterned by social structure.**

## Part I: Language Variation — Types

```
VARIATION TYPES:

DIALECT — regional variety of a language
  Defined by geography (areal dialect) or social group
  No linguistic criterion separates dialect from language
  The old saw: "a language is a dialect with an army and a navy" (Weinreich)
  Mandarin and Cantonese are "dialects" (same political entity) but mutually unintelligible
  Norwegian and Danish are "languages" but mutually intelligible

SOCIOLECT — variety associated with social group
  African American English (AAE): systematic, rule-governed variety
  Multicultural London English (MLE): newer urban multiethnolect
  Upper-class British: "RP" (Received Pronunciation) — prestige dialect

REGISTER — variety associated with situational context
  Legal register: "the party of the first part hereby agrees..."
  Medical register: "the patient presents with..."
  Casual: "gonna," "y'all," "like" as discourse particle

STYLE — individual variation within a person's repertoire
  Everyone has a stylistic range from casual to formal
  The same individual uses different styles with different interlocutors
```

### Standard Language and Prestige

```
STANDARD VARIETY:
  No linguistic criterion — purely social construct.
  Standard = variety associated with education, writing, formal institutions.
  Has OVERT PRESTIGE: socially recognized as "correct."

COVERT PRESTIGE:
  Some non-standard varieties have covert prestige within their community.
  Labov's Martha's Vineyard study (1963):
    Young islanders centralized /ay/ and /aw/ vowels (sounding more like old fishermen)
    NOT because of unconscious drift — as affirmation of island identity
    vs. mainland tourists.
    Covert prestige of the traditional islander variety.
```

## Part II: Labov and Quantitative Variationist Sociolinguistics

William Labov transformed sociolinguistics into a quantitative science.

### The New York Department Store Study (1966)

**Research question**: Is the variable (r) — presence or absence of post-vocalic /r/ in words like "fourth floor" — socially stratified in New York?

```
METHOD:
  Three department stores representing different social strata:
  Saks Fifth Avenue (upper)
  Macy's (middle)
  S. Klein (lower)

  Labov approached clerks and asked for something on "the fourth floor"
  (forced production of "fourth floor" = two post-vocalic r environments)
  If clerk gave answer, he said "Excuse me?" to elicit careful repetition.

RESULTS:
  (r) presence as % of maximum:

                Casual      Emphatic
  Saks          62%          64%
  Macy's        51%          64%   ← Style shift when emphatic
  S. Klein      21%          28%

FINDINGS:
1. Social stratification: higher-prestige store → more (r)
2. Style shifting: ALL stores shifted toward more (r) when emphatic
   → speakers track the prestige variable and shift toward it when careful

This shows: variation is not free, it's socially and stylistically constrained.
```

### The Variable Rule

Labov codified: **variable rules** — phonological rules that apply probabilistically, with probability conditioned by multiple factors:

```
VARIABLE (r)-DELETION:
  Factors favoring r-deletion:
  + following consonant (more than vowel)
  + word-final position
  + casual style
  + working-class speaker
  + African American English

  Statistical: VARBRUL analysis (now Rbrul)
  Yields: regression coefficients for each factor's contribution
  to the probability of the variant being chosen
```

### Martha's Vineyard — Covert Prestige and Change from Below

```
MARTHA'S VINEYARD (1963):
  Variable: centralization of /ay/ and /aw/ diphthongs
  "right" = [rɐɪt] (centralized onset) vs [raɪt] (standard onset)

  Traditionally: old Yankee fishermen centralized
  New trend: younger islanders were MORE centralized than expected

  Why? Resistance to mainland tourism and economic encroachment.
  "We're islanders, not tourists" — language as identity marker.

  KEY INSIGHT: Language change is not random drift.
  It can be a conscious or semi-conscious identity act.
```

### The Philadelphia Vowel Study

Labov's major longitudinal project: mapped vowel shifts across Philadelphia by social class, age, sex, neighborhood. Key findings:

1. Women lead change in progress (advance incoming variants faster)
2. Working-class speakers often lead phonological change
3. Change propagates through social networks

## Part III: Language and Identity

### Language and Ethnicity

```
AFRICAN AMERICAN ENGLISH (AAE):
  NOT a deficient form of Standard American English.
  Systematic rules distinguishing it:

  BE DELETION (copula): "She happy" (= she is happy)
    Rule: copula deletable in same environments where Standard English
    allows contraction ("she's") — never in positions like "Yes, she __" (final)
    This is more systematic than Standard English, not less.

  HABITUAL BE: "He be working late" ≠ "He's working late"
    "He be working" = he characteristically/habitually works (no SAE equivalent!)
    A SEMANTIC DISTINCTION SAE LACKS.

  NEGATIVE CONCORD (multiple negation): "I didn't do nothing"
    Rule-governed in AAE; also found historically in Chaucer's English,
    in French, Spanish, Italian — "double negative" = intensification in many languages.

  STRESSED BIN: "She BIN married" — she has been married for a long time
    Another AAE-specific semantic distinction.

Labov's NYC studies: AAE is as systematic and rule-governed as SAE.
The stigma is social, not linguistic.
```

### Language and Gender — Four Frameworks

```
1. DEFICIT MODEL (Lakoff, 1975):
   Women's language is deficient — hedges, tag questions, empty adjectives,
   indirect speech indicate lack of confidence.
   "Women speak less directly because they have less power."
   Criticized: descriptive data was anecdotal, not empirical.

2. DOMINANCE MODEL (Zimmerman & West, 1975):
   Gender differences in language reflect male dominance.
   Men interrupt more in mixed-sex conversation.
   Women's accommodation = submission to power structure.
   (Interruption studies largely replicated, though less clear-cut than claimed)

3. DIFFERENCE MODEL (Tannen, 1990):
   Men and women belong to different subcultures with different conversational goals.
   Women: rapport talk (maintain relationships)
   Men: report talk (exchange information, establish status)
   Neither is deficient — they're just different communicative norms.

4. SOCIAL CONSTRUCTIONIST / PERFORMANCE MODEL (Butler; Cameron):
   Gender is PERFORMED through language, not fixed.
   Language use constitutes gender identity, not just reflects it.
   Identity is fluid, contextual, and can challenge norms.
   Focus: how speakers use language to position themselves re: gender.
```

**Current view**: No single framework is adequate. Gender variation is real but highly intersectional (interacts with class, ethnicity, sexuality, age) and context-dependent.

## Part IV: Diglossia

Ferguson (1959) — key concept: **diglossia** — two varieties coexist in a speech community with distinct functions.

```
DIGLOSSIA:
  HIGH (H) VARIETY:                  LOW (L) VARIETY:
  +-----------------------------+     +-----------------------------+
  | Formal, written, prestigious|     | Informal, spoken, everyday  |
  | Sermons, news, education    |     | Family, friends, market     |
  | Learned in school           |     | Acquired naturally           |
  | No native speakers          |     | Everyone speaks it           |
  +-----------------------------+     +-----------------------------+

CLASSIC EXAMPLES:
  Arabic:    MSA (Modern Standard Arabic) H / Colloquial dialects L
  German:    Standard German H / Swiss German L (in Switzerland)
  Greek:     Katharevousa H / Demotiki L (until 1976)
  Haitian:   French H / Haitian Creole L

FUNCTIONS STRICTLY SEPARATED:
  You cannot give a speech in Colloquial Arabic at a formal event.
  You cannot have a natural conversation with family in MSA.
  The two varieties are NOT interchangeable in their respective domains.
```

**Extended diglossia** (Fishman 1967): The H/L distinction can apply across different languages, not just varieties of one language. Quebec: English H / French L (historically). Singapore: English H / Mandarin, Malay, Tamil in different domains.

## Part V: Language Policy

```
TYPES OF LANGUAGE POLICY:

OFFICIAL LANGUAGE STATUS:
  Some countries: one language official (France — Toubon Law: French in official contexts)
  Some countries: multiple official (Switzerland: German/French/Italian/Romansh)
  USA: no official language at federal level (de facto English)
  India: 22 constitutionally recognized languages

LANGUAGE IN EDUCATION:
  Mother-tongue instruction vs. dominant language instruction
  Immersion programs (French immersion in Canada — ~100% input in L2, high success)
  Transitional bilingual education vs. maintenance bilingual education

LANGUAGE STANDARDIZATION:
  Academy enforcement (Académie Française: "courriel" not "email")
  Generally ineffective for spoken language — prescriptivism vs. descriptivism again
  Codification (official orthography, grammar books) = necessary for written standard

LANGUAGE RIGHTS:
  UN Declaration on rights of persons belonging to minorities (1992)
  Languages of instruction, minority language courts, official translations
```

## Part VI: Language Endangerment and Revitalization

### The Scale of Loss

```
CURRENT STATUS:
  ~7,000 languages in the world (Ethnologue estimate)
  ~3,000 are considered endangered (UNESCO Atlas of Endangered Languages)
  A language dies every 2 weeks (estimate)
  By 2100: ~50% of current languages may be extinct

TOP 10 LANGUAGES: spoken by ~50% of the world's population
  Languages 11–100: spoken by most of the rest
  Languages 2,000–7,000: each has <1,000 speakers in many cases

WHY LANGUAGES DIE:
  1. Physical extinction of speakers (genocide, disease)
  2. Language shift: community adopts dominant language
     (economic incentive, education only in dominant language,
      stigma attached to minority language, urbanization)
  3. Language endangerment cycle: parents don't pass on minority language
     → children grow up without it → no transmission
```

### Revitalization Successes

```
HEBREW:
  By far the most dramatic case.
  Hebrew functioned as liturgical/literary language for ~1800 years.
  No native speakers from ~200 CE to late 19th century.
  Eliezer Ben-Yehuda coined thousands of new words starting 1881.
  Mandate Palestine — deliberate revival as spoken language.
  Today: ~9 million native speakers.
  The one case of a language brought back from dormancy to full native use.

WELSH:
  1981: ~500,000 Welsh speakers (down from ~930,000 in 1901)
  Welsh Language Acts (1967, 1993, 2011): equal status, bilingual signage,
  Welsh-medium education schools (Ysgol Gymraeg), BBC Wales in Welsh (S4C)
  2022: ~900,000 Welsh speakers — reversal of decline
  KEY: legal status + media + education + intergenerational transmission

MAORI:
  1970s: Maori predominantly older speakers; children not acquiring it.
  1982: Kōhanga Reo (language nest) — total immersion preschools
  1985–onward: Kura Kaupapa (full immersion schools)
  Te Reo Māori as official NZ language (1987)
  2019: ~186,000 speakers — but intergenerational transmission still fragile

HAWAIIAN:
  1970s: ~2,000 speakers, mostly elderly
  Pūnana Leo (1984) — Hawaiian language immersion preschools
  Hawaiian medium schools K-12
  University of Hawaiʻi programs
  Today: ~24,000 speakers, now including children — genuine revival

WHAT WORKS:
  - Immersion education (language nests)
  - Official legal status
  - Media (TV, radio, print) in the language
  - Intergenerational transmission (grandparent–child)
  - Economic value attached to language use
  - Community-led, not just top-down government policy
```

### Limitations

```
IRISH GAELIC:
  Official language of Ireland since 1922.
  Required in schools.
  Gaeltacht areas (Irish-speaking) preserved.
  BUT: ~75,000 daily speakers (down from millions pre-famine)
  Institutional support without community use = limited revival.
  Teaching in schools ≠ acquisition as native language.
```

## Part VII: Labov's Principles of Linguistic Change

From decades of variationist work, Labov extracted principles:

```
1. UNIFORMITARIAN PRINCIPLE:
   Language change today works the same as it always has.
   Evidence from 1800s dialects relevant to understanding change today.

2. GENDER PARADOX:
   Women are more conservative with respect to overt prestige features
   (avoiding stigmatized variants in formal speech) but lead change in progress
   (advance incoming innovative variants in casual speech).

3. SOCIAL STRATIFICATION:
   Ongoing change often starts at middle/working class.
   Upper class is often conservative (defending prestige).
   Upwardly mobile middle class shows most hypercorrection.

4. CHANGE FROM BELOW vs ABOVE:
   "From below" = change originating outside conscious awareness,
   spreading through social networks.
   "From above" = change introduced by prestige, education, formal channels.

5. S-CURVE OF CHANGE:
   Sound changes spread through populations in an S-shaped curve:
   slow adoption initially → rapid spread → slow completion.
   Like technology diffusion (cf. Rogers' diffusion of innovations).
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Language vs. dialect — the distinction is political, not linguistic:**
Standard Mandarin and Cantonese are mutually unintelligible yet both called "Chinese dialects." Norwegian and Danish are mutually intelligible yet both counted as separate languages. The criterion is political/cultural, not linguistic.

**AAE is not "broken English":**
Systematic rules, internal consistency, historical development from multiple sources (West African substrate, southern American English, Scots-Irish English). Stigmatization is social, not linguistic.

**Diglossia ≠ bilingualism:**
In diglossia, two varieties are for distinct, non-overlapping domains. In bilingualism, two (full) languages coexist. Both are possible simultaneously — bilingual diglossia (Arabic-speaking communities using MSA H + colloquial L, but both are Arabic, not two languages).

**Revitalization ≠ preservation:**
A "revitalized" language may be significantly different from the historical variety. Modern spoken Hebrew is not the same as Biblical Hebrew. Modern Welsh differs from medieval Welsh. This is normal — but "saving a language" often means saving a community of speakers, not freezing the language at a historical stage.

**Prescriptivism can't stop sound change:**
The Académie Française rails against "le weekend" and "le email." People say them anyway. Sound change and lexical borrowing operate beneath conscious control. Prescriptivism can affect written norms and formal contexts; it rarely affects core phonological or morphosyntactic change.

**Women leading change is not the same as women innovating:**
"Women lead change" means they adopt incoming variants faster and spread them. It doesn't mean women are the source of changes (though they may be). Labov's gender paradox reflects the position of women in social networks and prestige dynamics, not an intrinsic creativity difference.
