# Pragmatics — Meaning in Context

## The Big Picture

Pragmatics is the study of how context determines the meaning conveyed beyond what is literally said.

```
+-------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    PRAGMATICS LANDSCAPE                           |
|                                                                   |
|  SEMANTICS              PRAGMATICS                                |
|  "What the sentence     "What the speaker means                   |
|   literally means"       by saying the sentence                   |
|  (truth conditions,       in this context"                        |
|   compositional)                                                  |
|                                                                   |
|  "Can you pass the salt?"                                         |
|  SEMANTIC: Do you have the ability to pass the salt? (T/F)        |
|  PRAGMATIC: Please pass the salt. (request)                       |
|                                                                   |
|  The gap between semantics and pragmatics is:                     |
|  Gricean implicature | Speech acts | Deixis | Presupposition      |
+-------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

The line between semantics and pragmatics is one of linguistics' most contested boundaries.

## Part I: Grice's Cooperative Principle

H.P. Grice (1975) argued that communication is rational and cooperative. Speakers and hearers jointly assume a **Cooperative Principle**:

```
COOPERATIVE PRINCIPLE:
  "Make your conversational contribution such as is required,
   at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or
   direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."

  Broken down into four MAXIMS:

  QUANTITY:  Give the right amount of information.
             1. Make your contribution as informative as required.
             2. Do not make your contribution more informative than required.

  QUALITY:   Be truthful.
             1. Do not say what you believe to be false.
             2. Do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence.

  RELATION:  Be relevant.
             (Only one submaxim: "Be relevant.")

  MANNER:    Be clear.
             1. Avoid obscurity of expression.
             2. Avoid ambiguity.
             3. Be brief.
             4. Be orderly.
```

## Part II: Implicature

**Conversational implicature**: meaning that arises because the hearer assumes the speaker is being cooperative.

```
MECHANISM:

  1. Speaker says S.
  2. S does not (by itself) fit the conversational context.
  3. Hearer assumes speaker is cooperative.
  4. Hearer calculates what the speaker must mean for S to be cooperative.
  5. That calculated meaning = the implicature.
```

### Quantity Implicatures (Scalar Implicatures)

The most-studied type. Arise from ordered scales:

```
HORN SCALES:
  ⟨all, most, many, some, few⟩
  ⟨always, often, sometimes, rarely⟩
  ⟨and, or⟩
  ⟨must, should, may⟩

RULE: Saying a weaker form implicates that the stronger form doesn't hold.

"Some students passed" → +> "Not all students passed"
  Because: if all passed, and speaker knows this, they would have said "all."
  Speaker used the weaker "some," so they must not know/believe "all."

"I might come to the party" → +> "I might not come"
  (otherwise: "I will come" or "I'll definitely be there")

"John or Mary will come" → +> "Not both" (in most contexts)
  (if speaker knew both were coming, they'd say "both"/"and")
```

**Cancellability** (what distinguishes implicature from entailment):
```
"Some students passed — in fact, all of them did." ✓ NOT CONTRADICTORY
→ The implicature "not all" is cancelled.

"John broke his leg — and he didn't break his leg." ✗ CONTRADICTION
→ Entailments cannot be cancelled.
```

### Quality Implicatures — Figures of Speech

When quality is flouted:
```
IRONY: "Oh, great weather today!" (said in a blizzard)
  Violates Quality (speaker knows it's false) → hearer calculates: they mean the opposite.

METAPHOR: "John is a pig."
  Violates Quality (he's a human) → hearer calculates: he shares some property with pigs.

HYPERBOLE: "I've told you a million times!"
  Violates Quality (probably not literally true) → emphatic exaggeration.

MEIOSIS (understatement): "It's a bit cold in Siberia."
  Quality violation → communicates the opposite emphasis.
```

### Relation Implicatures

```
"A: Does Mary have a boyfriend? B: She's been going to Paris a lot lately."
  B's answer is apparently off-topic. Hearer: B must be cooperative → infer relevance.
  Implicature: Mary has a boyfriend in Paris, or something making the question
               answerable from this information.
```

### Manner Implicatures

```
"John turned off the light and went to bed" → before-during implied (temporal order from Manner/ordering)
"John went to bed and turned off the light" → after implicature

"The king died and the queen wept" → temporal + causal ordering implicature
  (not just logical AND which is symmetric)
```

## Part III: Conventional Implicature

Different from conversational implicature — not cancellable, not computed via Cooperative Principle.

```
CONVENTIONAL IMPLICATURE: encoded in the word itself, not contextual.

"but" — implicates contrast between conjoined clauses
  "John is tall but Mary is short" — implicates contrast
  "John is tall but Mary is tall" — odd (contrast expectation violated)

"even" — implicates surprise/unexpected
  "Even John passed" → implicates John was the least expected to pass

"although" — implicates contrast, concessive
  "Although she's ill, she came to work"

"unfortunately" — implicates speaker regret
  (not truth-conditional — "Unfortunately, it rained" and "It rained" have same truth conditions)
```

Grice distinguished conventional from conversational implicature precisely to handle these "extra" meanings that words carry conventionally.

## Part IV: Speech Act Theory

J.L. Austin (1962) and J.R. Searle (1969):

Language doesn't just describe the world — it **does things**.

### Austin's Three-Level Analysis

```
LOCUTIONARY ACT:
  The act of saying something with a meaning.
  "There's a dog in the yard."
  = uttering the sentence with that proposition

ILLOCUTIONARY ACT:
  What the speaker is DOING in saying it.
  Warning, asserting, requesting, promising, ordering, apologizing...
  "There's a dog in the yard" can be:
    - A warning (if you're about to go out barefoot)
    - An assertion (telling someone what's there)
    - An explanation (why the gate was left open)

PERLOCUTIONARY ACT:
  The effect on the hearer.
  Persuading, scaring, amusing, insulting, comforting...
  The speaker can warn (illocutionary) but fail to scare (perlocutionary)
```

### Searle's Taxonomy of Illocutionary Acts

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Assertives** | Commit speaker to truth of proposition | Stating, claiming, asserting, describing |
| **Directives** | Try to get hearer to do something | Requesting, commanding, asking, begging |
| **Commissives** | Commit speaker to future action | Promising, offering, swearing, threatening |
| **Expressives** | Express speaker's psychological state | Thanking, apologizing, congratulating, complaining |
| **Declarations** | Change the world by saying so | "I now pronounce you married," "You're fired," "I resign" |

**Declarations** are particularly interesting — they change social reality by uttering them in the right institutional context. Austin called these **performatives**:

```
PERFORMATIVE UTTERANCES:
  "I promise to pay you back." ← not describing a promise — IS the promise
  "I sentence you to 10 years." ← constitutes the sentence
  "I name this ship the HMS Victory." ← constitutes the naming

  These are neither true nor false — they SUCCEED or FAIL (felicitous/infelicitous).

FELICITY CONDITIONS (Searle):
  1. Preparatory: speaker has the authority/ability
  2. Sincerity: speaker has the relevant mental state
  3. Essential: the act counts as what it purports to be
  4. Propositional content: appropriate propositional content
```

### Indirect Speech Acts

Many speech acts are performed indirectly:

```
DIRECT:   "Pass the salt."        (directive — imperative form)
INDIRECT: "Can you pass the salt?" (literally a yes/no question about ability,
           pragmatically = a request)

Why indirect? Politeness — indirect forms are less face-threatening.

"I wonder if you could possibly help me."
Literally: expressing a wondering
Pragmatically: a very polite request

These are CONVENTIONAL indirect speech acts — hearers recognize the
pattern without full pragmatic calculation every time.
```

## Part V: Relevance Theory

Sperber & Wilson (1986) — a radical simplification and extension of Grice:

**Single principle**: Relevance. No four maxims — just one.

```
RELEVANCE:
  Communication is a search for RELEVANCE.
  An utterance is relevant to the degree that:
    1. It achieves COGNITIVE EFFECTS (new information, confirmed beliefs, changed beliefs)
    2. At low PROCESSING EFFORT

  RELEVANCE = Cognitive Effects / Processing Effort

  Speaker: choose the most relevant form for your intended meaning
  Hearer: interpret in the way that yields most relevance with least effort
```

**Pragmatic inferences** (what relevance theory calls implicatures) are computed as part of interpretation:

- Explicatures: what was said, fully enriched ("He was having a bath" → he = contextually identified individual, present tense = past event, bath = bathtub, not just any body of water)
- Implicatures: what is additionally communicated

Relevance theory treats pragmatics as a cognitive module — part of a general mechanism of mind-reading and communication, not a special linguistic faculty.

## Part VI: Deixis

**Deixis**: meaning that can only be interpreted by reference to features of the utterance context.

```
TYPES OF DEIXIS:

PERSON DEIXIS:
  I / you / he / she / we / they — whose perspective?
  "I" shifts with each new speaker.
  "you" shifts with each new addressee.

SPATIAL DEIXIS:
  here / there / this / that / come / go
  "here" = location of speaker
  "come" vs "go" — toward or away from deictic center

TEMPORAL DEIXIS:
  now / then / yesterday / tomorrow / this year
  "now" = time of utterance
  "yesterday" = day before day of utterance

DISCOURSE DEIXIS:
  "the latter" / "the former" / "as I said earlier" / "in the following"
  Points to discourse segments rather than world entities

SOCIAL DEIXIS:
  tu/vous, du/Sie, tú/usted — encoding social relationship
  Your Majesty, Doctor, mate — honorifics and terms of address
```

**Deictic center**: the default is speaker's location, at utterance time, as deictic origin. "Deictic shift" (in narrative): the center shifts to a character — "He walked over to the table and sat down" (center is now character's location).

## Part VII: Presupposition (Pragmatic Treatment)

From formal semantics (04-SEMANTICS.md): presupposition as truth-value gap. Pragmatic view adds:

**Presupposition as common ground:**

```
STALNAKER'S VIEW:
  Presuppositions are propositions taken for granted as common knowledge
  by speaker and hearer (common ground).

  "Your sister is late again."
  Presupposes: you have a sister AND she has been late before.
  Accommodated if not already in common ground — hearer automatically
  adds "addressee has a sister" and "she was late before" to common ground.

ACCOMMODATION:
  "I need to pick up my daughter from school."
  Presupposes: speaker has a daughter.
  Hearer accommodates this even if previously unknown.
  Accommodation can be strategic: "Have you stopped beating your wife?"
  → presupposes you have/were beating her; hearer forced to accommodate or challenge.
```

### Presupposition Triggers

Words/constructions that invariably introduce presuppositions:

| Trigger | Presupposition | Example |
|---------|----------------|---------|
| **Definite descriptions** | Existence + uniqueness | "The king of France" → ∃ king of France |
| **Change of state verbs** | Prior state | "stop" → was doing it; "start" → wasn't doing it |
| **Factive verbs** | Truth of complement | "know/realize/regret" → complement is true |
| **Iterative "again"** | Prior occurrence | "She came back again" → she came before |
| **Temporal clauses** | Truth of temporal clause | "Before she left" → she left |
| **Clefts** | All-but-focus is presupposed | "It was John who left" → someone left |
| **Implicative verbs** | | "He managed to leave" → it was difficult to leave |

## Part VIII: Politeness Theory (Brown & Levinson)

**Face**: The public self-image that every person has and wants to protect.

```
FACE:
  POSITIVE FACE: desire for approval, solidarity, being liked
  NEGATIVE FACE: desire for autonomy, freedom from imposition

FACE-THREATENING ACT (FTA): any act that threatens face
  Threats to hearer's POSITIVE face: criticism, disagreement, insults
  Threats to hearer's NEGATIVE face: requests, commands, offers, reminders
  Threats to speaker's POSITIVE face: apologies, confessions, thanks
  Threats to speaker's NEGATIVE face: promises, offers of help

POLITENESS STRATEGIES (off → on record):
  1. Bald on record: direct, no mitigation — emergency situations
     "Stop!" / "Watch out!"
  2. Positive politeness: solidarity, attend to hearer's positive face
     "We could go to the movies sometime, right?"
  3. Negative politeness: deference, minimize imposition
     "I'm sorry to bother you, but could you possibly...?"
  4. Off record: indirectness, hint
     "It's cold in here." (request to close window — no FTA)
  5. Don't do the FTA at all
```

**Cultural variation**: What counts as face and how much mitigation is expected varies enormously. English tends toward negative politeness strategies. East Asian languages may embed positive politeness more strongly.

## Common Confusion Points

**Pragmatics is not just "context":**
Pragmatics has a formal, principled structure — Gricean maxims, speech act theory, presupposition triggers. It's not "everything that's contextual and fuzzy." There are systematic theories with predictive power.

**Gricean maxims are not rules:**
They're principles that rational agents follow in cooperative communication. Flouting them (violating them visibly) is what generates figurative language and indirect speech acts.

**Scalar implicatures are cancellable — entailments aren't:**
"Some cats are black — in fact, all of them are" ✓ (implicature cancelled).
"John ate the pizza — and he didn't eat anything" ✗ (entailment violated = contradiction).

**Relevance theory subsumes Grice:**
Relevance theory is meant to replace the four Gricean maxims with one principle. It's a competing, not complementary, framework. Whether it's simpler or just relocates the complexity is debated.

**Performatives don't have to be first-person present:**
"Passengers are requested to..." performs a directive. "Authorized personnel only" is a prohibition. The institutional context, not the grammatical form, is what makes something performative.

**Deixis is not just pronouns:**
Tense is deictic (relative to utterance time). Demonstratives are deictic. Many verbs (come/go, bring/take) encode deictic information. Articles (this/that) encode spatial deixis. It's pervasive in language.
