# Language Acquisition — How Language Is Learned

## The Big Picture

## Part I: L1 Acquisition — Stages

### Pre-linguistic Phase (0–12 months)

```
0–2 months:   Crying, reflexive vocalizations
2–4 months:   Cooing (back-of-mouth vowel-like sounds)
4–7 months:   Marginal babbling (simple CV syllables: [ba], [da])
7–10 months:  Canonical babbling (reduplicated: [bababa], [mamama])
              KEY MILESTONE: consonant-vowel structure emerges
              Cross-linguistic universality: all babies babble regardless of exposure
              Deaf babies babble with hands (signed language)
10–12 months: Variegated babbling ([bagiba]) + intonation contours of ambient language
              Perceptual narrowing: discriminate native phoneme contrasts better,
              lose sensitivity to non-native contrasts (Werker & Tees, 1984)
```

**Perceptual narrowing** is key: at 6 months, Japanese infants discriminate English /l/ vs /r/. By 12 months, they've stopped — Japanese doesn't contrast these.

### One-Word Stage (12–18 months)

```
12 months:    First words (holophrase — single word communicates full proposition)
              "juice" = "I want juice" OR "this is juice" OR "you dropped the juice"
              Context + intonation = disambiguation

PRODUCTIVE VOCABULARY AT 12 MONTHS: ~1–5 words
VOCABULARY SPURT (18 months): ~50 words → fast-mapping begins
  Fast-mapping: child hears word once, stores rough meaning, refines later
  "Give me the chromium one" (next to the regular cup) → child maps "chromium" to that cup
```

### Two-Word Stage (18–24 months)

```
"Mommy gone", "more milk", "big dog", "where kitty"
MEAN LENGTH OF UTTERANCE (MLU) ≈ 2 morphemes

TELEGRAPHIC SPEECH:
  Content words retained; function words, inflections dropped.
  "Mommy sock" = "Mommy is putting on a sock" or "that's Mommy's sock"
  Semantic relations expressed without grammatical marking.
```

### Multi-Word Stage and Beyond (24 months+)

```
24 months:   3-word utterances, simple sentences begin
             "Daddy hit ball" — Subject-Verb-Object structure appears
30 months:   Auxiliaries, questions, negation begin
             "Can I go?" "Why dat boy cry?"
36 months:   Complement clauses: "I think it's raining"
42 months:   Relative clauses: "The man who I saw"
5 years:     Near-adult competence in most domains

RULE OVERGENERALIZATION:
  Children acquire the regular rule before memorizing exceptions.
  "I goed", "I runned", "two foots", "more gooder"
  This is diagnostic of rule learning, not just memory.

  The U-shaped curve:
    Stage 1: "went" (memorized — irregular correct)
    Stage 2: "goed" (rule learned → overgeneralized)
    Stage 3: "went" (exception re-stored alongside rule)
```

### Roger Brown's 14 Morphemes (Acquired in Order)

Brown (1973) tracked three children (Adam, Eve, Sarah) and found:

```
ORDER OF ACQUISITION (approximate, not exact — but strikingly consistent):
1.  Present progressive (-ing)
2.  Preposition in
3.  Preposition on
4.  Regular plural (-s)
5.  Past irregular (went, broke)
6.  Possessive ('s)
7.  Uncontractible copula (are in "Who is happy?" "She is")
8.  Articles (a, the)
9.  Past regular (-ed)
10. Regular third person (-s: he runs)
11. Irregular third person (has, does)
12. Uncontractible auxiliary (is, are in "He is running")
13. Contractible copula ('s in "He's happy")
14. Contractible auxiliary ('s in "He's running")

Why this order? A combination of:
- Semantic complexity (past irregular = high semantic salience)
- Phonological salience (longer forms acquired before shorter)
- Grammatical complexity
- Frequency in input
```

## Part III: The Poverty of Stimulus — Nativist Core Argument

The central nativist argument, formalized:

```
THE POVERTY OF STIMULUS (POS) ARGUMENT:

PREMISE 1: Children acquire grammatical knowledge K.
PREMISE 2: K could not be learned from the input they receive:
           - Input is finite
           - Input contains performance errors
           - Children receive no negative evidence
             (no one says "that's wrong" and explains why)
           - Input underdetermines K
CONCLUSION: K must be (partly) innate — Universal Grammar.

CLASSIC POS EXAMPLE: Aux-Inversion in Questions

  "The man who is tall is happy." → "Is the man who is tall ___ happy?"
  NOT: "Is the man who ___ tall is happy?" ← structure-dependent rule

  LINEAR RULE (wrong): "Move the first 'is' to the front"
     → would give "Is the man who ___ tall is happy?" ← WRONG
  STRUCTURE-DEPENDENT RULE (correct): "Move the matrix auxiliary"
     → correctly gives "Is the man who is tall ___ happy?" ✓

  PINKER'S ARGUMENT: No child ever makes the linear-rule error.
  This suggests the structure-dependent rule is innate (not learned from input).

  COUNTER (corpus-based): Such sentences are extremely rare in child-directed speech.
  Children may infer structure-dependence from simpler sentences + statistical patterns.
  (Pullum & Scholz 2002 challenge the POS claim on empirical grounds)
```

## Part IV: Usage-Based Alternative — Statistical Learning

The nativist account is not uncontested. Usage-based linguistics argues statistical learning + social cognition is sufficient.

### Saffran et al. (1996) — Statistical Word Segmentation

**The ML bridge:** Saffran's 8-month-olds are running unsupervised bigram learning over a phoneme stream. Transitional probability P(B|A) = freq(AB)/freq(A) is exactly the bigram co-occurrence ratio used in n-gram language models. The infants are computing maximum-likelihood bigram statistics over 2 minutes of input and using the learned distribution to identify word boundaries.

The broader acquisition debate maps cleanly onto the central ML architecture choice:

```
NATIVIST                              USAGE-BASED / EMPIRICIST
(Chomsky, Pinker)                     (Tomasello, Goldberg, Elman)

Strong inductive bias:                Minimal inductive bias:
  UG provides a highly                  Architecture is domain-general
  constrained hypothesis space          (associative, statistical)
  over possible grammars

Few parameters to set:                Many parameters:
  Acquire grammar by setting            Build grammar from data;
  a small number of binary              no pre-specified parameter space
  parameters (Principles &
  Parameters)

Poverty of the stimulus argument:     Data-driven argument:
  Input is insufficient to             Input is richer than claimed;
  determine the grammar                 statistical cues are sufficient

Analogy to ML: hand-designed          Analogy to ML: large neural
  feature engineering +                  network trained on raw data;
  constrained model class               no hand-engineered features;
  (SVM with linguistic kernels)         learns representations end-to-end
  Sample-efficient but assumes          Data-hungry but makes fewer
  the right inductive bias              assumptions
```

**Current state:** The field has moved toward a synthesis position analogous to the "inductive bias is real but it's domain-general, not language-specific" view. Recurrent neural networks and transformers trained on child-directed speech corpora do learn grammatical abstractions without explicit linguistic feature engineering — supporting the empiricist side. But the amount of data required still exceeds what children receive, and specific structural biases (structure-dependence, island constraints) appear early and robustly — supporting nativist priors.

The debate is genuinely unresolved. It maps onto one of the deepest open questions in deep learning: how much of what GPT-4 "knows" is from pretraining inductive biases versus data, and whether there exists a domain-general architecture that achieves human-level sample efficiency.

```
EXPERIMENT:
  8-month-old infants exposed to 2 minutes of artificially concatenated CVC syllables
  with statistical structure:
    "tupiro" — high transitional probability between syllables (it's a word)
    "ti.budo" — low TP (crosses word boundary)

  After 2 minutes, infants preferred novel syllable sequences over "words" — showing
  they had computed transitional probabilities and learned "words."

TRANSITIONAL PROBABILITY: P(B|A) = freq(AB)/freq(A)
  Within words: high TP (pre → ty in "pretty")
  Between words: low TP (ty → dog between "pretty dog")

This shows remarkable statistical learning from minimal input.
```

### Item-Specific vs. Abstract Rules

Tomasello and colleagues:

```
OBSERVATION: Children learn verb constructions item-specifically at first.
  "I want milk" / "I want Mommy" / "I want that"
  → Use "want" in DO construction before generalizing to other verbs.

  "Make it go" — go used in caused-motion construction
  → Does NOT immediately extend to novel verb in same construction.

  This suggests early grammar = stored exemplars, not abstract rules.
  Abstraction comes gradually, with frequency and cue reinforcement.

IMPLICATION: The abstract rules (X-bar, merge) may be learned, not innate.
  They emerge from pattern extraction over sufficient input.
```

## Part V: The Critical Period — Genie and Related Evidence

### Lenneberg's Critical Period Hypothesis (1967)

```
CLAIM: Language acquisition is biologically timed.
  A critical period exists roughly from birth to puberty.
  After the critical period closes (puberty ≈ ~12 years), native-like
  acquisition is no longer possible.

EVIDENCE:
  - Left hemisphere specialization for language (normally lateralized ~age 5)
  - Aphasia recovery: better in children than adults
  - L2 acquisition: learners after puberty rarely achieve native-like proficiency

SENSITIVE PERIOD vs CRITICAL PERIOD:
  Critical = hard cutoff (like a light switch)
  Sensitive = gradient decline in plasticity (more like a dimmer)
  Evidence supports "sensitive" rather than strict "critical."
```

### Genie — The Tragic Natural Experiment

```
GENIE (pseudonym, born ~1957):
  Isolated from birth to age 13 in a room, strapped to a potty chair.
  Father beat her if she made noise.
  No linguistic input during childhood.

  DISCOVERED 1970, age 13.
  Despite intensive intervention, Genie never acquired:
  - Normal syntax (no auxiliary inversion, no complex sentences)
  - Normal morphology (articles and function words sparse)
  - Normal phonology

  But she DID acquire:
  - Extensive vocabulary
  - Communicative intent
  - Understanding of semantics/pragmatics at some level

  INTERPRETATION: Post-critical-period L1 acquisition shows:
  - Lexicon and semantics more resilient than syntax/morphology
  - Consistent with period-sensitive syntactic acquisition
```

**Confound**: Genie also experienced severe social deprivation, neglect, trauma — not purely a linguistic deprivation experiment. Isolating the linguistic variable from the psychological is impossible.

**Victor of Aveyron** (wild child found 1800) — similar profile.

**Chelsea** (born deaf, not diagnosed until age 32, then fitted with hearing aids) — despite years of hearing input post-period, never acquired normal syntax.

## Part VI: Bilingual Acquisition

### Simultaneous Bilingualism (two languages from birth)

```
MYTH: "Bilingual children are confused / develop slower"
REALITY: Mild early delays in each language individually, NO overall delay.
         Bilinguals develop one language system with two subsystems.

EARLY MIXING (code-switching):
  Not confusion — systematic. Children mix across languages even when
  speaking to monolingual adults (early period).
  By age 3-4, they reliably separate languages for different interlocutors.

CROSSLINGUISTIC INFLUENCE:
  Dominant language can influence weaker language's structure.
  Temporary structural borrowing of syntax/morphology from L1 into L2 and vice versa.

BALANCED vs. DOMINANT:
  Few bilinguals are truly balanced. Input frequency shapes dominance.
```

## Part VII: L2 Acquisition

Second language acquisition (SLA) is profoundly different from L1.

### Why L2 Is Harder

```
L1                              L2
+----------------------------+  +----------------------------+
| No prior linguistic system |  | L1 fully in place          |
| All learning is acquisition |  | L1 interferes             |
| Critical period open        |  | Critical period closing/  |
| Near-universal success       |  |   closed                 |
|                             |  | Near-universal incompletion|
| Error = developmental      |  | Error = transfer from L1   |
| Motivation: survival       |  | Motivation: varies widely  |
+----------------------------+  +----------------------------+
```

### Krashen's Monitor Model (1982)

Influential (and controversial) framework:

```
5 KRASHEN HYPOTHESES:

1. ACQUISITION vs LEARNING:
   Acquisition: subconscious, natural, through meaningful input (L1-like)
   Learning: conscious, explicit rule knowledge
   Acquired language → fluency; learned language → editing/monitoring

2. NATURAL ORDER:
   Morphemes/structures acquired in a predictable order (similar to L1 Brown morphemes)
   regardless of L1 or instructional order.

3. MONITOR:
   Explicit rules used only for self-editing (monitor).
   Conditions: time, focus on form, know the rule.
   Over-users = slow/hesitant; Under-users = errors

4. INPUT HYPOTHESIS (i + 1):
   Acquisition occurs when input is comprehensible but slightly above current level.
   Not speaking but understanding is the key.

5. AFFECTIVE FILTER:
   Anxiety, motivation, self-confidence affect "filter" height.
   Low filter → input gets in → acquisition
   High filter → even comprehensible input blocked
```

**Criticisms of Krashen:**
- Acquisition/learning distinction is not empirically testable
- "i + 1" is not operationalized
- Evidence that explicit instruction accelerates acquisition (contradicts pure input theory)

### Interlanguage

Selinker (1972): L2 learners have a systematic internal grammar at each stage — **interlanguage** — that is neither L1 nor L2:

```
INTERLANGUAGE PROPERTIES:
  - Systematic: learner's errors are rule-governed (not random)
  - Dynamic: changes over time
  - L1-transfer: many errors reflect L1 structure
  - Fossilization: some structures freeze below native-like level
    even after years of exposure and instruction

TRANSFER EXAMPLES:
  Japanese → English: "I yesterday went store" (SOV word order transferred)
  French → English: "He has much books" (mass/count distinction different in French)
  Spanish → English: "He is very boring the meeting" (ser/estar confusion)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Children learn language, not words:**
The vocabulary spurt is not the key event. Grammar acquisition — the abstract rules for combining words — is the central puzzle. A child with 10,000 words who can't combine them has not acquired language.

**Negative evidence ≠ explicit correction:**
"You said 'goed' — that's wrong, say 'went'" = explicit correction (rare, generally ineffective in changing grammar). What children DON'T get is negative evidence in the logical sense — they aren't told "sentence X is not grammatical in your language." This is what Chomsky means by absence of negative evidence.

**Critical period doesn't mean "can't learn L2 after puberty":**
Adult L2 learners CAN become highly proficient. What the critical period predicts is that native-like phonology and automatic grammar become extremely hard post-puberty. A VP of engineering who learned English at 25 can communicate brilliantly — but probably has an accent and makes occasional morphological errors that a childhood L2 learner wouldn't.

**Brown's morpheme order is correlational, not causal:**
The 14 morphemes are acquired in a consistent order, but this doesn't mean you have to acquire #7 before #8. The order reflects a combination of factors; it's a statistical tendency, not a strict developmental gate.

**Bilingual code-switching is systematic, not deficit:**
In bilingual communities, code-switching (mixing languages within a sentence or conversation) is rule-governed and can serve social and expressive functions unavailable in either language alone. It is not a failure to separate the languages.
