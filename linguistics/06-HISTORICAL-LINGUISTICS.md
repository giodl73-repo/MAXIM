# Historical Linguistics — Language Change

## The Big Picture

Historical linguistics studies how languages change over time — phonologically, morphologically, syntactically — and uses systematic comparison to reconstruct ancient languages and classify languages into families.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    HISTORICAL LINGUISTICS                         |
|                                                                  |
|  LANGUAGE CHANGE              COMPARATIVE METHOD                |
|  (how and why languages       (reconstruct proto-languages       |
|   change over time)            from systematic correspondences)  |
|                                                                  |
|  LANGUAGE FAMILIES            LANGUAGE CONTACT                  |
|  (trees, branches,            (borrowing, pidgins,              |
|   genetic classification)      creoles, convergence)            |
|                                                                  |
|  TYPES OF CHANGE:                                               |
|  Phonological (sound shifts)                                     |
|  Morphological (paradigm leveling, analogy)                     |
|  Syntactic (word order change, grammaticalization)              |
|  Semantic (narrowing, widening, amelioration, pejoration)       |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Part I: The Discovery — William Jones (1786)

Sir William Jones, addressing the Asiatic Society in Calcutta:

```
"The Sanskrit language, whatever be its antiquity, is of a wonderful
 structure; more perfect than the Greek, more copious than the Latin,
 and more exquisitely refined than either; yet bearing to both of them
 a stronger affinity, both in the roots of verbs and in the forms of
 grammar, than could possibly have been produced by accident; so strong,
 indeed, that no philologer could examine them all three, without believing
 them to have sprung from some common source, which, perhaps, no longer exists."
```

The key insight: **systematic correspondences** across languages cannot be accidental. They indicate **common ancestry**.

## Part II: Types of Language Change

### 1. Phonological Change (Sound Change)

**Neogrammarian Hypothesis** (Brugmann, Osthoff, 1878):

```
NEOGRAMMARIAN HYPOTHESIS:
  "Sound laws admit no exceptions."

  Sound change is:
  1. EXCEPTIONLESS: operates on ALL words containing the relevant sound
     in the relevant environment
  2. REGULAR: the same input in the same environment → same output
  3. BLIND: doesn't care about word meaning, morphological category, etc.

  Apparent exceptions are explained by:
  - Analogy (morphological pressure)
  - Borrowing (words that entered after the change)
  - Dialect mixture
```

**Types of sound change:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Lenition** | Weakening of consonants | Latin /p/ → Spanish /b/: capitu → cabeza |
| **Fortition** | Strengthening | /w/ → /β/ in Spanish |
| **Vowel shift** | Systematic vowel quality change | Great English Vowel Shift (below) |
| **Merger** | Two phonemes collapse into one | /ʍ/ (which) merging with /w/ in many dialects |
| **Split** | One phoneme becomes two in different environments | Latin /k/ → Italian /k/ or /tʃ/ before front vowel |
| **Assimilation** | Sound becomes more like neighbor | Anticipatory: nasal assimilation |
| **Dissimilation** | Sound becomes less like neighbor | Latin *anima* → *alma* in Spanish (via dissimilation) |
| **Epenthesis** | Sound inserted | Old English *thrum* → Modern English *thumb* |
| **Metathesis** | Sounds reordered | Old English *brid* → Modern English *bird* |
| **Apocope** | Word-final loss | Latin *canem* → Spanish *can* → French *chien* |

## Part III: Grimm's Law — The Germanic Consonant Shift

Jacob Grimm (1822) — the most famous sound law in historical linguistics.

```
PROTO-INDO-EUROPEAN (PIE) → PROTO-GERMANIC CONSONANT SHIFT

CHAIN SHIFT 1: Voiced aspirates → Voiced fricatives
  bʰ → b (or β/v)    dʰ → d (or ð)    gʰ → g (or ɣ)
  PIE *bʰrāter  → Gmc *brōþēr    → English "brother"
  (cf. Sanskrit bhrāta, Latin frāter)

CHAIN SHIFT 2: Voiced stops → Voiceless stops
  b → p    d → t    g → k
  PIE *dekm  → Gmc *tehun   → English "ten"
  (cf. Latin decem, Greek deka, Sanskrit daśan)

CHAIN SHIFT 3: Voiceless stops → Voiceless fricatives
  p → f    t → θ    k → x (h)
  PIE *pṓds  → Gmc *fōts    → English "foot"
  (cf. Latin pēs/pedis, Greek pous/podos)
  PIE *treyes → Gmc *þrīz   → English "three"
  (cf. Latin trēs, Greek treis)
  PIE *ḱerd   → Gmc *hertō  → English "heart"
  (cf. Latin cor/cordis, Greek kardia)
```

Visual summary:

```
PIE:    bʰ dʰ gʰ     b  d  g     p  t  k
           ↓              ↓          ↓
PGmc:    b  d  g      p  t  k     f  θ  x/h
           (spirants/   (stops)   (fricatives)
            stops)
```

**The correspondences:**

| Meaning | PIE | Latin | Greek | Sanskrit | English (Gmc) |
|---------|-----|-------|-------|---------|----------------|
| father | *ph₂tḗr | pater | patēr | pitr | father |
| foot | *pōds | pēs (ped-) | pous (pod-) | pāt | foot |
| three | *treyes | trēs | treis | trayaḥ | three |
| ten | *deḱm | decem | deka | daśan | ten |
| heart | *ḱerd | cor (cord-) | kardia | hṛd | heart |
| tooth | *h₂dónts | dēns (dent-) | ōdous (odont-) | danta | tooth |
| night | *nókʷts | nox (noct-) | nux (nukt-) | nakta | night |

## Part IV: Verner's Law — Grimm's "Exceptions"

Grimm's Law had apparent exceptions:

```
PIE *pater → should give English *fader (p→f, t→θ)
But we have "father" (p→f, t→d)

PIE *bhrāter → "brother" (bh→b, t→d)
PIE *swekrū → "sweher" (OE) → "father-in-law"

VERNER (1875) discovered:
  The "exceptions" were REGULAR — conditioned by PIE ACCENT position.

  Voiceless fricatives (from Grimm shift 3) became VOICED
  when the PIE accent did not immediately PRECEDE them.

  PIE *ph₂tḗr: stress on -tḗr → accent DOES precede /t/ → /t/ → /θ/ (father) [f+θ=father — wait]

  Actually:
  PIE *ph₂tér: accent on second syllable → accent precedes t → t→θ → OHG "fater", OE "fæder"
  PIE *bhrā́ter: accent on first syllable → accent does NOT precede t → t→d → "brother"
```

The full pattern:

```
VERNER'S LAW:
  Grimm's voiceless fricatives (f, θ, x, s) were voiced to
  (β/v, ð, ɣ, z) when immediately following an unaccented syllable
  (in the PIE accentuation system).

  Later, PIE free accent was replaced by fixed Germanic initial stress,
  obscuring the conditioning environment — hence it looked irregular.
```

**Significance**: Verner's Law demonstrated that apparent irregularities in sound change are **regularly conditioned** by factors that may have since been lost. The Neogrammarian hypothesis was vindicated.

## Part V: The Comparative Method

The procedure for reconstructing proto-languages:

```
COMPARATIVE METHOD STEPS:

1. COLLECT cognate sets across related languages:
   English: father  / Latin: pater / Greek: patēr / Sanskrit: pitr

2. ESTABLISH REGULAR CORRESPONDENCES:
   Eng  Lat  Gk   Skt
   f    p    p    p    (for initial consonant in this set)
   æ    a    a    i    (for first vowel)
   ð    t    t    t    (for medial consonant)

3. IDENTIFY MOST LIKELY PROTO-PHONEME:
   Latin/Greek/Sanskrit agree on /p/ → Proto-IE had *p
   Germanic shows f (from Grimm's Law: p → f)
   So PIE *p → PGmc *f → Eng f is the derivation

4. RECONSTRUCT PROTO-FORMS:
   PIE *ph₂tḗr (the laryngeal h₂ explains vowel differences)

5. POSIT REGULAR CHANGES for each daughter language:
   PIE *p → Latin p (no change), Greek p (no change), Sanskrit p (no change), Gmc f (Grimm)
   Each change is a regular rule, not an exception.
```

## Part VI: Types of Language Change

### Phonological Change (covered above)

### Morphological Change

**Analogy**: New forms created by analogy with existing patterns, breaking irregular alternations:

```
OLD ENGLISH strong verbs (like German):
  helpan/healp/holpen → Modern English help/helped/helped
  (strong ablaut pattern → regularized to -ed)

  "holp" still appears in Shakespeare ("the rain it raineth every day, and so it holp him")
  → gradually replaced by "helped" by analogy with regular -ed class

REGULARIZATION OF PLURALS:
  kine (archaic plural of cow) → cows (by analogy)
  brethren → brothers (by analogy, except in religious contexts)
```

**Paradigm leveling**: Allomorphic variation reduced to a single form:

```
OLD ENGLISH: foot/fet (singular/plural — umlaut)
Modern: foot/feet (retained — still irregular)
But: cow/kine → cow/cows (leveled to regular)
Middle English: boc/bec → book/books (leveled)
```

### Syntactic Change

**Grammaticalization**: A lexical item becomes a grammatical morpheme:

```
WILL (lexical verb "desire/want") → future tense auxiliary:
  Old English: "ic wille faran" = "I want to go" (lexical)
  Modern English: "I will go" (future tense — grammaticalized, not about wanting)

GOING TO (lexical motion) → future auxiliary:
  "I am going to the store to buy milk" (motion)
  → "I'm going to buy milk" (intention/future)
  → "I'm gonna buy milk" (fully grammaticalized, phonologically reduced)

BE ABLE TO → can:
  "can" was lexical "know how to" in Old English
  → grammaticalized to general ability/possibility modal

HAVE → perfect auxiliary:
  "I have [VP the letter [written]]" (possess a written letter)
  → "I have written the letter" (grammaticalized perfect)
```

**Word order change:** Old English was (relatively) V2 and SOV-like with flexible order:

```
Old English: Hē hine met → subject, object, verb
Modern English: He met him → fixed SVO

The loss of case endings (which allowed flexible order) → pressure toward fixed order.
```

### Semantic Change

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Broadening (generalization)** | Meaning becomes more inclusive | "dog" (OE: a specific breed) → all canines |
| **Narrowing (specialization)** | Meaning becomes more restricted | "hound" (OE: any dog) → hunting dog |
| **Amelioration** | Meaning improves | "knight" (OE: youth, servant) → noble warrior |
| **Pejoration** | Meaning degrades | "villain" (OF: farmhand) → scoundrel |
| **Metaphorical extension** | Concrete → abstract | "grasp" (physical hold) → "grasp an idea" |
| **Bleaching** | Meaning weakens | "very" (Latin *verus* = true) → mere intensifier |
| **Euphemism treadmill** | Euphemism inherits old word's negative meaning | toilet → restroom → bathroom → lavatory → ... |

## Part VII: Language Contact — Pidgins, Creoles, and Convergence

### Borrowing

Languages in contact borrow vocabulary, sounds, and structures:

```
LOANWORD LAYERS IN ENGLISH:
  Anglo-Saxon (Germanic) core: man, woman, child, eat, drink, sleep, live, die
  Norse (from Danelaw): sky, egg, window, knife, they/their/them
  French (Norman Conquest 1066): beef, pork, veal (vs. cow, pig, calf — class split)
                                  government, justice, noble, parliament
  Latin (Renaissance, scholarly): science, intelligence, academic, senator
  Modern borrowings from everywhere: algebra (Arabic), kindergarten (German), kimono (Japanese)
```

**Cultural domains predict borrowing direction.** French influenced English in law, cuisine, fashion (higher prestige domains during Norman period). English borrowed Norse in everyday rural vocabulary.

### Pidgins

```
PIDGIN:
  Develops when speakers of different languages need to communicate
  but share no common language.

  Characteristics:
  - Highly simplified morphology (little or no inflection)
  - Reduced syntax
  - Small vocabulary (from one dominant source language = "lexifier")
  - No native speakers

  Examples:
  - Tok Pisin (Papua New Guinea) — English-based
  - Nigerian Pidgin English
  - Haitian Creole (before creolization)
  - Historical: West African Pidgin, Maritime pidgins
```

### Creoles

```
CREOLE:
  A fully-developed language that arises when pidgin-speaking parents
  raise children who learn it as a native language.

  Children EXPAND the pidgin:
  - Regular morphology
  - Full syntactic complexity
  - Systematic semantics/pragmatics
  - Native speakers

  BICKERTON'S LANGUAGE BIOPROGRAM HYPOTHESIS (1981):
    Creoles across unrelated locations (Caribbean, Pacific, Indian Ocean)
    show striking structural similarities:
    - TMA (Tense-Mood-Aspect) markers as three separate preverbal particles
    - Specific patterns for copula, passive, questions
    These similarities suggest children are accessing innate grammatical templates
    when input is impoverished (pidgin) — the "bioprogram."

  Evidence: Haitian, Jamaican, and Tok Pisin creoles structurally similar despite
  different lexifier languages and different geographic origins.

  Criticism: Substrate influence (African languages) may explain similarities.
  The Bioprogram hypothesis is controversial but influential.
```

### Sprachbund (Linguistic Area)

Languages in geographic contact that share features despite different ancestry:

```
BALKAN SPRACHBUND:
  Albanian, Romanian, Bulgarian, Macedonian, Greek (and partially Serbian)
  Share features NOT shared by their IE relatives:
  - Postposed definite article (Romanian: om-ul "man-THE")
  - Loss of infinitive (replaced by subjunctive clauses)
  - Merged dative/genitive
  - Future with "want" verb

  These languages are NOT genealogically related (Albanian, Romanian, Greek, Slavic)
  but share contact-induced features.

MESOAMERICAN SPRACHBUND: shared features across Aztec, Maya, Totonac, etc.
SOUTH ASIAN SPRACHBUND (Indian subcontinent): retroflex consonants cross Dravidian/IE boundary
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Analysis |
|----------|----------|
| Why does English "father" ≠ Latin "pater" in initial consonant? | Grimm's Law: PIE *p → PGmc *f |
| Why "brother" (d) vs "father" (th)? | Verner's Law: accent position conditioned voiced/voiceless |
| How do we know what PIE sounded like? | Comparative method: find regular correspondences across daughters |
| Why do English plurals use -s mostly? | Analogy/leveling from originally plural Latin/French -s |
| Why does "will" mark future? | Grammaticalization: lexical "want" → future marker |
| Why do Haitian/Jamaican creoles look similar? | Bioprogram (Bickerton) or substrate convergence |
| Why do Romanian/Bulgarian/Albanian share features despite different ancestry? | Balkan Sprachbund — contact-induced convergence |
| How did "villain" come to mean "scoundrel"? | Pejoration: farmhand → person of low moral character |

## Common Confusion Points

**Language families are genealogical, not typological:**
Spanish and Hungarian are both VSO (in some analyses) and share features, but they're unrelated. Language family = common ancestor, not shared features.

**"Proto-language" doesn't mean "primitive":**
PIE was as complex as any modern language. "Proto" means ancestral/reconstructed, not simple or early in some developmental sense.

**Sound change ≠ decay:**
Historical linguists are descriptivists. Latin "decaying" into French is not decay — it's regular, systematic change. Every stage of the language is complete and functional.

**Grimm's Law vs. the Great English Vowel Shift:**
Grimm's Law = consonant changes in the pre-history of Germanic (affecting all Germanic languages). The Great English Vowel Shift (1400–1700) = vowel changes specific to Middle English, explaining why "name" (formerly /naːmə/) sounds different from "Name" in German (same ancestor). These are different events, separated by ~2000 years.

**Borrowing vs. cognates:**
"Beef" (French) and "cow" (Germanic) are not cognates — English borrowed "beef" from French *bœuf*. "Pater" (Latin) and "father" (English) ARE cognates — both from PIE *ph₂tḗr. Etymology vs. cognacy.

**The Neogrammarians were not right about everything:**
The "no exceptions" claim is too strong — analogy, dialect mixture, and borrowing all introduce irregularity. But it was a productive research methodology: when you find exceptions, look for the regular conditioning environment you missed.
