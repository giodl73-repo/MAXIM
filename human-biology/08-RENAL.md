# Renal System

## The Big Picture

```
KIDNEYS: two ~150g organs sitting retroperitoneally at T12–L3

FUNCTIONS (more than just "make urine"):
  1. Filter and excrete metabolic waste (urea, creatinine, uric acid)
  2. Regulate blood volume (and therefore blood pressure)
  3. Regulate plasma osmolality (water balance)
  4. Regulate acid-base (H⁺ secretion, HCO₃⁻ reabsorption)
  5. Regulate electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, phosphate)
  6. Endocrine: EPO (erythropoietin → RBC production), calcitriol (vit D activation), renin
  7. Gluconeogenesis (during prolonged fasting)

SCALE:
  Blood flow: 1,200 mL/min = 25% of cardiac output at rest
  GFR: ~120 mL/min → filters entire plasma volume every ~25 min
  Filtrate produced: ~180 L/day
  Urine excreted: ~1.5 L/day (99.2% reabsorbed!)
```

---

## Kidney Anatomy

```
MACROSTRUCTURE:
  Cortex (outer): glomeruli, PCT, DCT, early collecting tubules
  Medulla (inner): loops of Henle, collecting ducts
    Pyramids: triangular medullary segments
    Papillae: tips of pyramids, drain into minor calyces
  Renal pelvis → ureter → bladder → urethra

VASCULATURE (critical for understanding GFR):
  Renal artery → segmental → interlobar → arcuate → interlobular arteries
  → Afferent arteriole → GLOMERULUS (capillary tuft) → Efferent arteriole
  → Peritubular capillaries (cortex: surrounds PCT/DCT)
  → Vasa recta (medulla: hairpin loops follow Henle's loop — countercurrent)
  → Venous return

The glomerulus is sandwiched between two arterioles (both resistors):
  Afferent dilation / efferent constriction → ↑ GFR
  Both constrict (severe hypotension) → ↓ GFR → acute kidney injury
```

---

## Nephron: The Functional Unit

~1 million nephrons per kidney, two types:
- **Cortical nephrons** (85%): short loops, mostly cortical
- **Juxtamedullary nephrons** (15%): long loops extending deep into medulla — key for concentrating urine

```
NEPHRON ANATOMY:
  Glomerulus + Bowman's capsule (filtration)
       ↓
  Proximal convoluted tubule — PCT (bulk reabsorption)
       ↓
  Loop of Henle:
    Descending limb (thin, water-permeable) → Hairpin turn
    Ascending limb (thick, impermeable to water, ion-pumping)
       ↓
  Distal convoluted tubule — DCT (fine-tuning; hormone-sensitive)
       ↓
  Collecting duct (CD) — hormone-sensitive water permeability (ADH)
       ↓
  Medullary collecting duct → papilla → minor calyx → urine
```

## Glomerular Filtration

### Filtration Barrier

```
BLOOD → FILTRATE must cross:
  1. Fenestrated capillary endothelium (large pores, blocks cells)
  2. Glomerular basement membrane (GBM): type IV collagen + heparan sulfate
     (negatively charged → repels albumin, other anions)
  3. Podocyte foot processes with slit diaphragm (size + charge selection)
     Nephrin + podocin proteins; mutations → nephrotic syndrome

Normally filtered: water, electrolytes, glucose, amino acids, small proteins
NOT filtered: RBCs, WBCs, platelets, albumin (mostly), large proteins
```

### GFR and its Determinants

```
GFR = Kf × (P_GC − P_BS − π_GC + π_BS)

Kf  = filtration coefficient (area × permeability)
P_GC = glomerular capillary hydrostatic pressure (promotes filtration)
P_BS = Bowman's space hydrostatic pressure (opposes filtration, ↑ in obstruction)
π_GC = oncotic pressure of glomerular plasma (opposes filtration)
π_BS = Bowman's space oncotic pressure (promotes filtration, normally ~0)

Normal GFR: ~120 mL/min (140 mL/min in young men, corrected for body surface area)

Regulation:
  ↑ GFR: afferent dilation (prostaglandins), efferent constriction (Ang II)
  ↓ GFR: afferent constriction (NSAIDs block prostaglandins → constrict afferent)
          both constrict: hypotension, sepsis, RAAS activation

  NSAIDs in heart failure: prostaglandins are maintaining GFR against Ang II
    Add NSAID → remove vasodilatory PGs → unopposed Ang II → acute kidney injury
```

### Filtration Fraction and Autoregulation

Filtration fraction = GFR / RPF (renal plasma flow) = ~20% normally

**Autoregulation** (myogenic + tubuloglomerular feedback): maintains GFR between MAP 60–180 mmHg:
- Myogenic: afferent arteriole stretches → contracts (↑ BP → ↑ resistance → GFR stable)
- TGF (tubuloglomerular feedback): macula densa cells (in DCT near glomerulus) sense NaCl delivery → signal back to afferent arteriole → adjust GFR

## Tubular Reabsorption by Segment

### Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT)

Reabsorbs **67% of filtered load** of Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, water, and **100%** of glucose and amino acids under normal conditions:

```
APICAL MEMBRANE (facing lumen):
  Na⁺/glucose cotransporter (SGLT2): 90% glucose reabsorption (drug target: gliflozins)
  Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE3): reabsorbs Na⁺, secretes H⁺ → carbonic anhydrase → HCO₃⁻
  Na⁺/amino acid cotransporters, Na⁺/phosphate, Na⁺/lactate

BASOLATERAL MEMBRANE:
  Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase: pumps Na⁺ out → low intracellular Na⁺ → drives all apical Na⁺ cotransport
  GLUT2: glucose exits into blood

Water follows Na⁺ osmotically (aquaporins 1 + 7 constitutively expressed)
Isosmotic reabsorption: no change in tubular fluid osmolality as it moves through PCT
```

**Glucose Tm**: Transport maximum ~375 mg/min. When filtered glucose exceeds Tm → glucosuria (blood glucose > ~180 mg/dL = renal threshold for glucose → T2DM spills glucose).
SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin): block SGLT2 → glucosuria → weight loss + BP reduction + cardioprotective + renoprotective.

### Loop of Henle: Countercurrent Mechanism

```
PURPOSE: Create an osmotic gradient in medullary interstitium (300 → 1,200 mOsm/kg cortex → papilla)
This gradient is used by collecting duct (with ADH) to concentrate urine

DESCENDING LIMB (thin):
  Permeable to water, NOT Na⁺
  Medullary gradient draws water out → tubular fluid becomes hyperosmotic as it descends
  Urea enters from medullary interstitium (recycling)

ASCENDING LIMB (thick — TAL):
  IMPERMEABLE to water (always)
  Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporter (NKCC2) pumps NaCl out into interstitium
  → Dilutes tubular fluid → makes medullary interstitium hyperosmotic
  NKCC2 is the target of LOOP DIURETICS (furosemide, bumetanide):
    Block NKCC2 → massive NaCl + water loss → powerful diuresis

COUNTERCURRENT MULTIPLIER:
  Hairpin arrangement + differential permeabilities amplify the gradient:
  Small single-effect per turn, but multiplied along length
  Vasa recta: countercurrent exchanger — preserves gradient (takes up NaCl/urea going down,
  releases going up) → doesn't wash out gradient like straight capillaries would
```

### Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT)

Reabsorbs ~5% Na⁺ (important for fine-tuning):

```
DCT1:
  NCC (Na⁺/Cl⁻ cotransporter) — blocked by THIAZIDE DIURETICS (HCTZ, chlorthalidone)
    ↑ Urinary NaCl loss; initial ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption (clinical: hypercalciuria, kidney stones)
    Also: ↓ serum K⁺, ↑ serum Ca²⁺, ↑ uric acid (gout), ↑ glucose

DCT2 / Connecting tubule:
  ENaC (epithelial Na⁺ channel) — regulated by ALDOSTERONE
  ROMK (K⁺ channel) — K⁺ secretion (aldosterone increases)
  Ca²⁺ reabsorption: TRPV5 channel (PTH ↑, thiazide ↑)

  ALDOSTERONE (from adrenal zona glomerulosa):
    → binds MR (mineralocorticoid receptor) → nuclear → ↑ ENaC + Na/K-ATPase + ROMK expression
    → Na⁺ reabsorption → K⁺ secretion → H₂O retention (follows Na)
  POTASSIUM-SPARING DIURETICS:
    Spironolactone/eplerenone: aldosterone receptor antagonists
    Amiloride/triamterene: ENaC blockers directly
    Side effect of both: hyperkalemia
```

### Collecting Duct (CD)

ADH (vasopressin) determines final urine concentration:

```
ADH absent (diabetes insipidus):
  CD impermeable to water
  Hyperosmotic medullary interstitium cannot draw water from CD
  → Dilute, large-volume urine (> 3 L/day, 50–100 mOsm/kg)

ADH present (normal/concentrated urine):
  ADH → V2 receptor → cAMP → PKA → AQP2 vesicles exocytosed to apical membrane
  AQP2 water channels open → water flows down gradient into hyperosmotic interstitium
  → Concentrated urine (up to 1,200 mOsm/kg)
  AQP3/4 on basolateral membrane (constitutive) → water into peritubular capillaries

UREA RECYCLING:
  CD permeable to urea (UT-A1/3 transporters, ADH-stimulated)
  Urea exits CD into medullary interstitium → diffuses into descending thin limb
  → contributes ~600 mOsm to papillary gradient
  This is why protein malnutrition (low urea) → impaired concentrating ability
```

## Acid-Base Regulation

The kidney is the *precise* long-term acid-base regulator (respiratory is fast but imprecise):

```
Daily H⁺ production: ~70 mEq from protein metabolism
Kidney must excrete exactly this to maintain pH 7.35–7.45

THREE MECHANISMS:
  1. Bicarbonate reabsorption (PCT mainly):
     HCO₃⁻ filtered → can't directly cross apical membrane
     H⁺ secreted (NHE3) → combines with filtered HCO₃⁻ → H₂CO₃ → CO₂ + H₂O (CA)
     → CO₂ enters cell → reconverted to HCO₃⁻ (CA) → basolateral exit
     NET: HCO₃⁻ "reabsorbed" (actually regenerated from CO₂)

  2. Titratable acid (phosphate buffer):
     H₂PO₄⁻ system: HPO₄²⁻ + H⁺ → H₂PO₄⁻ (filtered phosphate buffers secreted H⁺)
     ~ 30 mEq/day excreted this way

  3. Ammonium (NH₄⁺) excretion (most important in acidosis):
     Glutamine (from blood) → PCT → glutaminase → NH₄⁺ + HCO₃⁻
     NH₄⁺ secreted → excreted (urine NH₄⁺ can increase 10× in metabolic acidosis)
     HCO₃⁻ returned to blood → neutralizes acidosis
     This is the adaptable system (takes 3–5 days to fully upregulate)
```

## Electrolyte Regulation

### Sodium and Water (Volume Regulation)

```
Body Na⁺ content determines ECF volume (and therefore blood pressure)
Kidney adjusts Na⁺ excretion via:
  RAAS → aldosterone → ↑ ENaC → ↑ Na⁺ reabsorption (volume expansion)
  ANP/BNP → ↑ GFR + ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption (volume contraction)
  Sympathetic → ↑ renin + ↑ proximal tubule Na⁺ reabsorption

Water follows Na⁺ (or not, depending on ADH):
  Syndrome of inappropriate ADH (SIADH): ADH ↑ → water retained → dilutional hyponatremia
  DI: ADH ↓ or V2 deficient → water not retained → hypernatremia
```

### Potassium

Normal serum K⁺: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L. Small changes → large consequences (cardiac arrhythmia):

```
HYPERKALEMIA (>5.5): peaked T waves → wide QRS → sine wave → VF
  Causes: renal failure, ACE-I/ARBs, K-sparing diuretics, Addison's, severe acidosis
  Treatment: membrane stabilize (Ca gluconate), shift in (insulin + glucose, β2-agonist, bicarb),
             remove (kayexalate, patiromer, dialysis)

HYPOKALEMIA (<3.5): flat/inverted T, U wave, ↑ arrhythmia risk
  Causes: loop/thiazide diuretics, vomiting (alkalosis → renal K loss), diarrhea, Conn syndrome
  Treatment: K replacement, fix underlying cause
```

### Calcium, Magnesium, Phosphate

| Ion | Primary reabsorption site | Hormonal regulation |
|-----|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Ca²⁺ | PCT (60%), thick ascending limb (20%), DCT (15%) | PTH ↑ DCT reabsorption (TRPV5); loop diuretics ↑ Ca²⁺ loss; thiazides ↓ Ca²⁺ loss |
| Mg²⁺ | Thick ascending limb (50–60%) | Mainly paracellular; TRPM6 in DCT; hypomagnesemia → blocks ADH, hypokalemia refractory to K repletion |
| Phosphate | PCT (80%, NaPi cotransporter) | PTH ↑ phosphaturia (↓ NaPi) |

## Clearance and GFR Estimation

```
CLEARANCE: volume of plasma cleared of a substance per unit time

Clearance = (Urine concentration × Urine flow) / Plasma concentration
           = (Uₓ × V̇) / Pₓ

IDEAL GFR MARKER: freely filtered, not reabsorbed, not secreted, not metabolized
  Inulin: gold standard (not practical clinically)
  Creatinine: freely filtered + small amount secreted → slight overestimate of GFR
  Cystatin C: no secretion, more accurate in muscle wasting (creatinine falsely low)

CKD-EPI equation (2021): uses creatinine ± cystatin C + age + sex
  eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m² for ≥3 months = CKD
  eGFR < 15 = kidney failure (GFR, not dialysis criteria)

CREATININE AS MARKER:
  Produced from muscle creatine at constant rate (~20 mg/kg/day)
  Low muscle mass (elderly, frail, cachexia) → low creatinine → falsely high eGFR
```

## RAAS in Detail

```
Low renal perfusion / Low Na⁺ delivery to macula densa / Sympathetic stimulation
  ↓
Juxtaglomerular cells (specialized afferent arteriole smooth muscle)
  → Renin release
  ↓
Angiotensinogen (liver) → Angiotensin I (10 AA) [by renin]
  ↓ [ACE — angiotensin-converting enzyme, in lung capillaries]
Angiotensin II (8 AA)
  ├── AT1R: Vasoconstriction, ↑ aldosterone, ↑ ADH, ↑ proximal Na⁺ reabsorption
  ├── AT1R on efferent arteriole: vasoconstriction → ↑ filtration fraction
  └── Adrenal zona glomerulosa → Aldosterone → Na⁺ retention, K⁺ loss

ACE inhibitors (lisinopril): block ACE → ↓ Ang II → ↓ aldosterone, ↓ efferent constriction
  → ↓ BP; renoprotective (↓ proteinuria) in DM and CKD; side effect: cough (↑ bradykinin)
ARBs (losartan, valsartan): block AT1R → same benefits; no cough
ACEi/ARBs: AVOID in bilateral renal artery stenosis (lose efferent tone → GFR collapses)
```

## Engineering Bridges

**Nephron as filter pipeline with active reclamation**
The nephron is a 50-mm tube that filters aggressively (180 L/day of plasma ultrafiltrate) then reclaims 99.2% of it through staged active transport. The architecture is: bulk unselective filtration at the glomerulus → isosmotic bulk reclamation in the PCT (65%) → gradient-based concentration in the loop → fine-tuned hormonal adjustment in the DCT/CD. This is cheap to filter and expensive to reclaim: the glomerular filtration barrier is simple pressure + size/charge selection; reclamation requires Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase energy at every nephron segment. Total renal ATP expenditure ≈ 10% of resting metabolic rate.

**Countercurrent multiplier as gradient amplifier**
The loop of Henle exploits the hairpin geometry to amplify a small single-effect (NaCl pumped out of thick ascending limb, impermeable to water) into a large osmotic gradient (300 → 1,200 mOsm/kg from cortex to papilla). The key insight: each turn of the hairpin adds only a small increment (the "single effect"), but because the two limbs run counter to each other, the increments multiply along the length. The vasa recta run the same countercurrent geometry to exchange solutes and preserve the gradient rather than washing it out (as a straight capillary bed would). This principle is identical to heat exchanger design: countercurrent flow maximizes the gradient between the two streams along the entire length.

**GFR autoregulation as dual-feedback pressure regulator**
GFR is maintained between MAP 60–180 mmHg by two independent feedback loops:
1. **Myogenic response**: afferent arteriole stretch → smooth muscle contraction (fast, mechanical). Analogous to a pressure-relief valve that closes under excess inlet pressure.
2. **Tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF)**: macula densa cells in the DCT sense NaCl delivery → signal back to afferent arteriole. If GFR is too high, too much NaCl arrives at macula densa → vasoconstriction → ↓ GFR. Two independent loops with different time constants and mechanisms: fast myogenic (milliseconds) + slower TGF (seconds). This is redundant control with orthogonal sensing — more robust than any single feedback path.

**Clearance as throughput measurement**
Clearance = (U_x × V̇) / P_x is the volume of plasma fully cleared of substance X per unit time. This is throughput measurement with a fixed-rate source: if production rate is constant (creatinine ~constant from muscle) and steady state holds, then clearance equals production rate divided by plasma concentration. Creatinine clearance ≈ GFR because creatinine is freely filtered and minimally secreted. The creatinine-GFR relationship is hyperbolic: when GFR halves, creatinine doubles (and keeps rising until production = excretion again). This non-linearity means early CKD is invisible in creatinine; large GFR losses appear as small creatinine changes at the high end.

**RAAS as cascading amplifier with negative feedback**
Renin (protease) cleaves angiotensinogen → Ang I → ACE (in lung) → Ang II. Each step is enzymatic amplification: a small number of renin molecules generates much more Ang I, and ACE converts efficiently. Ang II acts on multiple effectors in parallel: vasoconstriction (fast, direct), aldosterone release (medium, renal Na⁺ retention), ADH release (medium, water retention), proximal tubule Na⁺ reabsorption (direct). Negative feedback closes via BP → less renin. ACE inhibitors and ARBs interrupt the cascade at different points: ACEi blocks conversion (also prevents bradykinin degradation → cough); ARBs block the effector receptor. Both reduce downstream output regardless of upstream activity.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why is loop diuretic so powerful? | Blocks 25% of filtered Na⁺ reabsorption at TAL; also washes out medullary gradient → can't concentrate urine |
| Why do thiazides cause hypokalemia? | Block DCT NCC → more Na⁺ reaches collecting duct → aldosterone-driven K⁺ secretion increases |
| What does aldosterone do? | Retain Na⁺ (and water) + excrete K⁺. Acts at collecting duct via ENaC + Na/K-ATPase |
| Why does ACEi cause cough? | ACE also degrades bradykinin; inhibiting ACE → ↑ bradykinin → prostaglandin-mediated cough |
| eGFR 55 vs 85 — which is CKD? | eGFR 55 (< 60 for ≥3 months = CKD). eGFR 85 is normal. |
| What does SGLT2 inhibitor do to glucose? | Blocks PCT glucose reabsorption → glucosuria → lower blood glucose + BP + weight; cardiorenal protection |

## Common Confusion Points

**GFR vs RPF vs filtration fraction**
- RPF (renal plasma flow) = ~660 mL/min (total blood flow ~1200, hematocrit ~45%)
- GFR ~120 mL/min (filtered fraction)
- Filtration fraction = GFR/RPF = 120/660 = ~20%
- ACEi ↓ efferent tone → ↓ filtration fraction (renoprotective)

**Serum creatinine doubles when GFR halves**
Creatinine is produced at a steady rate. If GFR halves (say 60 → 30 mL/min), creatinine rises until excretion = production again (creatinine doubles). So creatinine 1.0 → 2.0 = lost 50% of kidney function. This is non-linear: initial loss may not show until substantial function gone.

**Hyponatremia ≠ low total body sodium**
Hyponatremia = low serum Na⁺ concentration = too much water relative to Na⁺. The body can have low, normal, or high total body sodium with hyponatremia — assess volume status clinically. SIADH: euvolemic hyponatremia; heart failure: hypervolemic hyponatremia; diarrhea: hypovolemic hyponatremia.

**Metabolic alkalosis and the kidney**
The kidney can correct metabolic alkalosis by excreting HCO₃⁻. But it won't if there's volume depletion — in that case, aldosterone ↑, Na⁺ reabsorption prioritized, and H⁺ secreted in exchange (paradoxical aciduria in alkalosis). Treatment: fix volume depletion → kidney can now excrete the excess HCO₃⁻.
