# Integumentary System

## The Big Picture

```
INTEGUMENTARY SYSTEM: skin + hair + nails + glands
Largest organ: ~2 m², 3–5 kg (~7% body weight)

FUNCTIONS:
  Barrier: physical, chemical, microbial
  Thermoregulation: vasodilation/constriction, sweat, piloerection
  Sensation: touch, pressure, vibration, temperature, pain, proprioception
  Vitamin D synthesis: UVB → D3 precursor
  Immune sentinel: Langerhans cells, innate immune peptides
  Water retention: stratum corneum lipids prevent transepidermal water loss
  Communication: flushing, pallor, sweating, blushing (autonomic visible output)
```

---

## Skin Layers: Three Zones

```
SURFACE
  │
  ├── EPIDERMIS (~0.1–0.2 mm; up to 1.5 mm on palms/soles)
  │   Avascular — nutrients via diffusion from dermis
  │   Stratified squamous epithelium
  │   4–5 layers (strata) from deep to superficial:
  │
  ├── DERMIS (~1–4 mm)
  │   Vascular, sensory receptors, hair follicles, glands
  │   Collagen (type I + III) + elastin framework
  │
  └── HYPODERMIS (subcutaneous fat)
      Adipocytes + loose CT
      Thermal insulation, energy storage, cushioning
      NOT part of skin proper (not "integumentary" sensu stricto, but contiguous)
```

## Epidermis: Five Strata

From basement membrane to surface (keratinocyte maturational sequence):

```
STRATUM BASALE (deepest — proliferative layer):
  Columnar cells on basement membrane (laminin + type IV collagen)
  Keratinocyte stem cells: divide → push daughter cells upward
  Also contains: melanocytes, Merkel cells

STRATUM SPINOSUM ("prickle cell layer"):
  Several cells thick; cells connected by DESMOSOMES (intercellular bridges — "spines")
  Active keratin synthesis
  Langerhans cells interspersed

STRATUM GRANULOSUM:
  2–3 layers; cells contain KERATOHYALIN GRANULES (profilaggrin → filaggrin)
  LAMELLAR BODIES (membrane-coating granules): lipid-rich vesicles
  → exocytose at granular–cornified junction → form lipid bilayers of stratum corneum

STRATUM LUCIDUM (only in thick skin: palms + soles):
  Translucent, no nuclei, densely packed eleidin (intermediate keratin form)

STRATUM CORNEUM (outermost, 15–20 layers):
  Dead, anucleate, fully keratinized "corneocytes"
  Surrounded by lipid bilayers (ceramides + free FA + cholesterol) → "brick and mortar"
  → Barrier function: limits water loss + excludes hydrophilic molecules
  Continuously shed (desquamation): full epidermal turnover ~28–45 days
```

## Cell Types in Epidermis

| Cell | % of epidermis | Origin | Function |
|------|---------------|--------|----------|
| Keratinocytes | ~90% | Ectoderm | Produce keratin; terminal differentiation → barrier |
| Melanocytes | ~5–10% | Neural crest | Synthesize melanin (eumelanin = brown/black; pheomelanin = red/yellow); transfer melanosomes to keratinocytes; UV protection |
| Langerhans cells | ~3–5% | Bone marrow (monocyte lineage) | Antigen-presenting cells (DC type); migrate to lymph nodes on activation; first immune response line |
| Merkel cells | < 1% | Neural crest (or epidermal progenitor) | Mechanoreceptors for light touch; synapse with nerve endings; Merkel cell carcinoma (rare, aggressive) |

### Melanin and UV Protection

```
UV → keratinocytes → secretion of MSH (α-MSH, POMC-derived)
  ↓
Melanocyte: MC1R (MSH receptor) → cAMP → MITF transcription factor
  ↓
Melanin synthesis:
  Tyrosine → DOPA → Dopaquinone → Eumelanin (tyrosinase, copper-dependent)
  Melanosomes packaged → transferred to keratinocytes via "donation" (dendritic extrusion)
  Keratinocytes position melanosomes supranuclearly → nuclear "umbrella" → DNA shielding

FITZPATRICK SCALE: I (always burn, never tan) → VI (never burn, deeply pigmented)
  Reflects MC1R variants (red hair: loss-of-function) and baseline melanin content
  Albinism: tyrosinase absent → no melanin at all (all types)
  Vitiligo: autoimmune destruction of melanocytes → depigmented patches
```

## Dermis

```
PAPILLARY DERMIS (superficial):
  Loose CT, thin collagen fibers, anchoring fibrils
  Dermal papillae: projections that interdigitate with rete ridges of epidermis
  → Increase surface area for exchange + mechanical stability (fingerprints = dermal ridge patterns)
  Contains: Meissner's corpuscles, free nerve endings, capillary loops

RETICULAR DERMIS (deep):
  Dense irregular CT: thick collagen bundles (type I) + elastin
  Mechanical strength and elasticity
  Contains: hair follicles, sweat glands (deep in dermis or hypodermis), sebaceous glands,
            Pacinian corpuscles, nerves, lymphatics

COLLAGEN TYPES:
  Type I: most abundant, high tensile strength (bone + skin + tendon)
  Type III: thinner, more elastic (found in fetal skin, early wound repair)
  Type IV: basement membrane
  Ratio of I:III shifts with wound healing (initially high III → remodels to mostly I)
```

## Skin Appendages

### Hair Follicles

```
HAIR FOLLICLE STRUCTURE:
  Bulb (deep dermis/hypodermis): matrix cells surround dermal papilla
  Matrix cells: rapidly dividing, produce hair shaft + inner root sheath
  Arrector pili muscle: smooth muscle, attaches to outer root sheath + dermis
    → piloerection ("goosebumps") — sympathetic NE → α1-adrenergic → contracts

HAIR GROWTH CYCLE:
  Anagen (active growth): 2–7 years (scalp) — 80-90% of follicles
  Catagen (regression): 2–3 weeks — follicle shrinks, apoptotic phase
  Telogen (rest): 3 months — club hair, 10-20% of follicles
  Exogen: hair shaft shed, new anagen begins

HAIR LOSS:
  Androgenetic alopecia: DHT (5α-reductase converts T → DHT) → miniaturizes follicles
    Treatment: finasteride (5α-reductase inhibitor), minoxidil (vasodilator/K⁺ channel → ?)
  Telogen effluvium: stress, illness, postpartum → synchronize telogen → diffuse shedding
  Alopecia areata: autoimmune (T-cell attack on hair follicle) → patchy circular hair loss
```

### Sebaceous Glands

Holocrine glands (entire cell ruptures to release product): secrete **sebum** (triglycerides + wax esters + squalene + fatty acids)
- Associated with all hair follicles except palms/soles
- Functions: waterproofing, antimicrobial (fatty acids low pH), pheromone context
- Acne: C. acnes bacteria overgrow in blocked follicle + sebum → inflammation
  - Comedones (open = blackhead, closed = whitehead), papules, pustules, nodules
  - Treatment: retinoids (↓ sebum, ↑ cell turnover), benzoyl peroxide (antimicrobial), antibiotics, isotretinoin (severe)

### Sweat Glands

```
ECCRINE SWEAT GLANDS (2–4 million):
  Distribution: entire body surface (most dense: palms, soles, forehead)
  Duct opens directly to skin surface
  Secretion: hypotonic sweat (initially isotonic in coil, Na⁺ reabsorbed as rises)
  Innervation: sympathetic cholinergic (ACh → muscarinic M3)
  Function: primary thermoregulation (evaporative cooling)
  Output: up to 2 L/hr in heavy exercise/heat

APOCRINE SWEAT GLANDS:
  Distribution: axilla, groin, areola, external ear canal
  Open into hair follicle (not skin surface directly)
  Innervation: sympathetic adrenergic + cholinergic
  Onset: puberty (androgen-driven)
  Secretion: viscous, milky — odorless until bacterial breakdown → body odor
  Function: not primarily thermoregulatory; vestigial scent gland

Modified apocrine: mammary glands (milk secretion, pregnancy-activated)
Ceruminous glands (earwax): apocrine-type
```

## Thermoregulation

The skin is the primary thermostat output organ:

```
HEAT DISSIPATION:
  ↑ Core temperature → hypothalamus
    → ↑ Sympathetic cholinergic to eccrine glands → SWEATING
       Evaporation: 580 kcal/L sweat (huge heat loss)
    → ↑ Sympathetic vasomotor: ARTERIOLAR VASODILATION in skin
       ↑ Cutaneous blood flow (from 0.5 → 8 L/min in extreme heat)
       → radiation + convection heat loss from skin surface

HEAT CONSERVATION:
  ↓ Core temperature → hypothalamus
    → ↓ Sweating (sympathetic withdrawal)
    → VASOCONSTRICTION (cutaneous arterioles) → shunt blood from skin → core
    → PILOERECTION (arrector pili, αl-adrenergic) → trap insulating air (vestigial in humans)
    → SHIVERING: involuntary skeletal muscle contractions → ATP → heat (thermogenesis)
    → Non-shivering thermogenesis: brown adipose (β3-adrenergic → UCP1 uncouples ETC → heat)
       Significant in neonates; decreasing in adults

RADIATION BALANCE:
  Skin color affects heat radiation minimally (far infrared, all skin emits ~black body)
  Vasodilation → skin reddens (↑ blood), visible flush
  Autonomic failure (Parkinson's, diabetic neuropathy) → impaired thermoregulation
```

## Skin Barrier Function

```
STRATUM CORNEUM BARRIER:
  "Brick and mortar" model:
    Bricks: corneocytes (dead, keratin-packed)
    Mortar: extracellular lipid lamellae
      Ceramides (50%), free fatty acids (10%), cholesterol (25%)
      Organized in crystalline bilayers — hydrophobic, water-restricting

TRANSEPIDERMAL WATER LOSS (TEWL):
  Barrier intact: ~2–10 g/m²/hr
  Barrier disrupted (eczema, burns, ichthyosis): 10–100× higher → significant dehydration

ANTIMICROBIAL BARRIER:
  Low pH (pH 4.5–5.5): inhibits pathogens, favors commensal flora (Staph epidermidis, Cutibacterium acnes)
  Defensins (β-defensins): cationic peptides → disrupt bacterial membranes
  Cathelicidin (LL-37): mast cell antimicrobial; also immunomodulatory
  IgA: secretory antibodies in sweat and sebum

DRUG PENETRATION:
  Lipophilic drugs penetrate stratum corneum: nitroglycerin, fentanyl, nicotine, hormone patches
  Hydrophilic drugs do not (need formulation enhancers or broken skin)
  Absorption ↑: hydration, heat, broken skin, inflammation, thin skin (face vs palm)
```

## Vitamin D Synthesis

```
UVB radiation (290–315 nm) penetrates to stratum spinosum
  ↓
7-dehydrocholesterol (pro-D3) → photoisomerization → Pre-vitamin D3
  ↓ (thermal isomerization, minutes)
Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3) — enters circulation via DBP (D-binding protein)

LIVER: 25-hydroxylase (CYP2R1) → 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D]
  → Storage form, measured clinically (normal: 20–50 ng/mL)

KIDNEY: 1α-hydroxylase (CYP27B1) → 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D [calcitriol]
  → Active form (stimulated by PTH, low phosphate; inhibited by FGF-23, calcitriol itself)

ACTIONS of calcitriol:
  Gut: ↑ Ca²⁺ + phosphate absorption (calbindin, TRPV6)
  Bone: ↑ bone resorption (PTH permissive); mineralization
  Kidney: ↑ Ca²⁺ + phosphate reabsorption
  Immune: ↓ Th1/Th17, ↑ Treg (immunomodulatory — ↓ autoimmune risk with adequate D)

DEFICIENCY:
  Children: rickets (osteomalacia + growth plate failure → bowing of legs)
  Adults: osteomalacia (↓ bone mineralization → bone pain, fragility fractures)
  Risk: dark skin (↓ UVB → D3), sunscreen, indoor lifestyle, northern latitude, malabsorption
```

## Wound Healing

Four overlapping phases:

```
PHASE 1: HEMOSTASIS (minutes to hours)
  Vessel injury → platelet adhesion (vWF–GPIb) + activation (ADP, TxA₂, collagen)
  Platelet plug + coagulation cascade → fibrin clot
  Clot contains: fibrin, fibronectin, vitronectin → provisional matrix + growth factor depot

PHASE 2: INFLAMMATION (hours to days)
  Neutrophils (first 24–48 hr): debride bacteria + dead tissue; oxidative killing; NETs
  Macrophages (days 2–5): phagocytose debris + apoptotic neutrophils
    M1 macrophages: pro-inflammatory (TNF, IL-1, IL-6, IL-12)
    M2 macrophages: anti-inflammatory + pro-healing (IL-10, TGF-β, VEGF, PDGF)
  Macrophage transition M1→M2: required for healing to progress

PHASE 3: PROLIFERATION (days to weeks)
  FIBROBLAST recruitment (from dermis + bone marrow) → proliferate + migrate
    Synthesize type III collagen (initial), fibronectin, hyaluronan → granulation tissue
    TGF-β → myofibroblast differentiation → wound contraction
  ANGIOGENESIS: VEGF → endothelial sprouting → new capillaries feed granulation tissue
  RE-EPITHELIALIZATION: keratinocytes at wound edges lose hemidesmosomes,
    express MMPs (dissolve fibrin), migrate over wound surface
    Keratinocyte migration: requires fibronectin, type IV collagen substrate
    Contact inhibition: stops when wound surface covered

PHASE 4: REMODELING (weeks to years)
  Type III collagen → replaced by type I collagen (stronger)
  Cross-linking: lysyl oxidase → covalent cross-links → tensile strength
  MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases) vs TIMPs balance determines remodeling rate
  Scar maturation: ↓ vascularity, ↓ fibroblasts, ↑ strength
  Maximum tensile strength: ~80% of original (never 100%)
```

### Abnormal Wound Healing

| Condition | Mechanism | Features |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Hypertrophic scar | Excess collagen, stays within wound borders | Raised, red, improves over months |
| Keloid | Excess collagen, extends BEYOND wound borders | Common in darker skin; recurs after excision |
| Wound dehiscence | Mechanical failure: suture breakdown, infection, malnutrition | Reopening of wound layers |
| Chronic ulcer | Impaired healing: diabetes (neuropathy + ischemia), venous stasis, pressure | Fails to progress through phases; biofilm common |
| Healing factors: ↑ by adequate protein, vitamin C (collagen hydroxylation), zinc; ↓ by diabetes, steroids, hypoxia, malnutrition, infection |

## Burns

```
CLASSIFICATION:
  1st degree (superficial): epidermis only — red, painful, no blisters (sunburn)
  2nd degree superficial: epidermis + superficial dermis — blisters, very painful
  2nd degree deep: epidermis + deep dermis — less painful (nerves damaged), mottled
  3rd degree (full thickness): all skin layers + possibly subcutaneous → painless,
    leathery/waxy — requires grafting (no epithelial cells to regenerate from)
  4th degree: muscle, bone involved

RULE OF NINES (total body surface area, TBSA):
  Head: 9%
  Each arm: 9% (18% total)
  Anterior trunk: 18%
  Posterior trunk: 18%
  Each leg: 18% (36% total)
  Perineum: 1%

PARKLAND FORMULA (fluid resuscitation in first 24 hrs):
  4 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA burned
  Half in first 8 hrs (from time of burn), half over next 16 hrs
  Use Lactated Ringer's (isotonic, least inflammatory)
  Goal: urine output 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr

MAJOR BURN COMPLICATIONS:
  Fluid shifts: massive capillary leak → edema + hypovolemia
  Infection: loss of barrier → major infection risk (P. aeruginosa, Staph aureus)
  Hypermetabolism: 150–200% BMR elevation post-burn
  Inhalation injury: more lethal than surface burn; CO poisoning (carboxyhemoglobin)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Where are new keratinocytes made? | Stratum basale — daughter cells pushed upward, terminally differentiate over ~28–45 days |
| Which skin layer provides water barrier? | Stratum corneum — lipid bilayers between corneocytes (ceramides dominant) |
| Eccrine vs apocrine sweat: which for thermoregulation? | Eccrine (entire body, cholinergic sympathetic). Apocrine is vestigial scent gland. |
| What photon wavelength activates vitamin D synthesis? | UVB (290–315 nm) converts 7-dehydrocholesterol to pre-D3 in stratum spinosum |
| What cell links skin to immune system? | Langerhans cells (epidermal DCs) — sample antigens, migrate to lymph nodes |
| When does a wound reach max tensile strength? | ~6–12 months of remodeling; maximum ~80% of unwounded skin strength |

## Common Confusion Points

**Dermis vs epidermis vascularity**
Epidermis is completely avascular. All nutrition comes from dermal capillary diffusion. This is why epidermal grafts survive without vascular anastomosis (plasma imbibition for first 48 hrs, then ingrowth from below).

**Psoriasis vs eczema (atopic dermatitis): different mechanisms**
- Psoriasis: Th17-mediated (IL-17, IL-23), accelerated keratinocyte proliferation → silvery plaques; epidermal turnover ~4 days (vs 28–45 normal)
- Eczema: Th2-mediated (IL-4, IL-13, IL-5), filaggrin mutation (↓ barrier) + IgE-driven allergy; pruritic, oozing
Different treatment targets: TNF/IL-17/IL-23 biologics for psoriasis; IL-4Rα biologic (dupilumab) for eczema.

**3rd degree burns are painless — not better**
3rd degree burns destroy nerve endings in dermis → no pain at burn site itself. Paradoxically, 2nd degree burns are more painful (nerves intact but exposed/activated). 3rd degree requires surgical debridement and grafting; cannot self-heal.

**Melanocytes do not vary in number by race**
Skin color is determined by melanin production and melanosome transfer rate — NOT melanocyte cell number. All humans have similar melanocyte density. Differences are in melanin chemistry (eumelanin vs pheomelanin), melanosome size, and persistence of melanosomes in keratinocytes.
