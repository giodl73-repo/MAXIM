# Reproductive System

## The Big Picture

```
REPRODUCTIVE SYSTEM: Gametogenesis + Hormonal signaling + Fertilization + Development

MALE                                    FEMALE
Continuous gametogenesis                Fixed gamete pool (follicles from fetal life)
74-day spermatogenesis cycle            28-day cyclic ovulation
HPG axis: GnRH → LH + FSH              HPG axis: cyclic GnRH → LH surge → ovulation
  LH → Leydig (testosterone)             FSH → follicle growth → estrogen
  FSH → Sertoli (spermatogenesis)        LH surge → ovulation → corpus luteum → progesterone
No natural endpoint                     Menopause: follicle depletion, ~age 50-51
```

## Male Anatomy

```
TESTIS:
  Seminiferous tubules — spermatogenesis occurs here
    Sertoli cells (lining): nurse cells, secrete inhibin + ABP (androgen-binding protein)
                            form blood-testis barrier (tight junctions)
    Spermatogenic cells: between and supported by Sertoli cells
    Leydig cells: interstitial, between tubules → testosterone production (LH-stimulated)

  Structure → function:
    Spermatogenic cells divide and differentiate as they move from basement membrane → lumen
    Blood-testis barrier (Sertoli tight junctions): isolates haploid cells from immune system
    (haploid antigens are "self" not trained — could trigger autoimmune attack)

Epididymis: 6-meter coiled tube on posterior testis
  Sperm maturation: ~12 days; acquire motility and fertilizing capability
  Storage: cauda epididymis

Vas deferens → ejaculatory duct (joins seminal vesicle duct) → urethra

ACCESSORY GLANDS:
  Seminal vesicles (60% of ejaculate volume): fructose, prostaglandins, clotting factors
  Prostate gland (20-30%): PSA (prostate-specific antigen), zinc, citric acid, seminalplasmin
  Bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands: pre-ejaculatory mucus, lubrication, pH neutralization
```

## Spermatogenesis

```
TIMELINE: ~74 days from stem cell to spermatozoon + 12 days epididymal maturation

SPERMATOGONIA (diploid, 2n) — mitotic self-renewal and commitment
  Type A spermatogonium: self-renews (stem cell reservoir)
  Type B spermatogonium: commits to differentiation
  ↓ Mitosis
PRIMARY SPERMATOCYTE (diploid, 4n after S-phase)
  Undergoes MEIOSIS I (longest phase — stays in prophase I for YEARS in female; in male: ~3 weeks)
  ↓ Meiosis I
SECONDARY SPERMATOCYTES (haploid n, but still 2 chromatids per chromosome)
  ↓ Meiosis II (rapid, ~8 hours)
SPERMATIDS (haploid, n, 23 single-chromatid chromosomes)
  ↓ SPERMIOGENESIS (differentiation — no division)
SPERMATOZOA:
  Acrosome cap (Golgi-derived): contains hyaluronidase + acrosin for zona pellucida penetration
  Head: condensed nucleus
  Midpiece: mitochondria (ATP for flagellar motility)
  Tail (flagellum): 9+2 axoneme (same as cilia); dynein ATPase drives movement
```

### Male Hormonal Axis

```
Hypothalamus: GnRH (pulsatile, every 90 min)
  ↓
Anterior pituitary:
  LH → Leydig cells → TESTOSTERONE
    Testosterone → protein synthesis, muscle, bone, secondary sex characteristics
    Testosterone → DHT (5α-reductase, primarily in prostate + skin)
    DHT: more potent at androgen receptor; drives prostate growth, male pattern baldness
    5α-reductase inhibitors: finasteride (BPH treatment, hair loss prevention)
  FSH → Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis (+ inhibin B production)

NEGATIVE FEEDBACK:
  Testosterone → inhibits GnRH (hypothalamus) + LH (pituitary)
  Inhibin B → inhibits FSH specifically
  High testosterone (exogenous steroids) → ↓ FSH + LH → testicular atrophy + infertility
```

## Female Anatomy

```
OVARIES: paired, almond-sized, cortex (follicles) + medulla (vessels/nerves)
  Follicles at all stages of development (or atresia)
  Corpus luteum: post-ovulation structure, progesterone factory

FALLOPIAN TUBES (uterine tubes): 10 cm
  Infundibulum (fimbriae): sweeps oocyte into tube after ovulation
  Ampulla: where fertilization normally occurs
  Isthmus → uterotubal junction
  Ciliated epithelium: beats oocyte/embryo toward uterus (~3-5 days transit)

UTERUS:
  Endometrium: inner layer, cyclically shed (menstruation) or receptive to implantation
    Functional layer: shed each cycle
    Basal layer: regenerates functional layer after menstruation
  Myometrium: thick smooth muscle, contractions for delivery
  Cervix: cylindrical lower segment, opening into vagina (os)
    Produces mucus: thin/watery mid-cycle (sperm-permissive), thick luteal phase (sperm barrier)

VAGINA: fibromuscular tube, Lactobacillus-dominant microbiome, pH ~3.5–4.5
```

## Oogenesis: The Fixed Pool Problem

```
Female gametes are NOT continuously produced like sperm:
  ~20 million oogonia in fetal ovary at 5 months gestation
  → 2 million primary oocytes at birth (arrested in PROPHASE I)
  → 400,000 at puberty
  → ~400 actually ovulated in reproductive life
  Remainder undergo ATRESIA (apoptosis) throughout

Arrest in MEIOSIS I PROPHASE:
  Primary oocyte stays here from fetal life until ovulation (up to 50 years!)
  This is why chromosomal nondisjunction (Down syndrome etc.) increases with maternal age:
  cohesins holding chromatids together degrade over decades

LH SURGE → resumes Meiosis I → completes → secondary oocyte + first polar body
FERTILIZATION → resumes Meiosis II → completes → mature ovum + second polar body
(If no fertilization: secondary oocyte disintegrates after ~24 hours)
```

## Menstrual Cycle (28 Days)

```
DAY 1: Menstruation begins (progesterone withdrawal → functional endometrium shed)

FOLLICULAR PHASE (Days 1–14):
  FSH ↑ (withdrawal of progesterone/estrogen from previous cycle → FSH released)
  FSH → cohort of primordial follicles recruited
  One becomes dominant follicle (Graafian follicle) — others undergo atresia
  Granulosa cells (FSH-stimulated): aromatize androgens → ESTROGEN ↑
  Theca cells (LH-stimulated): produce androgens (substrate for aromatase)
  Estrogen effects: endometrial proliferation, ↑ LH receptors on granulosa cells,
                   cervical mucus thinning
  Estrogen feedback: initially NEGATIVE on LH/FSH
  At peak estrogen: POSITIVE feedback (estrogen > threshold) → LH SURGE

OVULATION (Day 14):
  LH surge (36–48 hrs duration) → oocyte maturation + follicle rupture
  Prostaglandin-mediated follicle wall breakdown
  Secondary oocyte (in prophase II arrest) released into peritoneal space
  Swept into fallopian tube fimbriae

LUTEAL PHASE (Days 15–28):
  Ruptured follicle → CORPUS LUTEUM (luteinization of granulosa/theca cells)
  Corpus luteum: PROGESTERONE (dominant) + estrogen
  Progesterone effects:
    Endometrial secretory transformation (↑ glands, glycogen, blood vessels = implantation-ready)
    ↓ LH/FSH (negative feedback)
    ↑ Basal body temperature (~0.2–0.5°C — used in fertility awareness)
    Thickens cervical mucus (sperm barrier)

  IF NO IMPLANTATION:
    hCG not produced → corpus luteum lacks support → degenerates (day 23–24)
    Progesterone + estrogen fall → endometrium shed → menstruation

  IF IMPLANTATION:
    Trophoblast → hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin, LH-like) → rescues corpus luteum
    Corpus luteum maintained for ~10 weeks → progesterone maintained → no menstruation
    Placenta takes over progesterone production at ~10–12 weeks
```

## Fertilization and Early Development

### Fertilization

```
SPERM CAPACITATION (in female tract):
  ~6–8 hours of exposure to uterine/tubal secretions
  Cholesterol removed from sperm membrane → ↑ membrane fluidity → ↑ Ca²⁺ signaling
  Hyperactivated motility (whip-like)

ZONA PELLUCIDA BINDING:
  Sperm binds ZP3 glycoprotein → triggers ACROSOME REACTION
  Acrosomal enzymes (hyaluronidase, acrosin) released → digest zona pellucida

MEMBRANE FUSION:
  Sperm head fuses with oocyte membrane
  → Ca²⁺ wave in oocyte → cortical granule exocytosis → zona hardening
  ZONA REACTION (block to polyspermy):
    Cortical granules release enzymes → modify ZP3 → no more sperm can bind

SECOND MEIOTIC DIVISION:
  Fertilization stimulus → oocyte completes Meiosis II → mature ovum + 2nd polar body
  Pronuclei form (male + female) → syngamy → zygote (46 chromosomes)
```

### Implantation and Placentation

```
CLEAVAGE (days 1–4): rapid mitosis without growth → morula (16–32 cells)
  → BLASTOCYST (day 4–5):
    Inner cell mass (ICM): embryo proper → ectoderm/mesoderm/endoderm
    Trophoblast: outer layer → placenta
    Blastocoel: fluid-filled cavity

IMPLANTATION (days 6–10, posterior uterine wall typically):
  Trophoblast invades endometrium (decidua)
  Syncytiotrophoblast: invasive, erodes maternal vessels
  Cytotrophoblast: mitotically active precursor

  hCG produced immediately → basis of pregnancy tests (immunoassay for β-hCG)
  β-hCG detectable in blood ~8 days after ovulation, urine ~12 days

PLACENTATION:
  Villous structure: fetal capillaries surrounded by cytotrophoblast + syncytiotrophoblast
  Maternal blood fills intervillous space (hemomonochorial placenta in humans)
  Exchange: O₂, nutrients, IgG, drugs, some viruses (CMV, HIV, Rubella, Zika) cross
  Barriers: Large proteins, RBCs (mostly) don't cross
  Placental functions: gas/nutrient exchange + hormone synthesis (hCG, progesterone, estrogens, hPL)
  hPL (human placental lactogen): antagonizes insulin → maternal glucose spared → gestational DM
```

### Embryonic Development

```
GASTRULATION (week 3): ICM → three germ layers
  Ectoderm → CNS (neural tube), skin, sensory organs, lens, enamel
  Mesoderm → muscle, bone, cartilage, connective tissue, kidney, heart, gonads, blood
  Endoderm → GI epithelium, liver, pancreas, thyroid, lungs, bladder

NEURAL TUBE CLOSURE (weeks 3–4):
  Failure → neural tube defects: anencephaly (cranial) or spina bifida (caudal)
  Folic acid (B9) deficiency major risk factor → recommend 400μg/day pre-conception

ORGANOGENESIS (weeks 3–8): most sensitive period for teratogens
  Heart: tubes fuse + loop (week 3–8)
  Limb buds: appear week 5–8
  Palate closes: weeks 5–12

FETAL PERIOD (week 9 → birth): growth and maturation
  Lung surfactant: adequate by ~34 weeks (earlier with steroid acceleration)
  Brain: neuronal migration weeks 12–20; myelination into postnatal life
```

## Parturition (Labor)

```
TRIGGER (multifactorial, not completely understood):
  Fetal cortisol ↑ (lung maturation signal → also signals readiness)
  Estrogen/progesterone ratio shifts (↑ estrogen, ↓ progesterone effect)
  Prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGF₂α) → cervical ripening + uterine contractions
  Stretch receptors in cervix → oxytocin release (Ferguson reflex)

POSITIVE FEEDBACK LOOP:
  Uterine contractions → stretch cervix → hypothalamus → oxytocin ↑
  → more contractions → more stretch → more oxytocin → delivery
  → placenta delivers → stretch stimulus removed → loop breaks

THREE STAGES OF LABOR:
  Stage 1: latent (cervical effacement/dilation 0→10 cm, ~8–12 hrs)
           active (4→10 cm, ~4–6 hrs); transition (8→10 cm)
  Stage 2: fetal expulsion (10 cm to delivery, ~1–2 hrs)
  Stage 3: placental delivery (~5–30 min)

Oxytocin (Pitocin): exogenous used to induce or augment labor
Prostaglandins (misoprostol, dinoprostone): cervical ripening agents
```

## Menopause

```
MECHANISM:
  Follicle pool depleted by atresia (~51 years average, range 40–58)
  No follicles → no estrogen/progesterone/inhibin from ovaries
  → FSH + LH surge (no negative feedback) — confirmed by FSH > 30 mIU/mL
  → Permanently elevated FSH/LH

SYMPTOMS:
  Vasomotor: hot flashes (thermoregulatory set point instability)
  Urogenital atrophy: vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, ↑ UTI risk
  Bone: ↓ estrogen → ↑ bone resorption → osteoporosis (50% of trabecular bone lost in first decade)
  Cardiovascular: estrogen cardioprotective (↑ HDL, ↓ LDL, vasodilatory) — lost at menopause
  Mood/sleep disturbances

HORMONE THERAPY:
  Systemic estrogen (+ progestogen in those with uterus): most effective for symptoms
  Benefits: ↓ hot flashes, ↓ bone loss, ↓ CVD risk (< 60 yrs, < 10 yr since menopause)
  Risks: ↑ breast cancer risk (combined E+P), ↑ VTE, ↑ stroke
  Local estrogen (vaginal): for genitourinary symptoms only, minimal systemic absorption

Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI): < 40 yrs; spontaneous or iatrogenic (chemo/radiation)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why does spermatogenesis need low temperature? | Optimal ~34°C (2–3° below core); undescended testes (cryptorchidism) → infertility + ↑ cancer risk |
| What triggers the LH surge? | High sustained estrogen crosses a threshold → positive feedback → GnRH surge → LH surge |
| What is hCG's function in pregnancy? | Rescue corpus luteum from luteal regression → maintains progesterone → prevents menstruation |
| What does fertilization trigger in the egg? | Ca²⁺ wave → cortical granule exocytosis → zona hardening (block to polyspermy) + Meiosis II completion |
| Why does maternal age increase Down syndrome risk? | Primary oocytes arrested in Meiosis I prophase for decades → cohesin degradation → chromosome 21 nondisjunction risk increases |
| Progesterone: what is it doing throughout pregnancy? | Prevents uterine contractions (blocks oxytocin receptor), maintains decidua, suppresses immune rejection of fetus |

## Common Confusion Points

**Spermatogenesis vs spermiogenesis**
Spermatogenesis = entire process (spermatogonia → spermatozoa). Spermiogenesis = last step only: spermatid differentiation into spermatozoon (no cell division — just morphological remodeling: acrosome, flagellum, nucleus compaction).

**Corpus luteum vs corpus albicans**
Corpus luteum = active post-ovulation progesterone factory (13–14 days if no pregnancy, then degenerates). Corpus albicans = the scar tissue remnant after corpus luteum regression. Multiple corpora albicantia = record of past ovulations.

**hCG vs LH: similar but different**
Both LH and hCG bind the same receptor (LH/hCG receptor on Leydig cells and granulosa/luteal cells). That's why hCG rescues the corpus luteum. hCG has a longer half-life (t½ ~24 hrs vs LH ~20 min) due to extensive glycosylation → more prolonged stimulation.

**When does placenta take over progesterone?**
"Luteo-placental shift": around weeks 8–10. Before that, corpus luteum is essential for progesterone. Surgical removal of corpus luteum before 8 weeks → miscarriage. After 10 weeks, placenta is self-sufficient.

**Estrogen in menopause: not zero**
Adrenal androgens (DHEA, androstenedione) are still produced → converted to estrone (E1) by aromatase in adipose tissue. Obese postmenopausal women have higher estrogen → ↑ endometrial cancer risk. This is why obesity ↑ endometrial cancer.
