## Bone: Structure and Microarchitecture

### Macroscopic Bone Types

| Type | Examples | Structure |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Long | Femur, humerus, tibia | Diaphysis (shaft) + epiphyses (ends) |
| Short | Carpals, tarsals | Roughly cuboidal, mostly cancellous |
| Flat | Skull, sternum, scapula | Two cortical layers sandwiching cancellous |
| Irregular | Vertebrae, facial bones | Complex shapes, cancellous core |
| Sesamoid | Patella | Embedded in tendon, reduces friction |

### Bone Microstructure

```
CORTICAL (COMPACT) BONE — 80% of skeleton mass
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  OSTEON (Haversian system)                          │
│                                                     │
│  Haversian canal (center)                          │
│    └── blood vessel + nerve                        │
│  Concentric lamellae around canal                  │
│  Osteocytes in lacunae (spaces in lamellae)        │
│  Canaliculi connecting osteocytes to each other    │
│    and to Haversian canal (nutrient/waste pathway)  │
│  Volkmann's canals: perpendicular, connect osteons  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

CANCELLOUS (TRABECULAR) BONE — 20% mass, high surface area
Lattice of trabeculae aligned along stress lines (Wolff's law)
Marrow-filled spaces — red marrow (hematopoiesis) or yellow (fat)
```

### Bone Cells

| Cell | Origin | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| Osteoblast | Mesenchymal stem cell | Synthesize osteoid (unmineralized matrix: collagen I + proteoglycans); mineralize with hydroxyapatite |
| Osteocyte | Osteoblast trapped in lacuna | Mechanosensation; regulate remodeling via sclerostin and RANKL signaling |
| Osteoclast | Hematopoietic (monocyte lineage) | Resorb bone: acidify lacuna (carbonic anhydrase → HCl), protease (cathepsin K) dissolves matrix |

### Bone Remodeling and Calcium Homeostasis

Bone is not static — constant remodeling (formation coupled to resorption):

```
Low serum Ca²⁺ (< 8.5 mg/dL)
  ↓
Parathyroid chief cells → PTH ↑
  ├── Kidney: ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption (DCT), ↑ phosphate excretion
  ├── Kidney: ↑ 1α-hydroxylase → calcitriol (active vit D) ↑
  │     ↓
  │     Gut: ↑ Ca²⁺ absorption
  └── Bone: ↑ osteoclast activity → Ca²⁺ released into blood

High serum Ca²⁺ (> 10.2 mg/dL)
  ↓
Thyroid C cells → Calcitonin ↑
  ├── Inhibit osteoclasts
  └── Kidney: ↑ Ca²⁺ excretion
```

Vitamin D status determines intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption efficiency (TRPV6 channel expression in enterocytes). Vitamin D deficiency → rickets (children) / osteomalacia (adults).

## Joints

```
JOINT CLASSIFICATION BY TISSUE TYPE:

FIBROUS joints — no cavity, minimal motion
  Sutures (skull), syndesmoses (distal tibiofibular), gomphoses (teeth)

CARTILAGINOUS joints — no cavity, limited motion
  Synchondroses: hyaline cartilage (epiphyseal plates — fuse at puberty)
  Symphyses: fibrocartilage (pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs)

SYNOVIAL joints — cavity, wide range of motion
  Articular cartilage (hyaline) covers bone ends
  Synovial membrane → synovial fluid (viscous, lubricating, nutrient-supplying)
  Joint capsule (fibrous) reinforced by ligaments
  Subtypes: hinge (elbow), ball-and-socket (hip), pivot (atlantoaxial),
            condyloid (MCP), saddle (CMC thumb), plane (intercarpal)
```

Articular cartilage is **avascular** — nutrition via diffusion through synovial fluid. This is why cartilage heals poorly: no blood supply, no stem cells delivered.

## Sliding Filament Mechanism

The molecular basis of muscle contraction:

```
SARCOMERE — functional unit of skeletal/cardiac muscle
  ├── Z discs: anchor actin thin filaments
  ├── A band: full length of thick filament (myosin)
  ├── I band: only thin filaments (actin + troponin + tropomyosin)
  └── H zone: only thick filaments (shrinks to zero during full contraction)
  M line: center, anchors myosin thick filaments

THIN FILAMENT:  F-actin + tropomyosin + troponin complex
  Troponin I: inhibitory (blocks myosin binding site)
  Troponin C: Ca²⁺ binding
  Troponin T: anchors troponin to tropomyosin

THICK FILAMENT: ~300 myosin II molecules
  Each myosin: two heavy chains (coiled-coil tail + two globular heads)
  Head domain: ATPase site + actin-binding site
```

### Cross-Bridge Cycle

```
1. RIGOR STATE: myosin head bound tightly to actin, no nucleotide

2. ATP BINDING: ATP binds → myosin releases actin (↓ affinity)

3. ATP HYDROLYSIS: ATP → ADP + Pi → myosin head cocks (high-energy
   position), moves ~10 nm relative to actin

4. WEAK BINDING: cocked head binds actin weakly

5. POWER STROKE: Pi release → conformational change → myosin pulls
   actin ~10 nm toward M line → ADP released → back to rigor state

Repeat → thick filament "walks" along thin filament → sarcomere shortens
```

### Ca²⁺ Regulation

```
At rest:
  Tropomyosin covers myosin-binding sites on actin
  Troponin I keeps tropomyosin in blocking position
  No cross-bridge cycling

On activation:
  Ca²⁺ (released from SR via ryanodine receptors) → binds Troponin C
  Conformational change moves tropomyosin → exposes binding sites
  Cross-bridge cycling begins

On relaxation:
  Ca²⁺ pumped back into SR (SERCA pump, ATP-dependent)
  Tropomyosin returns to blocking position
```

## Neuromuscular Junction and Excitation-Contraction Coupling

```
Motor neuron action potential
  ↓
Axon terminal: voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open
  ↓
Ca²⁺ influx → synaptic vesicles fuse → ACh released into synaptic cleft
  ↓
ACh binds nicotinic ACh receptors (nAChR) on motor end plate
  ↓
nAChR: ligand-gated Na⁺/K⁺ channel → Na⁺ influx → end-plate potential (EPP)
  ↓
EPP triggers action potential in sarcolemma
  ↓
Action potential propagates along T-tubules into muscle fiber
  ↓
T-tubule voltage sensor (DHPR) mechanically activates RyR1 (SR)
  ↓
Ca²⁺ floods myoplasm → troponin C binding → cross-bridge cycling
  ↓
ACh in cleft hydrolyzed by acetylcholinesterase → end-plate repolarizes
  ↓
Ca²⁺ returned to SR by SERCA → relaxation
```

## Motor Units and Force Grading

A **motor unit** = one α-motor neuron + all muscle fibers it innervates.

```
FINE CONTROL (eye, hand): small motor units — 1 neuron : 3-6 fibers
GROSS FORCE (quadriceps): large motor units — 1 neuron : 1,000+ fibers
```

Force is graded by two mechanisms:

| Mechanism | How | Range |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| **Recruitment** | Activate more motor units (Henneman's size principle: small → large) | 0 → max force |
| **Rate coding (summation)** | Increase firing frequency: twitch → summation → unfused tetanus → fused tetanus | 1× → ~5× twitch force |

**Henneman's size principle**: Small (Type I, slow-twitch) motor units recruited first, large (Type II, fast-twitch) last. Ensures metabolically efficient gradation.

### Fiber Type Comparison

| Property | Type I (Slow) | Type IIa (Fast Oxidative) | Type IIx (Fast Glycolytic) |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Contraction speed | Slow | Fast | Fastest |
| Fatigue resistance | High | Moderate | Low |
| Mitochondria | Many | Many | Few |
| Myoglobin (color) | High (red) | High (red) | Low (white) |
| Energy source | Oxidative (fat + glucose) | Oxidative + glycolytic | Glycolytic |
| Force production | Low | Moderate | High |
| Examples | Postural muscles, marathon | Sprinting, cycling | Power lifting, sprinting |

## Muscle Energy Systems

Three ATP production systems, used in sequence as intensity increases:

```
DURATION: 0─────2 sec─────30 sec─────2 min──────────hours
SYSTEM:   [ATP-PC]──────[Glycolytic]─────[Oxidative oxidative]

ATP-PC (phosphocreatine system):
  ATP already present + PCr → ATP + Cr
  Very fast, ~2–7 sec supply
  No O₂ needed, no lactate

Glycolytic (anaerobic):
  Glucose → 2 ATP + 2 lactate
  Fast, ~30 sec – 2 min capacity
  Lactate = pyruvate + NADH excess (not metabolic poison per se)

Oxidative:
  Glucose/fat + O₂ → 36-38 ATP (glucose) or ~129 ATP (palmitate)
  Slow onset (VO₂ max limit), hours of capacity
  Limited by O₂ delivery (cardiovascular), not substrate
```

## Pathologies

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Features |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Osteoporosis | ↓ bone density: resorption > formation | T-score < -2.5, DEXA scan, fragility fractures (hip, vertebrae, wrist) |
| Osteoarthritis | Mechanical wear of articular cartilage | Joint space narrowing, osteophytes, no systemic inflammation |
| Rheumatoid arthritis | Autoimmune synovitis (anti-CCP, RF) | Symmetric, systemic, pannus formation, bone erosion |
| Duchenne muscular dystrophy | X-linked, dystrophin gene mutation | Frameshift → absent dystrophin → membrane fragility → necrosis |
| Myasthenia gravis | Autoantibodies against nAChR | Fatigable weakness, ptosis; reversed by AChE inhibitors |
| Rhabdomyolysis | Massive muscle necrosis | Myoglobinuria → renal tubular damage; ↑ CK |

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why does ATP binding release actin? | ATP → myosin head conformational change → ↓ actin affinity (rigor mortis = ATP depletion, can't release) |
| What triggers Ca²⁺ release from SR? | T-tubule AP → DHPR voltage sensor → RyR1 opens (skeletal: mechanical coupling, not Ca²⁺-induced) |
| Why can't cartilage repair well? | Avascular — no blood supply to deliver progenitor cells or nutrients for repair |
| Slow vs fast twitch: which fatigues first? | Fast (Type IIx) — glycolytic only, lactate accumulates quickly |
| PTH vs calcitonin? | PTH ↑ Ca²⁺ (chronic defender); calcitonin ↓ Ca²⁺ (acute, minor role in adults) |
| Why bone remodels along stress lines? | Wolff's law: piezoelectric/mechanosensing by osteocytes → sclerostin suppression → remodeling |

## Common Confusion Points

**Tendons vs ligaments**
- Tendons: connect muscle to bone (dense regular collagen)
- Ligaments: connect bone to bone (similar collagen, slightly more elastic)
Both are poorly vascularized and heal slowly.

**Isotonic vs isometric contraction**
- Isotonic: muscle changes length, tension roughly constant (lifting a weight)
- Isometric: muscle generates tension but doesn't shorten (holding a position against resistance)
- Auxotonic: both change simultaneously (real muscle contractions)

**Twitch vs tetanus**
A twitch is a single AP → single Ca²⁺ transient → brief force spike. Tetanus = high-frequency stimulation: Ca²⁺ doesn't fully return to SR before next release → sustained elevated Ca²⁺ → sustained force. Fused tetanus = smooth maximal contraction.

**Creatine kinase (CK) as muscle damage marker**
CK is cytoplasmic — released when muscle membranes rupture. CK-MM = skeletal muscle; CK-MB = cardiac muscle (troponin I/T are more specific markers for MI).

**Muscle "memory" is neural, not muscular**
"Muscle memory" for skilled movements is stored in motor cortex, cerebellum, and basal ganglia patterns — not in the muscle itself. Muscles atrophy with disuse; the neural patterns remain partially.
