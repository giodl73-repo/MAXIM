# Respiratory System

## The Big Picture

```
RESPIRATORY SYSTEM: Two Jobs
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  1. GAS EXCHANGE: O₂ in / CO₂ out                        │
│  2. pH REGULATION: blow off CO₂ → raise pH               │
│     (fastest pH regulator — renal is slower but more     │
│     precise; respiratory: seconds-minutes)               │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

ANATOMY: Two Zones

CONDUCTING ZONE (anatomical dead space, ~150 mL)
  Nose/mouth → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea (16 rings)
  → Primary bronchi → Secondary (lobar) bronchi
  → Tertiary (segmental) bronchi → Bronchioles
  → Terminal bronchioles
  NO gas exchange — air conditioning: warm, humidify, filter

RESPIRATORY ZONE (gas exchange, ~2.5 L)
  Respiratory bronchioles → Alveolar ducts
  → Alveolar sacs → ALVEOLI (~300 million)
  Surface area: ~70 m² (tennis court)
  Wall thickness: ~0.5 μm (near-zero diffusion resistance)
```

## Alveolar Architecture

```
ALVEOLUS CROSS-SECTION:

  Capillary
  ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
  │  RBC ──────────────────────────  │
  └──┬───────────────────────────┬──┘
     │ Endothelium (0.1–0.2 μm)  │
     │ Fused basement membranes   │
     │ Type I pneumocyte (0.1 μm) │
  ┌──┴───────────────────────────┴──┐
  │     ALVEOLAR AIR SPACE          │
  │  Surfactant layer (DPPC)        │
  └─────────────────────────────────┘

Type I pneumocyte: flat, covers 95% alveolar surface — gas exchange
Type II pneumocyte: cuboidal, 5% surface area — secretes surfactant, stem cell for repair
Alveolar macrophages ("dust cells"): patrol alveolar surface, phagocytose debris
```

### Surfactant

Dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC) + proteins (SP-A, B, C, D):
- Reduces surface tension at air-water interface (LaPlace's law: P = 2T/r)
- Without surfactant, small alveoli (smaller r) would have higher pressure → collapse into large alveoli
- Surfactant effect increases as alveoli shrink (reduces T at small r) → stabilizes alveolar size
- Produced after ~28 weeks gestation; premature birth → respiratory distress syndrome (RDS)
- Treatment: exogenous surfactant (beractant, poractant) + CPAP/ventilation

## Lung Volumes and Capacities

```
VOLUME                          Typical (mL)
  ─────────────────────────────
  Tidal Volume (TV)              500   ← normal breath
  Inspiratory Reserve (IRV)     3,000  ← maximum extra inhalation
  Expiratory Reserve (ERV)      1,200  ← maximum extra exhalation
  Residual Volume (RV)          1,200  ← can't be expired (airways collapse)

CAPACITIES (sum of volumes)
  Vital Capacity (VC) = TV + IRV + ERV = 4,700
  Total Lung Capacity (TLC)    = VC + RV = 5,900
  Functional Residual Capacity (FRC) = ERV + RV = 2,400
  Inspiratory Capacity (IC) = TV + IRV = 3,500

FRC is equilibrium point — where elastic recoil of lung (inward)
balances chest wall spring force (outward). No active muscle needed.
```

### Spirometry Pattern Recognition

```
           Normal    Obstructive    Restrictive
FVC        normal    ↓ or normal    ↓
FEV1       normal    ↓↓             ↓
FEV1/FVC   >70%      <70% (key!)    normal or ↑
TLC        normal    ↑ (air trap)   ↓ (key!)

Obstructive: asthma, COPD, bronchiectasis (can't exhale fast)
Restrictive: pulmonary fibrosis, neuromuscular disease, obesity
```

## Mechanics of Ventilation

```
INSPIRATION (active):
  Diaphragm contracts (descends) + external intercostals
  → thoracic volume ↑
  → pleural pressure drops (more negative, from −5 to −8 cmH₂O)
  → lung expands (transpulmonary pressure ↑)
  → alveolar pressure drops below atmospheric
  → air flows in (ΔP drives flow)

EXPIRATION (passive at rest):
  Diaphragm relaxes
  → elastic recoil of lungs drives air out
  → Active expiration: internal intercostals + abdominal muscles
  → Used during exercise, COPD, forced maneuvers
```

### Compliance

```
Compliance = ΔVolume / ΔPressure

High compliance = lung expands easily for small pressure (emphysema)
Low compliance = stiff lung, needs large pressure to expand (fibrosis, pulmonary edema)

COPD/Emphysema:
  ↑ compliance — destroyed elastin → lung too floppy
  Air trapping on expiration (airways collapse)

Pulmonary fibrosis:
  ↓ compliance — stiff collagen deposits
  Small, stiff lungs, restrictive pattern
```

### Airway Resistance

Resistance highest in medium-sized bronchi (not smallest airways — parallel arrangement provides low total resistance).
- Bronchodilators (β₂ agonists → smooth muscle relaxation) ↓ resistance
- Bronchoconstriction (histamine, cold air, methacholine) ↑ resistance → asthma

## Gas Exchange: Fick's Law

```
Rate of diffusion = (D × A × ΔP) / T

D  = diffusion coefficient (O₂ > CO₂ by 1.3×, but CO₂ ~20× more soluble → similar rates)
A  = surface area (~70 m²)
ΔP = partial pressure gradient
T  = membrane thickness (~0.5 μm)

ALVEOLAR GAS      MIXED VENOUS      ARTERIAL
pO₂  ~105 mmHg ←→ 40 mmHg      → 95–100 mmHg (after equilibration)
pCO₂ ~40 mmHg  ←→ 46 mmHg      → 40 mmHg
```

O₂ and CO₂ fully equilibrate within ~0.25 sec; capillary transit time ~0.75 sec — large reserve.

### V/Q Matching

Ventilation (V) and perfusion (Q) must be matched for efficient exchange:

```
IDEAL: V/Q = 1.0 (every unit of perfusion has matching ventilation)

V/Q = 0 (perfusion without ventilation): SHUNT
  → Collapsed alveolus, consolidation, vascular malformation
  → Deoxygenated blood reaches arterial system unchanged
  → NOT corrected by supplemental O₂ (blood bypasses gas exchange)

V/Q = ∞ (ventilation without perfusion): DEAD SPACE
  → Pulmonary embolism (perfusion cut off)
  → Physiological dead space = anatomical + alveolar dead space
  → Wasted ventilation

NORMAL VARIATION IN UPRIGHT LUNG:
  Base: ↑ perfusion (gravity), V/Q ~0.6 (relatively overperfused)
  Apex: ↑ ventilation proportion, V/Q ~3.3 (relatively underperfused)
```

## Oxygen Transport

Hemoglobin carries 98.5% of O₂; dissolved in plasma only 1.5% at normal pO₂.

```
Hb (deoxy) + O₂ ⇌ HbO₂ (oxy)

Oxygen-Hb dissociation curve (sigmoid shape):
                        100%
Saturation              ─────
(SaO₂)      ─────────/
                     /
                    /  ← steep portion (~40-80 mmHg)
                   /
                  /
    ────────────/
  0%
        0    40    80   100  pO₂ (mmHg)

P50 = pO₂ at 50% saturation, normally ~27 mmHg
```

**Right shift** (↓ O₂ affinity, ↑ O₂ delivery to tissues):
- ↑ Temperature, ↑ CO₂ (Bohr effect), ↓ pH, ↑ 2,3-BPG (altitude, chronic anemia)

**Left shift** (↑ O₂ affinity, ↓ O₂ delivery):
- ↓ Temperature, ↓ CO₂, ↑ pH, ↓ 2,3-BPG, fetal Hb (HbF: γ-chains bind 2,3-BPG less → left shift, loads O₂ from maternal blood), CO poisoning (carboxyhemoglobin)

**Oxygen content**:
CaO₂ = (Hb × 1.34 × SaO₂) + (0.003 × pO₂)
Normal ~20 mL O₂/dL blood. Delivery = CO × CaO₂ = ~1,000 mL O₂/min at rest.

## CO₂ Transport

```
CO₂ in blood (3 forms):
  Dissolved (CO₂aq):  ~7%    (fast, but small capacity)
  Carbamino (HbNH₂ + CO₂ → HbNHCOO⁻): ~23%  (deoxy-Hb binds CO₂ better)
  Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻): ~70%  (dominant form)

INSIDE RBC:
  CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻   (carbonic anhydrase — fast)
  HCO₃⁻ exits RBC via Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger (chloride shift)
  H⁺ buffered by Hb (deoxy-Hb is better buffer)

IN LUNGS (reversed):
  HCO₃⁻ re-enters RBC, CO₂ reformed, diffuses to alveolus, exhaled
```

## Acid-Base Balance

Henderson-Hasselbalch:
```
pH = 6.1 + log([HCO₃⁻] / (0.03 × pCO₂))

Normal: pH 7.40, [HCO₃⁻] 24 mEq/L, pCO₂ 40 mmHg

Think of it as a ratio: pH ∝ [HCO₃⁻] / pCO₂
```

### Four Primary Disorders and Compensation

```
DISORDER          Primary Change   Compensation      Expected Compensation
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Metabolic         ↓ HCO₃⁻         ↓ pCO₂           pCO₂ = 1.5×[HCO₃⁻] + 8
  Acidosis                         (hyperventilate)  (Winter's formula)

Metabolic         ↑ HCO₃⁻         ↑ pCO₂            pCO₂ ≈ 0.7×[HCO₃⁻] + 21
  Alkalosis                        (hypoventilate)

Respiratory       ↑ pCO₂           ↑ HCO₃⁻           Acute: +1 per 10 ↑ pCO₂
  Acidosis                         (renal retention)  Chronic: +3.5 per 10 ↑ pCO₂

Respiratory       ↓ pCO₂           ↓ HCO₃⁻            Acute: −2 per 10 ↓ pCO₂
  Alkalosis                        (renal excretion)   Chronic: −5 per 10 ↓ pCO₂
```

If compensation overshoots → mixed disorder (two simultaneous primary disorders).

## Respiratory Control

### Central Pattern Generator

```
BRAINSTEM RESPIRATORY CENTERS:
  Pre-Bötzinger complex (ventral medulla): rhythmic breathing generator
  Dorsal respiratory group (DRG): integrates sensory input → drives inspiration
  Ventral respiratory group (VRG): active expiration + upper airway muscles
  Pneumotaxic center (pons): limits inspiration depth, controls rate
  Apneustic center (pons): promotes inspiration (inhibited by pneumotaxic)
```

### Chemoreceptors

```
CENTRAL CHEMORECEPTORS (medulla, near respiratory centers)
  Sense: CO₂ → reacts with CSF water → H⁺ (CO₂ crosses BBB, H⁺ does not)
  Sensitive primarily to CO₂/pH
  Major driver of normal ventilation

PERIPHERAL CHEMORECEPTORS (carotid bodies + aortic bodies)
  Sense: ↓ pO₂ (< 60 mmHg primarily), ↑ pCO₂, ↓ pH
  Carotid bodies dominant (CN IX → NTS)
  O₂ drive "hypoxic drive" — kicks in below 60 mmHg
  COPD patients: chronic ↑ CO₂ → central receptors desensitized → O₂ drives breathing
    Giving high-flow O₂ → removes O₂ drive → may suppress ventilation (rarely acutely dangerous)
```

## Pulmonary Circulation

| Parameter | Pulmonary | Systemic | Implication |
|-----------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Systolic pressure | ~25 mmHg | ~120 mmHg | RV wall much thinner than LV |
| Diastolic pressure | ~10 mmHg | ~80 mmHg | — |
| Mean pressure | ~15 mmHg | ~93 mmHg | 5–6× pressure difference for same CO |
| Vascular resistance | ~1 WU | ~15–20 WU | Pulmonary is low-resistance high-flow |
| Response to hypoxia | **Vasoconstriction** (HPV) | Vasodilation | Opposite response — redirects blood to ventilated alveoli |
| Wall thickness | Thin, compliant | Thick, muscular | Capillaries can accommodate entire CO at low pressure |
| Fluid dynamics | Starling forces favor filtration | Net reabsorption at venular end | Pulmonary edema occurs at lower pressure rises than systemic edema |

**Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV)**: Regional low alveolar pO₂ → local arteriolar constriction → diverts blood to better-ventilated segments. This is the V/Q matching optimization mechanism — the vasculature routes flow toward high-ventilation regions rather than low ones. Mechanistically, HPV is mediated by inhibition of voltage-gated K⁺ channels in pulmonary arterial smooth muscle → membrane depolarization → Ca²⁺ influx → contraction.

**Global hypoxia breaks HPV into a problem**: At altitude or in COPD with diffuse V/Q mismatch, HPV acts everywhere simultaneously → global pulmonary vasoconstriction → pulmonary hypertension → RV pressure overload → RV hypertrophy → eventual RV failure (cor pulmonale). The same local optimization mechanism becomes destructive when applied globally.

## Engineering Bridges

**V/Q matching as load balancing**
The lung runs ~500 million alveolar units in parallel. Each unit needs matched ventilation (V) and perfusion (Q) for efficient gas exchange. V/Q = 0 (shunt) is a zero-throughput unit that still consumes perfusion — equivalent to a server that receives requests but is completely unresponsive; traffic bypasses it without gaining anything. V/Q = ∞ (dead space) is a ventilated-but-unperfused unit — equivalent to a server that gets no traffic but burns CPU idling. Optimal load balancing minimizes both failure modes: reroute perfusion away from collapsed alveoli (HPV), reroute ventilation away from unperfused regions (via CO₂ mediated bronchodilation). The lung's auto-balancing is imperfect, which is why a healthy young person has an A-a gradient of 5–10 mmHg.

**Henderson-Hasselbalch as a ratio controller**
pH = 6.1 + log([HCO₃⁻] / 0.03·pCO₂). The fraction inside the log is the controlled ratio; the respiratory system adjusts pCO₂ in seconds (the fast actuator); the renal system adjusts [HCO₃⁻] over days (the slow but high-precision actuator). Think of it as proportional control (respiratory) paired with integral control (renal) — the integral term eliminates steady-state error but is slow to respond. Compensation in mixed acid-base disorders is the system trying to restore the ratio without correcting the primary variable.

**Surfactant as interfacial stabilization**
The LaPlace equation (P = 2T/r) predicts that smaller alveoli should collapse into larger ones (higher internal pressure). Surfactant (DPPC) reduces surface tension as alveoli shrink — the opposite of most surfactants. This is a nonlinear stabilizing spring: the smaller the alveolus, the more it resists further collapse. DPPC operates at a lipid phase transition: at low surface area it forms a condensed film with near-zero tension; at high surface area it expands and tension rises, resisting over-inflation. Without this property, the lung would be mechanically unstable (all air into one big bubble).

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What's the fastest blood pH regulator? | Respiratory (pCO₂ changes in seconds). Renal is slower (~days) but more precise. |
| Why does COPD cause air trapping? | Destroyed elastin → ↑ compliance, airways collapse on expiration before lungs empty |
| Why is shunt not corrected by O₂? | Blood bypasses gas exchange entirely — supplemental O₂ can't reach it |
| Why does CO poisoning cause ↑ SaO₂ on pulse ox? | Pulse ox can't distinguish COHb from OxyHb — falsely high SpO₂. Need co-oximetry. |
| What's the Henderson-Hasselbalch intuition? | pH goes up if HCO₃⁻ ↑ or pCO₂ ↓. Think of it as a seesaw ratio. |
| Fetal Hb vs adult Hb: which holds O₂ tighter? | HbF — left-shifted curve, loads O₂ from maternal HbA at the placenta |

## Common Confusion Points

**Alveolar vs arterial pO₂**
Alveolar pO₂ (PAO₂) ≈ 100 mmHg in healthy lungs. Arterial pO₂ (PaO₂) ≈ 95 mmHg. The 5 mmHg gradient = A-a gradient, due to V/Q mismatch and anatomical shunt. An elevated A-a gradient indicates a parenchymal/vascular problem (not hypoventilation alone).

**Respiratory compensation is never complete**
Metabolic acidosis → hyperventilation lowers pCO₂ → raises pH, but pH never fully normalizes from respiratory compensation alone. If pH is completely normal, there's either a mixed disorder or the diagnosis is wrong.

**Oxygen saturation vs oxygen content**
SpO₂ 98% looks fine. But if Hb = 4 g/dL (severe anemia), O₂ content = (4 × 1.34 × 0.98) ≈ 5.2 mL/dL — less than 1/4 normal despite normal saturation. Saturation tells you how well what Hb you have is loaded, not how much O₂ is in the blood.

**Dead space is additive**
Total dead space = anatomical (airways, fixed ~150 mL) + alveolar (ventilated but not perfused, variable). Pulmonary embolism increases alveolar dead space → pCO₂ falls in exhaled gas (diluted by dead space air). Can use PaCO₂ − PetCO₂ gradient to detect.
