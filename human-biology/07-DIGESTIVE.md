# Digestive System

## The Big Picture

```
DIGESTIVE SYSTEM: 9-meter tube from mouth to anus
Four functions: Ingestion → Digestion → Absorption → Excretion

ANATOMY OVERVIEW:

MOUTH          Mechanical digestion (chewing) + salivary amylase + lipase
    ↓
PHARYNX        Swallowing (deglutition reflex — CN IX/X)
    ↓
ESOPHAGUS      Peristalsis only; LES (lower esophageal sphincter) prevents reflux
    ↓
STOMACH        Acid + pepsinogen; mechanical churning; gastric emptying
    ↓
DUODENUM       Bile (liver) + pancreatic enzymes; most digestion
    ↓
JEJUNUM        Most absorption (~90%)
    ↓
ILEUM          Vitamin B12 + bile acid reabsorption; terminal valve → colon
    ↓
LARGE INTESTINE Water + electrolyte absorption; microbiome fermentation; defecation
    ↓
RECTUM + ANUS  Fecal storage + controlled evacuation
```

---

## GI Wall: Four Layers

All GI segments share a basic architecture:

```
LUMEN
  │
  ├── MUCOSA (innermost — function layer)
  │   ├── Epithelium (varies by segment — columnar mostly)
  │   ├── Lamina propria (loose CT, lymphatics, vessels, immune cells)
  │   └── Muscularis mucosae (thin smooth muscle — can ruffle mucosa)
  │
  ├── SUBMUCOSA (vessels + nerves)
  │   Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus — ENS, secretomotor control
  │
  ├── MUSCULARIS EXTERNA (movement)
  │   Inner circular layer
  │   Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus — ENS, motility control (between layers)
  │   Outer longitudinal layer
  │
  └── SEROSA / ADVENTITIA (outer coat)
      Serosa: peritoneal organs (mesothelium)
      Adventitia: retroperitoneal organs (connective tissue)
```

Epithelium is specialized per segment:
- Esophagus: stratified squamous (for abrasion)
- Stomach: simple columnar with mucous neck cells
- Small intestine: simple columnar with villi + microvilli (brush border) → 600× surface area
- Large intestine: simple columnar with crypts, no villi, many goblet cells

## Stomach

### Cell Types and Secretions

```
GASTRIC GLANDS (in body/fundus):
  Parietal cells → HCl (gastric acid, pH ~1.5–2) + Intrinsic factor (B12 binding)
  Chief cells → Pepsinogen (activated by HCl → pepsin, protease)
  Mucous neck cells → mucus + bicarbonate (barrier protecting gastric wall)
  ECL cells (enterochromaffin-like) → Histamine (potent parietal cell stimulus)

ANTRUM:
  G cells → Gastrin → ↑ HCl, ↑ pepsinogen, ↑ motility, ↑ ECL histamine

HCl SECRETION MECHANISM:
  H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump) on canalicular membrane of parietal cell
  Pumps H⁺ into lumen in exchange for K⁺ (requires K⁺ channel for K⁺ recycling)

  Stimulators: Acetylcholine (M3) + Gastrin (CCK-B) + Histamine (H2) → all converge
  Inhibitors: Somatostatin (δ cells), Secretin (acid in duodenum), PGE₂

PROTON PUMP INHIBITORS (PPIs): irreversibly inhibit H⁺/K⁺-ATPase
  → Omeprazole, esomeprazole; gold standard for GERD, PUD, H. pylori
  H2 blockers (famotidine): block H2 receptor on parietal cell, less potent
```

### Gastric Phases

| Phase | Trigger | Mechanism | Acid stimulated |
|-------|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Cephalic | Sight/smell/thought of food | Vagus nerve (ACh → G cells + direct parietal) | ~30% |
| Gastric | Food in stomach (distension + protein) | Local vagal reflexes + gastrin (G cells → parietal) | ~60% |
| Intestinal | Chyme enters duodenum | Initially pro-acid; then secretin + CCK + GIP inhibit | ~10% |

## Small Intestine: The Absorption Machine

### Anatomy for Absorption

```
Surface area amplification (~600× relative to bare tube):
  Plicae circulares (circular folds) — entire wall fold → 3× area
  Villi — finger-like projections → 10× area
  Microvilli (brush border) — apical membrane projections → 20× area
  Total: ~250 m² (doubles a standard tennis court)
```

### Duodenum: Digestion Hub

Receives:
1. **Bile** (from bile duct): emulsification of fats
2. **Pancreatic juice** (from pancreatic duct): proteases, lipases, amylase, nucleases
3. **Pancreatic bicarbonate**: neutralizes gastric acid (duodenal pH rises from ~2 → ~6)

Brunner's glands (duodenal submucosa): secrete bicarbonate-rich mucus as additional protection against acid.

## Pancreas (Exocrine)

```
ACINAR CELLS: synthesize and secrete digestive enzymes
  Released as ZYMOGENS (inactive precursors) — prevents autodigestion:
    Trypsinogen → Trypsin (activated by enteropeptidase on duodenal brush border)
    Chymotrypsinogen → Chymotrypsin (by trypsin)
    Proelastase → Elastase (by trypsin)
    Prolipase → Lipase (by colipase + trypsin)
    Procarboxypeptidase → Carboxypeptidase (by trypsin)
    Prophospholipase → Phospholipase (by trypsin)
  Trypsin is the master activator: once activated, it activates everything else
  Guard: pancreatic trypsin inhibitor (neutralizes accidental premature trypsin)

DUCTAL CELLS: secrete bicarbonate-rich fluid
  CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator) Cl⁻ channel → Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger
  Secretin → ↑ HCO₃⁻ secretion
  Volume ~1.5 L/day of pancreatic juice

Acute pancreatitis: premature trypsin activation within pancreas → autodigestion
  Causes: gallstones (ampullary obstruction), alcohol (direct toxic + spasm)
  Marker: serum lipase (more specific than amylase)
```

## Liver

The liver does everything. Key functions:

### Metabolic Hub

```
GLUCOSE METABOLISM:
  Fed: portal blood glucose → hepatic GLUT2 → glycogen synthesis
       Excess glucose → de novo lipogenesis → VLDL export
  Fasted: glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose-6-phosphate → glucose)
          gluconeogenesis (AA, lactate, glycerol → glucose)

LIPID METABOLISM:
  Fatty acid β-oxidation (fasted/ketosis) → acetyl-CoA → ketone bodies
  VLDL synthesis + export → TG delivery to adipose
  HDL metabolism, lipoprotein remodeling

PROTEIN METABOLISM:
  Synthesizes most plasma proteins:
    Albumin (dominant oncotic pressure, carrier)
    Clotting factors (I, II, V, VII, IX, X, XI — all but VIII and vWF)
    Complement proteins
    Acute phase reactants (CRP, fibrinogen, ferritin, haptoglobin)
  Urea cycle: NH₃ (from amino acid catabolism) → urea → excreted in urine
```

### Bile Synthesis and Function

```
BILE COMPOSITION (500–800 mL/day):
  Bile salts (50%): primary (cholic, chenodeoxycholic) made from cholesterol
  Lecithin (phospholipid)
  Cholesterol
  Bilirubin (waste product)
  Bicarbonate + water + electrolytes

BILIRUBIN METABOLISM:
  Heme (from senescent RBC) → biliverdin → unconjugated bilirubin (insoluble)
  Liver: UCB + 2 glucuronate (UGT1A1) → conjugated bilirubin (water-soluble)
  → secreted into bile → intestine → urobilinogen → stercobilin (brown stool)
  Some urobilinogen reabsorbed → urobilin (yellow urine)

  Jaundice types:
    Pre-hepatic: ↑ UCB (hemolysis — too much bilirubin for liver to conjugate)
    Hepatic: ↑ UCB + CB (hepatocellular — Gilbert's, hepatitis)
    Post-hepatic: ↑ CB (obstruction — gallstones, pancreatic cancer → bile backs up)

ENTEROHEPATIC CIRCULATION:
  95% of bile salts reabsorbed in terminal ileum (active transport)
  → Portal blood → liver → re-secreted into bile
  5% lost in feces (fecal excretion cholesterol lowering mechanism)
  Ileal disease (Crohn's) or resection → bile salt malabsorption → steatorrhea
```

## Nutrient Absorption

### Carbohydrates

```
Starch → (salivary/pancreatic amylase) → maltose/dextrins
         (brush border enzymes: maltase, sucrase, lactase) → monosaccharides
Absorption:
  Glucose + galactose: SGLT1 (Na⁺-coupled, secondary active transport) → basolateral GLUT2
  Fructose: GLUT5 (facilitated, apical) → basolateral GLUT2
Lactase deficiency: undigested lactose → bacterial fermentation → gas + osmotic diarrhea
```

### Proteins

```
Pepsin (stomach) → polypeptides
Pancreatic proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidase) → oligopeptides + AA
Brush border peptidases (aminopeptidase, dipeptidyl peptidase IV) → di/tripeptides + AA
Absorption:
  AA: Na⁺-dependent cotransporters (multiple specific transporters by AA class)
  Di/tripeptides: PepT1 (H⁺-dependent cotransporter) → hydrolyzed intracellularly
```

### Fats

```
Stomach: gastric lipase → some FFA from short-chain TG
Duodenum:
  Bile salts emulsify TG → smaller droplets → ↑ surface area for lipase
  Pancreatic lipase + colipase → sn-2 monoglyceride + 2 FFA

MICELLAR SOLUBILIZATION:
  FFA + monoglycerides + fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) → enter bile salt micelles
  Micelles diffuse to brush border → FFA/MG diffuse into enterocyte
  (bile salts stay in lumen → recycled in terminal ileum)

CHYLOMICRON ASSEMBLY (in enterocyte ER):
  TG + cholesterol + phospholipid + ApoB-48 → chylomicron
  → secreted from basolateral membrane → enters LACTEALS (lymphatic capillaries in villi)
  → thoracic duct → subclavian vein → systemic circulation
  (bypass liver portal system — fats go directly to circulation)

Vitamin B12:
  Requires intrinsic factor (parietal cell) → IF-B12 complex → ileal receptor (cubilin)
  Pernicious anemia: autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → no IF → B12 deficiency
```

## GI Hormones

| Hormone | Source | Trigger | Key actions |
|---------|--------|---------|-------------|
| Gastrin | G cells (antrum) | Protein, distension, vagus | ↑ HCl, ↑ pepsinogen, ↑ ECL histamine, ↑ motility, trophic |
| Secretin | S cells (duodenum) | Acid in duodenum | ↑ Pancreatic HCO₃⁻, ↓ gastric acid, ↑ bile flow |
| CCK (cholecystokinin) | I cells (duodenum) | Fat + protein in duodenum | ↑ Pancreatic enzymes, gallbladder contraction, ↓ gastric emptying, satiety |
| GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide) | K cells (duodenum) | Glucose + fat | ↑ Insulin (incretin), ↓ gastric acid |
| GLP-1 | L cells (ileum) | Nutrients | ↑ Insulin, ↓ glucagon, ↓ gastric emptying, satiety |
| Motilin | M cells (duodenum) | Fasting (interdigestive) | Migrating motor complex (housekeeping waves between meals) |
| VIP | Myenteric neurons | Neural | Relax gut smooth muscle, ↑ secretion |
| Somatostatin | D cells (throughout) | Acid, fat, protein | Global inhibitor: ↓ most GI hormones and secretions |

## Motility

```
PERISTALSIS: Coordinated ring contraction behind bolus + relaxation ahead
  Intrinsic: ENS (myenteric plexus) — works without CNS ("brain in the gut")
  Modified by: vagus (pro), sympathetic (inhibit), hormones

MIGRATING MOTOR COMPLEX (MMC):
  Between meals: sweeping contractions (motilin → Phase III)
  Cleans small intestine of bacteria and debris
  "Housekeeping waves" — interrupted by eating (CCK suppresses motilin)

GASTRIC EMPTYING:
  Rate controlled by antroduodenal coordination
  Slowed by: fat (CCK), acid, hyperosmolarity in duodenum
  Solid food: 2–4 hours; liquids: 20–40 min
  Gastroparesis: diabetic neuropathy or idiopathic → slow emptying → bloating, nausea

DEFECATION REFLEX:
  Rectal distension → internal anal sphincter relaxes (involuntary, ENS)
  External anal sphincter (voluntary, pudendal nerve) maintains continence
  Defecation: voluntary relaxation of EAS + increased intraabdominal pressure
```

## Enteric Nervous System

"The second brain" — operates independently of CNS:

```
~500 million neurons (more than spinal cord)
Two plexuses:
  Myenteric (Auerbach's): between longitudinal and circular muscle → motility
  Submucosal (Meissner's): inner submucosa → secretion + blood flow

Key neurotransmitter: Serotonin (5-HT from enterochromaffin cells)
  ~95% of body's serotonin is in the gut
  5-HT4 agonists: ↑ motility (prucalopride — IBS/constipation treatment)
  5-HT3 antagonists: ↓ visceral sensation + secretion (ondansetron — nausea; alosetron — IBS-D)

Communicates bidirectionally with CNS via vagus nerve (gut-brain axis)
Gut microbiome signals to ENS + brain via metabolites (SCFAs, tryptophan → 5-HT)
```

## Gut Microbiome

~10¹³ bacteria (mostly colon); now thought roughly equal to human cell count:

```
DOMINANT PHYLA (colon):
  Firmicutes (Lactobacillus, Clostridium, Ruminococcus) — ~65%
  Bacteroidetes (Bacteroides, Prevotella) — ~25%
  Actinobacteria (Bifidobacterium) — ~5%
  Proteobacteria (E. coli) — ~1%

KEY FUNCTIONS:
  Ferment indigestible fiber → Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs): butyrate, propionate, acetate
    Butyrate: primary colonocyte fuel, anti-inflammatory, promotes tight junctions
  Synthesize vitamin K2 + some B vitamins
  Modify bile acids (primary → secondary: chenodeoxycholic → lithocholic, etc.)
  Colonization resistance: prevents pathogen overgrowth by competing for niche
  Immune training: essential for normal immune development (germ-free mice → dysregulated immunity)

DYSBIOSIS CONSEQUENCES:
  C. difficile colitis: antibiotic disruption → C. diff overgrowth → toxin A/B → pseudomembranous colitis
  Treatment: vancomycin or fidaxomicin; recurrent: fecal microbiota transplant (FMT)
  IBD, IBS, metabolic syndrome, mood disorders — microbiome composition altered (causation unclear)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Where is most absorption? | Jejunum (most carbs, proteins, fats, vitamins). Ileum specialized: B12, bile acids, fat-soluble vitamins |
| Why are fats absorbed into lymph, not portal blood? | Chylomicrons are too large for capillaries; absorbed into lacteals → thoracic duct → subclavian. Bypasses liver first pass. |
| What activates all pancreatic zymogens? | Trypsin is the master activator; enteropeptidase (duodenal brush border) activates the first trypsinogen |
| What stimulates gallbladder contraction? | CCK (from I cells when fat + protein enters duodenum) |
| Parietal cell: what does it make? | HCl (via H⁺/K⁺-ATPase proton pump) + Intrinsic factor (B12 binding) |
| Why is terminal ileum removal problematic? | Loses: B12 absorption (intrinsic factor receptor cubilin), bile acid reabsorption (enterohepatic circulation), + may overflow to colon → diarrhea |

## Common Confusion Points

**Amylase vs lipase in pancreatitis**
Both elevated, but lipase is more specific and remains elevated longer. Serum amylase rises in many conditions (salivary glands, bowel obstruction). Lipase > 3× upper normal = diagnostic standard.

**Conjugated vs unconjugated bilirubin in jaundice**
Unconjugated: lipid-soluble, not water-soluble, cannot be renally excreted, pre-hepatic or hepatic uptake/conjugation failure.
Conjugated: water-soluble, excreted in bile, backs up in obstruction → enters blood → excreted in urine (dark urine) + less reaches gut → pale stools.

**GERD vs peptic ulcer vs gastritis: all different**
- GERD: acid reflux into esophagus (LES incompetence)
- Peptic ulcer: mucosal break in stomach or duodenum (H. pylori 70%, NSAIDs 20%)
- Gastritis: mucosal inflammation without ulceration
H. pylori: gram-negative curved rod, urease (splits urea → NH₃, raises local pH to survive), produces virulence factors (CagA). Diagnosis: urea breath test, stool antigen, or biopsy.

**Incretin effect: why oral glucose > IV glucose in insulin response**
Same blood glucose rise → oral route causes 2× more insulin response. GLP-1 + GIP from gut → "incretin effect" → potentiate β-cell insulin secretion. Basis for GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) and DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin).
