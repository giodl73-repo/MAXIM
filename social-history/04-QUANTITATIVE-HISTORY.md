# Quantitative and Cliometric History

## The Big Picture

Quantitative history applies statistical and econometric methods to historical questions.
"Cliometrics" (from Clio, the muse of history + metrics) uses formal economic models.
It flourished from the late 1950s through the 1980s, produced Nobel Prize-winning work,
generated major controversies, and remains an active field now transformed by digital tools.

## Origins and Development

### The Cliometrics Revolution

```
  TRADITIONAL ECONOMIC HISTORY (pre-1950s):
  Narrative-descriptive. Based on archival documents, business records.
  Questions: What happened? Who did what?
  Limited quantification; qualitative judgment.

  THE CLIOMETRIC REVOLUTION (1957 onwards):
  Alfred Conrad and John Meyer, "The Economics of Slavery in the
  Antebellum South" (1958): first major cliometric paper.
  Applied formal economic theory and statistics to ask:
  Was slavery economically rational?
  Answer: yes, it was profitable for slaveholders.
  (This was NOT a moral argument; it was a historical-economic one.)

  CLIOMETRICS SOCIETY (1960s):
  Annual meetings (NBER/DAE: Development of the American Economy).
  Dominated by a generation trained in economics, not history.
  Methods: regression, counterfactual analysis, formal models.

  KEY JOURNAL:
  Journal of Economic History (founded 1941, but transformed by cliometrics).
  Explorations in Economic History.
```

### The French Tradition: Serial History

```
  ANNALES "SERIAL HISTORY" (histoire sérielle):
  Different from American cliometrics but also quantitative.
  Focus: long time series of socioeconomic data.
  Prices, wages, harvests, births, deaths, marriages.

  PIERRE CHAUNU: Seville and the Atlantic (1955-60).
  Ship tonnage data from the Casa de Contratación: quantitative history
  of the Atlantic trade system.

  LABROUSSE: price and wage series from 18th-century France.
  Economic fluctuation and social crisis.
  The pre-revolutionary crisis: wages fell, bread prices rose.

  DIFFERENCE FROM AMERICAN:
  French: longer time series, more structural, less formal modeling.
  American: more formal economics, explicit models, counterfactuals.
```

## Robert Fogel: Railways and Slavery

### Railroads and American Economic Growth (1964)

```
  FOGEL'S FIRST MAJOR WORK:
  Did railroads cause American economic growth?
  Traditional assumption: yes, obviously.

  COUNTERFACTUAL METHOD:
  "What would American GNP have been in 1890 without railroads?"
  Build a hypothetical: an America with only canals and roads.
  Estimate the "social savings" of railroads over this alternative.

  RESULT:
  Railroads raised 1890 GNP by about 5%.
  Not negligible, but not the indispensable engine of growth.
  Canals and rivers would have substituted significantly.

  SIGNIFICANCE:
  Demonstrated counterfactual method in economic history.
  Showed that widely accepted "common sense" historical claims
  can be tested rigorously — and can fail.

  CRITICISM:
  "What if" questions are inherently speculative.
  The counterfactual world is constructed from assumptions.
  Different assumptions, different results.
  Is 5% small? What counts as "indispensable"?
```

### Time on the Cross (1974)

```
  TIME ON THE CROSS: THE ECONOMICS OF AMERICAN NEGRO SLAVERY
  (Fogel and Engerman, 1974):
  The most controversial work in American historical scholarship.

  THE CLAIMS:
  1. Slavery was highly efficient: slave agriculture was 35% more efficient
     than free family farming in the North (in terms of output per worker).
  2. Slaves were not lazy or incompetent; they worked hard and efficiently.
  3. Slavery was profitable: the rate of return was about 10%.
  4. Material conditions of slaves were comparable to Northern industrial workers
     (in nutrition and calories — NOT in other respects).
  5. Slave family structure was stable; slaveholders had economic incentives
     to preserve slave families.

  THE RESPONSES:
  Immediate controversy. Volume 2 (responses) published 1979.
  Herbert Gutman (Slavery and the Numbers Game): methodological critique.
  Statistics were misread; conclusions overstated.
  Critics: the efficiency claim ignored the violence required to achieve it.
  Brutality is the mechanism of efficiency.

  THE LARGER DEBATE:
  Can economic methods capture the morality of slavery?
  High efficiency and high brutality are not mutually exclusive.
  Fogel was NOT arguing slavery was good or acceptable.
  His later work (Without Consent or Contract, 1989) emphasized
  the moral case against slavery.
```

## Douglass North: Institutions and Economic History

```
  DOUGLASS NORTH (Nobel Prize with Fogel, 1993):
  The Rise of the Western World (1973, with Robert Thomas).
  Structure and Change in Economic History (1981).
  Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance (1990).

  THE CORE ARGUMENT:
  Economic history is the history of INSTITUTIONS.
  Institutions = "rules of the game": formal rules (laws, constitutions)
  and informal constraints (norms, conventions).

  WHY DID THE WEST GROW?
  The development of secure property rights.
  The shift from redistributive to productive institutions.
  Transaction cost economics: institutions reduce the costs of exchange.

  PROPERTY RIGHTS ARGUMENT:
  Agricultural growth in medieval England: secure long-term tenures
  encouraged investment in the land.
  Where property rights were insecure: underinvestment, stagnation.

  PATH DEPENDENCE:
  Once institutional arrangements are in place, they are hard to change.
  The QWERTY keyboard is the classic example (though contested).
  Institutions lock in; "path dependence" explains persistence of
  inefficient arrangements.

  INFLUENCE:
  New Institutional Economics (Williamson, Coase, North).
  Development economics: why are some countries poor?
  "Good institutions" argument in development policy.
```

## Demographic History

### The Cambridge Group

```
  E.A. WRIGLEY AND ROGER SCHOFIELD
  (Population History of England 1541-1871, 1981):
  Reconstruction of English demographic history from parish registers.
  358 Anglican parish registers, totaling millions of events.
  Births, marriages, deaths over 330 years.

  "FAMILY RECONSTITUTION":
  Link individual records across time: this birth record to this marriage
  to these children to this death. Build complete family histories.
  First large-scale systematic use of this method.

  KEY FINDINGS:
  English population growth was not just Malthusian.
  Marriage rate and age at marriage were key variables.
  "European marriage pattern": late marriage, high celibacy.
  This demographic regime was distinctive and had major economic consequences.

  MALTHUSIAN CHECK IN ENGLAND:
  Real wages and marriage were correlated (homeostatic mechanism).
  When wages fell: people delayed marriage; birth rate fell; pressure reduced.
  This "preventive check" was more important than the "positive check" (famine/disease).
  England avoided the catastrophic oscillations of other European regions.
```

### Global Demographic History

```
  GLOBAL STANDARDS OF LIVING DEBATE:
  Robert Allen (wage and price data from multiple cities globally):
  Real wages in 1820: London and Amsterdam were the highest in the world.
  Chinese and Indian living standards were lower than European.
  This supports a "Great Divergence" in living standards before industrialization.

  KENNETH POMERANZ (The Great Divergence, 2000):
  China's Yangtze Delta was roughly comparable to England ca. 1750.
  The divergence after 1800 was contingent, not predetermined.
  Britain's advantages: coal geography, colonial resources (cotton, sugar).

  ALLEN RESPONSE:
  The wage data shows European (especially NW European) advantage earlier.
  High wages drove labor-saving innovation (the Industrial Revolution).

  THE DEBATE:
  When did the "great divergence" begin?
  Was it pre-ordained by institutions or contingent on specific factors?
  Quantitative sources essential but contested in their interpretation.
```

## Methods and Their Limits

### Counterfactual History

```
  COUNTERFACTUAL ("what if") METHOD:
  Construct a hypothetical world without X.
  Measure the difference between actual and hypothetical.
  This gives the causal effect of X.

  REQUIREMENTS:
  1. The counterfactual must be plausible (a "small" change, not magic).
  2. The hypothetical world must be internally consistent.
  3. All secondary effects must be traced through.

  CRITICISMS:
  "Unhistorical": history didn't happen that way.
  Counterfactuals are speculative; different assumptions give different results.
  The past is a unified system; removing one element changes everything.

  RESPONSE (Fogel):
  Counterfactual reasoning is implicit in all historical explanation.
  "X caused Y" implies "without X, Y would not have occurred."
  Making the counterfactual explicit is more honest, not less.
```

### Regression and Causation

```
  ORDINARY LEAST SQUARES (OLS) REGRESSION:
  Most common statistical method in economic history.
  Y = a + b1*X1 + b2*X2 + ... + error

  OMITTED VARIABLE BIAS:
  If a variable Z causes both X and Y, the estimated effect of X on Y
  will be biased (it will also capture Z's effect).
  Major challenge in historical research where controls are limited.

  INSTRUMENTAL VARIABLES (IV):
  An instrument is a variable correlated with X but affecting Y only
  through X. Allows identification of causal effect.
  Classic instrument for geography: Acemoglu, Johnson, Robinson (2001):
  Settler mortality as instrument for colonial institutions.
  High mortality -> extractive institutions -> low growth today.

  REGRESSION DISCONTINUITY (RD):
  If treatment is assigned by a threshold rule, compare observations
  just above and below the threshold.
  Works because near-threshold observations are otherwise similar.
  Applied to: electoral outcomes, historical policy effects.

  NATURAL EXPERIMENTS:
  Exploiting quasi-random variation in historical events.
  Famines, wars, policy changes, geographic features.
  Daron Acemoglu: "reversal of fortune" — colonies that were richer in 1500
  are now poorer. Why? Extractive institutions installed where elites were present.
```

## Cliodynamics: Quantitative Macro-History

```
  CLIODYNAMICS (Peter Turchin, 2010s):
  Mathematical modeling of large-scale historical dynamics.
  Draws on dynamical systems theory, not just econometrics.

  KEY MODELS:
  SECULAR CYCLES:
  Population -> elite overproduction -> state weakness -> civil war -> collapse.
  Turchin and Nefedov: Secular Cycles (2009).
  Applied to: Rome, Medieval England, France, China.

  POLITICAL STRESS INDEX:
  Combines: income inequality, elite numbers, state finances.
  Quantified from historical data.
  Prediction: stress peaks lead to civil unrest and instability.

  APPLICATION TO US:
  Turchin predicted in 2010 that the 2010s-2020s would see peak instability.
  Published in Nature, 2010. Subsequent events (2020) seen as confirming.

  CRITICISM:
  Overfitting: complex systems can always be fit post-hoc.
  Historical data quality varies enormously.
  The models are suggestive frameworks, not precise predictions.
  Most historians skeptical of the pretension to "laws" of history.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Cliometrics showing slavery was "efficient" is not a moral argument.**
Fogel explicitly stated this. Economic efficiency and moral acceptability are orthogonal.
That slavery was profitable for slaveholders is a historical fact; that it was a moral
atrocity is also a historical fact. The confusion of the two is a category error.

**Counterfactual reasoning is not speculation.**
It is hypothesis testing. The question "what if railroads didn't exist?" requires the
same rigor as any causal claim. Fogel's point: making the counterfactual explicit is
MORE rigorous than implicit causal claims historians routinely make.

**Path dependence does not mean "nothing can change."**
Path dependence means: history matters; initial conditions constrain future options.
It does not mean institutions never change. North's own work traced massive
institutional change. Path dependence describes constraints, not determinism.

**Demographic history is not just population counts.**
It includes family structure, marriage patterns, fertility, mortality, age at marriage,
migration — all of which have deep social and economic implications. Wrigley and Schofield's
work on England showed that marriage age and rates were a more important demographic
mechanism than famines and epidemics.
