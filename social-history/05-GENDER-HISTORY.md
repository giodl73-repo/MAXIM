# Gender History and Feminist Historiography

## The Big Picture

Gender history emerged from women's history in the 1970s and evolved into a broader analytical
approach. Joan Scott's 1986 essay "Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis" marked the
theoretical turning point: gender is not just about women but is a fundamental organizing category
of social life, power, and meaning. Gender history transformed not just women's history but
the entire discipline.

## Women's History: The Beginnings

### Recovering Hidden Lives

```
  WOMEN'S HISTORY (1960s-70s):
  Emerged from the women's movement.
  Fundamental question: "Where are the women?"
  Traditional history was about men (political leaders, generals, thinkers).

  THE CASE FOR WOMEN'S HISTORY:
  Women are half the population. Why are they absent from history?
  Two answers:
  (a) They were absent from the sources because excluded from public life.
  (b) Historians hadn't looked in the right sources.
  Both were true; the answer was: look in different places.

  NEW SOURCES FOR WOMEN'S HISTORY:
  Wills and probate records.
  Church courts (adultery, marriage disputes, slander).
  Medical records (reproduction, health).
  Letters and diaries.
  Domestic household accounts.
  Oral histories.
  Charitable organization records.
  Industrial labor records.

  EARLY SCHOLARSHIP:
  Carroll Smith-Rosenberg, "The Female World of Love and Ritual" (1975):
  Intense female friendships in 19th-century America.
  Access to previously ignored sources: diaries, letters.
```

### "Separate Spheres" Ideology

```
  19TH CENTURY "SEPARATE SPHERES":
  Middle-class ideology (especially Anglo-American):
  Men: public sphere (work, politics, law, commerce).
  Women: private sphere (home, family, children, religion).

  HISTORICAL QUESTIONS:
  Was "separate spheres" an accurate description of women's lives?
  (No: working-class women always worked outside the home.)
  When did this ideology emerge? Why? What did it do?
  How did women negotiate, resist, and use this ideology?

  LIBERAL FEMINIST HISTORY:
  Eleanor Flexner (Century of Struggle, 1959): suffrage movement history.
  Traced women's political activism despite exclusion.
  Central narrative: the fight for equality and rights.

  CRITIQUE:
  "Adding women" doesn't transform history; it supplements it.
  The framework (progress toward equality) is itself male-defined.
  The typical subject is middle-class, white, Western women.
```

## Joan Scott: Gender as Analytical Category

```
  JOAN SCOTT, "Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis" (1986):
  The most cited article in American Historical Review.
  Transformed women's history into gender history.

  SCOTT'S DEFINITION:
  "Gender is a constitutive element of social relationships based on
  perceived differences between the sexes, and gender is a primary
  way of signifying relationships of power."

  TWO PARTS:
  1. Gender is a constitutive element of social relations:
     Core symbols (Eve, Mary, nature, nation), normative concepts
     (legal, religious, scientific, educational), social institutions,
     and subjective identities all involve gender.

  2. Gender is a primary way of signifying power:
     Political authority, economic organization, cultural hierarchy —
     all use gender as a structuring category.
     "Kings are men; women are excluded from power" is not just about
     women — it is how power is organized and legitimated.

  INFLUENCES:
  Foucault: power-knowledge, discourse.
  Derrida: deconstruction of binary oppositions.
  Lacan: psychoanalytic theory of identity formation.

  IMPLICATIONS FOR HISTORICAL PRACTICE:
  Look at gender in ALL historical topics, not just women's history.
  War, politics, science, religion, economics — all are gendered.
  The French Revolution defined the "citizen" as male; this was a political act.
  Medical discourse on hysteria was a form of power over women's bodies.
```

## Feminist Theory and Historiography

### Liberal, Socialist, and Radical Feminism

```
  LIBERAL FEMINISM:
  Goal: equal rights and opportunities for women.
  History: tell the story of the progress toward equality.
  Focus: legal and political barriers, suffrage, workplace equality.
  Key historians: Eleanor Flexner, Ellen DuBois (women's suffrage).

  SOCIALIST/MARXIST FEMINISM:
  Capitalism and patriarchy are intertwined.
  Class shapes women's experience: working-class women, enslaved women,
  colonial women had very different lives from middle-class women.
  History: labor history, working women, domestic labor as economic work.
  Key historians: Sally Alexander, Temma Kaplan.

  RADICAL FEMINISM:
  Patriarchy is primary, not reducible to capitalism.
  Male domination is universal and must be analyzed in its own terms.
  History: violence against women, sexuality, the body.
  Catharine MacKinnon, Andrea Dworkin.

  LIMITATIONS OF ALL THREE:
  "Women" as a category homogenizes enormous differences.
  Race, class, sexuality, colonialism all fractured "women's experience."
  Black feminist thought (Patricia Hill Collins, Audre Lorde, bell hooks):
  Black women's experience is not the sum of "Black" + "woman."
  It is a distinct intersection with its own specificity.
```

### Intersectionality

```
  KIMBERLÉ CRENSHAW, "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex" (1989):
  Introduced "intersectionality" as a legal-analytical concept.
  Extended to social theory and history.

  THE ARGUMENT:
  Black women workers' discrimination cannot be analyzed as:
  (race discrimination) + (sex discrimination).
  It is a distinct form of discrimination at the intersection.
  Courts and legal theory that treat them separately miss this.

  HISTORICAL APPLICATION:
  The history of "women" usually meant white, middle-class women.
  The history of "race" (in the US) often meant Black men.
  Black women fell between the analytical categories.

  MULTIPLE INTERSECTIONS:
  Race, class, gender, sexuality, disability, nationality, religion.
  Each person occupies multiple social positions simultaneously.
  Historical experience is shaped by the combination, not each separately.

  METHODOLOGICAL IMPLICATIONS:
  Can't write "women's history" without asking: which women?
  Categories like "worker," "peasant," "citizen" carry gender and racial assumptions.
  Intersectionality requires disaggregating to specific historical subjects.
```

## The History of Masculinity

```
  MEN'S HISTORY / HISTORY OF MASCULINITY (1980s-):
  If gender is a category of analysis for women, it must also apply to men.
  What did it mean to be a man in specific historical contexts?

  KEY WORKS:
  George Mosse (The Image of Man, 1996): bourgeois masculine ideal.
  John Tosh (A Man's Place, 1999): masculinity and domesticity in Victorian England.
  Michael Kimmel: history of American masculinity.

  "CRISIS OF MASCULINITY" NARRATIVE:
  Industrialization, urbanization, suffrage movements -> crisis of manhood.
  Various historical responses: muscular Christianity, imperialism,
  sport, fraternal organizations.

  MILITARISM AND MASCULINITY:
  George Mosse: modern war mobilized masculine ideals.
  The soldier-hero was a model of nationalism and masculinity.
  The "fellowship of the trenches" in WWI.

  HEGEMONIC MASCULINITY (R.W. Connell):
  Not all men embody the dominant form of masculinity.
  Hegemonic: the dominant, culturally approved form.
  Subordinated: forms that don't conform (gay, effeminate, etc.).
  These are historically specific and contested.
```

## Sexuality and the History of the Body

```
  HISTORY OF SEXUALITY (Foucault, 1976):
  Sexuality is not a natural fact but a historical construction.
  The category "homosexuality" was invented in the 19th century.
  Before ca. 1870: sodomy was an act that anyone might commit.
  After: homosexuality was a type of person (identity, not just act).

  MEDICALIZATION OF SEXUALITY:
  19th-century medicine created categories: homosexual, heterosexual,
  nymphomaniac, frigid, perverted.
  These categories were tools of power/knowledge, not discoveries of nature.

  QUEER HISTORY:
  Uses Foucault's insights to historicize all sexual categories.
  John D'Emilio: the gay identity emerged with capitalism (detachment from
  family economy enabled same-sex households).
  George Chauncey (Gay New York, 1994): pre-Stonewall queer culture in NYC.

  HISTORY OF THE BODY:
  How have bodies been understood, disciplined, displayed, produced historically?
  Medical history, history of dress and gesture, history of pain, disability.
  Barbara Duden (The Woman Beneath the Skin): 18th-century German women's
  bodies in medical case records — a different bodily experience.
```

## Gender and Empire

```
  GENDER AND COLONIALISM:
  Ann Laura Stoler (Race and the Education of Desire, 1995):
  Applied Foucault's History of Sexuality to colonial history.
  Colonial sexuality was central to maintaining racial hierarchies.
  European women in the colonies: policed, protected, used to mark racial boundaries.
  Interracial sexuality: feared, regulated, legally controlled.

  ANTOINETTE BURTON / CATHERINE HALL:
  British feminism was entangled with imperialism.
  The suffrage movement's arguments often used colonial subjects
  as foil: "We are as civilized as colonial men."
  Imperial feminism: women's rights arguments premised on colonial hierarchy.

  GENDER AND NATIONALISM:
  Nations have typically been figured as female (Mother Russia, Britannia, La France).
  Women are "bearers of the national" (biologically reproducing the nation).
  Men are "defenders of the nation" (military service).
  These gendered nationalisms shaped women's citizenship differently from men's.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Women's history and gender history are not the same.**
Women's history recovers women's experiences. Gender history analyzes gender as a
category of social organization and power, which includes men, masculinity, and the
gendering of all aspects of social life. Women's history is necessary but not sufficient.

**"Gender is socially constructed" does not mean "gender is not real."**
Social construction means gender norms, categories, and meanings are historically variable
and produced by social practices — not that gender has no effects or that bodies don't
matter. Constructed things are real: money is socially constructed and also real.

**Intersectionality is not just "multiple identities."**
The claim is not just that people have multiple identities (race AND class AND gender).
It is that these categories co-constitute each other — the intersection produces a distinct
social position not reducible to the sum of its parts. Black women's experience is not
white women's experience plus Black men's experience.

**Foucault on sexuality doesn't say there were no same-sex relations before 1870.**
Of course there were. Foucault's argument is about identity: the category of the
"homosexual" as a type of person with a psychology, an etiology, and a social role
emerged in the 19th century. Before that, same-sex acts were categorized differently
(sin, crime) and did not constitute a distinct identity type.
