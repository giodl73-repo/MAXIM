# Postcolonial and Subaltern History

## The Big Picture

Postcolonial history examines the history, legacy, and ongoing effects of European colonialism.
It encompasses the Subaltern Studies project (recovering colonial subjects' perspectives),
Said's critique of Orientalism, and efforts to de-center European historical frameworks. The
field challenges who tells history, whose perspective counts, and what categories are used.

## Intellectual Precursors

### Frantz Fanon (1925-1961)

```
  THE WRETCHED OF THE EARTH (1961):
  Written as Algeria fought for independence; preface by Sartre.
  Fanon was a Martinican psychiatrist; worked in Algeria.

  COLONIALISM AS TOTAL SYSTEM:
  Colonialism is not just economic exploitation but a total social system.
  It produces a "colonized mind" — internalization of inferiority.
  The colonized learn to see themselves through the colonizer's eyes.
  Violence of colonialism extends to the psyche (Black Skin, White Masks, 1952).

  DECOLONIZATION:
  Decolonization must be total — not just political independence
  but a transformation of the colonized self.
  Violence as purging: Fanon argued that revolutionary violence could
  restore the colonized person's agency and dignity.
  (Controversial; Arendt criticized as glorifying violence.)

  THE PITFALLS OF NATIONAL CONSCIOUSNESS:
  After independence: if the national bourgeoisie simply inherits the
  colonial state structure, nothing changes.
  The national middle class becomes a new exploiting class.
  Fanon's warning about postcolonial elites proved prescient.
```

### Edward Said: Orientalism

```
  EDWARD SAID (Orientalism, 1978):
  The founding text of postcolonial literary studies.

  THE ARGUMENT:
  "Orientalism" = a Western discourse about "the Orient" (Middle East, Asia).
  Not a description of the Orient but a way of producing it as an object of knowledge.
  The Orient is constructed as: irrational, sensual, despotic, timeless, feminine,
  opposed to Western rationality, dynamism, freedom, masculinity.

  FOUCAULDIAN FRAMEWORK:
  Said uses Foucault's concept of discourse.
  Orientalism is a power-knowledge system: it supports colonial domination
  while claiming to describe "reality."
  The discourse produces its object.

  EVIDENCE:
  Novels (Flaubert, Kipling), scholarly texts, administrative reports, paintings.
  Consistent representation of "the Orient" across different genres and periods.
  Even sympathetic accounts reproduce Orientalist assumptions.

  CRITICISMS:
  Said's Orient is monolithic — different colonialists had different views.
  Said didn't give enough agency to actual orientals.
  Ignores Islamic and Asian scholarship and self-representation.
  Presents "the West" as unified (it was not).
  But: foundational for the field; critique of knowledge-power in scholarship.

  CULTURE AND IMPERIALISM (1993):
  Extended to literary canon: how English literature sustained and naturalized empire.
  Jane Austen's Mansfield Park: the slave-owning property in Antigua that
  funds the English estate goes unremarked. Said makes it visible.
```

## Subaltern Studies

### Origins and Program

```
  SUBALTERN STUDIES GROUP (1982):
  Founded in India: Ranajit Guha, Shahid Amin, Partha Chatterjee,
  David Hardiman, Sumit Sarkar, Gyanendra Pandey.
  "Subaltern" from Gramsci: those subordinated by class, caste, gender, age.

  PROGRAM:
  Write history of colonial India from the perspective of those
  excluded from the nationalist/colonial archives.
  Recover: peasant revolts, tribal uprisings, women's resistance,
  subaltern consciousness.

  RANAJIT GUHA (Elementary Aspects of Peasant Insurgency, 1983):
  Analyse 19th-century peasant revolts in India.
  Peasant consciousness was not "prepolitical" (Hobsbawm's term).
  Peasant action had its own rationality, codes, and objectives.
  Reject the condescension that peasants "don't understand" politics.

  SOURCES:
  Colonial administrative records (police reports, court records)
  are the main sources — but they represent peasants through the
  colonizer's lens.
  Subaltern Studies read "against the grain": extract traces of
  subaltern experience from hostile official sources.
```

### Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak: "Can the Subaltern Speak?"

```
  "CAN THE SUBALTERN SPEAK?" (1988):
  The most famous and most debated article in postcolonial theory.

  THE ARGUMENT:
  Can we recover the subaltern's voice from the historical record?
  Spivak says: No — or at least, not without severe distortion.

  THE CASE: Sati (widow self-immolation) in colonial India.
  Two "sides" in colonial discourse:
  (a) British: "white men saving brown women from brown men."
  (b) Hindu nationalists: "women choose to die for tradition."
  The widow herself is absent from both narratives.
  She cannot speak in the terms either discourse provides.

  IF THE SUBALTERN SPEAKS:
  She is heard through existing discursive frameworks that distort her speech.
  The intellectual who "represents" her also distorts.
  There is no transparent access to the subaltern's consciousness.

  IMPLICATION (controversial):
  This does not mean subalterns have no agency or voice.
  It means: the production of the subaltern as a recoverable subject
  is itself an effect of power, even in sympathetic scholarship.
  Spivak's later work: responsibility of the scholar is to "unlearn"
  privilege and to learn to listen without projecting.

  RESPONSE:
  Some historians: too pessimistic; oral history, material evidence,
  practice theory can recover subaltern experience partially.
  The warning stands: self-awareness about the position from which
  you "give voice" to others.
```

### Homi Bhabha: Hybridity and Mimicry

```
  HOMI BHABHA (The Location of Culture, 1994):

  MIMICRY:
  Colonial subjects are required to mimic colonial culture
  (dress, language, values) — but never fully.
  "Almost the same, but not quite."
  The "almost" is the gap that undermines colonial authority.
  The mimic is a disturbing parody of the colonizer.

  HYBRIDITY:
  Colonial culture produces hybrid identities — neither purely colonizer
  nor colonized but a "third space."
  This space is not a compromise but a site of disruption.
  Hybrid culture cannot be contained or controlled by colonial authority.

  AMBIVALENCE:
  The colonizer both desires and fears the colonized.
  Colonial discourse is never pure domination — always troubled by
  ambivalence, fascination, projection.

  CRITICISM:
  Bhabha's abstraction makes it hard to ground in specific historical cases.
  Celebrates hybridity in ways that may be available only to elites.
  "Third space" can romanticize structural violence.
```

## Dipesh Chakrabarty: Provincializing Europe

```
  DIPESH CHAKRABARTY (Provincializing Europe: Postcolonial Thought
  and Historical Difference, 2000):
  The most theoretically developed argument in postcolonial historiography.

  THE PROBLEM:
  All non-Western historical writing uses European conceptual frameworks:
  capitalism, democracy, civil society, the public sphere, the nation.
  These concepts were developed to describe European experience.
  Applied to India, Africa, China: they produce a narrative of lack
  ("India hasn't achieved X yet").

  "HISTORY 1" AND "HISTORY 2":
  History 1: the abstract universal narrative of capital (Marx's Capital).
  History 2: the concrete, heterogeneous histories that capital encounters
  and must work through — lives, relationships, practices that are not
  reducible to the logic of capital.

  "PROVINCIALIZING EUROPE":
  Not reject European thought (Chakrabarty uses Marx and Heidegger extensively).
  But: recognize it as provincial — one tradition among many.
  Europe's categories must be "defamiliarized" — seen as historical, not universal.
  Non-Western histories must be written on their own terms, not as
  deviations from a European norm.

  THE SUBJECT OF HISTORY:
  "History is the discourse that refuses to allow the past to be simply
  the past." It is always a conversation with the present.
  Whose past? Whose present? Postcolonial history asks this constantly.
```

## Colonial Archives and Historical Method

```
  THE PROBLEM OF COLONIAL ARCHIVES:
  Most historical sources for colonial subjects were produced by colonizers.
  Administrative records, missionary reports, medical records, legal documents.
  These sources encode colonial power relations.

  ANN LAURA STOLER (Along the Archival Grain, 2009):
  Colonial archives should be read "along the grain" — understanding
  what anxieties, desires, and projects shaped their production.
  Archives are not neutral repositories but records of colonial knowledge-making.
  The structure and categories of the archive reveal colonial epistemology.

  READING AGAINST THE GRAIN:
  Extract traces of subaltern action, resistance, and consciousness
  from sources designed to surveil, control, or deny them.

  ORAL HISTORY AND MATERIAL CULTURE:
  When written sources fail, turn to: oral traditions, archaeology,
  material culture, comparative ethnography.
  But all these also require critical methodological attention.

  SENSORY HISTORY:
  Recent turn: smell, sound, touch, taste in historical experience.
  Harder to access from archives but attempted: literary representation,
  medical sources, ethnographic description.
```

## Decolonial Theory

```
  DECOLONIAL VS. POSTCOLONIAL:
  POSTCOLONIAL (Said, Bhabha, Spivak): responds to British/French colonialism;
  emphasis on discourse, hybridity, representation.

  DECOLONIAL (Latin American school: Quijano, Mignolo, Maldonado-Torres):
  COLONIALITY: not just the period of colonial rule but the ongoing
  structuring of knowledge, being, and power by the colonial legacy.
  "Colonialism is over; coloniality continues."

  ANÍBAL QUIJANO (Coloniality of Power, 2000):
  The colonial racial hierarchy (European > mestizo > indigenous > Black)
  was not dismantled by independence.
  It was internalized and reproduced in postcolonial Latin American societies.
  Race was invented as a colonial category and persists.

  WALTER MIGNOLO (Local Histories / Global Designs):
  Colonial modernity and its "darker side."
  Western knowledge (science, economics, philosophy) claims universality
  but is inseparable from colonial power.
  "Decolonial option": think from the margins, from colonial wounds.

  RESPONSE TO CHAKRABARTY:
  Decolonial: doesn't go far enough.
  "Provincializing Europe" still works within European frameworks.
  Need complete epistemic decolonization — alternative knowledge systems.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Postcolonial is not anti-Western.**
Said, Spivak, and Bhabha all work with Western theory (Foucault, Derrida, psychoanalysis).
Postcolonial theory is a critique of specific historical formations of knowledge and power,
not a rejection of all Western scholarship.

**"Subaltern cannot speak" is not fatalism.**
Spivak's argument is about the conditions under which subaltern speech is heard
and what distortions occur. It is a methodological warning, not a statement that
resistance is impossible or that recovery is hopeless.

**Hybridity is not just "mixing."**
Bhabha's hybridity is a structural analysis of how colonial discourse produces
ambivalent, destabilizing effects — not simply a celebration of multicultural mixing.
Hybridity is not always positive; it can be a product of violence and coercion.

**Postcolonial theory is not only about "developing countries."**
Colonialism shaped the "metropolis" as much as the colony. Britain, France, Spain,
Portugal were transformed by their colonial projects. Postcolonial analysis applies
to racism, immigration, and cultural politics in former colonial powers.
