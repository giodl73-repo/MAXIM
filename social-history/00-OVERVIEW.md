# Social History — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Social history is the study of how ordinary people lived — their work, families, communities,
daily practices, and collective experiences. It emerged in the mid-20th century as a reaction
against political and diplomatic history's focus on elites and great events. The Annales school,
"history from below," quantitative methods, and the later cultural turn all contributed to
making social history the dominant mode of historical practice from the 1970s onward.

## The Turn from Political to Social History

```
  TRADITIONAL HISTORY (19th C - 1950s):
  Kings, battles, diplomats, great events.
  Ranke: "what actually happened" — based on state archives.
  History of great men (Carlyle: "the history of the world is but
  the biography of great men").

  THE SOCIAL HISTORY TURN (1950s-1970s):
  Reaction against political/diplomatic history.
  Inspired by social sciences: sociology, economics, demography.
  Questions: How did ordinary people live? What shaped their lives?
  How were economies and societies organized?

  KEY IMPETUS:
  Expansion of universities; new historical profession.
  Left politics: labor movement, Marxism, feminism.
  New sources: parish registers, tax records, court records,
  census data — not just diplomatic archives.
  Social science methods: statistics, demography, economics.
```

## Major Schools and Approaches

### The Annales School (Module 01 in this directory)

```
  Founded 1929 by Lucien Febvre and Marc Bloch (Annales journal).
  Fernand Braudel: longue durée (long run), structures, cycles.
  Third generation (1960s-70s): quantitative methods, serial history.
  Fourth generation (1980s-): cultural history, microhistory.

  CONCEPT OF TOTAL HISTORY:
  History of all aspects of human life: economy, demography, geography,
  mentality, material culture, language, religion.
  Not narrative event-history but structural analysis.
```

### History from Below (Module 03)

```
  British tradition: E.P. Thompson, Eric Hobsbawm, Christopher Hill.
  Recovering the experience of workers, peasants, women, the poor.
  Against economic determinism: agency, consciousness, culture matter.
  "Making of the English Working Class" (Thompson, 1963): paradigm text.
```

### Quantitative and Cliometric History (Module 04)

```
  American school: Robert Fogel, Stanley Engerman, Douglass North.
  Econometric methods applied to history.
  Counter-intuitive results: slavery was economically efficient (NOT morally justified).
  Time on the Cross (1974): slavery argument caused huge controversy.
```

### Gender History (Module 05)

```
  Women's history -> feminist historiography -> gender as analytical category.
  Joan Scott's "Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis" (1986).
  Gender is not just about women; it structures all of social life.
```

### Postcolonial and Subaltern History (Module 06)

```
  Subaltern Studies Group (India, 1982): Ranajit Guha, Gayatri Spivak.
  Recovering colonial subjects' perspectives.
  Challenging Eurocentric frameworks in world history.
```

## Key Concepts

| Concept | School | Meaning |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Longue durée** | Braudel/Annales | Long time spans of structural history |
| **Mentalités** | Annales | Collective worldviews, mental frameworks |
| **Serial history** | Annales 3rd gen | Statistical analysis of repetitive data series |
| **History from below** | British school | History of ordinary people, not elites |
| **Cliometrics** | US econometrics | Economic methods applied to history |
| **Gender as analytic category** | Scott | Gender structures power/meaning, not just women's sphere |
| **Subaltern** | Spivak/Guha | Those excluded from power, whose voices are absent |
| **Lieu de mémoire** | Nora | Sites of memory (monuments, places, texts) |
| **Microhistory** | Ginzburg, Levi | In-depth study of a small case to reveal larger structures |
| **Path dependency** | Economic history | Historical trajectories constrain future choices |

## The "New Social History" and Its Critics

```
  "NEW SOCIAL HISTORY" (1960s-1980s):
  Dominant mode in Anglo-American history.
  Focus: family, community, demography, labor, race, class.
  Methods: quantitative (demographic records, tax lists, court records).
  Question: How were lives structured by social forces?

  CRITICISMS / THE CULTURAL TURN (1980s-90s):
  1. Over-emphasis on structure; insufficient attention to experience and meaning.
     E.P. Thompson's rebuttal: class is not just structural position but "making."
  2. Quantitative methods miss meaning, language, subjectivity.
  3. The "social" is itself a category that needs examination (linguistic turn).
  4. New cultural history emerged: Chartier, Hunt, Darnton.
     Focus: representations, rituals, meanings, cultural practices.

  RESOLUTION:
  Social history absorbed cultural methods.
  "New cultural history" incorporated social questions.
  By 2000: most historians worked across both.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Social history is not the same as political history of the left.**
Social history includes conservative and right-wing movements, religious communities,
wealthy families — not just the working class. The subject is social structures and
ordinary life, not political ideology.

**The Annales School is not a single monolithic approach.**
Bloch and Febvre (founders), Braudel (second generation), and Le Roy Ladurie, Duby
(third generation) all had distinct approaches. By the fourth generation (1980s),
"Annales" was more a journal and tradition than a coherent school.

**Quantitative history is not just number-crunching.**
Cliometrics involves formulating historical hypotheses, building models, and testing
them against data. The methods are those of econometrics, but the questions are
historical. Fogel's controversial work on slavery used counterfactual modeling.

**"History from below" does not exclude elites.**
The phrase means prioritizing those previously excluded from historical narratives.
It does not mean ignoring elites — understanding the powerful is necessary to
understand the powerless. E.P. Thompson studied the relationship between classes.
