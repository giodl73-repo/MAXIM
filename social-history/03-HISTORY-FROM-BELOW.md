# History from Below: Workers and Everyday Life

## The Big Picture

"History from below" recovers the experiences, perspectives, and agency of those largely absent
from traditional historical narratives: workers, peasants, women, the poor, enslaved people, the
colonized. The British Marxist historians (Thompson, Hobsbawm, Hill) created the paradigm in the
1960s; it then spread globally and merged with cultural history.

## E.P. Thompson: The Making of the English Working Class

```
  THE MAKING OF THE ENGLISH WORKING CLASS (1963):
  900 pages; the foundational text of "history from below."
  "I am seeking to rescue the poor stockinger, the Luddite cropper,
  the 'obsolete' hand-loom weaver, the 'utopian' artisan, and even
  the deluded follower of Joanna Southcott, from the enormous
  condescension of posterity."

  THE ARGUMENT:
  Class is not a thing or a structure — it is a PROCESS.
  The working class was not created by industrialization as a passive
  response to economic forces. Workers MADE themselves into a class
  through shared experiences, culture, politics, and struggle.

  "Class happens when some men, as a result of common experiences
  (inherited or shared), feel and articulate the identity of their
  interests as between themselves, and as against other men whose
  interests are different from (and usually opposed to) theirs."

  KEY CLAIM:
  Class consciousness is not a mechanical reflex of economic position.
  It is formed culturally, through shared traditions, religious experience,
  artisan pride, radical politics — Methodism, jacobinism, the free-born
  Englishman tradition.

  THE PERIOD:
  1780-1832: the period of the Industrial Revolution.
  The "making" is not just economic but moral, cultural, political.
```

### Thompson's Moral Economy

```
  "THE MORAL ECONOMY OF THE ENGLISH CROWD" (1971):
  Famous article. Analysis of 18th-century food riots.

  THE ARGUMENT:
  Food riots were not just hunger and disorder.
  They expressed a "moral economy": a set of social norms about
  fair price, just dealing, and the obligations of the powerful.
  Crowds enforced these norms against market mechanisms.

  EXAMPLES:
  When bakers raised bread prices in times of scarcity, crowds
  would seize grain, sell it at a "fair price," and return the proceeds.
  Not theft but enforcement of a moral standard.

  PATERNALIST-PLEBEIAN EQUILIBRIUM:
  Gentry accepted responsibilities (grain sales in markets, not for export)
  in exchange for deference. When gentry violated this:
  crowds "legitimated" their action by enforcing traditional norms.

  INFLUENCE:
  James Scott (Weapons of the Weak): moral economy extended to
  Southeast Asian peasantry.
  Applied globally to understanding popular protest that looks
  "irrational" to modern economic analysis.
```

## Eric Hobsbawm: Collective Biography of the Dispossessed

```
  KEY WORKS:
  Primitive Rebels (1959): millenarian movements, bandits, Mafia, etc.
  Bandits (1969): the social bandit as a type.
  Age of Revolution (1962), Age of Capital (1975), Age of Empire (1987),
  Age of Extremes (1994): four-volume history of the world 1789-1991.

  SOCIAL BANDITS:
  The "primitive rebel" and the "social bandit" are figures who exist
  outside the law but are protected by and serve their communities.
  Robin Hood as type: robs the rich, gives to the poor.
  Real historical examples: Sicilian banditry, 19th-century brigands.

  CONDITIONS FOR SOCIAL BANDITRY:
  Pre-capitalist peasant societies.
  Weak state authority in peripheral regions.
  Sharp inequality between lords and peasants.
  Strong community solidarity.

  THE FOUR AGES:
  Narrative and analytical history from a Marxist perspective.
  Hobsbawm traced the arc of capitalism and its social consequences.
  Age of Extremes (1994): the "short twentieth century" 1914-1991.
  The communist experiment and its failure; the triumph and limits of capitalism.
```

## Christopher Hill: The Radical English Revolution

```
  KEY WORKS:
  The World Turned Upside Down (1972): radical religion in the English Revolution.
  The Century of Revolution (1961): 17th-century England.
  Milton and the English Revolution (1977).

  THE ARGUMENT:
  The English Civil War (1640-60) was a revolution — not just a political
  conflict but a social transformation.
  Beneath the surface of Parliament vs. King: radical social movements.

  THE RADICAL GROUPS:
  Levellers: political democracy, universal (male) suffrage.
  Diggers (Gerrard Winstanley): agrarian communism; common lands.
  Ranters: antinomian; rejection of all morality and law.
  Fifth Monarchists: millenarian; awaiting Christ's kingdom.
  Quakers (early): egalitarianism, rejection of hierarchy.

  SIGNIFICANCE:
  These groups articulated ideas — democracy, communism, religious
  freedom — that were 200 years ahead of their time.
  The Revolution failed to institutionalize them; the Restoration suppressed them.
  But the ideas went underground and re-emerged.

  METHODOLOGICAL NOTE:
  Hill's work is more cultural and religious than Thompson's economic.
  Shows how "history from below" can be the history of ideas — just from below.
```

## Everyday Resistance: James Scott

```
  WEAPONS OF THE WEAK (1985):
  Fieldwork in Malaysian village; study of everyday peasant resistance.

  HIDDEN TRANSCRIPT / PUBLIC TRANSCRIPT:
  PUBLIC TRANSCRIPT: what subordinates say in front of the powerful.
  Deference, compliance, apparent acceptance of the dominant order.

  HIDDEN TRANSCRIPT: what subordinates say behind the powerful's back.
  Criticism, mockery, rage, resistance — expressed only in safe spaces.

  "Every subordinate group creates, out of its ordeal, a 'hidden transcript'
  that represents a critique of power spoken behind the back of the dominant."

  EVERYDAY RESISTANCE:
  Foot-dragging, feigned ignorance, pilfering, gossip, minor sabotage.
  Not dramatic rebellion but constant low-level non-compliance.
  "The normal condition of the peasantry... is not rebellion and not
  submission, but the everyday forms of resistance."

  DOMINATION AND THE ARTS OF RESISTANCE (1990):
  Extended to all forms of subordination: slavery, serfdom, colonial rule.
  When do hidden transcripts erupt publicly? When the risk-reward calculation shifts.
  Rebellion = public assertion of the hidden transcript.

  INFLUENCE:
  Labor history: understanding worker resistance without formal unions.
  Colonial history: everyday resistance under colonialism.
  Gender history: women's everyday resistance.
```

## Oral History

```
  ORAL HISTORY:
  Recording and analyzing spoken accounts of historical experience.
  Particularly important for groups that left few written sources.

  PIONEERS:
  Studs Terkel (Working, Hard Times, The Good War): interviews with
  ordinary Americans about their experiences.
  Alessandro Portelli (Italy): oral history methodology.

  WHAT ORAL HISTORY OFFERS:
  Access to experiences not documented elsewhere.
  Subjectivity and memory: what events meant to participants.
  Alternative chronologies: memory doesn't follow official calendars.

  PORTELLI'S KEY INSIGHT:
  "Oral sources tell us not just what people did, but what they
  wanted to do, what they believed they were doing, and what they
  now think they did."
  Memory is not just a record of the past; it is an interpretation.
  The gap between what happened and what is remembered is itself
  historical data.

  METHODOLOGICAL ISSUES:
  Memory is unreliable: dates confused, sequences merged, meanings
  projected backward from present knowledge.
  But "errors" reveal: how people construct meaning, what matters to them.
  The gap between oral account and documentary record is the interesting zone.
```

## Natalie Zemon Davis: History from Below with Cultural Depth

```
  NATALIE ZEMON DAVIS (b. 1928):
  The Return of Martin Guerre (1983): the impostor case.
  Society and Culture in Early Modern France (1975).
  Women on the Margins (1995).

  MARTIN GUERRE:
  Famous imposture in 16th-century France.
  Martin Guerre left his village; years later, an impostor (Arnaud du Tilh)
  returned and lived as Martin for years.
  Questions: What did it mean for identity, gender, marriage, community?
  Davis used the case to explore early modern subjectivity and culture.

  METHODOLOGY:
  Davis used the court records but also imaginative reconstruction
  (she worked as historical advisor on the film).
  Her work influenced microhistory and the cultural history of ordinary people.

  WOMEN AND RELIGION:
  Three women (a Jew, a Protestant, a Catholic) in 17th-century France.
  Each wrote autobiographies; Davis analyzed their different self-presentations.
  Women from below: not passive victims but active constructors of meaning.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"History from below" does not exclude class analysis.**
Thompson, Hobsbawm, and Hill were all Marxists. They wrote from below BUT with attention
to class relations. The lower class is understood in relation to the upper class, not in
isolation.

**Thompson's "making" is not idealist.**
Thompson's stress on culture and consciousness does not deny that material conditions matter.
His point is that economic position doesn't mechanically produce class consciousness — the
process of formation involves culture, tradition, and political experience. Both matter.

**Moral economy is not nostalgia for pre-capitalist norms.**
Thompson was analyzing how specific norms functioned in specific historical contexts.
He was not arguing that pre-capitalist moral economy was superior, or that it should
be revived. He was recovering the internal logic of collective action.

**Oral history is not just recording reminiscences.**
It is a rigorous methodology: systematic collection, transcription, interpretation of
oral sources with attention to their specific properties (subjectivity, memory, meaning).
The unreliability of memory is a methodological problem to be addressed, not ignored.
