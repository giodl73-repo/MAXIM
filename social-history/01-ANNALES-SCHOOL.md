# The Annales School and Total History

## The Big Picture

The Annales school (named for its journal, Annales d'histoire économique et sociale, founded
1929) transformed historical practice in the 20th century more than any other single movement.
It challenged narrative political history with structural, interdisciplinary, long-term analysis.
Its four generations each took the project in new directions.

## Generation 1: Bloch and Febvre

### Marc Bloch (1886-1944)

```
  KEY WORKS:
  Feudal Society (1939-40): systematic analysis of medieval European society.
  Not a narrative but structural: how did feudalism work as a social system?

  COMPARATIVE METHOD:
  Bloch advocated comparison across regions and periods.
  "To understand France, compare it with England."
  Method: identify similar phenomena in different contexts to isolate causes.

  THE HISTORIAN'S CRAFT (written in hiding; incomplete at his death):
  Methodological testament.
  "What is history for? What does the historian do?"
  "For a long time, history was understood as the science of the past. This
  is an absurd formula. How could the past, as such, be an object of science?"
  History is the science of human societies in time.

  DEATH:
  Bloch was a French Jew who joined the Resistance.
  He was captured by the Gestapo and executed in 1944.
  His death became symbolic of the stakes of historical commitment.
```

### Lucien Febvre (1878-1956)

```
  KEY WORKS:
  The Problem of Unbelief in the Sixteenth Century (1942):
  Was Rabelais an atheist? Febvre's answer: you could not be an atheist
  in the 16th century because the mental equipment (outillage mental)
  for atheism did not yet exist.

  MENTAL TOOLS (outillage mental):
  Each epoch has its own categories, concepts, and mental habits.
  You cannot think outside the available mental toolkit of your time.
  This prefigures Kuhn's paradigms and Foucault's epistemes.

  THE PROGRAM:
  Febvre wanted history to engage with geography, economics, psychology,
  sociology — an interdisciplinary "total history."
  Against "narrow" political and diplomatic history.
  "L'histoire totale": capturing the full range of human experience.

  THE JOURNAL:
  Annales d'histoire économique et sociale (1929).
  Changed name several times; now: Annales. Histoire, Sciences Sociales.
  Published from Paris; became the flagship of French historiography.
```

## The Anti-Narrative Stance

```
  TRADITIONAL HISTORY'S NARRATIVE:
  Event-based: wars, reigns, treaties, great decisions.
  Chronological: one thing after another.
  Focused on the exceptional (great men, great moments).
  Archival: based on state archives and official documents.

  ANNALES CRITIQUE:
  Events are surface foam on the deep sea of history.
  Narrative emphasizes contingency; structures are what actually matter.
  Political history is epiphenomenal: real causes are economic and social.
  The dramas of politics are secondary to the rhythms of agriculture,
  demography, climate, and mentality.

  THE NEW SOURCES:
  Parish registers (demography, family structure)
  Tax records (wealth distribution, economic activity)
  Court records (crime, conflict, social norms)
  Agricultural surveys (production, prices)
  Archaeological evidence (material conditions)
  Geographical data (climate, terrain)

  THE NEW QUESTIONS:
  Not "what happened?" but "how did people live?"
  Not "why did kings decide X?" but "what determined economic cycles?"
  Not "who won the battle?" but "what were the long-term social consequences?"
```

## Generation 2: Braudel's Dominance

Fernand Braudel (1902-1985) dominated Annales from 1945-1968 and made it the most
prestigious school in world historical scholarship.

```
  THE MEDITERRANEAN (La Méditerranée et le monde méditerranéen, 1949):
  Braudel's doctoral thesis, written partly in German captivity.
  Model of the three-level temporal structure (see Module 02 in full).

  MATERIAL CIVILIZATION AND CAPITALISM (3 vols., 1967-79):
  Global history of material life and capitalism, 15th-18th centuries.
  Volume I: material life (food, drink, housing, clothing, technology).
  Volume II: market economy and capitalism.
  Volume III: the world-economy.

  BRAUDEL'S CONCEPT OF CAPITALISM:
  Capitalism is not the market economy — it is the layer above the market.
  The market is open competition; capitalism is monopoly and manipulation.
  Capitalists succeed not by competing in markets but by transcending them:
  controlling information, monopolizing trade routes, working with states.
  This anticipates and influenced Wallerstein's world-systems theory.

  INFLUENCE ON SOCIAL SCIENCES:
  Wallerstein: world-systems theory (core, periphery, semi-periphery).
  Skocpol: states and revolutions.
  Arrighi: The Long Twentieth Century (Braudelian capitalism cycles).
```

## Generation 3: Serial History and Mentalités

### Serial History (Quantitative Annales)

```
  "SERIAL HISTORY" (histoire sérielle):
  Systematic analysis of long quantitative series.
  Prices, wages, births, deaths, marriages, wills, notarial records.
  Data collected from archives and analyzed statistically.

  KEY PRACTITIONERS:
  Pierre Chaunu: quantitative history of Atlantic trade.
  Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie: peasant societies via serial data.
  François Furet: French Revolution via quantitative analysis.

  LE ROY LADURIE (Peasants of Languedoc, 1966):
  Agrarian history of Languedoc 1300-1700 using serial data.
  Malthusian oscillation: population growth -> land pressure ->
  subsistence crisis -> population collapse -> new growth.
  "History that stands still" — long-run stability despite apparent change.

  MICROHISTORY (adjacent but distinct):
  Montaillou (Le Roy Ladurie, 1975): the Inquisition's interrogation records
  of a Cathar village in 14th-century Languedoc.
  Rich in individual voices; not serial but qualitative and narrative.
  Bridge from serial history to the cultural turn.
```

### The History of Mentalités

```
  MENTALITÉS (mental worlds, collective mentalities):
  How did people in the past think, feel, perceive, and imagine?
  Not elite philosophy but collective worldviews.

  PIONEERS:
  Bloch: the psychology of feudal society.
  Febvre: the "mental tools" of different periods.
  Philippe Ariès: attitudes toward death (The Hour of Our Death, 1977),
  childhood (Centuries of Childhood, 1960).

  KEY WORKS:
  Georges Duby: the age of cathedrals; medieval marriage; warrior society.
  Jacques Le Goff: medieval civilization; time, money, and medieval mentality.
  Robert Mandrou: popular culture in early modern France.

  ATTITUDES TOWARD DEATH (Ariès):
  Medieval: "tamed death" — familiar, accepted, public ritual.
  17th-18th C: "one's own death" — individualized, feared.
  19th C: "thy death" — grief for the other, bereavement rituals.
  20th C: "forbidden death" — hidden, medicalized, denied.

  CHILDHOOD (Ariès):
  Medieval period had no concept of "childhood" as separate phase.
  Children were treated as small adults.
  Childhood as a distinct life stage is a modern construction.
  (CONTESTED: critics argue Ariès's evidence is fragmentary.)
```

## Generation 4: Cultural Turn and Microhistory

```
  CULTURAL TURN (1980s-90s):
  Influenced by: Clifford Geertz, Victor Turner (anthropology);
  Derrida, Foucault (poststructuralism).
  Shift from structure to culture, from statistics to interpretation.

  "THICK DESCRIPTION" (Geertz):
  The task is to interpret the meaning of cultural practices.
  Not to explain behavior by causal laws but to understand meanings.
  Geertz's analysis of the Balinese cockfight: it's about status, not just money.

  ROGER CHARTIER:
  "Cultural history of the social."
  Practices and representations.
  History of reading: how texts were appropriated differently in different
  social contexts.

  MICROHISTORY (Italian school: Ginzburg, Levi, Revel):
  Intensive study of a small case (person, village, event) to reveal
  broader historical structures.
  Carlo Ginzburg, The Cheese and the Worms (1976):
  Menocchio the miller: his cosmological views from Inquisition records.
  Reveals a popular culture independent of elite and church culture.

  TENSIONS:
  Quantitative Annales vs. cultural Annales.
  Structure vs. experience.
  Statistical pattern vs. individual voice.
  By 2000: both coexist as complementary approaches.
```

## Total History: Program and Critique

```
  THE PROGRAM OF TOTAL HISTORY:
  "History that takes into account all human activities."
  Combine economics, demography, geography, anthropology, psychology.
  No aspect of human life is outside history's scope.

  WHAT IT EXCLUDED (initially):
  Politics (considered superficial epiphenomenon).
  Events (surface foam; what matters is structures).
  Individuals (replaced by collective patterns).
  Narrative (replaced by analysis).

  THE RETURN OF:
  Political history (Roger Chartier, Keith Michael Baker: politics
  through culture, not just events).
  Events (Nora's Les Lieux de Mémoire: how events become memory).
  Individuals (microhistory: the individual as entry to the collective).
  Narrative (Paul Ricoeur: narrative is how we understand time).

  CONTEMPORARY VERDICT:
  Total history as aspiration remains valid.
  The specific prescriptions (anti-narrative, anti-event) were too rigid.
  The structural/cultural hybrid is the working mode of most social historians.
```

## Engineering Bridge: Structural Analysis and Systems Thinking

The Annales school's methodological intervention maps directly onto the transition from anecdotal to structural reasoning in any analytical discipline:

**Annales' anti-narrative stance → postmortem culture.** Traditional history focused on events and decisions: "the king declared war." Annales asked: what structural pressures (economic cycles, demographic stress, fiscal limits) made war likely regardless of who decided? Engineering postmortems make the same move: not "the engineer pushed bad code" but "what structural properties of the deployment pipeline made this failure mode possible?" The event is surface foam; the structural cause is what matters for prevention.

**Braudel's three temporal levels → system stability layers.** The three levels map directly onto infrastructure change rates:
- *Longue durée* (barely moves) → hardware constraints, physical laws, fundamental protocol choices (TCP/IP, the von Neumann architecture)
- *Conjuncture* (decade-scale cycles) → platform generations (cloud, mobile, AI wave), organizational structures, technology stacks
- *Événements* (fast, ephemeral) → individual commits, incidents, feature releases, A/B tests

A platform migration decision made at the conjuncture level looks like an event but is actually responding to longue-durée pressures (Moore's law plateauing, new physics of GPU compute). Braudel's framework prevents conflating timescales: an event-driven analysis would explain cloud adoption by individual company decisions; a structural analysis asks what economic and technical conditions made cloud adoption overdetermined regardless of who decided first.

**Febvre's "outillage mental" → Kuhn's paradigms → your tech stack.** Febvre argued you cannot think outside the available mental toolkit of your era — you cannot be an atheist in the 16th century because the conceptual apparatus doesn't exist yet. This is structurally identical to Kuhn's paradigms and to the tech-stack constraint: you cannot build serverless-first architecture if your mental toolkit is mainframe batch processing. The outillage mental of a team trained on relational databases shapes what architectural patterns are even conceivable to them, independent of technical possibility.

**Serial history → time-series analysis and telemetry.** The Annales third generation's serial history — systematic collection and statistical analysis of long data series (prices, births, deaths, harvests) — is the historical form of operational telemetry. The question "what are the long-run structural patterns in this data?" is the historian's version of anomaly detection and capacity planning. Le Roy Ladurie's Malthusian oscillation (population grows → resources strained → crisis → collapse → recovery) is a feedback loop with a well-specified causal structure — exactly the kind of model you'd build for a capacity planning system.

## Common Confusion Points

**The Annales school is not homogeneous.**
Generations 1-4 have very different emphases. Bloch and Febvre would not recognize
(and might disown) the purely quantitative third generation. Le Roy Ladurie's serial
history is quite different from Ginzburg's microhistory.

**Annales was not exclusively French.**
The school had massive international influence. German social history (Gesellschaftsgeschichte,
Bielefeld school: Wehler, Kocka) was strongly influenced by Annales. So was British
social history and Italian microhistory.

**Mentalités are not the same as ideology.**
Mentalités are the unconscious mental frameworks of an era — not explicit beliefs or
political programs. They change very slowly (like the longue durée) and are shared
across social classes. Ideology (in Marxist sense) is class-specific and political.

**Braudel's capitalism is not the standard economic definition.**
For Braudel, capitalism is specifically the non-market layer: monopoly, state-merchant
alliance, long-distance trade in luxury goods. This is idiosyncratic but historically
productive. It should not be confused with the market economy.
