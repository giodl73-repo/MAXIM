# Material Culture and the History of Things

## The Big Picture

Material culture history studies the physical objects, artifacts, environments, and substances
that humans make, use, and inhabit. Things are not just passive products of society — they shape
human practice, embody social relations, and carry meanings. The "material turn" in the 1990s-2000s
brought objects back to the center of historical analysis.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Marcel Mauss: The Gift

```
  MAUSS (The Gift, 1925):
  Cross-cultural analysis of gift exchange in non-market societies.

  THE PUZZLE:
  In many societies, gifts create obligations: you must receive a gift,
  you must give in return, you must give more than you received.
  Why? What force compels reciprocity?

  THE HAUA:
  Maori concept: the spiritual force that inhabits an object.
  A gift carries something of the giver; it must be returned to its source.

  MAUSS'S ANALYSIS:
  Gift exchange is a "total social fact": it is simultaneously economic,
  religious, legal, aesthetic. It binds social relations.
  Failure to reciprocate is a social rupture.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  "Pure gift" (one-way, no return expected) is culturally rare and special.
  Market exchange (impersonal commodity exchange) is a historical exception,
  not the anthropological baseline.
  Gift exchange underpins reciprocity, alliance, social solidarity.

  APPLICATIONS:
  Potlatch (Northwest Coast): competitive destruction of wealth.
  Kula ring (Melanesia): ceremonial exchange of armbands and necklaces.
  Christmas gift-giving as residual gift economy in market society.
```

### Arjun Appadurai: The Social Life of Things

```
  APPADURAI (The Social Life of Things, 1986):
  Objects have "social lives" — biographies of use, exchange, and meaning.

  THE COMMODITY:
  A thing is a commodity only when in a particular phase of its life.
  The same object can be: a commodity (for sale), a gift, an heirloom,
  a sacred object, a trophy — at different times.

  "REGIMES OF VALUE":
  Different social contexts have different standards for evaluating objects.
  What makes something valuable, appropriate, sacred, polluting.

  SINGULARIZATION:
  Commodities can be "singularized" — removed from the commodity
  sphere and treated as unique, inalienable.
  Museum objects: bought, but then singularized (no longer for sale).
  Wedding rings: bought in market, then inalienable.

  TOURNAMENT OF VALUE:
  Special occasions where value itself is contested and defined.
  Potlatch, kula, auction houses — not routine exchange but social drama.

  ENTANGLEMENT (more recent: Hodder, 2012):
  Humans are entangled in things — we depend on them and they shape us.
  The internet as total entanglement: we can't function without it.
```

## Commodity History

### Following the Thing

```
  "FOLLOW THE THING" METHODOLOGY:
  Trace a commodity from production through exchange to consumption.
  Reveal the hidden human relations behind the finished product.

  CLASSIC EXAMPLES:

  SUGAR (Sidney Mintz, Sweetness and Power, 1985):
  Sugar transformed from luxury spice to daily staple in Britain 1650-1900.
  Production: Caribbean slave plantations.
  Distribution: Atlantic trade system.
  Consumption: working-class caloric supplement; social ritual (tea + sugar).
  Sugar's trajectory reveals: slavery, capitalism, empire, diet, time-discipline.

  COTTON:
  Sven Beckert (Empire of Cotton, 2014):
  Global history of cotton 1780-1900.
  Manchester's mills depended on:
  Slave-grown US cotton -> raw material.
  Enslaved and colonized markets -> consumption.
  The "war capitalism" of slavery and cotton preceded industrial capitalism.

  SPICES (Jack Turner, Spice, 2004):
  European desire for spice drove the Age of Exploration.
  Spice as luxury, preservative, medicine, prestige.
  The spice trade built the first global economy.

  COD (Mark Kurlansky, Cod, 1997):
  Atlantic cod fisheries and the making of New England, Canada.
  Ecological collapse and the end of a civilization built on one fish.
```

### Consumer Culture

```
  CONSUMER REVOLUTION (Neil McKendrick, 1982):
  18th-century England saw a "consumer revolution" preceding industrialization.
  Rising incomes -> demand for manufactured goods.
  Fashion, emulation, social aspiration drove consumption.
  Josiah Wedgwood: proto-modern marketing and branding of ceramics.

  DEBATE:
  Grant McCracken: consumption has very long history (not just 18th C).
  Colin Campbell: Protestant ethic transformed into "romantic ethic" of
  pleasure-seeking consumption.
  Jan de Vries: "industrious revolution" — households worked more to consume more.

  DEPARTMENT STORES AND MODERN CONSUMPTION:
  Michael Miller (The Bon Marché, 1981): Paris department store as
  modern consumption institution.
  Fixed prices (no bargaining), display, the female consumer as primary target.
  Zola's Au Bonheur des Dames (1883) fictionalized this.

  VEBLEN (The Theory of the Leisure Class, 1899):
  "Conspicuous consumption": display of wealth through ostentatious spending.
  "Conspicuous leisure": demonstrating that you don't have to work.
  Upper-class consumption patterns percolate down (trickle-down emulation).
```

## Technology as Material Culture

```
  HISTORY OF TECHNOLOGY:
  Not just "great inventions" but the social embedding of technologies.

  RUTH SCHWARTZ COWAN (More Work for Mother, 1983):
  Domestic technology and women's labor.
  Did labor-saving devices save labor?
  Answer: No. They shifted labor, changed expectations, and
  often INCREASED women's housework.
  Washing machines: previously sent laundry out or servants did it.
  Now housewives did all laundry themselves, more frequently.
  Standards of cleanliness rose to match new capacities.

  THOMAS HUGHES (Networks of Power, 1983):
  Electric power systems as "large technical systems."
  The "reverse salient" concept: bottleneck in a large system
  attracts invention to solve it.
  Edison's system: not just the lightbulb but a whole infrastructure.
  Technological style: American vs. German electrification differed
  because of different institutional contexts.

  AFFORDANCES (Gibson, applied to technology):
  Objects offer possibilities for use to those with the right skills.
  A hammer affords hammering; a chair affords sitting.
  Technology history: what uses did technologies enable, constrain, invite?

  BICYCLE AND GENDER:
  The safety bicycle (1880s): transformed women's mobility.
  Rational dress reform: women couldn't ride in corsets and long skirts.
  Social anxiety: cycling = women's independence = threat to gender norms.
  Susan B. Anthony: "I think [the bicycle] has done more to emancipate women
  than anything else in the world."
```

## Material Culture of Everyday Life

### Clothing and Dress

```
  HISTORY OF DRESS:
  Not fashion history (the spectacular) but the social history of clothing.

  SUMPTUARY LAWS:
  Medieval and early modern laws restricting who could wear what.
  Silk, furs, specific colors (purple = royalty) reserved for specific ranks.
  Revealed: clothing as status marker; social anxiety about status mobility.

  LIVERY AND IDENTIFICATION:
  Servants wore their master's livery (identifying clothing).
  Slaves branded and clothed to mark status.
  Military uniform: discipline, identification, esprit de corps.

  INDUSTRIALIZATION AND DRESS:
  Ready-to-wear clothing (19th century): democratization of fashion.
  But also: factory workers in uniform poverty.
  Second-hand clothing markets: vast informal economy.

  DANIEL ROCHE (The Culture of Clothing, 1994):
  Clothing in 18th-century France from inventories after death.
  How many items of clothing did people own? What kinds?
  Reveals: social stratification, economic change, cultural values.
```

### Food Culture

```
  HISTORY OF FOOD:
  What did people eat? Where did food come from? What did it mean?

  MINTZ (Sweetness and Power): sugar as case study (see above).
  FERNAND BRAUDEL (Volume I, Material Civilization): food and drink globally.

  MASSIMO MONTANARI (Food is Culture, 2004):
  Food is a cultural product, not a natural fact.
  Even what counts as "edible" is culturally defined.
  Cuisine = a system of values expressed through food choices.

  HISTORICAL TURNING POINTS:
  Columbian Exchange: American crops (potato, maize, tomato) transformed
  European and global food systems.
  Industrial food (19th-20th C): canning, refrigeration, factory farming.
  Processed food and its social consequences.

  ANTHROPOLOGICAL APPROACHES:
  Mary Douglas (Purity and Danger): food taboos as boundary maintenance.
  Lévi-Strauss: raw vs. cooked as fundamental cultural coding.
  "The Culinary Triangle": raw, cooked, rotten — universal structure.
```

## Museum Studies and Material Culture

```
  MUSEUMS AS MATERIAL CULTURE INSTITUTIONS:
  How have objects been collected, classified, displayed, and narrated?

  EILEAN HOOPER-GREENHILL (Museums and the Shaping of Knowledge):
  The museum as a knowledge-producing institution.
  The "cabinet of curiosities" -> the modern museum: different epistemic regimes.
  Display choices are not neutral: they produce knowledge about "other" cultures.

  REPATRIATION DEBATES:
  Elgin Marbles (British Museum vs. Greece).
  Benin Bronzes (many museums vs. Nigeria).
  Native American remains and objects (NAGPRA, US federal law, 1990).
  The debate: universal museum vs. cultural property.
  Who has the right to objects made in or taken from colonial contexts?

  IVAN KARP AND STEVEN LAVINE (Exhibiting Cultures, 1991):
  Museums represent cultures — but whose culture? In whose terms?
  "Primitive art" exhibitions: African objects classified by Western aesthetic categories.
  The frame (museum building, label, narrative) shapes how objects are seen.
```

## Appadurai's Exchange Modes at a Glance

| Mode | Mechanism | Social Function | Examples | What Makes It Distinct |
|------|-----------|-----------------|----------|------------------------|
| **Commodity exchange** | Anonymous, impersonal market transaction; price set by supply/demand | Efficient allocation; no lasting social bond created | Buying bread, trading stocks, paying SaaS subscription fees | Social relations between producers and consumers are hidden; "commodity fetishism" |
| **Gift exchange** | Personal transfer with obligation of reciprocity; the gift carries something of the giver | Creates and maintains social bonds; establishes hierarchy and alliance | Christmas gifts, wedding presents, potlatch, kula ring exchanges | Cannot be purely calculated; calculation violates the social meaning; reciprocity is obligatory |
| **Singularization** | Object removed from the commodity sphere; treated as unique and inalienable | Marks sacred, heritage, or personal significance; removes from circulation | Museum objects, wedding rings, inherited heirlooms, donated organs | The same physical object shifts register; singularization is a social act, not an intrinsic property |

Key: the same object can move between all three modes. A ring is a commodity (in the jewelry store), becomes a gift (at the wedding), and is then singularized (inalienable; not resellable without social meaning-destruction).

## Engineering Bridge: The Social Life of Software Artifacts

Appadurai's framework — objects shift between commodity, gift, and singularized modes depending on social context — describes exactly how software artifacts move through their lifecycle:

**The social life of a library.**
- *Commodity phase*: published on npm/PyPI/NuGet; has a price (free or paid), a version, a changelog. It is a commodity in Appadurai's sense — exchanged in an anonymous market transaction.
- *Dependency phase*: imported into your project. Now it is a "gift" in a modified sense: you have a relationship with the maintainer community; you may contribute issues, PRs, or documentation. The artifact is embedded in social relations.
- *Legacy phase*: still in your codebase, no longer updated, depended on by too much code to remove without major risk. Now it is "singularized" — it has been removed from the commodity sphere (you cannot simply upgrade or replace it) and has become an inalienable part of your architecture. Its meaning is now its history in your system, not its market value.

**"Follow the thing" and supply chain security.** The commodity chain methodology (trace sugar from Caribbean slave plantation → Atlantic trade → British working-class tea ritual) is exactly what software supply chain security requires: trace a dependency from its author → its CI/CD pipeline → its registry publication → your `package.json` → your production container. SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) is "follow the thing" applied to software artifacts. Log4Shell became a crisis precisely because most organizations did not know where Log4j appeared in their supply chain.

**Singularization and technical debt.** Once a system component is heavily depended upon, it undergoes singularization: it can no longer be treated as a replaceable commodity (upgrade, swap, deprecate). Its "social life" in the codebase — the other systems entangled with it, the institutional memory attached to it — makes it inalienable in Appadurai's sense. Technical debt is partly the accumulation of singularized components.

**Mauss's gift exchange and open-source contribution.** OSS contribution is structurally a gift economy: you give time and code with an implicit expectation of reciprocity (recognition, influence, community membership), not a contractual exchange. Violations of gift economy norms (demanding features without contributing, taking forks without attribution) generate exactly the social rupture Mauss describes when gift obligations are violated. OSS burnout often occurs when maintainers discover they are operating a gift economy while users treat them as a commodity provider.

**Veblen's conspicuous consumption and developer tooling.** Technology choices in engineering organizations often signal status as much as they solve problems. Adopting Kubernetes before your scale justifies it, using Rust for a CRUD API, over-engineering with microservices — these exhibit Veblen's conspicuous consumption: the visible complexity demonstrates technical sophistication and membership in a status community, independent of whether it maximizes utility. Understanding this dynamic explains why "obviously simpler" solutions often lose to more elaborate ones.

## Common Confusion Points

**Material culture history is not the same as archaeology.**
Archaeology recovers and analyzes physical remains of the past. Material culture history
asks how objects were used, exchanged, and given meaning within social contexts.
They overlap (historians use archaeological evidence) but have different primary questions.

**Commodity fetishism (Marx) and fetishism (anthropology) are different.**
Marx: commodity fetishism = the social relations between producers appear as relations
between things (the commodity's value seems intrinsic). Anthropological fetishism = objects
are believed to have spiritual power. Appadurai explicitly deploys Marx's concept.

**"Consumer revolution" does not mean everyone became consumers.**
McKendrick's argument about 18th-century England was about the spread of consumption
to middling and working people (not just elites). But vast numbers remained in poverty.
The "revolution" was relative, not absolute.

**"Follow the thing" is a method, not a claim about commodity determination.**
Tracing a commodity doesn't mean the commodity causes history. It is a method for
making visible the human relations (production, exchange, consumption) that are
otherwise hidden behind the finished product.
