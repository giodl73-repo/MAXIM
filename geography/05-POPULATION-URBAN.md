# Population & Urban Geography

## The Big Picture

Demography is destiny — population size, age structure, and distribution shape
everything from labor markets to geopolitical power. The demographic transition
model describes where every country has been or is heading. Urbanization is the
dominant spatial process of the 21st century — more people now live in cities
than outside them for the first time in human history. Understanding Zipf's Law,
megacity formation, and the mechanics of migration ties human geography directly
to economic and political geography.

## Section 1: Demographic Transition Model

```
  THE DTM (Thompson 1929, Notestein 1945):
  A theoretical model of population change as societies industrialize

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ STAGE 1: Pre-industrial                                        │
  │ High birth rate + High death rate → stable, low population     │
  │ High infant mortality; disease/famine/war → high CDR           │
  │ Examples: no contemporary examples; historical hunter-gatherers│
  │                                                                │
  │ STAGE 2: Early industrial / sanitary revolution                │
  │ Death rate drops (sanitation, medicine, food security)         │
  │ Birth rate remains high → POPULATION EXPLOSION                 │
  │ Examples: early 20th C Europe; most of sub-Saharan Africa now  │
  │ Sub-Saharan Africa: CDR fallen sharply; CBR still high         │
  │                                                                │
  │ STAGE 3: Late industrial                                       │
  │ Birth rate begins to fall (education, urbanization,            │
  │ women's rights, child labor laws, social security)             │
  │ Still population growth but slowing                            │
  │ Examples: India, Mexico, much of Latin America, SE Asia        │
  │                                                                │
  │ STAGE 4: Post-industrial / developed                           │
  │ Low birth rate + Low death rate → stable/slow growth           │
  │ TFR near replacement (2.1 in developed world)                  │
  │ Examples: USA, Australia, W Europe, China (now)                │
  │                                                                │
  │ STAGE 5: Post-transition decline                               │
  │ TFR below replacement (<2.1) → population decline without      │
  │ immigration                                                    │
  │ Examples: Japan (TFR 1.2), Germany, South Korea (TFR 0.72!),   │
  │ Italy, Russia                                                  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY METRICS:
  CBR (Crude Birth Rate): births per 1000 population
  CDR (Crude Death Rate): deaths per 1000 population
  TFR (Total Fertility Rate): avg children per woman over lifetime
    Replacement TFR: ~2.1 in developed; ~2.3 in high-mortality countries
  NIR (Natural Increase Rate): CBR − CDR
  RNI (Rate of Natural Increase): NIR/10 = % per year

  DEMOGRAPHIC DIVIDEND:
  As Stage 2→3 transition occurs, large working-age cohort + small
  dependent cohort → high labor supply + savings rate
  → Window of accelerated economic growth (E Asian "miracle" partly
    demographic dividend, 1960s-1990s)
  Sub-Saharan Africa: potential dividend if birth rates fall + youth
  employed; or demographic burden if employment not created
```

## Section 2: Global Population Projections

```
  UN MEDIUM VARIANT (2022 revision):
  Current: ~8.1 billion (2024)
  2050: ~9.7 billion
  2100: ~10.4 billion (plateau in medium variant)
  High variant: 12.3B by 2100
  Low variant: 8.9B by 2100
  → Huge uncertainty driven by Sub-Saharan Africa TFR trajectory

  REGIONAL DIVERGENCE:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Sub-Saharan Africa: 1.2B → ~3.9B by 2100 (medium)            │
  │ South Asia: peaked; India already = China ~1.4B                │
  │ E Asia: China declining; Japan already shrinking               │
  │ Europe: stable/declining without immigration                   │
  │ N America: stable (immigration-driven)                         │
  │ Latin America: slowing; several countries near Stage 4         │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  AGING SOCIETIES:
  Dependency ratio: (children <15 + elderly >65) / working age (15-64)
  Old-age dependency: growing rapidly in Stage 4/5 countries
  Japan: ~48 elderly per 100 workers (2023) → projected 60+ by 2050
  Implications: pension system stress, healthcare costs, labor shortages
  Solutions: immigration, higher TFR (hard to engineer), automation,
    older retirement age, female labor force participation

  SOUTH KOREA CASE:
  TFR 0.72 (2023) — lowest ever recorded for major country
  Causes: extreme housing costs, career culture, gender inequality,
    competitive education system
  → Population already declining; military recruitment gap
  → Government incentives (cash payments) have had minimal effect
```

## Section 3: Urbanization

```
  GLOBAL URBANIZATION TREND:
  1950: ~30% urban
  2007: first year >50% urban (milestone)
  2024: ~57% urban
  2050: ~68% projected urban
  → Fastest urbanization in Africa and Asia (from low base)

  URBANIZATION-DEVELOPMENT CORRELATION:
  Strong positive correlation between urbanization % and income/HDI
  Causation debate: does urbanization cause development, or is it a symptom?
  Evidence for urbanization → growth: agglomeration economies,
    labor market matching, knowledge spillovers, infrastructure efficiency
  Urbanization without industrialization (Africa): urbanization from
    rural push (poverty/conflict) without industrial jobs → informal economy

  URBAN ECUMENE:
  Major population clusters:
  E Asia (Pearl River Delta, Yangtze Delta, Beijing-Tianjin,
          Tokyo-Osaka mega-corridor)
  S Asia (Ganges Plain, Mumbai, Karachi)
  Europe (NW European megalopolis, Rhine-Ruhr)
  E North America (BosWash corridor)
  SE Asia (Java-Bali, Bangkok, Manila)
```

## Section 4: City Size Distribution & Internal Structure

```
  BRIDGE — ZIPF'S LAW AS DOMAIN-INVARIANT POWER LAW:
  The rank-size rule for cities (rank × size ≈ constant) is the same
  mathematical structure as Zipf's Law for word frequencies in natural
  language, income distributions (Pareto 80/20), internet traffic
  (Ethernet frame size distribution), and firm size distributions.
  All share the log-log linear signature: ln(size) = -z × ln(rank) + C.
  The exponent z ≈ 1.0 for US cities (nearly perfect Zipf); deviates
  in primate city systems (z > 1; one city dominates) or uniform systems
  (z < 1; flatter distribution). Mechanistic explanation: Gabaix (1999)
  showed that random multiplicative growth (each city grows at a random
  rate each period, independently) generates a Zipf distribution as the
  stationary distribution — the same argument as random multiplicative
  processes generating log-normal or power-law distributions in physics.
  The invariance of z across countries and historical periods suggests
  the mechanism is deep — not a policy artifact.

  ZIPF'S LAW FOR CITIES:
  Rank × Size ≈ constant
  → 2nd largest city ≈ 1/2 size of largest
  → 3rd largest ≈ 1/3, etc.
  Power law distribution (log-log linear relationship)
  Empirically robust in most large, developed economies (USA, EU)
  Mechanism: unknown; potentially random multiplicative growth (Gabaix)

  URBAN PRIMACY:
  Many countries have ONE city dramatically larger than #2
  Primates emerge where: historical colonialism concentrated urban functions
    in a single port; small country; centralized government
  Examples: Paris (10× Lyon?), Bangkok (25× Chiang Mai), Jakarta, Cairo
  US: unusual — polycentric (NY, LA, Chicago, Houston roughly power law)

  URBAN INTERNAL STRUCTURE MODELS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ BURGESS CONCENTRIC ZONE (1925):                                │
  │ CBD → transition zone (factory/blight) → working class →       │
  │   middle class → commuter zone                                 │
  │ Based on Chicago 1920s; invasion-succession dynamics           │
  │ Limitation: oversimplified; city grows in all directions       │
  │                                                                │
  │ HOYT SECTOR MODEL (1939):                                      │
  │ Cities grow in sectors along transportation axes               │
  │ High-income sector extends from CBD along best route           │
  │ Better fits pre-freeway cities with radial rail                │
  │                                                                │
  │ HARRIS-ULLMAN MULTIPLE NUCLEI (1945):                          │
  │ Multiple centers of activity (CBD + industrial district +      │
  │   suburb + airport) → decentralized structure                  │
  │ Better fits post-WWII auto-oriented US cities                  │
  │                                                                │
  │ CONTEMPORARY: POLYCENTRIC SPRAWL                               │
  │ Edge cities (Garreau 1991): suburban nodes with office/retail  │
  │ Tyson's Corner VA, Schaumburg IL, Plano TX                     │
  │ Tech corridors: Route 128 MA, Silicon Valley (polycentric)     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  GENTRIFICATION:
  Higher-income residents displace lower-income in urban neighborhoods
  Mechanisms: artists → creative class → development → luxury → displacement
  Causes: amenity value of urban living, transit access, school quality
  → Housing costs rise; longtime residents priced out
  Evidence on effects: debated; displacement vs investment/improvement

  INFORMAL SETTLEMENTS (SLUMS):
  1B+ people in informal housing worldwide (UN-Habitat)
  Characteristics: insecure tenure, inadequate infrastructure, overcrowding
  Governance challenges: services delivery, disaster vulnerability, crime
  Examples: Dharavi (Mumbai), Kibera (Nairobi), Rocinha (Rio), Ciudad Neza (Mexico City)
  Trend: proportionally declining but absolute numbers stable as cities grow
```

  TOP MEGACITIES:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

## Section 6: Migration

```
  PUSH-PULL FRAMEWORK:
  PUSH factors (origin): violence, poverty, environmental stress,
    land degradation, political persecution, discrimination
  PULL factors (destination): economic opportunity, higher wages,
    family connections, services, safety

  RAVENSTEIN'S LAWS (1885, still largely valid):
  1. Most migrants travel short distances
  2. Migration proceeds step by step (rural → small town → city)
  3. Major streams have counter-streams
  4. Urban residents less migratory than rural
  5. Economic motives dominate

  TYPES OF MIGRATION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ INTERNAL: within country                                       │
  │ Rural-urban: single largest flow globally (~200M/decade)       │
  │ China's rural-urban: 300M+ moved 1980-2015 (largest in history)│
  │ Seasonal/circular migration                                    │
  │                                                                │
  │ INTERNATIONAL: between countries                               │
  │ Economic migrants: ~260M total international migrants (2020)   │
  │ Refugees + asylum seekers: ~100M+ displaced (2022 record)      │
  │ Climate migration: growing, often overlaps with poverty        │
  │                                                                │
  │ FORCED vs VOLUNTARY: blurry in practice                        │
  │ Conflict + poverty + climate → compound drivers                │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  REMITTANCES:
  Money sent by migrants to home country
  $860B (2023) — largest since 2020 record
  > Foreign Direct Investment for most low-income countries
  > Official Development Assistance (ODA) 3-4×
  Critical for El Salvador (24% GDP), Nepal (27%), Tonga (50%)!
  Countercyclical: migrants send more during home country crises

  BRAIN DRAIN vs BRAIN GAIN:
  Brain drain: high-skilled emigration from developing countries
    (doctors, engineers, IT workers) → reduces destination's benefit
  Brain gain: skills acquired abroad may return; diaspora networks
  Brain circulation: optimal — skilled workers move between countries

  CLIMATE MIGRATION:
  ~216M internal climate migrants projected by 2050 (World Bank 2021)
  "Climate refugee" has no legal status under 1951 Refugee Convention
  (which covers persecution but not climate)
  Most movement: within-country, to secondary cities — not cross-continental
  Sea level threat: Bangladesh, Maldives, Pacific Islands (existential)
```

## Section 7: Urban Heat Islands & Urban Environment

```
  NOTE: Urban Heat Island is a cross-cutting environmental-urban topic
  linking the physical geography of cities (impervious surfaces, altered
  energy balance, reduced vegetation) to the human geography of health,
  energy demand, and inequality. It belongs with urban structure but
  operates through physical processes not captured in the demographic
  models above.

  URBAN HEAT ISLAND (UHI) EFFECT:
  Cities 1-3°C warmer than surrounding rural areas on average
  Peak difference at night (up to 10°C on calm, clear nights)

  MECHANISMS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Dark surfaces: asphalt + roofing absorb more solar radiation   │
  │ Reduced evapotranspiration: impervious surfaces + less vegetation│
  │ Urban geometry: "canyon" effect traps longwave radiation       │
  │ Waste heat: vehicles, A/C, industry, people                    │
  │ Reduced sky view factor: tall buildings reduce radiative cooling│
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  IMPACTS:
  Energy: A/C demand 2-5% higher per 1°C UHI
  Health: heat mortality concentrated in dense urban cores (2003 Paris
    heatwave: 14,800 deaths, disproportionately urban elderly)
  Water: reduced infiltration → increased stormwater runoff
  Biodiversity: altered urban ecology

  MITIGATION:
  Green roofs + urban trees: shade + evapotranspiration cooling
  Cool roofs (white/reflective): reduce absorption
  Permeable pavement: infiltration → evaporative cooling
  Urban parks: "park cool island" effect
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Stage 5 of DTM is not "the end"**: it's population decline, not extinction.
All European countries, Japan, S. Korea, and potentially China face this. The
question is how fast and whether immigration offsets natural decrease. Japan
has chosen to manage decline rather than open immigration — unique case study.

**Megacity ≠ dense city**: Tokyo-Yokohama at 37M spans a huge area; Paris inner
city is far denser per km². Megacity is a population threshold, not a density
measure. Some sprawling megacities have lower density than mid-sized European cities.

**"Climate refugee" has no legal status**: the 1951 Refugee Convention covers
persecution-based displacement. People fleeing sea level rise or drought have
no recognized legal protection under international law — a significant gap with
growing importance. Debate ongoing to extend Convention.

**Urbanization without industrialization is a distinct pattern**: European and
East Asian urbanization was driven by manufacturing jobs pulling people from farms.
African urbanization is increasingly driven by rural poverty pushing people out
without matching urban job creation — a different dynamic with different outcomes.
