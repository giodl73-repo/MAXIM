# Economic Geography

## The Big Picture

Economic geography asks why economic activity concentrates where it does and
what the implications of that concentration are for development, trade, and
inequality. Agglomeration economies explain why Silicon Valley persists despite
astronomical rents. World-systems theory explains why some places stay peripheral.
The container revolution explains modern trade geography. Supply chain
vulnerabilities exposed by COVID and Suez are now reshaping the map.

## Section 1: Location Theory

```
  VON THÜNEN (1826): Agricultural Land Use:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Model: single isolated city + uniform agricultural plain       │
  │ Insight: different crops will occupy different distance rings  │
  │ from the city based on land rent bid + transportation costs    │
  │                                                                │
  │ Rings (inner → outer):                                        │
  │ 1. Market gardening/dairy: perishable; high transport cost →   │
  │    must be near market                                        │
  │ 2. Forestry (19th C: firewood → wood heavy/cheap)             │
  │ 3. Intensive grain cultivation                                 │
  │ 4. Extensive grain + pasture                                   │
  │ 5. Three-field rotation (fallow)                               │
  │ 6. Livestock ranching                                          │
  │                                                                │
  │ Modern relevance: explains land use gradients around cities;  │
  │ urban fringe dynamics; "exurban" food production patterns;    │
  │ airport cargo proximity → perishable flower/produce industry  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ALFRED WEBER (1909): Industrial Location:
  Minimize total transport cost = (input materials to factory) +
                                    (output products to market)
  Material Index: weight of inputs / weight of outputs
  High MI (>1): locate near raw materials (steel, cement, sugar refining)
  Low MI (<1): locate near market (beer, bread — water added in production)
  MI = 1: locate anywhere on transport cost gradient

  Labor cost deviation: if labor savings > additional transport cost → move
  Agglomeration deviation: co-location benefits may override cost minimization

  CHRISTALLER CENTRAL PLACE THEORY (1933):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Goods/services have different RANGE (max distance people travel)│
  │ and THRESHOLD (min population needed to support it)            │
  │                                                                │
  │ High-order goods (jewelry, hospitals): long range, high thresh │
  │ Low-order goods (bread, haircuts): short range, low threshold  │
  │                                                                │
  │ Result: nested HIERARCHY of central places                    │
  │ Hamlets → Villages → Towns → Cities → Regional Centers        │
  │ Hexagonal market areas (closest packing without overlap)       │
  │                                                                │
  │ Empirical: retail geography generally confirms hierarchy;     │
  │   online commerce disrupting by extending effective range     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Section 2: Agglomeration Economies

```
  MARSHALL'S EXTERNAL ECONOMIES (1890):
  Co-location of similar firms generates external economies:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ LOCALIZATION (same-industry) ECONOMIES:                       │
  │ 1. Shared labor pool: workers with specialized skills;        │
  │    lower search costs; workers take career risks (can find    │
  │    new jobs nearby)                                           │
  │ 2. Specialized suppliers: upstream industries co-locate;       │
  │    parts/services available; lower inventory costs           │
  │ 3. Knowledge spillovers: ideas spread across firms; informal  │
  │    conversations at coffee shops; tacit knowledge diffuses    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ URBANIZATION (cross-industry) ECONOMIES:                      │
  │ Diverse city → cross-sector innovation; financial services;   │
  │ legal/accounting; transport infrastructure; thick labor market │
  │ across all occupations                                        │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE SILICON VALLEY PARADOX:
  Why does SV persist despite housing ~$2M median, wages far above
  alternatives, and remote work enabling dispersal?
  → Labor pool: deepest global pool of AI/semiconductor talent
  → VC infrastructure: Sand Hill Road network, deal flow
  → Knowledge spillovers: still significant even post-COVID
  → Path dependency: "agglomeration shadow" — hard to replicate

  INDUSTRIAL DISTRICTS (Marshallian or Italian):
  Flexible specialization clusters in N Italy, German Mittelstand,
  Danish/Swedish innovation districts
  Small/medium firms in same industry → collective efficiency
  Trust-based long-term relationships + shared institutions
  → Competitive even without economies of scale (quality/customization)

  AGGLOMERATION SHADOWS:
  Near major agglomeration → hard for smaller cities to compete
  Warsaw effect: much of Poland's talent concentrates in Warsaw
  Seoul effect: South Korea's regional inequality driven by
    agglomeration pulling from all provinces
```

## Section 3: New Economic Geography (Krugman 1991)

```
  CORE INSIGHTS (Krugman won 2008 Nobel Prize in Economics):

  WHY DOES MANUFACTURING CONCENTRATE?
  Two forces:
  CENTRIPETAL: increasing returns to scale (fixed costs → larger plants
    → lower unit cost) + labor market pooling + forward/backward linkages
    → Concentration pays
  CENTRIFUGAL: immobile factors (land, local services), congestion,
    commuting costs → dispersion pays

  WHEN DOES CONCENTRATION OCCUR?
  Low transport costs + strong scale economies → concentration (core forms)
  High transport costs → dispersed production (serve each local market)
  Medium transport costs → transition; tipping points possible

  CORE-PERIPHERY MODEL:
  Initial symmetric geography → random shock → one region captures
  manufacturing → cumulative causation (workers move to core →
  larger market → more manufacturing → more workers) → lock-in
  → Peripheral regions deindustrialize (path dependence)

  HISTORICAL APPLICATION:
  North/South US divide (manufacturing belt vs South)
  EU core (Germany/France) vs periphery (Portugal/Greece/Romania)
  China coastal manufacturing → interior still catching up
  British deindustrialization as manufacturing moved globally post-1970s
```

## Section 4: World Systems Theory

```
  WALLERSTEIN (1974): Capitalist World-System

  THREE ZONES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ CORE: capital-intensive production; high wages; high skill;    │
  │   strong state; extracts value from periphery                 │
  │   USA, W Europe, Japan, Australia                             │
  │                                                                │
  │ SEMI-PERIPHERY: transitional; both exploit and are exploited; │
  │   mix of core and periphery production                        │
  │   Brazil, China, India, Mexico, Turkey, E Europe              │
  │   (China's rapid ascent from periphery → semi → approaching  │
  │   core challenges Wallerstein's static framework)             │
  │                                                                │
  │ PERIPHERY: labor-intensive production; raw material extraction │
  │   low wages; weak state; subject to core terms of trade       │
  │   Sub-Saharan Africa, much of SE Asia historically,          │
  │   parts of Latin America                                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MECHANISMS OF EXTRACTION:
  Unequal exchange: peripheral labor embodied in commodities →
    exported at prices that don't reflect actual labor value
  Foreign direct investment: repatriates profits to core
  Debt trap: structural adjustment conditions (IMF/World Bank) →
    forced liberalization → export orientation maintains periphery status
  Terms of trade: declining relative price of commodities vs manufactures
    (Prebisch-Singer thesis) → primary exporters structurally disadvantaged

  CRITIQUES:
  Static: doesn't explain S Korea/Taiwan/China moving from periphery to core
  Agency: understates domestic institutional variation within zones
  Teleological: assumes system always reproduces itself

  WHAT SURVIVES:
  Smile curve dynamics (see below) confirm unequal value distribution
  Power asymmetries in international trade DO disadvantage commodity exporters
  Capital repatriation IS a significant transfer mechanism
```

## Section 5: Global Value Chains & the Smile Curve

```
  GLOBAL VALUE CHAINS (GVCs):
  Production fragmented across multiple countries, each specialized
  in one stage of value chain based on comparative advantage

  APPLE iPhONE — CANONICAL EXAMPLE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Design + R&D: California (US) — highest value                │
  │ Chip design: Apple Silicon, ARM (UK/US IP)                   │
  │ Memory chips: Samsung/SK Hynix (Korea)                       │
  │ Display: Samsung/LG/BOE (Korea/China)                        │
  │ Camera: Sony sensors (Japan)                                  │
  │ Rare Earth magnets: China                                    │
  │ Assembly: Foxconn/TSMC contract (China/Taiwan/India now)      │
  │ Software: iOS — California (US) — highest value             │
  │ Brand + marketing: California (US)                           │
  │ → Assembled in China for ~$5-10 labor value of $600 product  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SMILE CURVE (Stan Shih, Acer, 1992):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │         Value                                                  │
  │           ↑                                                   │
  │    High ──│─────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
  │           │ \                                           /     │
  │           │  \   R&D/                       Branding  /      │
  │           │   \  Design                     Marketing/       │
  │           │    \                            Services/        │
  │    Low  ──│─────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
  │           │         \                      /                  │
  │           │          \  Manufacturing     /                   │
  │           │           ────────────────────                    │
  │           +──────────────────────────────────→               │
  │                    Stage in value chain                       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Curve has DEEPENED since 1990s: manufacturing value share declining;
  intellectual property + brand value increasing
  → "Factory of the world" = low-margin commodity
  China's industrial policy goal: move up the smile curve (COMAC aircraft,
    Huawei 5G equipment, BYD EVs with own design) — moving up the curve
```

## Section 6: Special Economic Zones

```
  HISTORY:
  First modern SEZ: Shannon Free Zone, Ireland (1959)
  Key innovation: Shenzhen SEZ, China (1980)
  → Deng Xiaoping's laboratory for market reforms
  → Foreign investment + export-led manufacturing → explosive growth
  → Model for China's coastal development strategy
  → Now: 30M+ population, global tech hub (Huawei, Tencent, DJI)

  GLOBAL SPREAD:
  ~5,400 SEZs across 147 countries (UNCTAD 2022)
  Types: Export Processing Zones (EPZ), Free Trade Zones, Science Parks,
    Industrial Parks, Special Purpose Zones

  INCENTIVE PACKAGE (typical):
  Tax holidays (corporate income tax exemption 5-10 years)
  Duty-free imports of inputs
  Streamlined regulation (labor laws sometimes suspended)
  Infrastructure provision (roads, power, telecoms)
  FDI attraction → technology and management transfer

  SUCCESS FACTORS:
  Strong state capacity to manage zone (Singapore/Korea → success)
  Linkages to domestic economy (spillovers → development)
  Adequate infrastructure (power reliability crucial)
  Labor skills + political stability

  FAILURE FACTORS:
  Enclave economy: no links to domestic → "island of capitalism"
  Labor exploitation (lax laws attract race to bottom)
  Corruption in zone administration
  SSA zones: mostly enclave; limited development impact
```

## Section 7: Port Geography & Logistics

```
  THE CONTAINER REVOLUTION (McLean 1956):
  Malcolm McLean converted tanker to carry standardized containers
  ISO standard (TEU): 20-foot equivalent unit (~33 cubic meters)
  Effect: break-bulk cargo loading (manual) 1-2 tons/hour →
    container crane 25-40 BOXES/hour
  Cost of ocean shipping: ~1/10th of pre-container era per ton

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Enabled global fragmentation of production (GVCs)
  Made previously uncompetitive labor markets competitive (transport
    friction eliminated as trade barrier)
  Destroyed traditional port labor (longshoremen) worldwide
  Container ships now: 24,000 TEU capacity (Ever Given class)

  PORT HIERARCHY:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ TOP CONTAINER PORTS (TEU, 2022):                             │
  │ Shanghai: 47M TEU (world's busiest since 2010)               │
  │ Singapore: 38M TEU (transshipment hub; >100 shipping lines)  │
  │ Shenzhen (Yantian/Shekou): 30M TEU                           │
  │ Ningbo-Zhoushan: 33M TEU                                     │
  │ Rotterdam: 15M TEU (Europe's largest)                        │
  │ Los Angeles/Long Beach: 19M TEU (combined, US West Coast)    │
  │ Dubai (Jebel Ali): 14M TEU (dominant Middle East hub)       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  TRANSSHIPMENT HUBS vs GATEWAY PORTS:
  Gateway: serves its own hinterland (LA/Long Beach, Rotterdam)
  Transshipment hub: transfers between large vessels → feeder ships
    (Singapore, Dubai, Hong Kong, Colombo) — location matters: chokepoint
  Competitive advantage: deep water, efficiency, connectivity

  JUST-IN-TIME (JIT) VULNERABILITY:
  JIT minimizes inventory → supply chain has zero slack
  → Single disruption propagates immediately
  COVID 2020-2021: port congestion, chassis shortages, crew shortages
    → $30K+ container costs vs $1.5K normal
  Ever Given (Suez 2021): 6 days → months of delay as stuck ships
    backed up; $9.6B/day estimated impact
  Taiwan earthquake 2024: TSMC disruption → chip shortage amplified
  → Nearshoring/friendshoring trend: trade efficiency vs resilience tradeoff
```

## Section 8: Economic Corridors & Connectivity

```
  BELT AND ROAD INITIATIVE (BRI, China, 2013):
  $1T+ committed across 150+ countries
  Land corridors:
  CPEC: China-Pakistan Economic Corridor ($62B; Kashgar → Gwadar port)
  BCIM: Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar corridor
  Central Asia-China: Eurasian Land Bridge (China-Europe by rail)
  Maritime Silk Road: SE Asia → Indian Ocean → E Africa → Mediterranean

  GEOPOLITICAL READING:
  Debt trap concerns: Hambantota Port (Sri Lanka) leased to China 99 years
  Commercial reading: Chinese banks making commercial loans, not aid
  Infrastructure gap: LICs need $15T in infrastructure by 2040 (ADB)
  US/EU response: PGII (Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment)
    + EU Global Gateway → counter-BRI but far smaller committed funds

  ECONOMIC CORRIDOR THEORY:
  Linear infrastructure (road/rail/pipeline) connecting production zones
  → Reduces transport costs → enables agglomeration along corridor → development
  Evidence: mixed — corridors sometimes create islands without spillovers

  TOURISM GEOGRAPHY:
  International tourism: $1.5T (pre-COVID 2019); ~10% global GDP including
    indirect effects
  Mass tourism + CARRYING CAPACITY:
  Venice: 30M visitors/yr to city of 250K → resident flight, UNESCO threat
  Amsterdam: 20M/yr; city attempted tourist tax + dispersal strategies
  Overtourism responses: reservation systems, tourist caps, pricing

  COLD CHAIN GEOGRAPHY:
  Temperature-controlled logistics: $300B+ market
  Geographic implications: fresh food supply chains require airport
    proximity (flowers, seafood, fresh produce)
  Reefer (refrigerated) container share growing: 10% of container fleet
  Kenya flowers → Amsterdam → Europe (air cold chain via Nairobi hub)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Agglomeration doesn't mean the biggest city wins everything**: localization
economies work for specific industries. Silicon Valley is not better for auto
manufacturing than Detroit (was). Stuttgart is better for precision engineering
than London. The right agglomeration matters — not generic bigness.

**GVCs create value but also vulnerability**: geographic fragmentation gave us
cheap electronics but created critical dependencies. Taiwan TSMC produces ~90%
of leading-edge chips. This is agglomeration at global scale with geopolitical
implications that took 30 years of business efficiency logic to create.

**SEZs are not free trade zones**: they're politically controlled, incentive-rich
manufacturing platforms within otherwise protected economies. China's SEZs
coexisted with a tariff-protected domestic economy. The "free" in some names
is aspirational marketing, not open-border trade policy.

**The smile curve is not static**: China's industrial policy explicitly targets
moving up from manufacturing into R&D and branding. BYD (design + manufacture
+ sell EVs), Huawei (chip design + manufacturing + network systems), DJI (design
+ consumer brand) are deliberate moves up the smile curve. The question is how
fast and in which sectors.
