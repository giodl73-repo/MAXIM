# Biogeography

## The Big Picture

Biogeography asks why species are where they are. The answer requires two
histories: evolutionary (continental drift + speciation) and ecological
(climate, competition, dispersal). The Wallace Line is the canonical example
of continental drift's lasting signature. Island biogeography theory (MacArthur-
Wilson) gave us the quantitative framework that now drives conservation design.
Climate change is now rewriting species distributions in real time.

## Section 2: The Wallace Line

```
  ALFRED RUSSEL WALLACE (1860s):
  Naturalist collecting in Malay Archipelago observed abrupt fauna
  boundary between Bali-Lombok (just 35km apart) and Borneo-Sulawesi

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ORIENTAL (Indomalayan) side     │  AUSTRALASIAN side         │
  │  (Bali, Borneo, Java, Sumatra)   │  (Lombok, Sulawesi, etc.) │
  │                                  │                            │
  │  Tigers, rhinos, elephants       │  Marsupials                │
  │  Deer, pigs, monkeys             │  Cockatoos, megapodes      │
  │  Pheasants, woodpeckers          │  Monitor lizards (different)│
  │  Asian freshwater fish           │  Australian freshwater fish │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHY THE LINE OCCURS:
  Gondwana (southern supercontinent) broke up ~130 Ma
  Australia + New Guinea drifted away from Asia
  → Separate evolutionary lineages for tens of millions of years
  Deep ocean trench between Bali/Borneo (Sunda Shelf, exposed during
  glacial sea level drops) and Lombok/Sulawesi side (Sahul Shelf)
  → Never connected by land even at glacial maxima (120m sea level drop)
  → Even 35km of water = insurmountable barrier for most terrestrial fauna

  REFINEMENTS:
  Lydekker Line: slightly further east (Weber's line bisects Wallacea)
  Wallacea: transition zone between Wallace and Lydekker lines
    → Contains endemic species from both realms + unique regional ones
    (Sulawesi, Maluku, Nusa Tenggara) — high endemism, evolutionary mixing zone
  Weber's Line: balanced point where Oriental and Australian fauna equal

  MARSUPIALS ELSEWHERE:
  ~70 species in S America (opossums): Gondwana origin (South America once
  connected to Australia via Antarctica)
  Fossil marsupials in Europe and N Africa: confirm Gondwana/Laurasia dispersal
```

## Section 3: Continental Drift & Species Distributions

```
  VICARIANCE vs DISPERSAL:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ VICARIANCE: populations separated by geological event          │
  │ (continental drift, mountain uplift, sea level change)         │
  │ → Diverge in isolation → separate species                     │
  │ "Populations were split; evolution diverged in place"          │
  │                                                                │
  │ DISPERSAL: organisms cross barriers by their own movement,    │
  │ wind, ocean current, rafting, or human introduction           │
  │ "Organisms crossed the barrier from elsewhere"                │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  GONDWANAN DISTRIBUTIONS (classic vicariance examples):
  Ratite birds (ostrich/emu/rhea/cassowary/kiwi/moa): flightless;
    distributed across former Gondwana fragments
    → Vicariance: ancestral bird in Gondwana; continents drifted
    → But molecular evidence now suggests DISPERSAL (neoflightless)
    and sequence of drift events → ongoing debate

  Lungfish: South America, Africa, Australia only (Gondwana fragments)
  Protea plants: South Africa + Australia (Gondwana origin)
  Marsupials: S America + Australia (Gondwana, separated by Antarctica)

  BERING LAND BRIDGE (BERINGIA):
  Land connection N America-Asia during glacial maxima (sea level -120m)
  → Human migration ~15-20 ka BP
  → Mammoth, horse, camel dispersal (horse evolved N America → dispersed to
    Old World via Beringia → went extinct N America ~10 ka BP → returned
    with Spanish conquistadors 1493)
  → Wolf, brown bear, elk movement between realms
```

## Section 4: Island Biogeography Theory

```
  MacARTHUR & WILSON (1967): The Theory of Island Biogeography

  THE EQUILIBRIUM MODEL:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Species richness on an island = equilibrium between:          │
  │                                                                │
  │ IMMIGRATION RATE: from species pool on mainland               │
  │ → Decreases as more species already present (fewer new)       │
  │ → Higher rate on NEAR islands (shorter dispersal distance)    │
  │                                                                │
  │ EXTINCTION RATE: of species already on island                 │
  │ → Increases as more species (more competition, limited space)  │
  │ → Higher rate on SMALL islands (smaller populations → more    │
  │   vulnerable to stochastic extinction)                        │
  │                                                                │
  │ EQUILIBRIUM: where immigration rate = extinction rate          │
  │ → Turnover continues (different species at same richness)     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SPECIES-AREA RELATIONSHIP:
  S = cA^z
  S = species richness
  A = area
  c = constant (regional species pool)
  z = ~0.25-0.35 (empirically derived exponent)

  10× area increase → species richness approximately DOUBLES
  90% area loss → lose ~50% of species (slow, depends on z)

  EMPIRICAL VALIDATION:
  Simberloff & Wilson (1969): small mangrove islands in Florida
  fumigated (removed all arthropods); monitored recolonization
  → Species richness returned to predicted equilibrium levels
  → Confirmed immigration-extinction balance and area relationship

  SLOSS DEBATE (Single Large Or Several Small):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ For reserve design: one large reserve vs several small?      │
  │                                                              │
  │ One large (SL): lower extinction rate per unit area; avoids  │
  │   edge effects; supports large-range species                 │
  │ Several small (SS): more total edge/habitat diversity;        │
  │   insurance against catastrophe; may capture beta diversity  │
  │                                                              │
  │ Modern consensus: LARGE is generally better, BUT the best    │
  │ design depends on the species of concern, connectivity       │
  │ (corridors), and matrix habitat quality                      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CONSERVATION APPLICATIONS:
  Habitat fragmentation = island biogeography in terrestrial landscape
  Forest patches in agricultural matrix = islands in a sea
  → Minimum viable reserve size; corridor design
  IUCN uses species-area relationship to estimate extinction rates
```

## Section 5: Species Distributions & Niches

```
  HUTCHINSON'S N-DIMENSIONAL HYPERVOLUME (1957):
  Ecological niche = all combinations of environmental variables
  within which a species can maintain viable population

  FUNDAMENTAL NICHE: full range of conditions species can tolerate
  REALIZED NICHE: subset actually occupied due to competition/predation

  COMPETITIVE EXCLUSION PRINCIPLE (Gause 1934):
  Two species competing for identical resources cannot coexist
  → One competitively excludes the other (or they diverge — character displacement)

  CHARACTER DISPLACEMENT:
  Darwin's finches in Galápagos: co-occurring species have more
  different bill sizes than on islands where only one occurs
  → Competition drives divergence in resource use
```

## Section 6: Productivity & Net Primary Production

```
  NET PRIMARY PRODUCTIVITY (NPP):
  Rate of organic carbon fixation minus plant respiration
  Units: gC/m²/year

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ BIOME NPP RANKING (approximate):                               │
  │ Tropical rainforest: 1000-3500 gC/m²/yr                       │
  │ Tropical seasonal: 600-1500                                    │
  │ Temperate deciduous: 600-800                                   │
  │ Temperate grassland: 200-600                                   │
  │ Boreal forest: 200-500                                         │
  │ Desert: 10-200                                                 │
  │ Tundra: 10-400                                                 │
  │ Open ocean: 50-150 (but vast area → ~50% of global total)      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  DRIVERS: temperature × moisture (land); light × nutrients (ocean)
  NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index): satellite-derived
  proxy for vegetation density/greenness; ~0.9 tropical forest, ~0.1 desert

  GLOBAL GREENING TREND:
  NDVI increasing in many areas (~2000s satellite data)
  Drivers: CO₂ fertilization, warming, N deposition, land management
  NOT uniformly positive: tropical greening may coexist with
  biodiversity loss from savanna encroachment
```

## Section 7: Range Shifts & Climate Change

```
  DOCUMENTED RANGE SHIFTS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Mean shift: poleward 17 km/decade + upslope 11m/decade         │
  │ (Parmesan/Yohe 2003; meta-analysis of 1700 species)            │
  │                                                                │
  │ Species-specific variation: some faster, some stagnant         │
  │ → Dispersal ability, habitat connectivity, physiology all     │
  │   mediate response speed                                       │
  │                                                                │
  │ Examples:                                                      │
  │ UK butterflies: ranges shifted N ~50km since 1970s             │
  │ Treelines advancing upslope (Alps, Rockies, Scandinavia)       │
  │ Fish stocks: North Sea cod → northward; tropical reef fish in  │
  │   temperate waters (Mediterranean)                             │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PHENOLOGICAL MISMATCH:
  Timing shifts are also occurring (earlier spring, later autumn)
  Problem: different species respond at different rates
  Oak leafing vs winter moth emergence vs great tit breeding — if
  food peak (caterpillars) occurs before bird nesting, chicks starve
  → "Trophic mismatch" reduces reproductive success even without
    range change; already documented in multiple systems

  CLIMATE DEBT:
  Communities are not yet in equilibrium with current climate
  (lag due to long-lived organisms, dispersal limitations)
  → Even if warming stabilized today, extinctions would continue
    as slow-dispersers fail to track climate envelope
  Estimated: 20-40% of species already "committed to extinction"
    under current trajectories (but vast uncertainty)

  BIODIVERSITY HOTSPOTS:
  Myers (1988/2000): 36 regions with exceptional concentration of
  endemic species + exceptional threat (habitat loss)
  Cover ~2.5% of land area; home to ~50% of plant species, ~42% of
  vertebrate species as endemics
  Hotspots: SW Australia, Mediterranean Basin, Cape Floristic Region,
    Sundaland, Indo-Burma, Mesoamerica, Tropical Andes, Caribbean, etc.

  INVASIVE SPECIES:
  Successfully invasive species often share traits: phenotypic
  plasticity, r-selected (high reproduction), broad diet, high
  dispersal ability, no co-evolved enemies in new range
  Impacts: ~42% of threatened species on IUCN Red List affected by
  invasive species; ecological + economic costs
  Economic cost: ~$423 billion/year globally (Diagne et al. 2021)
  Examples: brown tree snake (Guam → eliminated 10 bird species);
    zebra mussel (Great Lakes); cane toad (Australia)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Biogeography Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Why are there no tigers in Africa? | Asian vs African faunal history — India plate collision + Indomalayan realm |
| Why do Australian mammals look so different? | Gondwana isolation 80-65 Ma → independent marsupial evolution |
| How many species should a 10× larger island have? | ~2× (species-area relationship, z~0.25-0.3) |
| Why is the Amazon so biodiverse? | Af climate + long geological stability + large area + refugia during glacials |
| What's the fastest way to lose species? | Habitat destruction (area loss) → island biogeography predicts immediate commitment to extinction |
| Why are range shifts not universal? | Dispersal ability + habitat connectivity + physiology all mediate; slow-dispersers create climate debt |

## Common Confusion Points

**Island biogeography doesn't predict static communities**: the equilibrium is
dynamic — species are constantly going locally extinct and being replaced by
new immigrants. The NUMBER stays constant; the IDENTITY changes. Turnover
is part of the theory, not a failure of it.

**High NPP ≠ high biodiversity**: the open ocean has ~50% of global NPP but
low per-area biodiversity. Tropical reefs have high biodiversity at low NPP.
Productivity explains species richness in some systems, not others; stability,
heterogeneity, and energy history also matter.

**Wallace Line explains today's fauna, but the cause is deep time**: the 35km
strait between Bali and Lombok today is not what kept the faunas separate —
it's the millions of years of separate evolution on the Gondwana vs Laurasia
fragments. The water is just the current expression of that ancient boundary.

**Range shifts don't prevent extinction**: if a species shifts poleward,
it still needs suitable habitat to shift INTO. If that habitat is fragmented,
degraded, or already occupied, range shift is impossible. Conservation of
habitat corridors is critical for climate adaptation.
