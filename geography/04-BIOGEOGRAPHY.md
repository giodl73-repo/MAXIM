# 04 — Biogeography

## Species Distributions, Wallace Line, Island Biogeography, Range Shifts

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Biogeographic regions (realms): Nearctic (N America), Neotropical (C+S America), Palearctic (Europe/Asia/N Africa), Afrotropical (sub-Saharan Africa), Indomalayan (S+SE Asia), Australasian (Australia/NZ/New Guinea), Oceanian (Pacific islands), Antarctic; boundaries set by continental history + climate
- Wallace Line: Alfred Russel Wallace's observation (1860s) of sharp faunal boundary between Bali/Lombok and Borneo/Sulawesi; Asian vs Australasian fauna; Lydekker Line, Weber Line, Wallacea (transition zone); reflects Gondwana vs Laurasia continental history
- Continental drift and biogeography: Gondwanan distributions (marsupials in South America + Australia, lungfish, ratites — explain by Pangaea breakup sequence), vicariance vs dispersal debate
- Island biogeography theory (MacArthur/Wilson 1967): species richness = equilibrium between immigration and extinction rates; larger islands → lower extinction rate; nearer islands → higher immigration rate; species-area relationship (S = cAᶻ); SLOSS debate (single large vs several small reserves); conservation applications
- Dispersal mechanisms: wind (passerine birds), water currents (ocean dispersal), rafting (overwater colonization — primates to Madagascar), human-mediated (intentional/accidental introductions)
- Species distributions: climate envelope / ecological niche (Hutchinson n-dimensional hypervolume), fundamental vs realized niche, competitive exclusion, character displacement
- Biome productivity: Net Primary Productivity (NPP) — tropical rainforest highest, tundra lowest; correlation with temperature × moisture; NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) from satellites
- Range shifts under climate change: poleward/upslope shifts already documented (butterflies/birds/trees), phenological mismatches, novel communities, climate debt (communities not yet in equilibrium with current climate), biodiversity hotspots under threat
- Invasive species: characteristics of successful invaders, invasion curve, ecological impacts, economic costs (~$423B/yr globally), management strategies
