# Physical Geography

## The Big Picture

Physical geography describes the processes that shape Earth's surface.
Everything connects through plate tectonics — it drives mountain building,
controls rock type distribution, determines volcanic hazard, and shapes
drainage patterns. Overlay climate and you get soils and landforms.
Understanding the physical template is prerequisite to understanding where
humans have settled and why.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    PHYSICAL GEOGRAPHY SYSTEMS                         |
|                                                                      |
|  INTERNAL PROCESSES                  EXTERNAL PROCESSES              |
```

## Section 1: Plate Tectonics

```
  MECHANISM:
  Earth's lithosphere (crust + upper mantle) broken into ~15 major plates
  Driven by: mantle convection (density-driven circulation) + slab pull
  (dense oceanic lithosphere sinking) + ridge push (thermal expansion)

  THREE BOUNDARY TYPES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ DIVERGENT: plates move apart                                   │
  │ Mid-ocean ridges: new oceanic crust formed (basaltic)          │
  │ Examples: Mid-Atlantic Ridge (Iceland is above sea = ridge)    │
  │           East African Rift (continent splitting — Red Sea     │
  │           is a young ocean; Ethiopian Rift → future ocean)     │
  │ Hazards: volcanic (gentle, effusive); mild earthquakes         │
  │                                                                │
  │ CONVERGENT: plates collide                                     │
  │ Ocean-Ocean: denser subducts → island arc + oceanic trench    │
  │   Examples: Japan, Philippines, Lesser Antilles, Mariana Trench│
  │   (deepest: 11km); explosive volcanism (silicic magma)        │
  │                                                                │
  │ Ocean-Continent: oceanic subducts under continent →            │
  │   volcanic arc + trench                                        │
  │   Examples: Andes/Cascades (Subduction of Nazca/Juan de Fuca) │
  │                                                                │
  │ Continent-Continent: neither subducts → collision + uplift    │
  │   → fold mountains; no subduction zone volcanism              │
  │   Examples: Himalayas (India-Eurasia), Alps (Africa-Europe)   │
  │   Still rising: Himalayas +5mm/year                           │
  │                                                                │
  │ TRANSFORM: plates slide laterally; no creation/destruction    │
  │ Examples: San Andreas Fault (Pacific-N America), North         │
  │   Anatolian Fault (Turkey — seismically analogous to SA Fault)│
  │ Hazards: large strike-slip earthquakes; no volcanism           │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HOT SPOTS:
  Mantle plumes: fixed heat source; plates move over them → island chains
  Hawaii: Pacific Plate NW motion → age gradient SE (youngest) to NW (oldest)
  Yellowstone: continental hotspot; supereruption potential
  Used to measure plate velocity from radiometric dating of chain

  RING OF FIRE:
  70%+ of world's earthquakes and volcanoes around Pacific Ocean rim
  = circum-Pacific subduction zones
```

## Section 2: Earthquakes

```
  SEISMIC WAVES:
  P waves (Primary/compressional): travel through solid + liquid; arrives first
  S waves (Secondary/shear): only solid; arrives second
  Surface waves (Love + Rayleigh): most destructive; slowest

  MEASUREMENT:
  Richter scale (ML, 1935): log₁₀ of amplitude; no longer standard
  Moment Magnitude (Mw): measures seismic moment (rigidity × slip × area)
  ≡ Richter at moderate magnitudes but works at all scales
  Each +1 magnitude = 31.6× more energy release
  Intensity (MMI scale): describes shaking at specific location (not objective
  property of earthquake — same quake rated differently at different locations)

  SECONDARY HAZARDS:
  Liquefaction: saturated loose sediment → behaves like liquid under shaking
                → severe building damage (1964 Niigata, 2011 Christchurch)
  Landslides: earthquake triggering of unstable slopes
  Tsunamis: submarine earthquakes → ocean floor displacement → long waves
             Travel at ~700 km/h; amplitude increases as depth shallows
  Fires: ruptured gas lines (1906 San Francisco: more fire damage than shaking)

  INDUCED SEISMICITY:
  Wastewater injection from oil/gas operations → lubrication of fault planes
  → Earthquakes in previously aseismic areas
  Oklahoma: went from ~1 Mw≥3 quake/year (2008) to 900+ (2015) from injection
  → State regulators imposed injection rate limits; seismicity dropped
```

## Section 3: Volcanism

```
  VOLCANO TYPES (reflect magma composition):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ SHIELD VOLCANOES:                                              │
  │ Basaltic magma (low silica, low viscosity, low gas)           │
  │ Gentle slopes (~5°); effusive eruptions; lava flows           │
  │ Examples: Mauna Loa/Kilauea (Hawaii), Iceland volcanoes        │
  │ Hazard: lava flow property destruction; rarely kills directly  │
  │                                                                │
  │ STRATOVOLCANOES (COMPOSITE):                                  │
  │ Felsic/andesitic magma (high silica, high viscosity, high gas)│
  │ Steep sides; explosive eruptions; pyroclastic flows            │
  │ Examples: Mt. Fuji, Mt. St. Helens, Pinatubo, Rainier         │
  │ Hazard: most dangerous — pyroclastic flow, lahar, ashfall     │
  │                                                                │
  │ CALDERAS / SUPERVOLCANOES:                                     │
  │ Collapse feature after large magma chamber evacuation          │
  │ Yellowstone (640k years ago eruption — VEI 8)                 │
  │ Toba (74k BP — nearly caused human extinction per some models)│
  │ Hazard: global cooling from sulfur aerosols (nuclear winter-like│
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  VOLCANIC EXPLOSIVITY INDEX (VEI):
  0: Hawaiian gentle effusive
  3-4: moderate (Pinatubo 1991: VEI 6 → cooled global temps 0.5°C for 1 year)
  8: supervolcano (Yellowstone scale)

  LAHARS:
  Volcanic mudflows: water + volcanic ash/debris → flows down river valleys
  at 50-100 km/h; can travel far from volcano on existing river channels
  Mt. Pinatubo lahars: more deaths and damage than eruption itself (over years)
  Volcanic hazard zones = not just around volcano summit
```

## Section 4: Rock Cycle

```
  THREE ROCK FAMILIES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ IGNEOUS: formed from cooling magma or lava                    │
  │                                                                │
  │ Intrusive (plutonic): slow cooling underground → large crystals│
  │   Granite: continental crust; quartz + feldspar + mica         │
  │   Diorite, Gabbro, Peridotite (mantle)                        │
  │                                                                │
  │ Extrusive (volcanic): rapid cooling at surface → small crystals│
  │   Basalt: oceanic crust + shield volcano flows                 │
  │   Rhyolite, Obsidian (felsic volcanic)                        │
  │   Pumice: vesicular (gas bubbles) → floats on water            │
  │                                                                │
  │ SEDIMENTARY: lithified erosion products or chemical precipitates│
  │                                                                │
  │ Clastic: fragments of other rocks                              │
  │   Conglomerate (gravel), Sandstone (sand), Shale (silt/clay)  │
  │ Chemical: precipitation from solution                          │
  │   Limestone (CaCO₃ — marine organisms or direct precipitation) │
  │   Evaporites: Halite (salt), Gypsum (desert/restricted basins) │
  │ Organic: accumulated organic material                          │
  │   Coal (plant material), Chalk (microorganism shells)          │
  │                                                                │
  │ METAMORPHIC: existing rock transformed by heat + pressure       │
  │   Marble: limestone → metamorphic (recrystallized calcite)     │
  │   Quartzite: sandstone → metamorphic                           │
  │   Schist, Gneiss: high-grade metamorphism (alignment of micas) │
  │   Slate: low-grade metamorphism of shale                       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HUMAN SIGNIFICANCE:
  Granite: foundation and building stone (massive continental cratons)
  Limestone: aquifer (karst), building material, cement production
  Coal/oil: sedimentary (organic accumulation + burial)
  Basalt: road aggregate; oceanic floor globally
```

## Section 5: Geomorphology — Landform Processes

```
  FLUVIAL (river) PROCESSES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Upper course (headwaters): steep gradient; V-shaped valleys;  │
  │   vertical erosion dominant; waterfalls, rapids                │
  │                                                                │
  │ Middle course: meanders form as gradient decreases; lateral   │
  │   erosion + deposition; floodplain develops                   │
  │                                                                │
  │ Lower course: broad flat floodplain; oxbow lakes (cut-off     │
  │   meanders); delta/estuary formation at mouth                 │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  DRAINAGE BASIN CONCEPTS:
  Watershed (catchment): area drained by a river system
  Stream order (Strahler): #1 = headwater stream; merging two 1s = 2;
    two 2s = 3; etc.; Mississippi = ~8th order
  Base level: lowest elevation a river can erode to (sea level ultimately)
  Graded stream: long profile in equilibrium — no net erosion/deposition

  GLACIAL LANDFORMS:
  Valley glaciers: U-shaped valleys, arêtes (knife ridges), cirques (bowls),
    horns (pointed peaks: Matterhorn), hanging valleys (waterfalls where
    tributary meets main valley)
  Continental ice sheets: fjords (submerged U-shaped valleys), moraines
    (till deposits), drumlins (streamlined oval hills), erratics (foreign
    boulders — mapped to trace ice movement)
  Post-glacial: isostatic rebound (Scandinavia still rising ~10mm/yr after
    ice sheet melting); Great Lakes (glacially carved)

  KARST:
  Limestone dissolution by slightly acidic groundwater (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃)
  Features: caves, sinkholes (dolines), disappearing streams, springs
  Regions: Florida, Yucatan (cenotes — Mayan water source), Slovenia/Dinaric
    Alps (type locality — "karst" from Slovene "kras"), South China Tower Karst
  Agricultural challenge: thin soils; drainage often underground

  COASTAL:
  Erosional: sea cliffs, wave-cut platforms, sea stacks (Twelve Apostles)
  Depositional: beaches, sand bars, spits, tombolos, barrier islands (US East Coast)
  Coral reefs: warm (<18°C too cold), shallow, clear, low-nutrient water;
    bleaching under thermal stress; atoll formation (Darwin's theory confirmed)

  AEOLIAN (wind):
  Sand dunes: barchan (crescent, uni-directional wind), seif (linear),
    star (multi-directional wind)
  Loess: wind-deposited silt; extremely fertile; China Loess Plateau,
    US Midwest, Pampas — some of world's best agricultural land
```

## Section 6: Soils

```
  SOIL FORMATION FACTORS (Jenny 1941 — CLORPT):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ C — Climate: temperature + moisture (primary controls)         │
  │     → Warm, wet: intense weathering, thick leached profiles   │
  │     → Cold, dry: slow weathering, thin profiles               │
  │                                                                │
  │ O — Organisms: vegetation type, microbial community, earthworms│
  │     → Prairie soils: deep organic horizons from grass roots   │
  │     → Forest soils: acidic from leaf litter decomposition     │
  │                                                                │
  │ R — Relief (topography): slope, aspect, drainage position     │
  │     → Slopes: thinner soils (erosion-prone)                   │
  │     → Valley bottoms: thicker, waterlogged soils              │
  │                                                                │
  │ P — Parent material: rock type that weathers to form soil      │
  │     → Basalt: nutrient-rich (volcanic soils); limestone karst  │
  │     → Granite: nutrient-poor quartz residue                   │
  │                                                                │
  │ T — Time: older soils more developed, more leached            │
  │     → Hawaii: young volcanic soils high in nutrients           │
  │     → Laterites: ancient tropical soils highly leached        │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SOIL HORIZONS (profile from surface down):
  O horizon: organic matter (leaf litter, humus)
  A horizon: topsoil; organic-mineral mix; most biologically active
  B horizon: subsoil; leached or accumulated minerals (illuviation)
  C horizon: weathered parent material (saprolite)
  R horizon: bedrock

  KEY SOIL ORDERS (USDA, 12 total):
  Mollisols: grassland soils; deep dark A horizon; most fertile;
    North American prairies, Eurasian steppes → breadbaskets
  Oxisols: ancient tropical weathered soils; low fertility (leached);
    Amazon/Congo — don't support agriculture without nutrients
  Spodosols: cold coniferous forests; acid; E horizon (bleached)
  Vertisols: shrink-swell clays; Texas Blackland Prairie, India Deccan

  SOIL DEGRADATION:
  Erosion: ~33% of world's agricultural soils moderately/highly degraded
  Salinization: irrigation → salt accumulation at surface; Mesopotamia
    collapse partly attributed to soil salinization
  Compaction: heavy machinery → reduced pore space → reduced infiltration
  Soil as SLOW-RENEWABLE: 1 cm of topsoil takes ~100-1000 years to form
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Physical Geography Question | Key Concept |
|---|---|
| Why are volcanoes where they are? | Plate boundaries (convergent arcs + divergent ridges) + hotspots |
| Why is this valley U-shaped? | Glacial erosion (compare to V-shaped = fluvial) |
| Why is this soil fertile or poor? | CLORPT especially parent material + time + climate |
| Why is there a desert here? | Rain shadow (orographic), cold upwelling current, continentality, or subtropical high pressure |
| Why are there sinkholes here? | Karst (limestone dissolution) — check for karst region |
| Why does this earthquake area have no volcanoes? | Transform boundary (San Andreas) — no magma generation |
| Why are tsunami warnings relevant far away? | Ocean travel speed; wave amplitude grows as water shallows |

## Common Confusion Points

**Richter scale is not used for modern earthquakes**: seismologists use moment
magnitude (Mw). Media often says "Richter" as a generic term. For large quakes,
Richter and Mw diverge significantly. The 2011 Tōhoku earthquake was Mw 9.1.

**Volcanoes are not where most earthquakes occur**: transform faults (San Andreas,
North Anatolian) generate large earthquakes without any volcanoes because neither
plate subducts. Collision zones (Himalayas) have earthquakes but no subduction
volcanism. Only subduction zones get both.

**Soil fertility ≠ soil age**: young volcanic soils (Hawaii, Cascades) are highly
fertile. Old tropical soils (Oxisols, Ultisols) are nutrient-depleted from
millennia of leaching. Amazon soils are infertile despite the lush forest — the
forest nutrients are in the biomass, not the soil. Clearing → rapid fertility loss.

**Glaciers and fjords**: fjords are drowned glacial valleys, which means they occur
where glacier-carved valleys met rising sea levels after ice retreat. The deep
water is a glacial legacy, not a coastal erosion product.
