# Dyeing and Fiber Arts — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Dyeing and fiber arts encompass the complete chain from raw animal or plant fiber to finished colored cloth: preparation, spinning, dyeing, and weaving. The technical core is **mordanting chemistry** — the coordination chemistry that makes color permanent. Without mordanting, most natural dyes wash out. With the right mordant, colors survive centuries.

## Natural vs. Synthetic: The Historical Break

The year 1856 is the pivot point. William Henry Perkin, an 18-year-old chemistry student attempting to synthesize quinine, produced a purple sludge from coal tar aniline — mauveine. Within 40 years, the entire pre-industrial natural dye industry collapsed.

```
TIMELINE

  Antiquity -> 1856:   Natural dyes only
    Plants: indigo, madder, weld, woad
    Insects: cochineal, kermes, lac
    Mollusks: Tyrian purple
    Minerals: iron buff, ochres

  1856-1900:   Synthetic dye explosion (Germany dominates)
    1856: Perkin's mauve
    1869: alizarin synthesis (destroys madder industry)
    1880: Congo Red (first direct dye for cotton)
    1897: synthetic indigo (Baeyer / BASF)
    1901: commercial synthetic indigo replaces natural indigo globally

  1900-present:  Synthetic dyes dominant
    ~50,000 dyes catalogued
    Natural dyes < 1% of global dye production
    Revival: 1970s-present (ecological / craft interest)
```

## The Fiber Classification

```
FIBER TAXONOMY
  |
  +-- PROTEIN FIBERS (animal, amide bonds, -CO-NH-)
  |   +-- HAIR FIBERS
  |   |   Wool (Ovis aries): dominant, keratinous, scales
  |   |   Cashmere (Capra hircus): fine underfleece
  |   |   Alpaca (Vicugna pacos): no scales = less felting
  |   |   Angora (rabbit): very fine, hollow
  |   |   Mohair (Angora goat): lustrous, minimal scales
  |   +-- FILAMENT
  |       Silk (Bombyx mori): continuous filament, fibroin + sericin
  |       Wild silk (Tussah): coarser, yellow
  |
  +-- CELLULOSE FIBERS (plant, beta-1,4-glycosidic bonds, -OH groups)
      +-- SEED HAIR
      |   Cotton (Gossypium): short staple, seeds
      |   Kapok (Ceiba): non-spinnable hollow fibers
      +-- BAST (stem fibers)
      |   Flax (Linum usitatissimum) -> linen: strong, lustrous
      |   Hemp (Cannabis sativa): coarser, sustainable
      |   Jute (Corchorus): coarse, bags and hessian
      |   Ramie (Boehmeria): very strong, rigid
      +-- LEAF
          Sisal (Agave sisalana): rope
```

**Why fiber type matters for dyeing**: Protein fibers (wool, silk) have abundant amine groups (-NH2) that coordinate metal mordants. Acid dyes bond ionically to the protonated amine groups. Cellulose fibers (cotton, linen) lack these groups — they need reactive dyes (covalent bonds), vat dyes (physical entrapment), or tannin pre-mordanting before alum mordanting.

## Mordanting: The Technical Core

Mordants (from Latin mordere, "to bite") are metal salts that form coordination complexes with both the dye molecule and the fiber, creating a permanent three-way bond:

```
   FIBER -- fiber-OH or fiber-NH2
     |
     |  coordinate bond
     v
   METAL (Al3+, Fe2+, Cu2+, Sn2+, Cr3+)
     |
     |  coordinate bond
     v
   DYE MOLECULE (oxygen or nitrogen donor groups)

The metal ion acts as a bridge. Different metals change the geometry
and electronics of the complex -> shift in absorbed wavelength -> shift in color.

Same dye + different mordant = different color:
  Madder + alum mordant  = bright red-orange
  Madder + iron mordant  = dark purple-brown ("saddened")
  Madder + copper mordant = orange-brown
  Madder + tin mordant   = bright scarlet
```

## The Color Fastness Problem

Not all dyes are equal in permanence. Two main fastness criteria:

```
LIGHTFASTNESS (resistance to UV fading)
  ISO Blue Wool Scale: 1 (very poor) to 8 (excellent)

  Natural dyes:
    Weld (luteolin): BW 5-6 -- relatively good
    Indigo: BW 6-7 -- excellent for a natural dye
    Cochineal on alum: BW 4-5
    Madder on alum: BW 4-5
    Most flowers (quercetin, etc.): BW 1-2 -- fugitive

  Synthetic dyes:
    Reactive dyes: BW 5-7
    Vat dyes: BW 7-8 -- best class
    Acid dyes: varies widely

  UV DEGRADATION MECHANISM:
    Photons break the conjugated pi system (chromophore)
    O2 + UV -> reactive oxygen species -> oxidize chromophore
    Solution: UV absorbers in dye (difficult for natural dyes)
    Fiber content affects degradation rate (wool worse than cellulose)

WASHFASTNESS (resistance to bleeding in water)
  ISO 1-5 scale
  Covalent bonds (reactive dyes): best
  Ionic bonds (acid dyes, good mordant): moderate
  Physical entrapment only (vat dyes in cellulose): good
  Weak hydrogen bonds (direct dyes): poor
```

## Geographic and Cultural Distribution

Natural dye geography closely tracks the ecology of dye plants and the global trade routes that moved them:

```
DYE GEOGRAPHY

  BLUE:
    Europe: woad (Isatis tinctoria) -- dominant until ~1600
    Tropical Americas/Asia: indigo (Indigofera tinctoria) -- superior
    Japan: Persicaria tinctoria (Japanese indigo, aizome tradition)
    West Africa: Indigofera tinctoria (kanga, West African cloth)

  RED:
    Mediterranean: madder (Rubia tinctorum) -- Turkey Red pre-eminence
    Pre-Columbian Americas: cochineal on prickly pear cactus (scale insect)
    Europe (pre-cochineal): kermes (Kermes vermilio, oak scale insect)
    Asia: lac (Kerria lacca)
    Persia: henna (Lawsonia inermis, orange/brown, no mordant needed)

  YELLOW:
    Europe: weld (Reseda luteola) -- highest lightfastness natural yellow
    Mediterranean/Middle East: pomegranate rind, onion skins
    Asia: turmeric (poor fastness), saffron (expensive, very poor fastness)

  PURPLE:
    Mediterranean elite: Tyrian purple (Murex sea snails -- 6-bromoindigo)
    Post-Columbian: cochineal with alkali modifier
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Dye vs. pigment**: A dye dissolves in liquid and bonds to the substrate at the molecular level. A pigment is an insoluble particle bound to a surface by a binder (oil, acrylic, egg tempera). Same molecule can be either: indigo is a dye when reduced and dissolved in a vat; indigo is a pigment when ground and suspended in oil. See pigments/ directory for the pigment side.

**Mordant vs. dye**: The mordant fixes the dye but has no color of its own (alum = clear). The dye provides color but won't stay without the mordant (for most natural dyes). The combination creates the final hue. Confusing them causes failed dye results.

**Natural = non-toxic is false**: Chrome mordant (potassium dichromate) is toxic and a carcinogen. Tin mordant can damage fiber. Some natural dyes (saffron, turmeric) are fugitive. Some mordanted natural dyes are more colorfast and less toxic than certain synthetic dyes. The categories are independent.
