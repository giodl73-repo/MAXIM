# Historic Dyeing and Textile Traditions

## The Big Picture

The history of dyeing is inseparable from the history of trade, empire, and chemical discovery. Specific dyes and techniques emerged in specific ecologies (Murex snails in the Mediterranean, cochineal on Mexican cacti, indigo in tropical Asia), diffused along trade routes, and became embedded in cultural practices that persist or have been consciously revived.

```
GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION OF MAJOR TRADITIONS

  EUROPE:
    Woad/weld (blue/yellow) -> Lincoln green
    Madder from Levant via trade -> Turkey Red process (18th c.)
    Kermes (pre-Columbian luxury red)
    Tyrian purple (Phoenician monopoly, Roman imperial)

  JAPAN:
    Aizome (indigo vat, sukumo fermented) -- deep blues on cotton/linen
    Shibori resist dyeing
    Katazome (stencil + paste resist)

  INDIA:
    Block printing + resist: ajrakh (Kutch), kalamkari, bagh printing
    Indigo cultivation (Gujarat, Bengal)
    Turkey Red madder (mordant complexity)
    Ikat weaving (double ikat Patola silk -- Gujarat)

  INDONESIA / SE ASIA:
    Batik (wax resist, Java) -- court craft to global export
    Ikat (Sumatra, Timor, Flores -- double ikat Geringsing)

  WEST AFRICA:
    Bogolanfini (mud cloth, Mali -- tannic resist + mud iron reaction)
    Adinkra (stamped resist)
    Indigo resist and tie-dye (Nigeria, Mali, Senegal)

  PRE-COLUMBIAN AMERICAS:
    Cochineal (dominant Aztec tribute dye)
    Indigo (Indigofera suffruticosa)
    Andean tapestry (camelid fiber: llama, alpaca, vicuna)
    Paracas/Nazca interlocked tapestries (Peru)
```

## Tyrian Purple: The Most Expensive Dye in History

Produced from sea snails (Murex brandaris and Bolinus brandaris) in the eastern Mediterranean, Tyrian purple was the most valued dyestuff of antiquity -- worth more than gold by weight:

```
TYRIAN PURPLE CHEMISTRY

  SOURCE: Murex sea snails, primarily:
    Bolinus brandaris (spiny dye-murex)
    Hexaplex trunculus
    Stramonita haemastoma

  COMPOUND: 6,6'-dibromoindigo
    Structure: indigo molecule with bromine at position 6 on each ring
    Deep blue-purple: absorption peak ~570nm (absorbs yellow-green)
    Most lightfast known organic dye: Blue Wool Scale 7-8

  PRODUCTION:
    1. Crack shell, remove hypobranchial gland (small colorless gland)
    2. Expose mucous secretion to sunlight and air
    3. Precursor (colorless tyrindoxyl) photo-oxidizes to purple product
    4. Steep wool in the mucous before oxidation completes
    5. ~12,000 snails required per gram of dye

  YIELD ESTIMATE:
    10,000 Bolinus brandaris -> 1-1.5 grams of dye
    1 kg dye: ~10 million snails
    Roman pound (327g) of dyed wool: ~1 million snails

  ALTERNATIVE: Hexaplex trunculus produces a BLUE (not purple) product
    At low O2 / dim light -> indigotin (identical to Indigofera indigo)
    Some scholars propose this is the biblical tekhelet (blue thread of Jewish law)

  HISTORICAL CONTROL:
    Phoenician Tyre held early monopoly (hence "Tyrian")
    Rome: sumptuary laws -- only emperors could wear solid Tyrian purple
    Byzantine: "born in the purple" (porphyrogennetos) -- throne room lined with purple
    Production collapsed with Ottoman conquest of Constantinople (1453)
    Modern: several artisan producers revived shell-dye in Mediterranean
```

## Batik: Wax Resist Dyeing (Java)

Batik is a resist-dyeing technique using wax applied to cloth to prevent dye from reaching masked areas. Javanese court batik is considered among the most sophisticated textile arts in the world:

```
BATIK PROCESS

  TOOLS:
    Canting (tjanting): small copper cup with narrow spout
      Hand tool for drawing wax lines freehand
      Batik tulis (written batik): freehand canting work
    Cap (tjap/cap): copper stamp for repeating patterns
      Batik cap: faster, more commercial

  WAX:
    Blend of: beeswax (flexibility, adhesion) + paraffin (crackle effect)
    Typical blend: 70% paraffin + 30% beeswax
    Applied hot (melted, ~80°C) -- soaks into cloth
    Must cool and harden before dyeing

  DYEING SEQUENCE:
    1. Apply wax to areas to REMAIN the first color
    2. Dye cloth first color
    3. Apply wax to areas to REMAIN second color
    4. Dye second color (usually darker)
    5. Repeat for each additional color
    6. Boil out wax (hot water + soda ash); remaining wax joins water
    7. Mordant + fix if required

  CRACKLE EFFECT:
    Paraffin cracks on cold cloth -> dye seeps through cracks
    Characteristic spider-web crackle pattern is a batik identifier

  JAVANESE COURT PATTERNS:
    Parang (diagonal blade), Kawung (floral cross), Truntum, Megamendung (clouds)
    Specific patterns restricted to royal family (sumptuary tradition)
    UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage (2009)

  MODERN BATIK:
    Indonesia largest producer; also India, West Africa (Adire), Ghana
    "Wax print" fabric: machine-printed imitation of batik (not hand-resist)
```

## Ikat: Pre-Dye Resist on Fiber

Ikat resists dye before weaving: sections of warp or weft threads are bound (tied or clamped) before dyeing, creating dye-resist patterns that appear when woven. The characteristic feature: slightly blurred edges at color transitions (the ikat "look").

```
IKAT TYPES

  WARP IKAT: Resist-dye warp threads only
    After dyeing, wind dyed warp onto loom
    Weft is single color or undecorated
    Pattern appears along warp direction
    Examples: Uzbek adras, Mesoamerican jaspe

  WEFT IKAT: Resist-dye weft threads only
    Pattern appears across the cloth width
    Weft alignment critical: misalignment = "ikat blur"
    Examples: Japanese kasuri, Cambodian hol

  DOUBLE IKAT: Both warp AND weft pre-dyed with matching patterns
    Alignment required in BOTH directions for pattern to appear
    Most technically demanding textile in the world
    Two traditions survive:
      Patola silk (Patan, Gujarat, India): geometric designs
        Double ikat on silk; royal dowry cloth
        Only ~2 families still weave (Salvi family)
      Geringsing cloth (Tenganan, Bali, Indonesia):
        Cotton double ikat; ceremonial cloth
        Production takes years; used in rituals
        UNESCO recognition pending

  IKAT BLUR:
    Result of slight misalignment when threading warped yarn onto loom
    Considered aesthetically desirable in traditional contexts
    Machine-produced "fake ikat" fabric cannot replicate: edges too sharp
```

## Shibori: Japanese Shaped Resist

Shibori is a family of resist dyeing techniques from Japan, all involving manipulation of fabric (folding, binding, stitching, clamping) before immersion in dye (typically indigo):

```
SHIBORI TECHNIQUES

  ITAJIME (板締め) -- clamp resist:
    Fold cloth in accordion pleats (horizontal, diagonal, or complex)
    Clamp folded cloth between two shaped pieces of wood
    Dye penetrates edges; clamped interior resists
    Result: geometric, often circular or radial pattern

  ARASHI / POLE-WRAP (嵐絞り) -- storm:
    Wrap cloth diagonally around tall post
    Bind with thread from top to bottom
    Compress cloth down the pole
    Dye: diagonal lines (resembles rain in storm, hence "arashi")

  NE-MAKI (根巻き) -- bound resist:
    Bind bundles of cloth with thread at regular intervals
    Dye: circular rings, spots (similar to tie-dye in concept)
    More controlled than random tie-dye

  STITCHED SHIBORI (kumo, ne-maki, ori-nui):
    Running stitch sewn in pattern on cloth
    Thread pulled tight to gather cloth
    Dye; remove thread -> raised-and-lowered pattern

  INDIGO IN SHIBORI:
    Shibori typically uses indigo (multiple dip process)
    Resisted areas stay white or previous color
    Over-dyeing (dyeing over previous shibori pattern) builds complexity
    Japanese shibori artists: Itchiku Kubota (extraordinary silk works)
```

## West African Traditions: Bogolanfini and Adire

### Bogolanfini (Mud Cloth, Mali)

```
BOGOLANFINI (Bambara: bogo=earth, lan=with, fini=cloth)

  ORIGIN: Bamana people, Mali
  Fiber: handwoven cotton strips sewn together (~15cm wide strips)
  Process: unique -- NEGATIVE DRAWING with mordant, not dye

  STEPS:
    1. Boil cloth with N'Gallah leaves (Combretum glutinosum)
       -> deposits tannin + yellow-brown pigment on cloth
    2. Ferment mud: collect black river mud (rich in iron hydroxide)
       Allow to ferment in pottery jars for up to 1 year
    3. Apply fermented mud to cloth in geometric patterns with sticks/spatulas
    4. Mud reacts with tannin deposits: Fe(OH)x + tannin -> iron gallate = black
    5. Remove yellow background: bleach with soap and/or sun exposure
       OR apply caustic soda (N'Tjankara bleach)
    6. Result: black patterns on white/cream background
       (OR: white pattern lines within black areas -- negative drawing)

  PATTERNS: geometric, symbolic, read by Bamana
    Traditional patterns encode clan affiliation, life events, spiritual protection
    Each hunter wore unique bogolanfini; carried over corpse if killed

  MODERN:
    Mali artisans export globally; Chris Seydou modernized the aesthetic
    Imitation "mud cloth" fabric: screen-printed, not hand-made
```

### Adire (Nigeria, Yoruba)

Adire (Yoruba: "to tie and dye") encompasses several resist-dye traditions on cotton cloth using indigo:

```
ADIRE TYPES:

  ADIRE ELEKO: cassava starch paste resist
    Apply cassava starch (eko) in patterns using feather, matchstick, or comb
    Dye in indigo vat; paste resists dye
    Remove paste after dyeing -> blue cloth with white pattern

  ADIRE ONIKO: tie and dye (Yoruba version)
    Bind, tie, stitch fabric before indigo dyeing
    Multiple rounds = multiple colors/depths

  PATTERNS: symbolic vocabulary
    Ibadandun pattern named for Ibadan; Olokun (sea goddess); Abeke, etc.
    Knowledge traditionally held by women, passed mother to daughter
```

## Pre-Columbian Andean Textiles

The Andean civilizations produced arguably the most technically complex pre-industrial textiles anywhere:

```
ANDEAN TEXTILE TRADITION

  FIBERS:
    Camelid: four species
      Vicuna (Vicugna vicugna): 10-13 micron -- finest natural fiber in world
        Inca ari (tribute cloth): exclusively for Inca emperor and gods
      Alpaca (Vicugna pacos): 15-30 micron
      Llama (Lama glama): 25-40 micron, coarser
      Guanaco (Lama guanicoe): 16-18 micron
    Cotton: Gossypium barbadense (Sea Island type, fine)

  DYES:
    Cochineal (Dactylopius coccus): red, crimson, purple
      Mordants: alum, tin, iron modifiers
    Indigo (Indigofera suffruticosa): blue
    Yellow: Multiple plant sources
    Combinations: full spectrum achieved

  PARACAS CULTURE (700 BCE - 100 CE):
    Burial mantles with complex embroidery
    120+ color palette in some mantles
    3D figure embroidery on cotton ground cloth
    Fibers still vibrant after 2000 years (dry desert burial)

  NAZCA CULTURE:
    Interlocked tapestry (discontinuous weft interlocking)
    Double-faced cloth: different patterns on each face
    Some textiles woven with 400 ends per inch warp

  INCA:
    Cumbi cloth: finest tapestry, woven by acllas (chosen women)
    Tocapu: geometric symbols woven into cloth, possibly phonetic
    Quipu: knotted fiber recording device (still not fully decoded)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Wax print is not batik** -- "African wax print" fabric (made by Vlisco in Netherlands, Julius Holland, etc.) is factory-produced roller-printed cotton imitating batik appearance. It is made by machine, not hand-resist wax drawing. The name is a commercial category that caused significant confusion about authentic batik origin. Both have value; they are different things.

**Ikat blur is not a defect** -- The slightly blurred edges at color changes in ikat cloth are inherent to the process (slight misalignment when re-winding dyed yarn). Factory "ikat look" fabrics print sharp geometric edges. Blurring = authentic handmade ikat. The blur is sometimes specifically preserved by master weavers as part of the cloth's character.

**Shibori is not tie-dye** -- "Tie-dye" (American 1960s) is usually: knotted, rubber-banded cloth dipped in multiple dye baths for random psychedelic effects. Shibori is a disciplined technical practice with specific techniques (itajime, arashi, ne-maki), most often using a single dye (indigo), producing specific geometric results. Aesthetic intentionality is the difference.

**Vicuna fiber cannot be legally harvested from captive animals in Peru** -- Vicunas are protected under CITES. Traditional chaku (community roundup, shearing, release) is the only legal harvesting method. "Vicuna" products sold cheaply are either mislabeled or illegal.
