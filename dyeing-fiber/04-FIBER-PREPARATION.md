# Fiber Preparation: Scouring, Carding, Combing, Retting

## The Big Picture

Raw fiber from animal or plant is contaminated with oils, waxes, plant matter, dirt, and other impurities. Preparation removes these to give clean, aligned fiber ready for spinning. The preparation method also determines the character of the resulting yarn: carded fiber (random alignment) makes soft, airy woolen yarn; combed fiber (parallel alignment) makes smooth, strong worsted yarn.

```
FIBER PREPARATION PIPELINE

WOOL:         Raw        Scouring     Carding     Combing (worsted)
              fleece  -> (de-fat)  -> (open,   -> (align,
                                      entangle    remove short) -> TOP
                                      aligned)  -> ROVING (woolen)

FLAX:         Harvested  Retting      Breacking   Hackling
              plant   -> (rot stems)->  (break  -> (comb to
                                        woody     remove shives) -> LINE + TOW
                                        core)

COTTON:       Raw bolls  Ginning      Opening     Carding     Combing (high quality)
              with seeds -> (seed   ->            ->          -> SLIVER / ROVING
                            removal)
```

## Wool Preparation

### Wool Anatomy

Understanding the fiber structure is prerequisite for understanding why preparation matters:

```
WOOL FIBER CROSS SECTION

  +--------+  CUTICLE: overlapping scales (like roof tiles)
  | [=][=] |  Pointing from root -> tip
  | [=][=] |  Scale edge CATCHES other fibers -> felting tendency
  +--------+  Chemical properties: disulfide bonds, hydrophobic coating

  CORTEX:  Spindle-shaped cells, parallel to fiber axis
           Alpha-keratin: coiled-coil protein structure
           Contains melanin (color in natural-colored wool)
           Bilateral cortex (ortho + para cells) -> natural crimp

  MEDULLA: Air-filled hollow core
           Present in coarser wools, absent in fine Merino

WOOL PROPERTIES BY BREED:
  Merino: 15-25 micron, high crimp, next-to-skin soft
  Rambouillet: 18-24 micron, high crimp
  Border Leicester: 30-38 micron, luster, lower crimp
  Romney: 28-38 micron, moderate
  Herdwick: 35-50 micron, coarse, mountain breed
  Cotswold: 35-45 micron, long staple, luster

Fiber diameter in microns (µm) is the primary quality metric.
< 18.5 µm: Merino, next-to-skin wearable
> 30 µm: typically non-next-to-skin (outerwear, rugs)
```

### Scouring

Removal of lanolin, suint (dried sweat salts), vegetable matter, and dirt:

```
SCOURING PROCESS

  Grease content: 15-25% of raw (greasy) fleece weight
  Suint: dried sweat containing potassium salts, ~10-15%
  Vegetable matter (VM): plant fragments caught in fleece
  Moisture regain: wool absorbs ~16% water at 65% RH

  STANDARD METHOD:
  1. Pre-wet fleece in cold water (prevents scale opening = felting)
  2. First scour bowl: Na2CO3 (soda ash) + nonionic detergent
     Temperature: 45-50°C MAXIMUM (higher = felting begins)
     pH: 8.5-9.0
     Time: 10-15 minutes
  3. Rinse bowls (2-3): clean water, SAME temperature as scour
     (temperature shock = felting shock)
  4. Second scour bowl if heavily greased
  5. Final rinse: slightly acidic (splash of vinegar) to close scales

  CRITICAL: Never agitate felting-prone wool in hot water
    Felting requires: heat + moisture + mechanical action (all three)
    Scouring is done by SOAKING not wringing or stirring

  CARBONIZING (vegetable matter removal):
    For heavily contaminated fleeces
    Dilute sulfuric acid bath (pH 2-3), 65°C
    Drying -> acid concentrated -> VM (cellulose) chars and becomes brittle
    Gentle crushing + dusting removes charred VM (wool not damaged by acid)
    Neutralize: sodium bicarbonate bath after carbonizing
```

### Carding

Carding opens the fiber, removes any remaining VM, and produces a preparation with fibers oriented semi-randomly:

```
CARDING MECHANISM

  DRUM CARDER (mechanical or hand-powered)
    Large drum (licker-in) and small drum (doffer): opposite pitch wire teeth
    Fiber passed between drums: opened, aligned somewhat
    Output: batt (sheet of fiber)

  HAND CARDERS
    Two paddles with wire teeth
    Load one card, transfer to second, repeat
    "Roll" fiber off = rolag (rolled fiber preparation)

  FIBER ORIENTATION IN CARDED PREPARATION:
    Semi-random: some alignment, many crosswise fibers
    Air pockets trapped between fibers = LOFTY YARN
    Short + long fibers both present = irregular

  WOOLEN YARN from carded fiber:
    Air pockets -> warm, light, fuzzy yarn
    Good for: hand-knitting, blankets, outerwear
    Not good for: smooth suiting, tight construction

  DRUM CARDER vs. HAND CARDER:
    Drum: faster, more consistent, better for larger quantities
    Hand: more control, gentler on delicate fibers
```

### Combing: Worsted Preparation

Combing aligns fibers parallel and removes short fibers (noils):

```
COMBING

  PURPOSE:
    1. Remove fibers shorter than a threshold (noils = short fiber waste)
    2. Align remaining long fibers parallel (no crosswise fibers)
    3. Remove any remaining VM

  PROCESS (hand combing with English combs):
    Load combs: fiber draped over heated tines
    Transfer: pull fiber through tines of one comb while tines interleave
    Repeat until fiber is parallel
    "Diz" off: draw fiber through a diz (disk with hole) to form TOP

  TOP vs. ROVING:
    TOP: parallel-drafted fiber (pen thickness), for worsted spinning
    ROVING: slightly twisted attenuated preparation, also parallel

  WORSTED YARN from combed fiber:
    Dense, smooth, strong, lustrous
    No air pockets (parallel fibers pack tightly)
    Good for: suiting fabric, smooth hosiery, warp threads
    Classic description: "lies flat, smooth, shiny"

  CONTINENTAL vs. ENGLISH COMBING:
    English (Bradford): coarse-toothed combs, for long-staple wool
    Continental (French): fine-toothed, for short-staple Merino
```

## Plant Fiber Preparation

### Flax to Linen

Flax (Linum usitatissimum) is a bast fiber: strong fibers run along the outside of the stem in discrete bundles, cemented together by pectin and surrounded by woody core (shiv/hurds):

```
FLAX PROCESSING STAGES

  1. HARVESTING: Pull (not cut) to preserve root fiber length
     Fiber in mature stem: 60-100cm bundles

  2. RIPPLING: Remove seed pods (drawn through a ripple comb)

  3. RETTING: Decompose pectin binding fibers to stem core
     WATER RETTING: submerge in pond/stream 1-2 weeks
       Anaerobic bacteria consume pectin faster
       Produces consistent, cleaner fiber but pollutes water
       Traditional: rivers of Belgium (Lys river flax = finest linen)
     DEW RETTING: spread on grass for 4-6 weeks
       Aerobic fungi + slow moisture
       More variable; green-tinged fiber
       Lower environmental impact

  4. BREAKING: Pass through fluted rollers to crush woody shive
     Breaks shive (woody core) into pieces without damaging fiber bundles
     Hand: wooden "flax brake" (hinged boards struck down on stems)

  5. SCUTCHING: Beat remaining shive from fiber bundles
     Wooden scutching board + paddle: beat fiber against board
     Removes 80-90% of shive and tow fibers
     Scutching tow: short tangled fibers separated out (used for rope)

  6. HACKLING (HECKLING): Draw fiber through progressively finer toothed combs
     Removes remaining short fibers (tow)
     Aligns long fibers (line flax) parallel
     Output: LINE (long, fine) and TOW (short, coarse)

  LINE FLAX: long parallel fibers -> fine linen thread
  TOW: short tangled fibers -> coarser thread, twine, upholstery
```

### Cotton Preparation

Cotton (Gossypium hirsutum primarily) is a seed hair fiber -- unicellular, 2-4cm long:

```
COTTON PROCESSING

  1. GINNING: Separate fiber from seeds
     Saw gin (Whitney, 1793 -- cotton gin): wire saws catch fiber, seeds too large
     Roller gin: for longer-staple Sea Island / pima cotton
     Output: cotton lint + cottonseed (oil pressing, cattle feed)

  2. OPENING + PICKING: Open compressed bales, blend, remove trash
     Air jets + mechanical beaters

  3. CARDING: (same function as wool carding)
     Flat card (metallic wire card clothing between cylinder and flat bars)
     Removes: short fibers, neps (entangled fiber knots), remaining seed coat
     Output: card sliver (untwisted continuous rope, random orientation)

  4. COMBING (optional, for fine cotton):
     Removes fibers shorter than cutoff length
     Output: combed sliver (parallel alignment, high quality)
     Combed cotton: finer, smoother, stronger yarns (Egyptian cotton, Pima)
     Cost: 15-25% fiber loss as noil (short fiber waste)

  5. DRAWING: Multiple slivers combined + drafted (attenuated)
     Equalizes fiber distribution
     2-3 drawing passes typical

  6. ROVING: Light twist applied to thin sliver for transport to spinning frame
```

## Silk Preparation: Reeling and Degumming

Silk (Bombyx mori) is unique: a single continuous filament from the silkworm cocoon:

```
SILK PROCESSING

  COCOON: Single filament 600-900m long
  Two fibroin filaments cemented by sericin (silk gum) protein

  REELING:
    Cocoons softened in hot water (80-90°C): softens sericin
    Find filament end (brushing against the cocoon in water)
    Reel multiple filaments together (5-8 cocoons per thread) -> raw silk thread
    Filaments stick together by residual sericin while drying

  THROWN SILK: raw silk threads twisted/plied into yarn
    Organzine: twist in opposite directions -> strong warp thread
    Tram: light twist, multiple threads -> soft weft thread

  DEGUMMING: Remove sericin (silk gum = 20-30% of raw silk weight)
    Sodium carbonate + soap + hot water (90-95°C, 30 min)
    Or: enzyme degumming (protease enzymes)
    After degumming: pure fibroin -> lustrous, soft "soft silk"
    IMPORTANT for dyeing: sericin partially blocks dye absorption
    Mordant dyes on degummed silk: better color uptake

  SILK TUSSAH (wild silk):
    Antheraea mylitta, A. pernyi: darker, coarser fiber
    Difficult to fully desize: more tannin in sericin
    Natural color: cream to tan-brown
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet: Preparation by End Use

| End Use | Fiber | Preparation | Resulting Yarn Type |
|---------|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| Knitting yarn (soft) | Merino wool | Scour + card | Woolen, airy, soft |
| Suiting fabric | Wool | Scour + comb | Worsted, smooth, strong |
| Fine linen shirting | Flax | Full retting + hacking | Line flax, fine linen |
| Canvas, upholstery | Flax or hemp | Dew retting + tow | Tow, coarse linen |
| Fine percale cotton | Cotton | Gin + card + comb | Combed cotton, smooth |
| Denim, standard cotton | Cotton | Gin + card | Carded cotton ring spun |
| Silk charmeuse | Silk | Reel + desize | Degummed silk filament |
| Blended performance | Wool + cotton | Comb both separately | Blended worsted |

## Common Confusion Points

**Carding and combing are not interchangeable** -- Carding creates random-ish orientation for woolen preparation (soft, airy); combing creates parallel alignment for worsted preparation (smooth, strong). Both are done with toothed tools, but the process, the output, and the resulting yarn properties are fundamentally different.

**Temperature control in scouring is not optional** -- The felting point of wool is not a sharp threshold, but the risk rises sharply above 45-50°C combined with agitation. A fleece that looked fine in the scour bowl can emerge as a solid felt mass if the temperature drifted up and you squeezed it. The rule: handle hot wool gently, maintain consistent temperature, never wring.

**Retting pollutes water** -- Water retting of flax creates highly oxygen-depleting effluent (BOD spikes from anaerobic bacterial activity). The Lys River in Belgium, historically the finest water-retting location, had environmental issues from retting operations. Modern flax often uses enzyme retting (pectinase) instead.

**"Combed" on yarn labels does not always mean worsted spun** -- In the commercial cotton industry, "combed" means the short fibers were removed by a combing machine, producing smoother, stronger yarn. But cotton combed yarn is still ring-spun and structured differently from wool worsted. The term combed has different technical connotations depending on fiber.
