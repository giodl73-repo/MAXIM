# Natural Dye Sources: Plants, Insects, Lichens, Tannins

## The Big Picture

Natural dyes come from three kingdoms: plants (leaves, roots, bark, berries), animals (scale insects, shellfish), and fungi/lichens. The chemical taxonomy matters: the same dye class (e.g., indigoid) appears in unrelated organisms. What the dyer cares about is not botanical taxonomy but dye chemistry: which compounds produce which colors, with which mordants, with what fastness.

```
NATURAL DYE SOURCE TAXONOMY

  PLANTS
  |-- BLUE
  |   |-- Woad (Isatis tinctoria): leaves, Europe -- precursor indigo
  |   +-- Indigo (Indigofera tinctoria): leaves, tropical -- true indigo
  |       Polygonum (Persicaria tinctoria): leaves, Japan
  |       Lonchocarpus cyanescens (West Africa -- "African indigo")
  |-- RED/ORANGE
  |   |-- Madder (Rubia tinctorum): root -- alizarin, purpurin
  |   +-- Henna (Lawsonia inermis): leaves -- lawsone (no mordant needed)
  |-- YELLOW (most plant sources)
  |   |-- Weld (Reseda luteola): whole plant -- luteolin (highest lightfastness)
  |   |-- Onion skins: quercetin/kaempferol
  |   +-- Pomegranate rind, weld, dyer's chamomile
  |-- TANNINS (structural, mordanting-helper role)
      Oak gall, sumac, walnut, pomegranate rind, black tea

  ANIMALS
  |-- INSECTS
  |   |-- Cochineal (Dactylopius coccus): carminic acid -- red
  |   |-- Kermes (Kermes vermilio): kermesic acid -- pre-cochineal European red
  |   +-- Lac (Kerria lacca): laccaic acid -- red/purple
  +-- MOLLUSKS
      Murex snails (Murex brandaris, Bolinus brandaris): dibromoindigo -- Tyrian purple

  LICHENS
  |-- Orchil/Cudbear (Rocella, Ochrolechia): orcein -- purple, pH-sensitive
  +-- Litmus (Roccella, Lasallia): pH indicator origin

  MINERALS
  +-- Iron (ferrous sulfate, iron-rich water): tannate-iron complex -> grey/black
      Ochres (direct pigment, not mordant dye): iron oxides
```

## The Indigoid Dyes: Blue Chemistry

### Woad (Isatis tinctoria)

Woad is a brassica (mustard family) native to Central Asia, cultivated throughout Europe for centuries as the primary blue dye. The blue comes not from a simple compound but from an indigo precursor:

```
WOAD CHEMISTRY

  Fresh leaf: isatan B (colorless glycoside) + small amounts indigo
              (Indigofera contains 10x more indigo than woad)

  FERMENTATION VAT PROCESS:
  1. Harvest leaves, pound to paste
  2. Dry to "woad balls" (couched woad)
  3. Rewet, ferment with stale urine (ammonia source) for weeks
  4. Hydrolysis of isatan B -> indoxyl (leuco form)
  5. Exposure to air -> oxidation -> indigo (blue)

  HISTORICAL NOTE: The color was never as intense as Indigofera indigo,
  which is why woad was displaced after Portuguese traders opened sea
  routes to India (~1500). However, woad was used as a "base" pre-dye
  for Saxon green and Lincoln green (woad + weld).
```

### True Indigo (Indigofera tinctoria)

The dominant blue dye plant globally, containing 0.2-0.8% indigo by dry weight:

```
INDIGO CHEMISTRY

  Indigofera leaves contain: indican (indoxyl-beta-D-glucoside)

  VAT REDUCTION (traditional and modern):
  1. Fermentation or alkaline reduction to leuco-indigo (indigo white)
     indigo (C16H10N2O2, MW 262) + 2H -> leuco-indigo (soluble, yellow-green)
  2. Fiber immersed in reduced vat (oxygen-free)
  3. Fiber removed, air-oxidized -> insoluble indigo precipitates IN fiber
  4. Repeat dipping for depth: each dip adds ~0.01% indigo by weight

  Chemical structure: bis-oxindole with central C=C bridge
  Color: characteristic blue (absorption peak ~620nm, reflects blue-indigo)
  Lightfastness: Blue Wool Scale 6-7 (excellent for natural dye)

  TRADITIONAL VATS:
    Bengal vat (India): Indigofera fresh leaves, slaked lime, water
    Japanese sukumo vat: composted Persicaria tinctoria leaves + wood ash lye
    West African vat: Indigofera, potash from burned banana stalks

  MODERN NATURAL DYE VAT:
    Iron vat: ferrous sulfate + lime (strongly alkaline, suitable for wool)
    Fructose/lime vat (1,2,3 vat): safest for beginners
    Spectrachrome vat: zinc-lime (faster, less safe)
```

## Madder (Rubia tinctorum): Red Chemistry

Madder root is the most historically important red dye plant. Cultivated extensively in France, Turkey, India, and Persia. The chemistry centers on two anthraquinone compounds:

```
MADDER CHEMISTRY

  ROOT CONTAINS:
    Alizarin (1,2-dihydroxyanthraquinone): primary red compound
    Purpurin (1,2,4-trihydroxyanthraquinone): orange modifier
    Pseudopurpurin: converts to purpurin on boiling

  ALIZARIN CHROMOPHORE:
    Anthraquinone core = benzene ring fused to two carbonyl groups
    Hydroxyl groups at positions 1,2 -> chelate metal mordants
    Al3+, Ca2+, Mg2+ complex -> range of reds
    Fe2+/Fe3+ complex -> purple-black (Turkey Red with iron)

  COLOR SHIFTS BY MORDANT:
    Alum mordant: bright red-orange (classic "madder red")
    Iron mordant: dark purple-brown (saddened, shifted)
    Copper mordant: brownish orange
    Tin mordant: bright scarlet (brighter than alum)
    Alum + chalk: brighter red (calcium modifies chelate)

  TURKEY RED (historical prestige red):
    Technique from Anatolia and Levant
    Process: oil pre-mordant (bull's oil, dung oil) + alum + madder
    Achieved extraordinary fastness on cotton (normally difficult to dye)
    Industrial secret guarded in 18th-century Europe
    Destroyed economically by synthetic alizarin (1869)
```

## Weld (Reseda luteola): Best Natural Yellow

Weld is the highest-lightfastness natural yellow dye. Historically called "dyer's weld" and used across Europe for yellow and, combined with woad, for Lincoln green:

```
WELD CHEMISTRY

  Primary compound: luteolin (5,7,3',4'-tetrahydroxyflavone)
  Secondary: apigenin, chrysoeriol

  FLAVONOID STRUCTURE:
    Two phenyl rings connected by a three-carbon bridge (C15 core)
    Multiple hydroxyl groups -> excellent metal chelation
    More hydroxyl groups than most flavonoids -> better mordant binding

  LIGHTFASTNESS:
    Blue Wool Scale 5-6 on alum mordant (best for natural yellows)
    Compare: onion skins (quercetin) BW 3-4; turmeric BW 1-2 (fugitive)

  COLOR:
    Alum mordant: clear bright yellow
    Iron mordant: greenish yellow (olive)
    Copper mordant: deeper yellow
    Alum + copper: khaki green

  LINCOLN GREEN = weld (yellow) + woad (blue)
    Robin Hood's color: achieved by double dyeing
    Woad first (blue), then weld over top (blue + yellow = green)
    or: indigo overdye on weld-dyed cloth
```

## Cochineal (Dactylopius coccus): The Insect Red

Cochineal is a scale insect living on prickly pear cactus (Opuntia) in Mexico and Peru. The Aztecs used it as a tribute dye. Spanish conquest (1521) gave Spain a monopoly on cochineal for over a century:

```
COCHINEAL BIOLOGY AND HARVEST

  Insect: Dactylopius coccus, female body (dried for dye)
  Host plant: Opuntia ficus-indica (prickly pear cactus)
  Yield: ~70,000 dried insects per kilogram of dye
  Alternative source: Carmine (E120) food colorant = same compound

  CARMINIC ACID (C22H20O13):
    Anthraquinone glycoside (glucose attached to anthraquinone)
    10% of dry insect body weight
    Very stable -- used in cosmetics (lipstick), food, pharmaceuticals

  COLOR BEHAVIOR:
    Acidic pH (<4): orange-red
    Neutral pH (~7): red-crimson
    Alkaline pH (>9): purple
    Iron modifier: grey-purple
    Tin modifier: brilliant scarlet (stannous chloride + cochineal = Venetian scarlet)
    This pH sensitivity is what makes the dyeing pH so critical

  ECONOMIC IMPORTANCE:
    Second most valuable export from New Spain after silver (16th-18th century)
    Destroyed kermes and madder markets in Europe
    Guarded as state secret -- Spaniards executed for revealing source plant/insect
    Synthetic alizarin (1869) and synthetic carmine eventually replaced it
    BUT carminic acid still used in cosmetics today -- "natural" red E120
```

## Kermes and Lac: Pre-Columbian European and Asian Reds

Before cochineal reached Europe, kermes was the prestige red:

```
KERMES (Kermes vermilio)
  Scale insect on kermes oak (Quercus coccifera), Mediterranean region
  Active compound: kermesic acid (anthraquinone, like carminic acid)
  Yield: ~20x LESS dye per gram than cochineal
  Color: deep red, slightly bluer than madder
  Historical use: "grain" or "scarlet grain" -- ground into powder
  Vermillion and crimson both derive etymologically from kermes/worm

LAC DYE (Kerria lacca / Laccifer lacca)
  Scale insect on Indian/SE Asian host trees
  Deposits lac resin (shellac) + laccaic acid dye
  Color: red to red-violet
  Mordants: alum = red; iron = purple-black
  Still used in India
```

## Tannins: The Mordant Helpers

Tannins are not primarily dye molecules but play a crucial structural role in dyeing plant (cellulose) fibers. They act as "pre-mordants":

```
TANNIN CHEMISTRY

  Hydrolyzable tannins: esters of gallic acid and glucose
    Oak galls (Quercus infectoria): 50-70% tannin -- highest concentration
    Sumac (Rhus coriaria): 15-25% tannin
    Pomegranate rind: 10-20% tannin

  Condensed tannins (proanthocyanidins): flavonoid polymers
    Quebracho bark, mimosa bark, chestnut -- used in leather tanning

  ROLE IN DYEING COTTON AND LINEN:
    Cellulose fibers lack -NH2 groups for metal mordant binding
    Tannin deposits on cellulose via hydrogen bonding
    Metal mordant (alum) then binds to tannin's hydroxyl groups
    Dye binds to metal ion

    SEQUENCE:
    1. Tannin bath: fiber + oak gall solution, 60°C, 1hr
    2. Alum mordant bath: fiber + alum, 80°C, 1hr
    3. Dye bath: mordanted fiber + dye, 80°C, 1hr

    Without tannin step: alum barely sticks to cotton
    With tannin: good uptake and fastness

  OAK GALL INK:
    Oak gall tannin + iron sulfate + gum arabic
    Reacts to form iron gallate = intensely black
    Standard manuscript ink in Europe from ~400 CE to ~1850
    The ink "bites" the parchment (hence: mordant)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Color Needed | Primary Source | Mordant | Notes |
|-------------|----------------|---------|-------|
| Blue (any fiber) | Indigo (Indigofera) | No mordant -- vat process | Lightfast; vat chemistry required |
| Blue (historic, Europe) | Woad | No mordant -- vat process | Less intense than Indigofera |
| Red (wool, fast) | Cochineal | Alum | Brightest, most lightfast red |
| Red (brilliant scarlet) | Cochineal | Tin (stannous chloride) | Fiber damage risk |
| Red-orange (wool) | Madder | Alum | Earthier than cochineal |
| Dark brown-purple (wool) | Madder | Iron | "Saddened" color |
| Turkey Red (cotton) | Madder | Oil + alum (complex) | Historic cotton red process |
| Best yellow | Weld | Alum | BW 5-6, highest natural yellow |
| Quick yellow (fugitive) | Onion skins | Alum | Easy; BW 3-4 only |
| Purple | Cochineal + alkali | Alum + soda ash | Shift dye bath pH > 8 |
| Green | Weld + overdye indigo | Alum | Yellow base, blue over |
| Grey-black | Iron with any tannin | Iron | Iron sulfate in tannin-rich bath |
| Cotton/linen any color | Any + tannin first | Tannin -> alum | Pre-mordant with oak gall |

## Common Confusion Points

**Woad is not indigo, but produces indigo** -- Woad produces indigo through fermentation of its precursor compounds. The molecule is identical to Indigofera indigo. But woad leaves contain ~10x less indigo precursor than Indigofera, so woad vats are weaker and harder to maintain.

**Cochineal insects are killed by Spaniards, not the dye** -- The color is in the dried female body. The insects are killed (hot water immersion, hot air, steam) to stop fermentation. Ground dried insects = cochineal powder. This is why "carmine" in food/cosmetics is an insect product, not synthetic.

**Madder and alizarin are not synonyms** -- Madder is the root containing multiple compounds including alizarin. Synthetic alizarin (from 1869) was cheaper than growing madder but only reproduced one component. The full complexity of madder-dyed cloth (purpurin adds warmth and depth) was never exactly replicated synthetically.

**Turmeric looks great, fades fast** -- Curcumin (turmeric) has Blue Wool Scale 1-2 -- extremely fugitive. It photographs beautifully and is commonly used in craft tutorials because it is cheap and available, but it will visibly fade in months of light exposure.
