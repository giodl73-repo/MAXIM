# Mordanting: Chemistry of Color Fixation

## The Big Picture

A mordant (Latin: mordere, to bite) is a metal salt that forms a coordination complex bridging the dye molecule and the fiber. Without it, most natural dyes have insufficient affinity for fiber and wash out. The choice of mordant determines not just whether the color stays, but what color it becomes — the same dye yields completely different hues on different mordants.

```
MORDANT COORDINATION CHEMISTRY

     FIBER                   DYE MOLECULE
      |                           |
      |  -OH or -NH2 groups       |  -OH, -COOH, -NH2 groups
      |  (protein or              |  (natural dye chromophore)
      |   tannin-treated cellulose)|
      |                           |
      +------>  METAL ION  <------+
              (Al3+, Fe2+, Cu2+, Sn2+, Cr3+)
               forms coordination bonds to both

  The metal ion occupies a central position in a coordination
  complex: octahedral for Al3+, Cu2+, Fe3+; tetrahedral for some.

  RESULT: Dye molecule is spatially fixed within fiber matrix.
  The geometry of the complex determines which wavelengths are absorbed
  and therefore what color the dyed fiber displays.
```

## The Five Classic Mordants

### Alum (Potassium Aluminium Sulfate)

```
FORMULA: KAl(SO4)2 * 12H2O
         (potassium alum, also called "roche alum" historically)
         or: Al2(SO4)3 * 18H2O (aluminium sulfate, more common modern)

COORDINATION CHEMISTRY:
  Al3+ forms octahedral complexes with 6 ligands
  In fiber: binds to -OH and -NH2 groups
  With dye: binds to alizarin's catechol-like -OH groups

WORKING CONDITIONS:
  Concentration: 15-20% wof (weight of fiber)
  Temperature: 80-90°C, 45-60 minutes
  pH: slightly acid to neutral works well
  Pre-mordant sequence: mordant BEFORE dye bath (standard method)

COLORS PRODUCED:
  Madder: bright red-orange
  Weld: clear yellow
  Cochineal: bright red to crimson
  Black-eyed Susan: yellow-gold
  Indigo: not applicable (vat dye, no mordant)

CHARACTERISTICS:
  Most common mordant. Bright, clear colors.
  Safe: low toxicity. No fiber damage.
  Alum wool slightly tacky -- "over-alum" effect if too concentrated
  Good lightfastness and washfastness when properly used
  Historically available from natural alunite deposits (Tolfa, Italy -- 15th c.)
```

### Iron (Ferrous Sulfate)

```
FORMULA: FeSO4 * 7H2O (ferrous sulfate, "copperas", "green vitriol")

COORDINATION CHEMISTRY:
  Fe2+ (ferrous) -> gradually oxidizes to Fe3+ (ferric) in solution
  Fe3+ forms very stable complexes with catechol groups (like alizarin)
  Forms intense dark complexes -- shifts hue darker and cooler

WORKING CONDITIONS:
  Concentration: 2-4% wof (MUCH less than alum -- iron is powerful)
  Temperature: 50-60°C, 20-30 minutes
  Can be used as post-mordant (add AFTER dyeing) to "sadden" color
  Or as modifier in the dye bath itself

COLOR SHIFTS (vs. alum):
  Any dye + iron = darker, cooler, greyer
  Madder + iron = purple-brown to khaki
  Weld + iron = greenish olive
  Cochineal + iron = grey-purple
  Oak gall + iron = black (classic iron gall ink chemistry)
  Tannin + iron = grey through black (depends on tannin concentration)

CHARACTERISTICS:
  "Saddening" effect -- universally darkens and shifts color toward grey/green
  Protein fiber damage: Fe3+ can catalyze oxidative degradation of wool
  Use sparingly. Post-mordant technique: less fiber damage than pre-mordant.
  "Iron water": leave iron tools in water -- iron-rich water used as mordant
```

### Copper (Copper Sulfate)

```
FORMULA: CuSO4 * 5H2O (copper(II) sulfate, "blue vitriol", "bluestone")

COORDINATION CHEMISTRY:
  Cu2+ forms square planar or distorted octahedral complexes
  Characteristic: shifts dyes toward green and blue-green
  Excellent antimicrobial properties (copper's oligodynamic effect)

WORKING CONDITIONS:
  Concentration: 2-3% wof
  Temperature: 80-90°C, 45 minutes
  Pre-mordant or in the dye bath

COLOR SHIFTS:
  Madder + copper = brownish orange to terracotta
  Weld + copper = deeper, slightly greenish yellow
  Cochineal + copper = muted orange-red
  Any yellow + copper = move toward green
  Oak gall + copper = black-green

CHARACTERISTICS:
  Greening effect -- shift toward cooler, greener tones
  Better fastness than iron for some dye combinations
  Moderate toxicity: wear gloves; dispose carefully
  Historic use: copper kettles contributed Cu2+ to all dye baths
    (Many historic recipes in copper pots produced colors no longer exactly replicable)
  Antimicrobial: copper-mordanted wool resists mildew
```

### Tin (Stannous Chloride)

```
FORMULA: SnCl2 * 2H2O (stannous chloride, "tin salt", "tin crystals")

COORDINATION CHEMISTRY:
  Sn2+ forms complexes that shift dye absorption toward shorter wavelengths
  Resulting color: brighter, more fluorescent appearance
  Shift toward orange and red from warmer starting colors

WORKING CONDITIONS:
  Concentration: 6% wof maximum (less is safer)
  Temperature: mordant bath at 50-60°C; dye bath at lower temp to avoid damage
  Acidic conditions (cream of tartar, 6% wof, added alongside tin)

COLOR SHIFTS:
  Cochineal + tin = brilliant scarlet to flame red (Venetian/Dutch scarlet)
  Madder + tin = orange-red, brighter than alum
  Weld + tin = bright golden yellow

CHARACTERISTICS:
  Brightening effect -- saturates and intensifies red-orange colors
  FIBER DAMAGE: Sn2+ can catalyze fiber degradation; "tender" wool results
  Minimum concentration and lower dye bath temperature mandatory
  Historic "Hungarian red" and "Scarlet of Venice" relied on tin
  Not used for plant fibers (damage risk, poor affinity)
```

### Chrome (Potassium Dichromate)

```
FORMULA: K2Cr2O7 (potassium dichromate, "chrome mordant")

COORDINATION CHEMISTRY:
  Cr3+ (reduced from Cr6+ in fiber) forms very stable octahedral complexes
  Chromium's d-orbitals allow strong coordination with multiple dye types
  Produces typically dark, muted, but very fast colors

WORKING CONDITIONS:
  Concentration: 2-3% wof
  Alkaline conditions + fiber -> Cr6+ reduces to Cr3+ in the fiber
  Temperature: 80-90°C

COLORS:
  Madder + chrome = warm red-brown
  Weld + chrome = olive to khaki
  Any dye + chrome = muted, dark, excellent fastness

CHARACTERISTICS:
  HEALTH HAZARD: Cr6+ is a known carcinogen (lung cancer, skin sensitizer)
  Not recommended for home dyeing without proper industrial safety protocols
  Still used in commercial wool dyeing for excellent fastness
  Chrome-mordanted wool has Blue Wool Scale 6-7 with some dyes
  Listed under ZDHC MRSL (Manufacturing Restricted Substances List)
```

## Mordanting Sequences

Three timing approaches, each with different outcomes:

```
PRE-MORDANTING (standard)
  1. Mordant bath with metal salt
  2. Dye bath
  ORDER: fiber soaks up metal -> metal-fiber bond forms first
  ADVANTAGE: Even mordanting; best for most dyes

SIMULTANEOUS MORDANTING (in-bath)
  1. Metal salt + dye in same bath
  ADVANTAGE: Convenient, one step
  DISADVANTAGE: Metal-dye complex may form in solution before bonding to fiber
    -> uneven results, lower uptake efficiency

POST-MORDANTING (modification / "saddening")
  1. Dye bath (with minimal or no mordant)
  2. Mordant bath after dyeing
  ADVANTAGE: Iron post-mordant can shift and fix color in one step
  Used for "saddening" -- iron added at end of dye process to darken
  Reduces iron damage vs. pre-mordant

DOUBLE MORDANTING:
  Example: alum pre-mordant, then copper post-mordant
  Achieves color not possible with either alone
  More labor-intensive; used for specific historical recipes
```

## pH Effects on Dye Color

pH dramatically shifts color for pH-sensitive dyes (especially cochineal):

```
COCHINEAL pH SENSITIVITY

  pH < 4:    Orange-red (acid)
  pH 5-6:    Red-crimson (slightly acid -- alum bath)
  pH 7:      Red
  pH 8-9:    Purple-red
  pH > 10:   Purple to blue-violet (alkali)

MODIFIERS USED:
  Cream of tartar (potassium bitartrate): acid, pH 4-5, brightens
  Vinegar: acid, brightens slightly
  Wood ash water: alkaline, purples
  Soda ash (sodium carbonate): strongly alkaline, violet-purple
  Ammonia: alkaline, purple
  Iron sulfate: acidic initially, shifts hue by chelation

pH MEASUREMENT:
  pH strips sufficient for craft dyeing (strips calibrated 4-10)
  Digital pH meter: more precise for consistent results
  NOTE: The pH of the dye bath changes during dyeing as dye is exhausted
```

## Tannin Mordanting for Plant Fibers

Cellulose fibers (cotton, linen, hemp) lack the amine groups that coordinate metal mordants. Tannins provide a bridge:

```
WHY CELLULOSE NEEDS TANNIN

  WOOL protein: -NH2 groups abundant -> coordinates Al3+ directly
  COTTON cellulose: only -OH groups -> weak Al3+ coordination

  TANNIN BRIDGE:
    1. Tannin deposits on cellulose via hydrogen bonding (-OH to -OH)
    2. Tannin's multiple catechol groups coordinate Al3+ strongly
    3. Al3+ then coordinates dye molecule

  TANNIN SOURCES FOR MORDANTING (not for color):
    Oak gall (Quercus infectoria): 50-70% tannin -- strongest
    Black tea: 3-10% tannin -- available, mild
    Sumac (Rhus coriaria): 15-25%
    Quebracho bark: 20-30%

  PROCESS:
    Wet fiber (pre-wetted in water)
    Tannin bath: 5-10% tannin by fiber weight, 60°C, 1 hour
    Rinse (don't wash out)
    Alum mordant bath: 15% wof, 80°C, 45 min
    Rinse
    Dye bath

  WITHOUT TANNIN:
    Cotton mordanted only with alum: ~30% of expected color depth
    Cotton mordanted with tannin + alum: ~90% of expected color depth
```

## Mordant Safety and Disposal

```
MORDANT      SAFETY LEVEL    DISPOSAL
----------------------------------------------
Alum         Safe for craft  Dilute, drain -- low toxicity
Iron sulfate  Low risk        Dilute, drain -- local regulations
Copper sulfate Moderate      Collect, hazardous waste disposal
Tin (SnCl2)   Moderate       Collect, hazardous waste disposal
Chrome (Cr6+) HAZARDOUS       Strict industrial protocols only

GENERAL PRINCIPLES:
  Use stainless steel, glass, or enamel vessels (not aluminum for acid baths)
  Never reuse mordant pots for food
  Wear gloves (nitrile, not latex)
  Ventilation for hot baths
  Keep mordant labeling clear (never in food containers)

pH NEUTRALIZATION BEFORE DISPOSAL:
  Acid dye baths (pH < 5): neutralize with baking soda to pH 7 before draining
  Alkaline baths (pH > 9): neutralize with vinegar to pH 7 before draining
  Metal ions: do not dilute and drain in areas with strict wastewater regulations
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Mordant" as verb vs. noun**: Mordanting is the process; the mordant is the chemical. "I mordanted the wool with alum" -- correct. "I alum'd the wool" -- less formal but understood.

**Iron "saddening" is a post-process option, not a failure**: Adding iron at the end of dyeing to darken and shift a color is a deliberate technique. Medieval dyers added iron-rich water to their dye baths routinely. What looks like a "ruined" bright red shifting to dark brown is exactly what was intended for shadow yarns.

**% wof means weight of fiber, not volume**: 15% alum wof means 15 grams alum per 100 grams dry fiber (not volume of water). The ratio is to the fiber mass. Water quantity affects concentration but the mordant-to-fiber ratio is what matters for uptake.

**Simultaneous mordant/dye baths save time but risk precipitation**: Metal ions and dye molecules can combine in solution before either reaches the fiber. This creates insoluble complexes that float in the bath and produce uneven, patchy dyeing. Pre-mordanting is more reliable.
