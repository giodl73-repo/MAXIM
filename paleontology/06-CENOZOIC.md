## Paleocene (66–56 Ma) — Mammal Explosion

```
AFTERMATH OF K-Pg:
  75% of species gone → massive ecological void
  Most mammals at K-Pg: <1 kg (shrew/rat-sized), generalist/omnivore/insectivore

ADAPTIVE RADIATION TIMESCALE:
  Within 300,000 years of K-Pg: mammals larger than 25 kg appear
  Within 700,000 years: mammals as large as 50 kg
  (cf. previous 150 Myr of dinosaur domination: mammals stayed small throughout)

FIRST MODERN ORDERS (Paleocene/Eocene boundary):
  Perissodactyla (horses, rhinos, tapirs): Eohippus ~56 Ma
  Artiodactyla (cows, deer, pigs): earliest ~56 Ma
  Primates: Purgatorius (~65 Ma) from Paleocene; uncertain but early
  Carnivora: earliest ~56 Ma (Miacids)
  Rodents: ~60 Ma
  Cetacea (whales): ~52 Ma — from land mammals (Pakicetus: wolf-sized, shore-dwelling)

CONDYLARTHS:
  Archaic ungulate-grade herbivores; not a natural group (wastebasket taxon)
  But ecological role: ground-level herbivore filling dinosaur herbivore niche
  Replaced by Artiodactyla and Perissodactyla once modern ungulates establish

PALEOCENE ENVIRONMENT:
  High CO₂ (~1000 ppm, 2× Eocene levels at 56 Ma) → warm greenhouse
  No polar ice; forests extend to 60°N
  Tethys Sea still open; Atlantic widening but narrow
  India: racing northward toward Asia (~15 cm/yr — continental collision imminent)
```

## Eocene (56–34 Ma) — Warm World and Key Transitions

```
EOCENE THERMAL MAXIMUM (ETM):
  Base of Eocene: PETM (Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum) ~55.8 Ma
  See 10-PALEOCLIMATOLOGY.md for detailed treatment
  +5–8°C global warming in <20 kyr; massive carbon injection; ocean acidification
  Mammal radiation associated with warm conditions; horse and primate dispersal event

EOCENE FAUNA:
  WHALES (Cetacea):
    ~52 Ma: Pakicetus — 4-legged, wolf-sized, shore-foraging (bone density → aquatic)
    ~47 Ma: Ambulocetus — "walking whale"; could walk and swim
    ~40 Ma: Basilosaurus — fully marine, elongated; no functional hind limbs
    ~35 Ma: first archaeocetes → mysticetes + odontocetes (modern whale lineages)
    Cetacean evolution: fastest major body plan transition in mammal record
    Molecular: closest living relatives are hippos (Artiodactyla)
    Isotope evidence: Pakicetus freshwater/estuarine diet → progressive marine shift

  EARLY HORSES:
    Hyracotherium (Eohippus) ~56 Ma: dog-sized, 4-toed, forest browser
    Mesohippus (~37 Ma): 3-toed; somewhat larger; begins grazing
    Progressive series: grazer ecology develops as grasslands form
    Diversification: North America; multiple dispersal events to Europe, Asia, Africa

  PERISSODACTYL DIVERSITY:
    Eocene: most diverse in Cenozoic (>200 species; now 17 species total)
    Brontotheres (Titanotheres): rhinoceros-sized; horn-like fused nasal ossifications
    Chalicotheres: horse-relatives with claws (for pulling branches?)

EOCENE/OLIGOCENE TRANSITION (~34 Ma):
  "Grande Coupure" (Great Break): sharp faunal change in Europe
  Antarctic ice sheet initiates → global cooling + sea level fall
  Cause: Drake Passage (Antarctica-S. America) and Tasmanian Gateway open
    → Antarctic Circumpolar Current (ACC) establishes
    → ACC thermally isolates Antarctica
    → Antarctic glaciation → global cooling

  LAND MAMMAL TURNOVER:
    European fauna replaced by Asian immigrants (continental connection re-established)
    Many archaic Eocene groups go extinct
```

## Oligocene (34–23 Ma) — Cooling and the Modern Landscape Begins

```
OLIGOCENE CLIMATE:
  Antarctic ice sheet established (~34 Ma onward)
  Global temperature 3–5°C cooler than Eocene peak
  Glacial-interglacial fluctuations begin (but less intense than Pleistocene)
  CO₂: ~500–700 ppm → driving the cooling toward ice-point thresholds

GRASS EVOLUTION:
  Poaceae (grasses): Eocene origin (~60 Ma), but EXPAND in late Eocene–Oligocene
  C₄ grasses: more efficient in warm, low-CO₂ conditions; expand ~8–4 Ma (Miocene-Pliocene)
  Why grass matters:
    Grazing mammals: need hypsodont (high-crowned) teeth to handle silica-rich grass
    Teeth track CO₂: low pCO₂ → C₄ grass → high-crowned tooth evolution
    Open savanna habitat: completely restructures mammal community ecology

OLIGOCENE MAMMALS:
  Paraceratherium (Indricotherium): largest terrestrial mammal ever
    Hornless rhinoceros; shoulder height ~5.5 m; mass ~15–20 tonnes
    Browsed from tall trees; fossil record: Kazakhstan to China
  Pig-like artiodactyls diversify
  Rodents: diversifying explosively
  Primates: anthropoids (monkey ancestors) dispersing from Africa to South America?
    ~35–30 Ma: first New World monkeys (platyrrhines) — how did they get there?
    Rafting hypothesis: vegetation mats across narrow Atlantic (still narrower)
```

## Miocene (23–5 Ma) — Grasses, Apes, and Grassland Ecosystems

```
MIOCENE CLIMATE:
  Middle Miocene Climatic Optimum (17–15 Ma): warm interval; CO₂ rose briefly
  After 14 Ma: RENEWED COOLING; East Antarctic ice sheet grows
  Tethys Sea closes (India-Asia collision → Himalayas; Arabia-Africa → Tethys cut off)

MIOCENE GRASSLAND EXPANSION:
  Open savanna-steppe spreads across Africa, Asia, Americas, Europe
  C₄ grass expansion: ~8–4 Ma (more abrupt in Africa and Americas)
  Coupled with: evolution of hypsodont (high-crowned) teeth in horses, rhinos, bovids
  Radiation of:
    Bovidae (cattle, antelope, gazelles): explosive in late Miocene
    Equidae: Hipparion (3-toed) disperses from N. America to Old World ~11 Ma
    Proboscidea (elephants): disperse globally; multiple genera
    Carnivores following: felids, hyenids, canids

APE RADIATION:
  Miocene: AGE OF APES — 30+ genera of hominoids globally (now 6 genera remain)
  ~20–8 Ma: apes dominant large primates in Africa, Europe, Asia
  Key taxa:
    Proconsul (~17 Ma, E. Africa): arboreal, no tail; ancestral to all hominoids
    Sivapithecus (~12–8 Ma, S. Asia): ancestral to orangutans (Pongo)
    Dryopithecus (~12–8 Ma, Europe): ancestral to great apes?
    Gigantopithecus (started ~8 Ma, Asia): largest primate ever (2–3 m, 200–300 kg)
      Survived to ~300,000 BP — overlapped with Homo erectus in Asia!

HOMINID-HOMININ SPLIT:
  Molecular clock: human-chimp divergence ~7–6 Ma
  Fossil record: Sahelanthropus (~7 Ma, Chad), Orrorin (~6 Ma, Kenya)
    Both bipedal (to some degree); hominin-like features
  Ardipithecus (~5.8–4.4 Ma): most complete early hominid fossils (Ardi)
    Arboreal + bipedal; less chimpanzee-like than expected
    → Common ancestor less chimp-like than modern chimps (chimps also evolved)
```

## Pliocene (5–2.6 Ma)

```
PLIOCENE CLIMATE:
  Early Pliocene warm (~5–3 Ma): MPWP (Mid-Pliocene Warm Period) ~3.3–3.0 Ma
  Global temperature ~2–4°C warmer than today
  CO₂: ~400 ppm (comparable to present-day!) → sea level ~15–25 m higher than today
  MPWP = closest analog to near-future climate under current emissions

  After ~3 Ma: Ice ages intensify → glacial-interglacial cycles → Pleistocene

ISTHMIAN CLOSURE (~3.5–2.8 Ma):
  Isthmus of Panama completed (Central America fully connected)
  "Great American Biotic Interchange":
    N. American → S. American: horses, camels, tapirs, giant ground sloths (partially)
    S. American → N. American: giant ground sloths, glyptodonts, opossums, armadillos
  Ocean: Atlantic and Pacific finally separated → Gulf Stream intensified
    → Atlantic became saltier → more NADW → AMOC strengthened
    → Some hypothesize: contributed to N. Hemisphere glaciation by enhanced moisture transport

AUSTRALOPITHECUS AND EARLY HOMO:
  Lucy (Australopithecus afarensis): 3.2 Ma, East Africa
    Bipedal, brain ~400–500 cc (chimp-sized), mosaic features
    Footprints at Laetoli: 3.66 Ma → bipedal hominins confirmed
  Paranthropus: robust australopithecines; 2.5–1.4 Ma; large cheek teeth for grinding
  Homo habilis: ~2.4–1.4 Ma; brain ~600 cc; associated with Oldowan stone tools
  Homo rudolfensis: slightly larger brain; coexisted with H. habilis
  First stone tools: Lomekwi (~3.3 Ma, possible) or Oldowan (~2.6 Ma, certain)
```

## Pleistocene (2.6 Ma–11,700 BP) — Ice Ages and Megafauna

```
PLEISTOCENE ICE AGES:
  2.6–0.01 Ma: repeated glacial-interglacial cycles
  Orbital forcing (Milankovitch cycles): 41-kyr obliquity cycles dominated 2.6–1 Ma
  Mid-Pleistocene Transition (~1 Ma): shift to ~100-kyr cycles
    Why?: CO₂ threshold, ice volume feedback, uncertain
  At glacial maximum (Last Glacial Maximum, 21,000 BP):
    Ice sheets: Laurentide (N. America), Fennoscandian (Europe), others
    Sea level: ~125 m below present
    Temperatures: global -4 to -6°C; N. American interiors -15 to -20°C from present

MEGAFAUNA OF THE PLEISTOCENE:
  Africa (least extinct):
    Elephants (Loxodonta, Elephas): multiple species present
    Most African megafauna survived → evolved with human hunters
  Eurasia:
    Woolly mammoth (Mammuthus primigenius): 3–4 tonnes, 3.4 m shoulder
    Woolly rhinoceros (Coelodonta antiquitatis): cold-adapted
    Cave bear (Ursus spelaeus): ~400 kg; Europe
    Cave lion (Panthera spelaea): 25% larger than African lion
    Irish elk (Megaloceros giganteus): 3.7 m antler span
  North America:
    Columbian mammoth: up to 4.5 tonnes, not woolly (warmer range)
    Mastodons (Mammut americanum): browser; woodland-adapted
    American horse (Equus ferus scotti): extinct; horses returned with Spanish 1493
    Giant ground sloth (Megatherium, 6 tonnes; Megalonyx, 1 tonne)
    Glyptodon: 2-tonne armadillo
    Short-faced bear (Arctodus simus): possibly largest terrestrial mammal carnivore
    Saber-tooth (Smilodon fatalis): 160–280 kg; 17 cm sabers
    Dire wolf (Aenocyon dirus): larger than grey wolf; 11,000+ specimens from La Brea
  Australia:
    Diprotodon: 2.8-tonne wombat-relative
    Procoptodon: giant short-faced kangaroo (3 m tall)
    Thylacoleo carnifex: marsupial lion, ~100 kg
    Genyornis: giant flightless bird; ground-nesting

PLEISTOCENE EXTINCTION (~15,000–10,000 BP):
  Most megafauna extinct: >60% of megafaunal genera in N. America, 46% in S. America
  Africa/Asia: less affected
  CAUSES DEBATED:
    Overkill Hypothesis (Martin, 1967): human hunting drove extinctions
      Evidence: extinctions follow human arrival timing globally
      Australia: megafauna extinct ~45,000 BP → humans arrived ~65,000 BP
      Americas: extinct ~12,000 BP → Clovis humans arrived ~13,000 BP
      New Zealand: moa extinct ~1300 AD → Maori arrived 1300 AD
    Climate change hypothesis: end-glacial rapid climate change → habitat loss
    Most likely: human hunting × climate vulnerability = synergistic collapse
  SURVIVOR BIAS: Animals that evolved with humans (Africa, Asia) were wary;
    naive prey (Americas, Australia, N. Atlantic islands) were easy targets

  NAIVE PREY / SELECTION EFFECT FRAMING:
    African megafauna: co-evolved with hominin hunters for ~2 million years
      → Developed anti-predator behavior (wariness, group defense, range use)
      → These behaviors are "learned" at the population level across generations
      → 2 Myr of selection pressure = robust behavioral defenses

    Americas/Australia megafauna: no prior hominin pressure
      → Naive with respect to coordinated human hunting
      → No fear response to upright bipeds; no herding anti-ambush behavior
      → Analogous to: incumbent system with no threat model for a new attack vector

    This is the same dynamic as security-naive incumbents meeting a novel threat:
      African megafauna = security-aware legacy system (multiple threat models)
      American megafauna = greenfield system with no threat model for this attack
      Humans = novel threat (new attack surface: coordinated, ranged, persistent)
      Overkill rate: mathematical models show hunting pressure 10–30× background
        extinction rate sufficient to collapse naive populations within 1–10 kyr
        (same speed as a supply-chain attack depleting an undefended network)

    Key variable: behavioral learning speed vs. hunting rate
      Selection can't keep up if hunting rate exceeds generational replacement
      "Evolutionary trap": fitness landscape changes faster than population can adapt
      Modern analog: antibiotic resistance in fast-reproducing bacteria vs.
        megafauna with 10–30 year generation times (no time to adapt)
```

## Homo Evolution Through the Pleistocene

```
HOMO EVOLUTION:
  Homo erectus (~1.9 Ma–100,000 BP):
    First Homo to leave Africa (~1.8 Ma to Eurasia)
    Acheulean tools: hand axe; used for ~1.5 Myr with minimal change ("cognitive stasis")
    Java Man, Peking Man: H. erectus variants; possibly used fire ~1 Ma
    Long temporal range: last surviving ~100,000 BP (Solo Man, Indonesia)

  Homo heidelbergensis (~600,000–200,000 BP):
    Larger brain (~1200 cc); probable ancestor of both Neanderthals and H. sapiens
    Hunted large game (Schöningen spears, ~300,000 BP — oldest wooden hunting weapons)

  Neanderthals (Homo neanderthalensis, 400,000–40,000 BP):
    European + W. Asian; cold-adapted (compact body, large nasal passages)
    Brain as large or larger than modern humans (~1500 cc; proportionally different)
    Mousterian tools; buried dead with ochre (possible ritual); cared for injured
    Admixture: ~2–4% Neanderthal DNA in non-African modern humans (interbreeding ~50,000 BP)

  Homo floresiensis (~100,000–50,000 BP, Flores Island, Indonesia):
    Body: ~1.1 m tall, ~30 kg; markedly small brain (~380 cc) despite making stone tools
    ISLAND DWARFISM: isolated populations shrink over generations due to resource limits
      Same mechanism: Pygmy mammoths on Channel Islands; dwarf elephants on Mediterranean islands
      Selection pressure: smaller body = lower caloric requirement on resource-limited island
    Coexisted briefly with modern Homo sapiens; extinction timing uncertain

  Denisovans (~300,000–50,000 BP, Siberia and Asia):
    Known primarily from ancient DNA extracted from a finger bone (Denisova Cave, 2010)
    No skull or complete skeleton — an entire species defined by genome sequence
    Admixture: 4–6% Denisovan DNA in Melanesians and Aboriginal Australians
    HLAB gene introgression from Denisovans to modern Tibetans → high-altitude adaptation
    (This is horizontal gene transfer at the population level: useful allele moves between
    species via hybridization — same as lateral gene transfer in bacteria, but via sex)

  Homo sapiens (~300,000 BP – present):
    Africa origin (Jebel Irhoud, Morocco, ~315,000 BP — earliest anatomically modern sapiens)
    Out of Africa: ~60,000–50,000 BP main dispersal (earlier waves ~120,000 BP documented)
    Multiregional vs. Out-of-Africa debate: RESOLVED in favor of OOA with regional admixture
      → H. sapiens from Africa replaced archaic populations, but with low-level hybridization
      → aDNA (ancient DNA) evidence decisive: archaic human DNA confirmed in modern genomes

  COGNITIVE REVOLUTION (~70,000–40,000 BP):
    Composite tools: multiple components (stone + wood + sinew) → complex manufacturing chain
    Symbolic behavior: personal ornaments (shells, beads), ochre use for body decoration
    Representational art: Sulawesi figurative art ~45,500 BP; Chauvet cave ~36,000 BP
    Long-distance trade networks: obsidian and shells transported 300–800 km
    Trigger debated: genetic mutation? social network threshold? environmental pressure?
    Net effect: behavioral modernity → rapid global colonization and ecological dominance
    CS analog: the cognitive revolution is a phase transition in information-processing
      capacity — the same brain hardware, but new "software" (symbolic communication,
      planning, cumulative culture) enabling qualitatively different capabilities
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Mammoths and dinosaurs did not coexist**: Woolly mammoths lived 300,000–4,000 BP. Non-avian dinosaurs went extinct 66 million years ago. The gap is 66 million years — more time separates mammoths from non-avian dinosaurs than separates us from mammoths. Humans DID coexist with mammoths, and hunted them.

**Australopithecus ≠ chimpanzee ancestor**: Australopithecus is in the human lineage (Hominini). Chimpanzees are in a separate lineage that diverged from the common ancestor of chimps and humans ~7 Ma. Australopithecus (~3–2 Ma) is our ancestor, not chimps'. Neither modern chimps nor humans are the "primitive" form — both lineages have evolved since the 7 Ma divergence.

**The Pleistocene extinction is not just North America**: It happened globally but with different timing tied to human arrival. Africa and South Asia (where hominins evolved alongside megafauna for millions of years) lost far fewer species. Islands and continents with no prior human presence (Americas, Australia, Madagascar, New Zealand, oceanic islands) suffered catastrophic losses shortly after human arrival. This is strong evidence for human causation.

**The ice ages were not one continuous ice age**: There were 20+ glacial cycles in the Pleistocene. The "ice age" we commonly refer to is the last glaciation, which peaked 21,000 BP. Interglacials were warm (and sometimes warmer than today — Eemian Interglacial ~125,000 BP: ~1–2°C warmer, sea level ~6–9 m higher). We are currently in an interglacial (Holocene).

**Neanderthals were not primitive brutes**: Neanderthals had brains as large as modern humans, used complex tools, buried their dead, used pigments, made jewelry (marine shells drilled and pigmented), and possibly had language. Their extinction ~40,000 BP coincides with modern human arrival in Europe. We interbred with them. They were a human species, not a subhuman precursor.
