# Mass Extinctions — The Big Five, Causes, Selectivity, Recovery Patterns

## The Big Picture

```
+===========================================================================+
|                THE FIVE MASS EXTINCTIONS OF THE PHANEROZOIC               |
+===========================================================================+
|                                                                           |
|  GEOLOGICAL TIME →                                                        |
|  538 Ma ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────► Now   |
|                                                                           |
|          E-O     L-Dev    P-T      T-J     K-Pg                          |
|          443     372     252      201       66                            |
|           |       |       |        |        |                             |
|  Genera  ▼       ▼       ▼        ▼        ▼                             |
|          ██     ███    █████     ███      ███                             |
|  Losses  57%    57%    >70%      47%      ~40%                            |
|         (marine genera; P-T worst by far; K-Pg most famous)              |
|                                                                           |
|  KEY:  E-O = End-Ordovician  L-Dev = Late Devonian  P-T = End-Permian   |
|        T-J = End-Triassic    K-Pg = Cretaceous-Paleogene                 |
+===========================================================================+
```

Mass extinction: a biologically meaningful episode of elevated extinction rates
affecting multiple major taxonomic groups globally, within a geologically short
interval (typically <1–3 Myr). The "Big Five" each eliminated >40% of genera.

**Framing as survival analysis**: Each taxon at each time interval has a per-lineage
extinction probability. Normally this background rate is ~0.1–0.5 genera/Myr.
During a mass extinction, rate spikes 10–100× for millions of years.
Selectivity = which traits predict survival (lower hazard ratio in Cox model terms).

## Background Extinction vs. Mass Extinction

```
NORMAL (BACKGROUND) EXTINCTION:
  ~0.1–1 extinction/million species-years (varies by clade and time)
  Turnover: new species originate faster than they die → net diversity gain
  Driven by: competitive displacement, habitat change, resource depletion
  Pattern: random with respect to clade, geography, ecology
  "Red Queen" dynamics: constant co-evolutionary arms races

MASS EXTINCTION:
  Rate: 10–1000× background for a geologically brief interval
  Severity: ≥40% of genera (operational threshold; not a law of nature)
  Selectivity: extinction is NOT random — specific traits predict survival
  Duration:
    Instantaneous (K-Pg): geologic boundary sharp to <1 cm in rock
    Extended (Late Devonian): multiple pulses over 15–25 Myr
  Recovery: 1–10 Myr typical; "reef gaps" and "disaster faunas" mark recovery

RAUP-SEPKOSKI KILL CURVE:
  Raup and Sepkoski (1982): plotted extinction intensity vs. taxonomic rank
  Showed: 5 events stood apart statistically from background
  Threshold: ~40% of families → "Big Five" defined this way
  Limitation: family-level data masks species-level severity
    (a family survives even if 99% of species die, if one lineage persists)

MEASURING SEVERITY:
  Species-level: P-T killed ~90-96% of marine species
  Genus-level: P-T killed ~70-80% of genera
  Family-level: P-T killed ~50-60% of families
  → Magnitude depends on the taxonomic level you measure
```

## 1. End-Ordovician (443 Ma) — ~57% of Genera

```
TIMING: ~443.8 Ma; two pulses separated by ~1 Myr
SEVERITY: 57% of genera; ~85% of species lost
DURATION: ~1–2 Myr

WHAT DIED:
  Marine invertebrates: brachiopods, bryozoans, trilobites, conodonts, graptolites
  Tropical carbonate shelf faunas hit hardest (reef communities)
  Graptolites: lost ~85% of species (some lineages survive in deeper water)
  Tabulate and rugose corals: decimated
  Cephalopods: severely reduced

WHAT SURVIVED:
  Taxa in deep water (cold, already adapted to low T and low O₂)
  Small-bodied, low-metabolic-rate taxa
  Taxa with wide geographic range
  Infaunal (burrowing) organisms: less exposed to surface catastrophe

CAUSE: Gondwana glaciation
  Gondwana drifted over South Pole (Africa → Antarctic position)
  Rapid ice sheet growth → global cooling → ~5–8°C sea cooling
  Mechanism:
    Sea level drop: ~100 m → loss of shallow shelf habitats (majority of marine biota)
    Cold water expansion: tropical biotas unable to migrate deeper
    Ocean circulation change: upwelling of cold, anoxic deep water
  TWO-PULSE STRUCTURE:
    Pulse 1 (glaciation onset): cold, low sea level → Hirnantian fauna briefly flourishes
      Hirnantia brachiopod assemblage: cold-adapted faunas replace tropical ones
    Pulse 2 (rapid deglaciation): glaciers melt → rapid warming → anoxia → second extinction
  HIRNANTIAN GLACIATION EVIDENCE:
    δ¹⁸O of carbonates: positive excursion (lighter water locked in ice → heavier ocean)
    Dropstones in sediments: glacial erratics at paleoequatorial latitudes
    Glacial diamictites in Sahara (then near South Pole)

RECOVERY:
  ~5–10 Myr
  Silurian: new coral reef communities rebuild
  Graptolites diversify from a few surviving lineages
```

## 2. Late Devonian (372 Ma) — ~57% of Genera

```
TIMING: ~372–359 Ma; extended over ~25 Myr; at least two main pulses
  Frasnian/Famennian (F-F) boundary ~372 Ma: most severe pulse
  Devonian/Carboniferous boundary 359 Ma: second pulse
SEVERITY: 57% of genera; ~75% of species; 70–80% of invertebrate species
DURATION: Unusual — extended event, not a single crisis

WHAT DIED:
  Reef ecosystems: stromatoporoids (the Devonian reef-builders) go extinct
    → "Reef gap" lasting ~10 Myr into Carboniferous
  Tabulate corals: severely reduced
  Placoderms: ALL extinct at D-C boundary
  Trilobites: major reduction (surviving only in few groups)
  Brachiopods: major reduction
  Ammonoids: multiple groups wiped out (survivors spark new radiation)
  Freshwater ecosystems: relatively less affected

WHAT SURVIVED:
  Early tetrapods (just emerging at this time)
  Actinopterygian fish (ray-finned — the lineage to all modern bony fish)
  Sharks and relatives
  Vascular plants on land (some affected, but survive)
  Small marine invertebrates with broad tolerance

CAUSE: Multi-causal, still debated
  ANOXIA (most supported):
    Black shales globally: organic-rich, laminated → anoxic bottom water
    Framboidal pyrite in sediments: indicator of anoxic, sulfidic (euxinic) conditions
    Anoxia spread over continental shelves (shallow water where most fauna lived)
  CO₂ DRAWDOWN BY FORESTS:
    Archaeopteris forests spread ~385–360 Ma
    Root weathering + organic C burial → CO₂ drop → global cooling
    → Loss of warm tropical seas
  SEA LEVEL FLUCTUATIONS:
    Multiple transgression-regression cycles throughout Late Devonian
    Each regression → habitat loss → extinction pulse
  BOLIDE IMPACT? (minor evidence):
    Some iridium anomalies at F-F boundary → possible small impact
    Not universally accepted; evidence weak compared to K-Pg

  DEVONIAN FOREST KILL MECHANISM:
    Trees invade continents → deep roots weather silicates → CO₂ consumed
    → Cooling → ice caps → sea level drop → tropical habitat loss
    → Nutrient runoff from forests → eutrophication → anoxia in coastal seas
    The forests essentially poisoned the Devonian marine world

SELECTIVITY:
  Tropical reef taxa: worst affected (temperature-sensitive)
  Warm-water specialists: eliminated
  Deep-water taxa: some survived (pre-adapted to cold, low-O₂)
  Freshwater taxa: less affected (no anoxia event on land)

RECOVERY:
  Reef gap: ~10 Myr before new reef-builders (new coral/sponge types replace stromatoporoids)
  Carboniferous "disaster fauna": crinoids and brachiopods in depauperate communities
  New ammonoid radiation from survivors → dominant Carboniferous cephalopods
```

## 3. End-Permian (252 Ma) — The Great Dying

```
TIMING: ~251.9 Ma; now dated to within 200 kyr (very sharp)
SEVERITY:
  Marine: ~90–96% of species; ~83% of genera; ~54% of families
  Terrestrial: ~70% of species (vertebrates); ~70–83% of insect species
  → Worst in the Phanerozoic by large margin
DURATION: ~60 kyr for main pulse (some argue 10 kyr for peak) + protracted recovery

WHAT DIED (marine):
  Fusulinids: ALL extinct (dominant foraminifera of Carboniferous-Permian)
  Tabulate corals: ALL extinct
  Rugose corals: ALL extinct (new Scleractinia arise after recovery)
  Productid brachiopods: ALL extinct (had dominated shallow seas since Cambrian)
  Trilobites: ALL extinct (had persisted since Cambrian — 270 Myr run ends here)
  Blastoids (echinoderm): ALL extinct
  Most ammonoid groups: only one lineage (Xenodiscus) survives → new radiation
  Bryozoans: >90% of species
  Crinoids: >95% of species (bottleneck to single lineage → Mesozoic radiation)
  Bony fish: severely reduced; many lineages eliminated

WHAT DIED (terrestrial):
  Gorgonopsians: ALL extinct (apex predators, sabertooth-like therapsids)
  Most therapsid groups
  Most large reptiles
  Majority of insect orders (the only mass insect extinction in history)
  Most Glossopteris flora (dominant southern hemisphere flora)

WHAT SURVIVED:
  Marine: small, low-metabolic-rate, mobile taxa; deep-water refugia species
    Surviving forams → Mesozoic foram radiation
    Nautiloids (with permeable, thick shell → acid tolerance?)
    Sharks (broad tolerance, deep water options)
  Terrestrial:
    Lystrosaurus: hardy dicynodont → dominated post-extinction landscape globally
    Cynodonts → lineage to mammals
    Archosaur ancestors → lineage to dinosaurs, pterosaurs, crocodilians
    Lycopods, horsetails, some seed plants

CAUSE: Siberian Traps Large Igneous Province (LIP)
  Siberian Traps: flood basalt volcanism, erupted ~252 Ma
  Volume: ~2–3 million km³ of lava (covers area of modern Europe)
  Duration: main pulse ~1 Myr; possibly 3–5 Myr total

  KILL MECHANISMS (cascade):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ SIBERIAN TRAPS ERUPTIONS                                            │
  │   ↓ CO₂ + SO₂ + HCl + halogens released                           │
  │   ↓                                                                 │
  │ SHORT-TERM (SO₂): Volcanic winter → rapid cooling → acid rain       │
  │   ↓                                                                 │
  │ LONG-TERM (CO₂): +5–8°C global warming                              │
  │   ↓                                                                 │
  │ OCEAN WARMING → decreased O₂ solubility → anoxia                    │
  │ OCEAN ACIDIFICATION → carbonate dissolution → reef/shell collapse   │
  │ HYPERCAPNIA: CO₂ directly toxic to marine organisms                 │
  │ OZONE DESTRUCTION: UV radiation spikes → land and ocean surface     │
  │ METHANE RELEASE: permafrost + methane clathrates melt               │
  │   → runaway feedback loop                                           │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY EVIDENCE:
    Global δ¹³C excursion: -5‰ to -8‰ at P-T boundary
      (massive input of isotopically light carbon → from volcanics + organic-C release)
    δ¹⁸O of carbonates: warming signal
    Ocean anoxia: black shales, pyrite framboids globally
    Acidification: "reef gap" at P-T; dissolution surfaces in carbonate rocks
    Mercury anomaly: volcanic eruptions → Hg spike globally in sediments
      (Hg is a proxy for LIP volcanism; found at P-T boundary worldwide)

  COMPLICATION — BURA COAL:
    Siberian Traps erupted through coal deposits → thermogenic methane
    Intrusions heated coal → CH₄ + CO₂ + halogenated compounds released
    Coal combustion may have amplified warming by 10-100× beyond basalt alone
    → "Super-killing" by organic carbon release in addition to volcanic CO₂

RECOVERY: 5–10 Myr (longest in Phanerozoic)
  DISASTER FAUNAS: Lystrosaurus monoculture globally; Claraia clam in seas
    "Dead zones": some deep ocean settings remain anoxic for 5+ Myr
    "Coal gap" in Early Triassic: forests didn't recover → no coal formed for 5 Myr
  ECOSYSTEM REBOOT:
    New reef builders: Scleractinian corals replace extinct rugose+tabulate
    New filter-feeders: bivalves replace productid brachiopods in most settings
    Synapsids → recovery by Middle Triassic, then replaced by archosaurs
```

## 4. End-Triassic (201 Ma) — ~47% of Genera

```
TIMING: ~201.3 Ma; appears to be geologically rapid (<100 kyr)
SEVERITY: 47% of genera; ~80% of marine species; major terrestrial losses
DURATION: Rapid main pulse; some argue multiple pulses over 0.6 Myr

WHAT DIED:
  Marine:
    Conodonts: ALL extinct (250+ Myr run ends here)
    Most ammonoid families: survivors produce Jurassic radiation
    Large marine reptiles: ichthyosaurs reduced (survive into Jurassic)
    Bivalve and brachiopod diversity: greatly reduced
    Most coral families: only few survive into Jurassic
  Terrestrial:
    Crurotarsi (crocodile-line archosaurs): most extinct
      Aetosaurs: ALL extinct
      Rauisuchians: ALL extinct
      Phytosaurs: ALL extinct (only true crocodilians survive)
    Large terrestrial herbivores: most synapsids remaining
    Many groups of prosauropod dinosaurs

WHAT SURVIVED:
  Dinosaurs (all 3 major clades: Theropods, Sauropodomorphs, Ornithischians)
  Pterosaurs
  Early mammals
  Crocodilians
  Marine: some plesiosaurs, many teleost fish, sharks

CAUSE: CAMP (Central Atlantic Magmatic Province) volcanism
  CAMP: erupted ~201 Ma as Pangaea rifted
  Volume: ~2.5–3 million km³ (similar scale to Siberian Traps but spread differently)
  Geography: rift-related eruptions along nascent Central Atlantic rift

  KILL MECHANISM:
    Same cascade as P-T but smaller: CO₂ → warming → ocean acidification
    δ¹³C excursion: sharp negative spike at T-J boundary (carbon injection)
    CO₂ proxy data: rapid rise in atmospheric pCO₂ (>2000 ppm from ~1000 ppm)
    Warmth + acidification → reef collapse, marine invertebrate die-off

  SELECTIVITY:
    Pseudosuchians (crocodile-line): devastated — they had been dominant
    Avemetatarsalia (bird-line, including dinosaurs): selectively survived
    WHY?: possibly physiological differences — avemetatarsalia may have had:
      More efficient respiration (ancestral air sac system)
      Different metabolic strategies
    → This is the reason dinosaurs dominated the Jurassic, not competitive superiority:
      T-J extinction opened ecological space, and dinosaurs happened to survive

RECOVERY:
  ~10–20 Myr
  Jurassic "Age of Dinosaurs" begins because pseudosuchians are gone
  Jurassic reef builders: new coral + sponge communities rebuild
  Ammonoids diversify from survivors → dominant Jurassic marine cephalopods
```

## 5. K-Pg Boundary (66 Ma) — ~40% of Genera

```
See 05-MESOZOIC.md for detailed kill mechanism and evidence.
Summary here for comparative purposes.

TIMING: 66.043 Ma; geologically instantaneous (<100 kyr; arguably <1 kyr for main pulse)
SEVERITY: ~40% of genera; ~75% of species; non-avian dinosaurs + pterosaurs + mosasaurs
          + plesiosaurs + ammonites + ~90% of planktic forams

CAUSE: Chicxulub asteroid impact (10–15 km diameter) + Deccan Traps (contributing)

WHAT SURVIVED:
  ALL groups that had survived: birds, mammals, crocodilians, turtles, lizards, snakes,
  frogs, salamanders, plants (via seeds/spores)

SELECTIVITY:
  Body size: small survivors (all surface survivors <25 kg — birds, mammals, crocodilians)
  Diet: dietary generalists survived; specialists (large herbivores, large predators) died
  Ecology: burrowing, aquatic, hibernating, seed-dispersing — all advantaged
  Physiology: ectotherms (lower caloric requirement) survived better in darkness

K-Pg UNIQUENESS:
  Only one of Big Five with confirmed extraterrestrial cause
  Fastest of the Big Five (days to years for main pulse vs. Myr for volcanic events)
  Best studied: global iridium layer, shocked quartz, Chicxulub crater mapped
  Most famous: iconic dinosaur extinction
  Paradox: "only" 40% of genera lost (less severe than P-T or L-Devonian by genera)
    but: most spectacular megafauna eliminated; total ecological reorganization
```

## Comparative Analysis of the Big Five

```
+============================================================================================+
| MASS EXTINCTION COMPARISON                                                                 |
+============================================================================================+
| Event       | Severity  | Duration  | Primary Cause         | Key Victims               |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| E-Ordovician| 57% genera| ~2 Myr    | Gondwana glaciation   | Reef fauna, graptolites  |
| L-Devonian  | 57% genera| ~25 Myr   | Anoxia + forest CO₂   | Placoderms, Devonian reef|
| P-T         | >70% genera| 60 kyr   | Siberian Traps LIP    | Trilobites, fusulinids   |
| T-J         | 47% genera| <100 kyr  | CAMP volcanism        | Crurotarsi, conodonts    |
| K-Pg        | ~40% genera| <1 kyr   | Chicxulub impact      | Non-avian dinosaurs      |
+============================================================================================+

RECOVERY TIMES (full ecosystem recovery, not just species rebound):
  E-Ordovician:  ~5–10 Myr
  L-Devonian:    ~10–15 Myr (reef gap = 10 Myr)
  P-T:           ~5–10 Myr (longest overall recovery)
  T-J:           ~10–20 Myr
  K-Pg:          ~5–10 Myr (mammal radiation rapid, but tropical reefs 10+ Myr)

COMMON PATTERNS:
  1. "Disaster faunas": immediately post-extinction, a few opportunistic taxa dominate
       → Lystrosaurus (P-T), Claraia (P-T marine), small mammals (K-Pg)
  2. Opportunistic radiation: surviving lineages rapidly diversify into vacated niches
       → Ammonoids after each extinction; mammals after K-Pg; dinosaurs after T-J
  3. Recovery overshoots: diversity sometimes temporarily exceeds pre-extinction levels
       → Ecospace utilization increases as new body plans evolve
  4. "Lazarus taxa": taxa disappear from record during extinction, reappear in recovery
       → Small populations in refugia survive, recolonize after conditions improve
```

## Selectivity — Who Dies and Why

```
SURVIVAL ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK:
  Model: Cox proportional hazards (conceptually)
    h(t) = h₀(t) × exp(β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + ...)
    h₀(t): background extinction rate at time t
    Xᵢ: traits (body size, metabolic rate, range size, etc.)
    βᵢ: log hazard ratios for each trait during extinction event

  Different events have DIFFERENT selectivity rules (different βᵢ):
    → An impact event selects differently than a glaciation

ESTIMATED HAZARD RATIOS (HR) BY TRAIT AND EVENT:
  HR > 1 → trait increases extinction risk
  HR < 1 → trait is protective
  Values below are approximate/qualitative, based on empirical selectivity studies

  TRAIT                     E-Ord  L-Dev  P-T    T-J    K-Pg   DRIVER
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Large body (>100 kg land) ~1.0   ~1.2   ~1.3   ~1.2   ~4.0   starvation
  Tropical specialist       ~2.5   ~2.0   ~1.8   ~1.6   ~1.3   thermal range
  Shallow marine habitat    ~2.0   ~2.5   ~2.0   ~1.8   ~1.5   anoxia/acid
  Deep-water habitat        ~0.4   ~0.5   ~0.6   ~0.6   ~0.8   buffered
  Wide geographic range     ~0.5   ~0.5   ~0.5   ~0.5   ~0.5   refugia avail
  Calcareous skeleton       ~1.0   ~1.0   ~2.0   ~1.8   ~1.5   acidification
  Siliceous skeleton        ~0.8   ~0.9   ~0.6   ~0.7   ~0.8   acid-resistant
  Ectotherm                 ~1.5   ~1.0   ~0.8   ~0.8   ~0.5   low food need
  High metabolic rate       ~0.8   ~0.9   ~1.2   ~1.1   ~1.8   food demand
  Dietary generalist        ~0.7   ~0.8   ~0.7   ~0.7   ~0.3   flexibility

  READING THIS TABLE:
    K-Pg: massive penalty for large body (HR ~4) + high metabolism → starvation event
    E-Ordovician: penalty for tropical + ectotherm (cold kills ectotherms) → cold event
    P-T: penalty for calcareous skeleton (acidification) + shallow marine (anoxia)
         modest penalty for large body → warming + chemistry event, not just darkness

  DETECTION NOTE: Real empirical hazard ratios estimated from:
    Foote (2005), Knoll et al. (2007), Rego et al. (2012), Payne and Finnegan (2007)
    Using genus-level databases (Paleobiology Database) and logistic/Cox regression
    Uncertainty: sampling biases in fossil record → HR estimates have wide CIs

KEY TRAITS AND THEIR SURVIVAL VALUE:

  1. BODY SIZE:
     Impact winter: large body = high food requirement → starvation
       Threshold ~25 kg: above this, essentially no survivor on land at K-Pg
     Gradual warming/anoxia (P-T, L-Dev): moderate effect; no clear threshold
     WHY small is better in sudden events:
       Food requirement scales ~BM^0.75 (metabolic scaling)
       Small animals can subsist on seeds, dead material, detritus

  2. METABOLIC RATE:
     Ectotherms: generally more resilient to food scarcity (don't need to eat as much)
     But: ectotherms vulnerable to cold events (end-Ordovician)
     Endotherms: high food demand → vulnerable to darkness/food chain collapse
     K-Pg paradox: birds (endotherm) survived, but were seed-eaters + small

  3. GEOGRAPHIC RANGE:
     Wide range = lower extinction risk (refugia available)
     Cosmopolitan taxa: consistently better survivors
     Local endemics: eliminated even by moderate regional events
     Evidence: genera with >3 continental occurrences: 2× survival rate at P-T

  4. TROPHIC POSITION:
     Primary producers and detritivores: most resilient
     Top predators: most vulnerable (cascading collapse below them)
     Specialist herbivores: moderately vulnerable (depends on plant survival)
     Omnivores: most resilient (dietary flexibility)

  5. HABITAT:
     Shallow tropical marine: most vulnerable in all events
     Deep-water taxa: more resilient (buffered from surface changes)
     Burrowing (infaunal): more resilient (buffered from water-column chemistry)
     Pelagic: variable — depends on whether phytoplankton base survives

  6. SKELETON TYPE:
     Heavily calcified: vulnerable to ocean acidification (P-T, T-J, K-Pg)
     Lightly calcified or organic: more resilient
     Siliceous (radiolarians, siliceous sponges): resilient to acidification
     Aragonite > calcite in solubility: aragonitic corals more vulnerable than calcitic brachiopods

EXTINCTION RULES DIFFER BY EVENT:
  End-Ordovician: cold tolerance wins; tropical specialists lose
  Late Devonian: deep-water and freshwater taxa favored; reefs eliminated
  P-T: physiological tolerance of hypercapnia + anoxia + warming + acidification
  T-J: similar to P-T but smaller magnitude
  K-Pg: small size + dietary flexibility + dormancy ability + seed-based diet
```

## Causes of Mass Extinctions — Summary Framework

```
THREE PRIMARY DRIVERS:
  1. LARGE IGNEOUS PROVINCES (LIPs):
     Mechanism: CO₂ + SO₂ → warming + acidification + ozone destruction
     Cases: P-T (Siberian Traps), T-J (CAMP), possibly L-Dev (Viluy Traps?)
     Timescale: 10³–10⁶ years
     Signature: negative δ¹³C excursion + Hg anomaly

  2. BOLIDE IMPACT:
     Mechanism: thermal pulse + impact winter + acid rain
     Cases: K-Pg (Chicxulub) — confirmed; others debated
     Timescale: instantaneous to 10³ years
     Signature: Ir anomaly + shocked quartz + tektites + iridium layer

  3. CLIMATE CHANGE (glaciation or warming):
     Mechanism: sea level change + thermal stress + oceanographic disruption
     Cases: End-Ordovician (glaciation)
     Timescale: 10⁴–10⁶ years
     Signature: δ¹⁸O + glacial deposits + black shales (if anoxic)

SECONDARY / AMPLIFYING FACTORS:
  Ocean anoxia / euxinia: secondary to LIP-driven warming (O₂ solubility ↓)
  Ocean acidification: secondary to CO₂ injection (from any source)
  Methane release: clathrate destabilization → runaway feedback
  Ozone destruction: volcanic halogens or impact-generated NOₓ → UV spike
  Ecological cascades: loss of keystone species → trophic collapse

PERIODICITY HYPOTHESIS (Raup and Sepkoski, 1984):
  Claimed: extinction events periodic at ~26 Myr intervals
  Proposed causes: comet showers from Oort Cloud (Nemesis star hypothesis)
  Status: NOT confirmed; statistical artifact (periodicity depends on time scale binning)
  Modern view: extinction events are NOT periodic; cluster in time due to multiple
    LIP episodes, not a regular astronomical driver

"MURDER ON THE ORIENT EXPRESS" PROBLEM:
  Many extinctions have multiple candidate causes
  P-T: Siberian Traps + coal intrusion + ocean anoxia + acidification + methane
  K-Pg: Impact + Deccan Traps + regression + biogeographic interchange
  Detection problem: how do you assign causation when all factors co-occur?
  Method: exact temporal correlation → which precedes the extinction?
    P-T: volcanism precedes boundary; impact evidence absent → volcanism primary
    K-Pg: impact coincides with boundary within <100 kyr; Deccan precedes by 200 kyr

  PARALLEL TO DISTRIBUTED SYSTEMS POSTMORTEM:
    The causation problem in mass extinction analysis is identical to root-cause
    analysis after a complex system failure:
    - Multiple correlated failures (all factors elevated simultaneously)
    - Unclear which triggered the cascade vs. which was a pre-existing condition
    - Temporal correlation is the primary method (did A precede B → A probably caused B)
    - "Contributing factors" vs. "root cause" distinction matters for remediation
    Postmortem technique: timeline reconstruction → find earliest anomaly that
      preceded failure; correlate with change events; separate triggering event
      from vulnerability that made system susceptible
    P-T example in postmortem terms:
      - Pre-existing vulnerability: already-warm, anoxia-prone ocean (climate)
      - Triggering event: Siberian Traps eruption at ~252 Ma
      - Amplifying factors: coal combustion, methane release, positive feedbacks
      - Root cause: volcanism (absent = no extinction; other factors = no extinction alone)
    K-Pg example in postmortem terms:
      - Pre-existing degraded state: Deccan Traps volcanic stress for 200 kyr
      - Triggering event: Chicxulub impact (instantaneous; absent = no acute crisis)
      - "Contributing but not causal": Deccan preceded impact; impact was proximate cause
```

## The "Sixth Extinction" — Modern Context

```
CURRENT EXTINCTION RATE:
  Background rate: ~0.1 E/MSY (extinctions per million species-years)
  Current estimated rate: 100–1000 E/MSY (10–1000× background)
  Vertebrate extinctions since 1500 CE: ~200 documented + thousands likely unrecorded

COMPARISON TO BIG FIVE:
  P-T: ~52% of families over ~60 kyr → ~0.9% of families/kyr
  Current: ~1–5% of species per century → potentially 100,000–500,000 species/century
  IF current rates continue 100 years: losses comparable to Big Five within 100–1000 yr

SELECTIVITY (modern vs. Pleistocene):
  Modern: megafauna disproportionately affected (already elevated since Pleistocene)
  Tropical deforestation: accelerates; island taxa: most vulnerable
  Ocean: coral bleaching, ocean acidification → marine version underway

IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:
  "Sixth extinction" is real in rate terms but:
    1. Still in early stages — not yet at Big Five severity
    2. Causes are direct (habitat destruction, overexploitation, invasives, climate)
       rather than a single abiotic event
    3. Recovery timescale: if humanity reduces pressures, partial recovery possible
       If P-T-equivalent: millions of years for full recovery

  Current trajectory models suggest recovery to pre-human diversity: 3–7 million years
  even if extinctions stopped today (evolutionary lag from small surviving populations)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What defines a mass extinction? | Rate spike 10–100× background, affecting multiple major groups globally, within <3 Myr |
| Which extinction was worst? | End-Permian (P-T): ~90–96% of marine species, ~70% of terrestrial species lost |
| What caused the P-T extinction? | Siberian Traps LIP volcanism → CO₂ + warming + ocean anoxia + acidification cascade |
| What caused the End-Ordovician? | Gondwana glaciation → rapid cooling + sea level drop → habitat loss |
| What caused the Late Devonian? | Multi-causal: forest CO₂ drawdown → cooling + anoxia in shelf seas; no bolide needed |
| What caused the T-J extinction? | CAMP volcanism as Pangaea rifted; selectively killed pseudosuchians, not dinosaurs |
| Why is K-Pg the "most famous" but not the worst? | ~40% genera lost (less than P-T at >70%) but icons like non-avian dinosaurs lost; best evidence |

| Is there a 6th extinction? | Yes by rate metrics (100–1000× background); not yet at Big Five severity but trajectory is alarming |

## Common Confusion Points

**The P-T was worse than the K-Pg but is far less famous**: By any metric — species, genera, families, ecological diversity — the End-Permian was catastrophically more severe than the K-Pg. It killed ~96% of marine species vs. ~76% at K-Pg. The K-Pg is famous because non-avian dinosaurs are culturally iconic and because the iridium story (Alvarez et al., 1980) was a scientific detective narrative that caught public attention. The P-T is harder to communicate: it killed fusulinids and productid brachiopods, which nobody has seen in a toy store.

**The Late Devonian extinction was not a single event**: Unlike the geologically sharp K-Pg (millimeters of clay) or P-T (tens of centimeters), the Late Devonian extinction was a protracted series of pulses spanning ~25 Myr. The "Kellwasser events" and "Hangenberg event" are distinct crises. Calling it a single extinction event is a simplification — it is more accurately an extended biodiversity crisis driven by multiple interacting stresses.

**LIP volcanism ≠ modern volcano emissions**: The Siberian Traps erupted ~2–3 million km³ of lava over ~1 Myr. Total CO₂ from modern human emissions since industrialization is ~300 billion tonnes (roughly 0.00015 km³ equivalent). LIP eruptions operated over 10⁵–10⁶ years at rates comparable to today's human CO₂ emissions — but without any technological civilization to potentially respond. The geological and anthropogenic forcing are similar in magnitude on century timescales, which is what makes paleoclimate comparisons relevant.

**Survival ≠ evolutionary success**: Many K-Pg survivors were merely lucky in the short term. Crocodilians survived — but they are not more evolutionarily "successful" than the non-avian dinosaurs they outlasted. The post-K-Pg world was dominated by the radiation of the survivors (mammals, birds), not by any inherent superiority. Evolution doesn't reward the deserving; it rewards whoever happened to be standing when the dust settled.

**The "Sixth Extinction" rate is real but the framing is complicated**: Current extinction rates are genuinely elevated 100–1000× above background. But the Big Five comparison requires caution: we have millions of years of post-event fossil record to measure the Big Five; we have ~300 years of post-1500 recorded extinctions to measure the Sixth. Undocumented extinctions are substantial. The rate metric is solid; the severity comparison to the Big Five is a projection, not a measured outcome.
