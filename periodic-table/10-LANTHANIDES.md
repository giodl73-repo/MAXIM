# Lanthanides — La through Lu (Z=57–71)

## The Big Picture

The 4f series: 15 elements with nearly identical chemistry, the hardest separation challenge
in analytical chemistry, and the physical properties that power electric vehicles and wind turbines.

## The Lanthanide Contraction — Core Phenomenon

### Why 4f Orbitals Don't Shield

```
NORMAL EXPECTATION: going right across a period, nuclear charge increases by 1 each step
  → outer electron radius increases OR stays constant
  → ionic radius should stay roughly constant across lanthanides if 4f shields as expected

WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS:
  Ionic radius SHRINKS by ~17 pm across the series (La³⁺ → Lu³⁺)
  This is LARGE — more than the difference between many periods

WHY:
  4f orbitals have their probability maximum INSIDE the 5s and 5p shells
  The 4f electrons spend much of their time in the core region → poor screening
  → Each successive 4f electron adds nuclear charge (+1 proton) but screens poorly
  → Effective nuclear charge felt by outer electrons INCREASES across the series
  → All outer orbital radii (5s, 5p, 5d, 6s) systematically contract

VISUAL:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  4f orbital shape: high angular nodes, stays near nucleus     │
  │                                                               │
  │         [nucleus]  ←── 4f electrons (poorly shield) ───       │
  │              ↑                                                │
  │         5s5p5d6s electrons feel increasing Z_eff              │
  │              ↓                                                │
  │         → contracts → ionic radius shrinks                    │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Consequences of the Lanthanide Contraction

```
1. PERIOD 5 vs PERIOD 6 TRANSITION METAL RADII:
   The 4f contraction compresses all 5d element radii → Period 6 metals have nearly
   IDENTICAL atomic radii to their Period 5 congeners:
     Zr (Period 5): 160 pm → Hf (Period 6): 159 pm  (normally expect larger!)
     Nb: 146 pm    → Ta: 146 pm
     Mo: 139 pm    → W: 139 pm
   This is why Hf and Zr are nearly impossible to separate (same radius → same chemistry)
   Why W properties so similar to Mo — but NOT similar to Cr despite being in same group

2. LANTHANIDE SEPARATION CHALLENGE:
   La³⁺ through Lu³⁺ differ by only 17 pm total across 15 elements
   → All prefer octahedral or higher coordination with O-donor ligands
   → Separation requires many steps (ion exchange, solvent extraction)
   Historically: first lanthanide element was Ce (1803); last stable was Lu (1907)
   Full separation took ~100 years of fractional crystallization/precipitation work

3. SIMILAR CHEMISTRY:
   All lanthanides overwhelmingly prefer +3 oxidation state
   All dissolve in dilute acid → Ln³⁺ + water → [Ln(H₂O)₉]³⁺ (tricapped trigonal prism)
   Hydroxides precipitate above pH ~6
   Oxalate/carbonate/phosphate salts all insoluble (analytical test)
```

## Lanthanide Separation — Industrial Methods

```
HISTORICAL: ion exchange chromatography
  EDTA eluent: Lu³⁺ (smallest) comes off column first, La³⁺ last
  → Works but slow and batch process

MODERN: liquid-liquid solvent extraction (PUREX/ORELEX type)
  Organic extractant (e.g., HDEHP — di(2-ethylhexyl)phosphoric acid)
  Heavier (smaller) lanthanides extracted preferentially (higher stability constant)
  → Countercurrent extraction columns → continuous separation
  → Multiple stages needed for high purity (5-6N = 99.9999%)

IONIC LIQUID SEPARATION: emerging research
  Task-specific ILs with lanthanide-selective ligands

SEPARATION ECONOMICS:
  La, Ce, Pr, Nd (first 4) — most abundant, produced in bulk, lower cost
  Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy — produced in smaller quantities, higher cost
  Lu — rarest (along with Tm), most expensive

MINE-TO-MAGNET supply chain:
  Bastnäsite (RECO₃F), monazite ((REE,Th)PO₄), xenotime (YPO₄) ores
  Crushed → acid leach → precipitation → separation → oxide → metal/alloy
  ~60% of lanthanide processing capacity: China
```

## Neodymium (Nd) — The Magnet Element

### Nd₂Fe₁₄B Permanent Magnets

```
DISCOVERED 1982: Sagawa (Sumitomo Special Metals, Japan) + Croat (GM Research)
Structure: tetragonal, Nd₂Fe₁₄B with uniaxial magnetocrystalline anisotropy

MAGNETIC PERFORMANCE:
  Maximum energy product (BH)max: ~400-450 kJ/m³  (vs Alnico: ~80, ferrite: ~40)
  Coercivity Hci: ~1000-2000 kA/m (resists demagnetization)
  Remanence Br: ~1.0-1.5 T

WHY SO STRONG:
  Fe provides high saturation magnetization (large B)
  Nd provides very large magnetocrystalline anisotropy (locks magnetic moment to crystal axis)
  B stabilizes the intermetallic crystal structure
  Together: impossible to demagnetize easily + large magnetization

TEMPERATURE LIMITATION:
  Curie temperature: 312°C — above this, magnetic order lost
  Operating limit: ~150°C (Br drops with T)
  Dy or Tb addition: increases Hci and Curie temp → high-temp grades (needs heavy REE!)
  EV motor: motor magnets must survive 150-200°C → critical Dy need

APPLICATIONS AND SCALE:
  EV motors: Toyota Prius gen 1 (1997) pioneered NdFeB in traction motors
    Each EV contains ~1-2 kg NdFeB magnets
    BEV scale: by 2030, ~300,000 tonnes Nd+Pr demand projected
  Wind turbine generators (direct-drive): eliminates gearbox, uses large NdFeB rings
    Offshore wind: 2 MW turbine → ~200 kg NdFeB
  Hard disk drives: voice coil + spindle motors
  Consumer electronics: speakers, headphones, vibrators
  MRI gradient coils: permanent magnet in some open MRI designs
  Industrial motors: highest-efficiency motors

CORROSION WEAKNESS:
  NdFeB corrodes rapidly → must be coated (Ni-Cu-Ni, Zn, epoxy)
  Nd oxidizes → Nd₂O₃ → magnet crumbles
  In EVs: encapsulated in rotor assembly
```

## Phosphors — Eu, Tb, and the Display Revolution

```
WHAT IS A PHOSPHOR?
  Material that absorbs energy (UV, electron beam, X-ray) and emits visible light

TRIVALENT RARE EARTH PHOSPHORS:
  Sharp emission lines (4f→4f transitions) — much more efficient and color-pure
  than broad-spectrum d-block emission

  Eu³⁺: intense RED emission (~615 nm from ⁵D₀→⁷F₂ transition)
  Eu²⁺: BLUE emission (~450 nm, broadened — 4f→5d transition)
  Tb³⁺: GREEN emission (~545 nm from ⁵D₄→⁷F₅ transition)

FLUORESCENT LAMP PHOSPHORS (tricolor):
  Combine: Eu³⁺ (red) + Tb³⁺ (green) + Eu²⁺ or Tm³⁺ (blue) + UV excitation
  → White light with high color rendering index (CRI > 80)
  Linear fluorescent tubes (T8, T5), compact fluorescents (CFL)
  Now being displaced by LED lighting

LED PHOSPHORS (white LED):
  Blue LED (InGaN, 450 nm) + yellow phosphor → white light
  Common phosphor: Ce³⁺:YAG (yttrium aluminum garnet with Ce³⁺)
  Ce³⁺ broad 4f→5d excitation → broad yellow-green emission → blue + yellow → white
  High-CRI LEDs: add red phosphor (Eu²⁺-doped nitrides like CaAlSiN₃:Eu²⁺)

LCD BACKLIGHTS:
  KSF (K₂SiF₆:Mn⁴⁺) red narrow-band phosphor → high color gamut for LCD TV

EURO BANKNOTE SECURITY:
  Eu-doped glass/powder: invisible under daylight → glows red under UV lamp
  Authentication of currency, official documents
  Eu is ideal: sharp red emission, UV-excitable, not naturally found in paper

QUANTUM DOTS (next gen):
  CdSe quantum dots: size-tunable emission → replacing RE phosphors in displays
  Samsung QLED TV: In-substituted ZnSe QDs (Cd-free) → precise color gamut
```

HOLMIUM (Ho, Z=67):

    ¹⁷⁰Tm: t½ = 128.6 days, β⁻ + γ emitter (84 keV γ)

## Gadolinium (Gd, Z=64) — MRI Contrast

```
[Xe] 4f⁷ 5d¹ 6s²  — half-filled 4f (7 unpaired electrons → maximum paramagnetism)
7 unpaired electrons → largest magnetic moment of any element (7.94 μB)

MRI CONTRAST AGENTS:
  MRI detects ¹H (water proton) relaxation: T1 and T2 times
  Paramagnetic Gd³⁺ (7 unpaired e⁻) → enhances relaxation rates → brightens T1-weighted images
  Bare Gd³⁺ is toxic (similar size to Ca²⁺ → blocks Ca channels) → must be chelated

  COMPLEXES:
    Gd-DTPA (Magnevist): first approved (1988), ionic linear chelate
    Gd-DOTA (Dotarem): macrocyclic, more stable (k_therm higher, much slower dissociation)
    Gd-DO3A-butrol (Gadovist): macrocyclic, highest relaxivity

  NEPHROGENIC SYSTEMIC FIBROSIS (NSF):
    Rare but severe: Gd dissociates from linear chelates in patients with severe kidney failure
    → Free Gd³⁺ → fibrosing disease of skin, joints, organs
    Macrocyclic chelates (DOTA-based) much safer (lower dissociation constant)
    Current guidelines: avoid linear chelates in GFR < 30 mL/min

  GADOLINIUM RETENTION:
    Trace Gd found in brain (dentate nucleus) after multiple contrast administrations
    Linear chelates > macrocyclic; clinical significance uncertain (ongoing research)
    Regulatory action (EMA): suspended several linear agents in EU; FDA label changes

NEUTRON CAPTURE:
  ¹⁵⁷Gd: 254,000 barns neutron cross-section (2nd highest known, after ¹³⁵Xe)
  Gadolinium neutron capture therapy (GdNCT) research — Gd₂O₃ nanoparticles in tumors
```

## Individual Lanthanide Profiles

```
LANTHANUM (La, Z=57):
  "Prototype" lanthanide, [Xe] 5d¹ 6s² (4f⁰)
  Nickel-metal hydride batteries: LaNi₅ absorbs H₂ → NiMH anode (hybrids)
  La₂O₃: high-index optical glass elements
  Auto catalytic converter washcoat component (with Ce)

CERIUM (Ce, Z=58):
  Most abundant lanthanide (~66 ppm crust, similar to copper)
  UNIQUE: stable +4 state (Ce⁴⁺ — only lanthanide with accessible +4 in aqueous solution)
  CeO₂ (ceria): MAIN USE
    Automotive catalyst washcoat: Ce⁴⁺/Ce³⁺ oxygen storage capacity → buffers lambda
    Polishing abrasive: glass lenses, semiconductor CMP (chemical mechanical planarization)
    UV absorber: glass, cosmetics
    Solid oxide fuel cell electrolyte
    Hydrogen production via thermochemical cycle (Ce⁴⁺/Ce³⁺ redox)

PRASEODYMIUM (Pr, Z=59):
  Nd-Pr often combined (didymium) in NdFeB magnets (Pr partially substitutes Nd)
  PrNi₅: extreme cryogenic cooler (adiabatic demagnetization refrigeration → mK temperatures)
  Pr yellow glass: didymium glass safety goggles for glassblowers (absorbs Na D-line)

SAMARIUM (Sm, Z=62):
  SmCo₅ and Sm₂Co₁₇: high-temperature permanent magnets
  Curie temp of Sm₂Co₁₇: 820°C (vs NdFeB 312°C)
  → Missile guidance, high-T motors where NdFeB fails
  Sm-153: β⁻ emitter → palliative treatment for bone metastases

EUROPIUM (Eu, Z=63):
  Key phosphor element (see above)
  Most important use: red phosphor for fluorescent lamps/LED/banknotes
  Eu²⁺ (f⁷ = same as Gd³⁺): blue emission → CFL blue phosphor, storage phosphor

DYSPROSIUM (Dy, Z=66):
  Critical additive to NdFeB magnets: DyFe₂ precipitates at grain boundaries
  → Increases coercivity → allows operation at higher temperatures
  EV motors with Dy can operate to ~200°C
  SUPPLY CONCERN: Dy production concentrated in China heavy rare earth mines
  ~2000 tonnes/yr production; only 2-5% of all REE production

ERBIUM (Er, Z=68):
  Er³⁺ absorption at 1535 nm = C-band of optical fiber communications
  Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA):
    Pump laser (980 or 1480 nm) excites Er³⁺ → ⁴I₁₃/₂ level → stimulated emission at 1535 nm
    → Amplifies data signal without optical-to-electronic conversion
    → Enabled transcontinental and transoceanic fiber optic networks (1989+)
    → EDFA made the internet infrastructure economically feasible

YTTERBIUM (Yb, Z=70):
  Yb lasers: 1030 nm, very efficient (pump at 940 nm → 1030 nm, small quantum defect)
  High-power Yb:fiber lasers (kW class) for industrial cutting/welding → displaced CO₂ lasers
  Yb optical lattice clock: most accurate timekeeper (10⁻¹⁸ level) → next SI second candidate
  Yb:glass: fiber lasers and amplifiers (competitor to Er:fiber in some bands)

LUTETIUM (Lu, Z=71):
  Heaviest lanthanide (4f¹⁴ complete), behaves most like Y and Sc
  Most expensive lanthanide (~$10,000/kg)
  Lu-177 DOTATATE: β⁻ + γ emitter, t½ = 6.7 days
    Chelated DOTATATE peptide targets somatostatin receptors → neuroendocrine tumors
    Lutathera: approved 2018 (EU, US) — peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT)
    Response rates ~40-80% in metastatic gastroenteropancreatic NETs
  LuAG scintillator: Lu₃Al₅O₁₂:Ce — PET scanner crystals (replaces NaI/BGO for some applications)
```

**Permanent magnet technology progression — what replaced what:**

| Generation | Material | Max energy product (BH)max | Key limitation |
|------------|----------|---------------------------|----------------|
| Lodestone (antiquity) | Fe₃O₄ (magnetite) | ~0.4 MGOe | Natural; weak |
| Alnico (1930s) | Al-Ni-Co alloy | 5–9 MGOe | Low coercivity; demagnetizes easily |
| Ferrite/ceramic (1950s) | BaFe₁₂O₁₉, SrFe₁₂O₁₉ | 3–4 MGOe | Cheap; still dominant by volume (car motors, fridge door seals) |
| SmCo (1970s) | SmCo₅, Sm₂Co₁₇ | 16–32 MGOe | High Curie temp (820°C); expensive; brittle |
| NdFeB (1983) | Nd₂Fe₁₄B | 52–64 MGOe | Highest energy product; Curie temp 312°C → needs Dy at high T; corrodes |
| Next gen (research) | MnBi, L1₀ FeNi, FePt | ~20–30 MGOe target | Avoid REE dependence; not yet commercial |

Each step roughly doubled the magnetic energy stored per unit volume. NdFeB enabled: electric vehicle motors, wind turbine generators, hard disk drive actuators, consumer electronics actuators — essentially the entire modern motor/generator economy. The Dy addition (to suppress high-T coercivity loss) is why Dysprosium is the most geopolitically sensitive rare earth: EV adoption × NdFeB motor use × high-T operation = increased Dy demand.

  2010: cut RE exports 40% → price spikes ($Nd: $45 → $450/kg in 12 months)

  <1% of REE currently recycled — major gap

  Fe₁₆N₂ (iron nitride): theoretical magnetic performance comparable to NdFeB, no RE
  Mn-Al, Mn-Bi alloys: medium-performance RE-free magnets
  None yet match NdFeB at scale for EV/wind applications

## Common Confusion Points

**"Why are rare earths 'rare' if Ce is as common as copper?"**
The name is historical. They were named "rare" because they were found in unusual minerals
initially, not because of crustal scarcity. Ce, La, Nd, Y are actually moderately common.
They are hard to find in economically concentrated deposits and extremely hard to separate
from each other. The "rare" refers to mineralogical rarity of rich deposits, not abundance.

**"Why does Gd work as MRI contrast when it's in a chelate and can't interact with water directly?"**
Gd-chelate complexes do have one (for Gd-DOTA-type) or more water molecules in the first
coordination sphere. The Gd³⁺ center still interacts with these bound water molecules, which
rapidly exchange with bulk water — transmitting the paramagnetic relaxation enhancement to
the bulk water pool. Second-sphere and outer-sphere effects also contribute.

**"Why can't we just substitute Nd with something less strategically concentrated?"**
The Nd magnetocrystalline anisotropy constant (K₁ = 4.9 MJ/m³) comes from Nd's specific
4f electron configuration and the crystal symmetry of the Nd₂Fe₁₄B structure. You can't
swap in a different element and preserve it. Fe₁₆N₂ and MnAl alloys have different mechanisms
and haven't yet achieved NdFeB's energy product in manufacturable form.
