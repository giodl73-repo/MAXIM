# Actinides — Ac through Lr (Z=89–103)

## The Big Picture

The 5f series: all radioactive, the elements that defined the 20th century through nuclear weapons
and power, and the heaviest atoms synthesized by human hands.

## Radioactive Decay Chains — The Four Series

All heavy nuclei decay toward stability. There are exactly four naturally occurring decay series,
ending at different stable lead (or bismuth) isotopes:

```
FOUR DECAY SERIES:

  SERIES        Starting isotope  t½          Ends at    Found in nature?
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Uranium       ²³⁸U             4.47 Gyr    ²⁰⁶Pb      Yes — most common
  Actinium      ²³⁵U             703 Myr     ²⁰⁷Pb      Yes — 0.72% of U
  Thorium       ²³²Th            14.05 Gyr   ²⁰⁸Pb      Yes — most stable
  Neptunium     ²³⁷Np            2.14 Myr    ²⁰⁹Bi      Virtually extinct on Earth
                                                          (t½ << Earth age)

URANIUM-238 DECAY CHAIN (key members):
  ²³⁸U (4.47 Gyr, α)
   → ²³⁴Th (24 days, β⁻)
   → ²³⁴Pa (1.2 min, β⁻)
   → ²³⁴U  (245 kyr, α)
   → ²³⁰Th (75 kyr, α)
   → ²²⁶Ra (1600 yr, α)  ← radium (Curie's element)
   → ²²²Rn (3.8 days, α) ← radon (indoor health hazard)
   → ²¹⁸Po (3 min, α)
   → ²¹⁴Pb (27 min, β⁻)
   → ²¹⁴Bi (20 min, β⁻)
   → ²¹⁴Po (164 μs, α)   ← very short — deposits in lung tissue
   → ²¹⁰Pb (22 yr, β⁻)
   → ²¹⁰Bi (5 days, β⁻)
   → ²¹⁰Po (138 days, α)
   → ²⁰⁶Pb (stable)

RADIOGENIC LEAD:
  Most Pb in Earth's crust is radiogenic (produced by U/Th decay over 4.5 Gyr)
  Lead isotope ratios (²⁰⁶Pb/²⁰⁴Pb, etc.) → geochronology, ore provenance
  U-Pb dating: most precise absolute age method in geology (concordia diagram)
```

## Uranium (U, Z=92) — The Fuel

### Natural Uranium

```
NATURAL COMPOSITION:
  ²³⁸U: 99.2745% (fissile only by fast neutrons — not practical in thermal reactors)
  ²³⁵U: 0.7200%  (fissile with thermal neutrons — the useful isotope)
  ²³⁴U: 0.0054%  (daughter of ²³⁸U decay chain)

URANIUM ORE:
  Uraninite/pitchblende: UO₂ (major ore); carnotite, autunite, coffinite
  Major producers: Kazakhstan, Namibia, Canada, Australia, Uzbekistan
  Yellowcake: U₃O₈ (triuranium octoxide) — concentrated ore product
  Grade: typical mine ~0.1% U; Cigar Lake (Canada) ~15%
```

### Enrichment — Separating U-235

```
ENRICHMENT LEVELS:
  Natural (NU):     0.72% ²³⁵U
  Low-enriched (LEU): 3-5% ²³⁵U → LWR (light water reactor) fuel
  High-assay LEU (HALEU): 5-20% → advanced reactors, research
  Highly enriched (HEU): >20% → weapons-grade is >90%
  Weapons grade:    ≥93% ²³⁵U (no longer produced by US/Russia under treaties)

ENRICHMENT METHODS:

GASEOUS DIFFUSION (old, US K-25 plant):
  UF₆ gas pushed through porous barriers
  ²³⁵UF₆ diffuses slightly faster (√(352/349) = 1.0043 per stage)
  Need thousands of stages (cascade) for weapons grade
  Enormous electricity consumption → not competitive now

GAS CENTRIFUGE (current standard):
  UF₆ gas in centrifuge at 50,000+ rpm
  ²³⁸UF₆ (heavier) → walls; ²³⁵UF₆ → center → extracted
  Separation factor per stage: ~1.3 (vs 1.004 diffusion) → far fewer stages
  Urenco (UK/Netherlands/Germany), Russia, US (USEC → Centrus) operate cascades
  Iran's centrifuge program: primary technical barrier to weapons capability

LASER ENRICHMENT (SILEX, developing):
  ²³⁵UF₆ selectively ionized by tuned laser → electrostatic separation
  Much more efficient in principle — demonstration scale only
```

### Nuclear Fission

```
U-235 FISSION CHAIN REACTION:

  ²³⁵U + n (thermal, 0.025 eV) → [²³⁶U*] → fission fragments + 2-3 neutrons + γ + energy

  Average neutrons per fission: ν̄ = 2.42
  Average fission energy: ~200 MeV = 3.2×10⁻¹¹ J
  (vs chemical: ~5 eV per atom → nuclear is ~40 million× more energy-dense)

FISSION PRODUCTS:
  Bimodal mass distribution: peaks around A~95 and A~138
  Common pairs: ¹³⁶Xe/⁹⁷Sr, ¹⁴⁰Ba/⁹⁴Kr, ¹³¹I/¹⁰¹Y
  All fission products radioactive (neutron-rich) → β⁻ decay chains toward stability

CRITICALITY:
  k_eff = (neutrons produced) / (neutrons absorbed + leaked) per generation
  k_eff = 1: critical (steady state)
  k_eff > 1: supercritical (reactor: controlled; bomb: prompt critical)
  k_eff < 1: subcritical

  Critical mass (bare sphere, weapons-grade ²³⁵U): ~52 kg
  With reflector: ~15 kg; with implosion: further reduced
  Commercial reactor fuel (3-5% enriched): cannot achieve prompt criticality (not enough ²³⁵U)
  → Reactors CANNOT explode like a nuclear bomb (different physics)

THERMAL vs FAST NEUTRONS:
  Thermal (slow) neutrons: fission cross-section of ²³⁵U = 583 barns → easy to sustain reaction
  Fast neutrons: cross-section ~1 barn → need higher enrichment or Pu-239
  Moderator (H₂O, D₂O, graphite): slows fast fission neutrons to thermal energies
  LWR: ordinary water moderates AND cools; slightly enriched U needed (3-5%)
  CANDU: D₂O moderator → can use natural uranium (0.72% ²³⁵U)
```

## Plutonium (Pu, Z=94) — The Engineered Element

### Production in Reactors

```
²³⁸U TRANSMUTATION:
  ²³⁸U + n → ²³⁹U (β⁻, t½ = 23.5 min) → ²³⁹Np (β⁻, t½ = 2.36 days) → ²³⁹Pu

  ²³⁹Pu: t½ = 24,110 yr; fissile (like ²³⁵U); critical mass ~10 kg (bare) / ~5 kg reflected
  In a reactor running at power, ²³⁸U is constantly converting to ²³⁹Pu
  In LWRs: ~1/3 of power comes from fissioning accumulated Pu at end of fuel cycle
  In production reactors (Hanford, Savannah River, Mayak): operated specifically for Pu-239 production

WEAPONS COMPLICATION:
  Reactor-grade Pu has significant ²⁴⁰Pu (from ²³⁹Pu + n → ²⁴⁰Pu)
  ²⁴⁰Pu has high spontaneous fission rate → initiates chain reaction too early if assembled slowly
  → Implosion design required (supersonic assembly to prevent pre-detonation)
  → Weapons-grade Pu: ≥93% ²³⁹Pu (low ²⁴⁰Pu) from short irradiation in special reactors
```

### Pu Chemistry — Why It's Unique

```
UNUSUAL OXIDATION STATE CHEMISTRY:
  Pu²⁺, Pu³⁺, Pu⁴⁺, Pu⁵⁺, Pu⁶⁺ can ALL coexist simultaneously in acidic solution!
  (No other element does this in a single container)
  Reason: 5f orbitals extend further than 4f → participate in bonding → Pu can access many states

  Pourbaix diagram for Pu is bewildering compared to lanthanides (all just +3)

PLUTONYL (PuO₂²⁺):
  Pu(VI) forms the plutonyl ion: linear O=Pu=O unit (like UO₂²⁺)
  Highly mobile in groundwater → environmental concern at nuclear sites

CRITICALITY ACCIDENTS:
  Pu-239 critical mass only ~10 kg → handling requires strict geometry control
  Los Alamos criticality accidents (Louis Slotin 1946, Harry Daghlian 1945): fatal exposures
  Tokaimura 1999 (Japan): uranium solution in precipitation tank went critical, 2 deaths
  Each criticality accident → "blue flash" of Cherenkov radiation from radiation ionizing air/water
```

**Nuclear vs. alternatives — the engineering policy tradeoffs:**

| Metric | Nuclear (LWR) | Offshore wind | Utility solar PV | Natural gas (CCGT) |
|--------|---------------|---------------|------------------|--------------------|
| Capacity factor | 90–93% | 35–45% | 20–28% | 50–85% (dispatchable) |
| LCOE (US, 2024) | $80–120/MWh (new build) | $80–130/MWh | $45–75/MWh | $40–80/MWh |
| CO₂ lifecycle (g/kWh) | 12 | 12–15 | 20–50 | 490 |
| Land use (km²/TWh/yr) | 0.3 | 70–150 | 50–100 | 0.4 |
| Baseload provision | Yes — continuous 24/7 | No — intermittent | No — intermittent | Yes — dispatchable |
| Key constraint | Capital cost + long build time (~10 yr); waste storage | Storage + grid integration | Storage + grid integration | Methane leaks; fuel price exposure |

The energy policy argument for nuclear is the capacity factor: a 1 GW nuclear plant produces ~8 TWh/year; a 1 GW solar farm produces ~2 TWh/year, requiring ~4× the nameplate capacity to match the output (and still doesn't match timing or reliability). The argument against is upfront capital cost: a new LWR costs ~$8–15B and takes 10–15 years to build in the West (vs 5–7 years in Korea/China). Small modular reactors (SMRs: <300 MWe, factory-built) are the current attempt to break the capital/schedule problem — NuScale, Rolls-Royce SMR, GE Hitachi BWRX-300 are in licensing.

## The Manhattan Project

```
TIMELINE:
  1938 Dec: Hahn and Strassmann (Berlin) discover U fission products (Ba, Kr)
  1939 Jan: Meitner and Frisch explain fission, estimate energy release
  1939 Aug: Einstein-Szilard letter → Roosevelt: warning Germany may develop bomb
  1942 Dec 2: Chicago Pile-1 (CP-1) achieves first controlled chain reaction (Fermi, U of Chicago)
  1943 Apr:   Los Alamos Lab established under Oppenheimer; Oak Ridge enrichment, Hanford reactors
  1945 Jul 16: Trinity test (Pu implosion bomb) → ~21 kiloton yield, Alamogordo NM
  1945 Aug 6:  Little Boy (U-235 gun-type, ~64 kg ²³⁵U) → Hiroshima, ~15 kt
  1945 Aug 9:  Fat Man (Pu-239 implosion, ~6.4 kg Pu) → Nagasaki, ~21 kt

TWO BOMB DESIGNS:

GUN-TYPE (Little Boy):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Propellant → target ring ← projectile slug (²³⁵U + ²³⁵U)       │
  │  Assembly rate: ~300 m/s → supercritical in microseconds        │
  │  Never tested before use (confidence in design was high)        │
  │  Inefficient: only ~1.3% of U fissioned                         │
  │  Cannot work with Pu (²⁴⁰Pu pre-detonation would occur)         │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

IMPLOSION (Fat Man / Trinity):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Explosive lenses (precisely shaped HE) surround Pu core        │
  │  Simultaneous detonation → symmetrical implosion shock wave     │
  │  Pu core compressed to ~3× normal density → critical            │
  │  Assembly time: ~2 μs (too fast for pre-detonation)             │
  │  More complex — required Trinity test to verify design          │
  │  More material-efficient: initiator + reflector + polonium-Be   │
  │  neutron initiator                                              │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

COST (1945 dollars): ~$2 billion (~$28 billion 2024)
Peak workforce: ~130,000 at Oak Ridge, Hanford, Los Alamos, Chicago, other sites
```

```
²⁵²Cf: t½ = 2.645 yr; 3.09% fission by spontaneous fission (high rate!)
  Spontaneous fission yield: 2.31×10¹² n/s per gram (extremely intense neutron source)

PRODUCTION:
  Reactor irradiation of ²⁴²Pu through multiple neutron captures:
  ²⁴²Pu + 6n → ²⁴⁸Pu → β⁻ chain → ²⁴⁸Cm + more n → ²⁵²Cf

  BNCT research: fast neutrons from Cf-252 source
```

## Neptunium (Np, Z=93)

```
[Rn] 5f⁴ 6d¹ 7s²  — first transuranic element

DISCOVERY:
  McMillan and Abelson (Berkeley, 1940): bombarded U-238 with neutrons (cyclotron)
  ²³⁸U + n → ²³⁹U (t½ = 23.5 min) → β⁻ → ²³⁹Np (t½ = 2.36 days) → β⁻ → ²³⁹Pu
  Named for Neptune (next planet after Uranus, as Np follows U)
  McMillan shared 1951 Nobel Chemistry with Seaborg (for discovering transuranic elements)

ISOTOPES:
  ²³⁹Np: t½ = 2.36 days; produced continuously in U reactors; decays to ²³⁹Pu
  ²³⁷Np: t½ = 2.14 MILLION years; main long-lived concern in nuclear waste
    Produced by: ²³⁵U + n → ²³⁶U + n (double capture), then β⁻; or ²³⁸U (n,2n) → ²³⁷U → β⁻
    1 tonne of spent LWR fuel contains ~0.5–1 kg of ²³⁷Np

NUCLEAR WASTE SIGNIFICANCE:
  In spent nuclear fuel, fission products (Cs-137, Sr-90) dominate radioactivity for ~300 years
  After 300 years, actinides dominate — and Np-237 is the dominant ALPHA EMITTER at ~10,000 years
  Its 2.14 Myr half-life means it persists through the full Yucca Mountain isolation requirement
  Np is also relatively mobile in groundwater (neptunyl NpO₂⁺ ion) → not retained by rock

  TRANSMUTATION strategies (advanced nuclear fuel cycles):
    Burn Np in fast reactors → fissions into shorter-lived products
    → Reduces required isolation time from ~1 Myr to ~300 years
    → Key rationale for Generation IV reactors (IFR, PRISM, Molten Salt)

CHEMISTRY:
  Np can access +2 through +7 oxidation states (more accessible than Pu)
  NpO₂⁺ (neptunyl): analogous to UO₂²⁺ (uranyl); linear O=Np=O²⁺ favored
  Used as spectroscopic reference for actinide chemistry (well-characterized)
```

## Thorium (Th, Z=90) — The Alternative Fuel Cycle

```
²³²Th + n → ²³³Th (β⁻, 22 min) → ²³³Pa (β⁻, 27 days) → ²³³U (fissile, t½ = 159 kyr)

ADVANTAGES OF Th CYCLE:
  • Th is ~3× more abundant than U in crust
  • Monazite ore → Th obtained as byproduct of RE mining
  • ²³³U has lower critical mass than ²³⁵U
  • Less long-lived transuranic waste (less Pu produced)
  • Cannot easily divert to weapons (²³²U produced → intense γ → hard to handle)
  • India's three-stage nuclear program uses thorite fuel + AHWR

DISADVANTAGES:
  • ²³³Pa intermediate (27-day half-life) must decay before ²³³U can be used
  • Requires initial fissile material to breed Th → can't start with pure Th
  • Fluoride salt (molten salt reactor) needed for liquid-fueled Th cycle
  • No commercial Th reactor exists (all are research/design stage)

MOLTEN SALT REACTOR (MSR/LFTR):
  Liquid fluoride thorium reactor (Kirk Sorensen champion)
  ORNL Oak Ridge Molten Salt Reactor Experiment (MSRE): 1965-1969 proof of concept
  Cannot melt down (fuel IS liquid → if temp rises, expands → k_eff drops → self-regulating)
  Canada, China building/designing molten salt demo reactors
```

## Radioactive Waste Management

```
WASTE CLASSIFICATION:
  Low-level (LLW):  contaminated lab equipment, clothing, filters, reactor components
                    Volume: large | Activity: low | Disposal: near-surface burial
  Intermediate (ILW): reactor structural components, ion exchange resins
                    Volume: medium | Activity: medium | Disposal: deeper burial
  High-level (HLW): spent nuclear fuel, reprocessing liquids
                    Volume: small | Activity: very high | Generates own heat
                    Disposal: deep geological repository (DGR) required

SPENT NUCLEAR FUEL COMPOSITION:
  ~95% ²³⁸U (by mass)
  ~1% Pu (all isotopes)
  ~3% fission products (Cs-137, Sr-90, Tc-99, I-129, etc.)
  ~0.1% minor actinides (Np, Am, Cm)

DEEP GEOLOGICAL REPOSITORY:
  Finland Onkalo: world's first approved DGR (under construction, Olkiluoto)
  Sweden: Forsmark site approved 2022
  US: Yucca Mountain (Nevada) — political controversy → no progress
  Concept: vitrify HLW in borosilicate glass → steel canister → copper overpack → bentonite clay → bedrock
  Isolation period required: 100,000 to 1,000,000 years

REPROCESSING (PUREX process):
  Dissolve spent fuel in HNO₃ → TBP solvent extraction separates U, Pu from fission products
  France (La Hague), Russia, Japan: reprocess → recover U and Pu for MOX fuel
  US, Germany: direct disposal policy (once-through fuel cycle)
  Proliferation concern: separated Pu is weapons-usable material
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Can a nuclear reactor explode like a bomb?"**
No — for a nuclear detonation, you need prompt criticality (k > 1 from prompt neutrons alone)
achieved in microseconds. Commercial LWR fuel (3-5% enriched) cannot reach the configuration
required. What can happen: steam explosion from rapid depressurization, hydrogen explosion
(Fukushima — zircaloy + steam → H₂), graphite fire (Chernobyl), and a meltdown where the
core melts through the vessel. All catastrophic, none are a nuclear explosion.

**"Is Pu-238 in RTGs a weapons concern?"**
No. RTGs use Pu-238 (t½ = 87.7 yr, α emitter, low spontaneous fission rate) — not weapons-grade
Pu-239. Pu-238 cannot sustain a fission chain reaction — it doesn't fission efficiently with
thermal or fast neutrons. It's used purely as a heat source (0.54 W/g decay heat).

**"Why is Am-241 the isotope in smoke detectors, not some other Am?"**
Am-241 is conveniently produced in large quantities as ²⁴¹Pu decays (β⁻, t½=14.4 yr) in
stockpiled weapons plutonium. The US/Russia have ~100 tonnes of weapons-grade Pu that is
being demilitarized; the Am-241 that grows in as daughter product is harvested.
Also: 432-year half-life means the source stays active for the product lifetime;
5.49 MeV α particles work well for the ionization chamber.

**"What does 'prompt critical' mean and why is it dangerous?"**
Nuclear chain reactions have two types of neutrons: prompt (emitted in ~10⁻¹⁴ s) and
delayed (emitted seconds to minutes later from fission product decay). Reactor control
depends on delayed neutrons — they slow the response time enough for mechanical control
rods to adjust reactivity. In "prompt critical," k>1 from prompt neutrons alone — the
delayed neutrons are irrelevant and the reactor accelerates on microsecond timescale,
impossible for mechanical control. This is what happens in reactor accidents and (rapidly)
in weapons. Normal operation: safely subcritical on prompt neutrons alone.
