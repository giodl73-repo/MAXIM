# Alkaline Earth Metals (Group 2) — Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra

## The Big Picture

Group 2 elements have two valence electrons in an ns² configuration. Losing both gives a stable
noble gas configuration — so the chemistry is dominated by **+2 ions**, with reactivity
increasing down the group (but never reaching the explosive violence of Group 1).

```
GROUP 2 — KEY DATA

  Element   Z   Config       IE₁   IE₂   Radius   mp(°C)  E°(V)   Hardness
                              (kJ/mol)    (pm)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Beryllium 4   [He]2s²      900  1757    112      1287    −1.85   9 (hard!)
  Magnesium 12  [Ne]3s²      738  1451    160       650    −2.37   2.5
  Calcium   20  [Ar]4s²      590  1145    197       842    −2.87   1.5
  Strontium 38  [Kr]5s²      550  1064    215       777    −2.89   1.5
  Barium    56  [Xe]6s²      503   965    222       727    −2.91   1.3
  Radium    88  [Rn]7s²      509   979    ~223      700    −2.91   —

Key differences from Group 1:
  • IE₁ higher (harder to ionize first electron — nuclear charge increased by 1)
  • IE₂ not prohibitively high → +2 consistently achievable (unlike Group 1 where +2 would
    require breaking a full shell)
  • Higher melting points (stronger metallic bonding — 2 electrons per atom)
  • Harder metals (Be is exceptionally hard for its position)
  • Less violently reactive with water (except Ba which reacts readily)
```

## Beryllium (Be, Z=4) — The Anomaly

Be is the strangest Group 2 member. Its small size (112 pm, smallest among Group 2) and high
charge density make it behave more like aluminum than magnesium.

### Diagonal Relationship: Be and Al

```
Be²⁺ (ionic radius 31 pm) vs Mg²⁺ (72 pm) vs Al³⁺ (54 pm)

Be behaves more like Al than Mg:
  • Both form hydrated ions Be(H₂O)₄²⁺ and Al(H₂O)₆³⁺ that are acidic
  • Both form amphoteric oxides: BeO + NaOH → Na₂[Be(OH)₄]
  • Both form covalent compounds (BeH₂, BeCl₂ — covalent character due to high polarizing power)
  • Both form stable organometallics (diethylberyllium, triethylaluminum)
  • Contrast: Mg does not dissolve in NaOH; forms ionic MgO

BeCl₂ structure:
  Gas phase: linear monomer (sp hybridized Be — electron deficient, 4 electrons around Be)
  Solid: polymeric chain with bridging Cl (3-center bonds)
  In Lewis acid/base: BeCl₂ + 2 Cl⁻ → [BeCl₄]²⁻ (accepts electron pairs)
```

### Beryllium Toxicity

```
BERYLLIOSIS (chronic beryllium disease):
  Be²⁺ binds proteins → immune sensitization → granulomatous lung disease
  Classified as known human carcinogen (Group 1, IARC)
  Threshold: ~2 μg/m³ (OSHA); beryllium sensitization can occur at lower levels
  Latency: 1–30 years between exposure and symptoms (insidious)

SOURCES OF EXPOSURE:
  Aerospace machining (Be-Cu alloys, Be metal)
  Nuclear weapons/reactor components
  Be windows on X-ray tubes and particle detectors
  Dental prosthetics (Be-Cu alloys historically)
```

### Beryllium Applications

```
Be metal:
  Specific stiffness (E/ρ) highest of any metal — 6× higher than steel
  Used in precision instruments, optical systems (JWST mirror substrates are Be)
  Neutron moderator/reflector in nuclear reactors (low absorption cross-section)
  X-ray windows (low Z → X-rays pass through; replaces Al in high-resolution setups)

Be-Cu alloys (beryllium copper, BeCu):
  2% Be in Cu → yield strength 1100 MPa (stronger than many steels)
  Non-sparking: safe in explosive atmospheres (oil rigs, mines)
  High conductivity + strength → connectors, springs, precision instruments
  Beryllium bronze used in specialized hand tools

BeO (beryllia):
  mp = 2507°C, very high thermal conductivity (similar to metals!) but electrical insulator
  Used as heat spreaders in high-power microelectronics, nuclear fuel cladding
  Also toxic (fine powder inhaled)
```

## Magnesium (Mg, Z=12)

### Mg in Chlorophyll — Photosynthesis's Metal

```
CHLOROPHYLL MOLECULE:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Mg²⁺ at center of porphyrin ring               │
  │  (4 nitrogen atoms coordinate to Mg)            │
  │  Long phytol tail provides hydrophobicity       │
  │  (anchors chlorophyll in thylakoid membrane)    │
  │                                                  │
  │  Chlorophyll a: absorption at ~430 nm + ~680 nm │
  │  Chlorophyll b: absorption at ~455 nm + ~645 nm │
  │  (together cover most of visible spectrum except│
  │   green → plants look green because they reflect│
  │   what they don't absorb)                        │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Mg role: coordinates axial ligand (His residue), facilitates delocalized π system
         that enables light harvesting and electron donation
Without Mg: chlorophyll cannot form → no photosynthesis → no plant life

Mg in metabolism:
  ATP functions as Mg²⁺ complex (MgATP²⁻) — free ATP is poor substrate for kinases
  300+ enzymes require Mg²⁺ as cofactor (kinases, polymerases, hexokinase, etc.)
  Bone mineral reservoir: bone contains ~60% of body's Mg
  Deficiency: muscle cramps, arrhythmia, metabolic syndrome association
```

### Magnesium Metal & Alloys

```
PRODUCTION: electrolysis of molten MgCl₂ (from seawater)
  Seawater → Ca(OH)₂ precipitation of Mg(OH)₂ → HCl dissolves → MgCl₂ → electrolysis

KEY PROPERTY: density = 1.74 g/cm³ (lightest structural metal)
  Al: 2.7 g/cm³, Ti: 4.5 g/cm³, Fe: 7.9 g/cm³

ALLOYS:
  AZ31, AZ91 (Al + Zn additions): automotive parts, laptop casings, camera bodies
  WE43 (W = Y, E = Nd/rare earths): creep-resistant to 200°C, medical implants
  Mg-Li alloys: even lighter, research for aerospace

Mg COMBUSTION:
  Burns extremely hot (3100°C), white light (MgO powder emission)
  Old-style camera flash: Mg ribbon
  Military illumination flares, thermite-adjacent applications
  Mg fires: do NOT use water (Mg + H₂O → MgO + H₂ → explosion)
  Extinguish with dry sand or Class D extinguisher

SACRIFICIAL ANODES:
  Mg has very negative E° (−2.37 V)
  Bolted to steel pipelines, ship hulls → preferentially corrodes (protects steel)
  More common: Zn anodes (cheaper) but Mg better in high-resistivity soils
```

## Calcium (Ca, Z=20)

### Bones, Teeth, and Biomineralization

```
HYDROXYAPATITE:
  Ca₅(PO₄)₃(OH) — main mineral in bone and tooth enamel
  ~65% of bone by weight; 99% of body's Ca is in skeleton

BONE STRUCTURE:
  Collagen fibers (Type I) + hydroxyapatite crystals = composite material
  Analog to fiber-reinforced composite: organic gives toughness, mineral gives stiffness

CALCIUM SIGNALING (Ca²⁺ as second messenger):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Cytosolic Ca²⁺: ~100 nM resting (very low!)            │
  │  Extracellular: ~2 mM (20,000× gradient maintained)     │
  │  ER lumen: ~0.5 mM (internal Ca²⁺ store)               │
  │                                                          │
  │  Signal cascade:                                         │
  │    Hormone → receptor → IP₃ release → ER releases Ca²⁺ │
  │    OR                                                    │
  │    Action potential → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel opens  │
  │    → Ca²⁺ floods in → calmodulin binds 4 Ca²⁺ → CaM   │
  │      activates kinases → cell response                  │
  │                                                          │
  │  Examples:                                               │
  │    Muscle contraction: Ca²⁺ binds troponin C → exposes  │
  │                        actin binding site → cross-bridge │
  │    Neurotransmitter release: Ca²⁺ triggers vesicle fusion│
  │    Fertilization: Ca²⁺ wave sweeps through egg → blocks  │
  │                   polyspermy                             │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

CALMODULIN: ubiquitous Ca²⁺-sensing protein
  4 EF-hand motifs, each binds one Ca²⁺
  Conformational change exposes hydrophobic groove → binds target proteins
```

### Calcium Industrial Chemistry

```
LIMESTONE AND THE LIME CYCLE:
  CaCO₃ (limestone/chalk) →(heat 900°C)→ CaO (quicklime) + CO₂
  CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime, milk of lime)  [exothermic!]
  Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O  (carbonation — setting of lime mortar)

USES:
  Portland cement:  CaO + SiO₂ + Al₂O₃ + Fe₂O₃ (heated to clinker) → complex silicates
                    World's most consumed manufactured material (~4 billion tonnes/year)
  Steel making:     CaO added to blast furnace → fluxes SiO₂/Al₂O₃ slag
  Water treatment:  Ca(OH)₂ to raise pH, precipitate phosphate
  Flue gas desulfurization: Ca(OH)₂ + SO₂ → CaSO₃ (scrubbing power plant exhaust)
  Food: CaSO₄ (gypsum) — tofu coagulant; CaCO₃ — antacid, dietary supplement; CaCl₂ — brine

HARD WATER AND SCALE:
  Ca²⁺ + Mg²⁺ dissolved from limestone → "hardness"
  Temporary hardness: Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O + CO₂ (boiling removes it)
  Permanent hardness: CaSO₄ (doesn't precipitate on heating) → ion exchange softening
  Boiler scale: CaCO₃ + CaSO₄ deposits → insulation → overheating → pipe failure
```

## Strontium (Sr, Z=38)

```
Sr behaves very similarly to Ca (same group, one period lower).
Bone metabolism incorporates Sr²⁺ into apatite (similar ionic radius to Ca²⁺).

FIREWORKS — RED COLOR:
  Sr compounds burned in fireworks/flares → intense red emission
  SrCO₃ + Mg + oxidizer → Sr* excited states → 606-680 nm (red)
  Sr(NO₃)₂ also common (provides both Sr and oxidizer NO₃⁻)

STRONTIUM-90 (nuclear):
  ⁹⁰Sr: fission product, t½ = 28.8 yr, β⁻ emitter
  Follows Ca in biochemistry → concentrates in bone
  Major health concern from fallout (Chernobyl, nuclear weapons testing)
  ⁹⁰Sr → ⁹⁰Y (t½ = 64 hr) → ⁹⁰Zr (stable)
  ⁹⁰Y used as therapeutic radioisotope (Y-90 radioembolization for liver cancer)
  RUPTURE → 90Y microspheres = SIR-Spheres / TheraSphere

STRONTIUM RANELATE:
  Oral drug for osteoporosis (withdrawn from EU market 2014 due to cardiovascular risks)
  Incorporated into bone → reduced bone turnover + some density increase
```

## Barium (Ba, Z=56)

```
Ba²⁺ (ionic radius 135 pm) — large, heavy

BARIUM SULFATE (BaSO₄):
  Insoluble in water/acid (Ksp = 1.1×10⁻¹⁰) → low systemic toxicity despite Ba²⁺ being toxic
  Radiopaque (Ba is dense, Z=56 → X-rays absorbed)
  Medical: barium swallow, barium enema for GI tract imaging
  Industrial: oil/gas drilling (barite weighting agent in drilling mud — density 4.5 g/cm³)

BARIUM TOXICITY (soluble salts):
  Ba²⁺ blocks K⁺ channels → hypokalemia → cardiac arrhythmia, paralysis
  Antidote: MgSO₄ → BaSO₄ precipitate (insoluble) in GI tract, plus IV potassium

BARIUM COMPOUNDS:
  Ba(NO₃)₂, BaCl₂ → flame test: apple green/pale green color
  BaTiO₃: piezoelectric (high piezoelectric coefficient), ultrasound transducers, capacitors
           Phase transitions at 120°C, 0°C, −90°C — relevant for dielectric properties
  Ba(OH)₂: strong base, used in titrations
  BaCO₃: rat poison (solubilized by stomach acid → Ba²⁺ released)
```

## Radium (Ra, Z=88) — Marie Curie's Element

```
ALL ISOTOPES RADIOACTIVE:
  Most stable: ²²⁶Ra, t½ = 1600 yr (in ²³⁸U decay chain → Rn → Pb)
  ²²⁶Ra decays: α → ²²²Rn (radon — the indoor radiation hazard)

MARIE CURIE:
  1898: Curie and Pierre discover Ra and Po in pitchblende (separated from tonnes of ore)
  1903 Nobel Physics (shared with Becquerel), 1911 Nobel Chemistry (sole)
  Curie's notebooks are still radioactive (stored in lead-lined boxes at BnF)
  Aplastic anemia likely caused by chronic radiation exposure

RADIUM DIAL PAINTERS (Radium Girls):
  1917–1920s: factory workers painted watch dials with Ra-based luminous paint
  Instructed to lip-point brushes → ingested Ra → bone cancer, jaw necrosis
  Ra²⁺ follows Ca²⁺ biochemistry → concentrates in bone
  Landmark labor safety case → OSHA precursors

MODERN RADIOPHARMACEUTICALS:
  Ra-223 (Xofigo): α-emitter for bone metastases from prostate cancer
  Concentrated where bone remodeling active → short-range α kills tumor cells
  ~70 μm range for α in tissue → minimal bystander damage

Element 88 completes Group 2. Its chemistry (confirmed where accessible) mirrors Ba.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Ca and Mg both in bone — what's the difference?"**
Hydroxyapatite is primarily calcium phosphate (Ca₅(PO₄)₃OH). Magnesium substitutes at ~1–2%
of Ca sites (magnesium-substituted apatite). Mg is more critical for enzymatic processes;
Ca is more structural. Both are regulated by parathyroid hormone / calcitonin / vitamin D axis.

**"Why is BaSO₄ safe to swallow when Ba²⁺ is toxic?"**
Ksp(BaSO₄) = 1.1×10⁻¹⁰ — essentially insoluble in any biological pH. The amount of Ba²⁺
released in the GI tract is orders of magnitude below toxic levels. Compare to BaCl₂: freely
soluble → toxic. This is the classic pharmaceutical solubility safety argument.

**"Beryllium is in Group 2 (alkaline earth) but acts like aluminum?"**
This is the **diagonal relationship**: elements in the top-left of the periodic table show
chemical similarity to the element one period down and one group to the right, because the
charge/radius ratio is similar. Li resembles Mg; Be resembles Al; B resembles Si. The diagonal
relationship is a consequence of the way effective nuclear charge and atomic radius evolve
moving diagonally vs horizontally/vertically.
