# Noble Gases (Group 18) — He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn, Og

## The Big Picture

Noble gases are the periodic table's argument-enders: complete valence shells, near-zero
reactivity, and properties that almost perfectly match ideal gas behavior.

```
GROUP 18 — THE NOBLE GAS SERIES

  Element  Z   Config         bp(°C)   Natural abundance in air
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Helium   2   1s²            −269     5.2 ppm (v/v)
  Neon     10  [He]2s²2p⁶     −246     18.2 ppm
  Argon    18  [Ne]3s²3p⁶     −186     9340 ppm (0.934% !) ← 3rd most abundant in air
  Krypton  36  [Ar]3d¹⁰4s²4p⁶ −153    1.1 ppm
  Xenon    54  [Kr]4d¹⁰5s²5p⁶ −108    0.087 ppm
  Radon    86  [Xe]4f¹⁴5d¹⁰6s²6p⁶ −62 ~6×10⁻²⁰ (trace, radioactive)
  Oganesson 118 [Rn]5f¹⁴6d¹⁰7s²7p⁶ predicted ~+50°C (!) superheavy

All are colorless, odorless, tasteless gases at STP.
All are monatomic (no diatomic bonding — no reason to).
```

## Discovery Arc: The 1890s Revolution

The discovery of noble gases is one of chemistry's great detective stories. Before 1894, Group 18
**didn't exist** — the periodic table had no column for them.

```
TIMELINE:
  1868  — Solar spectroscopy detects unknown yellow line during eclipse
          → named "helium" (from Helios, the sun) — before found on Earth

  1894  — Lord Rayleigh notices: atmospheric nitrogen (from air after removing O₂)
          is 0.5% denser than nitrogen from chemical reactions.
          → Collaborates with William Ramsay

  1895  — Ramsay isolates argon from air (argon = "lazy" in Greek)
          Same year: Ramsay isolates helium from uranium mineral cleveite
          (radioactive α decay releases ⁴He — Ramsay sees Lockyer's 1868 spectral line)

  1898  — Ramsay + Morris Travers isolate krypton, neon, xenon from liquefied air
          (krypton = "hidden," neon = "new," xenon = "stranger")

  1900  — Rayleigh and Ramsay win the 1904 Nobel Prize (Physics and Chemistry, respectively)

  1900  — Ernest Rutherford identifies radon as a radioactive emanation from radium decay

  2002  — Oganesson (Og, Z=118) synthesized at JINR Dubna; named 2016 for Yuri Oganessian
```

## Why Are They So Unreactive?

```
COMPLETE VALENCE SHELLS:

  He:  1s²    → 2 electrons, shell full (n=1 holds max 2)
  Ne:  2p⁶    → 8 electrons in n=2 shell (octet)
  Ar:  3p⁶    → 8 electrons in n=3 (3d empty but too high energy)
  ...

To react, an atom must:
  (a) gain electrons (needs positive electron affinity) → noble gas EA is +/0 (unfavorable)
  (b) lose electrons (needs low ionization energy) → noble gas IE is the highest in each period
  (c) share electrons → would require placing electrons in high-energy antibonding orbitals

    Ionization energies (first, kJ/mol):
    He:  2372   ← highest of any element
    Ne:  2081
    Ar:  1521
    Kr:  1351
    Xe:  1170   ← getting low enough for some chemistry
    Rn:  1037
```

## Noble Gas Compounds — They Do Exist

The canonical "completely unreactive" claim died in 1962. Neil Bartlett reacted XeF₂ after noticing
that PtF₆ (which had oxidized O₂ to O₂⁺) should also be able to oxidize Xe (since IE(Xe) < IE(O₂)).

```
XENON CHEMISTRY:

  Fluorides (most common):
    XeF₂   linear, colorless crystals, strong fluorinating agent
    XeF₄   square planar, colorless, can explode when heated
    XeF₆   distorted octahedral, reacts violently with water

  Oxides (from hydrolysis of fluorides):
    XeO₃   trigonal pyramidal (like SO₃) — explosive when dry!
    XeO₄   tetrahedral (like SO₄²⁻) — also explosive
    XeOF₄  square pyramidal

  Cations:
    Xe₂⁺, Xe⁺ in XeF⁺[AsF₆]⁻ (Bartlett's 1962 compound)

KRYPTON:
  KrF₂ exists — linear, decomposes at room temp slowly
  Highly reactive fluorinating agent
  No stable oxide (Kr is harder to oxidize than Xe)

ARGON:
  ArF exists transiently in matrix isolation (argon fluorohydride HArF at 7.5 K)
  Otherwise no stable compounds

HELIUM, NEON:
  No known stable compounds under normal conditions
  He@C₆₀ (helium trapped inside buckminsterfullerene) — endohedral fullerene, not a chemical bond

EXPLANATION — why Xe easier than He?
  Xe's valence electrons are in 5p orbitals (farther from nucleus, more polarizable)
  Lower IE means electrons are more available for bonding
  5d orbitals available as acceptors for back-bonding in hypervalent structures
  VSEPR / MO analysis: XeF₂ uses a 3-center 4-electron (3c-4e) bond (the same as I₃⁻):
    F–Xe–F  →  σ bonding + non-bonding MO on F atoms only
    Xe electrons go into non-bonding MO, net bonding order ½ per Xe–F bond
```

## Uses by Element

### Helium (He)

```
PROPERTIES DRIVING USE:
  • Lowest bp of any element (−269°C / 4.2 K) — critical for superconducting magnets
  • Non-flammable (unlike H₂) — safe for lifting
  • Inert — won't react inside equipment
  • Low density (second lightest element)
  • High thermal conductivity

APPLICATIONS:
  MRI scanners        Superconducting coils need liquid He at 4.2 K
                      (transitioning to cryocoolers, but He still backup)
  NMR spectroscopy    Same reason as MRI — Bruker/Varian magnets
  Welding (GTAW/TIG)  Ar works too, but He gives deeper penetration, faster travel speed
  Leak detection      He molecules are tiny — detect leaks in vacuum/pressure systems
  Balloons/airships   Lifted by buoyancy (but much less lift than H₂ — He is 2× heavier)
  Cryogenics          He-3 refrigerators reach 300 mK; dilution refrigerators go to <10 mK
  Diving              Heliox (He+O₂) for deep diving — N₂ narcosis avoided

SCARCITY CONCERN:
  He is non-renewable on human timescales: it forms from α decay in Earth's crust,
  seeps upward, escapes to space (too light to be gravitationally retained).
  Most commercial He comes from natural gas fields (Kansas/Texas/Qatar/Algeria).
  Balloon inflation is often criticized as wasteful for a finite resource.
```

### Neon (Ne)

```
APPLICATIONS:
  Signs     "Neon lights" glow orange-red at 600-700 nm when current ionizes Ne gas
            (Most colored "neon" signs actually use other gases: Ar/Hg gives blue-purple)
  Lasers    HeNe laser (632.8 nm red) — well-collimated, cheap, used in alignment/holography
  Cryogenics  Ne bp = −246°C; alternative cryogen for some applications between He and Ar

PRODUCTION: Fractional distillation of liquid air (Ne has bp between N₂ and He)
```

### Argon (Ar)

```
WHY ARGON IS SO COMMON:
  ⁴⁰Ar (99.6% of atmospheric Ar) comes from radioactive decay of ⁴⁰K (t½ = 1.25 Gyr)
  It has been accumulating in Earth's atmosphere for 4.5 billion years
  → Ar is 0.934% of air — far more than the other noble gases

APPLICATIONS:
  Welding (TIG/MIG)    Inert blanket shields molten weld pool from O₂ and N₂
  Furnace atmospheres  Prevent oxidation during heat treatment of metals
  Incandescent bulbs   Fill bulb — inert, doesn't react with tungsten filament
  Double-pane glass    Insulating spacer between panes (lower thermal conductivity than air)
  Argon lasers         Ar⁺ laser lines at 488 and 514 nm (cyan-green, used in confocal microscopy)
  Semiconductor fab    Inert atmosphere for silicon processing
  Wine preservation    Dense inert gas blanket prevents oxidation in opened bottles
```

### Krypton (Kr) and Xenon (Xe)

```
KRYPTON:
  High-intensity lighting  Kr-filled incandescent/halogen bulbs (brighter than Ar fill)
  Photographic flash       Flash tubes
  Laser (KrF excimer)      248 nm UV — photolithography for chip manufacturing
                           (critical for sub-micron features before EUV took over)
  Nuclear forensics        Kr-85 (t½ = 10.8 yr) released by nuclear reprocessing
                           → atmospheric monitoring detects clandestine nuclear programs

XENON:
  High-intensity lamps     Xe discharge lamps in IMAX projectors, car headlights (HID)
  Ion propulsion           Xe ion thrusters on spacecraft (Dawn, Hayabusa, GEO sats)
                           Xe⁺ accelerated by electric field → thrust (high Isp ~3000 s)
  Medical anesthesia       Xe is a general anesthetic via NMDA receptor inhibition
                           (expensive; xenon recycling systems exist)
  Radiation detectors      LXe (liquid xenon) and GXe in dark matter detectors (XENON1T, LUX)
                           High atomic number → good stopping power for γ/X-rays
  Nuclear medicine         ¹³³Xe lung ventilation scans
```

### Radon (Rn)

```
PROPERTIES:
  Heaviest naturally occurring noble gas (after Rn: only Og, synthetic)
  All isotopes radioactive; ²²²Rn is most significant (t½ = 3.8 days, from ²²⁶Ra decay)

HEALTH HAZARD:
  Source: radioactive decay chain of U-238 in soil/rock/building materials
  ²²²Rn → ²¹⁸Po → ²¹⁴Pb → ²¹⁴Bi → ²¹⁴Po → ²¹⁰Pb → ... → ²⁰⁶Pb
  Decay daughters (polonium, bismuth) are charged → deposit in lung tissue
  α particles at close range → DNA strand breaks → lung cancer
  Second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking (US: ~21,000 deaths/year)
  Action level: >4 pCi/L in US EPA guidance; >100 Bq/m³ in EU

TESTING: charcoal canisters or electret devices placed in lowest level of home 90 days
MITIGATION: sub-slab depressurization (pipe from under foundation → exterior vent + fan)
```

### Oganesson (Og, Z=118)

```
SYNTHESIS:
  ²⁴⁸Cm + ⁴⁸Ca → ²⁹⁴Og + 4n   (JINR Dubna, 2002)
  Only 5 atoms observed; t½ ≈ 0.69 ms (decays by α → Livermorium-290)

PREDICTED PROPERTIES:
  Relativistic effects are enormous at Z=118:
  • Inner electrons are highly relativistic (v close to c) → contracted s/p orbitals
  • Outer 7p½ orbital is highly stabilized → mixed with 7s
  • Predicted to be a solid at STP (unusual for a "noble gas")
  • Ionization energy predicted to be lower than Rn (relativistic destabilization of 6d/7p)
  • May NOT follow noble gas chemical inertness due to available low-energy orbitals

No practical applications — too few atoms, too short-lived.
Sits at bottom of Group 18 as the heaviest known element (nominally).
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"All neon signs contain neon"**
No. Classic neon (orange-red) uses neon. Blue/green signs use argon + mercury vapor.
Other colors come from colored glass tubes or phosphor coatings. The term "neon sign" is generic.

**"Helium makes your voice high-pitched because it's light"**
Partially correct. Helium doesn't change your vocal cords' resonant frequency (that's determined by
cord tension). It changes the **speed of sound** (He: 972 m/s vs air: 343 m/s). The same cord
vibration frequency radiates into a faster medium → the formants (resonant cavities) shift upward
→ you hear a higher-frequency timbre. The fundamental frequency stays the same.
Sulfur hexafluoride (SF₆) does the opposite (much heavier than air).

**"We're running out of helium"**
There is no shortage in terms of universal abundance (He is the 2nd most abundant element in the
universe). The shortage is in Earth's accessible reserves — He escapes to space, and the main
source is a few natural gas fields. Qatar is now a major supplier; the US released its strategic
reserve. Recovery from fusion reactors (which will produce He from D-T fusion) is a future option.

**"Noble gases are ideal gases"**
Very nearly true at room temperature and pressure — their van der Waals constants are the smallest
of all elements, and the small a (intermolecular attraction) and b (molecular volume) factors make
deviations from ideality tiny. At very low T (near liquefaction) or very high P, deviations matter.
