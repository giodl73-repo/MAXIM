# Halogens (Group 17) — F, Cl, Br, I, At, Ts

## The Big Picture

Halogens are the most electronegative elements in each period. With 7 valence electrons (ns²np⁵),
they are one electron short of a noble gas configuration — which defines everything about their chemistry.

```
GROUP 17 — KEY DATA

  Element   Z   Config          EN   IE₁    EA      bp(°C)  State@STP   Color
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Fluorine  9   [He]2s²2p⁵     3.98  1681   −328    −188    gas (F₂)    pale yellow
  Chlorine  17  [Ne]3s²3p⁵     3.16  1251   −349    −34     gas (Cl₂)   yellow-green
  Bromine   35  [Ar]3d¹⁰4s²4p⁵ 2.96  1140   −325    +59     liquid (Br₂) red-brown
  Iodine    53  [Kr]4d¹⁰5s²5p⁵ 2.66  1008   −295    +184    solid (I₂)  violet-black
  Astatine  85  [Xe]4f¹⁴5d¹⁰6s²6p⁵ ~2.2 ~890  −270(est.) ~337 solid     unknown
  Tennessine 117 [Rn]5f¹⁴6d¹⁰7s²7p⁵ — — — — —             superheavy

NOTE: EA values — more negative = more exothermic (stronger electron capture)
Cl has the highest (most negative) EA, NOT F — counterintuitive!
F's small size → electron-electron repulsion in compact 2p shell overrides expectation.
```

## Oxidizing Power vs Electronegativity

These are related but distinct:

```
ELECTRONEGATIVITY (Pauling scale): F > O > Cl > N > Br > I
  Tendency to attract electrons in a BOND

STANDARD REDUCTION POTENTIAL (oxidizing power in solution):
  F₂ + 2e⁻ → 2F⁻    E° = +2.87 V  ← strongest oxidizing agent of any element
  Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻  E° = +1.36 V
  Br₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Br⁻  E° = +1.07 V
  I₂ + 2e⁻ → 2I⁻    E° = +0.54 V

Oxidizing power decreases down the group: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂

DISPLACEMENT REACTIONS (follow from above):
  Cl₂ + 2 KBr → 2 KCl + Br₂  (Cl₂ displaces Br⁻)
  Cl₂ + 2 KI  → 2 KCl + I₂   (Cl₂ displaces I⁻)
  Br₂ + 2 KI  → 2 KBr + I₂   (Br₂ displaces I⁻)
  I₂ cannot displace Cl⁻ or Br⁻ (E° too low)
```

## HX Acid Strength — The Counterintuitive Trend

```
ACID STRENGTH IN WATER:
  HF < HCl < HBr < HI   (strength INCREASES down the group)

Wait — F is the most electronegative, so HF should be the strongest acid?

EXPLANATION:
  Acid strength = tendency to donate H⁺ in water
  Depends on: bond dissociation energy (BDE) AND solvation energy (of ions)

  H–F bond: 569 kJ/mol    ← very strong (short, compact bond)
  H–Cl bond: 432 kJ/mol
  H–Br bond: 366 kJ/mol
  H–I bond: 297 kJ/mol    ← weakest

  The H–X BDE DOMINATES the thermodynamic cycle.
  HI dissociates most readily → I⁻ easiest to release → strongest acid.

  HF specifics:
  pKa(HF) = 3.17 → WEAK acid in water (partially dissociated)
  But F⁻ is small and highly solvated → HF₂⁻ forms (F⁻ + HF → HF₂⁻)
  This autodissociation makes HF behave curiously:
    Very dilute HF → weaker than expected
    More concentrated HF → apparently stronger (HF₂⁻ equilibrium shifts)
  HF's CORROSIVE DANGER is not from its acidity but from F⁻ toxicity:
    F⁻ penetrates skin, chelates Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ → hypocalcemia → cardiac arrest
    Small area exposure (palm) can be fatal if untreated
    Treatment: topical/IV calcium gluconate (Ca²⁺ + 2F⁻ → CaF₂, precipitates)
```

## Fluorine (F, Z=9)

The most electronegative, most reactive nonmetal element. Can oxidize almost everything.

### Fluorite, Cryolite, and Production

```
F₂ PRODUCTION: electrolysis of KHF₂ / HF mixtures (Moissan process, 1886)
  Henri Moissan isolated F₂ — Nobel Chemistry 1906
  F₂ is so reactive that it corrodes glass (via SiO₂ + 4HF → SiF₄ + 2H₂O chain)
  F₂ cells use copper or nickel equipment (passivating fluoride layer protects)

KEY MINERALS:
  Fluorite (fluorspar): CaF₂ — main source of fluoride; mp 1418°C; smelting flux
  Cryolite: Na₃AlF₆ — historically critical for aluminum smelting (Hall-Héroult process)
            lowers Al₂O₃ melting point from 2054°C to ~1000°C → electrolysis feasible
            Natural deposits (Greenland) exhausted; now synthesized
  Fluorapatite: Ca₅(PO₄)₃F — accounts for fluoride in teeth enamel

FLUORIDE IN TEETH:
  Mechanism: F⁻ replaces OH⁻ in hydroxyapatite → fluorapatite
  Fluorapatite is less soluble in acid → enamel more resistant to bacterial acid attack
  Water fluoridation: 0.7 mg/L (US current standard) → reduces dental caries
  Excess: dental fluorosis (mottling), skeletal fluorosis (>10 mg/L chronic)
  Toothpaste: NaF or SnF₂ at ~1450 ppm F
```

### Organic Fluorine Chemistry

```
THE C–F BOND:
  Bond energy: 485 kJ/mol (strongest C–X bond)
  C–F is very short (135 pm), very polar
  F is electron-withdrawing → strong inductive effect

  This combination → metabolic stability + membrane permeability → medicinal chemistry gold

PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene / Teflon):
  Structure: (–CF₂–CF₂–)ₙ
  C–F bonds throughout → hydrophobic, non-stick, chemically inert
  mp ~327°C, continuous service to 260°C
  Low coefficient of friction (0.04 — among lowest of any solid)
  Uses: cookware coating, wire insulation, gaskets, bearings, stent-grafts

FLUOROPOLYMERS:
  PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride): piezoelectric, used in sensors, membranes
  FEP (fluorinated ethylene propylene): transparent, flexible Teflon analog
  PFAS (per/polyfluoroalkyl substances): "forever chemicals"
    PFOS, PFOA — highly stable (C–F bonds), bioaccumulate, link to health effects
    Water contamination crisis (AFFF firefighting foam at airports/military)
    Regulatory action globally (EPA MCLs, EU restriction)

FLUORINATED PHARMACEUTICALS:
  ~20% of marketed drugs contain at least one C–F bond
  Examples: Prozac (fluoxetine, CF₃ group), ciprofloxacin (ring F),
            Lipitor (atorvastatin, two F atoms), Prevacid (lansoprazole, CF₃)
  Why: metabolic stability (CYP enzymes struggle to cleave C–F), pKa tuning,
       conformational effects, membrane penetration

FLUORINE IN REFRIGERANTS (CFCs/HFCs):
  CFCl₃ (CFC-11), CF₂Cl₂ (CFC-12) → ozone-depleting (Cl radicals from UV photolysis)
  Montreal Protocol 1987 → phased out globally
  Replaced by HFCs (no Cl) → not ozone-depleting but potent GHGs
  Now being replaced by HFOs (hydrofluoroolefins) — lower GWP, short atmospheric lifetime
```

## Chlorine (Cl, Z=17)

### Industrial Chlorine — Foundation Chemical

```
CHLOR-ALKALI (see also 03-ALKALI-METALS.md):
  Scale: ~70 million tonnes Cl₂/yr globally
  Downstream: PVC (polyvinyl chloride), CHCl₃ (chloroform → drugs), CCl₄ (solvent),
              phosgene (COCl₂ → polycarbonates, polyurethanes),
              HCl (industrial acid), bleaching agents, disinfectants

BLEACHING (hypochlorite):
  Cl₂ + H₂O ⇌ HCl + HOCl  (hypochlorous acid — the active bleaching/disinfecting agent)
  NaOH + Cl₂ → NaOCl + HCl  (sodium hypochlorite = household bleach, 3-6% NaOCl)
  Mechanism: HOCl oxidizes chromophores (breaks conjugated systems → colorless) and
             kills microbes (oxidizes bacterial membrane proteins, cytoplasm)
  Ca(OCl)₂: calcium hypochlorite — pool shock, higher Cl content than liquid bleach

DRINKING WATER DISINFECTION:
  Cl₂ dosing → HOCl + HCl → kills pathogens
  Residual chlorine maintained (0.2–0.5 mg/L) to prevent regrowth in distribution
  Disinfection byproducts (DBPs): HOCl + natural organic matter → trihalomethanes (THMs)
  like chloroform (CHCl₃) — regulated carcinogen concern

CHLORINE IN BIOLOGY:
  Cl⁻: major extracellular anion (103 mEq/L plasma)
  Balances Na⁺ charge → osmotic regulation
  Gastric acid: HCl produced by parietal cells (proton pump secretes H⁺, Cl⁻ follows)
  GABA_A receptor is a Cl⁻ channel — opening hyperpolarizes neuron (inhibitory)
```

## Bromine (Br, Z=35)

```
ONE OF TWO LIQUID ELEMENTS AT STP (other: mercury)
  bp = 59°C, reddish-brown, toxic (oxidizing) vapors, lachrymatory
  Dense: 3.1 g/mL

PRODUCTION: from brine (seawater, oil-field brines, Dead Sea)
  Cl₂ + 2 Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2 Cl⁻  (displacement by Cl₂)

KEY APPLICATIONS:

Flame retardants: historically the dominant use
  Polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs), brominated polystyrene
  Mechanism: HBr radical scavenges flame chain → inhibits combustion
  Health/environmental concerns → phase-out of many PBDEs (EU REACH)

Pharmaceuticals: Br in drug molecules (~1% of drugs)
  Bromodomain inhibitors: BET proteins (epigenetic cancer targets)
  Sedatives: KBr (oldest anticonvulsant, still used in dogs)
  Vecuronium, pancuronium: neuromuscular blocking agents (quaternary ammonium Br salts)

Agricultural chemistry:
  Methyl bromide (CH₃Br): soil fumigant, critical pest control
  Also ozone-depleting → restricted under Montreal Protocol
  Replaced by chloropicrin, metam sodium, 1,3-dichloropropene

Photographic film (historical):
  AgBr: light-sensitive → silver halide photography backbone (still used in fine arts)

Organobromine in natural systems:
  Marine organisms (especially red algae, sea slugs) produce brominated secondary metabolites
  Many highly bioactive: bromoform, dibromomethane, indoles
  Reason: Br⁻ abundant in seawater (65 mg/L), peroxidases can oxidize it to Br+
```

## Iodine (I, Z=53)

### Thyroid Hormone — Why Iodine Is Essential

```
THYROID HORMONE SYNTHESIS:
  1. Thyroid gland concentrates I⁻ from blood (NIS transporter, Na/I symporter)
     Blood: ~80 μg/L; thyroid gland accumulates 10,000× concentration
  2. Thyroperoxidase oxidizes I⁻ → I• (iodine radical)
  3. I• adds to tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin protein
     → MIT (monoiodotyrosine) and DIT (diiodotyrosine)
  4. Two DIT units couple → T4 (thyroxine, 4 iodine atoms)
  5. One DIT + one MIT → T3 (triiodothyronine, 3 iodine atoms, more active)

  T4 and T3:
    Regulate metabolic rate, protein synthesis, growth, neurological development
    ~65% of T4 by mass is iodine → dietary iodine is literally required for thyroid hormone

  IODINE DEFICIENCY:
    #1 preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide
    Goiter: thyroid gland enlarges trying to trap more I⁻ → visible neck swelling
    Cretinism: severe I deficiency in pregnancy → brain damage, dwarfism
    Solution: iodized salt (KIO₃ or KI, 20-40 mg I/kg salt) since 1920s
              Still a problem in inland regions far from seafood

  IODINE-131 (I-131):
    t½ = 8 days, β⁻ + γ emitter
    Follows iodine biochemistry → concentrates in thyroid
    Used for: thyroid cancer treatment (ablation), hyperthyroidism
    Nuclear emergency concern: radioactive I-131 from reactors → contaminate food chain
    → thyroid in children
    KI tablets: stable iodide saturates thyroid → blocks uptake of radioactive I-131
    (must be taken before/immediately after exposure)
```

### Other Iodine Uses

```
POVIDONE-IODINE (PVP-I, Betadine):
  PVP polymer complexed with I₂ → slow-release iodine
  Broad-spectrum antiseptic, low toxicity to tissues
  Mechanism: I₂ penetrates cell walls, oxidizes/iodinates proteins → antimicrobial

CONTRAST AGENTS:
  Iodinated contrast media (e.g., iohexol, iodixanol) for CT imaging
  I has high atomic number → X-ray attenuation
  Water-soluble ionic or nonionic formulations
  Allergic reactions possible (anaphylactoid, not IgE-mediated for most)

I₂ in organic chemistry:
  Iodination of aromatics (electrophilic aromatic substitution with I₂/HNO₃ or ICl)
  Aryl iodides = useful Pd-coupling partners (C–I easier to oxidize add into Pd than C–Br, C–Cl)
```

## Astatine (At, Z=85) and Tennessine (Ts, Z=117)

```
ASTATINE:
  Rarest naturally occurring element: ~30 g on Earth at any time
  Produced by nuclear reactions in upper atmosphere (²¹⁰Bi + n → ²¹⁰At), but decays fast
  Most stable isotope: ²¹¹At, t½ = 7.2 hours
  Produced artificially: ²⁰⁹Bi + ⁴He → ²¹¹At + 2n (cyclotron bombardment)

  CHEMISTRY: metalloid behavior — partially metallic character
  Predicted to be more metallic than I (relativistic effects of heavy atom)
  At₂ may be a metallic solid at STP; significant uncertainty remains

  MEDICAL USE (At-211):
    α-emitter (5.87 MeV) → Targeted Alpha Therapy
    At-211 labeled antibodies/peptides → cancer cell destruction
    Short t½ and α range → local dose, minimal systemic damage
    Clinical trials: thyroid cancer, brain tumors, ovarian cancer

TENNESSINE (Ts, Z=117):
  Synthesized 2010: ²⁴⁹Bk + ⁴⁸Ca → ²⁹³Ts + 4n  (JINR/Oak Ridge collaboration)
  t½ of ²⁹³Ts: ~51 ms
  Named for Tennessee (home of ORNL, which produced the Bk-249 target)
  No chemistry performed — too few atoms, too short-lived
  Expected to be a solid (heaviest Group 17), relativistic effects unknown
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"F is most electronegative but Cl has highest electron affinity — contradiction?"**
No. Electronegativity measures tendency to attract electrons in a bond (relative, in-bond).
Electron affinity measures energy released when a free atom gains an electron (absolute).
F's electron affinity is lower than Cl's because F's 2p shell is very compact: adding an
electron to F⁻ experiences more electron-electron repulsion than adding to Cl⁻ in the larger
3p shell. In a bond, the fact that F pulls more strongly than Cl is about the bond polarity,
not about the isolated atom.

**"HF is dangerous but HCl is 'just' hydrochloric acid — isn't F more reactive than Cl?"**
HF burns are uniquely dangerous not because HF is a strong acid (it isn't — pKa 3.17 vs HCl pKa −7)
but because F⁻ ion is toxic. It penetrates through skin, chelates Ca²⁺ in tissue and blood,
causing systemic hypocalcemia → cardiac arrest. A 2.5% body surface HF burn can be fatal.
HCl burns are painful but mechanically — tissue damage from the proton, not systemic toxicity.

**"Why does bromine exist as a liquid when Cl₂ and I₂ are gas/solid?"**
All halogens form diatomic molecules. Intermolecular forces (London dispersion) increase with
molecular weight and polarizability. Br₂ (M = 160 g/mol) falls in the range where these forces
are just sufficient to maintain liquid phase at room temperature. Cl₂ (71 g/mol) — too light.
I₂ (254 g/mol) — intermolecular forces strong enough to maintain solid at STP (sublimes readily).
