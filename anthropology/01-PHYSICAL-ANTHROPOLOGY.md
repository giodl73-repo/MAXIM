## Section 1: Human Biological Variation — The Framework

### Lewontin's Analysis (1972) and Its Descendants

```
  THE KEY FINDING:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ ~85% of human genetic variation is WITHIN populations   │
  │ ~15% of human genetic variation is BETWEEN populations  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  What this means: any two randomly chosen humans from the same
  "racial" group differ MORE from each other genetically than
  the average between-group difference.

  Lewontin (1972) used protein polymorphisms.
  Confirmed repeatedly with microsatellites, SNPs, whole genomes.

  FST statistic: measures genetic differentiation between groups.
  Global human FST ~ 0.10-0.15.
  Compare: domestic dog breeds FST > 0.30 (more differentiated
  than human "races" despite being one species).
```

### Why "Race" as Biological Taxonomy Fails

```
  PROBLEM 1: Clinal variation
  Traits vary gradually across geography — there are no sharp
  boundaries where one "race" ends and another begins.

  PROBLEM 2: Non-concordance of traits
  Traits that define folk "races" do not cluster together.
  Dark skin + straight hair + thin lips can occur in the same
  individual without that being any known "racial" category.

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Skin color    ->  UV/latitude gradient              │
  │ Nose shape    ->  temperature/humidity gradient     │
  │ Eye fold      ->  UV glare adaptation (possibly)    │
  │ Blood type    ->  random drift + disease history    │
  │                                                     │
  │ These gradients DON'T ALIGN with each other.        │
  │ "Race" carves at the wrong joints.                  │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PROBLEM 3: Most variation is within groups
  Any classification system that puts 85% of variation inside
  the same bin is not a useful biological taxonomy.

  What IS real: populations have different frequencies of alleles.
  This is clinically relevant (pharmacogenomics, disease risk).
  But that's ancestry/population history — not typological "race."
```

## Section 2: Adaptive Variation — What Does Vary and Why

### Skin Pigmentation — The UV/Folate/Vitamin D Triangle

```
  THE TRADEOFF:

  UV radiation hits skin
         |
         +------> breaks down FOLATE (critical for neural tube
         |        development — deficiency causes spina bifida)
         |        HIGH-UV = FOLATE DESTRUCTION
         |
         +------> enables VITAMIN D synthesis
                  LOW-UV = VITAMIN D DEFICIENCY (rickets,
                           immune dysfunction, bone fragility)

  DARK SKIN: high melanin protects folate in high-UV environments
             (equatorial populations)
  LIGHT SKIN: low melanin maximizes vitamin D synthesis in
              low-UV environments (high-latitude populations)

  Selection is fast for this trait when populations migrate
  to very different UV regimes.
```

### Body Proportions — Bergmann's and Allen's Rules

```
  BERGMANN'S RULE (1847):
  Body size scales with latitude/temperature.
  Larger bodies have LESS surface area per unit volume —
  better heat retention in cold climates.

  ALLEN'S RULE (1877):
  Extremity (limb/appendage) length is shorter relative to
  body in cold climates — reduces surface area for heat loss.

  Evidence in humans:
  +------------------+--------------------+-------------------+
  | Group            | Climate            | Body proportions  |
  +------------------+--------------------+-------------------+
  | Inuit/Inupiaq    | Arctic             | Stocky, short limb|
  | Nilotic (Dinka)  | Hot/arid savanna   | Tall, long limb   |
  | Andean highland  | Cold altitude      | Stocky barrel chest|
  | Kalahari San     | Hot/variable       | Small, gracile    |
  +------------------+--------------------+-------------------+

  These patterns are both genetic (long-term selection) AND
  developmental (body responds to temperature during growth).
  Teasing these apart requires careful study design.
```

### Altitude Adaptation — Convergent Solutions to Hypoxia

```
  HYPOXIC STRESS:
  At high altitude, partial pressure of O2 falls.
  At 4,000 m (Tibetan Plateau, Andean altiplano):
    ~40% less available O2 than sea level.
  Physiological problem: sustain aerobic metabolism
    under chronic O2 deficit.

  THREE INDEPENDENT EVOLUTIONARY SOLUTIONS:
  ┌───────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Population    │ Adaptation mechanism                     │
  ├───────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Tibetans      │ EPAS1 variant (HIF-2α pathway): lower    │
  │               │ hemoglobin concentration than expected;  │
  │               │ more efficient O2 delivery per RBC.      │
  │               │ This allele was acquired from Denisovans │
  │               │ via introgression (adaptive introgression│
  │               │ — the most direct documented benefit of  │
  │               │ archaic-modern hybridization).           │
  ├───────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Andeans       │ EGLN1 variant: elevated hemoglobin       │
  │               │ concentration (polycythemia); different  │
  │               │ genetic solution to the same problem.    │
  ├───────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Ethiopians    │ Multiple variants across different gene  │
  │ (Amhara,      │ regions; neither the Tibetan nor Andean  │
  │  Oromo)       │ mechanism; third independent solution.   │
  └───────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CONVERGENT EVOLUTION:
  Same environmental pressure → different genetic solutions
  in independent populations. This is the human genetics
  parallel to convergent morphological evolution (wings
  in birds/bats/insects: same aerodynamic function,
  different developmental origins).

  Why Tibetan solution is different from Andean:
  The Andean populations arrived at the plateau more
  recently (~11 ka vs Tibetan high-altitude occupation
  potentially dating to Denisovan times). Different
  founding populations, different standing variation
  available for selection to act on.
```

### Disease Susceptibility — Sickle Cell as the Model Case

```
  Sickle cell allele (HbS) — hemoglobin variant:

  HbA/HbA (normal):      Normal RBCs, susceptible to malaria
  HbS/HbS (sickle cell): Severe anemia, usually fatal without
                         modern medical care
  HbA/HbS (carrier):     Mild sickling, SIGNIFICANT RESISTANCE
                         to Plasmodium falciparum malaria

  BALANCED POLYMORPHISM:
  In malaria-endemic regions, HbS carriers have higher fitness
  than both homozygotes. The allele is maintained by balancing
  selection — neither eliminated nor fixed.

  Distribution mirrors historical malaria distribution exactly:
  Sub-Saharan Africa, Mediterranean, Middle East, South Asia.
  Independently evolved in multiple populations (convergent).

  This is the textbook case of:
  1. Natural selection acting on human populations NOW
  2. Gene-environment interaction (the environment is the parasite)
  3. Why genetic variants need ecological context to be understood
```

### Dental Anthropology

```
  SHOVEL-SHAPED INCISORS:
  Upper incisors with ridged edges — high frequency in:
  - East Asian populations
  - Indigenous American populations (shared ancestry)
  - Very low frequency in European/African populations

  Caused by a SINGLE gene variant: EDAR (ectodysplasin A receptor)
  The same variant also causes:
  - Increased sweat gland density
  - Thicker hair shafts
  - More numerous mammary gland branches

  A single mutation with pleiotropic effects. Strong selection
  for one trait (possibly climate-related sweat glands) dragged
  along multiple correlated traits.

  CARABELLI'S CUSP:
  Extra cusp on upper first molar — higher frequency in European
  and Middle Eastern populations. Used in population affinity
  assessment (forensic and bioarchaeological contexts).
```

## Section 4: Forensic Anthropology

### The Forensic Context

```
  WHO CALLS A FORENSIC ANTHROPOLOGIST:
  - Law enforcement: unidentified skeletonized remains
  - Mass fatality events: plane crashes, mass disasters
  - Human rights / mass grave investigations
  - Military: JPAC/DPAA (Defense POW/MIA Accounting Agency)
    identifies US service members from WWII, Korea, Vietnam

  WHAT THEY DO:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Scene: recovery, documentation, taphonomy           │
  │ 2. Lab: biological profile (age/sex/stature/affinity)  │
  │ 3. Trauma analysis: perimortem injury pattern          │
  │ 4. Identification: compare to antemortem records       │
  │    (medical/dental X-rays, DNA comparison)             │
  │ 5. Testimony: expert witness in court (Daubert std)    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Perimortem vs. Postmortem Fracture — The Key Distinction

```
  PERIMORTEM (at or near time of death — bone still wet/fresh):
  - Green bone response: plastic deformation before fracture
  - Spiral fractures (torsion)
  - Butterfly fragments
  - Sharp edges at fracture margins
  - Flaking consistent with impact direction
  --> Indicates TRAUMA contributing to or occurring at death

  POSTMORTEM (after death — bone dry/degraded):
  - Transverse fractures (no plastic deformation)
  - Jagged, angular edges
  - White/bleached surface at fracture (not dark like internal)
  - No hematoma staining
  --> Taphonomic process: animal scavenging, soil pressure,
      excavation damage, archaeologist's trowel

  THE CRITICAL FORENSIC PROBLEM:
  Misidentifying postmortem damage as perimortem trauma leads to
  false homicide determinations. And vice versa. This is why
  forensic anthropologists take years to develop pattern recognition.
```

### Mass Grave Investigation

```
  HUMAN RIGHTS APPLICATIONS:

  Bosnia/Herzegovina (ICMP — International Commission on
  Missing Persons): ~8,100 Srebrenica victims. Secondary
  graves (remains moved to hide evidence). DNA-led ID —
  >7,000 identified. Largest forensic operation in history.

  Guatemala (FAFG — Guatemalan Forensic Anthropology
  Foundation): civil war victims 1960-1996. ~45,000 cases.

  Methodology:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ - Satellite imagery to locate grave sites              │
  │ - Archaeological excavation of primary/secondary graves│
  │ - Inventory and documentation of each individual       │
  │ - Biological profile for each                          │
  │ - Trauma analysis — gunshot wounds, perimortem injury  │
  │ - DNA extraction + family reference database matching  │
  │ - Repatriation to families for burial                  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Daubert standard (US federal): expert testimony must be
  based on peer-reviewed, testable methodology with known
  error rates. Forensic anthropology methods are generally
  accepted but must be validated for each application.
```

## Section 5: Human Growth and Life History

### Life History Theory Applied to Humans

```
  LIFE HISTORY = the schedule of growth, reproduction, and aging
  Organisms face tradeoffs: invest energy in growth/survival
  NOW or reproduction NOW? Fast life history vs. slow.

  HUMANS ARE EXTREME K-STRATEGISTS:
  - Very slow maturation (sexual maturity ~13-16 yr)
  - Extended juvenile period unlike any other primate
  - Small litter size (usually 1)
  - Extremely high parental investment
  - Post-reproductive lifespan (menopause — unique to humans
    among primates, also killer whales and some other cetaceans)
  - Very long total lifespan

  Why extended childhood?
  - Brain development: human brain is 25% of adult size at birth;
    reaches adult size by ~6-7 years but keeps developing to 25+
  - Skills acquisition: complex hunting, social skills, language
    take years to acquire at high fidelity
  - This requires extended provisioning from parents AND others
```

### The Grandmother Hypothesis

```
  PROBLEM: Why menopause? Fitness = reproductive output.
  A female who stops reproducing at 50 seems to be "wasting"
  30+ years of potential reproduction.

  KRISTEN HAWKES' ANSWER (Hadza hunter-gatherer data, 1997):

  Post-menopausal women provision grandchildren, especially
  weaned-but-not-yet-independent grandchildren. This frees
  mothers to have additional children sooner (shorter inter-
  birth intervals). Net effect on inclusive fitness is positive.

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Mother -> new infant                                   │
  │     ↑                                                  │
  │ Grandmother <-> provisioning weaned toddler            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Evidence: In societies without modern medicine, maternal
  grandmother presence is associated with higher grandchild
  survival rates. Paternal grandmother effect is smaller
  (paternity uncertainty reduces investment incentives).

  Model predicts: selection for post-reproductive survival
  once grandmothering helps enough. Explains human longevity.

  Competing hypothesis (Patrilocal band model): menopause
  evolves because females age out of reproductive competition
  with younger females in the same kin group, but not with
  males' kin group elsewhere — triggers reproductive cessation.
  Both models have supporting data; neither definitively wins.
```

### Concealed Ovulation

```
  UNIQUE TO HUMANS among great apes: no visible estrus signal
  (bonobo and chimp females show dramatic genital swellings at
  ovulation — behaviorally conspicuous)

  Competing explanations:
  1. Paternity confusion: if no male knows who fathered which
     offspring, males invest in all infants (infanticide reduction).
  2. Mate guarding: concealed ovulation forces males to
     guard females continuously to ensure paternity.
  3. Female choice: concealed ovulation enables females to
     choose mates without male interference.
  4. Pair bonding: continuous sexual receptivity maintains
     pair bonds for cooperative child-rearing.

  These are not mutually exclusive. The lack of a clear verdict
  illustrates how adaptationist "just-so stories" multiply in
  this area — multiple consistent explanations exist, and
  distinguishing them requires comparative and experimental evidence
  that is hard to obtain.
```

## Section 6: Human vs. Great Ape Anatomy

### Bipedal Adaptations

```
  BIPEDALISM ANATOMY — differences from ape quadrupedalism:

  SKULL:
  - Foramen magnum (skull base opening for spinal cord)
    HUMANS: positioned directly below skull (head balanced on top)
    CHIMPS:  positioned toward back (head held forward from spine)

  SPINE:
  - Lumbar lordosis (inward curve): present in humans, absent in apes
    Transfers body weight efficiently to bipedal legs
  - S-curve spine (cervical + lumbar lordosis): shock absorption

  PELVIS:
  - HUMANS: Short and wide (bowl shape), supports organs in upright
    posture, birth canal fully reoriented
  - APES: Long and narrow (flat blade), suited for quadrupedal
    locomotion

  LOWER LIMB:
  - Valgus knee angle: human femur angled inward from hip to knee
    (centers weight over foot in single-limb stance during walking)
  - Ape femur: vertical (suited for crouched gait)
  - Foot arch: humans have a rigid arch as energy storage spring
    Apes have flat, grasping feet
  - Long legs relative to arms (reversed in great apes)

  COST: Bipedalism creates back pain, knee problems, obstetric
  difficulty (wide baby head through narrow pelvis) — these are
  engineering tradeoffs, not optimized design.
```

### Encephalization

```
  BRAIN SIZE SCALING:

  Absolute brain size (cm³):
  - Gorilla:      ~500 cc
  - Chimpanzee:   ~400 cc
  - Human:        ~1350 cc (range 900-2100)
  - Neanderthal:  ~1500 cc (larger than modern human average)
  - H. erectus:   ~900 cc

  But body size matters: elephants have 5000 cc brains.
  Use ENCEPHALIZATION QUOTIENT (EQ): brain/expected brain for
  body size.
    Crow/raven: EQ ~2.5
    Chimpanzee: EQ ~2.5
    Dolphin:    EQ ~4
    Human:      EQ ~7

  Metabolic cost: human brain = 2% of body weight, 20% of
  resting metabolic rate. This is why cooking (Wrangham's
  hypothesis) was key — pre-digested food enabled high-quality
  calorie extraction to support the large brain.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Forensic anthropology is like CSI — they can tell everything from bones."**
They can build a biological profile from good material — but ranges are wide.
Age estimates on adults are often +/- 10-20 years. Population affinity is a
probability statement, not a determination. Stature equations have standard
errors. Court cases require communicating uncertainty clearly, which is not the
TV version.

**"Race is biological — DNA tests tell me I'm 40% Irish."**
Ancestry DNA tests measure your similarity to reference populations, which are
social/geographic groups — not biological taxa. The percentages reflect where
your ancestors lived and who they descended from, which is population history.
That's real information. But "Irish" is not a natural kind the way "mammal" is.

**"More brain = smarter."**
Within the human range, brain volume correlates weakly to insignificantly with
IQ scores after controlling for body size. Paul Broca (19th century) weighed
brains to prove European men were smartest — the data was fabricated. Stephen
Jay Gould's "The Mismeasure of Man" demolished much of that tradition. Brain
organization and connectivity matter more than total volume.

**"Bipedalism was selected because it freed our hands for tools."**
That is one hypothesis. But the best early bipeds (Australopithecus, Ardipithecus)
predate the oldest tools by 1-2 million years. Bipedalism was selected for
something — energetic efficiency, thermoregulation, display, carrying food — but
the "freed hands for tools" story conflates correlation with causation. Tools
exploited an already-bipedal body plan.

**"Sickle cell is a 'Black disease.'"**
Sickle cell distribution tracks historical malaria distribution, not race.
It is common in sub-Saharan Africa, the Mediterranean, the Middle East, and
South Asia — all malaria-endemic regions. Mediterranean Greek and Sicilian
populations have significant HbS frequencies. Treating it as a "Black disease"
is a category error that has caused real clinical harm (under-diagnosis in
non-Black patients with symptoms).
