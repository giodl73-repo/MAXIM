## Section 1: The Adaptationist Program

### Evolutionary Psychology's Core Claims

```
  THE FRAMEWORK (Cosmides and Tooby, Santa Barbara school):

  1. The brain is a physical system shaped by natural selection
  2. It evolved to solve adaptive problems in the EEA
     (Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness — roughly
     Pleistocene African savanna, though chimps in forest
     predate that)
  3. It contains domain-specific psychological mechanisms
     (modules), not general-purpose learning
  4. These mechanisms are universal human nature, even if
     expressed differently in different cultures

  DOMAIN-SPECIFIC MODULES (proposed):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Face recognition: neonates prefer face-like patterns  │
  │ Cheater detection: Wason selection task with social   │
  │   content (social contracts) is easier than abstract  │
  │   version — Cosmides's original finding               │
  │ Fear acquisition: snakes and spiders are acquired     │
  │   faster than cars and guns (preparedness learning)  │
  │ Kin recognition: Westermarck effect, incest avoidance │
  │ Language acquisition device (Chomsky): universal      │
  │   grammar constraints on language learning            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The EEA Concept — Useful and Abused

```
  Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness:
  The ancestral environment in which human psychological
  mechanisms were selected. NOT a specific place or time —
  a statistical composite of selection pressures over
  Pleistocene.

  USEFUL:
  - Explains why evolved mechanisms can be maladaptive NOW
    (e.g., craving fat/sugar was adaptive in calorie-scarce
    environments; causes obesity in calorie-dense modern world)
  - Predicts cross-cultural universals in domains where
    ancestral selection was consistent

  ABUSED:
  - EEA is often specified post-hoc to explain a hypothesis
  - The "ancestral environment" was diverse: savanna, woodland,
    coast, tropical forest — not a single environment
  - Mechanisms had to work in multiple environments
  - "Just-so story" risk: any behavior can be reverse-
    engineered to an adaptive story
```

### Critiques of Evolutionary Psychology

```
  1. "JUST-SO STORIES" (Gould and Lewontin 1979):
     Any phenotype can be given an adaptationist explanation
     post-hoc. The adaptationist program has no falsification
     criterion for individual cases. You need comparative data
     across species or cross-cultural data to test predictions.

  2. NATURALISTIC FALLACY:
     "This behavior evolved, therefore it is natural, therefore
     it is good/acceptable." Completely invalid. Infanticide is
     documented and explicable evolutionarily. That doesn't
     make it acceptable. Biology describes; ethics prescribes.

  3. SAMPLE BIAS (WEIRD):
     Most evol. psych experiments use US undergraduate students.
     Henrich/Heine/Norenzayan (2010): WEIRD populations are
     outliers on many psychological dimensions. "Confirmed" EP
     findings often fail cross-cultural replication.

  4. DEVELOPMENTAL PLASTICITY:
     Genes specify ranges of developmental outcomes, not
     fixed outcomes. The same genes in different developmental
     environments produce different traits. Evol. psych often
     underweights this.

  5. MODULARITY IS CONTESTED:
     The "massive modularity" of the Santa Barbara school
     (Fodor's original modularity was much narrower — input
     systems, not general cognition) has not been supported
     by neuroscience. The brain is more interactive than
     modular at the levels evol. psych requires.
```

## Section 2: Evolution of Cooperation

### Hamilton's Rule

```
  W.D. HAMILTON (1964): inclusive fitness theory

  rB > C

  r = coefficient of relatedness (probability that gene
      present in actor is also present in recipient)
  B = benefit to recipient (in fitness units)
  C = cost to actor (in fitness units)

  Altruism evolves when the benefit to relatives (weighted
  by relatedness) exceeds the cost to the actor.

  RELATEDNESS VALUES:
  Identical twins:    r = 1.0
  Parent/offspring:   r = 0.5
  Full siblings:      r = 0.5
  Half-siblings:      r = 0.25
  First cousins:      r = 0.125
  Unrelated:          r = 0.0

  J.B.S. Haldane (apocryphally): "I would lay down my life
  for two brothers or eight cousins." (Mathematically correct.)

  This explains: eusociality in Hymenoptera (haplodiploidy
  makes sisters r=0.75), worker bees, alarm calls in
  prairie dogs, and much human nepotism.
```

### Reciprocal Altruism

```
  TRIVERS (1971):
  Altruism toward non-kin can evolve if:
  - Interactions are repeated (iterated)
  - Actors can recognize each other
  - Cheaters can be detected and punished

  The iterated Prisoner's Dilemma:
  ┌────────────┬──────────────────────────────────┐
  │            │ Other: Cooperate  Other: Defect  │
  ├────────────┼──────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Cooperate  │    3, 3                0, 5      │
  │ Defect     │    5, 0                1, 1      │
  └────────────┴──────────────────────────────────┘

  Single round: defect always (dominant strategy).
  Iterated indefinitely: cooperation can evolve.

  AXELROD TOURNAMENTS (1984):
  Computer simulation — many strategies submitted by game
  theorists. TIT-FOR-TAT won:
  1. Start with cooperation
  2. Mirror partner's last move thereafter
  3. Never be the first to defect
  Simple. Forgiving. Retaliatory when provoked.

  Human reciprocal altruism: blood sharing among Hadza
  hunter-gatherers (delayed return), food sharing, labor
  exchange, coalition support — the expectation of future
  reciprocation underlies much of human prosocial behavior.
```

### Strong Reciprocity and Altruistic Punishment

```
  PROBLEM WITH SIMPLE RECIPROCAL ALTRUISM:
  In large anonymous groups (modern societies), "reputation"
  tracking breaks down. One-shot interactions with strangers.
  Yet humans cooperate in large anonymous groups — unique.

  GINTIS, FEHR, BOWLES (2000s):
  "Strong reciprocity": some humans are predisposed to punish
  norm violators even at personal cost, even in one-shot
  anonymous interactions.

  EVIDENCE — ULTIMATUM GAME:
  Proposer splits $10. Responder accepts or rejects.
  If rejected: both get $0.
  Rational actor theory: offer $1, accept any positive amount.

  Actual results (cross-cultural, Henrich et al. 2001,
  15 small-scale societies):
  - Modal offer: 40-50% (roughly equal split)
  - Offers below 20-30% are typically rejected
  - People pay to punish "unfair" proposers
  - Cross-cultural variation in what counts as "fair"

  Interpretation: humans have a "fairness" emotion that
  triggers rejection of inequitable outcomes even at cost.
  This could not evolve in simple kin selection or reciprocal
  altruism frameworks.

  COMPETING EXPLANATIONS:
  Cultural group selection: groups with strong norm enforcement
  outcompete groups without it -> cooperation spreads.
  Multilevel selection debate: Hamilton/Williams gene-selection
  framework says group selection is mathematically possible but
  requires very specific population structure. Boyd/Richerson
  argue cultural inheritance creates that structure.
```

## Section 3: Mating Systems and Sexual Selection

### Cross-Cultural Patterns

```
  HUMAN MATING SYSTEMS (cross-cultural survey, Murdock 1967):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Monogamy:        ~17% of societies (serial is    │
  │                  common; simultaneous rare)       │
  │ Polygyny:        ~83% of societies permit it     │
  │                  (but only ~20-30% of males in   │
  │                   polygynous societies have > 1  │
  │                   wife — resource-constrained)   │
  │ Polyandry:       ~1% of societies (fraternal     │
  │                   polyandry — Tibet/Nepal)        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  BUT: In most societies, most people are de facto
  monogamous most of the time. The "permitted" vs.
  "practiced" distinction is crucial.

  ANATOMICAL EVIDENCE FOR MATING SYSTEM:
  Sexual size dimorphism (SSD):
  - Gorilla: males 2x female size -> strong polygyny
  - Chimp: 1.3x -> moderate polygyny/multi-male
  - Human: 1.1-1.2x -> mild-moderate polygyny in evolutionary
    history, not strict monogamy, not gorilla-level polygyny
  - Gibbons: no SSD -> pair-bonded (monogamous)

  Testis size / body weight ratio:
  - High testis:body ratio = sperm competition = multi-male
    mating (chimps: large testes)
  - Low testis:body ratio = low sperm competition = mate
    guarding (gorillas: tiny testes relative to body)
  - Humans: intermediate (consistent with mild sperm competition
    — pair-bonded but with some infidelity)
```

### Pair Bonding and Paternal Investment

```
  LOVEJOY'S "PROVISIONING" MODEL (1981):
  Early hominin males provisioned females with food in
  exchange for sexual fidelity (paternity certainty).
  Pair bonding -> paternal investment -> infant survival
  -> selection for longer childhood and bigger brains.

  Evidence for:
  - Human males invest in offspring far more than chimp males
  - Concealed ovulation (makes continuous mate guarding
    necessary/efficient)
  - Long-term pair bonds in most human societies

  Evidence against:
  - The provisioning model requires monogamy, but anatomy
    (SSD, testis size) suggests moderate polygyny in ancestral
    populations
  - Hunter-gatherer paternal investment varies enormously
  - Pair bond ≠ exclusive bond (extra-pair paternity documented
    in "monogamous" species including humans)

  MHC-BASED MATE CHOICE:
  Major Histocompatibility Complex (immune system genes).
  Studies (Wedekind sweaty t-shirt experiments 1995):
  women preferred the scent of men with DISSIMILAR MHC.
  Adaptive: offspring with diverse MHC have broader immune
  defense. Effect is disrupted by oral contraceptives (which
  shift preference toward MHC-similar individuals — possibly
  because they mimic pregnancy state, when kin are preferred).
  Replication has been mixed; the effect is real but small.
```

## Section 4: Kinship and Cooperation Theory

### Westermarck Effect — The Incest Avoidance Mechanism

```
  EDWARD WESTERMARCK (1891): People who live together in close
  physical proximity during the first years of life develop a
  sexual aversion to each other.

  This is the MECHANISM for incest avoidance — not a taboo
  that "overrides" natural attraction; rather, the attraction
  never develops for co-residents.

  EVIDENCE:
  Israeli kibbutzim (Shepher 1971): children raised communally
  in peer groups (not related). Analysis of ~3,000 marriages:
  zero between children who were in the same peer group during
  the first 6 years of life. No rule required.

  Taiwan shim-pua marriages: families arranged marriages by
  adopting a girl (shim-pua) as an infant, raised with the
  future husband. Compared to same-family arranged marriages:
  shim-pua marriages had higher divorce rates, fewer children,
  more extra-marital affairs. Sexual aversion despite social
  arrangement.

  The cross-cultural INCEST TABOO (universal) is then not
  the cause of avoidance but a cultural elaboration of the
  Westermarck mechanism — and often extends it further (to
  non-co-resident kin, to symbolic kin categories).

  Failure mode: families separated in early childhood
  (adoption, war) can experience GENETIC SEXUAL ATTRACTION
  when reunited as adults — the Westermarck mechanism did
  not activate.
```

### Cross-Cousin Marriage — Structural Anthropology Meets Genetics

```
  Cross-cousin: Mother's brother's child, or Father's sister's
  child (i.e., cousins through a cross-sex sibling link).
  vs.
  Parallel cousin: Mother's sister's child or Father's brother's
  child (i.e., cousins through a same-sex sibling link).

  In many societies, cross-cousin marriage is preferred or
  prescribed; parallel cousin marriage is prohibited
  (treated as incest).

  STRUCTURAL EXPLANATION (Levi-Strauss):
  Cross-cousin marriage creates alliances BETWEEN lineages.
  Your father's sister married OUT (to another lineage) —
  her children are in that lineage. Marrying them maintains
  the alliance. Parallel cousins stay in your own lineage —
  marrying them is "keeping it in the family" with no
  alliance benefit.

  GENETIC EXPLANATION:
  Cross-cousins share r = 0.125 (same as parallel cousins).
  But cross-cousin marriage in a system of exogamy does NOT
  increase inbreeding over the baseline; parallel cousin
  marriage can. Some researchers find the preference has
  a weak genetic fitness logic; others see it as purely
  structural.

  Notably: Arabic parallel cousin marriage (patrilateral
  parallel cousin) is an exception — preferred in some
  cultures, related to lineage solidarity and property
  retention. Demonstrates culture overriding the structural
  logic.
```

## Section 5: Life History Theory

### The Human Life History — Extreme Slow Pace

```
  LIFE HISTORY TRADEOFFS:
  Energy is finite. Allocate it to:
  Current reproduction vs. Future reproduction
  Growth vs. Reproduction
  Parental investment vs. Number of offspring

  Fast life history: small body, early reproduction, many
  offspring, low investment per offspring, short lifespan.
  Examples: mice, rabbits, most insects.

  Slow life history: large body, late reproduction, few
  offspring, high investment per offspring, long lifespan.
  Examples: elephants, great apes, humans.

  HUMANS — THE OUTLIER AMONG PRIMATES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    Chimp      Human                │
  │ Age at first birth:  13       19-21                │
  │ Inter-birth interval: 5 yr    3-4 yr (with help!)  │
  │ Weaning:             4-5 yr   2-3 yr               │
  │ Max lifespan:        ~60      ~120                  │
  │ Post-reprod lifespan: ~0      20-40 yr (females)   │
  │ Male care of offspring: none  variable but present  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  The shorter human inter-birth interval DESPITE longer
  dependency is the key puzzle — resolved by cooperative
  breeding (alloparental care) and food provisioning to
  weaned children by non-maternal individuals.
```

### The Grandmother Hypothesis — Extended

```
  Kristen Hawkes (Utah), based on Hadza data (1997-):

  Among Hadza foragers (Tanzania), grandmothers dig tubers
  and give food to their weaned grandchildren. This enables
  mothers to wean earlier and reproduce again sooner.

  Simulation models: starting from a chimpanzee-like life
  history and adding grandmother provisioning, the model
  evolves extended lifespan and post-reproductive life.

  PREDICTIONS AND DATA:
  - Maternal grandmother presence -> grandchild survival
    (documented in pre-industrial Finland, rural Gambia,
    Ethiopia — demographic data)
  - Paternal grandmother effect smaller (possible paternity
    uncertainty reduces investment incentive)
  - Effect largest for second-born children (whose sibling
    competes with the new infant)

  ALTERNATIVE: "Patriarch hypothesis" (Marlowe):
  Older men in some societies provision much more than
  grandmothers. The older-male provisioning story also
  predicts long post-reproductive life in females
  (men prefer younger mates -> selection for female
  lifespan beyond reproduction). Data: mixed.
```

## Section 6: The WEIRD Problem

### Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan (2010)

```
  THE CLAIM:
  96% of subjects in the top psychology journals come from
  societies representing 12% of the world's population.
  These societies are Western, Educated, Industrialized,
  Rich, Democratic — WEIRD.

  AND: WEIRD populations are often statistical OUTLIERS on
  the very dimensions psychologists treat as universal
  human nature.

  EXAMPLES OF WEIRD EXCEPTIONALISM:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Visual perception: Muller-Lyer illusion (two lines     │
  │ with inward vs. outward arrowheads — which is longer?) │
  │ Americans: strong illusion. San foragers: almost       │
  │ none. Carpentered corners environment effect.          │
  │                                                        │
  │ Fairness: Ultimatum game — Western players offer       │
  │ ~50% and reject low offers. Machiguenga (Peru)         │
  │ offer ~26%, accept anything. Lamalera (Indonesia)      │
  │ offer >50% (hyperfair — tied to whale-hunting          │
  │ sharing norms). No universal "fairness module."        │
  │                                                        │
  │ Analytical vs. holistic cognition: WEIRD subjects      │
  │ tend to categorize by attributes (both triangles        │
  │ go together). East Asian subjects tend to categorize   │
  │ by relationship (cow goes with grass, not with horse). │
  │                                                        │
  │ Impersonal trust: WEIRD people extend trust to         │
  │ strangers and institutions (anonymous market). Most    │
  │ of the world is kin-based and in-group trust only.     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Henrich's "WEIRDest People in the World" (2020 book):
  The psychology that Western social science treats as
  universal was largely created by the Western Church's
  medieval campaign against cousin marriage — which
  destroyed kin networks, created impersonal institutions,
  and shaped the analytic, impersonal, fairness-oriented
  psychology that shows up in Western psych experiments.
```

### Implications for Product Design (Direct Bridge)

```
  FOR ANY GLOBAL PRODUCT ORGANIZATION:

  If your product is designed and tested by WEIRD engineers
  with WEIRD user researchers using WEIRD early adopters —
  and then shipped globally — you have systematically encoded
  WEIRD psychology into the product.

  STRUCTURAL EXAMPLES (apply to any global product org):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Individualistic account model: Western norm is one     │
  │ person = one account. Many global cultures share       │
  │ devices within families. Product assumes individual    │
  │ ownership — fails in shared-device markets.            │
  │                                                        │
  │ Direct/explicit communication defaults: collaboration  │
  │ tools (Slack, Teams, chat) are built for low-context   │
  │ communication styles. High-context cultures (Japan,    │
  │ Korea, many others) use indirect communication —       │
  │ the software feels culturally abrasive or forces       │
  │ inappropriate directness.                              │
  │                                                        │
  │ Trust model: Onboarding assumes trust in institutions  │
  │ (company, product). Markets where institutional trust  │
  │ is low require different trust-building flows.         │
  │ Payment flows differ for same reason.                  │
  │                                                        │
  │ Hierarchy representation: flat org-chart tools and     │
  │ "anyone can message anyone" communication defaults     │
  │ are built for low power-distance cultures. High        │
  │ power-distance cultures (most of the world) need       │
  │ different information architecture.                    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  This is not "cultural sensitivity" — it is product
  architecture informed by actual variation in human
  psychology documented across cultures. The WEIRD problem
  is a product quality issue, not a values issue.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Kin selection and group selection are opposed theories."**
They were framed as competing, but Price's equation (1970) shows they are
mathematically equivalent under specific parameter mappings. The debate is really
about which framing is more empirically useful and what conditions actually hold
in nature. Multi-level selection is now standard in formal treatments.

**"The selfish gene means humans are fundamentally selfish."**
Dawkins' "selfish gene" is about the level of selection (genes as units of
selection), not about human psychology. Genes that code for altruism can be
"selfish" — they propagate by benefiting copies of themselves in relatives.
The model predicts altruism, not selfishness, in many conditions.

**"Evolutionary psychology proves gender differences are innate."**
EP studies on sex differences are among the most contested in the field. The
claimed sex differences in spatial cognition, aggression, and mating preferences
are real in some datasets but often small, overlapping, and inconsistent across
cultures. The "massive sex difference" narrative is driven by a combination of
publication bias, WEIRD samples, and the naturalistic fallacy demand.

**"Hamilton's rule explains all human cooperation."**
Hunter-gatherer food sharing routinely violates Hamilton's rule predictions —
food is shared with distantly related or unrelated individuals with no clear
reciprocation tracking. Strong reciprocity, reputation effects, and cultural
norms extend cooperation beyond what r × B > C can explain.

**"The grandmother hypothesis is proven."**
It has consistent cross-cultural support in demographic data, but the causal
mechanisms are not definitively established. The simulation models are
plausible, not verified. Alternative life history stories (patriarch model,
reserve model) have not been ruled out. It is the best-supported hypothesis
for menopause, but not a closed case.
