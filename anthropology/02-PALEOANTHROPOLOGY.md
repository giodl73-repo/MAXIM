This is our evolutionary story as reconstructed from fossils, stone tools,
and ancient DNA. Every node on this tree represents decades of fieldwork and
laboratory analysis — and remains contested at multiple points.

## Section 1: The Earliest Hominins (7-4 Ma)

### What Makes a Hominin?

```
  HOMININ: any member of the human clade after the split from
  chimpanzees (~7-6 Ma based on molecular clocks)

  Distinguishing features (none alone is definitive):
  - Bipedal locomotion (primary marker)
  - Reduced canine teeth (relative to other apes)
  - Post-orbital narrowing
  - Foramen magnum position (under skull, not at back)

  Note: bipedalism predates large brains by ~3 million years.
  The big brain is late; upright walking is the founding adaptation.
```

### Sahelanthropus tchadensis (Toumai, ~7 Ma)

```
  Site: Djurab Desert, Chad (2001, Brunet team)
  Material: cranium, fragmentary jaw and teeth

  Significance:
  - Oldest potential hominin: 6-7 Ma (near the chimp-human split)
  - Small brain (~360 cc — chimp-sized)
  - Reduced canines vs. other apes
  - Foramen magnum position suggestive of bipedalism
    (but no post-cranial material to confirm)

  Controversy: Some researchers argue it is an ape ancestor,
  not a hominin. The foramen magnum claim has been disputed.
  A femur found at the same site (unpublished for 20 years,
  published 2022) shows possible quadrupedal features —
  the bipedalism claim remains contested.
```

### Ardipithecus ramidus (Ardi, 4.4 Ma)

```
  Site: Middle Awash, Ethiopia (White team, field since 1994,
  published 2009 after 15 years of preparation)
  Material: 45% complete skeleton (ARA-VP-6/500, "Ardi")

  The mosaic anatomy:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ BIPEDAL FEATURES:                                      │
  │ - Reduced big toe divergence vs. other apes            │
  │ - Pelvis intermediate between chimp and Australopithecus│
  │ - Foramen magnum position more ventral                 │
  │                                                        │
  │ ARBOREAL FEATURES:                                     │
  │ - Grasping big toe retained (arboreal)                 │
  │ - Long arms for above-branch movement                  │
  │ - Large, projecting canines reduced but still present  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Interpretation: Ardi could walk bipedally on the ground
  AND move through trees. This is NOT a knuckle-walker.
  This overturns the assumption that our ancestor looked
  like a chimpanzee. Chimps are DERIVED, not ancestral.
  The common ancestor of humans and chimps may have looked
  more like Ardi than like a modern chimp.
```

## Section 2: Australopithecus and the Bipedal Radiation (4-2 Ma)

### Australopithecus afarensis — Lucy and Laetoli

```
  Site: Hadar, Ethiopia (Lucy, 1974, Johanson team)
        Laetoli, Tanzania (footprints, 1976, Mary Leakey team)
  Age:  3.7-3.0 Ma
  Brain: ~400-450 cc (slightly larger than chimp)

  THE LAETOLI FOOTPRINTS: 3.66 Ma volcanic ash preserved
  the footprints of at least 3 individuals walking bipedally.
  The gait is fully bipedal — no push-off from big toe typical
  of ape walking. The arch is present. This is the smoking gun
  for obligate bipedalism in A. afarensis.

  LUCY (AL-288-1): 40% complete skeleton
  - ~1.1 m tall, ~29 kg
  - Fully bipedal locomotion confirmed from lower limbs
  - Retained curved finger bones (arboreal remnant?
    or just phylogenetic baggage?)
  - Small brain: 400 cc — intelligence is not driving bipedalism
  - Sexual dimorphism: males ~50% larger than females
    (similar to gorillas — suggests polygynous or competitive mating)
```

### The Robust Australopiths — Paranthropus

```
  THREE SPECIES:
  P. aethiopicus (~2.7-2.3 Ma) — East Africa
  P. boisei     (~2.3-1.3 Ma) — East Africa [Nutcracker Man]
  P. robustus   (~2.0-1.2 Ma) — South Africa

  DEFINING FEATURES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Sagittal crest: bony ridge on top of skull for         │
  │ attachment of massive temporalis (jaw-closing) muscles │
  │                                                        │
  │ Flared zygomatic arches (cheekbones): room for big     │
  │ muscles to pass underneath                             │
  │                                                        │
  │ Megadontia: very large premolars and molars with       │
  │ thick enamel — for hard, gritty foods (seeds, tubers,  │
  │ roots)                                                 │
  │                                                        │
  │ Small frontal teeth: canines and incisors not enlarged │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ISOTOPE EVIDENCE: Carbon isotope ratios (d13C) in Paranthropus
  teeth suggest a C4-dominated diet — grasses, sedges, or animals
  that ate grasses. Not the forest fruits initially assumed.

  SIGNIFICANCE: A highly specialized dietary adaptation.
  Paranthropus persisted for over 1 million years — they were
  successful. But they left no descendants. The heavily derived
  masticatory apparatus seems to be an evolutionary dead end.
  Homo, with a more generalist diet and expanding brain, was
  the other option.
```

## Section 3: The Genus Homo — Brains and Tools

### Early Homo and the Oldowan (~2.6-1.7 Ma)

```
  Homo habilis ("handy man") — Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania
  Brain: ~600-650 cc
  Age:   2.4-1.4 Ma

  OLDOWAN TOOLS (first technology, ~2.6 Ma):
  - Earliest at Ledi-Geraru, Ethiopia (2.8 Ma) and
    Lomekwi, Kenya (3.3 Ma — contested, predates Homo)
  - Simple percussion: strike one rock (hammerstone) against
    another (core) to remove a sharp flake
  - The flakes are the tools; cores are secondary
  - Requires cognitive spatial modeling of fracture mechanics
  - Not trivial: attempts to replicate by non-specialists fail

  Note: Stone tools at Lomekwi (3.3 Ma) predate Homo habilis.
  This means either Australopithecus made tools (possible —
  chimps use stone tools at Bossou), or there is an
  undiscovered early Homo lineage.
```

### Homo erectus — The First Global Hominin (1.9 Ma - 100 ka)

```
  GEOGRAPHIC RANGE:
  Africa (ergaster) -> Georgia (Dmanisi, 1.85 Ma) ->
  Java (1.8 Ma) -> China (Peking Man, 0.8 Ma) ->
  Flores (H. floresiensis — insular dwarf, 100 ka)

  This is "Out of Africa 1" — the first hominin dispersal
  from Africa. Occurs without the large brain associated
  with later modern humans.

  MORPHOLOGY:
  - Brain: 750-1100 cc (significant increase over Australopithecus)
  - Tall, long-legged (modern human body proportions)
  - Projecting brow ridges, no chin, low forehead
  - Thick skull walls

  TECHNOLOGY — THE ACHEULEAN HANDAXE:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ The Acheulean handaxe (~1.7 Ma - 200 ka):             │
  │                                                        │
  │ Bifacially worked (flaked on both faces) to a         │
  │ symmetrical tear-drop shape. The shape is imposed     │
  │ on the stone — a MENTAL TEMPLATE exists before        │
  │ the knapping begins.                                   │
  │                                                        │
  │ Used for ~1.5 MILLION YEARS with almost no change.   │
  │ Called "the most successful technology in human       │
  │ history." Very likely multipurpose: butchery,         │
  │ digging, processing plant material.                   │
  │                                                        │
  │ Why so static? No cumulative cultural evolution.     │
  │ Each individual learns the template; innovations      │
  │ don't accumulate. Compare to post-50ka Upper          │
  │ Paleolithic — explosive diversity of tool types.      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FIRE:
  - Best controlled evidence: Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa ~1 Ma
  - Bnot Yaakov Bridge, Israel ~780 ka (plant remains, bone)
  - Earlier claims (Swartkrans ~1.4 Ma) less certain
  - Richard Wrangham's COOKING HYPOTHESIS: controlled fire +
    cooking enabled higher caloric extraction, drove brain growth
    and gut reduction (human gut is small for body size compared
    to other primates — the "expensive tissue" tradeoff)

  DMANISI (Georgia, 1.85 Ma):
  Five skulls from the same site showing enormous variation —
  one is small-brained (~546 cc), one is large (727 cc).
  They may all be the same species. If so: what we call
  "habilis" and "early erectus" may be one species with
  high variability. This has implications for how many Homo
  species we recognize.
```

## Section 4: Archaic Humans and the Neanderthal Chapter

### Homo heidelbergensis (~700-200 ka)

```
  Distribution: Africa and Europe (some include Asian fossils)
  Brain: 1100-1400 cc — approaching modern human range

  KEY BEHAVIORS:
  - Levallois technique: prepared core method. Shape a core
    deliberately so that a single strike removes a pre-formed
    flake of predictable shape. Forward planning, 2-3 steps ahead.
  - Evidence of systematic big-game hunting: Schöningen spears
    (~300 ka Germany) — 8 wooden throwing spears found with
    butchered horse bones. Hunting, not scavenging.
  - Huts at Terra Amata, Nice (~400 ka) — debated but possible
    structure building.

  Likely ancestral to both Neanderthals (in Europe/W. Asia)
  and anatomically modern humans (in Africa).
```

### Neanderthals (400,000 - 40,000 years ago)

```
  MORPHOLOGY:
  - Brain: ~1200-1750 cc (LARGER than modern human average)
  - Cold-adapted: stocky body (Bergmann's rule), large nose
    (warm/humidify cold air), projecting mid-face
  - Enormous front teeth with heavy wear — possibly used as
    a "third hand" for gripping materials while working

  TECHNOLOGY — MOUSTERIAN:
  - Levallois technology plus scrapers, notches, points
  - Regional and temporal variation (Neanderthals ≠ static)
  - Hafted tools: stone points attached to wooden handles —
    requires adhesives, planning, conceptual binding of parts

  SYMBOLIC BEHAVIOR:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Ochre use: pigment processing at multiple sites        │
  │ Eagle feathers and talons: Krapina (Croatia) — cut     │
  │   marks on eagle talons suggest ornament use           │
  │ Shell pigment containers: Murcia, Spain ~50 ka         │
  │   (pre-modern human arrival in region)                 │
  │ Cave art at La Pasiega, Ardales (Spain) ~65 ka:        │
  │   U-series dating predates modern human arrival        │
  │   by 20,000 years. Controversial but significant.      │
  │ Burials: Shanidar Cave (Iraq) — disputed "flower       │
  │   burial." Simple interment is confirmed at some sites.|
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  EXTINCTION: ~40,000 years ago, after modern humans arrived
  in Europe. Causes: competition, climate, disease, assimilation?
  Probably all contributing. Not a simple replacement.

  INTERBREEDING (Svante Paabo's lab, Nobel Prize 2022):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 2010: First draft Neanderthal genome (3 specimens,     │
  │       Vindija Cave, Croatia)                           │
  │                                                        │
  │ Finding: Non-African modern humans carry 1-4% of      │
  │ Neanderthal DNA. Interbreeding occurred when modern    │
  │ humans left Africa and encountered Neanderthals in     │
  │ the Middle East (~60-50 ka).                           │
  │                                                        │
  │ Functional effects: some Neanderthal variants affect   │
  │ immune function (HLA region), skin/hair characteristics,│
  │ and potentially COVID-19 susceptibility (both risk and │
  │ protective alleles identified in different populations) │
  │                                                        │
  │ Africans: no Neanderthal ancestry (Neanderthals never  │
  │ lived in Africa)                                       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Denisovans — The Ghost Lineage

```
  KNOWN ONLY FROM DNA — almost no physical description possible

  MATERIAL:
  - A finger bone (Denisova Cave, Siberia, 2008)
  - A molar (same cave)
  - A partial skull (Xiahe, Tibet — confirmed Denisovan 2019)
  - One bone fragment from the same individual as DNA

  THE DNA STORY:
  - Genome sequenced from the finger bone (Paabo lab, 2010)
  - Diverged from Neanderthals ~400 ka
  - Closely related to Neanderthals but genetically distinct
  - Inhabited East/Southeast Asia (possibly very wide range)

  INTROGRESSION — MODERN HUMAN DESCENDANTS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Tibetans: EPAS1 variant enabling high-altitude         │
  │ adaptation (efficient O2 use) came from Denisovans.    │
  │ Adaptive introgression — the variant gave Tibetan      │
  │ ancestors survival advantage on the plateau.           │
  │                                                        │
  │ Melanesians/Papua New Guineans: ~5% Denisovan DNA      │
  │ (highest of any modern population)                     │
  │                                                        │
  │ Southeast Asian populations: 0.5-2% Denisovan         │
  │ depending on group. Aboriginal Australians have        │
  │ significant Denisovan admixture.                       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  The Xiahe mandible: a Denisovan from 160 ka on the Tibetan
  Plateau at 3,280 m elevation. Denisovans occupied high
  altitude environments long before modern humans — the
  EPAS1 adaptation was presumably already present.
```

## Section 5: Modern Homo sapiens — Origins and Dispersal

### African Origins — The Sites

```
  ANATOMICALLY MODERN HUMANS (AMH): skull morphology like us.
  BEHAVIORALLY MODERN HUMANS: symbolic behavior, complex technology.
  These two do NOT appear simultaneously.

  KEY AFRICAN SITES:
  +------------------+----------+-------------------------------+
  | Site             | Age      | Significance                  |
  +------------------+----------+-------------------------------+
  | Jebel Irhoud,    | 315 ka   | Oldest known AMH fossils.    |

  +------------------+----------+-------------------------------+

  |                  |          | beads, ochre processing kits  |
  +------------------+----------+-------------------------------+
```

### Out of Africa 2 — The Dispersal (~70-60 ka)

```
  QUESTION: When did modern humans leave Africa?

  SOUTHERN COASTAL ROUTE (dominant model):
  Africa -> Arabian Peninsula (now mostly desert, was greener
  during humid periods) -> South Asia -> Southeast Asia ->
  Sahul (Australia/New Guinea, by 65 ka, sea crossing required)
  -> East Asia -> Siberia -> Americas (~20-15 ka)

  MULTIPLE DISPERSALS:
  Earlier dispersals are documented (Apidima Cave skulls in
  Greece ~210 ka; Misliya Cave, Israel ~180 ka) but these
  likely did not contribute to the main modern human lineage
  (or contributed minimally). The primary successful Out-of-
  Africa was ~60-70 ka.

  Genetic evidence: non-African populations show less genetic
  diversity than African populations (founder effect at the
  bottleneck when a small founding population left Africa).

  THE ADMIXTURE PICTURE:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Modern humans exiting Africa encountered:               │
  │                                                         │
  │ Middle East: Neanderthals -> admixture ~60-50 ka        │
  │ Asia:        Denisovans   -> admixture, timing unclear  │
  │              (possibly multiple events)                 │
  │ Possibly:    other "ghost" lineages (DNA signals of     │
  │              unidentified archaic populations in        │
  │              West/Central African populations, and      │
  │              in some Asian populations)                 │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Modern humans are not "pure." We are a mosaic of lineages
  that merged, diverged, and merged again across 300,000 years.
```

### Behavioral Modernity — When and Why?

```
  THE DEBATE:
  "Human Revolution" model (Mellars, Klein):
  - Sudden explosion of symbolic behavior ~45-50 ka in Europe
  - Cave art (Lascaux ~17 ka, Chauvet ~36 ka, Sulawesi ~45 ka)
  - Blade tools, figurines, long-distance exchange
  - Possibly tied to language capacity becoming modern

  "Gradual emergence" model (McBrearty and Brooks 2000):
  - All the components of "modernity" appear earlier in Africa:
    ochre at 300 ka, beads at 120 ka, engraving at 75 ka
  - They don't appear simultaneously; they appear, disappear,
    reappear — context-dependent use, not cognitive threshold
  - The "revolution" in Europe is an artifact of better-preserved
    sites and more archaeologists per square kilometer

  Current consensus: gradual accumulation of capacities in Africa,
  not a single cognitive event. European cave art is brilliant but
  not the debut of symbolic thought — it's a later elaboration.

  WHY: The leading hypothesis is population size and density.
  When populations are small and isolated, knowledge accumulates
  slowly and is often lost. When populations connect (via trade
  networks, migration), innovations propagate and compound.
  The "creative explosion" correlates with increased population
  connectivity, not increased brain size.
```

## Section 6: Stone Tool Technology — The Sequence

```
  OLDOWAN (~3.3/2.6 Ma - 1.7 Ma):
  Simple percussion flaking. Strike hammerstone to core.
  Flakes and cores as tools. Produced by H. habilis (and possibly
  Australopithecus based on Lomekwi evidence).

  ACHEULEAN (~1.7 Ma - 200 ka):
  Bifacial reduction — shape both faces of a core to a
  template. Handaxes, cleavers. Produced by H. erectus and
  H. heidelbergensis. Used for 1.5 million years unchanged.

  MOUSTERIAN / MIDDLE PALEOLITHIC (~300-30 ka):
  Levallois prepared core technique. Scrapers, notches, points.
  Hafting. Produced by Neanderthals (and early modern humans
  in Africa simultaneously — parallel development).

  UPPER PALEOLITHIC / LATER STONE AGE (~45-12 ka):
  Blade technology: long, thin, parallel-sided flakes from
  specialized prismatic cores. Microliths. Bone/antler/ivory
  tools. Projectile points. Produced by modern humans.
  Rapid regional variation — cultural styles proliferate.

  MESOLITHIC / MICROLITHIC (~12-8 ka):
  Tiny stone tools (microliths) set in composite hafts.
  Post-Pleistocene adaptation to changed environments.

  NEOLITHIC ("New Stone Age", ~10 ka - Bronze Age):
  Ground and polished stone tools. Not necessarily better
  for cutting, but suited for woodworking in agricultural
  societies. Replaced by metal eventually.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Humans evolved from chimpanzees."**
No. Humans and chimpanzees share a common ancestor (~7 Ma). Both lineages have
evolved since then. Modern chimps are as derived from that common ancestor as
we are — in different directions.

**"Neanderthals were dumb brutes."**
Their brains were larger than ours on average. They made hafted tools, used
ochre, possibly made cave art, buried their dead, and lived successfully in
Europe's Ice Age for 400,000 years. The "brute" image comes from a mistaken
reconstruction of La Chapelle-aux-Saints individual (a pathologically arthritic
old male, taken as typical). Boule's 1911 reconstruction set popular culture
back 50 years.

**"The Acheulean handaxe proves H. erectus was intelligent."**
The handaxe requires spatial modeling of 3D fracture mechanics and a mental
template — real cognitive demand. But 1.5 million years of near-identical tools
suggests limited cumulative cultural evolution. Intelligence is present; the
social transmission of innovations across generations (cumulative culture) appears
later.

**"Australopithecus was an ancestor of modern humans."**
A. afarensis is probably ancestral to Homo, but paleoanthropology almost never
has single-ancestor certainty. The fossil record is fragmentary. Any particular
specimen could be an ancestor, a collateral relative, or a dead-end side branch.
What we have is a nested pattern of shared derived characters (cladogram), not
a verified genealogical record.

**"Behavioral modernity = the Upper Paleolithic cave art revolution."**
This Euro-centric view was standard until McBrearty and Brooks (2000) documented
the full African MSA record. The "revolution" is partly a visibility artifact —
European Pleistocene caves are extraordinarily well-preserved and densely
researched. Sub-Saharan African sites are rarer, more acidic soils destroy
organics, and fewer archaeologists work there.
