## Section 1: Ethnography — The Method

### What Participant Observation Actually Is

```
  NOT: visiting a community for a few days and interviewing people
  NOT: distributing surveys to community members
  NOT: reading documents about a community
  NOT: observing from a distance as a neutral observer

  IS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Prolonged engagement (typically 12+ months)         │
  │    Living in the community, not visiting it            │
  │                                                        │
  │ 2. Language competence                                 │
  │    Not through interpreters; direct access to          │
  │    ordinary speech, jokes, arguments, casual talk      │
  │                                                        │
  │ 3. Participation                                       │
  │    Actually doing the activities: fishing, farming,    │
  │    ritual performance, market trading                  │
  │    Competence cannot be faked; doing reveals things    │
  │    that watching does not                              │
  │                                                        │
  │ 4. Systematic fieldnotes                               │
  │    Written the same day or night. Detailed. Observable │
  │    behavior + context + interpretation separately      │
  │    marked. Sensory details. Quotes verbatim.           │
  │                                                        │
  │ 5. Multiple methods layered                            │
  │    Observation + interview + genealogy + mapping +     │
  │    artifact collection + life histories                │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE HERMENEUTIC CIRCLE in fieldwork:
  You begin with no context. Your first observations are
  meaningless. As you learn context, earlier observations
  gain meaning. That meaning informs what you look for next.
  Understanding builds iteratively — you can never stand
  fully outside the system you're observing.
```

### The Observer Effect and Reflexivity

```
  HAWTHORNE EFFECT: being observed changes behavior.
  In fieldwork: more insidious because long-term. Over time,
  the researcher becomes a known, categorized person in the
  community's social structure. The community accommodates.

  REFLEXIVITY: the ethnographer must account for:
  - Their own social position (race, gender, class, nationality)
    and how it shapes access and what people tell them
  - The "front stage" vs. "back stage" distinction (Goffman):
    what people perform for outsiders vs. what they do
    when the researcher is not watching
  - The researcher's own assumptions brought from their culture
    (cannot be eliminated, must be examined)

  NOT going native: losing analytical distance by over-
  identifying with the community. The ethnographer should
  maintain dual consciousness: participating AND analyzing.

  The "Writing Culture" (Clifford and Marcus 1986) crisis:
  who has the authority to represent another culture?
  The ethnographic text is authored by the outsider, in the
  outsider's language, for outsider audiences, citing the
  insider as "data" not as co-author. This power relationship
  is now standard to address explicitly.
```

## Section 2: The Culture Concept

### Definitions Through the Decades

```
  TYLOR (1871): "Culture is that complex whole which includes
  knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other
  capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society."
  ─> Culture as a list of traits; can be ranked evolutionarily.

  BOAS: Culture is historical particularity — each culture is
  the product of its own history, environment, and contacts.
  Cannot be ranked. Must be understood from within.
  ─> Anti-evolutionary; comparative but non-hierarchical.

  BENEDICT (1934): Culture has a "pattern" or "configuration" —
  an integrated style. Zuni = Apollonian (restrained, cooperative,
  ceremony). Kwakiutl = Dionysian (ecstatic, competitive, display).
  ─> Culture as personality writ large. Later critiqued for
     homogenizing, ignoring internal conflict and variation.

  GEERTZ (1973): "Culture is the webs of significance [the human]
  has himself spun... analysis of it is not an experimental
  science in search of law but an interpretive one in search
  of meaning."
  ─> Culture as shared meaning system. Not in heads — in public
     symbols, performances, texts. Anthropologist reads culture
     like a text.

  BOURDIEU (1977): Culture is not a system shared by all but an
  arena of contest. The dominant culture is naturalized as
  "taste" or "common sense." Habitus perpetuates hierarchy by
  making the arbitrary seem natural.
  ─> Culture as contested terrain; power as central.

  CONTEMPORARY: Culture as neither fully shared nor fully
  contested — some overlap enables communication; some
  disagreement is productive. Dynamic, not static.
  Multiple cultures coexist within any community.
```

### Thick Description — Geertz's Method

```
  From "The Interpretation of Cultures" (1973):

  "The concept of culture I espouse is essentially a semiotic
  one... [the job of ethnography is] thick description."

  RYLE'S EXAMPLE (borrowed by Geertz):
  A boy rapidly contracts his eyelid.

  THIN DESCRIPTION: "He winked."

  THICK DESCRIPTION: "He contracted his right eyelid rapidly —
  not a twitch (involuntary spasm), not a mock-wink (rehearsing
  a wink for an imitation), not a parody of a wink, but a
  conspiratorial wink signaling to his accomplice that the
  approach they agreed on should begin. It required cultural
  competence (knowledge that such a wink convention exists),
  social context (the accomplice, the innocent bystander),
  and intentionality (communicating, not twitching)."

  THE BALINESE COCKFIGHT:
  On the surface: gambling event.

  Thick description reveals it as: a "story Balinese tell
  themselves about themselves." The cock is identified with
  its owner's masculinity, status, and fate. High-stakes
  betting is an enactment of the social hierarchy — prestige
  wagering by status equals against each other. The cockfight
  is a "metasocial commentary" on status, masculinity, and
  the Balinese experience of time and fate.

  You cannot derive this from the observable behavior alone.
  You need language competence + months of context + the
  ability to connect the cockfight to Balinese cosmology.
```

## Section 3: Kinship Systems

### Descent Systems

```
  PATRILINEAL: trace descent through fathers.
  Group membership, property, identity pass father -> child.
  Common in many African, Asian, Middle Eastern societies.

  MATRILINEAL: trace descent through mothers.
  ~15% of world's societies. NOT matriarchal (political
  authority is usually still male — through mother's brother).
  Examples: Navajo, Hopi, Minangkabau (Sumatra), Khasi (India),
  many West African groups, Iroquois nations.

  BILATERAL: trace descent through both parents equally.
  Each person belongs to both parents' families.
  Common in Western Europe, foraging societies, Southeast Asia.

  DOUBLE DESCENT: patrilineal AND matrilineal groups coexist,
  used for different purposes (e.g., patrilineal for land
  inheritance, matrilineal for ritual objects). Rare.

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ WHY IT MATTERS:                                        │
  │ Descent determines: group membership, inheritance,     │
  │ marriage eligibility (who is "too close"), residence   │
  │ after marriage, ritual obligations, political identity │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Kin Terminology Systems (Morgan 1871)

```
  Six basic systems for categorizing relatives:

  LINEAL (Eskimo/Inuit):
  Distinguishes lineal kin (parents, siblings, children) from
  collateral (aunts/uncles/cousins). Your "mother" is only your
  mother; your "aunt" is clearly different.
  Common in industrial/Western societies and bilateral foragers.
  Reflects: relatively weak extended kin groups, nuclear family
  is the salient unit.

  IROQUOIS:
  Parallel cousins (father's brother's children; mother's
  sister's children) are called by same terms as siblings.
  Cross-cousins (father's sister's children; mother's
  brother's children) have separate terms.
  Correlated with: unilineal descent; cross-cousin marriage
  preferred (the cross-cousin is in a different lineage).

  CROW (matrilineal variant of Iroquois):
  Father's sister's line is "merged" regardless of generation.
  Your father's sister's son is called by same term as father.
  Reflects: matrilineal principle — your father's matrilineage
  is a unified category across generations.

  OMAHA (patrilineal variant):
  Mother's brother's line is "merged" regardless of generation.
  Your mother's brother's son = same term as mother's brother.
  Reflects: patrilineal principle — your mother's patrilineage
  is unified across generations.

  The terminology encodes the kinship logic of the society —
  it is not arbitrary; it reflects who counts as equivalent
  for purposes of inheritance, marriage, and obligation.
```

### Marriage Rules and Exchange

```
  EXOGAMY: must marry outside your group (lineage, clan, village).
  Rationale: creates alliances between groups; incest avoidance.

  ENDOGAMY: must marry within your group (caste, ethnic group,
  religion, tribe).
  Rationale: maintains group boundaries, keeps property within.

  BRIDEWEALTH (brideprice): groom's family pays wife's family.
  NOT purchase of a wife — compensation to her family for
  loss of her productive and reproductive labor. Creates
  alliance obligation. Common in sub-Saharan Africa.

  DOWRY: bride's family pays groom's family.
  Common in South/East Asia, historically Europe.
  Associated with hypergamy (marrying up in status) —
  dowry compensates groom's family for lower-status bride.
  High dowry societies correlate with female infanticide
  (the evolutionary logic is contested).

  BRIDEPRICE vs. DOWRY DISTRIBUTION:
  Not random — correlates with women's economic contribution
  and labor patterns. High female labor contribution (hoe
  agriculture, herding) -> bridewealth. Low contribution ->
  dowry. Boserup's original analysis, confirmed in cross-
  cultural samples.
```

## Section 4: Exchange Theory

### Mauss's "The Gift" (1925)

```
  CLAIM: In archaic societies, exchange is never "free."
  Every gift carries three obligations:
  1. Obligation to GIVE
  2. Obligation to RECEIVE (refusal is an insult/hostile act)
  3. Obligation to RECIPROCATE (delay is acceptable;
                                 failure destroys the relationship)

  THE HAU (Maori concept):
  The gift retains a spiritual connection to the giver.
  The hau of the gift demands it return to its origin.
  The gift is never fully detached from the person.

  This contrasts with market exchange: a commodity sale
  completely severs the object from the seller.
  The gift maintains relational threads; the commodity severs them.

  INALIENABLE POSSESSIONS (Weiner 1992):
  Extension of Mauss. Some objects are "kept while given" —
  they circulate but the giver retains a claim on them.
  Maori cloaks, Samoan fine mats — circulated as "gifts" but
  the original lineage retains symbolic attachment. This is
  how high-prestige items maintain long-term alliance networks.

  MODERN RELEVANCE:
  - Corporate gifting norms: the obligatory Christmas gift
    to clients/partners is reciprocal exchange, not charity
  - Open source software contributions: not purely altruistic;
    create reputation, obligation, social capital
  - Peer code review: creating debt and social capital in
    engineering teams, governed by informal gift exchange logic
```

### The Kula Ring — Malinowski's Masterpiece

```
  TROBRIAND ISLANDS, PAPUA NEW GUINEA:

  Kula ring: inter-island exchange system connecting ~18
  island groups over ~500 km of ocean.

  Two types of objects circulate in opposite directions:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Mwali (arm shells): travel COUNTERCLOCKWISE            │
  │ Soulava (necklaces): travel CLOCKWISE                  │
  │                                                        │
  │ Rules:                                                 │
  │ - Objects always move in their direction; never reverse│
  │ - Never traded for each other directly                 │
  │ - Must always be in motion — holding too long is shame │
  │ - The kula partner relationship is lifelong            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE POINT: These objects have no utilitarian value.
  No one uses an armshell or wears a necklace daily.
  Their value is entirely relational — they carry histories
  of partnerships, events, famous exchanges.

  EMBEDDED TRADE: the actual practical trade of utilitarian
  goods (canoes, pottery, food) is called gimwali and occurs
  alongside kula but is NEVER confused with it. Mixing the
  two is shameful. The kula creates the alliance network
  within which practical exchange can happen safely.

  ANTHROPOLOGICAL LESSON: Economic exchange is embedded in
  social relations — it is never purely "rational market
  behavior." The kula ring demonstrates this most vividly.
```

### The Potlatch — Competitive Giving

```
  PACIFIC NORTHWEST COAST (Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida, Tlingit,
  Tsimshian — Boas's primary fieldwork area):

  Potlatch: ceremonial redistribution of wealth by a chief or
  family, given to guests as display of status and obligation.
  At major potlatches: canoes, blankets, and other goods
  distributed to hundreds of guests.

  STATUS THROUGH GENEROSITY:
  In market economies: status through accumulation.
  In potlatch: status through giving away.
  The chief who gives the most demonstrates the greatest
  productive capacity and the most extensive network.
  Guests are obligated to reciprocate (at their own potlatch)
  or be shamed.

  Extreme form (contested by some anthropologists): the
  "destructive potlatch" — burning or destroying goods to
  demonstrate such superabundant wealth that you have more
  than you can give away.

  COLONIAL PERIOD: Canadian government banned potlatch 1885-1951
  as "wasteful" and "uncivilized." This demonstrates the
  ethnocentrism of projecting market economy values onto
  a prestige economy — from the inside, the potlatch is
  highly rational given its social objectives.

  BOURDIEU CONNECTION: the potlatch converts economic capital
  (goods) into symbolic capital (prestige/honor) — which then
  translates to political capital (obligation from recipients).
  This conversion between capital forms is the Bourdieusian
  mechanism at scale.
```

## Section 5: Ritual

### Van Gennep — Rites of Passage (1909)

```
  ALL RITES OF PASSAGE HAVE THREE PHASES:

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                          │
  │  SEPARATION        LIMINALITY          INCORPORATION     │
  │  ----------        ----------          -------------     │
  │  Individual        Threshold state:    Reintegration     │
  │  removed from      "betwixt and        into society      │
  │  prior social      between." No        in new status.    │
  │  position.         longer X, not       New identity      │
  │                    yet Y.              acknowledged.     │
  │                                                          │
  │  Examples:                                               │
  │  Birth:     Infant   -> unbaptized/nameless -> full      │
  │             separate   member of society               │
  │  Initiation: Child  -> seclusion/ordeal    -> adult      │
  │  Marriage:   Single -> betrothal period    -> married    │
  │  Death:      Living -> funeral rites       -> ancestor   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  The rite is not simply marking a change — it EFFECTS the change.
  The person who goes through it is not the same person on the
  other side. Social reality is constituted by ritual performance.
```

### Turner — Communitas and Anti-Structure

```
  VICTOR TURNER (1969) extends Van Gennep:

  LIMINALITY creates COMMUNITAS:
  During the liminal phase, normal social distinctions are
  suspended. The rich and poor, high-caste and low-caste,
  adult and child are equal. This temporary equality is
  intensely felt and remembered. Turner calls this communitas —
  a social bond that transcends structural categories.

  Examples:
  - Military boot camp: all recruits equal before the drill
    sergeant; intense bonding; "we" vs. the institution
  - Carnival/festival: hierarchy inverted (king fools, fool
    kings); temporary license; intense solidarity
  - Religious pilgrimage (Turner studied Ndembu, also
    pilgrimage to Guadalupe): pilgrims from different classes
    travel together, share hardship, become bonded
  - Liminal states in illness, mourning, adolescence —
    all share anti-structural features

  SOCIAL DRAMA:
  Breach -> Crisis -> Redressive action -> Reintegration
  or schism. Turner's model of social conflict as theatrical —
  communities resolve contradictions through performance.
  This connects to organizational "turnaround" narratives
  in corporate contexts.

  STRUCTURE vs. ANTI-STRUCTURE:
  Structure = normal hierarchy, roles, rules, obligations.
  Anti-structure = communitas, equality, ambiguity.
  Healthy societies need BOTH: structure for coordination,
  communitas for solidarity and renewal.

  Parallels: Hackathons, offsites, team retreats — deliberately
  creating liminal space within organizational structure to
  generate communitas and lateral bonds across hierarchy.
```

### Durkheim — The Sacred and Profane

```
  DURKHEIM (1912): "The Elementary Forms of Religious Life"

  All religion divides reality into:
  SACRED: set apart, forbidden, surrounded by prohibition
          and ritual protection — the totem, the altar,
          the holy book, the flag
  PROFANE: ordinary, everyday, instrumental, routine

  THE SOCIAL FUNCTION:
  Ritual is social glue. When Australians perform the
  corroboree (collective ritual), they are not actually
  worshipping the totem — they are worshipping SOCIETY ITSELF,
  which the totem symbolizes. God is society experienced
  as transcendent.

  COLLECTIVE EFFERVESCENCE:
  The feeling of being lifted outside oneself in collective
  ritual — crowd excitement, shared ecstasy, loss of individual
  boundary. This experience IS the religious experience, and it
  is real (as psychological phenomenon) even if its stated
  referent (the divine) is a social construction.

  Relevance: sports events, concerts, political rallies,
  product launch events — all generate Durkheimian collective
  effervescence. The "culture" in company culture is generated
  and maintained through such collective ritual moments.
```

## Section 6: Gender in Cross-Cultural Perspective

```
  MEAD'S CONTRIBUTION AND CONTROVERSY:
  "Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies" (1935):
  Three New Guinea groups — Arapesh (both sexes gentle),
  Mundugumor (both aggressive), Tchambuli (sex roles reversed).
  Conclusion: temperament is culturally determined, not
  biologically fixed.

  FREEMAN'S CRITIQUE (1983): Mead's Samoan data was wrong.
  His own data showed violence, sexuality, competition.
  He alleged she was deceived by informants.

  The debate:
  - Neither fully vindicated. Freeman's own data had problems.
  - The "blank slate" conclusion Mead drew is oversimplified.
  - But biological determinism is equally wrong.
  - Cross-cultural variation in gender behavior is REAL and
    substantial, but within biological constraints.

  THIRD GENDERS (documented across cultures):
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Hijra (India)    │ Third gender; ritual role at births  │
  │                  │ and weddings; neither male nor female │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Two-Spirit       │ Indigenous North America (~150+       │
  │                  │ documented nations); spiritual role;  │
  │                  │ Western "berdache" term is rejected  │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Fa'afafine       │ Samoa; "in the manner of a woman"    │
  │                  │ — male-assigned, feminine expression; │
  │                  │ socially recognized and integrated   │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Muxe (Mexico)    │ Zapotec third gender; valued in      │
  │                  │ domestic roles; religious functions  │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────┘

  The distinction between biological sex, gender identity,
  gender expression, and sexual orientation is culturally
  variable in content but present as a distinction in all
  known cultures — the distinction is universal, the categories
  filling it are not.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Ethnography = interviews."**
Interviews are one component. The core method is participant observation —
being there, doing it, over extended time. Interviews without observation
get front-stage performance; observation catches back-stage behavior.

**"Culture is just values and beliefs — you can change it by changing those."**
Bourdieu's critique of this view: habitus is pre-reflective and embodied.
It is not a list of propositions that can be replaced with a better list.
Organizational culture change programs that focus on stated values ("we now
value collaboration") without changing the structural conditions that produce
the habitus (incentive systems, promotion criteria, resource allocation)
fail because they address symptoms, not the generative mechanism.

**"The potlatch was irrational — why give everything away?"**
From within a market economy logic, yes. From within a prestige economy, no.
The chief converts material goods (economic capital) into prestige (symbolic
capital) and political obligation (social capital). The conversion is rational
given the goal structure. The Canadian government's ban on potlatch was
ethnocentrism — projecting capitalist values onto a non-capitalist system.

**"Geertz's interpretive anthropology is just subjective opinion."**
Thick description has standards: internal consistency, coherence with
observed behavior, confirmation from multiple informants, testing against
counter-evidence, accounting for researcher position. It is not an
arbitrary interpretation — it is an argument with evidence, evaluated by
whether it illuminates more of the phenomena than competing interpretations.

**"Rites of passage are just symbolic — they don't do anything."**
Austin's speech act theory (performatives): some utterances DO things —
"I now pronounce you married" is not a description; it constitutes the
marriage. Ritual is performative in this sense. After the initiation rite,
the community genuinely treats the initiate as an adult — their social
status has changed, and behavior toward them changes accordingly.
The "symbolic" change produces real material effects.
