## 1. Solar Photovoltaics вҖ” Physics and Economics

### The Photoelectric Effect and P-N Junction

```
  PHOTON ABSORPTION:

  Photon (hОҪ) strikes semiconductor (silicon)
         вҶ“
  If hОҪ вүҘ bandgap energy (Eg) вҶ’ electron promoted from valence to conduction band
  If hОҪ < Eg вҶ’ photon not absorbed (transparent to that wavelength)
  If hОҪ >> Eg вҶ’ excess energy dissipated as heat

  Silicon Eg = 1.1 eV вҶ’ absorbs visible + part of NIR

  P-N JUNCTION:

  p-type (holes) | n-type (electrons)
                 в”ңв”Җв”Җ depletion region (built-in electric field)
                 в”Ӯ
  Excited electron вҶ’ swept by built-in field toward n-side
  Remaining hole  вҶ’ swept toward p-side
  Net: electron current flows out the n-side contact вҶ’ external circuit
```

### Shockley-Queisser Limit

Fundamental thermodynamic limit on single-junction solar cell efficiency.

```
  Maximum theoretical efficiency for single-junction = ~33%
  at optimal bandgap ~1.3 eV for AM1.5 solar spectrum

  Why not 100%?
  1. Sub-bandgap photons: not absorbed (~20% of spectrum)
  2. Thermalization loss: photons above bandgap вҖ” excess energy вҶ’ heat (~30%)
  3. Radiative recombination: unavoidable electron-hole recombination
  4. Voltage/fill factor losses

  REAL-WORLD EFFICIENCIES:

### Cost Decline: The Learning Curve

Wright's Law governs solar PV costs: every doubling of cumulative production yields a roughly constant percentage cost reduction вҖ” the same phenomenon as Moore's Law but for manufacturing cost per unit rather than transistor density. Solar's learning rate (~20-25% cost reduction per production doubling) has been stable for four decades, making its cost trajectory one of the most accurately predicted in energy economics. The difference from Moore's Law: solar improvements come from manufacturing scale and process optimization, not from miniaturization hitting physical limits.

```
  Solar PV module cost:

  2010: ~$2.00/Wp (watts-peak)
  2015: ~$0.60/Wp
  2020: ~$0.25/Wp
  2023: ~$0.15-0.18/Wp (utility scale)

  ~88% cost reduction in 13 years

  Wright's Law (learning curve): every doubling of cumulative production
  вҶ’ ~20-25% cost reduction (solar historical learning rate)

  LCOE (Levelized Cost of Electricity):
  2010: $350/MWh (utility scale solar)
  2023: $30-50/MWh (sunniest regions), $50-80/MWh (moderate insolation)

  Comparison:
  New coal: $65-150/MWh
  New natural gas (CCGT): $45-80/MWh
  New nuclear: $80-150/MWh (developed economies)

  Solar is now cheapest electricity source in history in most regions.
  The remaining challenge is integration, not generation cost.
```

## 2. Wind Energy вҖ” Physics and Economics

### Betz Limit

```
  FUNDAMENTAL PHYSICS: why can't we extract 100% of wind energy?

  If wind completely stopped at turbine вҶ’ no airflow through turbine at all
  вҶ’ no energy extraction (zero flow rate = zero power)

  Trade-off: must allow air to continue flowing (downwind air must move
  faster than if turbine removed all energy)

  BETZ (1919): theoretical maximum = 16/27 = 59.3% of wind kinetic energy

  Wind power equation:
  P = ВҪ Г— ПҒ Г— A Г— vВі Г— Cp

  Where:
  ПҒ = air density (1.225 kg/mВі at sea level, 15В°C)
  A = rotor swept area (ПҖrВІ) вҖ” why bigger rotors matter
  vВі = wind speed cubed вҖ” why high-wind sites are SO much better
  Cp = power coefficient (max 0.59, real turbines 0.35-0.50)

  vВі rule: doubling wind speed вҶ’ 8Г— power potential
  A rule: doubling rotor diameter вҶ’ 4Г— swept area вҶ’ 4Г— power

  This is why offshore wind is valuable: higher, more consistent wind speeds
```

### Nuclear as Firm Power

Nuclear doesn't fit the "cheap new electrons" story but fills a different need: always-on,
high energy density, zero-operational-carbon. Current context:
- Existing fleet: cheapest dispatchable generation in US (fully amortized capital)
- New builds: FOAK (first of a kind) cost overruns (Vogtle, Hinkley Point C)
- SMRs (small modular reactors): NuScale, GE-Hitachi BWRX-300 вҖ” factory fabrication
  model intended to address cost problem; still unproven at scale
- Role: provides firm capacity вҶ’ reduces storage requirements вҶ’ enables higher VRE penetration

## 4. Hard-to-Decarbonize Sectors

### Steel

```
  CONVENTIONAL STEEL (Basic Oxygen Furnace / Blast Furnace):
  Iron ore (FeвӮӮOвӮғ) + coke (C) вҶ’ pig iron + COвӮӮ
  Coke is both fuel AND reducing agent вҶ’ COвӮӮ is chemically unavoidable
  ~1.8 tonnes COвӮӮ per tonne steel (world average)
  Steel = ~7% of global COвӮӮ emissions

  GREEN STEEL PATHWAY (Hydrogen Direct Reduction):

  1. Green HвӮӮ produced via electrolysis (renewable electricity + water)
  2. HвӮӮ replaces coke as reducing agent:
     FeвӮӮOвӮғ + 3HвӮӮ вҶ’ 2Fe + 3HвӮӮO  (water, not COвӮӮ)
  3. Direct reduced iron (DRI) fed to electric arc furnace (EAF)
  4. EAF powered by renewable electricity

  NET: zero COвӮӮ (if HвӮӮ is green)

  Status: HYBRIT (SSAB/LKAB/Vattenfall, Sweden) вҖ” first commercial green
          steel delivered to Volvo in 2021; Salzgitter (Germany) scaling
  Challenge: green HвӮӮ cost + electrolyzer scale + DRI conversion capital
  Timeline: competitive at ~$1.50-2.00/kg green HвӮӮ (current: ~$3-6/kg in
            most regions; declining on electrolyzer cost learning curve)
```

### Cement

```
  CEMENT COвӮӮ SOURCES:

  ~40% from energy (kiln combustion вҶ’ can decarbonize with green energy)
  ~60% from calcination вҶ’ CANNOT be avoided by energy switching:

  CaCOвӮғ (limestone) вҶ’ CaO (quicklime) + COвӮӮ

  This COвӮӮ is from the carbonate chemistry, not combustion.
  ~0.5-0.6 tonnes COвӮӮ per tonne cement (calcination alone)
  Cement = ~8% of global COвӮӮ emissions

  MITIGATION STRATEGIES:
  1. Carbon capture at cement plant (capture calcination COвӮӮ, store or use)
  2. Supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs): replace clinker with fly ash,
     GGBFS (ground granulated blast furnace slag), calcined clay
     вҶ’ Reduces clinker per mВі concrete, reduces calcination COвӮӮ
  3. Alkali-activated materials (geopolymers): no Portland clinker;
     activates blast furnace slag or fly ash with alkali
     вҶ’ Near-zero process COвӮӮ; not yet mainstream
  4. Re-absorption: cured concrete slowly reabsorbs COвӮӮ from air (re-carbonation)
     over decades; meaningful at portfolio scale
```

  - Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF): biomass-derived jet fuel

    Problem: ~3-5Г— cost of fossil jet fuel; feedstock limited; slow scaling
    Blending mandates: EU ReFuelEU requires 2% SAF by 2025, 70% by 2050

  - Power-to-liquid (PtL / e-kerosene): green HвӮӮ + captured COвӮӮ вҶ’ synthetic

  Long-term (uncertain):
  - Hydrogen aircraft: HвӮӮ combustion or fuel cells; requires cryogenic
    fuel storage, aircraft redesign; Airbus ZEROe studies (2035 target
    for short-haul); range and infrastructure challenges
  - Battery aircraft: energy density gap too large for commercial aviation
    (today's batteries carry ~100Г— less energy per kg than jet fuel)

  Non-COвӮӮ effects: contrails + NOвӮ“ + water vapor cause ~2-4Г— warming
  effect beyond COвӮӮ alone (non-COвӮӮ radiative forcing). Major uncertainty.

     KвӮӮCOвӮғ вҶ’ limestone kiln (regenerates KOH, releases pure COвӮӮ)
     Energy: ~1.5 GJ heat + ~0.4 GWh electricity per tCOвӮӮ

  2. SOLID SORBENT (Climeworks / Global Thermostat):
     Air вҶ’ amine-functionalized solid sorbent
     COвӮӮ adsorbs to sorbent
     Heat to ~80-120В°C вҶ’ COвӮӮ desorbs, collected
     Solid sorbent regenerated, repeat
     Energy: ~5-10 GJ per tCOвӮӮ (lower grade heat = more flexible sourcing)

  CURRENT SCALE:
  Climeworks Mammoth (Iceland, 2024): 36,000 tCOвӮӮ/yr capacity
  (global emissions: ~37 billion tCOвӮӮ/yr вҶ’ scale difference = 10вҒ¶)

  COST TRAJECTORY:
  Current: $400-1,000/tCOвӮӮ (depending on source)
  IPCC scenarios: need $50-100/tCOвӮӮ at scale for significant deployment
  IEA Net Zero: 1 GtCOвӮӮ/yr DAC by 2050 (from near-zero today)
```

### BECCS

Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage. In theory, plants absorb COвӮӮ as they grow
(biogenic), then that biomass is burned for energy, and the released COвӮӮ is captured
and stored geologically вҖ” net negative.

**Why it appears in almost every IPCC 1.5В°C scenario:**
The math is attractive вҖ” energy output PLUS carbon removal. Integrated Assessment
Models (IAMs) use it heavily because it's cheap and energy-generating.

**Why it's contested:**
- Land: growing bioenergy crops competes with food production and forests
- Water: energy crops require irrigation in many scenarios
- Permanence of biological carbon (fire, disease, land use change risks)
- CCS infrastructure requirement same as for fossil CCS
- Only "negative" if biomass genuinely additive (not displacing existing forest)

Credible potential: ~1-3 GtCOвӮӮ/yr globally under sustainability constraints.
IAM models often assume 5-10 GtCOвӮӮ/yr вҖ” likely infeasible without unacceptable
land use trade-offs.

## 6. Solar Radiation Management (SRM)

SRM does not remove COвӮӮ. It reduces incoming solar radiation to offset radiative
forcing from COвӮӮ. Critically: if SRM deployed and then stopped, COвӮӮ remains at
elevated levels and temperature rebounds rapidly вҖ” "termination shock."

### Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI)

```
  MECHANISM:

  Analogous to large volcanic eruptions:

  Mt. Pinatubo (1991):
  - 20 Mt SOвӮӮ injected to stratosphere
  - Converted to HвӮӮSOвӮ„/sulfate aerosol droplets
  - Increased Earth's albedo
  - Global temperature: вҲ’0.5В°C for ~2 years

  SAI concept:
  - Inject SOвӮӮ or other aerosol precursors at ~20 km altitude
  - Stratospheric transport spreads globally within weeks-months
  - Particles settle out over 1-2 years (continuous injection required)
  - Scale to achieve вҲ’1В°C: ~1-5 Mt SOвӮӮ/yr (compared to Pinatubo's 20 Mt for
    a single 2-year event)

  PROPOSED DELIVERY MECHANISMS:
  - High-altitude aircraft (purpose-built; existing aircraft can't reach 20 km)
  - High-altitude balloons
  - Stratospheric hose (SPICE project, never implemented)
  - Artillery/rockets (expensive)

  ESTIMATED COST: very cheap relative to mitigation
  SCoPEx (Harvard research program, canceled outdoor experiments):
  modeled cost ~$2-8 billion/year for 1-2В°C offset
  Compare to global climate finance flows of $600+ billion/year
```

### SAI Risks and Uncertainties

```
  KNOWN RISKS:

  1. TERMINATION SHOCK
     SAI must continue indefinitely once started
     If stopped (war, economic collapse, political disruption):
     COвӮӮ still at high levels вҶ’ rapid warming at rate ~0.1-0.5В°C/decade
     Ecosystem shock from fast temperature change may be worse than
     the slower warming SAI was preventing

  2. PRECIPITATION PATTERN CHANGES
     Pinatubo 1991 associated with:
     - Weakened Asian and African monsoons (~5-10% reduction)
     - Drought in Sahel
     Modeled SAI also shows regional precipitation shifts
     Monsoon disruption вҶ’ billions of people, agriculture

  3. OZONE DEPLETION
     Sulfate aerosols provide surface for heterogeneous chemistry
     вҶ’ activates chlorine вҶ’ catalytic ozone destruction
     Delays recovery of ozone hole

  4. DIFFUSE LIGHT (impact on ecosystems and solar PV)
     Aerosol scattering converts direct beam to diffuse radiation
     - Reduces output of concentrating solar (CSP), negligible effect on flat PV
     - Changes plant photosynthesis (diffuse light can increase canopy penetration)

  5. SKY COLOR
     Milky, whitened sky (Pinatubo's sunsets were famously colorful)
     Estimated to reduce starlight visibility significantly

  WHAT IT DOES NOT ADDRESS:
  - Ocean acidification (COвӮӮ chemistry unaffected)
  - COвӮӮ fertilization effects
  - Long-term warming (only masks, doesn't reverse)
```

### Marine Cloud Brightening (MCB)

Spray sea salt particles into marine low clouds вҶ’ more cloud condensation nuclei вҶ’
smaller droplets вҶ’ brighter clouds вҶ’ higher albedo. More localized than SAI.

```
  MECHANISM:
  Twomey effect вҖ” marine cloud droplets:

  Few CCN: fewer, larger drops, lower albedo
  Many CCN: more, smaller drops, higher albedo (same liquid water content)

  Status: small-scale field trials (University of Washington/CALFIN);
  scaling physics uncertain; regional rather than global effect
  Advantage over SAI: reversible, no stratospheric chemistry effects
  Disadvantage: localized effects, may affect rainfall downwind
```

### SRM Governance

```
  GOVERNANCE GAP:

  No international framework exists for:
  - Research authorization
  - Small-scale outdoor experiments
  - Large-scale deployment
  - Consent from affected nations
  - Liability for impacts

  Fundamental problem:
  - SAI would be transboundary by definition вҖ” aerosols spread globally
  - Country A deploys SAI вҶ’ benefits Country A; potentially harms Country B
    (altered monsoon)
  - No mechanism for consent, compensation, or control

  Moral hazard debate:
  - "If SRM is available, will it reduce pressure to cut emissions?"
  - The "insurance analogy": having a fire extinguisher doesn't mean you stop
    fireproofing the building вҖ” it's an emergency backup
  - Counter: political psychology вү  engineering logic; moral hazard is real

  Current governance approaches:
  - "Research governance" frameworks: SCoPEx (Harvard), DEGREES Initiative
  - UNEA Resolution on SRM (discussed but not adopted)
  - Calls for UN-level deliberative process before any deployment

  CURRENT CONSENSUS: outdoor SAI experiments require broad governance framework
  first. Indoor/modeling research broadly accepted. No responsible actor
  advocates unilateral deployment.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Renewables need 100% backup from gas"**
False for all but very high penetrations. At 30-40% VRE on a grid, storage + demand
response + transmission covers most variability. At 80%+ VRE, firm backup matters.
Current UK grid: 45%+ renewables share with existing gas backup + interconnects.

**"Germany proves renewables don't work вҖ” high prices and high COвӮӮ"**
Germany's Energiewende context: it shut down nuclear (political decision post-Fukushima)
while building out renewables вҖ” the nuclear shutdown was the driver of the COвӮӮ and
price issues, not the renewables. Countries with hydro-backed renewables (Norway,
Iceland, Costa Rica) show 95-100% clean electricity is achievable.

**"We can just plant trees and solve climate change"**
Global afforestation potential: ~1-3 GtCOвӮӮ/yr under realistic land use constraints.
Global emissions: ~37 GtCOвӮӮ/yr. Trees help at the margin; they don't come close to
compensating for continued fossil fuel use.

**"DAC is science fiction"**
Commercial DAC plants exist and operate. Climeworks' Mammoth plant in Iceland (2024)
captures 36,000 tCOвӮӮ/yr for $400-800/tCOвӮӮ and stores it geologically via CarbFix.
It's real, expensive, and small. The question is whether cost comes down fast enough.

**"SAI would fix climate change"**
SAI would temporarily mask warming but: doesn't reduce COвӮӮ (ocean acidification
continues), requires permanent maintenance, creates termination shock risk, doesn't
address long-term COвӮӮ trajectory. It's a tourniquet, not surgery. The IPCC frames
it as a potential risk-reduction tool for the most severe near-term impacts, not
as an alternative to mitigation.
