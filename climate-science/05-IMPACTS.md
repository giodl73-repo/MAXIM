## Sea Level Rise

```
  TWO COMPONENTS:

  1. THERMAL EXPANSION (~40% of observed SLR):
    Ocean warms → water expands in volume (steric SLR)
    Already committed: ocean continues warming for centuries
    even if atmospheric warming stops (thermal lag)
    Current contribution: ~1.5 mm/year

  2. ICE MASS LOSS (~60% of observed SLR):
    Greenland Ice Sheet: surface melt + calving
    West Antarctic Ice Sheet: basal melt (warm water underneath)
    Mountain glaciers: melting worldwide
    Current contribution: ~2.2 mm/year combined

  OBSERVED RATE (satellite altimetry since 1993):
    ~3.7 mm/year current rate (was ~2.0 mm/year in 1990s)
    ACCELERATING: ice mass loss increasing as ice sheets destabilize

  COMMITTED SLR:
    Already locked in from current atmospheric CO₂:
    ~0.5m SLR above current by 2100 even under aggressive mitigation
    (thermal expansion continues for centuries; ice sheets slow to respond)
    Committed multi-century SLR: much larger (~1-2m for 1.5°C scenario;
    several meters if WAIS or GIS thresholds exceeded)

  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  IPCC AR6 LIKELY RANGES (m above 2005 baseline, by 2100):     │
  │  SSP1-2.6 (low emissions): 0.32-0.62 m                        │
  │  SSP2-4.5 (intermediate):  0.44-0.76 m                        │
  │  SSP5-8.5 (high):          0.63-1.01 m                        │
  │                                                               │
  │  LOW-PROBABILITY, HIGH-IMPACT TAIL:                           │
  │  Marine ice sheet instability → 2m+ by 2100                   │
  │  (Ice Sheet Model Intercomparison Project — ISMIP6:           │
  │   low-confidence but non-negligible probability)              │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CONSEQUENCES:
    100-year flood events become annual in many cities by 2050
    Saltwater intrusion into coastal freshwater aquifers
    Bangladesh, Pacific island nations, major deltas (Mekong, Ganges,
    Nile, Mississippi): extreme exposure
    US coastal assets at risk: ~$1 trillion (NOAA estimates)
    Managed retreat vs adaptation cost trade-off already active
    (Miami: pumped elevation; Rotterdam: engineered surge barriers)
```

## Extreme Heat

```
  THE WET-BULB TEMPERATURE LIMIT:

  Wet-bulb temperature (Tw) = temperature of evaporative cooling
  (what thermometer reads wrapped in wet cloth in air flow)

  SURVIVAL LIMIT: Tw = 35°C (95°F) for ~6 hours
    At Tw 35°C: human body cannot cool itself via sweating
    (when air is at saturation, evaporation = zero)
    → Core temperature rises → hyperthermia → death
    Regardless of fitness or health; affects everyone

  WHERE THIS THRESHOLD HAS BEEN REACHED (as of 2023):
    Persian Gulf: Tw > 35°C events recorded since 2015
    South Asia: brief events in Rajasthan, Pakistan
    At +2°C global: modeled to affect hundreds of millions/year
    At +4°C: large parts of tropics uninhabitable during summer

  COMPOUND HEAT-HUMIDITY:
    Humidity matters more than temperature alone
    40°C (104°F) dry heat: survivable with shade/water
    35°C (95°F) at 75% RH: at or near physiological limit
    Urban heat island amplifies by 2-5°C vs rural surroundings

  HEAT MORTALITY:
    2003 European Heatwave: ~70,000 excess deaths in 2 weeks
      France: 15,000 (hospitals overwhelmed; August vacation)
      Spain, Italy, UK, Germany: similar
      Attribution: this event 2× more likely due to climate change
    2021 Pacific Northwest Event: Lytton BC = 49.6°C (121°F)
      ~1,400 excess deaths in Canada + NW US
      Event would have been "virtually impossible" without
      climate change (World Weather Attribution)

  ATTRIBUTION SCIENCE:
    World Weather Attribution (WWA): rapid studies of whether
    specific events made more likely/intense by climate change
    Method: compare observed event in real-world climate model
    vs counterfactual model without anthropogenic forcing
    Key finding: virtually all major heat events now more likely;
    many events essentially impossible in pre-industrial climate
```

## Precipitation, Flooding, Drought

```
  CLAUSIUS-CLAPEYRON CONSTRAINT:
    Water vapor holding capacity of air: +7% per +1°C
    MORE moisture in warm air → heavier precipitation events
    (same storm dynamics, more water vapor available)

  PRECIPITATION CHANGES:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Increase in heavy precipitation intensity: ~7%/°C        │
  │  BUT: precipitation frequency and duration may decrease   │
  │       in many regions (fewer but heavier events)          │
  │                                                           │
  │  REGIONAL PATTERNS (approximate, GCM agreement):          │
  │  Wetter: high latitudes (polar amplification + moisture)  │
  │           Equatorial tropics; monsoon intensification     │
  │  Drier: Mediterranean, southwestern US/Chile, S Africa    │
  │         Amazon (dieback risk creating drought feedback)   │
  │  Complex: South Asian monsoon (intensity up, but timing   │
  │           shifts; droughts AND floods)                    │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  "WET-GET-WETTER, DRY-GET-DRIER":
    Oversimplification but directionally supported
    Mediterranean regions, western US drying on annual basis
    Tropical convective regions more extreme precipitation

  ATMOSPHERIC RIVERS:
    Narrow corridors of concentrated water vapor in atmosphere
    10-20× Mississippi River discharge in vapor
    Pineapple Express to California as example
    Intensifying with warming → more extreme California flooding
    (2023 atmospheric river series: ~$5B damage estimate)

  SNOWPACK AS WATER TOWER:
    Western US: ~70% of water supply from snowpack
    Himalayas: >2 billion people depend on glacial meltwater
    Andes: major cities (Lima, La Paz) rely on glaciers
    As glaciers shrink: initial increase (melt surge) then
    permanent reduction in late-summer water supply
    Peak melt already passed in many smaller glacier systems
```

## Drought and Water Stress

```
  DROUGHT TYPES:
    Meteorological drought: deficit in precipitation alone
    Agricultural drought: soil moisture deficit → crop stress
    Hydrological drought: reduced streamflow, groundwater
    Socioeconomic drought: when impacts hit human systems

  PDSI (Palmer Drought Severity Index):
    Widely used index incorporating precipitation, temperature,
    soil moisture; range ±4 (extreme wet/dry)
    Climate warming increases drought severity through:
      → Increased evapotranspiration (hotter → dries faster)
      → Reduced snowpack (less spring water release)
      → Earlier spring snowmelt (mismatch with demand period)

  GROUNDWATER:
    ~2 billion people depend on groundwater as primary source
    Many major aquifers being overdrawn: Ogallala (US Great Plains),
    Central Valley California, North China Plain, Indus
    Climate warming: increases demand; may reduce recharge
    Aquifer depletion: some irreversible (land subsidence)
    San Joaquin Valley: sunk 9 meters in some areas from extraction

  SNOWPACK (Western US):
    April 1 snowpack (traditional water year measurement)
    declining 10-20% per decade in many Western ranges
    Shift to earlier melt → spring flooding, summer drought
    Ski industry impact; but primary concern is water supply
    for agriculture (80% of consumptive water use in West)
```

## Wildfire

```
  FIRE WEATHER INDEX:
    Temperature + low humidity + wind + drought compound
    → Extreme fire weather days increasing

  MECHANISMS:
    Extended fire season: longer dry period → more fuel drying
    Bark beetle outbreaks (amplified by warming) → dead standing fuel
    Fire suppression legacy (US): 100 years of suppression →
    fuel accumulation far above historical fire-adapted levels
    Drought stress: weakened trees less resistant to beetles
    Jet stream changes: blocking patterns extend heat domes
    → Fire weather events that historically lasted days
    now last weeks

  US EXAMPLES:
    Western US: annual burned area increased 500-800% since 1970s
    2020 California fires: largest in state history to that point
    2023 Maui fire (Lahaina): urban fire in unusual conditions
    Australian "Black Summer" 2019-2020: 24 Mha burned; 3 billion
    animals killed; smoke reached stratosphere globally

  SMOKE HEALTH IMPACTS:
    PM2.5 fine particles penetrate deep into lungs
    Wildfire smoke PM2.5: composition includes toxic compounds
    (benzene, formaldehyde, heavy metals from burning structures)
    During 2020 Western US fires: smoke air quality cancels out
    decades of improvement in urban air quality
    Annual wildfire smoke PM2.5 exposure: growing faster than
    projected; health burden largest for western US
    children and elderly
```

## Agricultural Impacts

```
  TEMPERATURE THRESHOLDS FOR MAJOR CROPS:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CROP   │ OPTIMAL RANGE │ UPPER THRESHOLD                 │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Maize  │ 15-25°C       │ >35°C → pollen sterility      │
  │  Wheat  │ 10-24°C       │ >30°C → yield decline begins  │
  │  Rice   │ 20-30°C       │ >35°C → spikelet sterility    │
  │  Soybean│ 20-28°C       │ >36°C → flower/pod abortion   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CO₂ FERTILIZATION EFFECT:
    Higher CO₂ → enhanced photosynthesis (C3 plants: wheat, rice, soy)
    Partially offsets warming-induced yield losses
    BUT: effect smaller than early projections
    C3 food quality decreases: lower protein, zinc, iron per calorie
    (faster growth = diluted nutrients)
    Weeds benefit as much or more than crops
    C4 plants (corn, sorghum, sugarcane): smaller CO₂ fertilization
    benefit; more vulnerable to temperature

  REGIONAL WINNERS AND LOSERS:
    Northern winners (shorter term): Canada, Russia, Scandinavia
    → Longer growing season + cold regions warming faster
    Southern losers: Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, tropics
    → Already at or near optimal temperatures
    → Any further warming → yield decline
    Net global: modeling suggests net negative even with
    fertilization effects above ~1.5-2°C global warming

  FOOD SYSTEM RESILIENCE:
    Single crop species grown globally: wheat/rice/maize
    = narrow genetic base = correlated failure risk
    Crop failure in multiple regions simultaneously:
    Compound shocks (COVID + conflict + climate) → 2022 food
    crisis as preview
    Agricultural adaptation: new varieties (drought/heat tolerant);
    irrigation efficiency; shifting planting dates; crop switching
    CGIAR (Consultative Group for International Agricultural Research):
    global public-good crop breeding programs
```

## Ocean Impacts

```
  CORAL BLEACHING:
    Coral reef = coral animal + zooxanthellae (symbiotic algae)
    Zooxanthellae provide 70-90% of coral's energy via photosynthesis
    THERMAL STRESS: sustained SST anomaly +1°C above usual summer max
    → Coral expels zooxanthellae → bleaches white
    → If temperature declines quickly: coral can recover
    → Extended bleaching (weeks-months): coral dies

    DHWMAX (Degree Heating Weeks): accumulated thermal stress metric
    DHW 4-8: bleaching; DHW >8: significant mortality

    GREAT BARRIER REEF:
    Mass bleaching events: 1998, 2002, 2016, 2017, 2020, 2022
    2016-2017: back-to-back events; first time pre-1998 never occurred
    At 1.5°C: 70-90% of reefs severely bleached annually
    At 2.0°C: >99% severely bleached → functional extinction

  OCEAN ACIDIFICATION:
    CO₂(aq) + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
    pH since 1850: 8.18 → 8.07 (represents 26% increase in H⁺)
    Aragonite saturation state: key for calcifying organisms
    Tropical: still supersaturated; polar: approaching undersaturation
    Impacts:
      Pteropods (sea butterflies): shells dissolve in polar waters
      Oyster larvae: catastrophic failures in US Pacific Coast hatcheries
        (absorbed CO₂ makes seawater corrosive to larvae shells)
      Coral: reduced calcification rate

  DEOXYGENATION:
    Warmer water: lower O₂ solubility
    Increased stratification: less mixing → oxygen minimum zones expand
    Oxygen minimum zones: expanded 4.5 million km² since 1960
    Deep-sea anoxia: impacts benthic communities

  FISHERIES:
    Poleward range shifts: cold-water fish moving toward poles
    Cod: dramatic northward shift from Gulf of Maine to Scotian shelf
    Tuna: tropical expansion into higher latitudes
    Food web disruption: timing mismatch between prey and predator
    Ocean warming combined with overfishing: compound stressor
```

## Ecosystem Disruption

```
  PHENOLOGY MISMATCHES:
    Phenology = timing of biological events
    Many species cue on temperature (spring arrival → bud burst,
    emergence, bloom)
    BUT: different species respond at different rates
    → Decoupling of predator-prey, plant-pollinator timing

    CLASSIC EXAMPLE:
    Oak trees in UK: budburst advancing 15 days since 1980
    Great Tit caterpillar peak: advancing 15 days (good match)
    Pied Flycatcher (African migrant): arrives same date
    (cued by African photoperiod/climate, not UK spring)
    → Arriving AFTER caterpillar peak → food mismatch → population decline
    → 90% population decline in some Dutch woodland sites

    Similar mismatches documented in: salmon/insect emergence;
    flowering/pollinator timing; migrant bird arrival/food availability

  RANGE SHIFTS:
    Poleward shift: ~6 km/decade average (terrestrial species)
    Upslope shift: ~11 m/decade average (elevation)
    Not all species can shift at required rate
    Habitat fragmentation prevents migration for many species
    Biome boundaries shifting: boreal forest into tundra;
    tropical forest boundary upslope in Andes

  EXTINCTION RISK:
    IPCC AR5: 20-30% species face elevated extinction risk at 2°C
    AR6: ~18% at 1.5°C, 29% at 2°C, 39% at 3°C at high risk
    Coral reef ecosystem: functional extinction risk at 1.5-2°C
    Polar bear: sea ice loss → habitat loss → population decline
    Mountain ecosystems: "no room at the top" as species shift up

  PERMAFROST ECOSYSTEMS (see also 03-FEEDBACKS):
    Thermokarst lakes, "drunken forests" (trees tilting as
    permafrost subsidence occurs), active layer deepening
    Indigenous communities: infrastructure collapse;
    traditional food sources disrupted; culture disruption
```

## Human System Impacts

```
  CLIMATE MIGRATION:
    ESTIMATES (wide range; definition-dependent):
      World Bank 2021: 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050
      (in 6 regions modeled — not including international migration)
      Ranges in literature: 50 million to 1 billion+ by 2100
      (depending on emissions scenario and threshold definitions)
    Migration drivers:
      Sea level/flooding: Bangladesh, Vietnam delta, Pacific islands
      Agricultural failure: Sub-Saharan Africa, Central America
      Heat + water stress: Middle East/North Africa (MENA)
    Distinction: most projected climate migration is INTERNAL
    (within countries); international migrants face additional
    legal barriers
    Climate ≠ only driver: interacts with conflict, poverty, governance

  CONFLICT:
    Causal chain: Climate stress → resource scarcity (water, food)
    → migration → competition → conflict
    Evidence: drought in Middle East and North Africa preceded
    Syrian civil war (Syria drought 2006-2010 → agricultural collapse
    → rural migration to cities → pre-existing political tensions
    → civil war 2011); climate = necessary but not sufficient
    Statistical studies: modest but robust positive correlation
    between temperature anomalies and conflict
    Critique: conflict causation is multi-causal; overly simple
    attribution is misleading

  HEALTH:
    Malaria range expansion: higher temperatures → vector expansion
    into higher altitude and latitude areas
    Heat mortality: see Extreme Heat section
    Air quality: wildfire smoke, ozone (higher temps increase ozone
    formation) → respiratory disease
    Vector-borne: dengue, Zika, Lyme disease range expansion
    Mental health: disaster trauma; eco-anxiety; community disruption

  INFRASTRUCTURE:
    Roads: heat-induced pavement deformation; freeze-thaw changes
    Permafrost foundation failure: Russia, Alaska, Canada
    Energy demand: cooling load increases (especially peak demand);
    heating demand decreases (net may be positive or negative
    depending on region)
    Water systems: changing hydrology → dam operations, water rights
```

## Risk Framing

Climate risk is the canonical real-world case for risk-averse decision criteria rather than expected-value maximization. From stochastic optimization and decision theory: when outcomes have fat tails (small probability of catastrophic, irreversible consequences), expected-value calculations severely underestimate appropriate action. The "irreversibility premium" — option value of avoiding a state you cannot exit — is a core concept from real-options theory. Climate tipping points (AMOC collapse, WAIS destabilization) are exactly the low-probability, high-consequence, irreversible outcomes that break expected-value reasoning. The Stern-Nordhaus debate is fundamentally about discount rates and tail-risk weighting, not empirical disagreement.

```
  IPCC "BURNING EMBERS" FRAMEWORK:
    Five "Reasons for Concern" (RFC) assessed at each warming level:
    1. Unique and threatened systems (coral reefs, Arctic)
    2. Extreme weather events
    3. Distribution of impacts (unequal exposure)
    4. Global aggregate impacts (economic GDP loss)
    5. Large-scale singular events (tipping elements)

    Each RFC assigned risk color (white → yellow → red → purple)
    at different temperature levels
    Purple ("beyond adaptation") triggers much earlier in
    AR5/AR6 vs earlier assessments (scientific understanding improved)

  EXPECTED VALUE vs RISK AVERSION:
    EXPECTED VALUE approach: weight each outcome by probability
    Standard economics tool; gives "average" outcome

    PROBLEM for climate:
    Fat tail of probability distribution (small probability of
    catastrophic outcomes) dominated by extreme scenarios
    Expected value may severely underestimate appropriate response

    ANALOGY: You wouldn't decline fire insurance because expected
    value of house fire is less than premium cost

    APPROPRIATE FRAMEWORK:
    Risk aversion for low-probability/high-consequence outcomes
    Irreversibility premium: option value of avoiding catastrophe
    that can't be undone (tipping points; species extinction)

  COST-BENEFIT ANALYSIS CRITIQUE:
    Traditional CBA: Stern Review vs Nordhaus debate
    Discount rate: choosing 3% vs 5% discount rate →
    completely different present-value of future damages
    Distributional: climate damages disproportionately hit
    poorest people/countries; but standard CBA weights by wealth
    Non-market values: ecosystem services, cultural heritage,
    human lives not captured in GDP
    Catastrophic risk: models often exclude fat tail events

  ADAPTATION vs MITIGATION:
    MITIGATION: reduce emissions → reduce forcing → avoid impacts
    ADAPTATION: adjust to impacts we can't avoid

    Key finding: adaptation has limits ("hard limits")
    Coral reefs: no adaptation once bleached at scale
    Some coastal areas: no adaptation at >1.5-2m SLR
    Some agriculture: no adaptation above thermal thresholds
    Adaptation is complementary to mitigation, not alternative
    "Risk layering": mitigation reduces risk; adaptation manages
    residual risk; loss and damage for unavoidable impacts
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Sea level rise already "locked in" is large**: Even stopping all emissions today doesn't stop sea level rise. The thermal expansion committed to by current ocean warming will continue for centuries. ~0.5m is already committed from current CO₂ levels. This "committed SLR" is not in most people's mental models of the problem.

**Wet-bulb temperature is what kills people, not just temperature**: A 40°C day in Phoenix (low humidity) is survivable; a 35°C day in Mumbai (high humidity) at the same time may be at the physiological limit. Most media reports air temperature; wet-bulb or heat index is the more relevant metric for heat mortality.

**Attribution science has matured**: It's now possible to state specific probabilities for how climate change influenced specific events. "We can't say any single weather event was caused by climate change" was the pre-attribution era answer. The World Weather Attribution group has shown, for example, that the 2021 Pacific Northwest heat event was "virtually impossible" without climate change.

**CO₂ fertilization does not offset food security concerns**: The fertilization effect is real but: (1) it reduces food quality (protein dilution), (2) weeds benefit equally, (3) it does not offset heat stress beyond certain thresholds, (4) water stress often limits the benefit. It's a partial offset, not a solution.

**Adaptation has hard limits**: Managed retreat, flood barriers, drought-resistant crops — adaptation buys time. But for coral reefs, low-lying atolls, some agricultural zones above thermal thresholds, some mountain glaciers: there is no adaptation pathway. These are "hard limits" in IPCC terminology.
