# Scientific Realism: IBE, No-Miracles, Anti-Realism, van Fraassen, Structural Realism

## The Big Picture

The central question: do theoretical entities posited by successful science — electrons, quarks, electromagnetic fields, spacetime curvature — actually exist, or are they useful fictions that happen to generate correct predictions? The debate between scientific realism and its rivals is the most active area in philosophy of science today.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |

|                                                                  |
|  ANTI-REALISM VARIANTS:                                          |
|  +-----------------+------------------+----------------------+  |
```

## Inference to Best Explanation (IBE)

### The Realist's Core Inference

IBE (also called **abduction**, Peirce's term) is the inference form:
1. E is observed
2. If H, then E (H explains E)
3. H is the best explanation of E available
4. Therefore: (probably) H

This is the inference form that underlies both everyday reasoning and scientific reasoning. Sherlock Holmes: "You have been in Afghanistan, I perceive." How? Inference to the best explanation of the available evidence.

For the realist, IBE justifies belief in theoretical entities:
- **Observation**: The cathode ray tube deflects in characteristic ways
- **Best explanation**: There are negatively charged particles with specific mass/charge ratio
- **Conclusion**: Electrons exist (approximately as described)

```
WHY IS IBE THE BEST EXPLANATION OF SCIENTIFIC SUCCESS?

  We observe: modern science produces reliably accurate
  predictions, useful technologies, novel successful discoveries

  REALIST BEST EXPLANATION:
  Mature, successful scientific theories are approximately true.
  They work because they accurately describe real structure.

  ANTI-REALIST ALTERNATIVE:
  Theories are useful calculating devices. Their empirical
  success is a criterion of selection, not evidence of truth.

The no-miracles argument says the anti-realist explanation
is less parsimonious — it makes success mysterious.
```

## The No-Miracles Argument

Hilary Putnam (1975): "The positive argument for realism is that it is the only philosophy that doesn't make the success of science a miracle."

**Formalization** (following Boyd):
1. Mature scientific theories are reliably predictively successful
2. The best explanation of this success is that the theories are approximately true about both observables and unobservables
3. Therefore: mature scientific theories are approximately true about unobservables

**The miracle alternative**: If electron theory is just a useful fiction — if electrons don't exist — then the theory's ability to predict cathode ray behavior, generate semiconductors, enable electron microscopy, and correctly predict quantum mechanical phenomena is a massive coincidence. That coincidence is the "miracle" the realist says we should not postulate.

## The Pessimistic Meta-Induction

Larry Laudan (1981) responded to the no-miracles argument with devastating historical evidence:

```
THE PESSIMISTIC META-INDUCTION:

  Inductive premise: Consider the history of science.
  Caloric theory — successful in its time, now rejected as false
  Phlogiston theory — predictively useful, now rejected as false
  Luminiferous ether — required by Maxwell's equations, now rejected
  Ptolemaic epicycles — predictively accurate for 1400 years
  Newtonian absolute space — used in all mechanics, rejected
  Vital force in biology — explained organism specificity, rejected

  Pattern: Mature, successful theories have repeatedly been
  abandoned as false.

  Inductive conclusion: Current mature, successful theories
  (quantum field theory, general relativity, standard model)
  are probably also approximately false.

  → The no-miracles argument's premise — that success implies
  approximate truth — has historically been false.
  → The realist's argument undermines itself.
```

| Historical Theory | Was Successful | Was Retained | Post-Verdict |
|------------------|---------------|--------------|-------------|
| Caloric theory | Yes | Predictions confirmed | False (heat = molecular motion) |
| Phlogiston | Yes | Explained combustion | False (oxygen combining) |
| Luminiferous ether | Yes | Required by wave mechanics | Does not exist |
| Ptolemaic system | Yes | 1400 years of predictions | False (Earth is not center) |
| Newtonian absolute space | Yes | Foundation of mechanics | Does not exist (GR) |
| Vital force | Partially | Explained organism properties | Does not exist |

## Van Fraassen's Constructive Empiricism

Bas van Fraassen, *The Scientific Image* (1980), is the most sophisticated contemporary anti-realist position.

### Core Claim

Science aims to give us theories that are **empirically adequate** — correctly describing all observable phenomena. It does not aim at truth about unobservables. We should believe a successful theory is empirically adequate; we need not (and should not) believe it is true about unobservables.

**Acceptance vs. Belief**: Van Fraassen distinguishes *accepting* a theory (using it, working within it, treating it as a research framework) from *believing* it to be true. A pragmatist can accept quantum field theory without believing quarks literally exist.

### The Observable/Unobservable Distinction

Van Fraassen's observable/unobservable line:

```
OBSERVABLE: Can be directly observed by unaided human senses
  under favorable conditions
  → Bacteria (can be seen with naked eye under some conditions)
  → Medium-sized dry goods
  → Stars (visible with naked eye)

UNOBSERVABLE: Detected only through instruments
  → Electrons (only inferred through instrument readings)
  → Viruses (below optical resolution)
  → Quarks (never directly observed even in principle)

VAN FRAASSEN: We should believe what our theories say about
observables. We need not believe what they say about
unobservables.
```

### Objections to the Observable/Unobservable Line

1. **The line is vague**: Is Jupiter's moon Io observable? (Galileo saw it; it's just too dim for easy naked-eye viewing.) Are bacteria observable? Van Fraassen says yes — you can see them with the naked eye under favorable conditions (he admits this is edge-case).

2. **The line is anthropocentric**: Why should the limits of human perception determine what we believe? Grover Maxwell: the distinction is "observational in a technical sense only."

3. **Continuity argument**: Microscopes extend naked-eye observation. Better microscopes extend that. Electron microscopes extend further. At what point do we stop believing what we "see"?

Van Fraassen bites the bullet on the vagueness: yes, the line is vague. But every epistemic category is vague; vagueness doesn't collapse the distinction.

## Structural Realism

John Worrall (1989) proposed structural realism as a way to have it both ways:

```
THE FRESNEL/MAXWELL TRANSITION:

  Fresnel (1820s): Light is waves in the luminiferous ether.
  Theory: equations describing polarization, diffraction,
  interference — in terms of ether displacement.

  Maxwell (1860s): Light is electromagnetic waves.
  Theory: Maxwell's equations — same formal structure as
  Fresnel's for optical phenomena.

  Transition: Ether eliminated; electromagnetic field replaces it.
  What was RETAINED: the mathematical equations.
  Fresnel's equations are limiting cases of Maxwell's.

  WORRALL'S MORAL:
  The "nature" of what light consists in changed.
  The mathematical structure was preserved.

  → Believe the structure; be agnostic about the nature.
```

### Epistemic vs. Ontic Structural Realism

- **Epistemic SR** (Worrall, Ladyman): All we can know is the relational structure; the intrinsic nature of things is unknowable. We should believe in the structure.
- **Ontic SR** (French, Ladyman): There is no intrinsic nature — the structure *is* all there is. Objects are structures of relations all the way down (a view with connections to categorical theory, background-free physics).

### Structural Realism and the Pessimistic Meta-Induction

Structural realism handles the pessimistic meta-induction by saying: yes, the specific entities (ether, caloric) were abandoned, but the mathematical structure was retained (Fresnel → Maxwell; Carnot cycles retained in statistical mechanics). So the realist was right about what mattered — the structure.

## Entity Realism (Hacking)

Ian Hacking (*Representing and Intervening*, 1983) offers a different middle position: believe in entities you can *manipulate to produce effects*, regardless of whether the theory about them is true.

"If you can spray them, they are real." Electrons: we can shoot electrons at targets, manipulate their trajectories with magnetic fields, use them to produce specific outcomes. This causal/interventional criterion grounds belief in entities without requiring that any specific electron theory is true.

```
HACKING'S CRITERION:
  Entity X is real if:
  We can use X as a tool to produce other effects in the world.
  We can manipulate X's properties to achieve desired outcomes.

  Electrons: YES — we manipulate charge/mass to produce
  predictable effects in CRTs, accelerators, solid state devices
  → Electrons are real (we don't need to say which theory of
    electrons is true)

  Phlogiston: NO — never used as a causal tool to produce effects
  → Phlogiston was not real
```

## Current Landscape

No consensus. The main positions and their relative strength in contemporary philosophy of science:

| Position | Core Claim | Strength | Weakness |
|----------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Scientific realism** | True approximately; unobservables exist | No-miracles; IBE natural | Pessimistic meta-induction |
| **Constructive empiricism** | Aim = empirical adequacy; agnostic on unobservables | Avoids meta-induction; principled | Observable line vague; anti-realism seems arbitrary |
| **Epistemic structural realism** | Believe the mathematical structure | Handles meta-induction; explains continuity | What is structure without an object that instantiates it? |
| **Ontic structural realism** | Structure is all there is | Connects to category theory, modern physics | Counter-intuitive; objects as primary seems more defensible |
| **Entity realism** | Believe in what you can manipulate | Connects belief to practical competence | Theories are needed to manipulate; theory and entity inseparable? |
| **Instrumentalism** | Theories are tools; no truth question | Clean; no metaphysics needed | Science aims at truth — dismissing this distorts scientific practice |

## Common Confusion Points

**IBE is not deductively valid**: Abduction is ampliative — the conclusion goes beyond the premises. The "best explanation" is still defeasible. IBE is a form of probabilistic reasoning, not certainty. The realist needs IBE to be epistemically reliable, not logically certain.

**Empirical adequacy ≠ truth about observables**: Van Fraassen's position requires only that the theory's observable predictions are correct — not that its statements about observables are literally true. It's a functional/predictive criterion.

**Structural realism ≠ "only math is real"**: The thesis is not that only mathematical objects exist. It is that the structural relations described by mathematical equations are the extent of what we can know (epistemic SR) or the extent of what exists (ontic SR). There is a difference.

**The pessimistic meta-induction is a meta-level inductive argument**: It uses induction to undermine inductive confidence in current theories. Laudan is turning the realist's own inference form against them. The realist can respond: the inductive base is skewed (we remember the spectacular failures) or structured wrong (structure was retained).
