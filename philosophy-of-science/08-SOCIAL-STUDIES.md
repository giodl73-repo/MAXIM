# Social Studies of Science: SSK, Laboratory Studies, Actor-Network Theory

## The Big Picture

From the 1970s onward, a new approach to studying science emerged: instead of asking what rational standards ought to govern science (Popper, Lakatos), ask how science actually works as a social institution. The Strong Programme in sociology of scientific knowledge (SSK) and laboratory studies made the production of scientific facts itself the object of social analysis — not just institutional norms but the cognitive content of science.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|               THE SOCIAL STUDIES TRADITION                       |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  MERTONIAN SOCIOLOGY (1940s–60s)                                |
|  Studies NORMS of science: universalism, communism,             |
|  disinterestedness, organized skepticism (CUDOS)                |
|  NOT the content of knowledge itself                            |
|              |                                                   |
|              v (Kuhn's Structure opens the door: 1962)          |
|  STRONG PROGRAMME (Edinburgh, 1970s)                            |
|  Studies the CAUSES of scientific beliefs.                      |
|  True and false beliefs explained by same sociological methods. |
|  Symmetry principle: no privileged explanation for "correct"    |
|  beliefs.                                                        |
|              |                                                   |
|              v (moves from institution to lab bench)            |
|  LABORATORY STUDIES (1970s–80s)                                  |
|  Ethnographic study of what scientists actually do              |
|  Latour/Woolgar: facts constructed through inscription devices   |
|              |                                                   |
|              v (extends to technology, policy, artifacts)       |
|  ACTOR-NETWORK THEORY (1980s–90s)                               |

|  Science as practical achievement, not revealed truth           |
|                                                                  |

```

## The Strong Programme (Edinburgh School)

### Bloor's Tenets

David Bloor, *Knowledge and Social Imagery* (1976), articulated the Strong Programme in four principles:

| Principle | Statement |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Causality** | SSK should explain the beliefs which scientists hold — including true and correct beliefs — in terms of social causes |
| **Impartiality** | The programme should be impartial between true and false, rational and irrational, successful and unsuccessful theories |
| **Symmetry** | The same types of cause should explain both true and false beliefs |
| **Reflexivity** | SSK's own program should be explicable by the same methods — the sociologist applies the analysis to himself |

### The Symmetry Principle

The symmetry principle is SSK's most controversial claim and its most important methodological innovation. Before SSK, sociologists explained *false* beliefs sociologically (Newton believed in absolute space because of his religious views; scientists rejected continental drift because of professional conservatism) but explained *true* beliefs as simply tracking reality.

Bloor says: that's asymmetric and unscientific. Both true and false beliefs have social causes. Explaining why physicists accepted quantum mechanics requires the same kind of sociological analysis as explaining why they initially rejected continental drift.

```
ASYMMETRIC (REJECTED) APPROACH:
  Why did scientists accept oxygen theory?
  → Because it's true and the evidence forced them

  Why did scientists accept phlogiston?
  → Because of social factors, conservatism, Stahl's authority

SYMMETRIC (SSK) APPROACH:
  Why did scientists accept oxygen theory?
  → Social and cognitive factors: Lavoisier's status, French
    chemistry community structure, laboratory instrument
    networks, political context, + evidential factors
    (but these are also socially constituted)

  Why did scientists accept phlogiston?
  → Same type of analysis: Stahl's community, German chemistry
    traditions, evidential base available at the time
```

### Bloor's Epistemic Relativism

SSK implies a form of epistemic relativism: whether a belief is "rational" or "justified" is relative to the standards of a community. Different communities may have different standards, and there is no community-independent standpoint from which to judge.

This does not mean "anything goes" epistemically — within a community's standards, some things are more rational than others. But there is no absolute rationality transcending all standards.

## Barry Barnes and the Interest Model

Barnes developed a complementary account: scientific knowledge serves the **interests** of particular social groups. The interests include:
- **Technical interests**: solving practical problems
- **Social interests**: career advancement, institutional power, funding acquisition
- **Political interests**: ideological commitments, gender/race/class dynamics

Knowledge is "finalized" around these interests — what counts as established fact in a field reflects what serves the field's dominant interests.

This became the basis for science studies work on controversies: examining how controversies are settled reveals the social interests at stake, not just the balance of evidence.

## Laboratory Studies

### Latour and Woolgar: Laboratory Life (1979)

Bruno Latour (French sociologist/anthropologist) and Steve Woolgar spent two years doing **ethnographic fieldwork** in a neuroendocrinology lab at the Salk Institute. Their findings:

**Inscription devices**: In the lab, "facts" emerge through a sequence of material transformations:
```
MATERIAL → PROCESSED → GRAPH → STATEMENT → CITATION
  (rat)   (tissue     (paper  ("TRF has   (text-book
           extract)   trace)  this         fact")
                              structure")

Each step: inscriptions become more abstract and mobile.
Final products: text statements that are treated as facts.
```

**Fact construction**: Facts are not discovered but constructed through specific material practices, instrument configurations, and social negotiations. A "discovery" is a stabilized inscription that survives all challenges.

**Reality-making**: The lab generates "reality" — TRF (thyrotropin-releasing factor) did not pre-exist its determination; the lab practices that established its structure made it into a fact.

Note: Latour later backed away from some of this language. The provocative framing in Laboratory Life was intentional — meant to force readers to see the practices they take for granted.

### Karin Knorr-Cetina: The Manufacture of Knowledge (1981)

Knorr-Cetina's laboratory studies complemented Latour/Woolgar: scientific knowledge is **manufactured** through contingent practical decisions — choice of organism, reagents, timing, instrument calibration — that are driven by practical concerns (what's available, what works, what the funder wants) rather than pure theory.

The "tinkering" model of science: scientists cobble together results from available resources, not executing a pre-formed theory-test. The results are artifacts of the specific production process, not universal facts waiting to be revealed.

## Actor-Network Theory (ANT)

### Latour and Callon: Science in Action (Latour, 1987)

ANT proposes analyzing science as the construction of networks of **actants** — both human and non-human — that stabilize each other.

**Key concepts**:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Actant** | Any entity (human or non-human) that acts in the network: scientists, instruments, texts, organisms, funding agencies, governments |
| **Inscription** | The process of encoding information into a durable form that can be transported (graph, published paper, database) |
| **Immutable mobile** | An inscription that retains its form across translations and can be transported to distant centers (maps, standardized measurements) |
| **Translation** | Reinterpreting others' interests to align with your program; enrolling allies |
| **Black box** | A fact or device that is taken for granted; the controversy surrounding it has been closed |

### The "Sociology of Translation"

Michel Callon (1986), studying scallop aquaculture in Brittany, showed how a fact ("scallops can be farmed this way") was established through a process of:
1. **Problematization**: Framing a situation as requiring the researchers' solution
2. **Interessement**: Locking other actants into roles that advance your position
3. **Enrollment**: Getting actants to take on the roles you assign them
4. **Mobilization**: Representing the enrolled actants (speaking for them)

The "sociology of translation" applies to both scientific facts and technological systems.

### Non-Human Agency

ANT's most provocative move: non-human actors (microbes, door-closers, scallops, laboratory instruments) are treated symmetrically with human actors. A microbe acts in the network just as Pasteur acts in the network. Door-closers enforce social norms (keep the door closed) as much as signs do.

This principle of **generalized symmetry** (Callon) extends the Edinburgh symmetry principle from "true/false" to "human/non-human."

## Shapin and Schaffer: Leviathan and the Air-Pump (1985)

This is probably the most influential work in science studies outside Kuhn. Steven Shapin and Simon Schaffer examined the controversy between Robert Boyle and Thomas Hobbes about the air pump in 17th-century England.

**The controversy**: Boyle performed experiments with an air pump to demonstrate that vacuums exist. Hobbes denied the possibility of experimental knowledge in principle — not because of the specific results but because of his theory of knowledge and political philosophy.

**The argument**:
- Boyle's experimental method required a specific social form: **witnessing** by "modest observers" in controlled laboratory settings. Facts are established by collective witnessing.
- Hobbes rejected this: private club knowledge is not public knowledge. Experimental "facts" require social arrangement — they are not found but made.
- The dispute was simultaneously about what counts as evidence, who has the right to produce it, and what political order is legitimate.

```
BOYLE vs. HOBBES

  Boyle: Knowledge from controlled experiments,
         witnessed by credible gentlemen in
         the Royal Society
         → Facts are matters of established experience
         → Experimental method as foundation

  Hobbes: Knowledge requires demonstration
          from first principles
          → Experiments produce appearances, not facts
          → Experimental "witnessing" is politically
            suspect: private club, not public reason
          → Knowledge requires sovereign's authority

  Shapin/Schaffer: The settlement in favor of Boyle
  was also a political settlement. The experimental
  method was one solution among possible alternatives —
  it prevailed because of its social fit with emerging
  liberal political culture, not purely on epistemic merits.
```

## Feminist Epistemology and Standpoint Theory

Sandra Harding (*The Science Question in Feminism*, 1986) and Helen Longino (*Science as Social Knowledge*, 1990) argued that the social identity of scientists matters for the knowledge produced.

**Standpoint theory** (Harding, following Hartsock): Socially marginalized groups have epistemic privileges — they can see aspects of reality that dominant groups' position prevents them from noticing.

**Longino's social objectivity**: Objectivity is not a property of individual scientists but of scientific communities with the right social structure:
1. Recognized forums for criticism
2. Uptake of criticism (responses to objections must be taken seriously)
3. Publicly recognized standards for evaluation
4. Equality of intellectual authority (no group systematically excluded from the conversation)

This reframes objectivity as a social achievement rather than an individual epistemic virtue.

## The Science Wars

In 1994-1996, the social constructivist tradition in science studies collided with natural scientists who felt their practice was being delegitimized.

**Alan Sokal's Hoax (1996)**: Sokal, a physicist, submitted a nonsense article to the cultural studies journal *Social Text* titled "Transgressing the Boundaries: Towards a Transformative Hermeneutics of Quantum Gravity." The article was accepted and published. Sokal revealed the hoax immediately after publication.

The article argued (as parody) that physical reality is a social/linguistic construct, cited actual scientists incomprehensibly, and was full of non-sequiturs. The editors didn't catch it — Sokal argued — because it used the right ideological vocabulary.

```
SCIENCE WARS DIVIDE:

  SCIENTISTS (Gross/Levitt, Sokal/Bricmont):
  Social constructivism is self-refuting nonsense.
  Gravity doesn't care about social construction.
  Scientific method produces reliable knowledge.
  Science studies people don't understand the science
  they're writing about.

  SCIENCE STUDIES (Collins, Bloor, Latour):
  We are studying social practices, not claiming
  that electrons don't exist.
  The charge of misreading is often warranted (conceded).
  But the question of how facts are stabilized is real
  and the symmetry principle remains correct.

  POST-SOKAL CLARIFICATION:
  Most science studies practitioners now distinguish:
  (a) The process of fact construction (social, contingent)
  (b) The product (facts that can be real and robust)
  (a) doesn't undermine (b). Lab practices are social.
  The electron is real. Both can be true.
```

## Reconciling Constructivism and Scientific Success

The challenge: if facts are socially constructed, why does science work so well? Planes don't fall out of the sky because aerodynamics is "merely" socially constructed.

Several reconciling moves:

1. **Process vs. product**: Social construction describes *how* facts are established (the process), not whether the final facts are real (the product). Water is H2O regardless of how that fact was established.

2. **Robust facts**: Facts that survive translation across many networks, instruments, and communities gain special epistemic status — they are robust (Longino). The robustness is what matters, not any individual experiment.

3. **Pragmatic convergence**: Different communities pursuing different interests converge on the same facts because the world constrains what can be made to work.

4. **Latour's later position**: Science has two sides — the production of facts (social, contingent, messy) and the facts themselves (real, nonhuman, powerful). Both are true.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the symmetry principle? | Same sociological methods explain true and false beliefs; no privileged explanation for "correct" knowledge |
| What is an inscription device? | Laboratory instrument that transforms material into text — the process by which facts are made mobile and stable |
| What is an immutable mobile? | An inscription that retains form across translation: maps, standardized measurements, published papers |
| What is translation (ANT)? | Reinterpreting others' interests to align with your network; enrolling actants by reframing what they want |
| What is a black box? | A fact or device whose internal controversy has been closed; it is taken for granted and used as input |
| What was the Sokal affair? | Physicist submitted nonsense article to science studies journal; it was accepted; exposed credulity to ideologically compatible jargon |
| What did Shapin/Schaffer argue? | Boyle's experimental method involved political and social preconditions; the victory of experimental philosophy was partly a political settlement |

## Common Confusion Points

**SSK is not saying science is just politics**: The symmetry principle says *both* true and false beliefs have social causes. It doesn't say scientific beliefs are *only* social. The claim is methodological: sociological analysis applies to all beliefs, not just the false ones.

**ANT non-human agency is methodological, not metaphysical**: When Latour says microbes "act," he means that in the analysis of a network, the microbe plays a role that shapes outcomes. It's an analytic tool, not a claim that microbes have intentions.

**Latour moved away from radical constructivism**: By 2004, Latour had substantial second thoughts. "Has critique run out of steam?" asked whether the social constructivist tools built to criticize science were being misused (to deny climate change, undermine vaccines). He became more cautious about the political implications.

**Science studies and history of science are different**: History of science studies what happened (descriptive). Philosophy of science studies what should happen (normative). Science studies (SSK, ANT) is in between — empirical sociology but with normative implications for how we understand scientific authority.
