# 04 — Supply Systems

## Pressure, Water Heaters, Hammer, Recirculation

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Municipal supply**: typical service pressure 40-150 PSI at meter; residential target 40-80 PSI at fixtures; above 80 PSI → PRV required (code in most jurisdictions); below 40 PSI → pressure complaints, inadequate flow at multiple simultaneous fixtures
- **Pressure Reducing Valve (PRV)**: globe valve with spring + diaphragm; reduces high inlet pressure to set outlet; adjustable (spring tension screw); typical set point 60-70 PSI; has strainer screen (clean periodically); fails open or fails shut; if PRV fails, high pressure damages fixtures/supply lines — pressure relief valve (T&P valve) on water heater provides safety relief
- **Thermal expansion**: closed systems (backflow preventers on service entrance) don't allow expansion back to main; water heater heating cold water expands ~2%; expansion tank (pre-charged bladder tank) absorbs expansion; without expansion tank on closed system → PRV continuously dripping (relief valve bypass for expansion), eventual T&P discharge, supply line strain; required by code in closed systems
- **Water hammer** (hydraulic shock): fast-closing valves (solenoid valves in dishwashers/washing machines, quick-close cartridges) stop water flow suddenly → pressure wave travels back through pipe → banging; solutions: water hammer arrestors (mechanical shock absorber with gas chamber + piston — ASSE 1010 listed), air chambers (historically — pocket of air trapped, loses effectiveness as air dissolves), slower-closing valves; diagnosis: hammer right at valve = valve issue; remote hammer = pipe needs support
- **Hot water heaters — storage tank**:
  - First-hour rating (FHR): gallons of hot water delivered in first hour starting full and hot; key selection metric for household demand (shower = 20-30 gal, consecutive = overlapping demand)
  - Recovery rate: gallons per hour heated after tank depleted; determined by BTU input (gas) or wattage (electric)
  - Gas: power-vent (fan forces exhaust horizontally — flexible PVC flue), atmospheric (draft hood + B-vent), direct-vent (sealed combustion, two-pipe), condensing (90%+ efficiency, PVC flue, high efficiency)
  - Electric: resistance elements (top/bottom elements on timer relay); heat pump water heater (HPWH — draws heat from surrounding air, COP ~3.5, extremely efficient but needs space and heat source environment)
  - Temperature setting: 120°F (EPA recommendation, balance scald prevention vs Legionella risk); Legionella thrives 77-108°F → 140°F kills Legionella (with thermostatic mixing valve to prevent scald)
- **Tankless (demand/on-demand) water heaters**: no storage; heats as flow occurs; GPM capacity at temperature rise; typical residential: 5 GPM at 70°F rise; limited simultaneous use; flow switch initiates heat; modulating burner adjusts output; advantages: no standby loss, endless hot water (within GPM limit); disadvantages: cold water sandwich (brief cold burst), minimum flow rate to activate, high BTU gas demand (180,000-199,000 BTU/h for whole-house gas tankless → large gas line required), high electric demand (electric tankless impractical for whole-house unless low-demand)
- **Recirculation systems**: hot water supply loop from heater to fixtures and back; pump circulates continuously or on timer/demand; eliminates wait for hot water; energy penalty (heat loss from circulating pipe); options: dedicated return line, cold-line return (Metlund/Grundfos comfort system — uses cold water return, installs pump at farthest fixture), demand-activated (button or motion sensor triggers pump only when needed — most efficient)
- **Tempering/mixing valves**: ASSE 1017 thermostatic mixing valve at water heater outlet; allows heater at 140°F (for Legionella), mixes cold to deliver 120°F to fixtures; scald prevention; required for facilities with high-risk populations (seniors, children)
