## Gas Piping

### Black Iron (Carbon Steel)

```
  MATERIAL: Schedule 40 or 80 carbon steel (uncoated — black)
  ≠ Galvanized steel (never use galvanized for gas)
  Reason: galvanizing (zinc) flakes into gas stream → clogs orifices
  in appliance valves and controls

  JOINING: NPT (National Pipe Thread) tapered threads
    Male threads: taper 3/4" per foot
    Female threads: matching taper; threads engage on taper,
    not on thread face (unlike straight/parallel threads)
    Thread sealant: two options:
      Pipe dope (paste): Rectorseal, Megaloc, or equivalent
        gas-rated product (NOT standard plumber's putty)
      Yellow/pink gas Teflon tape (PTFE): NOT white plumbing tape
        (white is thinner and may not fill threads adequately)
    Sealant applied to male threads; 2-3 wraps tape or dab of dope

  TESTING:
    After assembly: pressure test before concealing
    Test medium: dry gas pressure (already connected supply),
    or nitrogen, or compressed air
    NEVER use oxygen (explosive with any hydrocarbon contamination)
    NEVER use an open flame (STILL done by homeowners — catastrophically dangerous)
    Test method: close downstream valves, apply pressure,
    soap bubble test all joints, watch pressure gauge

  TYPICAL SIZES:
    3/4" main service (most residential)
    1/2" to individual appliances (range, furnace, water heater)
    Larger for commercial or multiple high-BTU appliances
```

### CSST (Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing)

```
  MATERIAL: Stainless steel corrugated tube in yellow or black
  plastic jacket
  Flexible — bends around obstacles without fittings
  Significantly faster installation than rigid black iron

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CSST LIGHTNING ARC RISK:                                   │
  │                                                              │
  │  Lightning strikes near structure → induced current in      │
  │  gas piping → electrical arc can penetrate thin corrugated  │
  │  wall of CSST → gas escapes → fire                          │
  │                                                              │
  │  Black iron: thick wall; much harder to arc through         │
  │  CSST: thin corrugated wall; vulnerable to induced arc      │
  │                                                              │
  │  SOLUTION: BOND EVERY CSST SEGMENT                          │
  │  Bond wire (typically #6 AWG copper) connected to CSST      │
  │  at each segment → connected to grounding electrode system  │
  │  This equalizes potential → prevents arc forming            │
  │                                                              │
  │  CODE: NFPA 54 (National Fuel Gas Code) requires bonding    │
  │  Required by most jurisdictions since ~2006                 │
  │  Manufacturers have specific bonding instructions           │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  BRANDS: TracPipe (OmegaFlex), CounterStrike (OmegaFlex — bonded
  jacket), FlexPro (Gastite)
  CounterStrike and equivalent arc-resistant products have
  stainless over-braid that provides inherent bonding if
  installed per manufacturer's instructions

  WHERE CSST IS PREFERRED:
    Tight runs through framing (flexible threading)
    Long runs from meter to appliances
    Earthquake-resistant (flexible joints survive ground movement)
    California, Nevada, seismic zones: CSST strongly preferred
```

### Gas Pressure

```
  NATURAL GAS SYSTEM PRESSURES:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  UTILITY DISTRIBUTION MAIN: 0.25-200 PSI (varies)          │
  │          ↓                                                   │
  │  SERVICE REGULATOR (utility-owned at meter):                │
  │    Reduces to "service pressure" at meter                   │
  │    Typical residential delivery: 0.25 PSI (7" w.c.)        │
  │    OR 2 PSI with in-house regulator for longer runs         │
  │          ↓                                                   │
  │  HOUSE PIPING: 7" w.c. (0.25 PSI) or 2 PSI                 │
  │          ↓                                                   │
  │  APPLIANCE MANIFOLD PRESSURE:                               │
  │    Most appliances: 3.5" w.c. (0.125 PSI) for natural gas  │
  │    OR 11" w.c. (0.4 PSI) for gas ranges, some appliances   │
  │    Regulator at appliance reduces house pressure to this    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  INCHES WATER COLUMN (IWC):
    Pressure expressed as equivalent height of water column
    1 PSI = 27.7 inches water column
    7" w.c. = 7/27.7 = 0.25 PSI — common residential service

  PROPANE (LP GAS) DIFFERENCES:
    Tank pressure: 150-200 PSI (liquid propane)
    First-stage regulator (at tank): reduces to ~10 PSI
    Second-stage/service regulator: reduces to 11" w.c.
    Propane BTU content: ~2.5× natural gas per cubic foot
    → Propane appliance orifices are smaller
    → Gas range, water heater: MUST have conversion kit
      when switching fuel type (wrong orifice = fire hazard)

  BTU CAPACITY OF GAS PIPING:
    Function of: pipe diameter, run length, inlet pressure,
    and specific gravity of gas
    From NFPA 54 tables (or online calculators):
    1/2" × 20 ft run at 7" w.c.: ~76,000 BTU/hr (natural gas)
    3/4" × 20 ft: ~175,000 BTU/hr
    High-BTU appliances (tankless heater 200,000 BTU):
    often require upsizing from standard 1/2" supply
```

## Hydronic Heating

```
  CONCEPT: Water carries heat from boiler to spaces
  Water's specific heat: ~4× air → more energy per volume
  → Smaller distribution system; more responsive than massive
  ducted air systems

  BOILER TYPES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CAST IRON ATMOSPHERIC BOILER:                            │
  │    Heavy, durable (25-40 year life)                       │
  │    Slow to heat but retains heat; stable operation        │
  │    Standing pilot or electronic ignition                  │
  │    B-vent (atmospheric draft to chimney)                  │
  │    AFUE: 80-82%                                           │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  MODULATING-CONDENSING (MOD-CON) BOILER:                  │
  │    Modulating burner: ramps output 10-100% to match load  │
  │    At low output: flue gas cools below dew point          │
  │    → Water vapor condenses in heat exchanger              │
  │    → Recovers ~10-12% additional efficiency from latent   │
  │      heat of vaporization of combustion water             │
  │    AFUE: 90-98%                                           │
  │    PVC flue (not metal — condensate is acidic)            │
  │    Requires condensate drain (acidic pH 3-5)              │
  │    Best matched with: low-temperature distribution        │
  │    (radiant floor, low-temp baseboards) for maximum       │
  │    condensing operation time                              │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HOT WATER BASEBOARD (FIN-TUBE):
    Copper tube through aluminum fins
    Hot water inside tube heats fins; fins heat air by convection
    Room air circulates through fins via natural convection
    (no fan — quiet operation)
    Design temperature: 180°F supply / 160°F return (old standard)
    160/140°F supply/return: adequate for most residential
    Output per foot: at 180°F = ~600 BTU/hr/ft; at 140°F = ~250 BTU/hr/ft
    Lower temperatures → longer baseboard runs needed for same heat

  ZONE CONTROL:
    Zone valves: single circulator; motorized valves control
    flow to each zone; less expensive to install; one circulator
    to maintain
    Multiple circulators: one per zone; better hydraulic
    balance; allows different flow rates for each zone;
    more robust (zone failure = one circulator)
    Primary-secondary piping: primary loop always circulating;
    secondary circuits inject off primary; eliminates zone-to-zone
    hydraulic interference

  OUTDOOR RESET:
    Controller reads outdoor temperature
    → Calculates required supply temperature (heat curve)
    Cold day (0°F): need 180°F supply
    Mild day (40°F): need only 130°F supply
    Benefits: lower supply temp → more condensing time
    → Higher efficiency + more comfortable (less cycling)
```

## Radiant Floor Heating

```
  PHYSICS:
    Radiation from warm floor surface (~75-85°F floor temp)
    → Direct radiant heat transfer to occupants and objects
    Floor temperature must NOT exceed ~85°F (uncomfortable; damages
    some flooring; damages hardwood floors irreversibly)
    Works with LOW water temperature → ideal for condensing
    boilers and heat pumps

  COMFORT ADVANTAGE:
    Even temperature from floor to ceiling
    (vs forced air: stratification — hot air at ceiling)
    Warm feet + slightly cooler head = human preferred comfort profile
    Radiant heat "feels warmer" at same air temperature
    → Can set thermostat 2-3°F lower → energy savings

  HYDRONIC RADIANT FLOOR TYPES:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SLAB ON GRADE (in-slab):                                │
  │  PEX tubing embedded in concrete slab                    │
  │  Best conductivity (concrete contact with tube)          │
  │  Highest thermal mass (slow response — 2-4 hours)        │
  │  Best for: continuous occupation, large homes, slab      │
  │                                                           │
  │  BELOW SUBFLOOR (staple-up or heat transfer plates):     │
  │  PEX stapled under subfloor; aluminum transfer plates    │
  │  direct heat through subfloor                            │
  │  Lower thermal mass; faster response; lower output       │
  │  Best for: wood frame with crawlspace access             │
  │                                                           │
  │  ABOVE SUBFLOOR (gypcrete or sleeper):                   │
  │  PEX in 1.5" gypcrete or wood sleeper system             │
  │  Above existing floor → adds height                      │
  │  Better than staple-up; less than in-slab                │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MANIFOLD SYSTEM:
    Distribution center for multiple loops
    Supply manifold: hot water from boiler distributed to all loops
    Return manifold: water from all loops returns to boiler
    Each loop: 200-400 ft of 1/2" PEX (max loop length for
    pressure drop management)
    Flow indicators: glass tubes on manifold show flow per loop
    Isolation valves: allow balancing (throttle high-flow loops
    to equalize heat delivery)
    Mixing valve: reduces boiler temperature (160°F+) to
    floor-appropriate (100-110°F)

  HEAT PUMP COMPATIBILITY:
    Radiant floor is ideal partner for heat pump:
    Heat pump efficiency (COP) increases as supply temp decreases
    Heat pump at 100°F supply: COP ~3.5-4.0
    Heat pump at 140°F (baseboard): COP ~2.0-2.5
    Low-temp radiant = maximum heat pump efficiency match
    Combination: most energy-efficient space heating available

  WOOD FLOORING CAUTION:
    Hardwood floors: maximum allowable temperature gradient
    Check manufacturer specifications (typically 3-4°F/hr change)
    Wood with radiant: quartersawn or engineered preferred
    (less movement across grain with humidity changes)
    Solid hardwood: possible but requires careful moisture
    management during floor acclimation and throughout year
```

## Fire Suppression Systems

```
  FUNDAMENTAL MYTH CORRECTION:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  MYTH: When one sprinkler head activates, ALL heads         │
  │  in the building open simultaneously (like in the movies)   │
  │                                                              │
  │  REALITY: Each sprinkler head is independently triggered    │
  │  by heat. Only the heads DIRECTLY EXPOSED to heat from      │
  │  the fire activate. In 80%+ of fire incidents, ONE OR       │
  │  TWO heads control the fire.                                │
  │                                                              │
  │  The deluge system (all open) exists but is rare:           │
  │  aircraft hangars, stage theaters — specific high-risk      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HOW INDIVIDUAL HEAD WORKS:
    Sprinkler head = glass bulb (or fusible link) + deflector + seal
    Glass bulb: glycerin-filled; liquid expands with heat
    → Bulb shatters at rated temperature
    → Seal releases → water flows through that head only
    Standard temperature rating: 155°F (68°C) — red bulb
    High-temp rating: 200°F (93°C) — green bulb
    Extended coverage, quick response, ESFR variants available

  NFPA STANDARDS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  NFPA 13: Commercial and multi-family (3+ family)           │
  │  Full coverage except exempt areas                          │
  │  Minimum density based on hazard classification             │
  │  Light hazard: office, school, residential                  │
  │  Ordinary hazard: light manufacturing, retail               │
  │  Extra hazard: painting, auto service, rubber goods         │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  NFPA 13D: One and two family dwellings                     │
  │  Simplified requirements; lower demand                      │
  │  Allowed materials: PEX and CPVC (not just steel)           │
  │  Some spaces exempted: closets <24 SF, bathrooms <55 SF    │
  │  Designed on 2-head simultaneous flow (vs commercial)       │
  │  Significant cost reduction vs NFPA 13                      │
  │  Many jurisdictions require for new construction            │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  NFPA 13R: Residential, 1-4 stories                        │
  │  Low-rise multi-family: apartments, condos, townhouses      │
  │  Intermediate standard between 13D and 13                   │
  │  Allows some space exemptions (similar to 13D)              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WET vs DRY SYSTEMS:
    WET PIPE (standard): Water in pipes at all times; immediate
    discharge when head activates
    → Most responsive; simplest; most common
    → ONLY for heated spaces (pipes must not freeze)

    DRY PIPE: Nitrogen/air in pipes; water held back by
    differential valve; air releases when head opens
    → For unheated spaces: parking garages, attics,
      unheated warehouses, loading docks
    → Slower activation (air must vent before water arrives)
    → Requires air compressor for system maintenance

    PRE-ACTION: Requires two events (smoke/heat detector +
    individual head) before water enters pipes
    → For water-sensitive spaces: libraries, computer rooms,
      art collections (avoids accidental discharge)
      More complex; higher cost; less common

  WATER SUPPLY:
    Residential (NFPA 13D): single domestic supply connection
    with minimum flow/pressure for 2 heads × 10 minutes
    Commercial: dedicated supply, typically from street main
    Backflow preventer required
    Underground supply pipe sized for design demand
```

## Medical Gas Systems

```
  GASES IN HEALTHCARE:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  GAS            │ COLOR CODE  │ USE                       │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Oxygen (O₂)    │ Green       │ Patient breathing         │
  │  Medical Air    │ Yellow      │ Ventilators, aerosol      │
  │  Nitrous Oxide  │ Blue        │ Anesthesia                │
  │  Medical Vacuum │ White       │ Suction                   │
  │  Nitrogen (N₂)  │ Black       │ Surgical power tools      │
  │  CO₂            │ Gray        │ Laparoscopy, laser cooling │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  STANDARD: NFPA 99 (Healthcare Facilities Code)
  Category 1: Category most critical; positive patient outcome
    → most stringent requirements

  OXYGEN SAFETY:
    Oxygen: not flammable itself but strongly supports combustion
    Hydrocarbon contamination + O₂ = explosive
    NO: petroleum-based lubricants near oxygen lines
    NO: oil-containing compressors for oxygen
    Fittings: DISS (Diameter Index Safety System) — different
    gas = different fitting diameter → prevents mis-connection
    DISS thread: body size different for each gas; can't
    connect wrong gas hose to wrong outlet physically

  INSTALLATION REQUIREMENTS:
    Piping: Type K or L copper (cleaned for oxygen service)
    "O₂ cleaned" piping: degreased, capped, tagged
    All joints: brazed (not soldered — must withstand pressure
    test and not oxidize)
    Testing: helium leak test of entire system
    Certification: ASSE 6000 series — Medical Gas Installer
    (ASSE 6010 Installer, ASSE 6020 Verifier, ASSE 6030 Instructor)

  ZONE VALVES:
    Located outside each zone/room
    Allow shutdown of individual zone without system shutdown
    Clearly labeled with gas type and zone served
    Required accessibility for emergency shutdown
    Tamper-resistant (requires tool or key)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Scenario | System / Specification |
|---|---|
| Long gas run, new construction | CSST (with proper bonding per manufacturer) |
| Gas in tight spaces, earthquake zone | CSST with bonded fitting system |
| Retrofit gas: always check | CSST bonding, black iron thread sealant (gas-rated) |
| Most comfortable home heating | Hydronic radiant floor (PEX in slab) |
| Most energy-efficient heating combo | Radiant floor + condensing modulating boiler + heat pump |
| Residential fire sprinkler | NFPA 13D (PEX/CPVC allowed; simplified) |
| Commercial/multi-family sprinkler | NFPA 13 (full coverage, steel pipe typical) |
| Unheated garage sprinkler | Dry pipe (nitrogen pressurized) |
| Hospital oxygen installation | NFPA 99 Category 1; ASSE 6010 certified installer; O₂-cleaned copper |
| Zoned hydronic, multiple floors | Primary-secondary piping or zone valves with single circulator |

## Common Confusion Points

**CSST bonding is mandatory, not optional**: The lightning arc risk is real and documented. Multiple house fires from CSST arc-through have been attributed to improperly bonded or unbonded CSST installations. This is a code requirement in virtually all jurisdictions and a life-safety issue. Inspect CSST in any home and verify the bonding wire.

**Radiant floor thermal mass is a feature AND a limitation**: The slab absorbs heat slowly (takes 2-4 hours to warm up or cool down). This is excellent for stable, continuous operation but poor for rapid setback (turning off at night, setback while away). Control strategy for radiant floor: minimize setback; use outdoor reset for gradual modulation rather than on/off thermostats.

**Fire sprinklers do not all open at once**: This misconception leads to unfounded fear of water damage from accidental activation. A single head requires sustained heat to activate. In practice, a kitchen grease fire typically activates one head and is controlled before structural damage. The water damage from ONE head is minor compared to fire damage without suppression.

**Gas and propane appliances are NOT interchangeable without conversion**: Natural gas orifices are larger than propane orifices (propane has higher BTU/ft³, so requires less flow for same heat output). Converting gas range or water heater from natural gas to propane requires a conversion kit (different orifice inserts, sometimes different regulator). Running propane through natural gas orifice = dangerous over-gassing. Always specify fuel type when ordering appliances and request proper orifices.

**Mod-con boilers need low-temperature systems to maximize efficiency**: A modulating-condensing boiler running at 180°F supply (for old high-temp baseboards) cannot condense — the flue gas stays too hot. The condensing efficiency (the 95% AFUE) is only achieved when supply temperatures are low enough for flue gas to cool below dew point (~130°F). Pairing a mod-con with old high-temp baseboards gets 80%, not 95% efficiency.
