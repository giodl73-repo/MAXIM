# 08 — Specialty Systems

## Gas Lines, Hydronic Heating, Radiant Floor, Fire Suppression, Medical Gas

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Gas piping**: two materials dominate: (1) black iron (carbon steel, threaded); (2) CSST (corrugated stainless steel tubing); natural gas or LP (liquefied petroleum/propane — different pressure, BTU content, and odorant level than natural gas)
- Black iron installation: NPT tapered threads; thread sealant (pipe dope — Rectorseal, Megaloc; or yellow Teflon tape for gas); wrench tightening; test with manometer or soap bubbles (never a flame); pressure test before covering; black iron ≠ galvanized (galvanized never for gas — zinc flakes)
- CSST (corrugated stainless steel tubing): flexible, snakes through walls without many fittings; faster to install; approved in most gas codes; critical bonding requirement — CSST has lightning-induced arc vulnerability (thin corrugated wall can be penetrated by induced lightning current → fire); must bond every CSST segment to grounding electrode system (NFPA 70/NEC bond wire, typically #6 AWG); code-required since ~2006 in most jurisdictions
- Gas pressure: natural gas — utility delivers at high pressure, reduced at service regulator to appliance pressure (7" w.c. = inches water column, approximately 0.25 PSI for most appliances; 11" w.c. for some ranges); LP — typically 11" w.c. at appliance after tank regulator; high-pressure LP (2 PSI) for some commercial; BTU/hr capacity of pipe based on length, diameter, specific gravity of gas
- **Hydronic heating** (water-based heat distribution):
  - Boiler types: cast iron (durable, slow to heat but retains heat, atmospheric vent); steel condensing mod-con (modulating-condensing — 95%+ AFUE, PVC flue, variable output matches load — most efficient option)
  - Hot water baseboard (fin-tube baseboard): copper tube through aluminum fins; natural convection circulates room air; quiet, even heat; water at 140-180°F (higher temp = more output per foot)
  - Fan coil units: water coil + fan; faster response than baseboard; used for heating and cooling
  - Zone valves vs zone circulators: multiple heating zones via motorized zone valves (one circulator) or separate circulators per zone; circulator-per-zone provides better flow balance
  - Boiler reset controls: outdoor reset adjusts boiler supply temperature based on outdoor temperature (cold day → higher supply temp; mild day → lower → higher efficiency, condensing more time)
- **Radiant floor heating** (RFH):
  - PEX tubing embedded in floor slab or stapled to subfloor above or below; warm water (~85-110°F) circulates through loops; gentle, even radiant heat; extremely comfortable (warm floor, even temperature from floor to ceiling vs hot top from forced air)
  - Manifold (distribution center): balances flow through multiple loops; supply and return manifold with flow indicators and isolation valves; mixing valve reduces boiler temperature to floor-appropriate range
  - Thermal mass: slab requires 2-4 hours to heat/cool — slow response; staple-up (no slab) slightly faster but lower output; hydronic radiant in wood floors requires low-profile aluminum heat transfer plates
  - Compatible with any heat source: gas boiler, heat pump, solar thermal; heat pump compatibility requires low supply temperatures (heat pump most efficient at lower temperatures — ideal match with radiant floor at 90-105°F supply)
- **Fire suppression**:
  - NFPA 13 (commercial/multi-family): full wet-pipe system; sprinkler head activates at ~155°F (standard) or 200°F (high-temperature); only activated heads flow (not all heads simultaneously — common myth); spacing, coverage, and pipe sizing per NFPA 13
  - NFPA 13D (residential 1-2 family): simplified residential system; allows PEX and CPVC; allows some spaces unprotected (closets, bathrooms <55 SF); designed with single-family demand; significant cost reduction vs commercial
  - NFPA 13R (residential 4 stories or less): intermediate standard; common in low-rise multifamily
  - Wet vs dry pipe: wet = water in pipes year-round (standard); dry = nitrogen/air, water enters only when head activates (for unheated spaces — parking garages, attic systems)
- **Medical gas** (NFPA 99, healthcare facilities): oxygen (O₂), medical vacuum, nitrous oxide, nitrogen, carbon dioxide, compressed air — all separate, labeled, color-coded systems; extremely tight purity and installation standards; no hydrocarbon-containing lubricants near oxygen (fire risk); installer certification required (ASSE 6000 series Medical Gas Installer certifications)
