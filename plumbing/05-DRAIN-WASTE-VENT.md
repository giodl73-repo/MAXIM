# 05 — Drain, Waste & Vent (DWV)

## Gravity, Traps, P-Traps, Vent Stacks, AAVs

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **DWV physics**: all drain flow by gravity; pipe must slope continuously toward sewer; horizontal drains: 1/4" per foot fall standard (steeper sometimes required for 3" and larger); too steep = liquids run ahead of solids leaving solids stranded; too shallow = solids don't move; vertical stacks fall by gravity, no slope needed
- **Trap function**: every fixture needs a trap (P-trap for most); trap holds water seal (2" minimum per code); water seal blocks sewer gases (methane, hydrogen sulfide, CO₂ — toxic and/or flammable) from entering building; if trap dries (infrequently used fixture, evaporation) → sewer gas enters room; solution: pour water in drain to refill, or add trap primer for floor drains
- **P-trap vs S-trap**: P-trap vents to atmosphere through separate vent pipe — water seal maintained; S-trap (vertical outlet directly below trap) = "self-siphoning" — as water drains, siphon effect can pull water out of trap behind it → empty trap → gas entry; S-traps banned in US plumbing codes for new construction; common in older homes and often visible under bathroom sinks
- **Vent stack function**: if DWV were sealed, flowing water would create negative pressure behind it (like covering your thumb on a straw) → suction removes trap water seals; vent pipe connects to drain system, runs to atmosphere (typically through roof) → maintains atmospheric pressure throughout drain system → trap seals remain intact; venting also allows sewer gases to escape to atmosphere rather than building up in drain system
- **Venting configurations**:
  - Individual vent: each fixture has its own vent pipe back to stack; most reliable but uses most pipe
  - Wet vent: one pipe serves as both drain for upper fixture and vent for lower fixture; size must accommodate both functions; code-governed on combinations
  - Stack vent: vertical drain stack continues above highest fixture as vent (no separate vent pipe needed above highest fixture on stack)
  - Island vent (AAV): loop vent for kitchen islands where running vent to roof is impractical
- **AAV (Air Admittance Valve, Studor valve)**: one-way valve admits air into drain system when negative pressure occurs (draining), stays closed otherwise (no sewer gas out); replaces running vent through roof; code-accepted in most jurisdictions for individual fixtures and wet venting configurations; NOT a universal substitute for roof vents (building still needs at least one stack vented to atmosphere for positive pressure relief); cannot use in some locations (below grade, in inaccessible locations)
- **Drain sizing**: drainage fixture units (DFU); each fixture assigned DFU value (toilet = 4 DFU, lavatory = 1 DFU, kitchen sink = 2 DFU, clothes washer = 3 DFU); sum DFUs → pipe sizing table → minimum pipe diameter; different tables for horizontal drain vs vertical stack; 3" pipe accommodates 20 DFU horizontal, 4" accommodates 160; toilet requires minimum 3" drain (4" if on a horizontal branch with other fixtures in some codes)
- **Sewer gas composition**: methane (CH₄ — explosive 5-15% by volume), hydrogen sulfide (H₂S — toxic at 50 ppm, lethal at 300 ppm), CO₂, ammonia, other organic compounds; sewer gas deaths (workers entering manholes, homeowners ignoring smell); all the more reason trap seals are life-safety
- **Cleanouts**: access to drain system for rodding (clearing clogs); required at the base of each stack, at direction changes, at intervals on long horizontal runs; floor-level or wall-accessible; clean-out plug (CO plug) or two-way cleanout; camera inspection via cleanout ports for diagnostics
- **Common problems**: slow drains (partial clog vs venting problem — bubbling, gurgling after flush = venting problem), dry traps (sewer smell), improper slope (standing water in pipes → bacteria growth, odor), root intrusion in sewer lateral (annual root-cutting or liner), grease buildup (kitchen drain)
