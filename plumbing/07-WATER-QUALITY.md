# Water Quality

## Hardness, Scaling, Softeners, Filtration, Lead Pipes

## The Big Picture

```
RESIDENTIAL WATER QUALITY TREATMENT HIERARCHY:

  Municipal or Well source
          ↓
  WHOLE-HOUSE (point of entry):
    Sediment pre-filter (20 → 5 micron)
    Water softener (hardness removal)
    Iron filter (if well water)
    Whole-house carbon filter (chloramine removal)
          ↓
  POINT-OF-USE (point of use):
    Under-sink RO system (nitrates, heavy metals, fluoride)
    UV disinfection (bacteria/virus — post-RO polishing)

  DIAGNOSTIC FIRST:
    Test water before treating
    Municipal water: annual consumer confidence report (required)
    Well water: test annually (coliform, nitrates, pH, hardness)
    Target-specific treatment based on actual results
    Shotgun "whole-house filter" approach without testing
    = wasted money and potentially sub-optimal outcome

  UNITS USED:
    Grains per gallon (GPG): US hardness measurement
    Parts per million (PPM) = mg/L: most contaminant reporting
    1 GPG = 17.12 mg/L (as CaCO₃)
    MCL: Maximum Contaminant Level (EPA regulatory limit)
    Action Level: EPA trigger for corrosion control (lead, copper)
```

## Water Hardness

```
  CAUSE: Dissolved calcium (Ca²⁺) and magnesium (Mg²⁺)
  from limestone and dolomite aquifers; calcium sulfate
  from gypsum deposits

  HARDNESS SCALE:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Classification    │ GPG        │ mg/L as CaCO₃           │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Soft              │ < 1        │ < 17                    │
  │  Slightly hard     │ 1-3.5      │ 17-60                   │
  │  Moderately hard   │ 3.5-7      │ 60-120                  │
  │  Hard              │ 7-10.5     │ 120-180                 │
  │  Very hard         │ > 10.5     │ > 180                   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  GEOGRAPHY:
    ~85% of US has "hard" or "very hard" water
    Softest: Pacific Northwest, New England, parts of Southeast
    Hardest: Southwest, Great Plains, Midwest (calcium-rich aquifers)

  EFFECTS OF HARD WATER:
    Scale (CaCO₃) deposits in water heater, pipes, fixtures
    Soap scum (calcium + soap fatty acids → insoluble film)
    Reduced soap/detergent effectiveness
    White film on dishes, glassware (dishwasher)
    Shortened water heater life (scale insulates elements)
    Reduced flow in showerheads (calcite buildup in orifices)
    Dry skin and hair (mineral film)
```

## Scale Chemistry

```
  THE REACTION:
    Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃ ↓ + CO₂↑ + H₂O
    (calcium bicarbonate → calcium carbonate + CO₂ + water)

  TRIGGERS:
    HEAT: Above ~60°C (140°F), reaction strongly favors CaCO₃
    precipitation
    Explains why water heaters fail first — highest temp in system
    CO₂ also offgasses at high temp, shifting equilibrium toward
    carbonate precipitation

  ECONOMIC IMPACT:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Scale insulates heat exchange surfaces:                 │
  │  1/4" scale on heating element → 39% efficiency loss     │
  │  1/2" scale → 70% efficiency loss (DOE estimates)        │
  │                                                          │
  │  Tankless water heater vulnerability:                    │
  │  Very narrow heat exchanger passages → scale quickly     │
  │  blocks flow → catastrophic failure without maintenance  │
  │  Annual descaling required in hard water areas           │
  │  (citric acid or white vinegar circulated through)       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SCALE IN PIPES:
    Over decades: progressive reduction in interior diameter
    Iron pipes most susceptible (tuberculation)
    Copper: less prone but can scale significantly in very
    hard water at high temps (water heater outlet fitting)
```

## Water Softeners (Ion Exchange)

```
  PROCESS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  RESIN TANK: filled with sulfonated polystyrene resin beads  │
  │  Beads pre-charged with Na⁺ ions                             │
  │                                                              │
  │  SOFTENING:                                                  │
  │  Hard water (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) flows through resin                 │
  │  Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ have higher affinity for resin than Na⁺       │
  │  → Exchange: Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ bind to resin, Na⁺ releases       │
  │  → Softened water exits with Na⁺ (no Ca²⁺ or Mg²⁺)           │
  │                                                              │
  │  REGENERATION (when resin exhausted):                        │
  │  Concentrated NaCl brine from salt tank flushes resin        │
  │  High Na⁺ concentration reverses equilibrium                 │
  │  → Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ released from resin                         │
  │  → Drain to waste                                            │
  │  → Resin re-charged with Na⁺ → ready for next cycle          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SODIUM ADDITION:
    ~7.5 mg Na per gpg hardness removed per liter
    Very hard water (20 GPG): ~150 mg sodium per liter
    Standard drinking water reference: 200 mg/L from all sources
    Softened water sodium concern for:
      Persons on sodium-restricted diet (heart failure, hypertension)
      → Use separate filtered tap for drinking/cooking
      → Or switch to potassium chloride (KCl) instead of NaCl

  POTASSIUM CHLORIDE (KCl) ALTERNATIVE:
    Same regeneration chemistry as NaCl
    KCl softened water: potassium instead of sodium
    Higher cost: ~2× price vs NaCl
    Better for low-sodium diet constraints
    Beneficial fertilizer effect if softened water used on lawn
    (but generally: bypass softener for irrigation)

  BYPASS FOR IRRIGATION:
    Sodium softened water: harmful to soil structure and plants
    Sodium displaces calcium/magnesium in soil, causes clay
    particles to disperse → reduced drainage
    Install bypass on hose bibs and irrigation zones
    (most softener installations include this)

  SOFTENED WATER AND LEAD PIPES:
    CRITICAL: soft water is more corrosive than hard water
    Calcium/magnesium form protective carbonate coating inside pipes
    Removing Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ via softening → more aggressive water
    → Faster corrosion of metal pipes including lead
    Do NOT run softened water through lead service lines or
    lead solder joints in old plumbing
```

## Salt-Free Scale Inhibitors (TAC)

```
  TEMPLATE-ASSISTED CRYSTALLIZATION (TAC):
    Media with nucleation sites
    Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ form micro-crystals (aragonite form)
    on media surface → rinse off into water as seed crystals
    Crystals pass through system WITHOUT depositing on surfaces

  WHAT IT IS AND ISN'T:
    IS: scale inhibitor — prevents scale buildup
    IS NOT: water softener — Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ remain in water
    Water hardness (by test) remains the SAME
    Scaling tendency (Langelier Saturation Index) is reduced

  ADVANTAGES vs ION EXCHANGE SOFTENER:
    No salt addition (no sodium)
    No waste water (regeneration cycle wastes 60-100 gal/cycle)
    No electricity
    Minimal maintenance

  LIMITATIONS:
    Does not benefit people who want softened water feel
    Limited effectiveness evidence outside scale prevention
    May not work as well in very high hardness situations
    No EPA/NSF certification process specific to TAC claims

  MAGNETIC/ELECTRONIC DEVICES:
    Magnets or inductors around supply pipe claimed to alter
    scale behavior
    Multiple independent peer-reviewed studies: no reliable
    evidence of effectiveness
    FTC has taken action against misleading marketing
    VERDICT: no credible scientific basis; not recommended
```

## Filtration

### Sediment Filtration

```
  PURPOSE: Remove particulate matter (sediment, rust, silt)
  REQUIRED BEFORE: RO systems, UV systems (turbidity blocks UV)

  CARTRIDGE TYPES:
    Wound string (polypropylene): 5-25 micron; economical
    Pleated polyester: more surface area; higher flow rate
    Depth vs surface filter:
      Depth filter: particles trapped throughout matrix; more
        capacity; can't backwash
      Surface filter: trapped on pleated surface; longer life;
        backwashable types available

  SIZING:
    20 micron pre-filter → 5 micron main → optional 1 micron
    1 micron: removes Giardia (6-10 μm) and Cryptosporidium (4-6 μm)
    BUT: microorganisms may pass through 1 micron if filter is
    a loose depth medium (need absolute rating, not nominal)
    NSF/ANSI 53 rated for cyst reduction: reliable specification

  REPLACEMENT:
    Replace when pressure drop across filter becomes significant
    or on schedule (typically 3-6 months)
    Saturated filter: can harbor bacterial growth
```

### Activated Carbon Filtration

```
  MECHANISM: ADSORPTION (not absorption)
    Carbon has enormous surface area: 300-1,500 m²/gram
    (tea-cup of carbon has surface area of a football field)
    Organic molecules adsorb (stick) to carbon surface via
    van der Waals forces

  WHAT CARBON REMOVES:
    Chlorine (converts to chloride instantly)
    Chloramines (requires catalytic carbon or longer contact time)
    Taste and odor compounds (geosmin, 2-MIB from algae)
    Volatile organic compounds (benzene, TCE, PCE)
    Some pesticides and herbicides
    Radon (from water; also offgasses from water)
    Dissolved gases contributing to taste

  WHAT CARBON DOES NOT REMOVE:
    Nitrates (ionic, doesn't adsorb)
    Heavy metals (need specialty media or RO)
    Dissolved minerals (hardness, iron, manganese)
    Microorganisms (bacteria, viruses) — NOT a disinfectant
    Fluoride (ions, not organic)

  TYPES:
    GAC (Granular Activated Carbon): loose media; back-washable
    Carbon block: compressed; finer filtration; slower flow;
    more consistent contact time
    Catalytic carbon: modified surface; more effective for
    chloramines (which regular carbon removes slowly)

  CERTIFICATION:
    NSF/ANSI 42: taste, odor, chlorine reduction
    NSF/ANSI 53: health effects (specific contaminants listed)
    Only trust certified products; marketing claims vary widely

  REPLACEMENT:
    Carbon "exhausts" when all surface sites are occupied
    No visual indicator → requires testing or scheduled change
    Recommendation: every 6-12 months or per manufacturer specification
    Exhausted carbon can desorb previously captured compounds
```

### Reverse Osmosis (RO)

```
  PRINCIPLE:
    Semi-permeable membrane (cellulose acetate or TFC polyamide)
    Applied pressure exceeds osmotic pressure
    → Water molecules pass through membrane
    → Dissolved ions, large molecules rejected

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Feed water → [pre-filter] → [RO membrane] → product water   │
  │                                  ↓                           │
  │                           Reject/brine (to drain)            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHAT RO REMOVES:
    TDS (total dissolved solids): 95-99% reduction
    Nitrates: 90%+ reduction
    Lead, arsenic, fluoride: 90%+ reduction
    Most pharmaceuticals and personal care products
    PFAS (per/polyfluoroalkyl substances): 90%+ reduction
    Most dissolved organic compounds
    Microorganisms: 99%+ (but can pass if membrane damaged)

  WHAT RO DOES NOT IMPROVE:
    Some dissolved gasses (CO₂ passes through)
    Cannot remove all dissolved chlorine/chloramines
    (typically require pre-carbon)

  SYSTEM PERFORMANCE:
    Reject ratio: 3-5 gallons wasted per 1 gallon produced
    (400 ml reject per 100 ml product, approximately)
    Permeate water rate: slow; storage tank required
    Volume: typically 2-4 gallon storage tank under sink

  REMINERALIZATION:
    RO water: slightly acidic (CO₂ passes through; no buffering)
    Some people prefer remineralized water (add back Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺)
    Or post-carbon filter restores some mineral
    Debate: slight acidity of pure RO water is not a health
    concern at typical volumes consumed

  NSF/ANSI 58: certification standard for RO systems
  Verify specific contaminant removal claims via certificate
```

### UV Disinfection

```
  MECHANISM:
    UV-C radiation at 254 nm damages DNA of microorganisms
    → DNA cross-linking → cannot replicate → inactivation
    NOT killing in traditional sense: organism cannot reproduce
    No chemical addition; no residual; no taste/odor effect

  WHAT UV TREATS:
    Bacteria: E. coli, Legionella, Salmonella → effectively
    Viruses: enteric viruses inactivated at typical doses
    Protozoa: Giardia, Cryptosporidium → effectively
    IMPORTANT: Crypto is chlorine-resistant; UV is effective

  WHAT UV DOES NOT TREAT:
    Chemical contaminants
    Heavy metals
    Dissolved organic compounds
    Turbidity (cloudy water BLOCKS UV penetration → must pre-filter)

  LOG INACTIVATION:
    4-log (99.99%) inactivation of E. coli: typical at ~40 mJ/cm²
    Cryptosporidium: 3-log at ~12 mJ/cm²
    NSF/ANSI 55: Class A (40 mJ/cm² min, primary disinfection)
                  Class B (16 mJ/cm² min, supplemental)

  REQUIREMENTS:
    Pre-filtration: turbidity <1 NTU; iron <0.3 mg/L
    (coloration and turbidity absorb UV before reaching organisms)
    Regular lamp replacement: UV output degrades over time
    Typical lamp life: ~9,000 hours (1 year continuous)
    Ballast replacement every 2-3 years
```

## Lead in Plumbing

```
  SOURCES OF LEAD IN DRINKING WATER:
    1. Lead service line (from water main to house)
       Installed pre-1986 (Lead Ban Amendment); common in old cities
    2. Lead solder in copper pipe joints
       Used pre-1986; banned since (Safe Drinking Water Act)
    3. Lead-bearing brass fixtures
       Even "lead-free" brass legally allows 0.25% lead (2014 law)
    4. Lead flashing, lead valves in old systems

  FLINT MICHIGAN CRISIS (2014-2019):
    City switched water source from Detroit (Lake Huron) to
    Flint River to save money (April 2014)
    Problem: Flint River water was more corrosive (lower pH,
    higher chloride) than Detroit water
    And: corrosion control (orthophosphate treatment) was NOT added
    Previous Detroit water had built up lead phosphate protective
    coating inside pipes → Flint River water dissolved this coating
    → Lead from service lines + lead solder leached into drinking water
    → Blood lead levels elevated in children → irreversible cognitive damage
    Criminal charges filed; state and federal response delayed;
    lead service line replacement took years and external pressure

  CORROSION CONTROL (Lead and Copper Rule mechanism):
    EPA Lead and Copper Rule (1991, revised 2021):
    Action Level: 15 ppb lead at tap (90th percentile)
    If exceeded → mandatory corrosion control treatment:
      Orthophosphate: reacts with lead → insoluble lead phosphate
      coating on inside of pipe surfaces → lead sealed in
      pH adjustment: alkaline water less corrosive
    Requires monitoring at highest-risk taps (old homes, lead
    service lines) — the "tap sampling protocol"

  LEAD AND COPPER RULE REVISIONS (2021):
    10-year mandatory replacement of all lead service lines
    (both utility-side and customer-side)
    Previously: utilities responsible only for utility-side
    Now: utilities must inventory and replace ALL LSLs

  CONSUMER PROTECTION:
    NSF/ANSI 61: certification for plumbing products in
    contact with drinking water (limits lead leaching)
    NSF/ANSI 53: point-of-use filters certified for lead removal
    At-faucet pitcher/filter: must have NSF/ANSI 53 certification
    for lead specifically (not just carbon filter label)
    Flushing: run cold water 30-60 seconds before drinking
    if pipes have been stagnant (especially in old homes)
```

## Well Water Specifics

```
  TESTING RECOMMENDATIONS:
    Annual (minimum): total coliform bacteria, nitrates
    Initial and after flood/adjacent activity: bacteria + chemistry
    Comprehensive: pH, hardness, iron, manganese, arsenic,
    fluoride, heavy metals, volatile organics, radon

  COMMON WELL WATER CONCERNS BY GEOGRAPHY:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Arsenic: New England, Upper Midwest, mountain West,     │
  │  Southwest (naturally occurring in some bedrock)         │
  │  MCL: 10 ppb; exceeds in many wells without awareness    │
  │  Treatment: RO, activated alumina, iron coagulation      │
  │                                                          │
  │  Radon: New England, Appalachians, Southwest (granite    │
  │  rock releases radon into groundwater)                   │
  │  Offgasses in home when water used → lung cancer risk    │
  │  Treatment: aeration (outdoor) or activated carbon       │
  │                                                          │
  │  Iron/Manganese: Very common; causes orange staining     │
  │  (iron), black staining (manganese)                      │
  │  Sulfur smell (hydrogen sulfide): common in anaerobic    │
  │  aquifers; not a health threat at low levels             │
  │  Treatment: oxidation (chlorination or air) + filtration│
  │                                                          │
  │  Nitrates: agricultural areas, near septic systems       │
  │  MCL: 10 mg/L (as N); dangerous for infants <6 months    │
  │  ("blue baby syndrome" — methemoglobinemia)              │
  │  Treatment: RO, ion exchange                             │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SHOCK CHLORINATION:
    If coliform bacteria found → shock the well
    Add concentrated chlorine (unscented bleach, 1-2 gallons
    into 200 ft well) → let sit 24 hours → flush completely
    Retest before resuming use
    Repeat if coliform persists → find contamination source
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Scale in water heater, fixtures | Ion-exchange water softener (confirmed by hardness test >7 GPG) |
| Scale only, no sodium concern | TAC template crystallization (Nuvo, SoftPro TAC) |
| Chlorine taste and odor | Whole-house GAC filter or under-sink carbon block |
| Nitrates in well water (infants) | Under-sink RO system |
| Lead concern, old home | NSF/ANSI 53 certified point-of-use filter for lead |
| Coliform bacteria in well | UV disinfection (Class A, post-sediment/carbon filter) |
| Complete chemical reduction at tap | Under-sink RO + post-carbon + remineralization |
| Iron staining from well | Iron removal filter (Birm, Greensand) + pre-oxidation |
| Arsenic in well water | RO or activated alumina filter |

## Common Confusion Points

**Soft water is more corrosive, not less**: Hard water deposits a protective carbonate scale inside pipes. Soft water (or softened water with Na⁺) lacks this protective mineral layer → more corrosive to metals. Never route softened water through lead service lines — it accelerates lead leaching.

**RO waste water is normal and expected**: A 4:1 reject ratio is typical and by design. The concentrated reject water cannot be avoided (entropy of the separation process). Low-reject RO systems exist (~2:1) but at higher cost. In drought areas, consider piping reject water to laundry or irrigation rather than drain.

**Carbon filters don't remove bacteria**: Activated carbon removes organic chemicals and chlorine. It provides no disinfection. In fact, an exhausted or wet carbon filter can harbor bacteria. Never assume carbon-filtered water is microbiologically safe unless combined with UV or other disinfection.

**Water softeners don't filter water**: Ion exchange removes hardness ions and replaces with sodium. It does not remove bacteria, viruses, lead, nitrates, organic chemicals, or sediment. Softening and filtration are separate treatment steps often used together but with different purposes.

**NSF/ANSI certification numbers mean specific things**: NSF 42 = aesthetic effects (taste/odor/chlorine). NSF 53 = health effects (specific contaminants certified on product label). NSF 58 = RO. NSF 55 = UV. A filter with NSF 42 certification does NOT certify lead removal unless it also has NSF 53 with lead listed — check the specific certificate, not just "NSF certified."
