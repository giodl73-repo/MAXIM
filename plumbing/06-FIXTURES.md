# Fixtures

## Toilets, Faucets, Showers — Mechanisms, Water Efficiency, Code

## The Big Picture

```
FIXTURE SELECTION MATRIX:

  Performance criteria:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  WATER EFFICIENCY: GPF (toilet), GPM (faucet/shower)         │
  │  DURABILITY: cartridge type, valve material, finish          │

  │  SCALD SAFETY: pressure-balance vs thermostatic valves       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE FEDERAL BASELINES:
    Toilet:       1.6 GPF (Energy Policy Act 1994)
    Faucet:       2.2 GPM (EPA WaterSense ≤1.5 GPM)
    Shower:       2.5 GPM (EPACT 1992)
    California:   stricter on all three

  EFFICIENCY CERTIFICATION:
    WaterSense (EPA): ≤20% more efficient than standard
    ENERGY STAR: for water heaters, some appliances
    ANSI/ASME standards: performance and safety
```

## Toilets

### Anatomy and How a Toilet Works

```
  TANK COMPONENTS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    TANK                                      │
  │                                                              │
  │  FILL VALVE ──────────────────────────────────────────────   │
  │  (float-cup type)    Float rises as tank fills → valve     │
  │                      closes. Float drops when flushed →    │
  │                      valve opens → tank refills.           │
  │                                                              │
  │  OVERFLOW TUBE ───────────────────────────────────────────   │
  │  (vertical tube)     Water level must stay BELOW top.      │
  │                      If fill valve fails open: water runs  │
  │                      into overflow tube → into bowl → drain  │
  │                      (prevents tank overflow to floor)     │
  │                                                              │
  │  FLAPPER ─────────────────────────────────────────────────   │
  │  (rubber seal at    Lifts via chain when handle pulled.    │
  │   flush valve)       Water rushes from tank to bowl.       │
  │                      Drops when tank empties → seals.      │
  │                      Failure mode: hardens, warps, or      │
  │                      deposits prevent sealing → running    │
  │                      water (30,000+ gallons/year wasted)   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE FLUSH SIPHON:
    Bowl has integral siphon trap (S-curve visible from side)
    When tank water enters rapidly → creates siphon in trap →
    bowl contents siphon out completely
    Siphon breaks when bowl empties (air enters) → "gurgle" end
    Trapway size: larger = fewer clogs
    MaP (Maximum Performance) rating: tests mass flush performance
      (grams of solid waste in single flush)
      300g = acceptable; 500g+ = excellent; 1000g = exceptional
```

### Toilet Types

```
  GRAVITY FLUSH (standard):
    Water falls from tank through flush valve by gravity
    Simple, quiet, repairable
    Requires adequate tank volume and height above bowl
    95%+ of residential toilets

  PRESSURE-ASSIST:
    Compressed air inside sealed plastic vessel inside tank
    Air pressurized by incoming water (incoming 25+ PSI)
    Flush: valve opens → compressed air expels water forcefully
    Benefits: more forceful flush; fewer clogs; reduces sweating
    Drawbacks: louder (whoosh); $100-200 more expensive;
               more complex to service
    Common: commercial restrooms; master baths in homes

  DUAL FLUSH:
    Two buttons: 0.8 GPF (liquid waste) / 1.6 GPF (solid)
    Effective water saving if users actually use correctly
    Single flush handle versions also available
    Popular: Australia, Europe; growing US adoption

  MACERATING / UP-FLUSH (Saniflo brand):
    Pump with grinding mechanism
    Can install toilet ANYWHERE below slab without breaking concrete
    Grinds solid waste → pumps up and through 3/4" pipe
    Limitations: can't handle feminine products or wipes;
    pump motor life ~8-10 years; pump replacement cost
    Use case: basement bathroom addition

  FLUSHOMETER (COMMERCIAL, TANKLESS):
    Valve connects directly to water supply
    No tank; requires dedicated high-pressure, high-flow supply
    Minimum: 1" supply pipe at 25+ PSI residual
    Rapid, forceful flush; reset quickly for high-use applications
    All commercial building restrooms; not for residential
    (requires supply pressure/volume residential systems can't provide)
```

### Water Efficiency

```
  REGULATORY TIMELINE:
    Pre-1994: 3.5-7 GPF (older toilets)
    1994: Federal max 1.6 GPF (EPACT 1992 mandate)
    California: 1.28 GPF standard (adopted by others)
    WaterSense: ≤1.28 GPF with MaP ≥350 grams

  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  EFFICIENCY TIER    │ GPF   │ Annual water (3 flushes/day)│
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Pre-1994 old       │ 3.5+  │ ~3,800 gallons/yr          │
  │  Federal standard   │ 1.6   │ ~1,750 gallons/yr          │
  │  WaterSense/Calif.  │ 1.28  │ ~1,400 gallons/yr          │
  │  High-efficiency    │ 1.0   │ ~1,095 gallons/yr          │
  │  Ultra-high eff.    │ 0.8   │ ~875 gallons/yr            │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  REBATE PROGRAMS:
    Most water utilities offer rebates for WaterSense certified
    toilet replacements ($50-150 per toilet)
    Replacing old 3.5 GPF toilet: saves ~2,000 gal/yr/person
    ROI: typically <2 years including purchase cost
```

### Common Toilet Repairs

```
  RUNNING TOILET (water runs continuously into bowl):
    CAUSE 1: Flapper not sealing
      → Test: food coloring in tank; if appears in bowl
        without flushing = flapper leak
      → Fix: replace flapper ($5-10; 15 minutes)
    CAUSE 2: Water level above overflow tube
      → Water spills over overflow tube into bowl
      → Fix: adjust float or fill valve height
    CAUSE 3: Fill valve not shutting off
      → Fix: replace fill valve ($15-25)

  TOILET WON'T FLUSH COMPLETELY:
    Low water level in tank (float adjusted too low)
    Flapper closes too early (chain too short or heavy flapper)
    Weak flush: check for partial clog in trap or drain

  TOILET ROCKING:
    Wax ring failed or floor flange height wrong
    → Toilet base not sealed → potential sewer gas and water damage
    Fix: reset toilet on new wax ring; shim base if floor uneven
```

## Faucets

### Cartridge Mechanisms

```
  FOUR TYPES: different wear mechanisms, different repairs

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  COMPRESSION (two-handle, traditional):                      │
  │    Rubber washer screws down onto brass seat                 │
  │    Turn handle → shaft threads down → washer compresses      │
  │    Drip = seat wear or washer wear                           │
  │    Repair: replace washer OR replace seat                    │
  │    Serviceable; still found in 50+ year old homes; dated   │
  │                                                              │
  │  BALL (single handle, ball-type by Delta/Price Pfister):   │
  │    Rotating stainless ball with three holes (hot, cold,    │
  │    outlet) in spring-loaded rubber seats                     │
  │    Handle moves ball → aligns holes → mixes temperature    │
  │    Failure: worn springs, seats, O-rings                   │
  │    Repair: replacement ball + springs + seats kit (~$20)   │
  │    Characteristic: single-handle on circular base          │
  │                                                              │
  │  CARTRIDGE (single or double handle):                        │
  │    Plastic or brass cartridge with rubber seals              │
  │    Cartridge slides in/out of faucet body                  │
  │    Repair: replace entire cartridge as one unit            │
  │    Most repairable single-cartridge in US:                 │
  │    Moen 1225/1222 cartridge (pull handle style)            │
  │    → available everywhere; $20-30; 30-minute repair        │
  │    Delta, Kohler have own cartridge families                 │
  │                                                              │
  │  CERAMIC DISC (quarter-turn):                                │
  │    Two machined ceramic discs — flat to <0.001" tolerance  │
  │    Discs rotate relative to each other (1/4 turn on/off)  │
  │    Extremely durable: essentially no moving seal to wear     │
  │    Failure mode: grit/debris scratches lapped faces        │
  │    No lubrication needed; no drip expected for 10+ years   │
  │    Used by: Kohler, Hans Grohe, most European brands       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Flow Rates

```
  FEDERAL STANDARD: 2.2 GPM at 60 PSI
  WATERSENSE CERTIFIED: ≤1.5 GPM (lavatory faucets)

  HOW AERATORS WORK:
    Aerator screen at faucet outlet mixes air with water stream
    → full-feeling stream at lower flow rate
    → 2.2 GPM flow feels like 3.0 GPM visually
    Low-flow aerators: 0.5-1.0 GPM (public restrooms)
    Standard residential: 1.5-2.2 GPM
    Kitchen: 1.8-2.2 GPM (need more for filling pots)

  AERATOR MAINTENANCE:
    Mineral deposits clog aerator over time → reduced flow
    Remove, soak in white vinegar, reinstall
    Or replace (very cheap; threaded onto faucet end)
```

### Faucet Finishes

```
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Finish           │ How Made          │ Durability        │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Chrome           │ Electroplated Cr  │ Excellent if thick│
  │  Brushed nickel   │ Electroplated Ni  │ Good-excellent    │
  │                   │ or PVD            │                   │
  │  Oil-rubbed bronze│ Lacquer over brass│ POOR: lacquer     │
  │                   │ (or PVD if good)  │ wears; PVD lasts  │
  │  Polished brass   │ Lacquered brass   │ Lacquer wears;    │
  │                   │                   │ needs polishing   │
  │  Matte black      │ Paint, PVD, or    │ Varies widely;    │
  │                   │ powder coat       │ PVD = best        │
  │  Brushed gold     │ PVD gold          │ Excellent (PVD)   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PVD (Physical Vapor Deposition):
    Vacuum process deposits thin metallic or ceramic film
    Extremely hard; scratch-resistant; no lacquer to wear off
    Premium faucets specify "PVD finish" → significantly longer life
    vs lacquered versions of same appearance

  OIL-RUBBED BRONZE WARNING:
    Most "oil-rubbed bronze" faucets = dark lacquer over brass
    Lacquer wears at high-use points (base of spout, handles)
    → Brass visible underneath within 3-5 years
    True PVD oil-rubbed bronze = more durable (and more expensive)
    Ask specifically "is this PVD or lacquered?"
```

## Shower Valves: The Scald Safety Standard

```
  THE SCALD PROBLEM:
    Toilet flushes on cold supply → cold pressure drops
    Hot/cold mixing at shower suddenly imbalanced
    → temperature spikes toward hot → SCALD
    Children and elderly at highest risk
    Scald injuries: ~3,800 emergency visits/year US (CPSC data)

  PRESSURE-BALANCE VALVE (ASSE 1016):
    Required for new shower installations in US code
    (IRC P2708.3 — adopted since 1992-era)

    MECHANISM:
      Sensing spool inside valve detects pressure imbalance
      between hot and cold supplies
      Spool restricts the higher-pressure side
      → maintains hot/cold RATIO constant even if pressure changes

    WHAT IT DOES:
      If toilet flushes (cold pressure drops):
      → Spool shifts → restricts hot → ratio maintained → ±3°F
      → User stays comfortable

    WHAT IT DOES NOT DO:
      Does not control temperature if supply temperature changes
      (if hot water heater runs out → shower goes cold regardless)
      Does not provide multiple body spray controls

    EXAMPLES: Moen Posi-Temp, Delta Monitor
    ONE HANDLE: one lever controls both volume and temp

  THERMOSTATIC SHOWER VALVE (premium):
    Wax motor thermostatic element responds to actual mixed
    water temperature
    Maintains SET TEMPERATURE regardless of:
      → Supply pressure changes
      → Supply temperature changes
    Separate controls: thermostatic (temperature, set once)
                       volume (flow, adjust for preference)
    For multi-body shower systems with multiple outlets
    European standard; growing US adoption
    More precise scald protection than pressure-balance

  COMPARISON:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Feature           │ Pressure-Balance  │ Thermostatic     │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Scald from flush  │ Prevents ✅       │ Prevents ✅     │
  │  Temp from cold WH │ No protection ⚠️  │ Prevents ✅     │
  │  Multiple outlets  │ No               │ Yes             │
  │  Cost              │ $50-200          │ $200-800+       │
  │  Code minimum      │ Yes ✅           │ More than min   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ANTI-SCALD TEMPERATURE LIMIT STOP:
    Rotational limit stop built into most shower valves
    Adjustable stop prevents handle from rotating to full hot
    → Set max temperature at installation
    Set at ~110-115°F for homes with children or elderly
    Code-accessible via plate removal; no licensed plumber needed
```

## Accessibility (ADA)

```
  ADA STANDARDS FOR ACCESSIBLE DESIGN (2010 ADA Standards):

  CONTROLS:
    Lever handles (not round knobs): required for accessible
    → One-handed operable with closed fist or elbow
    Touchless/electronic: acceptable
    Round knobs: not ADA compliant for accessible fixtures

  LAVATORIES (ACCESSIBLE):
    Rim height: ≤34" AFF (Above Finished Floor)
    Knee clearance: 27" high × 30" wide × 19" deep under
    (for wheelchair roll-under access)
    Insulated pipes under sink (prevent burns to legs with
    limited sensation)
    Deck-mounted faucets: reach range 44-48" from clear floor

  TOILETS (ACCESSIBLE):
    Seat height: 17"-19" AFF (vs standard 14-17")
    Also called "comfort height" or "ADA height"
    Side transfer space: 60" min clear floor space beside toilet
    Grab bars: 42" side bar + 36" rear bar at specific locations
    Flush control: on open (transfer) side of toilet
    Flush mechanism: one-handed operable

  SHOWERS (ACCESSIBLE):
    Transfer shower: 36" × 36" minimum with seat
    Roll-in shower: 36" × 60" minimum, no threshold, fold-down seat
    Shower head: handheld on 60" min slide bar or adjustable mount

  PRACTICAL NOTE:
    "Comfort height" toilets (17-19") are popular broadly
    regardless of ADA need — easier for most adults
    Standard height (14-17"): traditional dimension
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Specification |
|---|---|
| Toilet for basement addition | Macerating/up-flush (Saniflo) |
| Low water use, good flush | WaterSense certified ≤1.28 GPF + MaP ≥500g |
| Commercial restroom toilet | Flushometer tankless (1" supply, 25 PSI min) |
| Leaking faucet (Moen pull-handle) | Replace 1225/1222 cartridge |
| Never dripping faucet | Ceramic disc cartridge (quarter-turn) |
| Shower valve code minimum | Pressure-balance ASSE 1016 |
| Multiple body sprays, scald protection | Thermostatic valve (separate temp + volume) |
| Home with elderly or children | Set anti-scald limit stop to ≤110°F |
| ADA toilet height | 17-19" AFF (comfort/chair height) |
| Faucet that won't tarnish/peel | PVD finish (not lacquered "oil rubbed bronze") |

## Common Confusion Points

**Running toilet diagnosis is sequenced**: The overflow tube being visible above the water line, vs. the flapper lifting the water level, vs. the fill valve not closing — are three different problems with different fixes. Check water level relative to overflow tube first (simplest), then flapper dye test, then fill valve.

**Pressure-balance valves don't control temperature**: They maintain the mixing ratio when pressure changes. If your hot water runs out, a pressure-balance valve doesn't help — it still sends you cold water at the same ratio. Thermostatic valves actually regulate temperature.

**Comfort height toilets are not just for disabled users**: The 17-19" seat height (ADA specification) is also preferred by most adults. It allows sitting down and rising with less joint stress. Many plumbers install comfort height as the default. The standard 14-17" is the older, lower standard.

**"Cartridge replacement" varies entirely by brand**: A Moen 1225 cartridge cannot be used in a Delta or Kohler faucet. Cartridges are brand-specific and sometimes model-specific within a brand. Identify the brand and model before ordering. Most major brands have cross-reference charts. Bring the old cartridge to the plumbing supply house.

**The anti-scald stop is user-adjustable**: Most shower valves have a limit stop that restricts maximum rotation toward hot. This is accessible by removing the handle and trim plate. It should be set at installation and checked periodically (especially when water heater temperature changes). No licensed plumber is required for this adjustment.
