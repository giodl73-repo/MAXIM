# 09 — Codes & Standards

## IRC, IPC, UPC, Permits, Sizing Tables

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Code framework**: building codes are model codes adopted (with amendments) by states and municipalities; nobody follows the model code exactly — local amendments are substantial and mandatory; the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) = local building/plumbing department; AHJ interpretation is final within their jurisdiction; never assume code book is the answer — call the inspector
- **International Plumbing Code (IPC)**: published by ICC (International Code Council); used in most Eastern/Southern/Midwestern US; same organization publishes IRC (residential) and IBC (commercial); updated every 3 years; states/localities adopt on lag (many still on 2018 or 2021 cycle in 2024)
- **Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC)**: published by IAPMO (International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials); used primarily in Western US (California, Washington, Oregon, Nevada, Arizona, Hawaii); same plumbing principles, different organization of rules and some different technical requirements; California Plumbing Code = modified UPC
- **International Residential Code (IRC) Chapter 25**: plumbing section of residential code; covers one/two family dwellings; references IPC for some topics; fixture requirements, water supply, DWV sizing
- **Permit process**: required for most new plumbing installations and major repairs (new fixtures, water heater replacement in many jurisdictions, sewer lateral work, gas additions); fee based on work value or flat rate; plans may be required for complex systems; rough-in inspection (pipes installed, not covered): inspector checks materials, supports, joints, trap/vent arrangement; pressure test (supply piped, capped, pressurized with water or air); final inspection (fixtures installed, covers on, trim complete); Certificate of Occupancy requires approved final inspections
- **Pipe sizing — supply**: Hunter's Method (fixture unit method) or direct GPM calculation; each fixture assigned supply fixture unit (SFU) value; total SFU → GPM demand table → pipe diameter based on available pressure and length of run; velocity maximum 8 ft/s to prevent erosion and noise; adequate pressure at worst-case fixture (most remote, highest elevation)
- **Pipe sizing — DWV**: drainage fixture units (DFU) assigned per fixture (different from SFU); sum DFUs → minimum horizontal drain diameter; vertical stack has higher DFU capacity than horizontal; vent pipe sizing based on drain pipe diameter and developed length; 4" stack can carry up to 500 DFU in some configurations
- **Water heater requirements**: temperature-pressure relief valve (T&P valve) mandatory — resets at 150 PSI or 210°F; discharge pipe from T&P: must not be trapped, must terminate visible (not into wall), within 6" of floor or over drain; seismic strapping required in Zones 3/4 (California and Pacific); thermal expansion tank required in closed systems; first-hour rating vs peak demand analysis for sizing
- **Backflow prevention**: cross-connection control essential; reduced pressure zone (RPZ) backflow preventer for high-hazard connections (irrigation systems, commercial kitchen connections to potable water); pressure vacuum breaker (PVB) for lower-risk (hose connections, irrigation without chemicals); double-check valve assembly (DCVA) for low-hazard; annual testing required for commercial/irrigation RPZs; testable backflow preventers require licensed tester; Watts, Febco as common manufacturers
- **Common code violations found at inspection**: missing or improperly oriented P-trap (S-trap), missing vent (AAV not approved in jurisdiction), improper drain slope (negative slope or too steep), unsupported pipe (hangers too far apart — copper 6', PVC 4', PEX 32" horizontal), improper materials (CPVC cemented with PVC cement), cross-connections (potable/non-potable), missing T&P discharge pipe, non-code-listed materials in new construction
