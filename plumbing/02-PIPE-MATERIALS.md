### Corrosion Vulnerabilities

```
  PITTING CORROSION (Pinhole Leaks):
    Soft water / low pH water attacks copper oxide passive layer
    Most common: Pacific Northwest, Arizona
    CO₂ dissolved in groundwater → carbonic acid → pH drops
    Prevention: pH adjustment, zinc orthophosphate dosing
    Recognition: small blue-green stains on copper pipe exterior;
                pinhole with green/blue mineral deposit around it
    Solution: switch to PEX, install pH neutralizer, or
              raise hot water heater temperature (bacteria control
              may conflict with scald prevention — balance required)

  FORMICARY CORROSION (Ant-Nest Pitting):
    Rare; from organic compounds (formaldehyde, acetaldehyde)
    Pattern: network of fine branching channels under examination
    Sources: off-gassing building materials, formaldehyde-based products
    Appears even in low-pH areas; requires organic acid presence

  GALVANIC CORROSION:
    Copper (noble metal) + galvanized steel (zinc, less noble)
    Zinc sacrificially corrodes → galvanized pipe fails faster
    Prevention: dielectric union (plastic isolation fitting) at joint
    Cast iron and copper: copper is cathodic, iron is anodic → iron corrodes
    Rule: put copper downstream (cathodic), iron/galv upstream (anodic)
          — or use dielectric union

  DEZINCIFICATION (brass fittings):
    In soft/low-pH water, zinc leaches from brass fittings
    → Spongy, copper-colored dezincified fitting
    Prevention: dezincification-resistant (DZR) brass — alloy with 70% Cu
    DZR fittings marked DZR or DR; required in aggressive water areas

  LEAD SOLDER (pre-1986):
    If building pre-1986: copper joints may have 50/50 lead-tin solder
    Lead leaches into water, especially hot water / first-draw
    EPA action level: 15 ppb lead in tap water
    Testing: NSF-certified labs; first-draw sample
    Mitigation: flush taps before drinking, use filter (NSF 53 certified),
                replace solder joints, or replace with PEX
```

### Joining Copper

```
  SWEAT SOLDER (torch soldering):
    Most common; requires torch (MAPP gas or propane)
    Lead-free solder only (law since 1986; stricter 2014)
    Flux: water-soluble preferred (no corrosive residue)
    Key technique: heat FITTING not the pipe; apply solder to cool side
                   capillary action pulls molten solder into joint
    DRY PIPE REQUIREMENT: residual water vaporizes → steam → prevents
                          solder from drawing in → cold joint = leak
    Drain/blow out water; use bread plug in cold pipe if needed

  PRESS-FIT (ProPress, Viega MegaPress):
    No flame; EPDM O-ring compressed by hydraulic press tool
    Code-accepted in most jurisdictions for copper, SS, CS
    "Smart Connect" technology: unpressed fitting leaks slightly
    → leak-before-press identifies unfirmed joints
    Higher fitting cost; tool available for rent
    Application: areas where open flame is prohibited (tenant-occupied,
                 near insulation, retrofit work)

  COMPRESSION:
    Brass ferrule + nut; no soldering
    Supply shutoffs, angle stops, appliance connections
    Not for long runs (too many potential leak points per joint)
    Reusable: remove nut, cut pipe past ferrule, reuse new ferrule
```

### PEX-a Expansion Fitting System

```
  UNIQUE TO PEX-a:
    1. Expansion ring (PEX-a ring or EVOH reinforcement ring)
       slid onto pipe end
    2. Expansion tool opens pipe end (radially expands it)
    3. Insert brass/poly fitting into expanded end
    4. Ring + pipe contract around fitting (memory effect)
    5. Permanently sealed — pipe tighter over time with temperature

  ADVANTAGES:
    Full-bore fitting (fitting ID = pipe ID → maximum flow)
    No protrusion inside pipe → no turbulence
    Most durable long-term joint type
    Can repair kinked PEX-a with heat gun (straightens and recovers)

  DISADVANTAGES:
    Requires PEX-a pipe specifically
    Expansion tool is expensive (but rentable)
    Cannot use expansion fittings on PEX-b or -c
```

### PEX Limitations

```
  UV DEGRADATION:
    PEX degrades rapidly in direct sunlight
    UV breaks polyethylene chain → brittleness within months
    Must be protected: pipe insulation, sleeve, or paint if outdoor
    Underground with sleeve: acceptable
    Under slab: requires sleeve for protection

  RECIRCULATION SYSTEMS:
    Some concern about PEX in hot water recirculation loops
    Long-term exposure to oxygen-rich hot water may degrade
    EVOH oxygen barrier PEX (most brand-name PEX) reduces this
    Ask manufacturer for recirculation system ratings

  WATER TASTE/ODOR:
    New PEX may impart slight taste/odor to water, especially warm
    Flush thoroughly before use; decreases over time
    Chlorinated water can degrade PEX over decades in some studies

  NOT FOR BURIED BURIAL without sleeve:
    Rodents chew PEX
    Soil movement can nick/kink
    Sleeve provides protection and allows future replacement
```

## PVC and CPVC

```
PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride):
  White or gray; rigid

  Schedule 40 (most common):
    DWV applications: 1.5" to 6"+ drain/waste/vent
    Cold water supply (SDR-pressure rated): some AHJ accept, some don't
    NOT for hot water (softens >140°F / 60°C)
    Solvent welded

  Schedule 80:
    Thicker wall; higher pressure rating; gray color
    Irrigation, chemical applications
    Rarely needed in residential plumbing

  Solvent welding:
    Chemical fusion — not glue, not adhesive
    Purple primer (IPS Purple Primer or equivalent): required by code
    in most jurisdictions for pressure PVC
    PVC cement (clear or grey) specific to PVC — not interchangeable
    with CPVC cement

CPVC (Chlorinated Polyvinyl Chloride):
  Cream/tan color
  Chlorine addition to PVC backbone → higher temperature rating
  Rated to 200°F (93°C) @ 100 PSI (vs PVC: 140°F max)
  Used for: hot and cold water supply; viable copper alternative
  CPVC cement: different chemistry from PVC cement
               DO NOT use PVC cement on CPVC — will fail
  Code: accepted in all major code jurisdictions
  Brittleness: more brittle than copper; vulnerable to impact,
               especially near freezing
  CRITICAL: do not expose CPVC to petroleum products
            (pipe dope, cutting oil, some PVC cement) — will crack
```

## Cast Iron

```
  STANDARD: CISPI 301 (Hubless Cast Iron Soil Pipe Institute)
             ASTM A888

  JOIN METHODS:
    Hub-and-spigot (traditional):
      Bell/hub on one end, plain/spigot other
      Oakum packing + molten lead caulk (old)
      Rubber compression joint (modern)
    No-hub (modern):
      Rubber gasket + stainless band clamp (Clamp-All, Fernco)
      No hot lead → safer, faster installation

  SOUND DAMPING:
    Cast iron transmits less drain noise than PVC
    Code may require or AHJ may require in multi-family residential,
    hotel, or commercial applications
    IBC (International Building Code) references CI for some occupancies

  WEIGHT:
    3" no-hub CI: ~3.3 lbs/foot vs PVC ~0.7 lbs/foot
    Labor cost much higher than PVC
    Structural support critical (CI must be supported every 5-10 feet)
```

## Galvanized Steel (Legacy — Do Not Install New)

```
  HISTORY: standard residential supply ~1900–1970s
  Hot-dip galvanized (zinc coating on carbon steel pipe)

  FAILURE MODE:
    Zinc coating corrodes from inside out over 40-70 years
    Corrosion products (rust flakes) restrict flow
    Eventually: pinhole leaks; pressure drops significantly
    Galvanized at end of life: flow from 3/4" pipe = flow of 1/4" pipe
    (interior deposits narrow effective bore)

  DIAGNOSIS:
    Low water pressure (especially upstairs/end-of-run)
    Rusty orange water at first draw
    Physical inspection: threaded joint discoloration, corrosion crust

  REPLACEMENT:
    Replace with copper or PEX
    Transition: dielectric union at transition from new copper/PEX
                to any remaining galvanized (prevent galvanic acceleration)
    Cannot directly join copper to galvanized (galvanic cell)
```

## Gas Piping Materials

```
BLACK IRON (Carbon Steel):
  Traditional gas piping; threaded NPT joints
  NOT galvanized (galvanized zinc can flake into gas appliances)
  Thread sealant: yellow PTFE tape (thicker than white, gas-specific)
                  + pipe dope compound approved for gas (many codes)
  Sizes: typically 1/2" to 2" residential
  Durable but: labor-intensive threading, corrosion at outdoor/damp

CSST (Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing):
  Flexible stainless steel tubing in yellow polyethylene jacket
  Brands: Gastite (Ward, now Tiemco), TracPipe (Omega Flex)
  Fittings: manifold at meter → flexible run to each appliance
  Advantages: fewer joints (routes without fittings), faster install
  BONDING REQUIREMENT CRITICAL:
    CSST is susceptible to arc damage from lightning-induced surge
    Without bonding: arc can perforate CSST → gas leak → fire
    NFPA 54/IRC: bond CSST to electrical grounding system
    Some CSST brands (Wardflex, Counterstrike) have arc-resistant jacket
    → still recommend bonding but lower risk
  Code: accepted in all major code jurisdictions with proper bonding
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Application | My pick | Why |
|---|---|---|
| New house, all supply | PEX-a or PEX-b | Fast, flexible, freeze-resistant |
| Retrofit, minimal torch | Push-fit (SharkBite) | No tools, no flame |
| Where code demands copper | Copper Type L | Universal acceptance |
| Hot supply, aggressive water | CPVC or Copper K | Both rated appropriately |
| Residential DWV | PVC or ABS | Cheap, easy, code-compliant |
| Hotel/condo DWV | Cast iron | Sound control |
| Gas distribution | CSST (bonded) or black iron | CSST = faster install |
| Underground gas service | Copper K or PE gas pipe | Flexibility + corrosion resistance |

## Common Confusion Points

**PVC and CPVC require different cement**: Using PVC cement on CPVC is a code violation and will fail — the solvent chemistry doesn't dissolve CPVC properly. The cement tube color helps: CPVC cement is typically orange or yellow; PVC is grey, clear, or blue. Read the label.

**Type L vs Type M copper**: Many plumbers use Type M to save money; many inspectors accept it. Some jurisdictions require Type L for all supply or at least for underground. Type L has 40% thicker wall than Type M at 1/2" — meaningfully longer service life in aggressive water chemistry.

**PEX expansion fittings (Uponor) only work on PEX-a**: The memory effect that contracts the expanded pipe end requires the high cross-link density of PEX-a. If you buy PEX-b or PEX-c, you need crimp or clamp fittings. Check the pipe marking.

**CSST bonding is mandatory, not optional**: Lightning-induced current can arc through CSST jacket, perforate it, and ignite gas. This has caused fires and fatalities. Bonding to the electrical grounding system provides a low-impedance path for fault current. This is not a suggestion — it's a code requirement.
