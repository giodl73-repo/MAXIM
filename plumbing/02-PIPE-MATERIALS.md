# 02 — Pipe Materials

## Copper, PEX, PVC, CPVC, Cast Iron — Properties, Ratings, Applications

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Selection matrix: application (supply cold/hot, DWV, gas, radiant) × environment (buried, interior, exterior, aggressive water chemistry) × code acceptance × cost × ease of installation
- **Copper**:
  - Type K (thickest wall): underground direct burial, gas service; green stripe or no stripe
  - Type L (medium wall): above-ground interior, some outdoor; blue stripe; standard residential
  - Type M (thinnest wall): interior only, some jurisdictions restrict; red stripe; cheaper
  - Type DWV: thinnest, drain/waste/vent only (not pressure service); yellow stripe
  - Joining: sweat/solder (most common), compression, flare, press-fit (ProPress), push-fit; lead-free solder only (since 1986 federal law, 2014 stricter "lead-free" definition ≤0.25% weighted average)
  - Corrosion vulnerabilities: dezincification (brass fittings in soft water), pinhole pitting (soft/low-pH water, particularly PNW), formicary corrosion (ant-nest pattern, rare — from formaldehyde vapor in buildings), galvanic corrosion at dissimilar metal connections (must use dielectric union between copper and galvanized steel)
- **PEX** (cross-linked polyethylene):
  - PEX-a: peroxide cross-linking (Engel method); most flexible; expansion fitting method (PEX-a expands, slide fitting in, ring contracts); can be heat-repaired; Uponor brand; highest cost
  - PEX-b: electron beam or silane cross-linking; slightly stiffer; crimp rings or clamp rings; most widely available; moderate cost
  - PEX-c: electron beam cross-linking, different process; less common
  - Not suitable: exterior UV exposure (degrades in sunlight — must be insulated or covered), burial without sleeve, recirculating hot water lines (some concerns about taste/odor in static hot lines)
  - Temperature rating: typically 200°F @ 80 PSI (radiant use OK); cold climate freeze resistance is an advantage
- **PVC** (polyvinyl chloride):
  - Schedule 40: most common DWV (3/4" to 6" and larger); solvent welded; white; not for hot water (softens >140°F); cold water supply in some applications (SDR-rated pipe)
  - CPVC (chlorinated PVC): hot and cold water supply; amber/tan color; rated to ~200°F; solvent welded with CPVC-specific cement; competitor to copper for supply; brittle — must not use petroleum-based pipe dope or products containing petroleum distillates; more crack-prone than copper
  - Not UV stable (outdoor use requires paint or sleeve)
- **ABS** (acrylonitrile butadiene styrene): black DWV pipe; used in Western US primarily; slightly more flexible than PVC; solvent welded with ABS cement; cannot mix ABS and PVC without transition cement; older ABS (pre-1990s) may have product liability issues (certain manufacturers' pipe failed)
- **Cast iron** (CI): hubless or hub-and-spigot; DWV only (not supply); heavy (labor cost); excellent sound/vibration damping (vs PVC which transmits drain sounds); required or preferred in high-rise multi-family for acoustic reasons; no-hub clamp couplings for modern installation; CISPI 301 standard
- **Galvanized steel**: legacy residential supply; zinc coating corrodes from inside out over 40-70 years → rust flakes, reduced flow, pinhole leaks; not installed new; replace when encountered in renovation; compatible with copper only via dielectric union
- **CSST** (corrugated stainless steel tubing): flexible gas supply (natural gas, propane); Gastite, TracPipe brands; routing around obstacles without multiple fittings; must be bonded/grounded (lightning-induced arc vulnerability); different installation requirements than black iron; faster installation; accepted by most gas codes
- **Black iron** (carbon steel, threaded): traditional gas piping; very durable; requires thread compounds (Teflon tape alone inadequate for gas in many codes); galvanic coating absent (not galvanized — galvanized pipe not used for gas, zinc can flake into gas appliances); typically 1/8" to 1-1/4" residential
