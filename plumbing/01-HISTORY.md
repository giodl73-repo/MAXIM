# Plumbing History

## Lead Pipes to PEX: 4,000 Years of Water Management

```
PLUMBING TIMELINE
=================

~1700 BCE  Minoan Crete — Knossos: terracotta pipe systems,
           pressurized supply, flush toilets
~312 BCE   Rome: Aqua Appia (first Roman aqueduct)
~100 CE    Rome: 11 aqueducts, ~300 million gallons/day to Rome
~100–400 CE Roman peak: lead pipe distribution, public baths,
           fountains, private supply for wealthy
~400–1800  Medieval regression: cesspits, wells, open sewers
~1800s    Cast iron for mains; cholera epidemics → reform
1842       Chadwick Report: link between sanitation and disease
1854       Snow's Broad Street pump: epidemiology origin story
1858–1875  Bazalgette builds London sewer system
~1890s    Lead + cast iron dominant
~1950s    Copper replaces lead/iron in residential supply
~1960s    PVC revolution — DWV applications
1986       Lead-free solder mandate (federal)
~2000s    PEX mainstream in residential construction
```

### Roman Aqueducts and Distribution

```
ROMAN AQUEDUCT SYSTEM:

  Mountain source
  (spring or stream)
        ↓
  Open channel (specus) with slight grade
  ≈ 1:200 to 1:5000 gradient — gravity all the way
        ↓
  Settling tanks (piscinae limariae) at intervals
  — suspended particles settle out
        ↓
  Arcaded section for valleys
  (arches keep grade constant)
        ↓
  Castellum divisorium (distribution tank)
  at Rome city boundary
        ↓
  Three outlets:
  1. Public fountains (top level — first to lose flow if pressure drops)
  2. Public baths (middle)
  3. Private homes (bottom — last to lose flow)

  This prioritization was explicit engineering: public welfare > private

  11 major aqueducts serving Rome by 100 CE:
  Total length: ~500 km
  Daily delivery: ~1 million m³ (roughly 250 gallons/person/day —
                  more than many modern cities)
```

### Roman Lead Pipes (*Fistulae Plumbeae*)

```
  Manufacturing: sheet lead wrapped around mandrel, edges beaten together
  Sizes named by weight per foot (quinaria = 5 denarii-width)
  Pipe diameter indicated delivery rate; stamped with owner's name

  LEAD CONTAMINATION CONTROVERSY:
  Arguments for significant contamination:
    - Skeletal analyses show elevated Pb in Roman urban remains
    - Romans also drank wine sweetened with defrutum
      (grape must boiled in lead vessels — extremely high Pb)
    - "Saturnism" (lead poisoning) described in ancient texts

  Arguments against decisive causation:
    - Flow-through of aqueduct water prevented long saturation time
    - Calcium carbonate scale coated inside of pipes (protective)
    - Lead toxicity at those levels likely chronic, not acute

  Consensus: Lead was a real health issue but not necessarily the
  cause of Rome's fall (that's a popular myth)

  ETYMOLOGY:
    plumbum (Latin) → plumber, plumbing, Pb (element symbol)
    "Plumber" = one who works in lead; the trade name persists
    even though modern plumbers rarely touch lead
```

## The Medieval Regression

```
AFTER ROMAN COLLAPSE (400–1800 CE):

  Public infrastructure abandoned
  Cities grew without sewer infrastructure
  Primary sanitation: cesspit privy vaults under/near buildings

  "Gong farmers" (night soil men):
    Emptied cesspits at night (by law, to avoid offense)
    Carted waste to agricultural fields (sold as "night soil")
    Medieval recycling of nutrients — but proximity to wells
    meant groundwater contamination

  London's Fleet River:
    Had become essentially an open sewer by 1300s
    "River of Wells" original name
    Bricked over 1737-1820 as too foul to be visible

  Plague pathways:
    Groundwater from cesspits → wells → drinking water
    → epidemic disease
    Although "Black Death" is primarily rat/flea-borne (Y. pestis),
    many other medieval disease outbreaks were waterborne
```

## The 19th Century Sanitation Revolution

This is arguably the greatest public health achievement in history — not vaccines, not antibiotics, but *civil engineering*.

### John Snow and the Broad Street Pump (1854)

```
  CONTEXT: Victorian London, summer 1854
  Disease: cholera (previously attributed to "miasma" — bad air)

  SNOW'S METHOD:
  Mapped cholera deaths in Soho
  → Dense cluster around Broad Street water pump

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ●●●●   BROAD STREET PUMP                  │
  │  ●●●                                       │
  │  ●●●●●                                     │
  │         ●●  Nearby pump                    │
  │                                             │
  │  (Snow plotted deaths as dots on a map)     │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Key evidence: Brewery nearby (workers drank beer, not pump water)
                — no deaths among brewery workers
                Workhouse with own well — no deaths
                Lion Brewery workers drank stout from their brewery

  Action: Convinced local board to remove pump handle
          Cholera outbreak ended (was already declining, but
          Snow's analysis was correct)

  Significance:
    - Origin of modern epidemiology (the "shoe-leather" method)
    - Proved waterborne disease transmission before germ theory
      (Koch isolated V. cholerae in 1883)
    - Framing for all subsequent public health intervention
```

### Bazalgette's London Sewer System (1858–1875)

```
  TRIGGER: The Great Stink (summer 1858)
    Thames at low tide — dry hot summer
    Untreated sewage concentrated → unbearable odor
    Parliament (riverside) couldn't function
    → Emergency funding for sewer system

  JOSEPH BAZALGETTE (Chief Engineer, Metropolitan Board of Works):
    Designed 82 miles of main interceptor sewers
    1,100+ miles of street sewers
    Brick-lined tunnels running parallel to Thames,
    intercepting sewage before it reached river
    Pumping stations lift sewage to treatment works
    downstream of London → treated, released

  SCALE:
    Bazalgette insisted sewers be built wider than estimated need
    ("We're only going to do this once, so let's do it properly")
    Those sewers still operate today (150+ years)
    Built for Victorian London population → adequate for 2024 population
    (Crossrail tunneling found and had to route around them)

  EFFECT:
    Cholera epidemics in London: 1831 (32,000 deaths), 1848 (62,000),
    1853 (10,738), 1866 (5,596 — partial system), 1872 (none)
    The last major cholera epidemic in London was in areas not yet
    served by the system
```

## Materials History: The Pipe Technology Arc

```
MATERIAL     ERA              JOINING         STATUS
--------     ---              -------         ------
Lead         Ancient–1950s    Hand-formed     Banned/removed
                              lead-soldered
Cast iron    ~1800s–present   Oakum+lead,     DWV still used
(mains)                       no-hub clamp    (noise control)
Galvanized   ~1900–1970s      Threaded        Replaced; corrodes
steel                                          internally
Copper       ~1950s–present   Sweat/solder,   Still used;
                              compression,     standard 60 yrs
                              press-fit
PVC/CPVC     ~1960s–present   Solvent weld    Standard DWV;
                                              CPVC for hot
PEX          ~2000s–present   Crimp, clamp,   Dominant new
                              expansion,       residential
                              push-fit         construction
```

### Copper: The 20th Century Standard

```
  Post-WWII: copper pipe becomes dominant supply material in US
  Reasons:
    - Corrosion-resistant vs galvanized steel
    - Can be soldered easily with acetylene torch
    - Smooth interior → less friction loss than galvanized
    - Long service life (50-100 years typical)

  TYPE CODES:
    K — thickest wall; green; underground/burial; gas service
    L — medium wall; blue; standard residential above-ground
    M — thinnest wall; red; some jurisdictions restrict to interior only
    DWV — thinnest; yellow; drain applications only (not pressure service)

  LEAD-FREE SOLDER MANDATE:
    1986: Banning lead solder in public water systems (SDWA amendment)
    2014: "Lead-free" definition tightened to ≤0.25% weighted average
           (previously "lead-free" could be 8% lead)
    Impact: All copper joints since 1986 should be lead-free;
            pre-1986 joints in older buildings may have leaded solder

  PINHOLE CORROSION:
    Problem: soft/low-pH water attacks copper → pinholes
    Most common in Pacific Northwest, Arizona
    Root cause: CO₂ dissolved in water → carbonic acid
    Solution: pH adjustment, zinc orthophosphate (protective film),
              or switch to PEX
```

### PEX: Cross-Linked Polyethylene

```
  CROSS-LINKING: creates covalent bonds between PE polymer chains
  → PEX cannot melt and re-flow (thermoset behavior)
  → More flexible than rigid plastics
  → Higher temperature/pressure rating than standard PE

  THREE TYPES:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  PEX-a  Peroxide (Engel) method                             │
  │         Most flexible; highest cross-link density           │
  │         Expansion fitting method (unique to PEX-a):         │
  │           expand pipe + ring with tool, insert fitting,     │
  │           ring contracts and locks permanently              │
  │         Can be repaired with heat (kinks straighten)        │
  │         Brand: Uponor/Wirsbo; Highest cost                 │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  PEX-b  Silane (moisture cure) or e-beam method            │
  │         Slightly stiffer; slightly less flexible            │
  │         Crimp or clamp ring fittings                        │
  │         Most widely available; lower cost                   │
  │         Brand: Rehau; widely available at home centers      │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  PEX-c  Electron beam cross-linking, different process      │
  │         Less common; crimp or clamp fittings               │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FREEZE RESISTANCE:
    PEX expands ~8% before failing
    Copper splits, PEX usually survives freeze events
    Still best to insulate, but PEX provides margin of safety

  UV DEGRADATION:
    PEX degrades in direct sunlight (UV breaks PE chains)
    Must be protected when run outside or in exposed areas
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Plumber" comes from lead**: *Plumbum* = Latin for lead. The trade is named after the material, not the skill. Modern plumbers rarely touch lead, but the name persists.

**Snow didn't know about germ theory**: Snow identified the pump as the source in 1854 by epidemiological mapping — before Koch isolated *Vibrio cholerae* in 1883. He proved *how* cholera spread without knowing *what* caused it. This is how epidemiology works.

**Bazalgette's sewers still function**: Over 150 years later, the Victorian interceptor sewers still carry London's sewage. Bazalgette built them 20% wider than needed — that margin is why they haven't needed full replacement.

**PEX types are not interchangeable fittings**: PEX-a expansion fittings (EVOH rings) cannot be used on PEX-b pipe. Crimp fittings work on PEX-b and -c. Clamp (ear/cinch) fittings work on all three types. Push-fit (SharkBite) works on PEX-a/b/c, copper, and CPVC.
