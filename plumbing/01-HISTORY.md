# 01 — Plumbing History

## Lead Pipes to PEX — From Roman Aqueducts to Modern Code

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Etymology: "plumbing" from Latin *plumbum* (lead); lead's malleability made it the premier pipe material for 2,000 years; plumber = worker in lead; the element symbol Pb from plumbum; the word endures long after lead pipes have been replaced
- **Minoan Crete** (~1700 BCE): terracotta pipe systems at Knossos — pressurized supply and drainage; flush toilets; remarkably sophisticated for the era
- **Roman aqueducts and pipe systems**: major aqueducts (Aqua Appia 312 BCE through Aqua Alexandrina 226 CE — 11 major aqueducts serving Rome); aqueducts = gravity-fed from mountain sources, running at slight grade; distribution in Rome via lead pipes (*fistulae plumbeae*); pipe diameter indicated delivery rate and named for owner; lead contamination in Roman water — controversial (historian Scarborough argues significant; Hodge argues flow-through prevented significant saturation); the *castellum divisorium* (distribution tanks), branch pipes serving fountains and wealthy households
- **Medieval period**: loss of Roman infrastructure; cesspit privy vaults under houses; privies projecting over streams; London's Fleet River = open sewer; water carried from wells; plagues partly from contaminated groundwater proximity to cesspits; cesspit emptying ("gong farmers" = night soil men)
- **19th century cholera and the sanitation revolution**: John Snow (1854 Broad Street pump — cholera mapping and source identification, epidemiology origin story); Edwin Chadwick's *Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population* (1842); Joseph Bazalgette's London sewer system (1858-1875) — 1,100 miles of sewers, solved the "Great Stink" (1858); similar reforms in Paris (Haussmann/Belgrand), New York, Chicago; germ theory (Pasteur/Koch 1870s) confirming what sanitary reformers suspected empirically; the sanitation revolution is arguably the greatest public health achievement in history
- **Cast iron era** (1800s-1950s): cast iron pipe for water mains and DWV drainage; heavy, durable, noise-dampening; caulked lead joints (oakum packing + molten lead); still found in older buildings; hub-and-spigot joints; no-hub cast iron (clamp couplings) = modern cast iron installation; still used for DWV in high-rise construction (noise control)
- **Copper** (post-WWII to present): copper tubing became dominant supply material in US ~1950s; Type K (thickest, underground), Type L (standard residential), Type M (thinnest, interior only); soldered/brazed joints; lasted 40+ years typically; corrosion issues in soft-water/low-pH areas (pinhole leaks — particularly problematic in Pacific Northwest US); lead-free solder mandate 1986
- **PVC revolution** (~1960s-present): polyvinyl chloride for cold water supply (SDR-rated) and DWV; CPVC for hot water; lightweight, corrosion-immune, cheap, easy installation (solvent welded); displaced cast iron DWV in residential by 1980s; DWV = ABS (acrylonitrile butadiene styrene, black) or PVC (white)
- **PEX** (cross-linked polyethylene, mainstream ~2000s): flexible tubing allows snake-routing without fittings; freeze resistance (expands rather than splitting); corrosion immune; radiant floor standard; three types: PEX-a (Uponor/Wirsbo — most flexible, cross-linked by peroxide method), PEX-b (Rehau — slightly stiffer, electron beam cross-linking), PEX-c (least common); push-fit, crimp, clamp, and expansion fittings; rapid adoption for remodeling
