# Global Freshwater — Freshwater Scarcity, Virtual Water, Transboundary Rivers, Groundwater Depletion

## The Big Picture

```
+===========================================================================+
|                  GLOBAL FRESHWATER — SUPPLY, DEMAND, AND CRISIS           |
+===========================================================================+
|                                                                           |
|  SUPPLY (fixed on century timescale):                                     |
|  Global renewable freshwater: ~42,750 km³/yr (river discharge)            |
|  Accessible (excluding flood pulse, remote regions): ~12,500 km³/yr       |
|  Geographically concentrated: 60% in 9 countries                         |
|                                                                           |
|  DEMAND (growing):                                                        |
|  Global freshwater withdrawal ~4,000 km³/yr (2020)                       |
|  Agriculture: ~70%; Industry: ~20%; Municipal: ~10%                      |
|  Consumptive use: ~2,500 km³/yr (50% withdrawn, not returned)            |
|                                                                           |
|  TREND:                                                                   |
|  Growing population + urbanization + climate change                      |
|  → demand rising, supply increasingly variable                            |
|  → 4 billion people experience severe water scarcity ≥1 month/year        |
|  → 500 million face year-round severe scarcity                            |
+===========================================================================+
```

## Water Scarcity Concepts and Metrics

```
FALKENMARK WATER STRESS INDICATOR (1989):
  Per capita renewable freshwater availability:
    > 1,700 m³/person/yr: water sufficient
    1,000–1,700 m³/person/yr: water stress
    500–1,000 m³/person/yr: water scarcity
    < 500 m³/person/yr: absolute scarcity

  Intuitive, simple, widely used.
  Limitations: ignores demand level, seasonal variability, water quality,
               groundwater, distribution infrastructure

WATER USE INTENSITY (WEI):
  WEI = total withdrawal / total available renewable water
  >0.20: water stress
  >0.40: severe water stress
  Used by EEA, Eurostat for EU monitoring

FALKENMARK'S BLUE WATER SCARCITY (Hoekstra et al.):
  Monthly water scarcity: demand exceeds supply in specific months
  Accounts for seasonal variability — more realistic than annual averages
  Result: 500 million people face year-round scarcity;
          4 billion face at least 1 month/yr of severe scarcity

WATER POVERTY INDEX (Sullivan, 2002):
  Multi-dimensional: access, resources, use, capacity, environment
  Better for policy (captures access + governance, not just physical availability)

TYPES OF WATER SCARCITY:
  Physical scarcity: not enough water in the basin
    → parts of Middle East, Central Asia, S. Australia, W. US
  Economic scarcity: water exists but lack infrastructure/investment to use it
    → sub-Saharan Africa (paradox: Congo River basin water-rich but population water-poor)
  Institutional scarcity: governance failure, corruption, lack of water rights systems
    → widespread in developing world
```

## Virtual Water and Water Footprints

```
VIRTUAL WATER (Allan, 1993):
  The water "embedded" in products — used in production but not visible in product
  Import of water-intensive goods = import of virtual water = "hidden" water transfer

  VIRTUAL WATER CONTENT (examples):
    Wheat:        1,300 L/kg (global average)
    Rice:         1,670 L/kg
    Beef:         15,400 L/kg (mostly green water from feed crops/pasture)
    Cotton shirt: 2,700 L (one shirt's worth of cotton)
    Cup of coffee: 140 L
    Hamburger:    2,400 L
    Liter of milk: 1,020 L
    Pair of jeans: 7,500–10,000 L

  GLOBAL VIRTUAL WATER TRADE: ~2,300 km³/yr (>50% of global water use!)
    Net exporters: US, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Argentina (water-rich, grain exporters)
    Net importers: Middle East, N. Africa, Europe (water-scarce + high demand)

WATER FOOTPRINT (Hoekstra, 2002):
  Total volume of freshwater used to produce goods/services

  THREE COLORS:
    Blue water: surface + groundwater (irrigation, industrial)
    Green water: rainwater (evapotranspiration from precipitation)
    Grey water: freshwater needed to dilute pollutants to acceptable level
                (= pollution / (max acceptable concentration - natural background))

  NATIONAL WATER FOOTPRINTS (top per capita consumers):
    USA: ~2,840 m³/person/yr
    Australia: ~2,315 m³/person/yr
    Italy: ~2,332 m³/person/yr
    Morocco: ~1,389 m³/person/yr (relatively low; limited water for production)
    China: ~700 m³/person/yr (efficient per capita given diet)
    Global average: ~1,243 m³/person/yr

POLICY IMPLICATION:
  Countries can "save" local water resources by importing water-intensive goods
  Saudi Arabia example: stopped wheat production 2016 → imports wheat instead
    → "saved" ~5 km³/yr of groundwater depletion
  But: creates dependence on food imports → political/economic vulnerability
  Water virtual trade can EXPORT water-scarcity problems to exporting countries
```

## Transboundary Water Resources

```
GLOBAL CONTEXT:
  ~286 transboundary river basins covering 47% of land surface
  ~39 billion people (37% of world population) live in transboundary basins
  ~300+ transboundary aquifers identified
  2 or more countries share almost every major river basin

MAJOR TRANSBOUNDARY CONFLICT REGIONS:

  NILE BASIN (11 countries, ~500 million people):
    Egypt: historical control, 1959 treaty with Sudan gives 87% of flow
    Ethiopia: Nile headwaters (Blue Nile = 85% of flow at Aswan)
    Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD): 74 GW, completed filling ~2022
    → Egypt's position: GERD threatens Aswan water security
    → Ethiopia's position: sovereign right to develop its resources
    → No final legal agreement; ongoing negotiations
    Underlying: colonial-era treaty (1929, 1959) giving no rights to upstream states

  INDUS BASIN (India, Pakistan):
    Indus Waters Treaty (1960): most enduring transboundary water treaty
    India: eastern rivers (Beas, Ravi, Sutlej)
    Pakistan: western rivers (Indus, Jhelum, Chenab) — 80% of flow
    Both countries have nuclear weapons; Indus is existential for Pakistan
    Climate threat: Himalayan glacier retreat → initially more flow, then less
    Indus waters critical for Pakistan's 90 million farmers

  MEKONG RIVER (China, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, Cambodia, Vietnam):
    China: ~7 large dams on upper Mekong (Lancang)
    Mekong River Commission: downstream cooperation mechanism
    Lancang dams control flow timing → affects downstream agriculture
    Tonle Sap Lake (Cambodia): seasonal flood pulse → fisheries productivity
    Chinese dam operations can decouple natural seasonal flood pulse → devastating
    2019–2020: China held water → extreme drought downstream during La Niña

  COLORADO RIVER (US + Mexico):
    Law of the River (1922 compact + subsequent treaties): over-allocated
    7 US states + Mexico allocated more water than river provides in dry years
    Lake Mead + Lake Powell: 2022 both at ~25% capacity (first time since Glen Canyon Dam filled)
    Tier 1 cuts: Nevada, Arizona, Mexico forced to reduce diversions
    Long-term sustainability: requires structural renegotiation of 100-year-old law

  JORDAN RIVER (Israel, Palestine, Jordan, Syria, Lebanon):
    70–90% of natural flow now diverted; Jordan River barely reaches Dead Sea
    Dead Sea dropping 1+ m/yr (terminal decline without intervention)
    Israel-Jordan peace treaty 1994: allocated flows (Jordan gets 50 MCM/yr more)
    Palestinian water rights: largely unresolved; West Bank aquifer control
```

### International Water Law

```
INTERNATIONAL WATER LAW PRINCIPLES:

  UN WATERCOURSES CONVENTION (1997, entered force 2014):
    "Reasonable and equitable use" — no state has absolute sovereignty
    Obligation not to cause significant harm to other states
    Prior notification of planned measures
    Peaceful dispute resolution
    Binding on ratifying parties (~36 countries); China, Turkey not party

  HARMON DOCTRINE (US 1895, repudiated):
    Absolute sovereign right over waters within territory
    No obligation to downstream states
    Discredited internationally but informally still claimed by some states

  PRIOR APPROPRIATION vs. EQUITABLE UTILIZATION:
    Prior appropriation: "first in time, first in right" — Western US water law
      → Downstream farmers with older rights > upstream newer users
      → Conflicts with international equitable utilization
    Equitable utilization: balance needs of all riparian states (international standard)

  TRANSBOUNDARY AQUIFERS:
    UN ILC Draft Articles on Transboundary Aquifers (2008): not binding
    Most transboundary aquifers: no governing agreement
    Nubian Sandstone Aquifer System (Libya, Egypt, Sudan, Chad):
      ~150,000 km³ of non-renewable fossil water
      No agreement — Libya's Great Man-Made River depleted Libyan portion
      Egypt, Sudan drawing faster than natural recharge (~0.5 mm/yr)
```

## Groundwater Depletion of Major Aquifers

```
GLOBAL GROUNDWATER DEPLETION:
  ~800 km³/yr being depleted globally (withdrawal > recharge)
  Mainly in: India, USA, Pakistan, China, Iran, Saudi Arabia
  Implications: food security (irrigation), subsidence, sea level rise
    (groundwater depletion to sea → ~0.3–0.4 mm/yr of global sea level rise)

OGALLALA AQUIFER (High Plains, USA):
  Area: ~450,000 km²; 8 states from S. Dakota to Texas
  Volume: ~3,600 km³ (comparable to Lake Huron)
  Current recharge: <10 mm/yr (Holocene era: similar rate)
  Current pumping: ~25 km³/yr (largely non-renewable)
  Depletion to date: ~340 km³ (9% of total volume)
  Local depletion: Kansas, Texas have lost 50–70% of original water in some areas
  Time to effective depletion (Kansas, TX): 25–50 years at current trends
  Agriculture supported: ~$20 billion/yr in crops; ~19% of US wheat, 30% cattle
  Consequence: "The day the Ogallala runs dry" → Kansas/Texas dryland farming
                would require complete restructuring

NORTH CHINA PLAIN AQUIFER:
  Most depleted aquifer in world by volume
  ~3.5 km depth to water in some areas (pumping 200 m below surface)
  Beijing dependent on groundwater for >50% of supply
  Deep aquifer: non-renewable fossil water; shallow aquifer: over-pumped
  Subsidence: 11 cm/yr in some Beijing suburbs
  South-to-North Water Transfer Project: $79 billion infrastructure to import
    Yangtze River water to North China Plain

INDUS AQUIFER (Pakistan, India):
  2nd most depleted globally by rate
  Supports ~60% of Pakistan's agriculture
  Depletion: ~27 mm/yr
  Pakistan groundwater: ~50% saline already (quality issues compounding quantity)

CENTRAL VALLEY AQUIFER (California):
  ~64 km³ depleted total; accelerated during droughts
  Subsidence: up to 8 m total in some San Joaquin Valley areas since 1920s
  Infrastructure damage: canals tilted, roads cracked, bridges misaligned
  SGMA (Sustainable Groundwater Management Act, 2014):
    First CA law requiring sustainable management
    Critically overdrafted basins must achieve sustainability by 2040
    Implementation: local agencies → many behind schedule
```

## Water-Energy-Food Nexus

```
THE NEXUS CONCEPT:
  Water, energy, and food are inextricably linked
  Policy in one sector affects the others

WATER FOR ENERGY:
  Thermoelectric power: ~41% of US freshwater withdrawals
    (cooling for coal, nuclear, gas plants — most returned but thermal pollution)
  Hydropower: ~20% of global electricity; minimal consumptive use
  Oil/gas: fracking → produced water management challenge
  Energy crops for biofuels: high water footprint
    Corn ethanol: ~780 L water/L ethanol produced
    vs. sugarcane ethanol (Brazil): ~90 L water/L ethanol

ENERGY FOR WATER:
  Desalination: 3–10 kWh/m³ (SWRO) — energy intensive but falling
  Groundwater pumping: energy ∝ lift height
    Ogallala: average lift increasing ~1 m/yr → energy cost rising
  Long-distance conveyance: California SWP pumps 1.2 TWh/yr over Tehachapi
  Wastewater treatment: 0.5–2 kWh/m³ (conventional WWT)

FOOD-WATER LINKAGE:
  Agriculture: 70% of global freshwater withdrawals
  Food trade = virtual water trade (see above)
  Groundwater depletion = liquidating water capital to produce food now
  Climate change: shifts precipitation → 30% of irrigated area may lose access

DESALINATION — THE ESCAPE VALVE:
  Technology: SWRO (seawater reverse osmosis), MSF (multi-stage flash)
  Capacity: ~100 million m³/day globally (2020); doubling ~every 10 yr
  Energy cost: SWRO 3–5 kWh/m³; MSF 10–20 kWh/m³ (hence SWRO dominant)
  Cost: $0.50–1.50/m³ (falling as membrane efficiency improves)
  Concentrated brine: high salinity reject → environmental management challenge
  Limited to: coastal countries with energy resources
    Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait): ~50% of desalination capacity
    Israel: ~80% of drinking water from desal
    California: Carlsbad (SWRO), Tampa Bay
  Cannot solve: inland water scarcity (transport too expensive)
```

## Climate Change and Global Freshwater

```
PROJECTED CHANGES:
  "Wet gets wetter, dry gets drier" (general but oversimplified principle)
    Clausius-Clapeyron: 7% more water vapor per °C warming
    → More intense precipitation events globally
    → But: warmer temperatures → more ET → soil drying between events
    → Net result: same or less annual precipitation → more droughts
               but higher-intensity storms when rain does occur

  SPECIFIC REGIONAL PROJECTIONS (IPCC AR6):
    Mediterranean: -20 to -40% precipitation by 2100 (RCP8.5)
    SE Asia: increased monsoon intensity
    Sahel: uncertain (inter-model disagreement)
    Southern Africa: -30 to -50% in western areas
    N. Australia: little change or slightly wetter
    SW US: -20 to -30%, continued aridification
    Greenland/Arctic: increased precipitation (but most as snow)

  GLACIER AND SNOWPACK LOSS:
    Global glaciers: -0.5% volume per year; accelerating
    Western US snowpack: -60 to -80% by 2080 (RCP8.5)
    Swiss Alps: 90% of glacier volume lost by 2100 (RCP8.5)
    "Peak water" from glaciers: short-term increase (more melt) → long-term decrease
    Himalayas: ~700 million people depend on glacial meltwater contribution

  GROUNDWATER RECHARGE:
    More erratic precipitation → less steady recharge
    More intense events → faster runoff, less infiltration
    Warmer temperatures → more ET → less surplus for recharge
    Most regions: net recharge decline under warming
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Total freshwater availability vs. accessible freshwater**: 42,750 km³/yr flows through rivers but large fractions occur as inaccessible flood pulses in remote regions (Amazon, Congo), during brief monsoon seasons, or in polar regions. Realistically accessible freshwater for human use is ~12,500 km³/yr. Even so, global withdrawal (~4,000 km³/yr) is only ~30% of accessible supply — but geographic and temporal maldistribution creates scarcity.

**Virtual water imports don't "save" global water**: Virtual water trade enables water-scarce countries to avoid depleting their local resources by importing water-intensive goods. But the water is still used somewhere — by the exporting country. This can deplete resources in exporting countries if they're mining groundwater. Globally, virtual water trade doesn't create water; it just redistributes its depletion.

**Groundwater depletion and sea level rise**: Groundwater pumped out of aquifers eventually reaches the ocean (through rivers, ET to atmosphere → rainfall → ocean). This contributes to global sea level rise: ~0.3–0.4 mm/yr from groundwater depletion and artificial reservoir storage loss — comparable to the contribution from glaciers. Land subsidence from extraction is local and can be more severe than global SLR at specific sites.

**Saudi Arabia wheat self-sufficiency was unsustainable**: Saudi Arabia grew wheat in the desert using fossil groundwater from the Arabian Aquifer System (non-renewable, Pleistocene-age). The program was abandoned in 2016 when aquifer levels dropped dramatically and pumping became uneconomical. The country now imports all wheat. This is a template for other groundwater-dependent agricultural systems facing similar choices.

**Climate change amplifies existing water scarcity patterns**: Regions already water-scarce (MENA, SW US, Australia outback) will generally get drier. Regions already water-rich (equatorial, monsoon Asia) will generally get wetter or more variable. This "Matthew effect" (more to those who have, less to those who lack) is a defining feature of climate change's hydrological impacts — political stability implications in already water-stressed regions are significant.
