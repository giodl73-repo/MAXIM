## Lake Stratification

### Physical Structure

```
THERMAL STRATIFICATION (summer, temperate lake):

  EPILIMNION (warm, well-mixed):
    0–10 m depth (varies with lake size, latitude)
    Well-mixed by wind → near-uniform temperature
    Warm: 15–25°C in summer
    Aerobic, productive, phytoplankton bloom zone

  METALIMNION (thermocline):
    Rapid temperature decrease with depth
    Temperature gradient: >1°C/m
    Creates density stratification that resists mixing

  HYPOLIMNION (cold, stratified):
    Below thermocline to sediment
    Cold: 4–10°C
    Low light (or none) → no photosynthesis
    Organic matter decomposition → oxygen consumption
    Often anoxic in productive lakes by summer end

  EPILIMNION ───── 20°C ──────────────────────────────────────────────
  THERMOCLINE ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
               temperature drops 10°C+ over 2–5 m
  HYPOLIMNION ──── 5°C ───────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### Mixing and Turnover

```
LAKE MIXING CYCLES (dimictic = mixes twice per year, typical N. temperate):

  SPRING TURNOVER:
    Winter: ice cover → 0°C surface; maximum density at 4°C → bottom
    Ice melts → surface warms → 4°C → maximum density → density same as bottom
    → no stratification → WIND CAN MIX ENTIRE LAKE
    Result: uniform temperature, high O₂ throughout, nutrients circulated

  SUMMER STRATIFICATION:
    Surface warms above 4°C → epilimnion develops → stratification
    Thermocline acts as physical barrier → prevents mixing

  FALL TURNOVER:
    Surface cools → 4°C → density same top to bottom → wind mixes lake
    Nutrients from hypolimnion redistributed → promotes fall algal bloom

  WINTER STRATIFICATION (ice-covered):
    Ice at 0°C on top; densest 4°C water at bottom
    INVERSE STRATIFICATION: cold (less dense) water on top!

LAKE CLASSIFICATION BY MIXING:
  Amictic:     never mixes (polar/ice-covered)
  Monomictic:  mixes once/year (tropical: cool season; polar: summer)
  Dimictic:    mixes twice/year (temperate standard)
  Polymictic:  mixes frequently (shallow lakes, windy, or tropical)
  Meromictic:  never fully mixes → permanent chemocline
    Example: Lake Tanganyika (Africa): stratified for >9000 years, deep anoxic layer
    Lake Vostok (Antarctica): subglacial, ~4 km deep, never mixes
```

## Eutrophication

### Nutrient Loading and Response

```
TROPHIC CLASSIFICATION:
  Oligotrophic: nutrient-poor, clear, low productivity, high O₂, cold hypolimnion
    TP < 10 μg/L, Secchi depth > 8 m, Chl-a < 2 μg/L
  Mesotrophic:  intermediate
    TP 10–30 μg/L, Secchi 4–8 m, Chl-a 2–8 μg/L
  Eutrophic:    nutrient-rich, turbid, high productivity, hypolimnetic O₂ depletion
    TP > 30 μg/L, Secchi < 4 m, Chl-a > 8 μg/L
  Hypereutrophic: extreme nutrients, persistent algal blooms, anoxia, fish kills

VOLLENWEIDER MODEL (nutrient loading):
  [TP] = L_P / (Q_w/A + s)

  [TP] = lake total phosphorus concentration (μg/L)
  L_P = phosphorus loading rate (g/m²/yr)
  Q_w/A = hydraulic flushing rate (m/yr) = inflow/area
  s = net sedimentation rate (yr⁻¹) ≈ 10/(mean depth)^0.5

  KEY INSIGHT:
    Deep lakes with fast flushing → dilute nutrients → oligotrophic
    Shallow lakes with slow flushing → concentrate nutrients → eutrophic
    More P input → more eutrophic (linear model)
    Lake Taupo (NZ), Lake Superior: oligotrophic (deep, large volume, limited loading)
    Lake Erie (shallowest Great Lake): hypereutrophic western basin

PHOSPHORUS LIMITATION (Liebig's Law applied):
  In most freshwater lakes: phosphorus (P) is limiting
  N:P ratio: if molar N:P > 16 (Redfield) → P-limiting; if < 7 → N-limiting
  Management implication: reduce P loading (not N) to control eutrophication
  Exception: some soft-water lakes (Scandinavia, Canada) are N-limited
```

### Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs)

```
CYANOBACTERIA BLOOMS:
  Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) dominate in hypereutrophic conditions:
    Microcystis, Anabaena, Aphanizomenon, Planktothrix
  ADVANTAGES over green algae:
    Gas vesicles: regulate buoyancy → float to surface → shade competitors
    N₂ fixation (some genera): grow even when NO₃ depleted
    Toxin production: reduce grazing pressure
    Broad tolerance: warm temperatures, low turbulence, high pH

CYANOTOXINS:
  Microcystins (hepatotoxins): most common; lethal to livestock, dogs, humans
    Inhibit protein phosphatases PP1 and PP2A → cell death
  Cylindrospermopsin: liver/kidney toxin
  Anatoxin-a: fast-acting neurotoxin (inhibits acetylcholinesterase)
  Saxitoxin: paralytic shellfish toxin (blocks Na+ channels)

INTERNAL LOADING:
  Eutrophic lake: P locked in sediment
  Summer anoxia in hypolimnion → reducing conditions → Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺
  Fe³⁺-P complex released → soluble P released into hypolimnion
  Fall turnover → internal P load distributed to epilimnion
  → Next summer bloom: NOT driven by external loading but by stored internal P
  IMPLICATION: even after external P reduction, lake remains eutrophic for
               DECADES due to internal loading "legacy"

MITIGATION APPROACHES:
  External load reduction: primary action — reduce P from agriculture, wastewater
  Alum (Al₂(SO₄)₃) treatment: binds P in sediment permanently
  Biomanipulation: remove zooplanktivorous fish → zooplankton recover → grazed algae
  Hypolimnetic oxygenation: maintain aerobic hypolimnion → prevent P release
  Drawdown: expose sediment to air → oxidize P bonds → reduce internal load
```

## Lake Water Balance and Residence Time

```
LAKE WATER BALANCE:
  dV/dt = P_A + Q_in + GW_in - E_A - Q_out - GW_out - ΔS_ice

  P_A = precipitation on lake surface × area
  Q_in = surface inflow
  GW_in/out = groundwater exchange
  E_A = evaporation × area (often dominant in arid climates)
  Q_out = surface outflow (regulated or unregulated)
  ΔS_ice = seasonal ice formation/melt

RESIDENCE TIME:
  τ = V / Q_out  (volume / outflow rate)

  Lake Superior:    ~180 years (massive volume, small outflow)
  Lake Ontario:     ~8 years
  Lake Erie:        ~2.6 years (shallow, large watershed, small volume)
  Lake Baikal (Russia): ~330 years (deepest lake on Earth, ~1620 m, 20% of world's fresh surface water)

  IMPORTANCE:
    Short τ (< 1 year): flushing can control eutrophication
    Long τ (decades): once contaminated, very slow recovery
    Long τ + deep: stratification → anoxic deep water (Baikal exception: oxygenated to bottom because of deep mixing by convection)
```

## Wetland Hydrology

### Hydrology of Wetlands

```
WETLAND DEFINING CRITERION:
  US WETLAND DEFINITION (EPA):
    1. Hydrophytic vegetation (water-tolerant plants)
    2. Hydric soils (formed under saturated, reducing conditions)
    3. Wetland hydrology (saturated for part of growing season)

  HYDROPERIOD: frequency, duration, timing of inundation/saturation
  Most important factor controlling wetland type, productivity, species composition

  Example hydroperiods:
    Tidal wetland: flooded twice daily (12.4 hr period)
    Playa (arid): flooded weeks per decade after large rains
    Prairie pothole: flooded seasonally (spring melt, summer dry)
    Bog: waterlogged continuously (sphagnum keeps water table at surface)

WATER TABLE IN WETLANDS:
  Bogs and fens: water table typically 0–30 cm below surface
  Riparian wetlands: water table tracks stream stage (fluctuates ±1–2 m)
  Coastal freshwater marsh: tidal fluctuation controls inundation
  Prairie pothole: snowmelt fills, summer evaporation draws down

FLOW PATHS:
  Horizontal: very slow (K typically 10⁻⁷–10⁻⁵ m/s through peat)
  Vertical: important for recharge in some wetlands
  Macropore flow: roots, animal burrows → preferential pathways
  Tidal exchange: dominant in coastal wetlands
```

## Peatlands

```
PEAT FORMATION CONDITIONS:
  Anaerobic (oxygen-free) decomposition MUCH slower than aerobic:
    Aerobic (forest litter): 95% of organic matter decomposes within 1–2 years
    Anaerobic (peat):        95% of organic matter preserved for thousands of years
  Net: plant production > decomposition → organic matter accumulates → PEAT

  Peat accumulation rate: 0.3–1 mm/yr compressed peat (centuries to millennia to reach depth)

PEATLAND TYPES:
  BOG (ombrotrophic = sky-fed):
    Receives ONLY atmospheric precipitation (rain, snow)
    → very nutrient-poor (oligotrophic)
    Sphagnum moss: dominant peat-former
    Sphagnum superpowers:
      Can hold 20× its weight in water
      Releases hydrogen ions → acidifies water (pH 3.5–5.0)
      Acidification + phenolic compounds → antimicrobial → further slows decomposition
    Distribution: NW Europe (Ireland, Scotland, Scandinavia), Canada, Siberia, Alaska

  FEN (minerotrophic = groundwater/river-fed):
    Receives mineral-rich groundwater or river water
    → more nutrient-rich, higher pH (5–7)
    Sedges, reeds, brown mosses dominant
    Can be calcareous (pH > 7) where limestone groundwater inputs

  TROPICAL PEAT SWAMPS:
    SE Asia (Borneo, Sumatra, Malaysia): ~10% of global peatland
    Fire-prone when drained for palm oil/pulpwood → massive CO₂ emissions
    2015 Indonesia peatland fires: ~1.7 billion tonnes CO₂ equivalent

PEATLAND CARBON BUDGET:
  Global peatland area: ~400 million ha (3% of land surface)
  Global peatland carbon stock: ~600 Gt C (compare: ~800 Gt C in atmosphere)
  Peatlands have accumulated this carbon over 8,000–12,000 years since last glaciation

  Annual flux (pristine peatlands):
    Net uptake: ~0.1–0.3 Gt C/yr (slow accumulation)

  Annual flux (drained/degraded peatlands):
    Net release: ~0.9–1.0 Gt C/yr
    (drainage → aerobic → rapid decomposition)
    → Peatland drainage is one of largest single land-use CO₂ sources

  Climate feedback:
    Warming + drying → water table drops → aerobic decomposition → more CO₂
    Permafrost thaw → peat thaws → rapid methane release
    Arctic peatland feedback to Arctic warming: positive, amplifying
```

NITROGEN CYCLING IN WETLANDS:

## Constructed Wetlands

```
CONSTRUCTED WETLAND TYPES:

  FREE WATER SURFACE (FWS):
    Open water surface, emergent vegetation
    Treats: BOD, TSS, nitrogen (nitrification/denitrification)
    Size: 5–50 ha for municipal systems (large area needed)

  SUBSURFACE FLOW (SSF):
    Water flows through gravel/sand below surface
    Emergent plants (Phragmites, Typha) with roots in saturated media
    Advantages: no mosquito breeding, less odor
    Types: horizontal flow (HF), vertical flow (VF), French drains

  HYBRID SYSTEMS:
    HF → VF → pond: combines aerobic and anaerobic zones
    VF provides nitrification (aerobic), HF provides denitrification
    Best nitrogen removal

PERFORMANCE:
  BOD removal: 80–95%
  TSS removal: 85–95%
  Nitrogen: 40–80% (highly variable, depends on design and loading)
  Phosphorus: 30–70% (variable; can be enhanced with media sorption)
  Pathogens: 1–2 log removal (not reliable for disinfection — need tertiary)

HYDRAULIC LOADING RATE (HLR):
  HLR = flow / surface area (m³/m²/day or cm/day)
  Too high → insufficient contact time → poor treatment
  Too low → clogging, plant dieback
  Typical: 0.02–0.2 m/day for HF systems
```

## Bridges from CS and Engineering

```
LAKE/WETLAND CONCEPT          CS / ENGINEERING EQUIVALENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vollenweider P model          Continuously-stirred tank reactor (CSTR) / leaky bucket
  dP/dt = L_in - σ·P·V         → first-order mass balance: in-flow – loss·state
  Steady state P = L/(σ + ρ)   → DC equilibrium of a first-order system
  σ = sedimentation coefficient → loss rate (analogous to drain rate)
  ρ = hydraulic flushing rate  → 1/residence time; more flushing → lower P
  "Internal loading"           → feedback from sediment (state-dependent source)
                                  analogous to cache re-fill from backing store

Lake thermal stratification   Density-stratified two-layer system
  Epilimnion / hypolimnion     → two compartments with low interface conductance
  Thermocline                  → insulating barrier; conductance → ~0 at turnover
  Density inversion (4°C)      → Rayleigh-Taylor instability; triggers convective
                                  overturn (same as buoyancy instability in fluids)
  Wind mixing vs. stability    → forcing vs. restoring force; Richardson number
                                  Ri < 0.25 → turbulent mixing; Ri >> 1 → stable

Wetland denitrification       Reactive transport / catalytic pipeline stage
  NO₃⁻ → N₂ (anaerobic bacteria) → irreversible removal from system
  Rate depends on: temperature, → same as Arrhenius kinetics in a CSTR
    C availability, residence time   optimal residence time for reactor design
  Hydraulic loading (Q/A)      → contact time; too fast → breakthrough

Trophic state classification  Multi-dimensional state clustering
  Oligotrophic / mesotrophic   → states separated by nutrient thresholds
  / eutrophic / hypereutrophic → Carlson TSI is a single-index projection
  Carlson TSI (0–100 scale)    → dimensionality reduction: maps 3 proxies to 1D

Peat accumulation             Integrator with bounded rate
  dC/dt = NPP - decomposition  → net C = integral of difference signal
  Net ~0 in mineral soil       → balanced; peat = NPP >> decomp (cold + anoxic)
  3–5 Gt CO₂/yr if drained    → releasing stored integral on sudden state change

Lake residence time τ = V/Q   Time constant of first-order system
  Concentration half-life      → τ × ln2 (same as RC circuit T_{1/2})
  Recovery after cleanup       → e-folding time for contaminant dilution
  Great Lakes: 1 yr (Superior) to 200 yr (Ontario) → very different recovery speeds
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why do lakes stratify? | Temperature → density gradient; warm light water floats over cold dense water |
| What is the thermocline? | Zone of rapid temperature decrease with depth; density barrier preventing mixing |
| What drives eutrophication? | Excess phosphorus loading (mainly from agriculture, sewage); N is secondary driver in most lakes |
| Why does internal P loading persist after cleanup? | Legacy sediment P releases under anoxic conditions → continues fertilizing blooms for decades |
| What is a bog vs. a fen? | Bog = rain-fed, Sphagnum, acidic, nutrient-poor; Fen = groundwater-fed, sedges, higher pH, more nutrients |
| Why is peat carbon significant? | 600 Gt C stored globally; drainage releases it rapidly as CO₂ → major climate emission source |
| What removes nitrogen permanently in wetlands? | Denitrification: anaerobic bacteria convert NO₃⁻ → N₂ gas |
| What is lake residence time? | Volume / outflow rate; controls contaminant dilution, recovery time |

## Common Confusion Points

**Eutrophication is anthropogenic but trophic state is natural**: Oligotrophic or eutrophic is a natural spectrum. Oligotrophic lakes occur naturally in nutrient-poor geological settings (granite catchments, boreal forest). Eutrophication specifically refers to human-accelerated nutrient enrichment. Some lakes are naturally eutrophic (shallow prairie lakes, tropical systems).

**Phosphorus vs. nitrogen management**: Freshwater lakes: reduce P. Marine systems: reduce N. This is because marine systems are typically N-limited (denitrification removes reactive N), while freshwater lakes are P-limited. Applying the same management to both doesn't work. Great Lakes P reduction program (1970s–80s) was very successful; equivalent N reduction in freshwater would have achieved less.

**Sphagnum bogs are not swamps**: Swamps are forested wetlands. Bogs are open peatlands with Sphagnum moss, sometimes with scattered stunted trees (black spruce, Scots pine). The bog water is profoundly acidic (pH 3.5–4.5) and nutrient-poor — more hostile to life than most environments. The anaerobic, acidic, phenolic conditions of bogs preserve organic material extraordinarily well — "bog bodies" (human sacrifices) preserved 2000+ years.

**Lake turnover is not instantaneous mixing**: The spring/fall turnovers in dimictic lakes occur over days to weeks, not hours. Even after thermal stratification breaks down, complete mixing depends on wind energy. Some deep lakes never completely mix even after "turnover" — residual deep water may not mix. Meromictic lakes (chemically stratified) show this permanently.

**Wetland carbon is only a benefit when wet**: Drained wetlands emit CO₂ and N₂O (both greenhouse gases) rapidly. The carbon stored over millennia can be released in decades. Restored wetlands begin re-sequestering carbon, but the sequestration rate (~0.1–0.3 GtC/yr globally for all pristine peatlands) is MUCH slower than the emission rate when drained. Net: wetland drainage is a one-way carbon bet — lose fast, regain slow.
