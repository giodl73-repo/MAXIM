# Sexual Selection and Life History Theory

## The Big Picture

Sexual selection is selection arising from differential mating success, not differential
survival. It drives the evolution of ornaments, weapons, and mate preferences — often
producing traits that reduce survival but increase reproduction.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│               SEXUAL SELECTION FRAMEWORK                         │
│                                                                  │
│  SEXUAL SELECTION                  NATURAL SELECTION             │
│  ────────────────                  ──────────────────            │
│  Currency: mating success          Currency: survival + reprod.  │
│  Often sex-limited expression      Acts on both sexes            │
│  Can oppose natural selection      Generally adaptive            │
│  Produces costly ornaments         Eliminates costly traits      │
│                                                                  │
│  MECHANISMS:                                                     │
│  ─────────────                                                   │
│  Intrasexual: male-male competition → weapons (antlers, fangs)   │
│  Intersexual: female choice → ornaments (peacock tail, song)     │
│               Mutual choice: both sexes choose (monogamous spp.) │
│                                                                  │
│  WHY ORNAMENTS ARE HONEST SIGNALS:                               │
│  Runaway selection (Fisher)   vs.   Handicap principle (Zahavi)  │
│  → Arbitrary but self-        →  Signal is costly and            │
│    reinforcing preference         condition-dependent:           │
│    + trait coupling               only high-quality males can    │
│                                   afford it                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Why Do Two Sexes Have Different Strategies?

**Anisogamy**: the fundamental asymmetry. Eggs are large and costly; sperm are
small and cheap.

```
  Parental investment (Trivers 1972):
  ───────────────────────────────────
  Sex that invests more per offspring = the "limited resource"
  → Subject to mate choice by the less-investing sex
  → Typically, but not always: females invest more → males compete for females

  Bateman's principle (fruit flies):
  - Male reproductive success limited by number of matings
  - Female reproductive success limited by number of eggs / resources
  → Males benefit more from additional matings
  → This asymmetry drives the evolution of male ornamentation + female choosiness

  Caveats:
  ─────────
  Sex roles can reverse:
  - Pipefish, sea horses: males brood → more invested → females compete for males
  - Jacanas: females hold territories; males incubate; female size > male
  - Degree of parental investment, not chromosomal sex, predicts which sex
    is more choosy
```

## Fisher's Runaway and the Good Genes Hypothesis

### Fisherian Runaway

Fisher (1930) proposed that female preferences and male ornaments can co-evolve in
a positive feedback loop:

```
  Initial state: female preference for slightly longer tails
                 (arbitrary or from weak natural selection on tail)
                          ↓
  Generation 1:  females with preference mate with long-tailed males
                 Sons: inherit long tails
                 Daughters: inherit preference for long tails
                          ↓
  Subsequent:    Alleles for "preference" and "long tail" become genetically
                 correlated (linkage disequilibrium at the phenotypic level)
                          ↓
  Runaway:       As preference becomes common, long-tailed males have higher
                 mating success even if slightly less viable
                 Selection for ornament accelerates
                          ↓
  Equilibrium:   Natural selection against the ornament eventually balances
                 (or the ornament becomes so extreme it causes extinction)
```

The model: a genetic correlation between "choosiness" alleles and "ornament" alleles.
The feedback loop is self-reinforcing — "arbitrary" preferences can evolve and produce
elaborated traits purely from internal evolutionary dynamics.

### Good Genes / Handicap Principle (Zahavi 1975)

Honest signal theory: the peacock's tail is costly to produce and maintain. Only
a genuinely healthy, high-quality male can afford to carry it. The tail is an
honest signal of genetic quality because it cannot be faked by low-quality males.

```
  Zahavi's handicap: W = B(signal) - C(signal)
  Where C(signal) is negatively correlated with male quality q

  High q male: C is low → can sustain large signal
  Low q male:  C is high → large signal would lower fitness below signaling threshold

  → Stable honest signaling equilibrium
  → Female preference is adaptive: sons of preferred males have high q
     ("sexy sons" carrying good genes)

  Types of good-genes traits:
  ─────────────────────────────
  Condition-dependent ornaments: reflect current health, parasite load
  Hamilton-Zuk hypothesis: bright coloration advertises parasite resistance
  Immunocompetence indicators: testosterone-dependent traits signal immunocompetence
                               (testosterone suppresses immunity → only healthy
                                males can afford high testosterone AND survive)
```

## Sperm Competition and Cryptic Female Choice

When females mate with multiple males, sperm compete for fertilization.

```
  SPERM COMPETITION ADAPTATIONS:
  ───────────────────────────────
  Testis size: larger testes → more sperm → advantage in sperm competition
               Chimpanzees (highly promiscuous): huge testes
               Gorillas (single dominant male): small testes
               Humans: intermediate (moderate sperm competition)

  Sperm morphology: heteromorphic sperm (some fertilize, some block rivals)
  Copulatory plugs: seals canal after mating (rodents, some insects)
  Mate guarding: prevents subsequent matings
  Displacement sperm: structured to remove rival sperm (some Odonata)

  CRYPTIC FEMALE CHOICE:
  ──────────────────────
  Females can bias fertilization success after mating (mechanism not always clear):
  - Selective reabsorption of sperm from different males
  - Differential use of stored sperm
  - Immunological biases (MHC-dissimilar sperm preferentially used in some species)
  MHC-based mate choice: T-shirt experiment (Wedekind 1995) — women rate
  odors of MHC-dissimilar men as more attractive. May function to produce
  MHC-heterozygous offspring with broader immune repertoire.
```

## Life History Theory

Life history theory explains how organisms allocate limited resources (energy, time)
among competing demands: growth, reproduction, survival, and immune defense.

### The Fundamental Trade-off

```
  SOMATIC MAINTENANCE ←——————— RESOURCE BUDGET ———————→ REPRODUCTION
  (growth, repair, immunity)                              (current reprod. effort)

  More investment in one = less in the other.

  KEY TRADE-OFFS:
  ───────────────
  Current vs. future reproduction:
    High current effort → faster aging (disposable soma theory)
    Low current effort → longer lifespan, more future reproduction
    Caloric restriction extends lifespan (in lab; uncertain in wild)

  Quality vs. quantity of offspring:
    Few large offspring vs. many small offspring
    r-strategy (opportunistic) vs. K-strategy (competitive):
      r: high fecundity, early maturity, small offspring, short lifespan
         Example: mice, annual plants, salmon
      K: low fecundity, late maturity, large offspring, long lifespan
         Example: elephants, humans, long-lived trees
```

### Disposable Soma Theory (Kirkwood)

```
  Resource allocation model:
  ──────────────────────────
  Somatic maintenance investment m:
  - If m = 1.0: perfect repair, no aging, but no energy for reproduction → extinct
  - If m < 1.0: inevitable accumulation of somatic damage → senescence

  Optimal m < 1.0 for all real organisms
  → Aging is the price of reproduction → "programmed obsolescence" is an
    unnecessary term; aging is a side effect, not a program

  This connects to genomics: mtDNA accumulates somatic mutations faster than
  nuclear DNA → mitochondrial dysfunction may be a primary aging mechanism
```

### Senescence — Why We Age

```
  MUTATION ACCUMULATION (Medawar 1952):
  Selection is weak for late-acting genes because few individuals live to old age
  (extrinsic mortality removes most individuals before old age)
  → Deleterious mutations that act only in old age are nearly neutral
  → They accumulate by drift
  → Result: age-specific decline in function

  ANTAGONISTIC PLEIOTROPY (Williams 1957):
  Genes that increase early fitness but decrease late fitness are positively selected
  → Beneficial for youth = selected; harmful for old age = tolerated
  → Example: testosterone: increases early reproductive success, elevates
             prostate cancer risk late
  → Example: p53: suppresses tumors but also accelerates stem cell senescence

  Both mechanisms likely contribute to aging.
```

## Kin Selection and Inclusive Fitness

Hamilton (1964): altruistic behavior can evolve if it benefits genetic relatives.

```
  Hamilton's Rule:    r·B > C

  r = relatedness (probability that an allele in actor is shared with beneficiary)
  B = benefit to beneficiary (in fitness units)
  C = cost to actor (in fitness units)

  Relatedness coefficients (for diploid outbreeders):
  r(parent-offspring) = 0.5
  r(full siblings)    = 0.5
  r(half siblings)    = 0.25
  r(first cousins)    = 0.125

  Examples:
  ──────────
  Worker bees are sisters: r = 0.75 in haplodiploids
  (queens are diploid; workers are haploid; full sisters share 3/4 of genes)
  → kin selection favored eusociality in Hymenoptera
  But eusociality also evolved in termites (diploid) and mole rats → not required

  Prairie dog alarm calls: caller exposes itself to predator, warns relatives
  → cost C (exposure) < r·B (relatives survive, carry same altruism alleles)

  Inclusive fitness = direct fitness (own reproduction) + indirect fitness
                     (effect on relatives' reproduction, weighted by r)
```

## Honest Signaling — Game Theory and Economics Bridge

The handicap principle is a special case of the general economic theory of costly
signaling. The formal structure is identical across biology, economics, and
mechanism design:

```
ZAHAVI HANDICAP ≈ SPENCE JOB MARKET SIGNALING (1973 Nobel):

  Spence (1973): Why do employers value costly education?
    High-quality workers can afford the cost of education (it's less costly for them)
    Low-quality workers cannot rationally afford the same cost
    → Education is a credible signal because cost is inversely correlated with quality
    → Even if education adds no productivity, equilibrium with signaling is stable

  Zahavi (1975): Why do peacocks have costly tails?
    High-condition males can afford the survival cost of the tail
    Low-condition males cannot sustain the same ornament without dying
    → The tail is a credible signal because cost is inversely correlated with quality
    → The ornament is honest precisely because it is costly

  FORMAL CONDITION FOR HONEST SIGNALING (Riley 1979, Grafen 1990):
    Cost of signal C(quality, signal_level):
      dC/d(quality) < 0: high-quality signalers pay lower marginal cost
      → separating equilibrium: each quality level chooses a different signal level
      → no incentive to "cheat" by signaling above your quality

  HONEST SIGNALING IN OTHER DOMAINS:
    Handicap:      survival cost of ornament (peacock tail, stotting gazelle)
    Physiological: immunocompetence handicap — testosterone suppresses immune function
                   → large secondary sex characters = honest immune status report
    Economics:     bond posting, warranties, "burning money" to signal commitment
    Mechanism design: incentive-compatible contracts require costly revelation

THE ALTERNATIVE: RUNAWAY SELECTION (Fisher's sexy sons):
  No honesty required — female preference and male trait co-evolve via genetic
  correlation. If females prefer long tails, sons of long-tailed males have
  both long tails AND the allele for female preference → runaway.
  This is NOT a signaling equilibrium — it is a co-evolutionary dynamic
  without an information content constraint.

DISTINGUISHING HANDICAP FROM RUNAWAY:
  Handicap prediction: ornament honestly reflects condition (correlates with
    parasite resistance, immune function, survival)
  Runaway prediction: arbitrary trait, just correlated with female preference
  Empirical tests: condition-manipulation experiments (control male condition →
    ornament size should change with condition if handicap)
```

## Sexual Conflict

Sexual conflict arises when the evolutionary interests of males and females diverge —
a major driver of rapid, sometimes coevolutionary, trait evolution:

```
INTRA-LOCUS SEXUAL CONFLICT:
  Same locus/allele is under selection in opposite directions in each sex
  (because the same gene is expressed in both sexes)

  Example: Body size
    Large body size: favored by male-male competition (advantage for males)
    Large body size: costly for females (energetic burden, predation risk)
    → Conflict over optimal allele at size-controlling loci
    → Resolution: sexual dimorphism (different expression in each sex)
    → Mechanism: sex-limited expression via sex chromosomes or hormonal regulation

  Constraint: if conflict is strong and resolution incomplete,
    mean fitness of each sex is below its unconstrained optimum
    → "intralocus sexual conflict load"
    → Experimental evidence: removing sexual conflict by enforcing monogamy
      → female fitness increases as population adapts to her optimum

INTER-LOCUS SEXUAL CONFLICT (antagonistic coevolution):
  Different loci in males vs. females "battle" over outcomes

  1. SEMINAL PROTEINS AND FEMALE RESISTANCE:
     Male accessory gland proteins (ACPs / seminal fluid): transferred at mating
     → manipulate female physiology (increase egg laying, reduce remating)
     → costly to female (reduced lifespan at high doses)
     Males evolve more potent ACPs → females evolve resistance
     → Red Queen dynamics between male and female genomes

     Evidence (Drosophila): experimental evolution of sex ratio
       → males and females co-evolve in arms race
       → faster evolution of seminal proteins than any other protein class

  2. TRAUMATIC INSEMINATION:
     Bedbugs (Cimex): male punctures female abdomen, injects sperm directly
     → completely bypasses female reproductive tract (extreme male control)
     → costly to female (wound infection, hemolymph loss)
     Females: evolved paragenitalia (abdominal structure that channels wound)
     → partial mitigation of cost

  3. CHASE-AWAY SEXUAL SELECTION:
     Female resistance to male signals evolves → males must escalate signal
     → signal evolves beyond original female preference optimum
     → "sensory exploitation gone wrong"
     → predicts faster evolution of female preferences than good-genes model
     Evidence: positive correlation between male signal elaboration and female
       resistance (reduced sensory sensitivity) across populations

ARMS RACE PREDICTION:
  Inter-locus sexual conflict predicts:
    Rapidly evolving genes at male-female interaction interface
    (reproductive proteins, sperm proteins, egg coat proteins)
  Data: highest dN/dS ratios in genome found in reproductive proteins
    (ZP3, ZP2 egg coat; ADAM enzymes; accessory gland proteins)
  Consistent with ongoing antagonistic coevolution between sexes
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Sexual selection is not a special type of natural selection.** Darwin treated them
separately. Modern theory treats sexual selection as natural selection where the
selective agent is conspecific individuals (mates and rivals) rather than physical
environment. The distinction is heuristic, not mechanistic.

**The handicap principle does not require the ornament to be physiologically costly.**
What is required is that the ornament is difficult or costly for low-quality males to
sustain. The cost can be immunological, energetic, or risk-of-predation. The key
property is that the signal is condition-dependent in a way that correlates with
the quality being advertised.

**r/K selection is now considered oversimplified.** Modern life history theory uses
a "fast-slow continuum" or pace-of-life syndrome rather than a discrete r/K
dichotomy. The key axes are: age at maturity, reproductive rate, lifespan, and
offspring size — these form a correlated syndrome across species.

**Inclusive fitness does not require conscious kin recognition.** Evolution selects
for behaviors that are consistently correlated with kin presence. Spatial viscosity
(offspring stay near birthplace → neighbors are likely relatives) can generate
effective kin selection without any kin recognition mechanism.

**Sperm competition vs. cryptic female choice are difficult to distinguish.** Both
are "post-copulatory sexual selection" but involve male vs. female control. The
same pattern (non-random fertilization) can result from either or both. Experimental
evidence for each requires controlled crosses with distinguished sperm.
