# Macroevolution, Mass Extinctions, and the Fossil Record

## The Big Picture

Macroevolution encompasses evolutionary processes above the species level: clade
diversification, mass extinctions, evolutionary trends, and the origin of body plans.
The fossil record is the primary data source.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                   MACROEVOLUTION LANDSCAPE                       │
│                                                                    │
│  PATTERN                   PROCESS                TIMESCALE      │
│  ───────                   ───────                ─────────      │
│  Speciation rate variation  Adaptive radiation     Mya           │
│  Extinction rate variation  Mass extinction        Mya           │
│  Evolutionary trends        Species selection      10-100 Mya    │
│  Body plan origins          Constraint + key innov.>500 Mya      │
│                                                                    │
│  TEMPO:                                                          │
│  ──────                                                          │
│  Gradualism: Darwin — slow, continuous change                    │
│  Punctuated equilibrium: Gould & Eldredge — stasis + rapid bursts│
│                                                                    │
│  EXTINCTION:                                                     │
│  ──────────                                                      │
│  Background extinction: ~1 species per million species-years     │
│  Mass extinction: >75% species lost in <2 million years          │
│  Big Five + current 6th mass extinction underway                 │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Fossil Record

### What It Preserves (and Doesn't)

```
  PRESERVED:
  ──────────
  Hard parts: bones, shells, teeth, wood, exoskeletons
  Exceptional preservation: Burgess Shale (soft tissue), amber (insects),
                            permafrost (frozen mammoths, DNA preserved)
  Trace fossils: footprints, burrows, feeding marks
  Chemical fossils (biomarkers): lipid molecules diagnostic of biological groups

  POORLY PRESERVED:
  ──────────────────
  Soft-bodied organisms (jellyfish, worms, early metazoans)
  Small organisms (<0.1 mm)
  Organisms in non-marine, non-anoxic environments
  Any organism in areas with high geological turnover

  SAMPLING BIASES:
  ─────────────────
  Marine organisms better preserved than terrestrial
  Organisms in certain geological formations better sampled
  Geographic biases in where paleontologists have worked
  Monger effect: more sampling effort → more species found
```

## Gradualism vs. Punctuated Equilibrium

### Darwin's Gradualism

Darwin expected the fossil record to show continuous, gradual change through time.
The gaps (missing transitional forms) he attributed to incompleteness of the record.

```
  Gradual model:
  Morphology ──────────────→ time

  species 1: ─────────────────
                               \ (gradual transition)
  species 2:                    ──────────────────────
```

### Punctuated Equilibrium (Gould & Eldredge, 1972)

Observation: most fossil lineages show STASIS (little change) for long periods,
interrupted by rapid morphological change associated with speciation events.

```
  Punctuated equilibrium model:
  Morphology → time

  species 1:  ──────────────────────────────
                                             | (rapid change at speciation)
  species 2:                                ──────────────────────────────
             stasis         stasis     rapid  stasis
              (0.9t)       (0.9t)    change   (0.9t)
```

**Mechanism**: most evolution happens in small peripheral isolates (peripatric
speciation), which are rarely preserved. The main population (well-represented in
fossil record) shows stasis. We observe: sudden appearance + long stasis.

**Is punctuated equilibrium real?**
- Supported by some fossil series (Foraminifera, horse evolution, snail lineages)
- Gradualism supported in others (e.g., some human fossils)
- The dichotomy is probably too simple: both patterns exist; relative frequency is
  the real question
- Statistical tests: P.E. predicts morphological change is concentrated at
  speciation nodes in phylogeny; supported in some but not all analyses

**Gould's broader claim**: macroevolutionary patterns cannot be predicted from
microevolution alone; higher-level selection (species selection) shapes long-term
trends. This remains controversial.

## Mass Extinctions

### The Big Five

```
  EXTINCTION EVENT      MYA     % SPECIES LOST    PROBABLE CAUSE
  ──────────────────   ─────   ───────────────   ─────────────────────
  End-Ordovician        444         86%           Glaciation + warming
  Late Devonian         375         75%           Multiple pulses,
                                                  anoxia, cooling
  End-Permian           252         96%           Siberian Traps volcanism:
  ("The Great Dying")                             ocean anoxia, acid,
                                                  global warming + cooling
  End-Triassic          201         80%           CAMP volcanism (Central
                                                  Atlantic Magmatic Province)
  End-Cretaceous (K-Pg) 66          76%           Chicxulub asteroid impact
                                                  + Deccan Traps volcanism
```

### K-Pg Extinction in Detail

```
  66 Ma: Chicxulub impactor, ~10-15 km diameter, Yucatan Peninsula

  IMMEDIATE EFFECTS:
  - Fireball: global forest fires
  - Impact winter: ejecta + soot blocks sunlight for months-years
  - Photosynthesis collapse: food web collapse from base
  - Acid rain (sulfur from evaporites vaporized by impact)
  - Tsunami: megatsunam on Caribbean coasts

  KILLED:
  - All non-avian dinosaurs, pterosaurs, most marine reptiles
  - ~75% of species overall
  - Ammonites (dominant marine cephalopods for 300 Mya — extinct)

  SURVIVED:
  - Birds (avian dinosaurs), mammals, crocodilians, lizards, snakes
  - Freshwater ecosystems better buffered than marine
  - Small body size correlated with survival (independent of taxonomy)

  RECOVERY:
  - Mammalian radiation: from ~5 orders to >25 orders in 10 Mya after K-Pg
  - Adaptive radiation into niches vacated by dinosaurs
```

### The End-Permian: The Great Dying

```
  252 Ma: Siberian Traps large igneous province
  ~2 million km³ of basalt erupted over <500,000 years

  Effects:
  - CO₂ emissions → rapid warming (~5-10°C in <100,000 years)
  - Ocean warming → stratification → anoxia in deep water
  - CO₂ absorption → ocean acidification
  - H₂S from anoxic ocean vented into atmosphere
  - Ozone depletion from volcanic halogens

  Killed: ~96% of marine species, ~70% of terrestrial species
  Recovery: took ~5-10 million years (longest recovery in Phanerozoic)

  Modern relevance: current ocean warming + acidification analogous to
  end-Permian conditions (though rate is much faster in anthropogenic forcing)
```

## Macroevolution — Systems Engineering and Optimization Bridge

```
KILL CURVES = POWER-LAW FAILURE RATE DISTRIBUTIONS:

  Mass extinction kill curve (Raup 1991):
    Extinction intensity (% species killed) vs. frequency of events at that intensity
    Follows roughly a power-law: P(intensity > x) ∝ x^(-α)

  Analogous structures in engineering:
    Reliability engineering: failure rate curves (bathtub curve, extreme event tail)
    Software failures: bug severity vs. frequency (power-law in many systems)
    Network outages: duration vs. frequency (power-law for large internet events)
    Financial crises: loss magnitude vs. frequency (fat-tailed distributions)

  The implication is the same across domains:
    Rare catastrophic events dominate total harm (integral of severity × frequency)
    Planning for "background rate" misses the dominant risk
    Mass extinctions: rare events that eliminate 75%+ of species matter more for
      long-run diversity than thousands of background extinction events combined

SPECIES SELECTION = MULTI-LEVEL OPTIMIZATION:

  Individual selection: differential survival/reproduction within species
    (standard natural selection — local optimization)
  Species selection: differential speciation/extinction rates among species
    (lineage sorting — "selection" at a higher level)

  FORMAL PARALLEL TO LOCAL vs. GLOBAL OPTIMIZATION:
    Individual selection = local search within the fitness landscape of a species
    Species selection = selection among local optima
    A species on a lower fitness peak may speciate faster (faster local search)
    → its descendants fill more of tree-of-life than species on higher individual peak

  HIERARCHY OF SELECTION LEVELS:
    Gene → Individual → Kin group → Population → Species → Clade
    Each level: selection can act if there is heritable variance in fitness
    Debate: how much of macroevolutionary trend is species selection vs.
      directional individual selection within species?
    Key test: is the "trait" causing differential speciation/extinction a
      species-level emergent property, or just correlated with individual fitness?

PUNCTUATED EQUILIBRIUM = STASIS + RAPID CHANGE:
  Eldredge & Gould (1972): most morphological change concentrated at speciation
  Stasis: stabilizing selection within species (functional constraint)
  Rapid change: ecological release at speciation, or new adaptive zone
  → Same as: complex systems that resist change until a phase transition
    (percolation threshold, catastrophic shift in dynamical systems)
  Statistical test: compare morphological change rate within vs. between lineages
    PE prediction: between-lineage rate >> within-lineage rate
```

### Extinction Selectivity — What Gets Hit?

Different types of extinction select for different traits:

```
  MASS EXTINCTION SELECTIVITY:
  ──────────────────────────────
  Small body size: generally favored (lower resource requirements)
  Wide geographic range: favored (some part of range survives)
  Wide diet / generalism: favored over specialists
  Freshwater: often buffered relative to marine
  Deep ocean: often hit harder (anoxia events)

  End-Permian and K-Pg: differently selective
  - K-Pg: body size most important discriminant
  - End-Permian: tolerance of anoxia and acidification most important

  BACKGROUND EXTINCTION SELECTIVITY:
  ──────────────────────────────────
  Narrow endemic species (small range)
  Specialists
  Habitat-specific (restricted ecosystem type)
  Low fecundity (slow demographic recovery)
```

## Adaptive Radiation

Mass extinctions create opportunity: ecological release into vacated niches drives
rapid speciation and divergence.

```
  KEY ADAPTIVE RADIATIONS:
  ─────────────────────────
  Post K-Pg mammals:
    Crown Placentalia diversified in 10 Mya after dinosaur extinction
    Whales (from artiodactyl ancestor, ~50 mya — fast transition)
    Bats (earliest fossils 52 mya — rapid diversification in Eocene)
    Primates: post-K-Pg radiation in Paleocene

  Cambrian explosion (541-520 Ma):
    Most animal phyla appear in fossil record within ~20 Mya
    Not truly explosive — Precambrian animal fossils now known
    Ediacaran fauna (~575-541 Ma): soft-bodied animal-grade organisms
    Cambrian: first hard parts (shells, eyes) → preservational artifact?
    + real ecological radiation as predation escalated (arms race + radiation)

  Cichlid fishes in African Rift Lakes:
    >500 species in Lake Victoria (<15,000 years old)
    Driven by: sexual selection on coloration + trophic specialization
    Fastest known vertebrate radiation
```

## Convergent Evolution — Macroevolutionary Patterns

At macroevolutionary scales, convergence reveals the limits of adaptive landscapes:

```
  CONVERGENT STRUCTURES / BIOCHEMISTRY:
  ───────────────────────────────────────
  Eyes: ≥40 independent origins (camera, compound, pit)
  Wings: 4 independent origins in vertebrates, many in insects
  C4 photosynthesis: ≥60 independent origins in plants
  Sabre teeth: ≥4 independent origins (saber-tooth cat, Thylacosmilus,
              Machairodus, Smilodon — similar ecological role)
  Echolocation: independent in Microchiroptera + Odontoceta
                convergent at molecular level in prestin (motor protein)
  Endothermy: evolved in birds, mammals, tuna, some sharks, leatherback turtles
  Viviparity: >100 independent origins in vertebrates

  WHAT DOES THIS TELL US?
  ─────────────────────────
  Adaptive landscape has predictable peaks — similar ecological problems
  generate similar solutions repeatedly
  BUT: the specific path to each peak differs (different genes, different
  developmental mechanisms)
  Mosauer's Law: adaptation is more convergent than mechanism
```

## Biogeography

The geographic distribution of species reflects the interaction of evolutionary
history, continental drift, and ecological limits:

```
VICARIANCE vs. DISPERSAL BIOGEOGRAPHY:

  Vicariance: a geographic barrier splits an existing population
    → allopatric speciation with disjunct distributions explained by barrier history
    Example: Atlantic and Pacific sister-species pairs separated by Isthmus of Panama
             (closed ~3.5 mya) — many marine species have twin species either side

  Dispersal (long-distance): rare dispersal events colonize new areas
    Example: Hawaiian islands: all terrestrial species are colonizers
             (islands are volcanic — never connected to continents)
             Colonization rates proportional to distance and organism dispersal ability

MacARTHUR-WILSON EQUILIBRIUM MODEL (1967):
  Species richness on an island = equilibrium between colonization and extinction

  Rate of colonization:    C(S) = λ (P − S)     decreasing as island fills
  Rate of extinction:      E(S) = µ S             increasing as island fills
  Equilibrium: S* = λP / (λ + µ)

  Predictions:
    Larger islands: lower extinction rate (larger populations) → higher S*
    Closer islands: higher colonization rate → higher S*
    Species-area relationship: S ∝ A^z  (z ≈ 0.25–0.35 for oceanic islands)
    Turnover: even at equilibrium, species composition changes over time

  FORMAL CONNECTION:
    Birth-death process on species richness
    Same structure as queuing theory (M/M/1 queue at steady state)
    S* = arrival rate / (arrival rate + service rate)

WALLACE'S LINE AND BIOGEOGRAPHIC REGIONS:

  Alfred Russel Wallace (1876): defined major biogeographic regions
  Wallace's Line (Indonesia): sharp boundary between Oriental and Australian faunas
    West of line: tigers, elephants, monkeys, deer → Asian fauna
    East of line: kangaroos, cockatoos, birds-of-paradise → Australian fauna
  Mechanism: deep water channel (Lombok Strait) — was always a deep-sea barrier
    even during ice-age sea-level lowering (no land bridge ever formed)

  CONTINENTAL DRIFT AND DISJUNCT DISTRIBUTIONS:
    Gondwana breakup (~180 mya): explains distributions of:
      Ratites (ostriches, rheas, emus, kiwis): vicariant distribution on former Gondwana
      Southern beeches (Nothofagus): South America, Australia, New Zealand, Antarctica
      Marsupials: evolved on Gondwana → dominant in Australia (isolated from placental
        mammals that diversified on Laurasia)

  REFUGIA AND ICE AGE BIOGEOGRAPHY:
    Pleistocene glaciations: species retreated to refugia → populations isolated
    → allopatric divergence in refugia → post-glacial expansion + secondary contact
    Phylogeography: molecular phylogeography reveals refugia from divergence patterns
    European refugia: Iberia, Italy, Balkans (confirmed by mitochondrial haplotype trees)
```

## Evolutionary Trends and Species Selection

Long-term trends in the fossil record:
- Horses: multi-toed to single-toed (Cope's "law of the unspecialized")
- Body size increase in many lineages (Cope's Rule)
- Complexity increase? (contested — regression to mean from Bacteria start)

```
  SPECIES SELECTION:
  ──────────────────
  Gould's proposal: differential speciation and extinction of SPECIES (not
  individuals) generates macroevolutionary trends without individual-level
  selection pointing that way

  Example: If large-bodied species speciate faster AND go extinct slower,
  the clade as a whole shows body size increase even if no individual-level
  selection for large body size exists

  Mechanism is identical to natural selection but with species as the unit:
  - Heritable variation in species-level traits (speciation rate, extinction rate)
  - Differential "fitness" of species
  → Lineage sorting based on species-level properties

  Counter-argument: most trends can also be explained by directional selection
  within species. Species selection is an additional level, not a replacement.
```

## The 6th Mass Extinction

Current extinction rates are estimated at 100-1,000x background:

```
  CURRENT STATE:
  ──────────────
  Background rate: ~1 extinction per million species-years
  Current rate:    ~100-1,000 extinctions per million species-years
  (estimated from IUCN Red List threat categories)

  Defaunation: loss of individuals, not just species
  - Large body size → extinction risk (K-strategists, slow reproduction)
  - Islands → severe historical wave (Polynesian spread: ~2,000 bird species lost)
  - Current threats: habitat loss (primary), overexploitation, invasive species,
                     climate change, pollution, disease

  Comparison to Big Five:
  - Current rate comparable to mass extinction events
  - But: current event is fastest-onset in Earth history
  - Recovery: takes 5-10 Mya after Big Five events
  - If current trajectory continues, irreversible on human timescales
```

## Key Innovations and Diversification Rate Shifts

A key innovation is a heritable trait that opens a new adaptive zone — enabling a
lineage to interact with the environment in a fundamentally new way and triggering
an elevated diversification rate:

```
DEFINITION:
  Key innovation: a trait that, once evolved, permits exploitation of a
  previously inaccessible ecological opportunity
  → Causes detectable increase in net diversification rate (speciation − extinction)
  → Identified by: rate shift on time-calibrated phylogeny coinciding with origin of trait

CLASSIC EXAMPLES:

  FLIGHT FEATHERS (avian radiation):
    Feathered wings: allowed powered flight → access to aerial niche, rapid dispersal,
    new foraging modes, reduced predation → ~10,500 extant species
    Contrast: non-avian dinosaurs without powered flight: much lower diversity
    Rate test: Bayesian analysis of bird phylogeny shows rate acceleration
      coinciding with development of refined flight morphology (~65 mya)

  PHARYNGEAL JAWS IN CICHLIDS:
    Teleost fishes have pharyngeal jaws (secondary jaw apparatus in throat)
    Cichlids: highly modified pharyngeal jaws → separate chewing from catching
    → enabled trophic specialization (crushing molluscs, scraping algae, eating fish)
    without changing the oral jaw morphology
    → One toolkit → many trophic ecotypes → explosive diversity
    African rift lake cichlids: >500 species in Lake Victoria, >1000 in Lake Malawi
    Diversification rate: among fastest recorded for vertebrates

  INSECT WINGS (~400 mya):
    Flight allowed colonization of aerial niche → plant-insect interactions
    → diversification of both insects and flowering plants in co-evolutionary spiral
    >1,000,000 described insect species (vs. ~60,000 vertebrate species)
    Timing: insect diversification rate increases sharply at wing origin

  VENOM IN SNAKES:
    Venom enabled subduing large, fast-moving prey without physical combat
    → access to prey size class previously unavailable
    Elapidae + Viperidae: highly diverse (>3,000 species)
    vs. non-venomous Boidae (pythons/boas): ~60 species

  ANGIOSPERM DOUBLE FERTILIZATION:
    Endosperm (triploid, nutritive): allows faster seed maturation
    + fruit rewards co-opted pollinators and seed dispersers
    → ~350,000 angiosperm species vs. ~1,000 gymnosperm species

STATISTICAL TESTS FOR DIVERSIFICATION RATE SHIFTS:
  BAMM (Bayesian Analysis of Macroevolutionary Mixtures): detects shifts on phylogeny
  MEDUSA: tests for rate shifts at specific nodes
  Null: constant diversification rate (Yule pure-birth model)
  Alternative: heterogeneous rates, with shift at innovation origin

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK:
  Key innovation → expanded niche space → reduced competition pressure
    → ecological release → high speciation rate in new zone
    → each new species can specialize on a sub-niche → further radiation
    → stabilizes when niche space saturated → return to background rate
  Identical to: phase transition from low-diversity steady state to
    diversification burst when a constraint is removed
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Punctuated equilibrium does not deny gradual change.** It claims that most morphological
change is concentrated around speciation events and that long periods of relative stasis
occur within lineages. Gradual change within a lineage over very long periods is not
excluded — it just appears rare relative to stasis in many empirical fossil sequences.

**The Cambrian "explosion" was not instantaneous.** It spans ~20 million years and was
preceded by Ediacaran soft-bodied metazoan diversification. It appears explosive in the
fossil record partly because the origin of hard parts dramatically improves preservation.
The body-plan diversity we see in the Cambrian did not spontaneously appear — it had
Precambrian roots that are poorly preserved.

**Mass extinctions don't "reset" evolution.** The survivors are not random — they are
disproportionately from groups with certain traits (generalist, small body, wide range).
Post-extinction radiations build on the surviving diversity, which is biased. This is
why mammals (not reptiles) radiated after K-Pg — not because mammals are "better" but
because they had the right body size and diet at the right time.

**Convergent evolution does not imply a predetermined evolutionary direction.** It
shows that the same ecological problem, solved with similar available toolkit genes,
tends to produce similar morphological solutions. But the specific genetic path is not
predetermined — it is contingent. Change the initial conditions and the solution
may differ, even if the problem is the same.

**The 6th mass extinction is not "natural."** Previous mass extinctions were caused by
extraterrestrial impact or geological processes. The current event is driven by one
species' activity — primarily land use change and overexploitation, now increasingly
compounded by climate change. The timescale (~decades to centuries) is unprecedented
in Earth history — faster even than the Chicxulub impact's ecological effects.
