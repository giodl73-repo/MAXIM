# End-of-Life: Recycling and Sustainability

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              COMPOSITE END-OF-LIFE LANDSCAPE                     |
|                                                                  |
|   THERMOSET COMPOSITES (~70% of all composites)                  |
|   ──────────────────────────────────────────────                 |
|   Cannot melt → no easy recycling route                          |
|   Options: Mechanical → filler/short fiber (low value)           |
|            Thermal    → pyrolysis, fluidized bed, microwave      |
|                      → fiber recovery (medium value)             |
|            Chemical   → solvolysis (high value, high cost)       |
|            Landfill   → banned in EU (2003 landfill directive)   |
|            Incineration → energy recovery only                   |
|                                                                  |
|   THERMOPLASTIC COMPOSITES (~30%)                                |
|   ─────────────────────────────                                  |
|   Can melt → standard thermoplastic recycling routes             |
|   Fiber shortening inevitable → property degradation             |
|   BUT: structural thermoplastic repair + reweld possible         |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

End-of-life composites represent a growing problem. ~100,000+ tonnes of CFRP
reach end of service annually (aircraft, wind blades, sporting goods), with
volume growing rapidly. No economically attractive closed-loop recycling exists
at scale as of 2024.

## The Scale of the Problem

### Sources of End-of-Life Composites

```
   WIND TURBINE BLADES — DOMINANT NEAR-TERM VOLUME:
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Typical blade: 8–12 tonnes GFRP + CFRP (for larger blades)
   Current global fleet: ~4 million blades
   Replacement cycle: 20–25 years
   Annual blade retirements 2024–2030: ~50,000 blades → ~400,000 tonnes/yr

   COMMERCIAL AIRCRAFT:
   ─────────────────────
   787: ~50% CFRP by weight = ~40 tonnes CFRP per aircraft
   Aircraft retirement: ~25–30 years service life
   Annual retirements: ~1,200–1,500 aircraft → ~40,000 tonnes CFRP/yr

   AUTOMOTIVE CFRP:
   ─────────────────
   Low volume today but growing
   BMW i-series: ~50 kg CF per vehicle
   End-of-life vehicle regulations (EU: 85% by weight recyclable)
   CFRP: counted toward recycled content only if actually recycled
   Pressure on automotive OEMs for CF recycling solution

   SPORTING GOODS AND CONSUMER:
   ──────────────────────────────
   Small volumes, highly dispersed, low recovery rate
   Golf shafts, bicycle frames, tennis rackets: typically landfilled
```

## Mechanical Recycling

The simplest but lowest-value approach.

```
   PROCESS:
   ─────────
   Shred / grind composite waste
   → Coarse: 10–50 mm chunks ("recyclate")
   → Fine: < 1 mm powder (filler grade)

   PROPERTIES OF RECYCLED MATERIAL:
   ───────────────────────────────────
   Coarse recyclate: irregular shape, variable dimensions
   Short/discontinuous fiber + resin matrix chunks
   Fiber aspect ratio: very low (< 10:1 after grinding)
   Tensile modulus (recyclate + new matrix): ~30–50% virgin CF/epoxy compound
   Application: non-structural filler, construction aggregate

   ECONOMIC VALUE:
   ───────────────
   Recyclate price: $0.5–3/kg (vs. virgin CF: $25–100/kg)
   Process cost: $0.5–2/kg
   Net margin: marginal or negative without carbon credit

   APPLICATIONS:
   ──────────────
   Construction: road surfaces (trial projects, UK, Germany)
   Cement additive: finely ground CF as reinforcement for concrete
   Injection molding: CFRP recyclate + PP/nylon → short fiber compound
   Bulk moulding compound (BMC): discontinuous rCF in thermoset binder
```

## Thermal Recycling

### Pyrolysis

```
   PYROLYSIS PROCESS:
   ───────────────────
   Heat composite in inert atmosphere (N2) to 450–700°C
   Polymer matrix decomposes → volatile organic compounds
   → Collected as pyrolysis oil or gas (fuel use)
   Remaining: carbon fiber skeleton

   PRODUCTS:
   ──────────
   rCF (recycled carbon fiber): long fibers, mostly intact
   Pyrolysis oil: fuel value (~22 MJ/kg)
   Char: some carbon deposition on fiber surface

   FIBER QUALITY:
   ───────────────
   Tensile strength retention: 75–90% of virgin (surface oxidation)
   Modulus retention: 90–100% (structure intact)
   Surface contamination: char/residue → reduces matrix adhesion
   Sizing: completely burned off → requires re-sizing for use
   Aspect ratio: maintained (no fragmentation)

   COMMERCIAL PLANTS:
   ───────────────────
   ELG Carbon Fibre (UK/Germany): pyrolysis of aircraft CFRP
   Karborek (Italy): batch pyrolysis
   Gen 2 Carbon (UK): working with aerospace clients
   Boeing: partnership with multiple recyclers for 787 out-of-life fiber

   ECONOMICS:
   ───────────
   Process cost: $3–8/kg rCF
   rCF selling price: $8–20/kg (vs. virgin IM7: $30–50/kg)
   Value gap: ~50% price discount vs. virgin — acceptable for some uses
```

### Fluidized Bed Process

```
   FLUIDIZED BED:
   ───────────────
   Composite particles fed into bed of hot sand/silica (400–500°C)
   Air-fluidized bed: oxidative conditions
   Resin burns → CO2, H2O, heat
   Fiber: lighter, carried up by fluidized air → separate by elutriation

   ADVANTAGES: handles contaminated, complex parts (painted, with inserts)
   DISADVANTAGES: fiber damage (abrasion in bed), shorter fibers, lower value

   University of Nottingham (Turner group): developed this process commercially
   Suitable for: contaminated manufacturing waste, complex parts
```

### Microwave-Assisted Pyrolysis

```
   MICROWAVE PYROLYSIS:
   ─────────────────────
   Carbon fiber absorbs microwave strongly (conductive)
   Internal heating → rapid pyrolysis of surrounding matrix
   Lower temperature possible (450–500°C)
   More energy efficient than conventional pyrolysis

   CHALLENGE: CF fiber arrangement affects uniformity of heating
   SCALE: pilot scale (2023), not yet commercial production scale
   POTENTIAL: faster cycle, lower energy, better fiber quality preservation
```

## Chemical Recycling (Solvolysis)

The most theoretically attractive approach — dissolve the matrix chemically
while preserving fibers. More selective than thermal.

```
   SOLVENT-BASED PROCESSES:
   ─────────────────────────
   Supercritical fluids (water, acetone, ethanol, methanol):
   T = 200–400°C, P = 5–40 MPa
   Epoxy backbone cleaves → monomers or oligomers in solution
   CF: essentially clean surfaces, minimal damage

   FIBER QUALITY (supercritical solvolysis):
   ──────────────────────────────────────────
   Tensile strength retention: 85–95%
   Surface: very clean, as-manufactured appearance
   Sizing: removed (requires re-sizing for structural use)
   Modulus: 95–100% retention

   ECONOMICS:
   ───────────
   Process cost: $15–40/kg (expensive pressurized equipment)
   This is the fundamental barrier to scale-up

   COMMERCIAL STATUS:
   ───────────────────
   Toray / CHOMARAT: solvolysis-based fine fiber tow
   Vartega (USA): sub-critical solvolysis, startup
   Connora Technologies: amine-hardener design for chemical recyclability

   DESIGNS FOR CHEMICAL RECYCLING:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Cleavable hardener epoxy (Connora, Arkema):
   Amine with hydrolyzable group → degradable at mild conditions (80°C, pH 2)
   Vitrimer epoxy: dynamic covalent bonds → dissolve/reform at elevated T
   These chemistries not yet widespread but growing interest
```

## Recycled Carbon Fiber Applications

### What rCF Is Used For Today

```
   NONWOVEN MAT / FELT:
   ─────────────────────
   Random recycled CF in web form
   Used with new thermoset or thermoplastic matrix
   → Automotive interior, under-hood panels
   → Non-structural panels
   Stiffness: 20–40 GPa (vs. woven 45–60 GPa, UD 100+ GPa)

   INJECTION MOLDING COMPOUND:
   ────────────────────────────
   rCF + PA66 or PP: chopped rCF compound
   Competition with virgin short GF: rCF better per unit weight
   → Automotive structural brackets, electronics housing
   Companies: SGL Carbon, ELG/Hanwha, Teijin (Tenax rCF)

   PAPER / NONWOVEN TECHNOLOGY:
   ──────────────────────────────
   Wet-laid rCF paper: very random, very short fibers
   Impregnated → low-cost CFRP sheet
   → Electromagnetic shielding, thermal management, gaskets

   PREPREG (emerging):
   ────────────────────
   rCF tow → recycled tow prepreg
   Airbus: trials of rCF non-crimp fabric in secondary structure
   Quality sufficient for non-primary structure
```

## Wind Blade End-of-Life: The Urgent Problem

Wind blade disposal has driven regulatory attention:

```
   BLADE DISPOSAL CHALLENGE:
   ──────────────────────────
   >95% glass fiber (GFRP), not carbon
   GFRP: lower value to recycle than CFRP
   Blade length: 50–80+ m → transport logistics
   Landfill: banned in Germany since 2003, EU trend
   Incineration: CO2 emission, fiber not recovered
   Current practice (USA): mostly landfill (~90%)

   REGULATORY RESPONSE:
   ─────────────────────
   Germany: GFRP landfill ban → all blades must be recycled
   Denmark: producer responsibility scheme in development
   EU: wind energy regulation 2022 → recyclability requirements
   US: pressure from major utilities and investors

   CEMENT CO-PROCESSING (dominant solution for GFRP blades 2020–):
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Shredded blade material added to cement kiln
   Organic fraction burns → energy
   Glass fiber + silica → substitute for raw meal (calcium silicate)
   Chemical value: ~$50/tonne (vs. $10–20 for landfill cost)
   Carbon recovered: none (glass, not carbon)
   Siemens Gamesa, Vestas: active cement kiln programs

   BLADE BUILDING MATERIALS (emerging):
   ─────────────────────────────────────
   GFRP chunks → aggregate for construction panels
   GFRP shreds → concrete reinforcement
   Structural applications: research stage
```

## Thermoplastic Composite Recycling Advantage

```
   CF/PEEK OR CF/PPS END-OF-LIFE:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Heat above Tm → melt → can be re-processed
   Fiber shortening: unavoidable in melt processing (shear cuts fiber)
   One melt cycle: 70–80% fiber length retention
   Multiple cycles: rapid degradation

   WELD-AND-REPAIR:
   ──────────────────
   Thermoplastic composites can be welded to each other (no adhesive)
   Induction welding, resistance welding, ultrasonic welding
   → In-situ repair by re-welding
   → Re-weld and reuse structural parts (vs. thermoset: no re-melt, discard)

   AIRBUS THERMOPLASTIC COMPOSITE PIVOT:
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   Airbus 2023 research: CF/PEKK and CF/PEEK structures
   Long-term sustainability argument: recyclable vs. thermoset CFRP
   Thermoplastic CFRP also faster to process (stamp, press — no autoclave)
   Weight penalty: minimal if designed correctly
```

## The CO2 Balance: Is CFRP Sustainable?

```
   LCA: CFRP PRODUCTION VS. LIFETIME FUEL SAVINGS (aircraft):
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   CFRP production (cradle to gate): ~25–35 t CO2eq per tonne CF
   CFRP in aircraft (50 tonnes, 787): ~1,500 t CO2eq to produce

   FUEL SAVING from CFRP weight reduction:
   50,000 lb weight saving × 20 year service life × 4,000 flights/yr:
   Fuel saving ~25,000 tonnes over life
   CO2 from saved fuel: ~78,000 tonnes CO2

   NET: ~76,500 t CO2 avoided vs. ~1,500 t to produce
   CO2 payback: ~100,000 km of flight (1–2 months of aircraft operation)

   WIND TURBINE:
   ──────────────
   CF spar cap: adds cost, slightly adds production CO2
   Energy gain: +5–8% energy capture per turbine (lighter spar → longer blade)
   Over 20 year life: energy gain >> production CO2
   CF wind energy LCA: strongly positive

   CAVEAT: Production CO2 is from PAN precursor + high-T carbonization
   If future electricity is renewable: CF production CO2 could drop 60–80%
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| End-of-life question | Answer |
|----------------------|--------|
| Best thermoset recycling for fiber quality | Pyrolysis or solvolysis |
| Best thermoset recycling for volume | Cement co-processing (especially GFRP) |
| What can rCF do today commercially? | Nonwoven mats, injection compounds, non-primary structure |
| Why not recycle all CFRP? | Economics: rCF at $8–15/kg vs. processing cost $3–8/kg — marginal |
| Wind blade disposal solution today | Cement kiln co-processing (Germany), landfill (USA) |
| Best sustainable composite choice? | CF/PEEK or CF/PPS thermoplastic (recyclable, repairable) |
| Is CFRP CO2-positive for aircraft? | Yes — fuel savings >> production CO2 within months of service |

## Common Confusion Points

**Recycled carbon fiber ≠ recycled carbon fiber prepreg**: rCF from pyrolysis
is loose fiber (short, random, or aligned in some processes). Converting to
prepreg requires re-sizing (expensive), arrangement into tow or fabric (labor),
and impregnation. The value chain to make rCF prepreg is currently uneconomic
for most producers. rCF goes into compounds and nonwovens, not structural prepreg.

**Thermoplastic composites are not yet "recyclable" in practice**: CF/PEEK
can be melted in principle. In practice, the recycling infrastructure does not
exist for mixed composite parts — disassembly, sorting by resin type, and
re-processing are not yet commercial at scale. "Recyclable" is a potential,
not a current claim.

**GFRP blade cement co-processing is chemical recycling in name only**: The
glass fiber contributes to the cement chemistry (as reactive silica/alumina),
but as a filler — not recovered as fiber. The value is primarily combustion
energy and raw material substitution (~$50–80/tonne), not fiber recovery.
The term "recycling" here is technically accurate (material is used) but the
fiber function is entirely lost.
