# Architecture as Discipline — Overview

## The Big Picture

Architecture sits at the intersection of three domains that rarely collaborate well. The tension between them is the discipline.

```
  ARCHITECTURE sits at the intersection of four domains:

  ART:
  +------------+
  | proportion |
  | light      |
  | beauty     |
  | meaning    |
  +------------+

  ENGINEERING:
  +--------------+
  | structure    |
  | thermal      |
  | acoustics    |
  | fire/safety  |
  +--------------+

  SOCIAL SCIENCE:
  +----------+
  | behavior |
  | culture  |
  | use      |
  | equity   |
  +----------+

  ECONOMICS:
  +--------------+
  | budget       |
  | schedule     |
  | ROI          |
  | real estate  |
  +--------------+

  None of these alone makes architecture.
  All four simultaneously is the architect's job.
```

Vitruvius wrote *De Architectura* (~15 BCE) and the organizing framework still holds:

```
  FIRMITAS           UTILITAS           VENUSTAS
  ----------------   ----------------   ----------------
  Structural         Functional         Beauty /
  Integrity          Purpose            Delight

  "Does it           "Does it           "Does it
  stand up?"         work?"             matter?"

  Engineering        Programming        Art + culture
  concern            concern            concern

  The building       The building       The building
  won't fall.        serves its         elevates the
                     purpose.           human spirit.
```

The triad has not been improved on in 2,000 years because it is complete: remove any leg and you lose something essential. A beautiful building that leaks is a failure (firmitas). A sound structure with wrong room sizes is a failure (utilitas). A functional box with no spatial quality is not architecture (venustas).

## The Five Actors

Every building project involves the same five roles regardless of scale.

```
THE PROJECT TEAM

  ARCHITECT:
    Leads design. Integrates all disciplines.
    Licensed, stamps drawings.

  STRUCTURAL ENGINEER:
    Sizes members: columns, beams, foundations.
    PE-stamps structural drawings.

  MEP ENGINEER:
    Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing.
    40-60% of building cost. PE-stamps MEP drawings.

  CONTRACTOR:
    Builds. Manages subs, schedule, cost.

  All four feed into:

  CLIENT:
    Pays. Sets program. NOT the end user.

  Which serves:

  END USER:
    Occupies. Often not consulted.

  The client-user gap is architecture's most persistent failure mode.
  A developer builds offices for tenants they have never met.
  A hospital CEO designs for patients they will never be.
  An office VP commissions a data center for ops engineers
  who are brought in after design is locked.
```

## Professional Licensure

Architecture is tightly regulated. You cannot legally call yourself an "architect" or stamp drawings without a license in most jurisdictions.

### International Comparison

| Country | Licensing Body | Path |
|---------|---------------|------|
| **US** | State boards / NCARB | B.Arch/M.Arch + AXP (5,600 hrs) + ARE 6.0 |
| **UK** | ARB (statutory) + RIBA (professional) | Part 1 + Part 2 + Part 3 (7 years minimum) |
| **EU** | Varies; EU Directive 2005/36 mutual recognition | Typically 5+2 years academic + experience |
| **Canada** | CACB / provincial associations | M.Arch + EOCA experience record + exam |
| **Australia** | State ARBs + AACA | B.Arch + 2 yrs experience + AACA competency |
| **Japan** | Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport | Class 1 Architect — notoriously rigorous exam |

The UK ARB/RIBA distinction: ARB is the statutory registration board (legal title protection). RIBA is the professional institute (chartered membership, CPD, conduct). You can be one without the other — but in practice most UK architects hold both.

## Scale and Cost Reality

Buildings are the most expensive, longest-lived artifacts humans create. This shapes everything about how architecture is practiced.

```
  COST RANGE (US construction, 2024)
  ===================================

  Garden shed             $50–100/sqft
  Single-family house     $200–500/sqft     (2,000 sqft = $500K)
  Mid-rise office         $250–450/sqft     (100,000 sqft = $35M)
  Hospital / medical      $1,000–2,500/sqft (500,000 sqft = $750M+)
  Laboratory / cleanroom  $1,500–5,000/sqft
  Data center             $10M–500M+        (power density determines cost)
  Performing arts center  $800–2,000/sqft   (acoustic spec drives cost)

  SERVICE LIFE
  ============
  Wood-frame residential     40–60 years
  Commercial steel/concrete  50–80 years
  Institutional / civic      75–150 years
  Historic / monumental      200–1,000+ years

  THE COST OF LATE DECISIONS
  ==========================
  Decision made in Programming     →  costs 1x to change
  Decision made in Schematic       →  costs 5–10x to change
  Decision made in Design Dev.     →  costs 20–50x to change
  Decision made in CDs             →  costs 100–200x to change
  Change made in construction      →  costs 500–1,000x+ (RFI + delay)

  This is the Boehm curve — same as software.
  Front-load the design, not the documentation.
```

## The Software Architecture Parallel

The term "software architect" was borrowed deliberately. The problems are isomorphic at a deep level:

```
  BUILDING ARCHITECTURE          SOFTWARE ARCHITECTURE
  =====================          =====================
  Program (space requirements)   Requirements / product brief
  Schematic Design               System design / ADRs
  Design Development             Detailed design / API contracts
  Construction Documents         Technical specification
  Construction                   Implementation / sprint
  Occupancy + POE                Production + monitoring

  Conway's Law (physical):       Conway's Law (Melvin Conway 1968):
  Organizations shape buildings  Teams design systems that mirror
  which in turn shape how        their communication structure.
  organizations work.

  "Big Ball of Mud" building     "Big Ball of Mud" (Foote & Yoder
  — no hierarchy, no clear       1997) — accumulation of expedient
  circulation, no program        patches; no coherent structure.
  logic. Common in renovated     Same failure mode, same name.
  buildings that accreted
  over decades.
```

Specific structural parallels:

```
  BUILDING ELEMENT               SOFTWARE ANALOGUE
  ----------------               -----------------
  Structural grid (column bay)   API contract / interface boundary
  Load path                      Call graph / dependency chain
  Shear wall (lateral force)     Rate limiter / circuit breaker
  Party wall (fire separation)   Service boundary / domain boundary
  Mechanical room                Infrastructure / platform layer
  Curtain wall (facade)          Presentation / API gateway layer
  Circulation corridor           Message bus / event stream
  Program adjacency matrix       Service dependency matrix
```

The software discipline borrowed the vocabulary because the cognitive challenge is identical: impose coherent order on a complex system that must satisfy simultaneous conflicting constraints, serve users who did not write the brief, remain maintainable for decades, and be extensible by people who were not involved in the original design.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Approach | Why |
|-----------|----------|-----|
| Long-span open floor plan (>12m) | Steel moment frame or composite | Steel spans longest; concrete needs post-tensioning for >12m |
| Lateral resistance in seismic zone | Moment frame or braced frame (steel); shear wall (concrete) | Ductility requirements drive system choice; shear walls are stiffest |
| Hot-dry climate, minimize HVAC | Thermal mass + night ventilation + small windows | Mass stores coolth overnight; re-radiates during day; reduces solar gain |
| Hot-humid climate, minimize HVAC | Cross ventilation + shading + elevated floors | Airflow and shade are primary; mass doesn't help when nights are warm |
| Speed of delivery paramount | Design-build or CM-at-risk | Overlapping design and construction phases; single point of responsibility |
| Quality / design control paramount | Design-bid-build (traditional) | Full design documentation before construction begins; competitive bidding |
| Sustainability certification needed | LEED / Passivhaus / BREEAM framework from schematic design | Integrated early; retroactive certification is expensive and limited |
| Adaptive reuse of existing structure | Structural assessment first; code compliance survey | Existing structure constrains everything; understand before designing |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Architecture" vs "design" vs "planning"**: In professional usage, "architecture" is licensed building design (ARE). "Design" is generic. "Planning" is land-use and policy at city scale (AICP). These are three different professions with three different exams.

**Who stamps what**: The architect stamps architectural drawings. The PE stamps structural and MEP drawings. Neither stamps the other's work — professional liability is separated. On a large project, there may be 5–10 licensed professionals each stamping their own sheets.

**Design-build**: In design-build delivery, one entity contracts for both design and construction. The architect reports to the contractor, not the owner. This compresses schedule but shifts design authority. Common in industrial, data center, and housing construction.

**CDs are not the design**: Construction Documents document what has already been designed. Design happens in SD/DD. CDs are the contract document — the specification that the contractor bids against and builds from. Treating CDs as a design phase is a project management failure and a cost overrun waiting to happen.

**The client is not the user**: In commercial development, the building owner is the client. The tenants are the users. In healthcare, the hospital administration is the client. The patients and clinical staff are the users. The design process that does not solicit user input early produces buildings that work for the client's business model but fail the people who actually inhabit them.

**Scale confusion**: Architecture operates simultaneously at 1"=1/16" (site plan at 1:200) and full scale (door hardware detail at 1:1). The same project requires decisions at both ends. The VP who thinks only at the macro scale and delegates all the details will be surprised when the details fail.
