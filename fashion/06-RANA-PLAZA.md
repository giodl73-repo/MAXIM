## The Sequence of Events

### The Day Before

```
APRIL 23, 2013
  Building inspectors examine Rana Plaza
  Visible cracks in the structure discovered
  Inspector's verdict: building unsafe; evacuate

  Bank and retail shops on lower floors: CLOSED following order

  Factory owners and managers on upper floors:
  OVERRULE the order; tell workers to return next morning
  Workers: many reported fear; some tried to refuse
  Managers: threatened workers with loss of pay; dismissal
  Garment workers (earning ~$38/month, minimum wage at the time):
  could not afford to lose a single day's wages

APRIL 24, 2013
  8:00 AM: Workers enter Rana Plaza
  8:45 AM: Power fails; generators start; additional vibration load
  8:57 AM: Building collapses
```

### The Collapse

```
THE BUILDING
  Originally permitted for 6 floors (commercial + residential)
  4 additional floors added illegally
  Heavy industrial equipment (generators, stitching machines)
  on floors never designed to hold that weight
  Design load: residential. Actual load: industrial.

WHY THE BUILDING WAS INADEQUATE
  Bangladesh construction oversight: weak; corruption widespread
  Factory owner (Sohel Rana) had political connections
  Building inspector Abdur Razzak Khan:
    reported cracks; issued closure order
    Was overruled
  Khan later jailed -- for the report that was correct

RESCUE
  17-day rescue operation
  Last survivor found: Reshma Begum, 17 days after collapse
  Her survival: remarkable; physical space in the rubble
  Her story: became symbol of resilience

ACCOUNTABILITY
  Sohel Rana: arrested; murder charges filed
  Trial: still ongoing as of 2024 (11+ years later)
  Factory managers: some charged; complex legal proceedings
  Brands: civil liability; compensation processes varied
```

## The Historical Context: Not an Isolated Event

```
PATTERN OF GARMENT INDUSTRY DISASTERS
  1911: Triangle Shirtwaist Factory (NYC) -- 146 dead
        -> US labor law reform; fire safety requirements
  2010: Spectrum Sweater factory fire (Bangladesh) -- 30 dead
  2012: Ali Enterprises fire (Karachi, Pakistan) -- 258 dead
  2012: Tazreen Fashions fire (Bangladesh) -- 112 dead
  2013: Rana Plaza collapse -- 1,134 dead
  2014: Hameem Group fire (Bangladesh) -- 7 dead
  ...

  Each disaster generated attention; each was followed
  by the next preventable catastrophe.

  WHY THE PATTERN?
  1. Price pressure: brands squeeze factory prices
     Factory owners cut costs; safety is a cost
  2. Legal structure: brands are not employers of factory workers
     Limited legal liability for brand
  3. Audit failure: announced audits miss systemic issues
  4. Supply chain length: brand may not know if sub-contracted
  5. Economic desperation: workers cannot refuse work
```

## The Systemic Cause: Price Pressure

```
HOW PRICE PRESSURE KILLS WORKERS
----------------------------------

Brand procurement meeting (London/NYC/Paris):
  Last year's unit price: $3.50
  Target this year: $3.20
  (10% cost reduction is standard annual ask)

Factory owner (Dhaka):
  Cannot reduce materials cost much
  Cannot reduce transport cost
  Can reduce: maintenance, safety equipment, worker wages,
              overtime compliance

  Safety inspection bribe: $500
  Building structural repair: $50,000
  Decision: don't repair

  The brand doesn't see this decision.
  The brand only sees: price was met.
  The workers bear the risk.

PRICE DISCOVERY DISCONNECT
  Brand knows what they pay factory.
  Brand does not know what happens when factory owner needs to
  shave 30 cents per unit off costs.
  The supply chain obscures the connection.
```

## Responses: The Bangladesh Accord and Alliance

### Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety

```
ACCORD (legally binding)
  Signed: May 2013 (weeks after Rana Plaza)
  Signatories: 220 mostly European brands (H&M, Inditex, PVH, Tesco,
               Primark, C&A, s.Oliver, and others)
  Key terms:
  - Independent safety inspections (not self-certification)
  - BINDING: brands legally obligated to pay for required remediation
  - Workers: can refuse unsafe work without penalty
  - Joint union-brand committee: governing body includes workers
  - Duration: initially 5 years; extended; became International Accord

  WHY "BINDING" MATTERS:
  Previous codes of conduct: voluntary; no enforcement mechanism
  Accord: legally enforceable in country of brand domicile
  If H&M doesn't fund repairs at an Accord factory, they can be sued
  (This has been used: Inditex faced arbitration)

RESULTS (2013-2018):
  Inspected: 1,600+ factories
  Remediation required: hundreds of safety issues per factory on average
  Completed remediation: significant improvement across covered factories
  Major structural failures: dramatically reduced in Accord factories
```

### Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety

```
ALLIANCE (voluntary)
  Signed: 2013
  Signatories: 26 North American brands (Walmart, Gap, Target, VF, etc.)
  Less binding: non-legally-enforceable
  Own inspection program; own reporting
  Criticism: brands policing themselves; weaker accountability

  COMPARISON TO ACCORD:
  Accord: Trade union involvement; legally binding; independent inspectors
  Alliance: Brand-controlled; voluntary commitments

  Both: expired 2018
  Accord evolved into: International Accord (2021-)
    Extended scope to include Pakistan
    Higher enforcement mechanisms
    Walmart and US brands still largely not signatories
```

## Compensation: Who Paid, and How Much

```
RANA PLAZA DONORS TRUST FUND
  Goal: $40 million (full compensation for all victims per ILO formula)
  Raised: ~$30 million (shortfall persisted)

  Who contributed:
  Primark: paid voluntarily (accepted moral responsibility)
  Loblaw (Joe Fresh): contributed significantly
  Benetton: contributed after initially denying connection
  Walmart: initially denied involvement despite evidence;
           eventually contributed small amount to trust fund

  Who held out longest / contributed least proportionally:
  Some North American brands
  El Corte Ingles (Spain): long holdout

  TOTAL COMPENSATION PER WORKER
  Median payment: $1,000-3,000 per family (final amounts varied)
  Many with permanent disabilities received inadequate amounts
  Compensation was less than comparable industrial accident settlements
  in developed countries by orders of magnitude
```

## The Triangle Shirtwaist Parallel

```
TRIANGLE SHIRTWAIST FACTORY FIRE (March 25, 1911)
  Location: Greenwich Village, NYC
  Dead: 146 workers (mostly young immigrant women; Italian, Jewish)
  Cause: locked fire exits (to prevent workers from taking breaks)
         flammable materials, no sprinklers

  AFTERMATH:
  Frances Perkins witnessed the fire from the street
  -> Became the first female US Secretary of Labor (1933)
  -> Helped pass New Deal labor protections
  Fire inspector: Rose Schneiderman: "We have tried you good people
  of the public and we have found you wanting"
  Result: New York Factory Investigating Commission
  -> Over 30 new labor laws in NY within 3 years
  -> National model for labor safety law

RANA PLAZA PARALLEL:
  Triangle 1911: owners locked exits to control workers
  Rana Plaza 2013: managers threatened workers to return to unsafe building
  Triangle: $75 per dead worker paid in civil settlements
  Rana: ~$1,000-3,000 per family (110 years later; similar structural inadequacy)
  Triangle -> US labor reform
  Rana Plaza -> Bangladesh Accord (meaningful but incomplete)

  THE DIFFERENCE:
  Triangle happened in the importing country.
  Citizens of NY witnessed it; demanded reform.
  Rana Plaza happened 8,000 miles from the consuming country.
  Consumers could not see it directly.
  This geographic distance enabled moral distance.
```

## Structural Ethics: The Accountability Problem

```
THE LEGAL STRUCTURE THAT ENABLES HARM
  Brand: incorporated in US, UK, EU
  Factory: incorporated in Bangladesh
  Workers: employed by factory, not brand

  RESULT: Brand has no legal employment relationship with workers
  Brand can argue: "We didn't employ them; the factory did"
  Factory can argue: "We had to cut costs; the brand demanded it"

  No one responsible: everyone responsible

PROPOSED SOLUTIONS UNDER DEBATE
  1. SUPPLY CHAIN DUE DILIGENCE LAWS
     Germany: Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (2023) -- supply chain
     due diligence law; brands liable for violations at suppliers
     France: Loi de Vigilance (2017): similar requirement for large French firms
     EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CS3D): 2024
     USA: No equivalent federal law yet

  2. BINDING WAGE FLOORS
     Living wage requirements in contracts
     ILO estimates living wage in Bangladesh: ~$500/month (2023)
     Current minimum: ~$110/month
     Gap: significant

  3. PRICE FLOOR REQUIREMENTS
     Legislation that would require brands to pay prices that allow
     living wages at suppliers
     Not yet enacted anywhere

  4. LEGAL STANDING FOR WORKERS
     Allowing workers to sue brands directly in brand's home country
     Currently: very limited (some tort-based cases in UK courts)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Rana Plaza was an accident"** -- It was a preventable catastrophe made
inevitable by: illegal construction, ignored inspection orders, economic coercion
of workers, and brand price pressure that made every safety shortcut rational
for the factory owner. Accident implies unforeseeable; this was foreseen.

**"Brands didn't know"** -- Some brands knew; some plausibly didn't due to
sub-contracting without disclosure. "Didn't know" is not a defense when the
supply chain opacity that creates that ignorance is the structural problem.
Due diligence laws address exactly this: you are responsible for knowing.

**"The Accord fixed Bangladesh"** -- The Accord significantly improved fire and
building safety in the covered 1,600 factories. It did not address: wage levels,
working hours, freedom of association, factory-level gender-based violence, or
the Tier 2/3 supply chain. The covered factories improved; the structure creating
the problem persisted.

**"Ethical fashion is too expensive for most people"** -- True for some consumers.
Fully internalizing labor and environmental costs would raise prices. The question
is who bears those costs: they are currently borne by workers and ecosystems,
not by consumers. Redistributing those costs is a political choice, not a
purely economic one.
