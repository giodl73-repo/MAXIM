## Materials: The Fiber Problem

### Conventional Fibers and Their Issues

```
COTTON (natural, plant-based)
  Good: biodegradable; comfortable; durable
  Bad: water-intensive (3,000L per T-shirt)
       heavy pesticide use (~24% of insecticides globally)
       land use intensive

  ORGANIC COTTON: no synthetic pesticides; no GMO
  Better: ~90% less water in some studies
  Problem: lower yield per acre; more land required
  Market: ~1% of all cotton production (GOTS certified)

POLYESTER / NYLON (synthetic, petroleum-based)
  Good: durable, cheap, versatile, hydrophobic (sportswear)
  Bad: petroleum-derived; doesn't biodegrade (decades to centuries)
       releases microplastics when washed (washing machine filter
       captures ~86% of microfibers; most don't have filters)

  RECYCLED POLYESTER (rPET): from PET bottles
  Better than virgin polyester: ~30-50% lower carbon
  Still: same microplastic shedding; still doesn't biodegrade
  "Recycled" does not mean "sustainable"

VISCOSE / RAYON / MODAL (semi-synthetic, wood pulp)
  Process: cellulose dissolved in chemicals -> fiber
  "Natural" origin; but chemical-intensive production
  Closed-loop process (Lyocell/TENCEL): solvent recycled
  Open-loop (conventional rayon): chemical waste discharged

WOOL (natural, animal)
  Good: renewable; biodegradable; moisture-wicking; insulating
  Bad: land use; methane (sheep); water use in processing
       Animal welfare concerns (mulesing in merino wool)
  Certified: ZQ Merino, Responsible Wool Standard (RWS)

SILK (natural, animal)
  Good: biodegradable; luxurious
  Bad: silkworm farming (Bombyx mori); limited scale
  Alternative: Ahimsa silk (no boiling of live cocoons)

DOWN (natural, bird feather)
  Good: excellent insulation; lightweight; compressible
  Bad: live plucking concerns; waterfowl welfare
  Certified: Responsible Down Standard (RDS)
```

## Production: How Things Are Made

### Dyeing and Chemical Use

```
TEXTILE DYEING (largest industrial water polluter after oil and gas)
  Conventional process:
  1. Fiber pre-treatment (scour, bleach)
  2. Dyebath (fiber + water + dye + chemicals + heat)
  3. Rinse (multiple)
  4. Finishing chemicals (softeners, water repellency, anti-wrinkle)

  Most dyeing: Asia (China, Bangladesh, India, Vietnam)
  Wastewater treatment: variable; regulatory enforcement: weak
  ZDHC (Zero Discharge of Hazardous Chemicals): industry program
  Reality: partial adoption; self-reported

  WATERLESS DYEING:
  DyeCoo (Netherlands): supercritical CO2 as dye medium; no water
  AirDye: compressed air; 95% less water
  Both: proven technology; very limited adoption (cost + retooling)

CERTIFICATIONS THAT MATTER FOR CHEMICALS
  OEKO-TEX Standard 100: tests for 100+ harmful substances in finished product
  GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard): organic fiber + social criteria
  bluesign: chemical management at dye house level; most rigorous for process
  Fair Trade: labor focus (wages, working conditions); not environmental
```

### Manufacturing Methods

```
3D KNITTING / SEAMLESS MANUFACTURING
  Shima Seiki, Stoll (machinery makers)
  Garment knitted to shape in one step; minimal or zero cutting waste
  Traditional cut-and-sew: 15-20% fabric waste (cut away and discarded)
  3D knitting: near-zero fabric waste
  Current limitation: cost; speed; suitable fiber range
  Market: Adidas Futurecraft, Nike Flyknit (footwear pioneered this)
           Apparel: growing slowly

DIGITAL PRINTING
  Inkjet printing directly onto fabric
  Advantages: on-demand; no minimum run; no dye setup waste
  Limitations: durability (wash fastness); range of fabrics; speed

ON-DEMAND MANUFACTURING
  Make only what is ordered
  Eliminates overproduction and markdown/disposal
  Digital sampling (3D/CLO virtual fashion) reduces physical sampling waste
  Brands: Unspun (on-demand jeans), others
  Scale: very limited; unit economics not yet comparable to mass production
```

## Business Models: Circularity

The circular economy applied to fashion: keep products and materials in use longer.

```
LINEAR MODEL (current dominant)
  Make -> Use -> Dispose
  Resource extraction -> production -> sale -> landfill

CIRCULAR MODEL (alternative)
  Make -> Use -> Collect -> Recycle/Reuse -> Make again
  Design for longevity and disassembly
  Take-back programs
  Material recovery at end of life

THE FIVE CIRCULAR STRATEGIES:
  1. DURABILITY: make it last longer
  2. REPAIRABILITY: make it fixable (design for repair)
  3. RESALE: extend life through second-hand market
  4. RENTAL/SUBSCRIPTION: one garment, many users
  5. RECYCLING: break down and reuse materials
```

### Resale Market

```
RESALE PLATFORMS AND SCALE
  The RealReal (US): luxury consignment; authenticated
  Vestiaire Collective (EU): luxury/contemporary resale; authenticated
  Depop: peer-to-peer; young consumer; fashion-forward
  Poshmark (US): broad range; community-focused
  StockX: primarily sneakers; auction model
  ThredUp: mass market; "online consignment"
  eBay: all categories; fashion significant share

  Market size: ~$80B globally by 2026 (per ThredUp estimates)
  Faster growth than primary market

BRAND-OWNED RESALE:
  Patagonia Worn Wear (2012): earliest luxury/performance brand resale
  Eileen Fisher Renew
  Gucci Vault (luxury resale by Kering)
  Balenciaga x Vestiaire Collective
  Stella McCartney x The RealReal

  MOTIVATION: control brand narrative; capture resale economics
  RISK: cannibalize new sales (brands accept this; resale market
        growing regardless -- better to participate)
```

### Rental Models

```
FASHION RENTAL LANDSCAPE
  Rent the Runway (US): subscription + one-time rental; designer
  HURR (UK): peer-to-peer rental; Selfridges partnership
  By Rotation (UK): peer-to-peer; mobile-first
  Nuuly (Urban Outfitters): subscription fashion rental
  Armarium: luxury/designer rental; concierge

  CHALLENGES:
  Logistics: cleaning, inspection, delivery, return
  Consumer behavior: "used clothes" stigma (diminishing but real)
  Per-use price: often 10-20% of retail; breaks even at 5-10 uses
  Fit: size uncertainty increases return rate

  WHO IT WORKS FOR:
  Special occasion (wedding guest, gala, party dress): clear use case
  Subscription wardrobe: controversial; net sustainability unclear
  (shipping intensity may exceed benefit of shared ownership)
```

### Repair and Longevity

```
DESIGN FOR REPAIR
  Nudie Jeans: free repair for life at any of their repair shops
  Patagonia: repair program; "Don't buy this jacket" (2011 ad)
  iFixit: independent repair culture (not fashion-specific)

  DESIGN IMPLICATIONS:
  Accessible stitching (not glued)
  Available spare parts (spare buttons included)
  Construction methods that allow disassembly
  Reinforced stress points (elbows, knees, crotch)

IKEAHIJACK / WORN WEAR MARKETING:
  Patagonia's "Don't buy this jacket" Black Friday ad (2011)
  Sales went up. This is the paradox: sustainability messaging
  can itself drive brand growth and therefore volume growth.
  The most honest sustainability position requires telling
  consumers to buy less. Most brands cannot do this commercially.
```

## Fiber Recycling Technology

```
CURRENT STATE OF TEXTILE RECYCLING (2024)
  Less than 1% of clothing is recycled into new clothing fiber
  (most "recycling" = downcycling to insulation, rags, etc.)

  WHY FIBER-TO-FIBER RECYCLING IS HARD:
  1. Blended fabrics: 60-40 cotton/polyester cannot be separated by
     current mechanical recycling
  2. Dyes and finishing chemicals: contaminate recycled material
  3. Zippers, buttons, elastics: must be removed
  4. Volume: massive input needed for viable processing plant
  5. Economics: virgin polyester often cheaper than recycled

PROMISING TECHNOLOGIES:
  Worn Again Technologies: solvent-based; separates polyester from cotton
    (licensing to H&M Group, Prada)
  Renewlane (Lenzing): cotton recycling back to lyocell fiber
  Evrnu: chemical recycling; cotton -> new cotton-like fiber
    (used by Levi's, Target in pilot programs)
  Carbios: enzymatic PET depolymerization; Michelin backing

  TIMELINE: Commercial scale likely 2027-2032 for leading technologies
  LIMITATION: Even perfect recycling doesn't solve the volume problem
              if production rates outpace recycling rates
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Organic cotton = sustainable cotton"** -- Organic cotton is better than
conventional on pesticides and GMOs. It uses similar or more water (per acre),
more land (lower yield), and still generates all the processing impacts.
"Organic" is a meaningful improvement in one dimension; it is not "sustainable"
in a complete sense.

**"Recycled polyester is sustainable"** -- Recycled polyester (rPET) has
~30-50% lower carbon footprint than virgin polyester. It still sheds microplastics;
it still doesn't biodegrade; it still comes from a petroleum-dependent supply
chain. Better, not good.

**"Rental is always better than ownership"** -- For special-occasion items with
high per-use emissions (production cost high; number of uses low), rental wins.
For items you would wear 100+ times, ownership is likely better if the shipping
and cleaning load per use of rental is significant.

**"Sustainability initiatives from fast fashion brands are greenwashing"** -- They
are often (not always) greenwashing. The test: is the sustainable line growing
as a percentage of total production, or is it growing as an absolute line while
total production also grows? The latter is marketing, not transformation.

---

The data throughout this module follows the **Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)** framework — the standard engineering methodology for comparing total environmental impact across product lifetime phases: raw material extraction -> manufacturing -> distribution -> use -> end-of-life. Every fiber comparison (cotton vs polyester vs linen) and every process comparison (conventional dyeing vs solution dyeing) is an LCA question. The ISO 14040/14044 standards define the methodology; the fashion-specific challenge is system boundary definition (do you include consumer washing? how many wears do you assume?).
