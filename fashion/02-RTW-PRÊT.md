## Historical Development of Ready-to-Wear

### Pre-History: Standardized Clothing

Before designer RTW, standardized clothing had two origins:

```
WORKWEAR / MILITARY
  19th century: military uniforms required standardized sizing
  Civil War (US): first systematic measurement of soldier bodies
  -> generated first US size chart
  Levi Strauss 501 jeans (1873): standard sizes for miners
  Workwear = mass produced; class association = low

DEPARTMENT STORE EVOLUTION
  1850s-1900s: Le Bon Marche (Paris), Macy's (NY), Harrods (London)
  Began selling ready-made coats, skirts, blouses
  Cheaper than dressmakers; faster
  Fashion without custom fitting = aspirational convenience

SEWING PATTERNS (McCall, Simplicity, Vogue Patterns)
  1860s-1950s: women made clothes at home from commercial patterns
  Vogue Patterns licensed couture designs (1950s)
  Brought couture silhouettes to home sewers
  This is NOT RTW but enabled the transition to it
```

### The 1960s Revolution

```
YOUTH CULTURE DISRUPTION
  Post-WWII baby boom reaches adulthood
  Young consumers with money; distinct taste from parents
  Old couture system: clothes for mature, wealthy, conservative women

  MARY QUANT (London, early 1960s)
  Carnaby Street; Bazaar shop
  Mini-skirt credited to Quant (contested with Courreges)
  Mass-produced at accessible price
  Chelsea / mod aesthetic; not Paris-derived

  YSL RIVE GAUCHE (1966)
  Yves Saint Laurent opens first luxury designer RTW store
  Left Bank address; affordable(r) designer clothes
  Radical act: luxury designer condescending to mass market?
  (No -- YSL understood the culture was shifting)
  Couture is for 2,000 women; Rive Gauche is for everyone with money

  PRET-A-PORTER (France)
  The French term literally: "ready to wear"
  Adopted as the high-fashion RTW designation
  Paris pret-a-porter shows created alongside couture calendar
```

## Size Systems and Standardization

The hidden enabling technology of RTW is size standardization.

```
SIZE SYSTEM PROBLEM
  Human bodies do not conform to categories.
  Standard sizes are statistical averages of a population sample.
  They fit some bodies well; most bodies imperfectly.

US WOMEN'S SIZES (historical background)
  1930s: USDA study measured 15,000 white women
  Developed first government sizing standard
  Industry adopted approximate versions
  Problem: sample was not representative (white, young, poor)

VANITY SIZING (1950s-present)
  Manufacturers gradually reduce the number on the label
  while keeping the actual measurement constant.
  A US size 12 in 1958 = a US size 6-8 today.
  Women buy more when the number is smaller.
  Comparison across brands meaningless; comparison across decades impossible.

EUROPEAN SIZING
  Based on body measurements (bust/waist/hip in cm)
  FR 38 = bust approx 82cm
  More consistent across brands
  Still has significant brand variation in practice

NUMERICAL vs. LETTER (XS/S/M/L/XL)
  Letter sizing: more flexible (covers a range)
  Numerical: implies more precision; delivers less
  Neither: reliably cross-brand or cross-country

IMPLICATIONS:
  Every brand has its own block (pattern template)
  "True to size" is a rating against a brand's own history
  Online RTW return rates: 30-40% (size-related)
```

## The RTW Production Cycle

```
TRADITIONAL RTW CALENDAR (pre-fast-fashion)
--------------------------------------------

SPRING/SUMMER COLLECTION
  Month -18: Design team begins; macro trend research
  Month -12: Fabric selection at trade shows (Premiere Vision, Paris)
  Month -9:  First design round; collection takes shape
  Month -6:  Samples made; editing; show preparation
  Month -4:  Press shows (February/March for SS)
  Month -2:  Buyer orders placed
  Month 0:   Goods manufactured; shipped from factories
  Month +2:  Arrives in stores (early June for SS)

  Approx 52-week cycle from design to retail

FALL/WINTER COLLECTION
  Same cycle, offset by 6 months
  Sept/Oct shows -> Jan/Feb store delivery

BETWEEN COLLECTIONS: Pre-Fall, Resort/Cruise
  Pre-Fall (December delivery): extends the cycle
  Resort/Cruise (May-June delivery): major commercial season
  (resort wear for vacations; significant revenue)

KEY INSIGHT:
  The traditional RTW cycle was designed for the buyer order model.
  Buyers commit to orders 6 months in advance.
  Brands produce exactly what was ordered (or slightly over).
  Inventory risk is shared with buyers (who pay for their order).
```

## Designer RTW vs. Luxury Accessories

```
THE LUXURY PYRAMID (economics)
-------------------------------

    /\
   /  \     COUTURE ($20K-150K) -- prestige, craft, press value
  /----\
 /      \   DESIGNER RTW ($500-$5K) -- brand expression, some profit
/--------\
/          \ ACCESSORIES ($200-$3K) -- THE PROFIT ENGINE
|------------|
|            | FRAGRANCE / BEAUTY ($30-$300) -- HIGH MARGIN MASS MARKET
|____________|

WHY ACCESSORIES DOMINATE LUXURY PROFITS:
  Bag: one size fits all bodies
  Bag: no sizing complexity; no returns for fit
  Bag: logo visible to others (Veblen signaling)
  Bag: same materials, lower labor than clothing
  Bag: high margin relative to cost of goods

  A $3,000 Gucci handbag: COGS (cost of goods) ~$200-400
  A $3,000 Gucci dress: COGS ~$400-600 + fitting rooms + returns

  This is why LVMH is fundamentally a handbag and perfume company
  that happens to show runway clothes.
```

## The Contemporary Segment

Contemporary sits between designer RTW and fast fashion:

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  CONTEMPORARY BRANDS (the "better" segment)                      |
|                                                                  |
|  US: Theory, Vince, Eileen Fisher, Cos (H&M premium)             |
|  UK: Reiss, LK Bennett, Whistles                                 |
|  EU: Sandro, Maje, Isabel Marant (diffusion lines)               |
|                                                                  |
|  CHARACTERISTICS:                                                |
|  - Higher quality than fast fashion; lower than designer         |
|  - Less logo-dependent than luxury                               |
|  - Targets professional adults ($75K+ income)                    |
|  - Department store + own retail + online                        |
|  - Seasonal model but faster than couture calendar               |
|                                                                  |
|  MARGIN STRUCTURE:                                               |
|  Wholesale markup: 2.2-2.5x cost                                 |
|  Retail markup: 2.2-2.5x wholesale                               |
|  Total: retail ~ 5x cost of goods                                |
|  Example: $25 factory cost -> $55 wholesale -> $120 retail       |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## The Department Store Channel (Declining)

```
TRADITIONAL DEPARTMENT STORE MODEL
  Buyer buys at wholesale; sells at retail
  Risk: unsold inventory markdown
  Revenue: margin + ancillary (beauty concessions, restaurant)

  Nordstrom, Bloomingdale's, Saks: dominant 1960s-2000s
  Department store = where middle/upper-middle class shopped

WHY IT DECLINED:
  1. Direct-to-consumer brands (DTC): brands sell online directly
  2. Luxury brands opened own flagships (less wholesale dependency)
  3. Fast fashion + Amazon: captured value/convenience seekers
  4. Mall traffic decline (US suburban mall crisis, 2010s)
  5. COVID accelerated all of the above

  Neiman Marcus: bankrupt 2020
  JC Penney: bankrupt 2020
  Lord & Taylor: bankrupt 2020
  Nordstrom survived by being best-positioned for omnichannel

  In Europe: Galeries Lafayette, Harrods, KaDeWe survive through
  luxury segment and international tourism
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Pret-a-Porter = affordable"** -- Pret-a-Porter means ready-to-wear. Chanel
pret-a-porter is RTW; it is not affordable. The term only means "off the rack,
in standard sizes" -- it says nothing about price.

**"Designer RTW is made the same way as couture, just in more sizes"** -- No.
Designer RTW is manufactured in factories (often in Italy, Portugal, or Eastern
Europe for European luxury brands). The quality is higher than fast fashion but
the production is industrial. Couture is hand-made in Paris ateliers.

**"Size 0 is a specific body measurement"** -- In US sizing, a size 0 is
approximately a 32" bust / 24" waist / 34" hip. But vanity sizing means
different brands put different measurements in a size 0. Sizing is an agreement
within a brand, not an industry standard.

**"Contemporary is just expensive fast fashion"** -- Better materials, more
durable construction, less trend-driven. The difference is observable but
requires attention: seam allowances, fabric weight, lining, construction method.
The consumer has to learn to see it.

---

Size standardization in RTW is the same coordination problem as any domain standardization: incompatible local conventions (each brand's proprietary fit block), coordination failure across the industry, and vanity sizing as a competitive race-to-the-bottom. The USDA 1930s anthropometric study was the ANSI/ISO equivalent attempt for apparel — it failed because brands had stronger incentives to differentiate than to standardize.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| When you need to...                                      | Strategy / Key point                    | Because                                               |
|----------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Maximize margin in luxury RTW                            | Lean into accessories over garments     | No sizing risk; Veblen signaling; 80-90% gross margin |
| Reduce demand forecasting risk                           | Made-to-order or see-now-buy-now model  | Eliminates inventory markdown; requires DTC channel    |
| Choose between wholesale and DTC distribution            | DTC: 2x margin; wholesale: broader reach| DTC costs more per customer acquisition               |
| Understand why contemporary segment exists               | Better RTW at $100-$500 price point     | Bridge between fast fashion and designer; professional wear|
| Diagnose sizing inconsistency across brands              | Each brand owns its fit block           | No binding industry standard; vanity sizing incentives |
| Plan a 52-week production calendar                       | Buyer orders 6 months ahead of season   | Fabric sourcing lead time drives the constraint        |
| Evaluate department store channel viability              | DTC + Amazon + luxury flagships eroded it| Multi-brand aggregation value declined with e-commerce |
