# The Fashion Industry: Supply Chain and Economics

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                  FASHION INDUSTRY STRUCTURE                       |
|                                                                  |
|  UPSTREAM (raw materials)                                        |
|  Fiber production -> Yarn spinning -> Fabric weaving/knitting    |
|  Cotton (US, India, China), Wool (Australia, NZ), Synthetics     |
|                                                                  |
|  MIDSTREAM (manufacturing)                                       |
|  Fabric -> Pattern -> Cut -> Sew -> Finish -> QC                 |
|  Bangladesh, China, Vietnam, Cambodia, Turkey, Morocco           |
|                                                                  |
|  DOWNSTREAM (distribution)                                       |
|  Brand -> Wholesale -> Retail -> Consumer                        |
|  OR: Brand -> DTC (direct to consumer online/own store)          |
|                                                                  |
|  FINANCIAL SCALE (2024)                                          |
|  Global apparel market: ~$1.5 trillion                           |
|  Luxury segment: ~$400 billion                                   |
|  Fast fashion: ~$200 billion (growing)                           |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## The Supply Chain in Detail

### Tier 1: Cut-and-Sew Factories (Final Assembly)

```
TIER 1 FACTORY
  Direct supplier to brand
  Receives: cut fabric panels, trim, thread, hardware
  Produces: finished garment
  Quality control: spec sheet compliance
  Audit: brand sends auditors; social compliance + quality

LABOR ECONOMICS (Bangladesh, 2024)
  Minimum wage: ~12,500 BDT/month (~$110/month)
  (Raised from 8,000 BDT in 2023 after protests)
  A $20 H&M shirt: factory receives ~$1.50-2.00
  Factory keeps ~$0.20-0.40 after materials
  Worker earns pennies per garment

  WHO MAKES THE MONEY:
  H&M retail markup: 3-4x factory price
  H&M gross margin: ~50-55% of revenue
  Factory margin: ~2-4% of factory price

TIER 1 GEOGRAPHY
  Bangladesh: ~40% of global garment exports; $44B industry
  China: declining share but still dominant in complex items
  Vietnam: fast-growing; $44B exports
  Cambodia: $10B exports
  Turkey: regional hub; shorter lead times for Europe
  Ethiopia: emerging (lower wages than Bangladesh; unstable)
```

### Tier 2: Fabric and Trim Suppliers

```
TIER 2 SUPPLIERS
  Weavers and knitters (produce fabric from yarn)
  Trim suppliers: zippers (YKK dominates globally), buttons, labels
  Print houses (screen print, digital print, dye)

KEY PLAYERS
  YKK: Japanese company; ~45% of global zipper market
  Coats: thread supplier; near-monopoly for certain markets
  Lenzing (TENCEL): Austrian; lyocell fiber for "sustainable" brands
  Invista (Koch Industries): Lycra/Spandex monopoly

FABRIC TRADE SHOWS (where brands source fabrics)
  Premiere Vision (Paris): premium; fashion fabrics
  Texworld (Paris): mid-market
  Intertextile (Shanghai): Asia-focused
  Kingpins (Amsterdam): denim specialist
```

### Tier 3: Raw Materials

```
FIBER ORIGINS
  Cotton:    US (Texas/California), India, China, Uzbekistan
  Wool:      Australia (~25% of global), New Zealand, China
  Silk:      China (75% of global production)
  Linen:     Belgium, France, Netherlands (flax-growing zones)
  Cashmere:  China, Mongolia, Iran
  Synthetics: Petroleum-based; mostly Asia

SUSTAINABILITY NOTES
  Cotton: water-intensive (3,000L per shirt); pesticide-heavy
  Polyester: made from petroleum; doesn't biodegrade; microplastic
  Viscose/Rayon: from wood pulp; may be sustainable if sourced properly
  Organic cotton: lower pesticides; no GMO; market premium
```

## The Economics of Fashion: Margins and Markup

```
RETAIL PRICE ANATOMY
---------------------

        COST OF GOODS (COG) = what it costs to make
              |
              | Brand markup (3-6x COG for designer)
              v
        WHOLESALE PRICE (what retailer pays)
              |
              | Retailer markup (2-2.5x wholesale)
              v
        RETAIL PRICE (what consumer pays)

EXAMPLES BY TIER:
                    COG     Wholesale  Retail  Total markup
Fast fashion         $3       ---       $15      5x
High street (Zara)   $7       ---       $30      4x
Contemporary         $25      $60       $130     5x
Designer RTW         $60      $200      $500     8x
Luxury RTW           $120     $400      $1,200   10x
Luxury bag           $150     $500      $2,000   13x

THE MARKDOWN RISK:
  Unsold inventory must be marked down.
  Each markdown erodes margin.
  Fast fashion has low per-unit margin; huge volume.
  Luxury has enormous per-unit margin; can absorb unsold inventory
  or destroy it (burning unsold goods = historical luxury practice
  to protect brand exclusivity; Burberry scandal, 2018).
```

## Working Capital and Inventory

```
THE FASHION CASH FLOW PROBLEM
  Brand places order: pays factory in 30-90 days
  Goods arrive: 60-90 days after order
  Goods sit in DC: 15-30 days
  Goods arrive in store: +7-14 days
  Consumer buys: 0-90 days after store arrival
  Retailer pays brand: 30-60 days net

  Total cycle from design to cash: 12-18 months for traditional RTW
  Working capital tied up = substantial

FAST FASHION SOLUTION:
  Faster cycle (2-3 weeks) = less capital tied up in transition
  Zara's model: designed partially in-season; less speculation risk

CONSIGNMENT vs WHOLESALE
  Wholesale: retailer buys; bears inventory risk
  Consignment: brand owns inventory in store; retailer takes % of sale
               Brand bears inventory risk; retailer bears none
  Luxury brands increasingly use shop-in-shop (concession):
  space in Selfridges run by the brand itself; full control, higher cost
```

## The Factory Compliance System

```
SOCIAL COMPLIANCE AUDIT SYSTEM
--------------------------------
Brands do not own factories.
Brands use third-party auditors (SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek)
to certify compliance with:
  - Local labor law
  - Hours (max 60/week; max 8/day with overtime)
  - Fire safety (Rana Plaza showed this was failing)
  - Chemical safety (REACH compliance in EU)
  - No child labor
  - Freedom of association (right to organize)

LIMITATIONS OF AUDIT SYSTEM:
  1. Announced audits: factories know when auditors come
  2. Brands' price pressure: forces shortcuts that auditors miss
  3. Subcontracting: factory hands orders to non-audited sub-factory
  4. Structural issues: auditors check conditions, not wage adequacy
  5. Cumulative non-compliance: pass each audit; violate daily

POST-RANA PLAZA REFORMS:
  Bangladesh Accord (2013): binding safety agreement; 220 brands sign
  Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety (US brands; less binding)
  International Accord (2021): extended; now covers Pakistan too
  Result: significant fire safety improvements in 1,600+ factories

  But: wage adequacy not addressed by safety frameworks
       Overtime enforcement varies widely
       Sub-tier factories (Tier 2, 3) largely unmonitored
```

## The Digital Disruption of Distribution

```
TRADITIONAL DISTRIBUTION (pre-2010)
  Designer -> Paris show -> Trade press coverage
  -> Buyer orders (department stores, boutiques)
  -> Wholesale delivery -> Retail sale

  Brands could NOT sell direct to consumer profitably
  (no fulfillment infrastructure; department stores had the relationships)

DIGITAL DTC (direct to consumer) TRANSFORMATION
  E-commerce platforms: Shopify, Magento
  Brands can sell direct; no wholesale markup to share
  Higher margin but: customer acquisition cost; returns; fulfillment

  DTC margin: brand captures 2x vs. wholesale
  DTC cost: customer acquisition ($30-100 per order); shipping; returns

LUXURY DTC TRANSITION
  Net-a-Porter (1999): first luxury multi-brand e-commerce
  Yoox, Matches, Farfetch: multi-brand luxury online
  Brands respond: own e-commerce (Burberry, 2009, pioneered digital luxury)
  Now: most luxury brands have own e-commerce
  Wholesale declining; own retail + DTC growing

RESALE MARKET (disruption from below)
  The RealReal, Vestiaire Collective, Depop, StockX
  Growing faster than primary market: est. $80B by 2026
  Implications: reduces demand for new goods; increases brand monitoring need
  Some brands (Gucci, Balenciaga) partnered with resale platforms
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Brands own factories"** -- Almost no major Western brands own factories.
They are brand and distribution companies that contract manufacturing globally.
This outsourcing structure enables cost minimization but creates accountability gaps.

**"Supply chain audits ensure fair conditions"** -- Audits improve measurable
conditions (fire exits, building safety). They do not address wage levels, the
fundamental driver of exploitation. A factory can pass every audit and still pay
poverty wages.

**"Luxury goods are expensive because they're costly to make"** -- A $1,500 LV bag
costs ~$100-150 to make. The price reflects brand value (decades of marketing,
exclusivity signaling, status), not production cost. The markup is a different
kind of pricing from cost-plus.

**"DTC is inherently more sustainable"** -- DTC removes the wholesale layer but
adds individual parcel shipping and returns. A single e-commerce order shipped,
returned, and reshipped may have higher carbon cost than a single wholesale pallet.

---

The supply chain tier structure (Tier 1 = cut-sew factory; Tier 2 = fabric mill; Tier 3 = fiber/chemical supplier) maps directly to software dependency management: Tier 1 = direct dependencies you can audit; Tier 2/3 = transitive dependencies with limited visibility. Brands can audit Tier 1 (their direct suppliers) but have minimal sight into Tier 2/3 — the same pattern as organizations that can vet direct vendors but cannot see into their vendors' dependencies (the Log4Shell pattern in software supply chain security).

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| When you need to...                                      | Strategy / Key concept                    | Because                                                  |
|----------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| Choose vertical integration vs outsourced manufacturing  | Vertical: time-to-market + quality control| Zara/Inditex model; higher capex, lower markdowns        |
| Assess audit reach across the supply chain               | Tier 1 auditable; Tier 2/3 are visibility gaps | Regulatory compliance (EU CSDDD) requires deeper reach   |
| Understand luxury conglomerate economics                 | LVMH bag: 80-90% gross margin             | Brand value, not production cost, drives price           |
| Evaluate DTC vs wholesale channel economics              | DTC: 2x margin; wholesale: lower CAC      | Trade-off is margin per unit vs customer acquisition cost|
| Diagnose why a $5 shirt exists                           | Volume + margin compression + labor cost externalization | Brand pricing power squeezes factory margins to <5%     |
| Assess post-Rana-Plaza regulatory landscape              | Bangladesh Accord (binding) vs Alliance (voluntary) | Binding accords with enforcement mechanisms are more effective |
| Map market concentration in luxury                       | LVMH + Kering + Richemont = ~60%+ of luxury market | Conglomerate model: shared services, brand portfolio    |
