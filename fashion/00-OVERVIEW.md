## How Fashion Moves: The Trickle-Down (and Trickle-Up)

```
CLASSIC TRICKLE-DOWN THEORY (Veblen, Simmel, 1890s-1900s)
  Haute Couture -> Luxury RTW -> High Street -> Mass Market

  Elite adopts new style
  -> Upper middle class copies to signal aspiration
  -> Middle class adopts as elite abandons
  -> Style becomes "common"; elite moves on

  TIMELINE: 2-5 year lag from haute couture to mass market (pre-1990)

CONTEMPORARY REALITY (post-internet)
  Trickle-down still operates for luxury/status goods
  Trickle-UP: Streetwear, workwear, sportswear move UPWARD
    Athletic wear -> luxury (Balenciaga x Adidas)
    Workwear -> fashion (Carhartt WIP as fashion brand)
    Sneakers -> luxury market

  FAST FASHION COLLAPSE OF THE TIMELINE
  Zara: 2-3 weeks from runway to store (not 2-5 years)
  SHEIN: 24-72 hours from trend identification to listing
  Timeline compression destroyed the exclusivity of fashion timing
```

## Fashion Weeks: The Show Calendar

```
FASHION WEEK PURPOSE
  Originally: buyers see collections, place orders
  Now also: press coverage, brand positioning, cultural event

  B2B function (buyer orders) declining -- many brands go
  direct to consumer, reducing wholesale dependence

FASHION WEEK STRUCTURE
  New York:  Women's + Men's; Feb and Sept
  London:    Innovative; younger designers; Feb and Sept
  Milan:     Italian luxury; Feb/Sept + Jan/June for menswear
  Paris:     Couture (Jan/July) + RTW (March/Oct) + Menswear

  SHOWS vs PRESENTATIONS
  Show: seated audience; models walk runway; often theatrical
  Presentation: models stand or move in space; more intimate
  Lookbook: no audience; photographed for press/buyers

  INVITATION HIERARCHY
  Front row: celebrities, press, major buyers
  Second row: influential buyers, junior editors
  Standing / general industry: everyone else
```

## Key Theoretical Frames

```
FASHION AS COMMUNICATION
  Clothing communicates before you speak:
  - Social class (fabric, fit, brand)
  - Tribe/group membership (uniform, subcultural)
  - Gender and gender performance
  - Modernity vs tradition
  - Aspirations (buying above your class, or performing another identity)

FASHION AS INDUSTRY
  $1.5 trillion global industry (2023)
  3rd largest manufacturing sector after automotive and electronics
  75% of garment workers are women
  Most garment workers earn poverty wages

FASHION AS ART vs FASHION AS COMMERCE
  The permanent tension:
  Couture as art (Galliano's Dior, McQueen's shows)
  vs. Fashion as commerce (the 4am production decision in Bangladesh)
  Same industry; incompatible self-presentations
```

## The Sustainability Crisis in Numbers

```
ENVIRONMENTAL SCALE
  10%   of annual global carbon emissions (textiles)
  20%   of industrial water pollution (textile dyeing)
  85%   of textiles end in landfill or incineration globally
  500,000 tons of microfibers released into ocean per year from washing
  3,000 liters of water to grow cotton for one T-shirt

LABOR SCALE
  ~75 million workers in global garment supply chain
  ~80% women
  Median wage: $3/day in Bangladesh; $2/day in Myanmar
  April 24, 2013: Rana Plaza collapse kills 1,134 workers

VOLUME SCALE
  2000: ~50 billion garments produced annually
  2023: ~100-120 billion garments produced annually
  Average US consumer: buys 65 garments/year; wears each ~7 times
  Average EU consumer: discards 11kg of textiles per year
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Couture means expensive ready-to-wear"** -- Haute Couture is a legally
protected designation in France, granted by the Chambre Syndicale. A brand must
meet specific production criteria. Expensive RTW is not couture. Vera Wang,
Oscar de la Renta, and other US designers make beautiful expensive clothes;
they are not haute couture.

**"Fashion is shallow"** -- Fashion encodes the deepest social anxieties and
desires of a period. What people wear communicates power, identity, aspiration,
belonging, and resistance. The sociology and theory is as rich as any cultural
field.

**"Sustainable fashion is expensive fashion"** -- Sustainable fashion costs more
because it prices in actual cost of production. Fast fashion is cheap because
it offloads costs to garment workers, ecosystems, and the future.

---

Fashion trend forecasting is a time-series prediction problem: seasonal decomposition of style adoption curves, lagged diffusion signals (runway to retail), and S-curve adoption models for new silhouettes. SHEIN uses algorithmic demand sensing (small-batch test → real-time sales signal → chase winners); Zara uses human observers in the same feedback loop. Both are OODA loops over demand data with different latency characteristics.

### Silhouette — The Primary Signal

Silhouette is to fashion what massing is to architecture: the first thing perceived,
the last thing forgotten. Every era has a dominant silhouette; departures from it read

### Proportion — The Rule of Thirds

The same principle as photographic composition: dividing the body at 1/3 and 2/3
creates visual dynamism; dividing at 1/2 creates stasis.

```
PROPORTION IN PRACTICE

  50/50 (AVOID)      1/3 + 2/3 (CLASSIC)     EMPIRE (HIGH DIVIDE)
  ┌──────────┐       ┌──────────┐             ┌──────────┐
  │  top     │       │  top     │             │ top      │
  │  half    │       │  (1/3)   │             │ (1/4)    │
  ├──────────┤       ├──────────┤             ├──────────┤
  │  bottom  │       │          │             │          │
  │  half    │       │ bottom   │             │ bottom   │
  │          │       │ (2/3)    │             │ (3/4)    │
  └──────────┘       │          │             │          │
                     └──────────┘             └──────────┘
  Visually static.   Cropped jacket +         Empire waist;
  Equal halves       long trousers.           elongates torso
  flatten the body.  Most flattering ratio.   visually.
```

Golden ratio (~1:1.618) appears in well-regarded garments: lapel width to shoulder
width, pocket placement relative to jacket length, heel height relative to leg length.
Not mystical — just a proportion that the eye parses as balanced.

### Drape — Fabric Hand and Bias

**Fabric hand** = how fabric feels and behaves: stiff (taffeta), crisp (poplin),
fluid (charmeuse), spongy (boiled wool). Hand determines silhouette possibilities.

```
FABRIC BEHAVIOR SPECTRUM

  STIFF                                                    FLUID
  ├──────────┬──────────┬──────────┬──────────┬───────────┤
  Taffeta    Denim      Poplin     Crepe      Charmeuse
  Organza    Canvas     Linen      Jersey     Chiffon
  Tulle      Tweed      Gabardine  Silk CDC   Habotai

  Stiff → holds structure (Balenciaga's sculptural coats)
  Fluid → reveals body (Madeleine Vionnet's bias-cut gowns)
```

**Bias cut**: fabric cut at 45° to the grain stretches and clings to the body.
Vionnet's 1930s innovation. Requires 2x the fabric of straight-grain cutting.
The engineering trade-off: bias-cut garments are self-fitting but consume more
material and are harder to sew (the fabric moves under the needle).

### Color Theory in Fashion

Fashion borrows from Munsell (hue, value, chroma) but adds seasonal and cultural
overlays:

| System | Basis | Application |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Seasonal analysis (Caygill/Jackson) | Skin tone + hair + eye color mapped to warm/cool, light/dark | Personal palette recommendation |
| Pantone Fashion Color | Industry standard swatch system | Designer-to-mill color communication |
| Trend forecasting palettes | WGSN, Pantone Color of the Year | Industry-wide seasonal color direction |
| Cultural color codes | White = mourning (East Asia) vs. purity (West) | Cross-cultural design constraints |

Contrast management is the practical skill: high-contrast coloring (dark hair, light
skin) works with high-contrast outfits; low-contrast coloring works with tonal
dressing. Same principle as typographic contrast ratios — legibility at a distance.

### Pattern Grading — Scaling Across Sizes

Pattern grading is the process of scaling a single base-size pattern (usually size 8
or 10) up and down to produce the full size range. It is not uniform scaling — different
body regions scale at different rates.

```
GRADING CHALLENGES

  Body region    Scaling behavior         Problem if done wrong
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Bust           Non-linear: cup size     Dart placement shifts;
                 grows faster than        neckline gapes
                 band circumference
  Waist          Roughly proportional     Least problematic
  Hip            Non-linear: hip-to-waist Pattern distortion at
                 ratio changes with size  side seam
  Shoulder       Barely changes across    Over-grading makes
                 3-4 sizes; then jumps    small sizes swim
  Sleeve length  Proportional to height,  Wrong grading makes
                 not to bust/waist        sleeves too short/long
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  This is why "vanity sizing" exists: brands adjust grade rules
  to make larger sizes label smaller, not because the pattern
  actually fits differently.
```

3D body scanning and parametric grading (algorithmically computed grade rules from
body-scan datasets) are replacing manual grading tables. Same progression as CAD
replacing hand drafting: the parametric model captures the variation space that
discrete size tables cannot.

### What Silhouette for What Occasion?

| Context / Goal                     | Silhouette        | Why                                                      |
|------------------------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| Universal flattery, unknown body   | A-line            | Skims rather than clings; forgives hip/thigh variance    |
| Formal evening, slim build         | Column / sheath   | Clean vertical line reads as elegant; demands good posture|
| Power signaling (boardroom)        | Structured jacket + straight trouser | Sharp shoulder + 1/3-2/3 ratio projects authority |
| Relaxed sophistication             | Trapeze / swing   | Volume without waist definition; effortless read         |
| Emphasize waist-hip ratio          | Hourglass / fit-and-flare | Nips at natural waist; New Look heritage          |
| Athletic / practical               | Dropped shoulder + tapered leg | Modern relaxed proportions; movement-friendly  |

### What Fabric for What Drape?

| Desired Effect                     | Fabric Class       | Key Property                                             |
|------------------------------------|--------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| Sculptural, holds shape            | Taffeta, organza, faille | High stiffness; won't collapse under own weight   |
| Architectural tailoring            | Gabardine, twill, canvas | Enough body to hold a press; takes fusing well    |
| Body-revealing evening wear        | Charmeuse, crêpe de chine | Fluid hand; falls with gravity; bias-cuttable    |
| Everyday comfortable structure     | Cotton poplin, linen, chambray | Mid-range hand; breathable; machine-washable  |
| Stretch / movement garments        | Jersey, ponte, scuba | Knit construction; 4-way stretch; body-hugging    |
| Transparent layering               | Chiffon, tulle, voile | Very low weight; used as overlay, not standalone   |

### How to Evaluate Construction Quality

| Check                              | Good Sign                        | Red Flag                                                |
|------------------------------------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| Seam allowances                    | 1.5cm+, finished (serged/bound)  | Raw edges, narrow allowances that will fray             |
| Pattern matching at seams          | Stripes/plaids align across seams| Misaligned patterns at side seams, pockets, yokes       |
| Buttonholes                        | Keyhole or bound; clean edges    | Fraying machine buttonholes; thread pulls               |
| Lining                             | Full or half lining; own seam allowances | Lining glued to shell; pulling at hem            |
| Hem                                | Blind-stitched or hand-rolled    | Visible topstitching on formal garments; glued hems     |
| Collar / lapel                     | Rolls naturally; no bubbling     | Fusing separation (bubbles after washing)               |
| Fabric grain                       | Straight grain vertical, cross grain horizontal | Twisting after washing; diagonal pull       |
| Zipper                             | Invisible zip lies flat; metal zips glide smoothly | Wavy zipper tape; catching; visible from outside |
