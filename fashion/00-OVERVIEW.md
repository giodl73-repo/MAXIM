## The Industry Participants

```
DESIGNERS / CREATIVE DIRECTORS
  The "name" -- may or may not actually design most of the line
  Creative director of a house oversees all visual output
  Fashion designer as author vs. brand as industrial product

HOUSES / BRANDS
  Can be: independent (small atelier) or
          conglomerate division (LVMH, Kering, PVH, Inditex)

CONGLOMERATES
  LVMH: Louis Vuitton, Dior, Givenchy, Celine, Loewe, Bulgari (+70 brands)
  Kering: Gucci, Balenciaga, Saint Laurent, Bottega Veneta, Brioni
  Richemont: Cartier, IWC, Van Cleef, Chloé
  Tapestry: Coach, Kate Spade, Stuart Weitzman
  PVH: Calvin Klein, Tommy Hilfiger
  Inditex: Zara, Massimo Dutti, Pull&Bear, Bershka, Stradivarius

BUYERS
  Department store buyers (Nordstrom, Harrods, Le Bon Marche)
  Boutique buyers
  Their orders determine what gets made

MANUFACTURERS
  Tier 1: Cut-and-sew factories (final garment assembly)
  Tier 2: Fabric and trim suppliers
  Tier 3: Raw material producers (fiber, yarn)
  Geography: Bangladesh, Cambodia, Vietnam, China, Turkey, Morocco

PRESS / MEDIA
  Vogue (US, UK, France, Italy -- each with editorial independence)
  WWD (Women's Wear Daily): trade press
  Business of Fashion (BoF): modern trade analysis
  Influential: shows get reviewed; coverage shapes market
```

## How Fashion Moves: The Trickle-Down (and Trickle-Up)

```
CLASSIC TRICKLE-DOWN THEORY (Veblen, Simmel, 1890s-1900s)
  Haute Couture -> Luxury RTW -> High Street -> Mass Market

  Elite adopts new style
  -> Upper middle class copies to signal aspiration
  -> Middle class adopts as elite abandons
  -> Style becomes "common"; elite moves on

  TIMELINE: 2-5 year lag from haute couture to mass market (pre-1990)

CONTEMPORARY REALITY (post-internet)
  Trickle-down still operates for luxury/status goods
  Trickle-UP: Streetwear, workwear, sportswear move UPWARD
    Athletic wear -> luxury (Balenciaga x Adidas)
    Workwear -> fashion (Carhartt WIP as fashion brand)
    Sneakers -> luxury market

  FAST FASHION COLLAPSE OF THE TIMELINE
  Zara: 2-3 weeks from runway to store (not 2-5 years)
  SHEIN: 24-72 hours from trend identification to listing
  Timeline compression destroyed the exclusivity of fashion timing
```

## Fashion Weeks: The Show Calendar

```
FASHION WEEK PURPOSE
  Originally: buyers see collections, place orders
  Now also: press coverage, brand positioning, cultural event

  B2B function (buyer orders) declining -- many brands go
  direct to consumer, reducing wholesale dependence

FASHION WEEK STRUCTURE
  New York:  Women's + Men's; Feb and Sept
  London:    Innovative; younger designers; Feb and Sept
  Milan:     Italian luxury; Feb/Sept + Jan/June for menswear
  Paris:     Couture (Jan/July) + RTW (March/Oct) + Menswear

  SHOWS vs PRESENTATIONS
  Show: seated audience; models walk runway; often theatrical
  Presentation: models stand or move in space; more intimate
  Lookbook: no audience; photographed for press/buyers

  INVITATION HIERARCHY
  Front row: celebrities, press, major buyers
  Second row: influential buyers, junior editors
  Standing / general industry: everyone else
```

## Key Theoretical Frames

```
FASHION AS COMMUNICATION
  Clothing communicates before you speak:
  - Social class (fabric, fit, brand)
  - Tribe/group membership (uniform, subcultural)
  - Gender and gender performance
  - Modernity vs tradition
  - Aspirations (buying above your class, or performing another identity)

FASHION AS INDUSTRY
  $1.5 trillion global industry (2023)
  3rd largest manufacturing sector after automotive and electronics
  75% of garment workers are women
  Most garment workers earn poverty wages

FASHION AS ART vs FASHION AS COMMERCE
  The permanent tension:
  Couture as art (Galliano's Dior, McQueen's shows)
  vs. Fashion as commerce (the 4am production decision in Bangladesh)
  Same industry; incompatible self-presentations
```

## The Sustainability Crisis in Numbers

```
ENVIRONMENTAL SCALE
  10%   of annual global carbon emissions (textiles)
  20%   of industrial water pollution (textile dyeing)
  85%   of textiles end in landfill or incineration globally
  500,000 tons of microfibers released into ocean per year from washing
  3,000 liters of water to grow cotton for one T-shirt

LABOR SCALE
  ~75 million workers in global garment supply chain
  ~80% women
  Median wage: $3/day in Bangladesh; $2/day in Myanmar
  April 24, 2013: Rana Plaza collapse kills 1,134 workers

VOLUME SCALE
  2000: ~50 billion garments produced annually
  2023: ~100-120 billion garments produced annually
  Average US consumer: buys 65 garments/year; wears each ~7 times
  Average EU consumer: discards 11kg of textiles per year
```

## Connections to Adjacent Fields

```
FASHION -- DESIGN
  Graphic design: brand identity, packaging, show visuals
  Industrial design: accessories, hardware, eyewear
  Architecture: retail space, flagship stores (Zaha Hadid, Foster)

FASHION -- ECONOMICS
  Luxury goods economics: Veblen goods (demand rises with price)
  Supply chain economics: margin extraction through labor arbitrage
  Fast fashion: inventory turns, markdown risk, logistics

FASHION -- SOCIOLOGY / ANTHROPOLOGY
  Dress codes as social regulation
  Fashion and identity construction (Bourdieu, Butler, Wilson)
  Subcultural dress (punk, hip-hop, mod)

FASHION -- TECHNOLOGY
  Textile innovation (Gore-Tex, Dyneema, e-textiles)
  Digital fashion / virtual dress (NFT wearables, gaming skins)
  AI in trend forecasting, demand planning, design generation
  3D knitting (Shima Seiki, Stoll) enabling near-zero waste
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Couture means expensive ready-to-wear"** -- Haute Couture is a legally
protected designation in France, granted by the Chambre Syndicale. A brand must
meet specific production criteria. Expensive RTW is not couture. Vera Wang,
Oscar de la Renta, and other US designers make beautiful expensive clothes;
they are not haute couture.

**"Fashion is shallow"** -- Fashion encodes the deepest social anxieties and
desires of a period. What people wear communicates power, identity, aspiration,
belonging, and resistance. The sociology and theory is as rich as any cultural
field.

**"Sustainable fashion is expensive fashion"** -- Sustainable fashion costs more
because it prices in actual cost of production. Fast fashion is cheap because
it offloads costs to garment workers, ecosystems, and the future.
