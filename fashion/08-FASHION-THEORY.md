## The Economic Frame: Veblen and Bourdieu

### Thorstein Veblen: Conspicuous Consumption (1899)

```
VEBLEN'S "THE THEORY OF THE LEISURE CLASS" (1899)
  Context: Gilded Age; newly wealthy industrialists
  Thesis: Wealth must be displayed to confer status

  CONSPICUOUS CONSUMPTION:
  Purchasing goods not for use but for display of wealth
  Expensive goods serve as social signals

  VEBLEN GOODS (economics term derived from his work):
  Demand increases as price increases
  Because: higher price = better status signal
  Normal goods: demand falls when price rises
  Veblen goods: demand rises when price rises

  FASHION APPLICATION:
  A $1,500 handbag does not function better than a $50 bag.
  Its function IS its price.
  The conspicuous cost IS the product.

  "PECUNIARY EMULATION":
  Lower classes emulate the consumption patterns of higher classes
  Higher classes must constantly move on to maintain distinction
  -> This is the trickle-down mechanism as social physics
```

### Pierre Bourdieu: Cultural Capital (1979)

```
BOURDIEU'S "DISTINCTION" (1979)
  Extended Veblen beyond economics
  Introduced: social capital, cultural capital

  FIELD: a structured social arena with its own rules
  CAPITAL: what you possess that confers power within a field
           Economic capital: money
           Social capital: who you know; networks
           Cultural capital: knowledge, taste, embodied dispositions

  HABITUS: internalized dispositions; "second nature"
  What feels natural, tasteful, appropriate to you
  is determined by your class origin and education.
  You do not choose your taste freely; taste is class reproduction.

  FASHION IMPLICATION:
  Knowing to wear the right thing for the right occasion
  is a form of cultural capital.
  "Good taste" is not universal -- it is class-coded.
  What the upper class calls "tasteful" = what they recognize
  as signals from their own class.

  BOURDIEU'S FIELD POSITIONS:
  Dominant class: makes the rules of taste
  Middle class: anxiously mimics; fails (too obvious)
  Working class: can't play; or plays its own game (authenticity)
```

## The Sociological Frame: Simmel and Barthes

### Georg Simmel: Fashion as Imitation and Differentiation (1904)

```
SIMMEL'S "FASHION" (1904)
  Fashion serves two contradictory functions simultaneously:
  1. CONFORMITY: wearing the fashion = group membership signal
  2. DIFFERENTIATION: wearing the new fashion = distinction from others

  THE PARADOX:
  You wear fashion to be like the fashionable group.
  But once everyone wears it, it's no longer fashionable.
  -> The cycle is driven by this tension.

  SIMMEL'S MECHANISM:
  Elite adopts new style (differentiation)
  -> Upper-middle copies (conformity to new elite)
  -> Middle class copies (conformity / aspiration)
  -> Style becomes mass market (elite's differentiation destroyed)
  -> Elite moves to new style (differentiation restored)

  This is a model of fashion as social dynamics, not individual psychology.
  The individual's "free choice" is structured by this macro-system.
```

### Roland Barthes: The Fashion System (1967)

```
BARTHES'S "THE FASHION SYSTEM" (1967)
  Barthes applied Saussure's semiotics to fashion

  SAUSSURE:
  Language (langue): the abstract system; the rules
  Speech (parole): an individual utterance within the system

  BARTHES'S APPLICATION:
  Fashion system (langue): the rules of what goes with what;
                           what is elegant / casual / professional
  Individual outfit (parole): one person's expression within the rules

  DENOTATION vs CONNOTATION:
  "A white shirt" (denotative meaning: a shirt, white)
  "A crisp white dress shirt with French cuffs" (connotative:
   professional, formal, wealth, European tradition)

  FASHION MAGAZINES as TEXT:
  Barthes analyzed how fashion writing creates the fashion object.
  The caption "This season's must-have" produces the meaning
  as much as the garment itself.
  Fashion is a linguistic system before it is a material one.
```

## The Identity Frame: Butler and Hebdige

### Judith Butler: Gender as Performance (1990)

```
BUTLER'S "GENDER TROUBLE" (1990)
  Thesis: gender is not an essence -- it is a performance
  There is no "true" gender underlying the performance
  Gender is constituted by its repeated enactment

  "PERFORMATIVITY" (not performance):
  Not: "I am a woman, therefore I wear a skirt"
  But: "Wearing a skirt is one of the acts that constitutes
       being a woman as I understand and enact it"
  The category is produced by the acts, not prior to them.

  FASHION IMPLICATION:
  Clothing is one of the primary technologies of gender enactment.
  Cross-dressing "exposes the imitative structure of gender itself"
  by showing that anyone can wear any category of clothing
  -- which reveals gender as convention, not nature.

  CONTEMPORARY RELEVANCE:
  This is the theoretical foundation for:
  - Non-binary fashion
  - Gender-neutral clothing lines
  - Drag as political critique
  - "Gender fluid" as market category (Gucci, JW Anderson)
```

### Dick Hebdige: Subculture as Resistance (1979)

```
HEBDIGE'S "SUBCULTURE: THE MEANING OF STYLE" (1979)
  Focus: British punk, teddy boys, mods, Rastafarians
  Thesis: subcultural style is a form of resistance
         against dominant culture

  BRICOLAGE:
  Taking materials from dominant culture and
  reassembling them with different meaning.
  Safety pin: industrial, mundane -> punk: violent aestheticization
  School blazer: conformity -> punk: worn with torn jeans = refusal
  These are not random; they are meaningful inversions.

  INCORPORATION:
  The dominant culture responds by incorporating subcultural style.
  Punk aesthetic: within 2-3 years, sold on high streets.
  By incorporating it (removing threat), mainstream culture
  defuses the resistance.
  -> The subculture must escalate or dies.

  CYCLE:
  Resistance through style ->
  Media coverage (signification) ->
  Commercial incorporation ->
  Original community disperses / escalates ->
  New subculture forms
```

## Fashion and Identity Construction

### Extended Self (Belk, 1988)

```
"POSSESSIONS AND THE EXTENDED SELF" (Belk, 1988)
  Our possessions are part of how we define who we are.
  Clothing is particularly identity-proximate:
  it is between the social world and our literal skin.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Losing possessions (fire, theft) feels like loss of self
  Wearing "power clothes" is not just pretend -- it changes behavior
  Second-hand resistance ("someone else's clothes") = identity anxiety

ENCLOTHED COGNITION (Adam & Galinsky, 2012)
  Wearing a doctor's coat increases sustained attention performance
  when participants are told it is a doctor's coat.
  Same coat described as a painter's coat: no effect.
  The symbolic meaning of the clothing affects cognition, not the garment itself.

  IMPLICATION:
  "Dressing for the job you want" is not entirely metaphor.
  Clothing activates associated schemas and dispositions.
  This is a small effect; it is a real one.
```

## Fashion and Modernity (Wilson)

```
ELIZABETH WILSON'S "ADORNED IN DREAMS" (1985)
  Challenge to feminist critiques of fashion as oppression
  Wilson: Fashion is part of modernity itself; it cannot be
  rejected without rejecting urban modern life

  "FASHION IS THE CAPITALIST ORGANIZATION OF THE BODY"
  But also: fantasy, pleasure, self-expression, play
  Fashion contains contradictions because modernity does.
  The problem is not fashion; the problem is the system it operates within.

  WILSON ON DRESS REFORM MOVEMENTS:
  Various reform movements (rational dress, 1880s; 1970s feminist)
  tried to reject fashion as oppressive.
  They all failed because humans want to adorn themselves.
  The impulse to dress-up is not false consciousness --
  it is part of being human.

  IMPLICATIONS FOR SUSTAINABILITY DISCOURSE:
  "Stop buying clothes" will never work as a mass movement.
  The desire for novelty and self-expression through dress is
  not an error to be corrected. The system that produces it
  at destructive scale and cost is the problem to be corrected.
```

## Fashion and Power: Uniforms, Dress Codes, and Resistance

```
THE SOCIAL REGULATION OF DRESS
  Sumptuary laws: historically regulated who could wear what by class
    Venice (13th-18th century): detailed laws on fabric, color by rank
    England (14th-16th century): reserved ermine, purple for royalty
    Japanese Edo period: strict class-based dress codes
  These laws existed because dress was a political threat:
  someone dressing above their station = claiming political power

  CONTEMPORARY EQUIVALENTS:
  School uniforms (suppress class signaling within school)
  Corporate dress codes (signal authority hierarchy; team membership)
  Prison uniforms (remove identity; enforce submission)
  Military uniform (enforce unit identity; suppress individuality)
  Religious dress (community membership; modesty; devotion signal)

  DRESS AS RESISTANCE:
  Blue jeans in Soviet bloc: American freedom; smuggled, precious
  ANC colors in apartheid South Africa: banned; wearing them = risk
  Hijab in 1970s Iran: religious identity against secular state
  Later: mandatory hijab = state control; removing it = resistance
  (The same garment: in different contexts, opposite politics)
```

## Fashion and Globalization

```
FASHION AS CULTURAL IMPERIALISM (critique)
  Paris fashion system assumes Paris = center
  Non-Western dress = "ethnic" (othered); Western = "fashion" (universal)
  Japanese designers' 1981 Paris debut: challenged this
  Indian, African, Brazilian designers: growing assertion of centrality

  The "global" fashion system has been Western-dominant.
  This is changing but slowly.

CULTURAL APPROPRIATION IN FASHION
  The debate:
  Who can wear what?
  When does borrowing become theft?

  Considerations:
  1. Power differential: borrowing FROM marginalized community BY
     dominant culture vs. vice versa (asymmetric)
  2. Context: sacred vs. secular; ceremonial vs. everyday
  3. Credit and compensation: does the originating community benefit?
  4. Transformation vs. reproduction: adaptation vs. copying

  Fashion has always been a field of cultural exchange.
  The ethical question is: under what conditions is exchange
  extractive vs. enriching for all parties?
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Fashion theory is just jargon for obvious ideas"** -- Veblen's conspicuous
consumption was obvious AFTER it was theorized; before 1899, economists treated
consumer preferences as rational utility. Bourdieu's demonstration that "taste"
is class-coded rather than objective was controversial and contested.

**"People dress to express their true self"** -- Butler and Bourdieu both challenge
this. Dress expresses what we understand ourselves to be within the categories
available to us in our social context. The "authentic self" is itself a social
construction.

**"Subcultures are just fashion trends"** -- Hebdige's point is that subcultural
style carries genuine political content (resistance to dominant norms). When that
content is incorporated and sold, the resistance is neutralized -- but the
political charge was real in the originating community.

**"Fashion is trivial compared to 'serious' culture"** -- It is the social system
closest to the body; it is regulated by law historically (sumptuary laws);
it is how class, gender, and identity are marked daily. It is arguably more
consequential in daily life than most "serious" culture.
