# Reptilia and Birds — Amniotes Conquer the Land

## The Big Picture

```
AMNIOTA — the land-independent vertebrates
Defined by: AMNIOTIC EGG (or equivalent live birth)

  TETRAPODA
   |
   +-- AMPHIBIA (water-dependent)
   +-- AMNIOTA
        |
        +-- REPTILIA (includes BIRDS!)
        +-- MAMMALIA (next file)

TRADITIONAL "REPTILIA":
  Turtles + lizards + snakes + tuatara + crocs + birds
  But excludes mammals (also amniotes)
  = PARAPHYLETIC (doesn't include all descendants of ancestor)

MODERN PHYLOGENY (Amniota):
  Synapsida → MAMMALIA (mammals)
  Reptilia (= Sauropsida in some usages):
    Anapsida → Testudines (turtles) — debated
    Lepidosauria → Tuatara, lizards, snakes
    Archosauria → Crocodilia + Dinosauria (incl. AVES)

LIVING REPTILE GROUPS:
  Testudines   ~360 sp   Turtles + tortoises
  Rhynchocephalia ~1 sp  Tuatara
  Squamata     ~11,000sp Lizards + snakes
  Crocodilia   ~28 sp    Crocodilians
  Aves         ~10,500sp Birds (AVIAN DINOSAURS)
```

## Layer 1 — The Amniotic Egg

```
THE KEY INNOVATION: amniotic egg

Amphibian egg:        Amniotic egg:
  No membranes          Four extraembryonic membranes:
  Dries out quickly     +-----------------------------+
  Must be in water      | AMNION: fluid-filled sac    |
  or very moist         |   surrounds embryo          |
                        |   cushions, prevents drying |
                        | CHORION: outermost gas      |
                        |   exchange membrane         |
                        | ALLANTOIS: waste reservoir  |
                        |   + gas exchange in reptiles|
                        | YOLK SAC: nutrient reserve  |
                        +-----------------------------+
                        Shell: leathery (most reptiles)
                               or calcified (birds, crocs)
                               or absent (live birth)

CONSEQUENCE:
  Eggs can be laid in dry environments
  No need to return to water to reproduce
  Colonization of dry inland habitats possible
  No aquatic larval stage — hatchlings are miniature adults

CONVERGENCE in VIVIPARITY:
  Amniotic egg evolved once; live birth evolved ~150+ times
  in lizards + snakes independently
  (Placenta evolved ~16 times in Squamata alone)
```

## Layer 2 — Testudines (Turtles and Tortoises)

~360 species. Most ancient surviving amniote body plan.

```
TURTLE BODY PLAN

+--------------------------------------------+
|  SHELL:                                    |
|  Carapace (dorsal) = fused ribs +          |
|    dorsal vertebrae + dermal bone          |
|    covered by scutes (keratinized scales)  |
|  Plastron (ventral) = modified clavicles + |
|    interclavicle + dermal bone             |
|  The shell IS the ribcage — fused.         |
|                                            |
|  RESULT:                                   |
|  Shoulder blades (scapulae) inside ribcage!|
|  Unique in all vertebrates                 |
|  Evolved Triassic (~230 Ma)                |
|  Pappochelys + Odontochelys (230–220 Ma):  |
|  partial shell showing the transition      |
+--------------------------------------------+

SKULL:
  Anapsid: no temporal fenestrae (fenestrae = holes in skull)
  But molecular data → turtles are REPTILES, not basal amniotes
  (Anapsid skull probably secondarily evolved)
```

### Temperature-Dependent Sex Determination (TSD)

```
TSD: incubation temperature determines offspring sex
(vs genotypic sex determination — GSD — most vertebrates)

Pivot temperature:  28–30°C for most species
Below pivot:        All males (some species)
Above pivot:        All females (some species)
Pattern Type I:     ♀ at high temp (most sea turtles)
Pattern Type II:    ♀ at both extremes, ♂ in middle

CLIMATE CRISIS IMPACT:
  Great Barrier Reef green sea turtle: >90% female (2018)
  Warming beach temps → near-complete feminization
  No males produced → population cannot reproduce
  Timeline: likely reproductive collapse within decades

MECHANISM:
  Temperature affects aromatase enzyme activity
  Aromatase converts androgens → estrogens
  High temp → high aromatase → estrogen → female
  Cortisol (stress hormone) also affects
```

### Marine Turtles

```
7 species of sea turtle (family Cheloniidae + Dermochelyidae)

All threatened to critically endangered

NAVIGATION:
  Magnetic imprinting on natal beach (as hatchling)
  Detect inclination + intensity of Earth's magnetic field
  Return to natal beach to nest after 15–30 years
  Accuracy: < 100 km of natal site

LEATHERBACK (Dermochelys coriacea):
  Largest turtle: 2 m, 900 kg
  No scutes: leathery carapace with cartilaginous rods
  Endothermic: maintains body temp above seawater
  (Gigantothermy + counter-current heat exchange)
  Dives to 1,200 m; jellyfish specialists
  Mouth: esophageal spines trap jellyfish

LOGGERHEAD (Caretta caretta):
  Powerful crushing jaws for molluscs + crabs
  Nest on same Florida beaches for 400+ years
```

## Layer 3 — Lepidosauria

~10,000 species. Lizards, snakes, and the tuatara.

### Tuatara (Rhynchocephalia)

```
SPHENODON PUNCTATUS — New Zealand only

SOLE SURVIVING member of Rhynchocephalia
  (once diverse worldwide — Mesozoic)
  All other rhynchocephalians extinct ~60 Ma

"LIVING FOSSIL" features:
  Diapsid skull (2 temporal fenestrae)
  Acrodont teeth (fused to jawbone, not in sockets)
  Two rows of teeth on upper jaw, one on lower
    → unique "shearing" bite
  No external ear opening
  Caudal autotomy (tail shedding)
  PARIETAL (THIRD) EYE:
    Distinct lens + retina on top of skull
    Covered by scales after ~4 months
    Detects light/dark + UV → photoperiod sensing
    Connected to pineal gland

LOW METABOLISM:
  Active at 7–18°C (most reptiles die of cold)
  Slowest reptile metabolism
  Grows until age 35
  Sexual maturity: 13–20 years
  Lifespan: 60–100+ years
  Henry: captive tuatara, fathered offspring at 111 years old
```

### Squamata — Lizards and Snakes

```
SQUAMATA (~11,000 sp) — diagnostic feature:
  Kinetic skull: multiple joints allow wide jaw opening
  Quadrate bone: moveable (vs tuatara)
  Jacobson's organ (vomeronasal): chemical sensing
    Tongue flick → picks up molecules → Jacobson's organ
    → brain decodes (SMELL not taste)

LIZARD DIVERSITY:
  Iguanidae: iguanas (herbivorous, arboreal)
  Chamaeleonidae: chameleons (zygodactyl feet, turret eyes,
    ballistic tongue = 1/3 of body length)
  Gekkonidae: geckos (setae adhesion — Van der Waals)
    ~2,000 sp; some vocal, some parthenogenetic
  Varanidae: monitor lizards
    Komodo dragon (Varanus komodoensis):
      Largest lizard: 3 m, 70 kg
      Parthenogenesis: females produce male-only offspring
      Venom glands (not bacterial — previously thought)
      + Ambush hunting of deer, goats, water buffalo
  Helodermatidae: Gila monster + beaded lizard
    Only venomous lizards with chewing venom delivery
  Agamidae: agamas, thorny devil (fog-drinking channels)

SNAKE EVOLUTION:
  Derived from lizard ancestor (~100 Ma)
  Lost limbs, lost eyelids (transparent scale "spectacle")
  Lost external ear
  Kinetic skull → extreme jaw distension
    (Quadrate + jugal + separate mandible halves)
```

### Snake Biology

```
VENOM EVOLUTION:

Venom glands: modified salivary glands
Evolved multiple times in squamates (at least 6×)

DELIVERY SYSTEMS:
  Aglyphous:  No grooves; inject via bite pressure (most non-venomous)
  Opisthoglyphous: Rear-fanged; chew venom in (boomslang, twig snakes)
  Proteroglyphous: Fixed front fangs; bite + hold (cobras, kraits, mambas)
  Solenoglyphous: Hinged front fangs (folded); most advanced
    (vipers, pit vipers, rattlesnakes)

VIPER FOLDING FANG:
  Fang folds back when mouth closed
  Rotates forward on strike (< 1 second rotation)
  Hollow syringe needle
  Vipers: longest fangs (Gaboon viper: 5 cm)

VENOM TYPES:
  Cytotoxic/hemotoxic (vipers): tissue damage, coagulation disruption
  Neurotoxic (elapids): block acetylcholine receptors (cobras)
    or voltage-gated channels (mambas)
  Mixed: many species

JACOBSON'S ORGAN + HEAT PIT ORGANS:
  Pythons + pit vipers: infrared-sensing pit organs
  Pit viper loreal pit: detects ΔT of 0.003°C
  Maps thermal image; overlaid on visual
  Hunts warm prey in total darkness
```

## Layer 4 — Crocodilia

~28 species. Alligators, crocodiles, gharials, false gharials.

```
CROCODILIAN FEATURES — THE MOST COMPLEX NON-AVIAN REPTILE

PHYLOGENY:
  Crocodilia = sister group to AVES (birds)
  Together = ARCHOSAURIA
  Crocodiles are more closely related to pigeons than to lizards!

FOUR-CHAMBERED HEART:
  Complete ventricular septum (unique among living non-avian reptiles)
  BUT: foramen of Panizza (hole between aortas)
  → Can shunt blood around pulmonary circuit during diving
  Most reptiles: three-chambered heart (ventricle partially divided)

ADVANCED FEATURES:
  True palate: complete secondary palate → breathe while mouth full of water
  Archosaur armor: osteoderms (bone in skin) as armor
  Parental care: most elaborate of non-avian reptiles
    Mound or hole nest; vocalizations trigger hatching
    Mother carries hatchlings to water in mouth
    Defends young for months

ECTOTHERMY + AMBUSH:
  Most of life: basking to thermoregulate
  Attack: explosive 0.5-second lunge
  Death roll: rapid body rotation to tear prey
    (generates up to 4× body weight torque)
  Bite force: 16,000 N (saltwater croc) — highest of any living animal

CROCODILE vs ALLIGATOR:
  Alligator: broad U-shaped snout; lower teeth hidden
  Crocodile: narrow V-shaped snout; lower 4th tooth visible
  Gavial/Gharial: extremely narrow snout (fish specialist)
```

### Archaeopteryx and the Origin of Flight

```
ARCHAEOPTERYX LITHOGRAPHICA
  Discovered: Solnhofen, Bavaria, 1861
  Age: 150 Ma (Late Jurassic)
  10 specimens known

MOSAIC: reptile + bird features
  REPTILE:         BIRD:
  Teeth            Feathers (asymmetric — flight feathers!)
  Clawed fingers   Wishbone (furcula)
  Long bony tail   Flight feathers on wings
  Unfused bones

ORIGIN OF FLIGHT — TWO HYPOTHESES:
  Trees-down: climbed trees, glided, refined to flapping
  Ground-up: fast running, wing-assisted incline running (WAIR)
              → flapping for lift

EVIDENCE NOW FAVORS: mostly ground-up + trees-down in different lineages
  WAIR observed in juvenile ground birds (partridges, chukars)
  Ancestral small theropods likely: primarily ground-running
  Arboreal microraptorans: gliding from trees
  (Multiple origins of flight within Maniraptora? Debated)
```

### Avian Innovations

The bird lung is a streaming pipeline; the mammal lung is a batch processor. Mammal tidal breathing: fill buffer (inhale), process (gas exchange), flush buffer (exhale), repeat — the "stale air" problem means at best ~25% O₂ extraction. Bird flow-through system: air enters posterior air sacs on inhale, flows through parabronchi (gas exchange surface) on exhale, exits anterior air sacs, exits on second inhale — continuous unidirectional flow. Counter-current exchange: blood flows opposite to air direction through the gas exchange capillaries, maintaining maximum partial pressure gradient across the entire exchange surface — analogous to counter-current heat exchangers in engineering. Result: 95%+ O₂ extraction. The engineering advantage is exactly the same as streaming vs. batch processing: continuous throughput, lower latency, higher utilization of the exchange surface.

```
ONE-WAY AIRFLOW LUNG:
+---------------------------------------------+
| MAMMAL LUNG (tidal):                        |
|   Air in → fills alveoli → O₂/CO₂ exchange  |
|   Same air OUT (stale air mixes with fresh) |
|                                             |
| BIRD LUNG + AIR SAC SYSTEM (flow-through):  |
|   9 air sacs + rigid lungs                  |
|   Air flows CONTINUOUSLY through parabronchi|
|   Same direction during both inhale + exhale|
|   Counter-current O₂ exchange (95%+ extract)|
|   (vs mammal: ~25% O₂ extracted)            |
+---------------------------------------------+

CONSEQUENCE: Birds can fly at 8,000+ m altitude
  Bar-headed goose crosses Himalayas (5,000–8,000 m)
  Rüppell's vulture: 11,300 m — highest bird recorded

HOLLOW BONES (pneumatization):
  Many bones air-filled (connected to air sac system)
  Penguin: dense bones (NOT hollow) — diving counter-adaptation
  Frigate bird skeleton = 5% of body weight

FEATHER DIVERSITY:
  Down: insulation (branched, no hooks)
  Contour: body shape (vane with interlocking barbules)
  Flight: asymmetric primaries/secondaries (lift + thrust)
  Tail: steering + braking
  Display: peacock, birds of paradise
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Birds are reptiles.**
Specifically, they're avian dinosaurs (Theropoda: Maniraptora: Avialae). Crocodilians are their closest living relatives. When you eat chicken, you're eating a dinosaur.

**Velociraptor had feathers.**
The Jurassic Park "raptors" were actually based on *Deinonychus* (much larger) and depicted without feathers (discovered after the film). Actual *Velociraptor mongoliensis* was turkey-sized with feathers — quill knobs found on forearm fossils in 2007.

**Snake venom evolved for prey, not defense.**
The neurotoxic/cytotoxic cocktail is optimized for rapid prey immobilization and digestion (digestive enzymes are part of the mix). Defensive use is secondary. This is why antivenom treats bites — the immune system can recognize and neutralize venom proteins.

**Three-chambered hearts in reptiles still work.**
The "incomplete" ventricular septum in lizards/snakes creates a functional double circulation. The interventricular canal can be selectively opened/closed. During diving, blood is shunted away from lungs (no point oxygenating if not breathing). It's a flexible system, not a broken one.

**Alligators do make good mothers.**
Crocodilians have the most complex maternal (and paternal) behavior of non-avian reptiles. American alligator: female guards nest for ~65 days, excavates it when she hears hatching calls, carries hatchlings to water, and guards a crèche for up to two years.

**Ratites (ostriches, emus) are not primitive birds.**
They're derived — secondarily flightless. Molecular evidence shows their flightlessness evolved multiple times (convergently) after colonizing southern continents when Gondwana fragmented. The kiwi is more closely related to the extinct moa than to the cassowary.
