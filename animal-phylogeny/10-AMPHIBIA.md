# Amphibia — The Water-to-Land Transition

## The Big Picture

```
THE TETRAPOD TRANSITION

     SARCOPTERYGII
          |
     +---------+----------+
     |                    |
  Coelacanth            Rhipidistia
                             |
                    +--------+--------+
                    |                 |
                Lungfish          TETRAPODA
                (sister to         (4 limbs)
                 tetrapods)            |
                                  +----+----+
                                  |         |
                               AMPHIBIA   AMNIOTA
                               (water-    (reptiles,
                                dependent) birds, mammals)

AMPHIBIA:
  Not a grade but a CLADE — all living amphibians
  form a monophyletic group (Lissamphibia)

THREE LIVING ORDERS:
  Anura        ~7,600 sp  Frogs + toads
  Urodela      ~740 sp    Salamanders + newts + axolotl
  Gymnophiona  ~215 sp    Caecilians

KEY CONSTRAINT:
  Permeable skin → desiccation risk → tied to moist habitats
  Most need water for reproduction (eggs dry out)
  EXCEPTIONS: some desert frogs, some direct-developing species
```

## Layer 1 — The Devonian Transition

```
TIMELINE: FISH → TETRAPOD

420 Ma   Lobe-finned fish (Eusthenopteron) — fins with bone
          Shallow water, swampy habitats
375 Ma   TIKTAALIK ROSEAE
          Fins with humerus, radius/ulna, proto-wrist
          Could raise head (neck present)
          Could prop body off substrate
365 Ma   ACANTHOSTEGA (Greenland)
          8-fingered limbs (more than tetrapod standard!)
          Still largely aquatic
          Limbs used for locomotion IN WATER first
          Gills still present
365 Ma   ICHTHYOSTEGA (Greenland)
          7-fingered limbs
          Could make short terrestrial excursions
          Heavy ribs → some weight support on land
360 Ma   Terrestrial tetrapods establishing
          5-digit limbs now standard
340 Ma   First amniotes (Carboniferous)

LIMB DIGIT REDUCTION:
  Early tetrapods: 8, 7, or 6 digits documented
  Stabilized at 5 (pentadactyly) in common ancestor
  Mammals: 1–5 (horses: 1, moles: 5)
  Frogs: 4 front + 5 rear (lost first digit front)
  Snakes: lost all
```

### Why Did Tetrapods Colonize Land?

```
THE MOTIVE WASN'T LAND ITSELF

Old hypothesis: escaping from drying pools
  (limbs = walk to next pool)

Current evidence:
  Limbs evolved IN WATER (Acanthostega had gills)
  Shallow, vegetated Devonian swamps
  Aquatic locomotion in complex, shallow habitat
  Pulling through dense aquatic vegetation
  Raising head to breathe air (supplemental air breathing)

Land colonization = gradual opportunistic expansion
  Not a single "leap onto land" event

DEVONIAN TERRESTRIAL OPPORTUNITY:
  Vast undepleted terrestrial food resources
  No terrestrial vertebrate predators yet
  Abundant invertebrates on land (insects, myriapods)
  Land plants well established (Carboniferous forests)
```

## Layer 2 — Amphibian Physiology

### Skin — The Critical Interface

```
AMPHIBIAN SKIN:
+----------------------------------------------+
|  No scales (unlike fish + reptiles)          |
|  No hair or feathers (unlike amniotes)       |
|  MOIST, PERMEABLE                            |
|  Mucus glands: keep skin moist              |
|  Granular glands: secrete toxins             |
+----------------------------------------------+

CUTANEOUS GAS EXCHANGE:
  O₂ and CO₂ diffuse directly through moist skin
  Many salamanders: NO LUNGS (Plethodontidae)
    breathe entirely through skin + mouth lining
  Frogs at rest: ~25% O₂ via skin; 75% via lungs
  Frogs hibernating underwater: 100% via skin

OSMOREGULATION:
  Most freshwater: skin absorbs water by osmosis
  "Pelvic patch": ventral skin with high aquaporin density
    → absorbs standing water without drinking
  Desert frogs: cocoon (dried mucus) limits water loss
    Store dilute urine in bladder as water reserve

SKIN TOXINS:
  Bufotoxins (toads): cardiotoxic steroids
  Tetrodotoxin: newts (Taricha) + some other amphibians
    Same toxin as pufferfish — from symbiotic bacteria
  Batrachotoxin: poison dart frogs — most toxic non-protein
    2 μg/kg lethal dose; blocks Na⁺ channels permanently open
  Pumiliotoxin, epibatidine: various frogs
```

### Reproduction

```
TYPICAL AMPHIBIAN LIFE CYCLE (aquatic reproduction)

Eggs:                 Tadpole (larva):     Metamorphosis:
Jelly-coated          Gill-breathing       Tail resorbed
No amniotic membrane  Aquatic              Lungs develop
Laid in water         Herbivore/filter     Limbs develop
(or moist places)     Lateral line         Gills absorbed
                      Tail for swimming    Move to land
                                           (if terrestrial sp.)

GAS EXCHANGE SHIFT:
  Tadpole: external gills → internal gills → LUNGS
  Metamorphosis trigger: thyroid hormone (TH)
  TH triggers: gill resorption, tail resorption, limb growth,
    gut remodeling (herbivore gut → shorter carnivore gut),
    cornea keratinization

REPRODUCTIVE DIVERSITY:
+--------------------------------------------------+
| External fertilization (most frogs)              |
| Internal fertilization (most salamanders,        |
|   all caecilians, some frogs)                    |
| Direct development (no free larva)               |
|   Eleutherodactylus: eggs on land → tiny froglet |
| Viviparous: some caecilians, some fire salamanders|
| Marsupial frog: eggs in dorsal pouch             |
| Gastric brooding (Rheobatrachus): EXTINCT        |
|   Swallowed eggs → developed in stomach          |
|   Prostaglandin E₂ suppressed gastric acid       |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

## Layer 3 — Anura (Frogs and Toads)

~7,600 species. Most diverse order. Global distribution.

### What Makes a Frog

```
FROG/TOAD BODY PLAN

+-----------------------------------------+
|  No tail as adult (Anura = "tailless")  |
|  Fused tibia + fibula (tibiofibula)     |
|  Elongated ilium + ischium (jumping)    |
|  Urostyle (fused caudal vertebrae)      |
|  Hindlimbs: long, powerful (jumping)    |
|  Forelimbs: shorter, landing shock      |
|  Tympanic membrane (eardrum) visible    |
|  Large eyes with horizontal pupil       |
|  Tongue: attached at front, flipped out |
+-----------------------------------------+

JUMPING MECHANISM:
  Tendon stores elastic energy (like a spring)
  "Counter-movement jump" — crouch → energy stored
  Ilio-sacral joint: allows extreme hip flexion
  Longest jump: Rana: ~3m (10× body length)
  Fastest: toad's tongue strike = 50m/s²

FROG vs TOAD (informal):
  "Frog" = smooth, moist skin, slim body, near water
  "Toad" = dry warty skin, robust body, terrestrial
  NOT taxonomic — Bufonidae are "true toads" but
  many "toads" are in other families, and vice versa
```

### Notable Frog Biology

```
POISON DART FROGS (family Dendrobatidae):
  ~170 species, Central/South American tropics
  Batrachotoxin (BTX) in skin — blocks Nav channels
    → permanent depolarization → paralysis
  Source: dietary (ants + mites + beetles)
    → captive frogs lose toxicity
    → how Indigenous people discovered toxicity???
  Aposematic: bright colors = honest signal
  Parental care: male guards eggs; carries tadpoles on back
    Oophagous species: mother returns to lay unfertilized eggs
    as tadpole food

WOOD FROG (Rana sylvatica) — FREEZE TOLERANCE:
  Lives in Alaska/Canada; freezes solid in winter
  Up to 65% of body water forms extracellular ice
  Mechanism:
    Ice forms between cells (extracellular)
    Liver releases glucose (cryoprotectant) in minutes
    Intracellular sugar prevents cell dehydration
  Heart stops. Brain activity stops.
  Thaw → complete recovery in hours
  Can freeze/thaw multiple times

SURINAM TOAD (Pipa pipa):
  Eggs embedded in female's back skin
  Skin grows over each egg → tiny pocket
  After ~3–4 months: fully-formed froglets PUSH THROUGH skin
  (one of the more remarkable developmental strategies known)

VOCAL DIVERSITY:
  Frogs: most vocal of non-avian tetrapods
  ~6,500 distinct advertisement calls described
  Produced by larynx + vocal sac amplification
  Some species: female calls (bidirectional)
  Ultrasonic: torrent frogs communicate above background roar
  Seismic: foot trembling → ground vibration (some species)
```

## Layer 4 — Urodela (Salamanders, Newts, Axolotl)

~740 species. Tailed amphibians.

```
SALAMANDER BODY PLAN
  Four limbs (reduced in some) + long tail
  Most: external fertilization in water
  Advanced: internal fertilization via spermatophore
    Male deposits sperm packet; female picks up with cloaca
  Plethodontidae: no lungs — breathe entirely via skin + mouth
```

### Paedomorphosis and the Axolotl

```
PAEDOMORPHOSIS: retention of larval features in sexually mature adult

NEOTENY: sexual maturity while remaining morphologically juvenile
  Most famous example: AXOLOTL (Ambystoma mexicanum)

AXOLOTL:
  Retains external gills, caudal fin, aquatic lifestyle
  Sexually mature, reproduces — but never completes metamorphosis
  Wild: Lake Xochimilco, Mexico (critically endangered)

MECHANISM:
  Neoteny caused by low thyroid hormone sensitivity
  (or low production)
  Inject thyroid hormone → axolotl DOES metamorphose
  → transforms into tiger salamander-like adult
  Evolutionary: thyroid hormone receptor insensitivity heritable
    → entire population becomes neotenic

MUDPUPPY (Necturus):
  Obligate neotene — cannot metamorphose even with TH
  Structural change at receptor level

WHY NEOTENY?:
  Aquatic environment offers food + protection
  Land may be harsh (cold, dry climate)
  Neoteny = evolutionary shortcut to adulthood in water
```

### Regeneration in Salamanders

```
SALAMANDER REGENERATION — most impressive in vertebrates

Axolotl can regenerate:
  Entire limbs (including bones, muscles, nerves, skin)
  Cardiac tissue
  Portions of retina and lens
  Parts of brain
  Spinal cord segments
  Lower jaw portions

MECHANISM:
  1. Wound healing + blastema formation
     Cells at amputation site dedifferentiate → stem-cell-like mass
  2. Blastema is position-specific
     Knows which part to regenerate (from position info)
  3. Cells redifferentiate into correct tissue types
     Nerve fiber re-innervates regenerated limb

TIMING: complete limb regeneration in ~60 days
  Each regeneration high-fidelity

vs mammalian repair: scar formation (fibroblasts)
  Mammals suppress regeneration pathway (tumor suppression?)
  Understanding axolotl regeneration → human regenerative medicine goal
```

### Plethodontidae (Lungless Salamanders)

```
~480 species. Largest salamander family.

NO LUNGS — breathe entirely via:
  Skin (~50% of gas exchange)
  Buccal cavity lining (~50%)
  (Nasolabial grooves: deliver chemical signals to nose)

HOW POSSIBLE:
  All species small + slender → high surface:volume ratio
  All in moist, cool habitats (O₂ diffusion rate sufficient)

PHYLOGENETIC IMPLICATION:
  Lunglessness evolved ONCE (monophyletic group)
  Ancestral Plethodontidae lost lungs ~65 Ma
  Lungs independently lost in at least 2 other salamander lines

DIVERSITY:
  Red-backed salamander (Plethodon cinereus):
    Most abundant vertebrate in NE US forests by biomass
    Purely terrestrial; direct development (no free larva)
  Olm (Proteus anguinus):
    Cave-dwelling; blind (eyes degenerated under skin)
    Neotenic; can live >100 years (longest-lived salamander)
```

## Layer 5 — Gymnophiona (Caecilians)

~215 species. The forgotten order.

```
CAECILIAN BODY PLAN
+--------------------------------------------+
|  No limbs — completely limbless            |
|  No pectoral or pelvic girdle              |
|  Burrowing lifestyle (mostly)              |
|  Annular groove rings (look like segments) |
|  Tentacle: chemosensory organ              |
|    (between eye + nostril, unique)         |
|  Internal fertilization (all species)      |
|  Small eyes (often under skin)             |
+--------------------------------------------+

DISTRIBUTION: Tropics only (South America, Africa, Asia)
HABITAT: Soil burrows (mostly), some aquatic (Typhlonectes)

REPRODUCTION:
  Oviparous: female guards eggs; larvae eat mother's skin!
    Mother produces extra lipid-rich skin layer
    Larvae have temporary teeth → peel off skin
    Skin regrows in ~3 days
  Viviparous: some species — fetal teeth scrape uterine wall
    for nutrition (oophagy/dermatrophe both known)

LIMBLESSNESS:
  Limbless body plan evolved independently many times in vertebrates
  (Caecilians, snakes, some lizards, some salamanders)
  Caecilian limblessness: complete limb girdle loss
  Smallest: 7 cm; Largest: 1.5 m (Caecilia thompsoni)

HOW POORLY KNOWN:
  New species discovered regularly
  Deep soil burrowers rarely encountered
  Several families described in last 20 years
```

## Layer 6 — The Amphibian Decline Crisis

```
GLOBAL AMPHIBIAN CRISIS — worst mass extinction of vertebrates
in recorded history

NUMBERS (IUCN Red List 2024):
  ~7,600 amphibian species assessed
  ~41% threatened (Vulnerable/Endangered/Critically Endangered)
  ~168 species extinct since 1980
  ~1,000+ likely extinct or functionally extinct

CAUSES:
+-------------------------------------------------------+
| Chytridiomycosis   Bd fungus (see below)              |
|                    ~90 species extinct or near-extinct |
| Habitat loss       Wetland drainage, deforestation    |
| Climate change     Desiccation, phenological mismatch |
| Pollution          Pesticides disrupt hormones        |
|                    (Atrazine → hermaphrodite frogs)   |
| Invasive species   Bullfrogs, trout eating tadpoles   |
| Overexploitation   Food (frog legs trade) + pet trade |
+-------------------------------------------------------+

CHYTRIDIOMYCOSIS:
  Causative agent: Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (Bd)
    + B. salamandrivorans (Bsal) — salamander-specific
  Mechanism: infects skin cells → keratinization
    Disrupts skin osmoregulation + electrolyte balance
    → cardiac arrest (sodium/potassium imbalance)
  Spread: global trade in American bullfrogs + fire salamanders
  Origin: Korean peninsula (Bsal); Asian origin (Bd)
  Amphibians that co-evolved with pathogen: asymptomatic
  Native populations of endemic species: catastrophic
  Panama: 30+ species extinct in <5 years (2004–2010)
  Australia: 7 species extinct; golden toad (Costa Rica): extinct 1989
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

```
AMPHIBIAN IDENTIFICATION

Frog or toad (squat body, 4 legs, no tail as adult)?
  → ANURA
  Smooth moist skin, slim? → likely "true frog" (Ranidae)
  Dry warty skin, robust?  → likely "true toad" (Bufonidae)
  Bright colors, tiny (<5cm)? → possibly poison dart frog
  Found frozen solid?       → possibly wood frog (Rana sylvatica)

Has tail + 4 limbs (slender body)?
  → URODELA (salamander or newt)
  External gills as adult?    → neotenic (axolotl, mudpuppy)
  In water during breeding?   → newt (Salamandridae)
  No lungs, cave/forest floor?→ Plethodontidae

No limbs, no tail, tropical, burrowing?
  → GYMNOPHIONA (caecilian)
  Has tentacle between eye + nostril → definitive caecilian
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Toad" is not a scientific category.**
All toads are frogs (Order Anura). "Toad" = colloquial for warty, terrestrial, robust-bodied anurans. Bufonidae is "the toad family" but tree frogs, spadefoot toads, etc. are in other families. When scientists say "toad" they mean Bufonidae only.

**Axolotl neoteny is not arrested development.**
The axolotl is sexually mature — it's a fully functional adult in an aquatic, larval-looking body. It's not stuck in larval stage; it IS a valid adult form of the species. The concept: heterochrony (change in developmental timing) is one of evolution's most powerful tools.

**Salamanders are not lizards.**
Salamanders (Amphibia: Urodela) — moist smooth skin, 4 toes on front feet, no claws, no scales, tied to moisture. Lizards (Reptilia: Squamata) — dry scaly skin, claws, independent of water for reproduction. Common confusion in temperate zone where both occur.

**Caecilians are not worms.**
They have vertebrae, a skull, internal organs, and all features of a vertebrate. The body rings (annuli) are superficial grooves, not true segments. Convergently worm-like for the same reason earthworms are worm-like: burrowing through soil.

**The "leap onto land" narrative is wrong.**
The tetrapod transition involved gradual incremental changes over tens of millions of years. Tiktaalik didn't walk onto land in a single heroic moment. Limbs evolved for aquatic locomotion. Air breathing evolved independently in fish long before limbs appeared. Each innovation was independently adaptive at the time.

**Permeable skin is not a flaw.**
It's a functional system enabling cutaneous respiration. The trade-off is water loss in dry conditions. Frogs solve this by behavioral thermoregulation, mucus glands, and the pelvic patch. The "primitive" label applied to amphibians is misleading — they've occupied the same niches with the same body plan for 360 million years.
