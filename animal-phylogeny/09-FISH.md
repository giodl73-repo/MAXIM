# Fish — The Vertebrate Radiation in Water

## The Big Picture

```
"FISH" IS NOT A CLADE.
It's a grade — the paraphyletic collection of non-tetrapod vertebrates.
Lungfish are more closely related to you than to a shark.

VERTEBRATE PHYLOGENY:
              VERTEBRATA
                  |
        +---------+---------+
        |                   |
     AGNATHA            GNATHOSTOMATA (jawed)
    (jawless)               |
    Lampreys         +------+------+
    Hagfish          |             |
                 CHONDRICHTHYES  OSTEICHTHYES
                 (cartilaginous)  (bony)
                 Sharks, rays          |
                 ~1,100 sp       +-----+-----+
                             Actinopterygii Sarcopterygii
                             (ray-finned)   (lobe-finned)

  Agnatha:         ~120 sp (lampreys ~38, hagfish ~82)
```

## Layer 1 — Agnatha: Jawless Vertebrates

~120 species. Ancient lineage. Two very different groups.

### Petromyzontida (Lampreys)

```
LAMPREY BODY PLAN

No jaws:    Oral disc with horny "teeth" (not true teeth)
No scales:  Smooth skin
No paired fins: Median fins only
Notochord:  Persists throughout life (no complete vertebrae)
7 gill pouches on each side (open separately, not under operculum)
Cartilaginous skeleton

SUCKER MOUTH:
  Circular oral disc with rasping teeth plates
  Attaches to host, secretes anticoagulant
  Rasps through scales/skin → feeds on blood + body fluids

LIFE CYCLE:
  Ammocoetes larva: blind, burrowed in stream sediment
    Filter feeds via endostyle (!) for 3–7 YEARS
  Metamorphosis: eyes open, oral disc develops
  Adult: migrates to sea/large lakes; parasitic
  Spawning migration: returns to freshwater
  Adults die after spawning (semelparous)

SEA LAMPREY (Petromyzon marinus) in Great Lakes:
  Accidentally introduced via Welland Canal
  Devastated lake trout fishery 1940s–50s
  Population controlled by lampricide (selective larval toxin)

PHYLOGENETIC IMPORTANCE:
  Lamprey genome sequenced — ~3 HOX clusters
  (intermediate between amphioxus 1 and gnathostome 4)
  Disputed whether 1R or 2R WGD before divergence
```

### Myxini (Hagfish)

```
HAGFISH BODY PLAN

No vertebrae at all (most primitive vertebrate)
  → Some classify outside Vertebrata as "Craniata"
No lateral line
No stomach
Eyes: vestigial (under skin) — functionally blind
6–10 pairs of gill pouches opening in common duct

SLIME:
  Mucus + protein threads released within milliseconds
  Threads hydrate rapidly → viscous gel
  1 g of thread material → ~20L of slime
  Clogs gill slits of predators → predator releases hagfish
  Hagfish can tie itself in a knot to scrape off slime

FEEDING:
  Scavengers + predators
  Burrow into carcasses, feed from inside
  Can absorb nutrients directly through skin
  (No stomach → skin absorption covers low pH)
  Can survive ~18 months without food

HAGFISH SLIME THREADS:
  Protein intermediate filaments (α and γ)
  Tensile strength > steel weight-for-weight
  Biomedical interest: spider silk alternative?
  Textiles research: slow-release from "skeins"

CARDIAC ACCESSORIES:
  Four accessory hearts in addition to main heart
  Cope with sluggish circulation in low-O₂ carcasses
```

## Layer 2 — Chondrichthyes (Cartilaginous Fish)

~1,100 species. Sharks, rays, skates, chimaeras.

```
CHONDRICHTHYES BODY PLAN INNOVATIONS

CARTILAGE SKELETON:
  Cartilage (not bone) throughout — but NOT primitive
  Bone evolved FIRST in vertebrates (Agnatha had bone armor)
  Chondrichthyes LOST bone → cartilage is derived state
  Advantage: lighter, flexible, faster acceleration

PLACOID SCALES (denticles):
  Same structure as teeth: enamel + dentine + pulp
  Cover entire body → rough skin (used as sandpaper historically)
  "Shagreen" leather
  TEETH = modified placoid scales (evolutionary continuity)
  Sharks continuously replace teeth: ~20,000 teeth in lifetime

HETEROCERCAL TAIL:
  Upper lobe larger than lower (asymmetric)
  Creates lift as well as thrust
  Compensates for negative buoyancy (no swim bladder)

AMPULLAE OF LORENZINI:
  Electroreceptive organs in head pores
  Detect weak electric fields (< 0.005 μV/cm)
  All living organisms generate bioelectric fields
  → Sharks locate prey buried in sand
  → Navigate using Earth's magnetic field

SPIRACLES:
  Vestigial first gill slit (behind eye in many)
  Ancestral function: intake water while mouth biting prey
  Rays (bottom-dwellers): enlarged, primary water intake
```

### Shark Biology

```
SHARK MAJOR GROUPS:
+---------------------------------------------------------+
| Elasmobranchii                                          |
|   Selachii (sharks) ~500 sp                             |
|     Carcharhiniformes (ground sharks) — most diverse    |
|       Tiger shark, bull shark, great white              |
|     Lamniformes (mackerel sharks)                       |
|       White shark, mako, thresher, megamouth            |
|     Squaliformes (dogfish sharks)                       |
|       Most deep-sea sharks; spiny dogfish               |
|     Orectolobiformes (carpet sharks)                    |
|       Nurse shark, whale shark                          |
|   Batoidea (rays, skates, sawfish) ~600 sp              |
|     Dorsoventrally flattened; pectoral fins = wings     |
|     Electric ray: 200V discharge                        |
|     Manta ray: 7m wingspan; filter feeder               |
| Holocephali (chimaeras) ~50 sp                          |
|   Deep sea; venomous dorsal spine                       |
+---------------------------------------------------------+

SHARK REPRODUCTION:
  Oviparous: egg case ("mermaid's purse") — dogfish, horn sharks
  Ovoviviparous: eggs hatch inside mother — most sharks
  Viviparous: placenta-like connection — some carcharhinids
  Intrauterine cannibalism: sand tiger shark embryos eat siblings
  → Usually 2 pups born (one from each uterus — the survivors)

WHALE SHARK (Rhincodon typus):
  Largest fish: 18+ m; 19 tonnes
  Filter feeder: 6,000 L water/hour; eats krill + small fish
  Ovoviviparous; may carry 300+ embryos
```

### Electric Rays and Sawfish

```
TORPEDO (electric ray):
  Electroplax organs: modified muscle (as in electric eel)
  Two kidney-shaped organs, 500–1000 stacked electrocytes
  200 V, up to 8 A — can stun adult human

PRISTIS (sawfish):
  Actually a ray, not a shark
  Rostrum: flattened saw with rostral teeth (modified scales)
  Electroreceptive + mechanosensory along rostrum
  Swipes through schools of fish; critically endangered
```

## Layer 3 — Actinopterygii (Ray-Finned Bony Fish)

~34,000 species = ~50% of all vertebrate species.

```
ACTINOPTERYGII INNOVATIONS

OPERCULUM: bony flap covering gills
  Enables two-stroke breathing (mouth open + operculum close
  creates negative pressure; mouth close + operculum open
  creates positive pressure)
  → More efficient gill ventilation than shark (one-way)

SWIM BLADDER: gas-filled sac for buoyancy regulation
  Derived from: dorsal outgrowth of gut (lung homolog!)
  Physostomous: connected to gut → fill by gulping air
    (ancestral; basal fish like bowfin, gars)
  Physoclistous: closed → fill by gas gland (O₂ secretion)
    (derived; most teleosts)
  Enables: neutral buoyancy at any depth
           passive depth maintenance → no swimming required
  Barotrauma: rapid decompression → swim bladder ruptures
    (problem with deep-caught fish; "fizzing" to release gas)

BONY SKELETON:
  Calcified bone throughout (not cartilage)
  Lighter relative to strength than cartilage
  Allows more complex, refined fin articulation

SCALES:
  Ganoid (primitive — diamond, enameled)
  Cycloid (smooth) — most teleosts
  Ctenoid (comb-edge) — perch, bass — gives rough feel
```

### Actinopterygian Diversity

```
ACTINOPTERYGII PHYLOGENY:

Polypteridae (bichirs, reedfish):
  Lungfish-like, basal — "living fossils"
  Africa; breathe air; ancestral fin lobes
  Retain ganoid scales + spiracle

Chondrostei (sturgeons, paddlefish):
  Largely cartilaginous skeleton (secondarily)
  Scutes instead of scales; rostral barbels
  Sturgeon: >100 years old; caviar; critically endangered
  Paddlefish (Polyodon): filter feeds on plankton with paddle

Holostei (gars, bowfin):
  Connecting grade to teleosts
  Gar: armored ganoid scales, can breathe air

TELEOSTEI (~30,000 sp) = dominant group:
  Clupeiformes  — herring, sardine, anchovy (huge schools)
  Salmoniformes — salmon, trout (diadromous lifecycle)
  Angulliformes — eels (leptocephalus larva)
  Cypriniformes — carp, zebrafish, loach
  Siluriformes  — catfish (barbels, electroreception)
  Gadiformes    — cod, haddock, pollock (commercial fish)
  Perciformes   — largest order: perch, bass, cichlids
  Tetraodontiformes — pufferfish, triggerfish, mola
  Syngnathiformes— seahorses, pipefish (male pregnancy!)
  Pleuronectiformes— flatfish (migrating eye!)
```

### Remarkable Teleost Adaptations

```
FLYING FISH (Exocoetidae):
  Enlarged pectoral fins = wings
  Taxi along surface → airborne to 45 m altitude
  Glide 400m, up to 70 km/h
  Escape predators in sea

DEEP SEA FISH:
  Anglerfish (Ceratiidae):
    Females: bioluminescent esca (lure) with bacteria
    Males: vastly smaller; parasitic attachment to female
    Male bites female → fuses permanently → shared circulation
    Female carries 6+ males as "sperm factories"
  Viperfish (Chauliodus):
    Photophores along body; fang teeth too large to close mouth
  Dragonfish (Melanostomias):
    Far-red bioluminescence — unique; invisible to most deep-sea prey
    Only known fish to see its own far-red light

SEAHORSE (Hippocampus):
  Male PREGNANCY: female deposits eggs in male's brood pouch
  Male gestates 2–6 weeks; births 100–1500 young
  Camouflage, prehensile tail, no caudal fin (gyrating dorsal)
  No stomach — must eat constantly

ELECTRIC EEL (Electrophorus electricus):
  Not a true eel (Gymnotiformes, South America)
  80% of body = electric organ (modified muscle)
  Up to 860 V, 1 A — can stun large mammals
  Three electric organs: main + Hunter's + Sachs's
  Prey stunning, navigation (weak discharge), communication
```

## Layer 4 — Sarcopterygii (Lobe-Finned Fish)

~8 living species. Our lineage.

```
SARCOPTERYGII:
  Lobe fins with internal skeletal elements matching tetrapod limbs
  Lungfish (Dipnoi) — 6 species in Africa, South America, Australia
  Coelacanth (Actinistia) — 2 species (Latimeria)
  All tetrapods (including humans!)

LOBE FIN vs RAY FIN:
  Ray fin: thin fin rays without internal skeleton
  Lobe fin: INTERNAL BONES within fin (proximal to rays)
    Humerus-like → radius/ulna-like → hand bones
    These are the homologs of your arm bones

FISH "SHOULDER" → HUMAN ARM:
+-------------------------------------------+
| Sarcopterygian fin   | Tetrapod limb      |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Proximal bone (1)   | Humerus            |
| Two intermediate (2)| Radius + ulna      |
| Distal bones        | Carpals            |
| Fin rays (branching)| Metacarpals+digits |
+-------------------------------------------+
```

### Coelacanth (Latimeria)

```
LATIMERIA CHALUMNAE — discovered 1938

Before 1938: known only from fossils, last ~65 Ma
1938: caught off East London, South Africa → stunned biology
1998: second species (L. menadoensis) in Indonesia

FEATURES:
  Lobed fins with internal skeleton (classic sarcopterygian)
  Hollow fluid-filled notochord (no vertebral centra)
  Intracranial joint: skull divided; jaw opens wider
  Rostral organ: electroreceptive (ampullae-like)
  Hindbrain: no cerebellum (unlike all other jawed vertebrates)

GENOME (2013):
  ~2.86 billion bp; ~19,000 genes
  Slowest-evolving vertebrate genome known
  Protein-coding genes: ~17 substitutions per Myr
    (vs ~857 for human branch)
  ← but genome-wide, regulation still evolving

HABITAT:
  ~150–700 m depth; Comoro Islands, Mozambique, Indonesia
  Day: shelters in volcanic caves
  Night: drifts headstand or inverted (passive drift feeding?)
  Live bearing: ~5 pups per litter; gestation ~3 years
```

### Lungfish (Dipnoi)

```
LUNGFISH — the tetrapod sister group

Six species:
  Africa:        Protopterus (4 species) — can aestivate
  South America: Lepidosiren
  Australia:     Neoceratodus — single "lung" (more primitive)

BILATERAL LUNGS (or single lung):
  Lungs = modified swimbladder
  (Or swimbladder = modified lung — debate resolved: LUNG came first)
  Breathe air; can survive months in dry season

AESTIVATION (Protopterus):
  Burrows into mud, secretes mucus cocoon
  Reduces metabolism ~1/60 normal
  Can survive 4 YEARS of drought in mucus-lined burrow
  When rains return: cocoon dissolves → fish swims away

TETRAPOD SISTER:
  Molecular phylogeny: Lungfish = closest living fish relative of tetrapods
    (Coelacanth = next closest)
  Lungfish have: similar hemoglobin, similar immune genes
  Share developmental genes with tetrapods
  BUT lungfish are NOT the direct ancestor (they have their own specializations)
```

### Tiktaalik — The Key Transitional Fossil

```
TIKTAALIK ROSEAE (2004, Ellesmere Island, Canada)
Age: 375 million years (Devonian)

+------------------------------------------+
|  FISH FEATURES     | TETRAPOD FEATURES   |
|--------------------|---------------------|
|  Scales            | Neck (can turn head)|
|  Gills             | Ribs (robust)       |
|  Fin rays          | Flat skull          |
|                    | Fin with:           |
|                    |  Humerus            |
|                    |  Radius + ulna      |
|                    |  Wrist (proto)      |
|                    |  "Hand" elements    |
+------------------------------------------+

SIGNIFICANCE:
  Predicted by Neil Shubin et al. before finding it
  Devonian strata in high arctic → where to dig
  Fills the gap between fish and tetrapods
  Limbs evolved IN THE WATER (locomotion in shallow water)
    before land colonization

NEAR-CONTEMPORARIES:
  Acanthostega (365 Ma): 8-fingered limbs, used in water
  Ichthyostega (365 Ma): 7-fingered limbs; partial land capable
  → "Fins became legs while animals still lived in water"
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

```
WHAT FISH IS THIS?

No jaws, eel-like body, no paired fins?
  → AGNATHA
  Sucker mouth with rasping teeth, 7 gill pores → Lamprey
  No eyes visible, releases slime when grabbed → Hagfish

Has jaws. Cartilage skeleton (X-ray shows no bone)?
  → CHONDRICHTHYES
  Streamlined, heterocercal tail, rough skin? → SHARK
  Flattened, pectoral fins as wings, eyes on top → RAY/SKATE
  Blade-like saw rostrum → Sawfish (critically endangered)
  Chimaera-like (long rat tail, plate teeth)  → Holocephali

Has jaws. Bony skeleton. Fins with thin rays?
  → ACTINOPTERYGII
  Very deep body, flattened laterally?  → likely Pleuronectiformes (flatfish)
  Elongated eel-like?                   → Anguilliformes
  Seahorse shape?                       → Syngnathiformes
  Puffs up when threatened?             → Tetraodontiformes

Has jaws. Bony skeleton. Fins with fleshy lobe?
  → SARCOPTERYGII
  Known only from Indian Ocean/Indonesia? → Coelacanth
  Has actual lungs, freshwater?          → Lungfish
  Has four limbs?                        → TETRAPOD (you're here)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Fish" is polyphyletic for practical purposes.**
Agnatha + Chondrichthyes + Actinopterygii + Sarcopterygii don't share a common ancestor that isn't also a tetrapod ancestor. When you say "fish," you're describing a body plan, not a clade.

**Sharks predate dinosaurs by ~250 million years.**
Earliest sharks: ~450 Ma (Ordovician). Modern shark body plan established by ~350 Ma. Dinosaurs: ~235 Ma. Sharks survived all five mass extinctions.

**The swim bladder is a modified lung, not the reverse.**
Lungfish and the most basal bony fish have lungs first, then some lineages converted them to swim bladders. Air-breathing came first; buoyancy control came later.

**Salmon don't all die after spawning.**
Pacific salmon (Oncorhynchus) are semelparous — spawn once, die. Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) are iteroparous — can survive and spawn multiple times, though many die. The death fuels the stream ecosystem: "marine-derived nutrients" from carcasses feed the forest.

**Flatfish eyes migrate — they're not born flat.**
Larval flatfish are bilateral with one eye on each side. During metamorphosis one eye migrates to the other side. The fish then lies on its blind side. The eye migration is driven by thyroid hormone and involves actual bone remodeling.

**Electric eels can recharge.**
The electric organ stores charge like a capacitor (multiple electrocytes in series). After discharge, recovery takes ~milliseconds for the weak electric field; the high-voltage hunting discharge requires ~1 second to rebuild. Eel hunts in pulses, not continuous discharge.
