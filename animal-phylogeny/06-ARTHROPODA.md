# Arthropoda — The Most Species-Rich Phylum

## The Big Picture

```
ARTHROPODA — ~1.1 million described species
= 80–85% of all described animal species

DEFINING FEATURES:
  Segmented body
  Jointed appendages (one per segment ancestrally)
  Rigid exoskeleton (cuticle: chitin + proteins)
  Hemocoel (open circulatory system)
  Moulting (ecdysis) to grow

ARTHROPOD TREE:
                    ARTHROPODA
           +----------+----------+
           |          |          |
       Chelicerata  Myriapoda  Pancrustacea
       (arachnids, (centipedes, +---+---+
        horseshoe   millipedes) |       |
        crabs)                Crustacea Hexapoda
                              (shrimp,  (insects)
                               crabs)

Note: Pancrustacea = Hexapoda + Crustacea form a CLADE
Insects are more closely related to crustaceans
than crustaceans are to myriapods.
(Myriapoda is sister to Pancrustacea)

SPECIES COUNTS:
  Insecta:      ~1,000,000 described (~8M estimated)
  Arachnida:    ~100,000 described
  Crustacea:    ~67,000 described
  Myriapoda:    ~13,000 described
```

## Layer 1 — The Arthropod Body Plan

### Exoskeleton Architecture

```
CUTICLE LAYERS (outside to inside)
+--------------------------------------------+
|  Epicuticle     Thin, waxy, waterproof     |
|                 (wax layer = key land       |
|                  conquest innovation)       |
+--------------------------------------------+
|  Exocuticle     Hardened by quinone        |
|                 tanning (sclerotization)    |
+--------------------------------------------+
|  Endocuticle    Soft, flexible,            |
|                 laminated chitin-protein    |
+--------------------------------------------+
|  Epidermis      Secretes above layers      |
+--------------------------------------------+

CHITIN: β-1,4-linked N-acetylglucosamine polymer
  Crystalline microfibrils embedded in protein matrix
  Light but strong (tensile strength ~ mild steel relative to weight)
  Flexible at joints (endocuticle only, no sclerotization)

SCLEROTIZATION: irreversible hardening
  Quinones cross-link chitin-protein → rigid
  Cannot be softened → must MOLT to grow
```

### Molting (Ecdysis)

```
ECDYSIS CYCLE:

1. PROECDYSIS (preparation):
   New cuticle forms under old
   Enzymes reabsorb inner old cuticle layers
   Animal swells with air/water

2. ECDYSIS (molting):
   Old exoskeleton splits (usually at head/thorax)
   Animal pulls out of old "shell"
   Soft, pale (teneral state)
   Minutes to hours depending on size

3. POSTECDYSIS (hardening):
   Expand to new size (air/water in hemocoel)
   Sclerotization of new cuticle
   Days to weeks for large species

VULNERABILITY: soft during molting = high predation risk
Blue crab (Callinectes sapidus): sold as "soft-shell crab"
  = harvested immediately after molting

HORMONE CONTROL:
  Ecdysteroids: trigger molting
  Juvenile hormone (JH): maintain larval state
    JH high → larva; JH absent → metamorphosis
```

## Layer 2 — Chelicerata

~115,000 species. Defined by chelicerae (first pair of appendages = pincers/fangs, not antennae).

```
CHELICERATA TREE
  Merostomata (horseshoe crabs) — 4 species
  Pycnogonida (sea spiders) — 1,300 species
  Arachnida — ~110,000 species

BODY PLAN:
  Prosoma (cephalothorax) — head + thorax fused
  Opisthosoma (abdomen)
  NO antennae
  NO mandibles
  Appendage pairs in prosoma:
    Chelicerae (pair 1): feeding, venom
    Pedipalps (pair 2): sensory/reproduction/prey manipulation
    Walking legs (pairs 3–6): 4 pairs in most arachnids
```

### Horseshoe Crabs

```
LIMULUS POLYPHEMUS (Atlantic horseshoe crab)
  Not a crab: closest living relative of Arachnida
  250 million years essentially unchanged

MEDICAL IMPORTANCE:
  Amebocytes in blood clot in response to bacterial endotoxin
  → Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) test
  Every injectable drug/vaccine/device tested for endotoxin
    using LAL (or recombinant Factor C test)
  ~600,000 horseshoe crabs bled annually in US
  Population concerns → push for synthetic rFC test

BOOK GILLS: 5 pairs of plate-like gills in abdomen
COMPOUND EYES: 2 lateral + 2 median + 5 more eyes
  Lateral eye: largest ommatidia of any arthropod
  Used in early vision research (lateral inhibition)
```

### Arachnida Overview

| Order | Appendages | Venom | Habitat | Examples |
|-------|-----------|-------|---------|---------|
| Araneae (spiders) | Chelicerae inject venom | Almost all venomous | Terrestrial | Black widow, tarantula, orb weaver |
| Scorpiones | Chelicerae + sting in tail | All venomous | Desert/tropical | Emperor scorpion, Leiurus |
| Acari (mites + ticks) | Chelicerae variously modified | Ticks: not venomous | All habitats | Ixodes (Lyme), Demodex (face mites), dust mites |
| Opiliones (harvestmen) | Long legs | Not venomous | Terrestrial | "Daddy long-legs" |
| Pseudoscorpionida | Pedipalps with venom | Pedipalp venom | Leaf litter | Tiny, travel on insects (phoresy) |

### Spider Biology

```
ARANEAE SILK — multiple types, one spider

GLAND TYPE        SILK                    USE
Ampullate (major) Dragline/frame silk     Capture threads, safety line
Ampullate (minor) Dragline               Structural web
Flagelliform      Spiral capture silk     Sticky capture threads
Tubuliform        Egg case silk           Egg sac protection
Aciniform         Wrapping silk           Immobilize prey
Pyriform          Attachment disc         Anchor silk to substrate

SILK PROPERTIES:
  Tensile strength: 1–4 GPa (strong as Kevlar relative to weight)
  Extensibility: up to 40% elongation before breaking
  Toughness: greater than any synthetic fiber
  dragline: stronger than high-grade steel by weight

WEB TYPES:
  Orb web: 2D geometric (Araneidae — Araneus, Argiope)
  Cobweb: 3D tangle (Theridiidae — black widow)
  Funnel web: grass funnel (Agelenidae)
  Sheet web: horizontal sheet (Linyphiidae)
  No web: active hunters (wolf spiders, jumping spiders)

BOOK LUNGS (arachnid gas exchange):
  Stacked lamellae of blood-filled sheets in hemocoel
  Air enters through slits (spiracles)
  Passive diffusion between air and hemolymph
  Supplemented by tracheae in many species
```

### Scorpion Venom

```
Scorpion venom: 1,500 known species with venom
~30–40 species dangerous to humans

Leiurus quinquestriatus (yellow death stalker):
  Multiple peptide toxins
  Target: voltage-gated Na⁺/K⁺ channels
  Chlorotoxin: used in cancer imaging research
    (binds specifically to Cl⁻ channels overexpressed in glioma)

Scorpion sensing:
  Pectines: comb-like ventral organs
    Chemoreception + vibration detection
    Males scan substrate for female pheromones
  Slit sensilla: detect surface vibrations (prey)
  Eyes: 2–5 pairs, but most scorpions are night hunters by chemical cues
```

## Layer 3 — Myriapoda

~13,000 species. Two subgroups: Chilopoda (centipedes) and Diplopoda (millipedes).

```
MYRIAPODA BODY PLAN
  Head + trunk (many segments)
  One pair antennae
  Tracheal respiration (like insects)
  Two subgroups differ in appendage number per segment:

  CHILOPODA (centipedes):         DIPLOPODA (millipedes):
  One pair legs per segment        TWO pairs per segment
  Predatory                        Detritivores
  Forcipules (venom claws)         Chemical defense
  Flattened body (fast)            Cylindrical body (burrowing)

  Arthropleura (Carboniferous):
    Giant millipede, 2.5m long
    Probably ate plants in coal forests
    Largest land arthropod ever known
```

### Centipedes

```
CHILOPODA — key details

Forcipules: modified first appendages → venom claws
  Inject neurotoxic/cytotoxic venom
  Scolopendra gigantea: 30 cm; hunts lizards, mice, bats
  Scolopendra subspinipes: painful, occasionally fatal

Speed: fastest myriapods (Scutigera can run 40 cm/s)
Always ODD number of leg pairs (can't add one pair per side)
  → always asymmetric

Scutigera coleoptrata (house centipede):
  15 pairs of legs, antennae-like rear legs
  Effective household pest predator
```

## Layer 4 — Crustacea

~67,000 described species. Dominant marine invertebrates.

```
CRUSTACEA BODY PLAN:
  Two pairs of antennae (unique diagnostic)
  Biramous appendages (two branches per limb)
  Nauplius larva in most groups
  Gills for gas exchange (mostly)
  Carapace in many (extends from head → covers thorax)

PANCRUSTACEA CLADE:
  Crustacea is PARAPHYLETIC — some crustaceans
  are more closely related to insects than to other crustaceans
  Remipedia (blind cave crustaceans) = sister to insects!
```

### Major Crustacean Groups

| Group | Characteristics | Examples |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Malacostraca | 8 thorax + 6 abdomen segments; largest class | Shrimp, lobsters, crabs, isopods, amphipods |
| Branchiopoda | Leaf-like legs with gills | Daphnia (water flea), brine shrimp (Artemia) |
| Maxillopoda | Reduced abdomen, specialized | Barnacles, copepods |
| Ostracoda | Bivalved carapace | Seed shrimp |
| Remipedia | Homonomously segmented, cave-dwelling | Rare, deep anchialine caves |

### Barnacles — The Sessile Crustacean

```
BARNACLE LIFECYCLE:
  Nauplius larva (free-swimming)
       ↓
  Cypris larva (settlement stage)
       ↓
  Cement: one of strongest biological adhesives
  (~700 kPa; maintained under water on rocks/ships)
       ↓
  Calcified plates → sessile adult

FEEDING:
  Cirri (modified legs) sweep through water
  Capture plankton + detritus
  Barnacle = "a shrimp standing on its head, eating with its feet"

PENIS: longest relative to body size of any animal
  Up to 8× body length — extends to fertilize neighbors
  (Barnacles are hermaphroditic)

PARASITIC BARNACLES (Rhizocephala):
  Sacculina: internal network of rootlets throughout crab
  Castrates the host; turns male crabs into "females"
  Manipulates host behavior to care for barnacle egg mass
```

### Deep Sea / Hydrothermal Vent Crustaceans

```
YETI CRAB (Kiwa hirsuta):
  Found 2005 at Easter Island vents
  Hairy claws culture chemosynthetic bacteria (food?)

RIMICARIS EXOCULATA (vent shrimp):
  Blind (no eyes) but has photoreceptor on back
  Detects faint glow from superheated vent water
  "Farms" bacteria on mouthparts at 300°C+ vents
```

## Layer 5 — Insecta (Class Hexapoda, Subclass Insecta)

~1,000,000 described species. Possibly 8 million total.

```
INSECT BODY PLAN
+-----------------------------------------------+
|  HEAD          THORAX         ABDOMEN          |
|  Antenna pair  Pro/Meso/Meta  10–11 segments   |
|  Compound eyes 3 pairs legs   Spiracles        |
|  Ocelli (3)    2 pairs wings  Reproductive     |
|  Mouthparts    (typically)    organs           |
+-----------------------------------------------+

KEY INNOVATIONS FOR LAND:
  Waterproof cuticle (epicuticle wax)
  Tracheae: direct air tubes to cells (no lung needed)
  Internal fertilization
  Metamorphosis: separate life stages
  Wings: only arthropods to independently evolve flight
```

### Metamorphosis

```
HEMIMETABOLA                    HOLOMETABOLA
(incomplete metamorphosis)      (complete metamorphosis)
                                Derived; more species
  Egg                            Egg
    ↓                              ↓
  Nymph (series)                 Larva (caterpillar, maggot)
  resembles adult,               COMPLETELY DIFFERENT
  no wings initially             body plan from adult
    ↓                              ↓
  Adult (winged)                 PUPA (chrysalis, cocoon)
                                 Imaginal discs build
Orders: Orthoptera, Hemiptera    adult body from scratch
Odonata, Dictyoptera              ↓
                                 Adult (imago)

                               Orders: Coleoptera, Lepidoptera
                               Diptera, Hymenoptera

WHY HOLOMETABOLA WINS:
  Larva and adult exploit completely different resources
  No competition within species between life stages
  Adults can specialize entirely for reproduction/dispersal
  ~88% of insect species are holometabolous
```

### Eusociality

```
EUSOCIALITY — the highest level of social organization
Defined by:
  1. Overlapping generations
  2. Cooperative brood care
  3. Reproductive division of labor (queens + workers)

EVOLVED INDEPENDENTLY ~20 times in:
  Hymenoptera (ants, bees, wasps) × 11+
  Blattodea (termites) × 1
  Thysanoptera (thrips) × 1
  Crustacea (snapping shrimp Synalpheus) × 1
  Mammalia (naked mole rat, damaraland mole rat) × 2

HAPLODIPLOID HYPOTHESIS (ants/bees/wasps):
  Females: diploid (2n); Males: haploid (n)
  Sisters share 75% of genes (vs 50% in normal diploid)
  Workers gain more fitness helping raise sisters
  than laying own eggs → altruism favored by kin selection

TERMITE EUSOCIALITY:
  Both sexes diploid
  Different explanation: mutualism + fortress defense
  Cellulose digestion requires gut symbionts
    → niche only works with large established colony

ANT SOCIALITY EXTREMES:
  Leaf-cutter ants (Atta): fungal gardens (10M year old)
    Largest non-human societies; complex task allocation
  Army ants (Eciton): bivouac colonies, nomadic raids
    Queens lay 3–4 million eggs in 35 days
  Weaver ants (Oecophylla): silk bridges by larvae
    Workers hold larva + squeeze silk → sew leaves
```

### Insect Chemosensory Systems

```
INSECT COMMUNICATION BY CHEMISTRY

Pheromones:
  Sex pheromones (find mates over km)
  Alarm pheromones (warn colony)
  Trail pheromones (ants → resource)
  Queen substances (suppress worker reproduction)

Bombyx mori (silkworm moth):
  Female releases bombykol (10-trans,12-cis-hexadecadienol)
  Male antennae: 17,000 sensilla, each with 2 receptor neurons
  Can detect single molecule per antenna
  Responds to gradient (climb upwind = klinokinesis)

Ant trail pheromone specificity:
  Different species use different chemicals
  Same species: different trail = 50-100× concentration threshold
  Trail evaporates in minutes → only fresh = active colony route
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

```
ARTHROPOD IDENTIFICATION

How many walking legs?
  4 pairs (8 legs) + no antennae → ARACHNIDA
    + Stinging tail → Scorpiones
    + Pincers (pedipalps) + 8 legs → if tiny, Pseudoscorpionida
    + Normal spider body → Araneae
    + Tiny, parasite → Acari (mite or tick)

  2+ many pairs + 1 antenna pair → MYRIAPODA
    1 pair legs/segment + forcipules → CHILOPODA (centipede)
    2 pairs legs/segment + cylindrical → DIPLOPODA (millipede)

  Variable legs + 2 antenna pairs → CRUSTACEA
    10 walking legs (5 pairs) + carapace → Decapoda
    No legs visible, attached to substrate → Barnacle (Cirripedia)
    Tiny, planktonic → Copepoda
    Flat oval, terrestrial, many legs → Isopoda (woodlouse)

  Exactly 6 legs + 1 antenna pair + 3 body sections → INSECTA
    Hard wing covers → Coleoptera (beetle)
    One pair wings → Diptera (fly)
    Scales on wings → Lepidoptera (butterfly/moth)
    Constricted waist → Hymenoptera (bee/wasp/ant)
    Long jumping legs → Orthoptera (grasshopper/cricket)
    Piercing mouthparts → Hemiptera (bug)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Insects are crustaceans (phylogenetically).**
Pancrustacea = hexapods + crustaceans. The ancestor of insects was an aquatic crustacean. Insects are not a separate lineage from crustaceans — they ARE crustaceans that colonized land.

**Spiders vs daddy longlegs.**
Opiliones (harvestmen/"daddy longlegs") are arachnids but not spiders. Differences: no waist constriction, no venom, no silk, eyes fused in one pair, single body oval. The house cellar spider (Pholcus) is an actual spider, also called "daddy longlegs" — different animal.

**A spider's silk is not a web.**
Spiders produce multiple types of silk (7 glands in some) — draglines, capture spirals, egg cases, attachment discs. The "web" is only one application of one silk type. Many spiders (wolf spiders, jumping spiders) hunt without webs.

**Beetle diversity isn't random.**
Coleoptera is so diverse partly because of the elytra (protective wing covers) which opened up soil, wood, and seed niches — protected microhabitats inaccessible to other insects. Also their life cycle: holometabolous larvae exploit rotting wood, soil, etc. while adults are mobile dispersers.

**Tracheal system bypasses the heart.**
Insects have an open circulatory system (hemocoel) with a dorsal tubular heart. But oxygen isn't carried by the blood — tracheae deliver O₂ directly to cells. This means insect circulation only transports nutrients, hormones, and cells. It works because insect bodies are small enough for direct gas diffusion through tracheae.

**Locust swarms are triggered by crowding.**
Desert locust (Schistocerca gregaria): solitary phase (green, cryptic) and gregarious phase (yellow/black, swarm). Crowding triggers serotonin release → behavior + morphology change. Swarms of 10⁹ individuals, 80 km/day, eat own body weight daily.
