# Bauhaus and Modernist Furniture

## The Big Picture

The Bauhaus (1919–1933) was the most consequential design school in history — not because of the objects it produced, but because it established the argument that industrial production requires a new design language rather than historical styles applied to machine-made goods.

```
THE MODERNIST ARGUMENT (simplified):

  Old logic:  beautiful = historical ornament applied to surface
              (Renaissance, Baroque, Victorian)

  New logic:  beautiful = honest use of material + industrial method
              The machine CAN produce beautiful things, but only
              if designed FOR the machine, not despite it.

INFLUENCES FEEDING BAUHAUS:

  Arts & Crafts (Morris) -----> Anti-ornament thesis
                                 (but Bauhaus rejects the hand-craft solution)
  Adolf Loos (1908) ----------> "Ornament is Crime" essay
  De Stijl (Mondrian, 1917) --> Geometric abstraction, primary colors
  Russian Constructivism -----> Art serving society + industrial production
  Frank Lloyd Wright ---------> Organic integration, Prairie style
  American industry ----------> Henry Ford, assembly line as method
```

## The Bauhaus: Structure and History

```
TIMELINE:
  1919: Founded in Weimar, Germany by Walter Gropius
        (merger of art school + applied arts school)
  1923: "Art and Technology — A New Unity" (shift from craft to industry)
  1925: Moves to Dessau — purpose-built Gropius-designed building
  1928: Gropius leaves; Hannes Meyer takes over (political left turn)
  1930: Ludwig Mies van der Rohe becomes director
  1933: Nazis force closure

EDUCATIONAL STRUCTURE (unique):
  Every student had TWO teachers:
  1. Meister der Form (Master of Form) — visual artist
  2. Meister des Handwerks (Master of Craft) — artisan/craftsman
  Theory: art without craft = impractical abstraction
          craft without art = mere decoration

FURNITURE WORKSHOP:
  Led by: Walter Gropius (initially), then Marcel Breuer
  Output: canonical modernist chairs, tables, storage systems
```

## Marcel Breuer and Tubular Steel

Marcel Breuer was 23 years old when he designed the first tubular steel chair in 1925. The origin story is instructive:

```
BREUER'S INSIGHT:
  Source: his Adler bicycle (steel tubing is strong, flexible, light)
  Problem: wood chairs require many joints; joints are weak points
  Hypothesis: seamless steel tube, bent to shape, eliminates joints
  First chair: B3 (Wassily Chair) — 1925

WASSILY CHAIR (B3, 1925):
  Named after Wassily Kandinsky (Breuer's Bauhaus colleague who
  admired it), though this name came later.

  STRUCTURE:
    +----- continuous bent chrome-plated tube -----+
    |                                               |
    |  Seat, back, arm supports: taut fabric sling  |
    |  No wood, no upholstery, no traditional joints|
    |                                               |
    +-- Two semi-independent tube frames intersect -+

  WHY IT MATTERS:
    Industrial material (steel tube, then used only in bikes/pipes)
    Mass-producible (tube bending is a machine operation)
    Minimum material (sling vs. foam vs. spring)
    Visual honesty (structure is visible, nothing hidden)
    Reduction: if you removed the fabric slings, you'd see the
               pure geometry of the tube structure
```

### The Cantilever Principle

```
CANTILEVERED CHAIR:
  Traditional chair: 4 legs touching floor
  Cantilever: 2 legs form U-shape; back legs don't touch floor
              Seat "floats" off the rear portion of the U

  +---------------+   <- seat (supported by cantilever)
  |               |
 /                 \
|                   |
|                   |
+                   +  <- front legs (only floor contact)
 \                 /
  +---------------+  <- U-base (the cantilever spring)

PHYSICS:
  The U-shape acts as a spring. When you sit, the U bends
  slightly, distributing load through the entire tube.
  The result is a chair that "gives" slightly — more comfortable
  than a rigid 4-leg structure.
  Steel tube in bending: elastic deflection returns to shape.
  This is not an accident — Breuer understood the mechanics.

PRIORITY DISPUTE:
  Three designers claimed to invent the cantilever chair:
  Mart Stam (Dutch, 1927): first to publish a cantilevered design
  Marcel Breuer (B32/Cesca, 1928): more refined; the one in production
  Mies van der Rohe (MR10, 1927): used curved tube for spring effect
  Courts eventually ruled in Stam's favor for invention priority,
  but Breuer's version became the commercial standard.
```

## Mies van der Rohe: Barcelona Chair (1929)

The Barcelona International Exposition of 1929 required Mies (as architect of the German Pavilion) to design furniture for Spanish royalty. The result was one of the most precisely designed chairs ever made.

```
BARCELONA CHAIR ANATOMY:
  Two X-frame steel bars, chrome-plated flat bar (not tube)
  Padded leather cushions on top of straps
  The X-frame: crossed flat steel, bolted at center

  WHY FLAT BAR (not tube):
  Flat bar can be polished to mirror finish on all faces
  Tube shows seam where it's welded or drawn
  Flat bar has specific geometry: each bar curves slightly
  to form the X — this is a subtle spring, like the cantilever

PROPORTIONS:
  Seat height: 435mm (very low)
  Seat depth: 760mm (deep — you recline into it)
  Width: 760mm (generous)

  These proportions are not ergonomically optimal for work.
  The Barcelona Chair is a throne: you don't work in it,
  you receive visitors from it. The low seat height requires
  guests to look slightly up at the occupant.
  The design encodes power even in its dimensions.

PRODUCTION REALITY:
  1929 original: welded steel (32 welds, extensive hand finishing)
  Current Knoll version: seamlessly welded (single piece), CNC-ground,
  hand-polished ~120 hours per chair, leather cut and welded (not sewn)
  Price: ~$9,000–14,000 per chair (authorized Knoll production)
```

## Le Corbusier's LC Series

Le Corbusier (with Charlotte Perriand and Pierre Jeanneret) designed the LC series for the 1929 Salon d'Automne. The chaise longue became the manifesto piece:

```
LC4 CHAISE LONGUE (1928):
  Concept: "relaxation machine" — specific body positions for health
  Structure: two separate components:
    1. H-frame base (adjustable angle, remains fixed)
    2. Curved tubular frame (slides on H-frame to adjust recline angle)
  Body supported by: sprung metal tube + leather cushion

  SEPARABLE COMPONENTS:
  The chaise in its normal position looks like one piece.
  But the curved tube can be removed from the base and placed
  on the floor — becomes a rocking piece.
  Or: tipped to maximum recline, head hangs off the end.
  The chair is a machine with multiple configurations.

PERRIAND'S ROLE:
  Charlotte Perriand designed most of the LC furniture.
  Le Corbusier initially rejected her job application ("we don't
  do cushions here"). She returned with a portfolio and was hired.
  For decades, the furniture was attributed to Le Corbusier alone.
  Historical revision now credits Perriand as primary designer
  of the seating; LC attribution retained by convention.
```

## The Principled Rejection of Ornament

The modernist aesthetic had a philosophical foundation, articulated before the Bauhaus existed:

### Adolf Loos — "Ornament and Crime" (1908/1913)

Loos's argument:
1. Primitive peoples tattoo themselves. As civilization advances, tattooing fades.
2. Ornament is wasted labor — it makes objects more expensive without making them better.
3. In an industrial age, ornament represents evolutionary regression.
4. The plain surface is the mark of the modern person.

This was more polemic than philosophy, but it gave modernist designers a manifesto against Victorian excess.

### Louis Sullivan — "Form Follows Function" (1896)

Sullivan (Chicago architect, mentor to Frank Lloyd Wright) coined the phrase in an essay about skyscraper design. The misquotation dropped "ever follows" — the original is "form ever follows function," as a prescriptive statement, not a description of how things actually are.

```
WHAT "FORM FOLLOWS FUNCTION" ACTUALLY MEANS:
  Not: beautiful things have no ornament
  Actually: the form of a designed object should be derived from
            its function, not from historical precedent
  Example: a chair should look like something for sitting in,
           not like a Gothic throne or Roman curule chair
           unless you actually want those cultural references

WHAT IT DOESN'T MEAN:
  That function alone determines form (all chairs do the same job;
  their forms vary enormously by cultural context and aesthetics)
  That ornament is always wrong
  (Sullivan's own buildings had extraordinary ornamental detailing)
```

## Bauhaus Furniture Workshop Products

```
KEY OBJECTS:
  B3 Wassily Chair (Breuer, 1925)  -- tubular steel + fabric sling
  B32 Cesca Chair (Breuer, 1928)   -- cantilever, cane seat and back
  B64 (Breuer, 1928)               -- cantilever with arms
  MR10 (Mies, 1927)                -- curved tubular steel cantilever
  MR20 (Mies, 1927)                -- cantilever with arms
  Barcelona Chair (Mies, 1929)     -- X-frame flat steel, leather
  LC1-LC4 (Le Corbusier/Perriand)  -- LC series chaise etc.

UNIFYING CHARACTERISTICS:
  All use industrial materials available in 1920s
  All expose structure (no wood covering the steel)
  All use minimum material for function
  All designed for industrial reproduction (not one-off craft)
  All reject historical ornament
  None are particularly comfortable for extended use
```

## Bauhaus Diaspora and American Design Education

When the Nazis closed the Bauhaus in 1933, its faculty dispersed to the United States and remade American design education:

```
EMIGRATION MAP:

  Walter Gropius --------> Harvard GSD (1937–1952, chair of architecture)
  Marcel Breuer ----------> Harvard GSD (with Gropius)
  Mies van der Rohe ------> IIT (Illinois Institute of Technology, Chicago)
                             Designed the campus; led architecture 1938–1958
  Laszlo Moholy-Nagy -----> New Bauhaus Chicago (1937); later IIT Institute
                             of Design — the most direct Bauhaus successor
  Herbert Bayer ----------> Graphic design; later consultant to Container Corp.
  Herbert Hirsche ---------> Various industrial design positions

CONSEQUENCE:
  American architecture schools from 1940–1970 were almost entirely
  taught by Bauhaus-trained faculty or their direct students.
  The International Style (Mies's glass box towers) became the
  default corporate American architecture.
  Herman Miller and Knoll hired designers trained in this tradition —
  Eames, Saarinen, Bertoia — and brought Bauhaus aesthetics into
  corporate America as the default office furniture aesthetic.
```

## International Style Furniture in Corporate America

By the 1950s–60s, Mies's dictum "less is more" had become the default language for corporate interiors:

```
CORPORATE ADOPTION OF MODERNISM:
  Seagram Building (Mies, 1958) lobby: Barcelona Chairs
  IBM headquarters (Eero Saarinen): Barcelona Chairs in reception
  Lincoln Center: Barcelona Chairs
  Time-Life Building lobby: Eames Aluminum Group (1958)

THE SIGNALING:
  Barcelona Chair = wealth, sophistication, modernity
  (A $9,000 chair signals "we can afford $9,000 chairs")
  White walls + Barcelona Chairs = contemporary professional
  (The uniform of art galleries, architect offices, tech lobbies)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Breuer's cantilever chair is often mislabeled**: the B32/Cesca Chair (woven cane seat and back) and B3/Wassily Chair (fabric slings) are different objects by the same designer. Many people say "Wassily" when they mean the cantilever.

**"Less is more" vs. "Less is a bore"**: Mies's phrase was directly countered by Robert Venturi's postmodern manifesto *Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture* (1966). The slogan war defines the Modernism vs. Postmodernism argument in design.

**The Bauhaus was not one aesthetic**: early Bauhaus (Weimar, 1919–1925) was influenced by Expressionism and was more mystical/craft-oriented. Middle Bauhaus (Dessau, 1925–1928) shifted to industrial production emphasis. Late Bauhaus (Dessau/Berlin, 1928–1933) under Mies was most rigorous and austere. Treating it as a single uniform school misrepresents its internal evolution.

**Le Corbusier's furniture was not "designed by Le Corbusier"**: Charlotte Perriand did the furniture design; Le Corbusier did the spatial concepts. The partnership attribution was asymmetric for decades. Modern scholarship credits Perriand appropriately.

**"Bauhaus" style in commercial contexts is usually pastiche**: IKEA's simple geometry is influenced by Bauhaus but is not Bauhaus. The ubiquitous chrome-leg glass-top tables in lobbies are inspired by Bauhaus but were designed by anonymous commercial designers, not Bauhaus faculty.
