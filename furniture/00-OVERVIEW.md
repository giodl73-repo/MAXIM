Every piece of furniture is an answer to the same question: how do we support and shelter human bodies and activities, using available materials and skills, within a cultural context that assigns meaning to those choices?

## The Trade Taxonomy

Furniture has never been one trade. Historically it was four, each with distinct guild standing and skills:

```
+---------------------+----------------------------------+------------------------+
| TRADE               | PRIMARY SKILL                    | MATERIAL DOMAIN        |
+---------------------+----------------------------------+------------------------+
| Cabinetmaker        | Fine joinery, veneering,         | Hardwoods, veneers,    |
|                     | finishing — casework (chests,     | glass, brass hardware  |
|                     | desks, wardrobes, bookcases)      |                        |
+---------------------+----------------------------------+------------------------+
| Joiner              | Structural timber framing and    | Softwood, structural   |
|                     | fitted interior work — chairs,    | hardwood, built-ins    |
|                     | tables, stairs, paneling          |                        |
+---------------------+----------------------------------+------------------------+
| Upholsterer         | Springing, padding, fabric cover  | Springs, webbing,      |
|                     | — seating surfaces and beds       | hair, foam, fabric     |
+---------------------+----------------------------------+------------------------+
| Woodturner          | Lathe work — round components:   | Hardwoods, bone,       |
|                     | legs, spindles, balusters, knobs  | ivory (historical)     |
+---------------------+----------------------------------+------------------------+
```

The modern furniture designer collapses all four roles conceptually, but factories still divide labor along these lines. A Herman Miller factory separates frame assembly, foam pouring, fabric cutting/sewing, and hardware installation.

## Furniture as Industrial History Lens

Furniture style periods map almost perfectly onto manufacturing capability shifts:

```
ERA                    CAPABILITY                     CHARACTERISTIC OUTPUT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Antiquity–1600        Hand tools only                Mortise-and-tenon, carved
                      No mechanical advantage        ornament, solid wood only,
                                                     expensive = high-status

1600–1800             Water-wheel powered mills      Veneering possible (thin
                      Veneer saw                     slices of exotic wood over
                      Finer hand tools               cheaper substrate);
                                                     marquetry, Boulle work

1800–1850             Early steam machinery          Steam-bent wood (Windsor
                      Steam bending                  chairs at scale), lathe-
                      Power lathe                    turned legs cheap, ornate
                                                     Victorian mass production

1850–1920             Michael Thonet bentwood        First industrial furniture
                      Industrial press, steam        (bent beechwood, pre-
                      forming                        assembled globally from
                                                     flat-pack parts — 1859)

1920–1945             Tubular steel rolling          Bauhaus steel furniture;
                      Welding                        tubular steel cantilever
                      Molded plywood (aircraft)      chairs; aircraft-industry
                                                     plywood techniques

1945–1970             WWII tech transfer             Eames molded plywood/
                      Fiberglass (boat industry)     fiberglass; foam replaces
                      Polyurethane foam              springs and horsehair

1970–present          CNC routing (1980s)            Mass customization;
                      CAD/CAM                        flat-pack KD (IKEA);
                      CNC tube bending               parametric design;
                      Injection molding              3D-printed experiments
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The key analogy: each era is a new instruction set that makes previously expensive operations cheap and restructures the entire design space. Mortise-and-tenon is the RISC of joinery — simple, general, repeated everywhere. Victorian steam carving is the equivalent of macro bloat — cheap to produce mechanically, now considered kitschy.

## Furniture as Cultural Signal

Every chair is a power statement. The throne is the extreme case — an elevated, canopied, isolated seat that literally raises the occupant above the room. But even ordinary chairs encode hierarchy:

```
MEDIEVAL HALL:
  Lord alone     --> chair (back + arms = full support = full authority)
  Senior household --> bench (back, no arms)
  Everyone else  --> stool (no back — no support = subordinate)

VICTORIAN HOUSEHOLD:
  Drawing room   --> best upholstered chairs (visitors)
  Dining room    --> mahogany formal chairs
  Kitchen        --> Windsor chairs (robust, no pretension)
  Servants       --> plain deal stools

MODERN OFFICE:
  C-suite        --> high-back executive chair (visual authority)
  Open plan      --> mesh task chair (egalitarian + ergonomic)
  Conference     --> identical chairs (consensus signaling)
```

The word "chair" still carries this: we speak of department chairs, chairs of meetings, chairpersons. The authority of the seat transferred to the role.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What's the difference between a joiner and cabinetmaker? | Joiner = structural timber + fitted work; cabinetmaker = fine casework, veneering, finishing |
| Why did Victorian furniture look so fussy? | Steam-powered mechanical carving made ornament cheap; no design constraint to remove it |
| What technology enabled Thonet's bentwood chairs? | Steam-bent solid beechwood rods, pressure forming; first truly industrial furniture 1859 |
| What connects Bauhaus furniture to aircraft manufacturing? | Both used tubular steel and molded plywood techniques developed/refined in aerospace |
| What is "Mid-Century Modern" actually describing? | Post-WWII ~1945–1969, primarily US/Scandinavian, organic forms + industrial materials |
| Why did Arts & Crafts movement arise? | Reaction against machine-produced Victorian ornament — Ruskin/Morris argument for visible craft |
| What is KD furniture? | Knock-Down: ships flat, assembled by buyer with cam-lock and confirmat hardware — IKEA model |
| How does furniture connect to power/status? | Chair = authority from antiquity; "chair" of a committee preserves this etymologically |

## Common Confusion Points

**Period names are not standardized across countries**: "Georgian" can mean 1714–1830 (all four Georges) or specifically 1714–1760 (early Georgian). "Regency" is technically 1811–1820 but the style extends to ~1830. Cross-reference dates and country of origin when assigning a piece.

**"Antique" has a legal definition**: furniture must be 100+ years old to be imported duty-free as antique in most countries. A 1930 piece is now legally antique; a 1985 piece is "vintage" or "retro" depending on context.

**Arts & Crafts is not Art Nouveau**: Arts & Crafts (Morris, Stickley) is rectilinear, construction-honest, explicitly reacting against industrialism. Art Nouveau (Majorelle, Guimard) is organic curves celebrating nature. Different aesthetics; overlapping dates; different countries of origin.

**Modernism is not "modern"**: In furniture, "Modern" (capital M) means the Bauhaus/Le Corbusier/Eames tradition of machine aesthetics and truth-to-materials, roughly 1920–1970. A piece made last year in classical style is contemporary but not Modern.

**Mid-Century Modern is a retronym**: The designers of the 1950s–60s didn't call it that. The label became popular in the 1980s–90s as collectors and antique dealers needed a handle for the period's pieces.
