# Modern Furniture Materials: Plastics, Steel, and Plywood

## The Big Picture

Every modernist furniture revolution was enabled by a specific material or manufacturing process becoming available or affordable. The map:

```
MATERIAL          WHEN FURNITURE-VIABLE  ENABLED BY               KEY FURNITURE APPLICATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tubular steel     1920s                  Bicycle/pipe industry     Bauhaus cantilever chairs
Bent/molded ply   1930s–45              Aircraft/Navy production  Aalto L-leg, Eames DCM
Fiberglass (GRP)  Late 1940s            Boat hull production      Eames shell chair
Polyurethane foam 1950s–60s            Chemical industry         Replaces springs + hair
Die-cast aluminum 1950s                Aircraft production        Eames 670 base
Injection-molded  1960s–70s            Consumer goods            Panton Chair, Kartell
polypropylene
CNC-cut MDF/ply   1980s                Manufacturing automation   Flat-pack precision
CNC-cut solid wood 1990s–2000s        Manufacturing automation   Custom furniture at scale
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Plywood Engineering

Plywood is the first major material departure from solid wood in furniture. Its properties are a direct consequence of its cross-grain laminate structure.

### Structure and Properties

```
PLYWOOD LAMINATE STRUCTURE:
  +------------------------------------------+
  | Face veneer:     grain runs E-W          |
  +------------------------------------------+
  | Core veneer:     grain runs N-S  ← alternates |
  +------------------------------------------+
  | Core veneer:     grain runs E-W          |
  +------------------------------------------+
  | Core veneer:     grain runs N-S           |
  +------------------------------------------+
  | Back veneer:     grain runs E-W          |
  +------------------------------------------+
  Always odd number of plies; outer faces parallel.

WHAT CROSS-GRAIN LAMINATION ACHIEVES:
  - Eliminates directional shrinkage: each ply constrains the others
  - Resists splitting: no single grain direction throughout
  - Enables large, flat, stable panels (solid wood warps at width)
  - Gives approximately equal strength in both directions (face direction)
  - Maintains about 65-70% of solid wood strength at 40-50% of weight
```

### Bending Plywood

```
BENDABLE PLYWOOD:
  Plywood CAN be bent if:
  1. The outermost grain direction is ALONG the bend axis
     (bending bends the cross-grain plies, which are weaker)
  2. Fewer, thinner plies are used (each ply bends more)
  3. The bend radius is within material limits

AALTO'S L-LEG (1932):
  Birch plywood; cuts at one end allow 90° bend without
  fracturing the outer faces. The cuts separate the fibers
  so each acts independently in the bend zone.

EAMES DCM (1945):
  The seat shell and back shell each bent in one plane only.
  This is why they are separate pieces — each is cylindrical
  (curves in one axis). A sheet cannot bend in two axes
  simultaneously without fracturing along grain.

BENTWOOD (Thonet method, 1856):
  SOLID wood, not plywood. Steam-heated for 30–90 minutes.
  Cell walls plasticize; wood bent in mold; cooled.
  Holds shape permanently after drying.
  Can only be bent in one plane (same limit as ply, different reason).
  Radius: minimum ~6× the wood thickness.
```

### CNC Routing of Plywood

```
CNC ROUTING CAPABILITY:
  Any 2D profile cut from a sheet
  Nesting software minimizes waste
  Tabs hold parts in sheet during cutting; broken out after
  Edge-banding applied to exposed edges (heat-activated tape)
  Flat-pack furniture depends entirely on CNC precision

  ACCURACY: ±0.1mm typical for furniture-grade CNC
  IMPLICATION: joinery that requires ±2mm by hand requires
               ±0.1mm by CNC; parts that would never fit by hand
               now fit precisely.
  The entire IKEA model (cam-lock joinery, precise panel sizing)
  depends on this accuracy.
```

## MDF and Particleboard

MDF (Medium Density Fiberboard) and particleboard (chipboard) are manufactured wood products that trade the properties of real wood for dimensional stability and consistent processing characteristics.

```
COMPOSITION:
  Particleboard:  wood chips + sawmill waste + urea-formaldehyde (UF) resin
                  pressed under heat + pressure
  MDF:            wood fibers (finer than particleboard) + UF resin
                  pressed under heat + pressure (denser than particleboard)

PROPERTIES:
                  Particleboard    MDF              Solid wood (approx)
Density (kg/m³)   600–750          680–800          450–900 (species-dep.)
Stability         Excellent        Excellent         Poor (movement)
Edge quality      Poor (crumbles)  Good (machinable) N/A
Screw holding     Poor             Fair              Good
Paintable surface Fair             Excellent         Fair (grain telegraphs)
Weight            Heavy            Very heavy        Variable
Cost              Low              Low–medium        Medium–high
Moisture resist.  Poor (swells)    Poor (swells)     Better (species-dep.)

WHERE USED:
  Particleboard: IKEA cabinet carcasses, flat-pack backs and shelves
  MDF: painted cabinet doors, router-profiled moldings,
       painted furniture that requires dead-flat surface
  Plywood: anywhere load-bearing; anywhere screw-holding matters;
           anywhere durability matters
```

**The substrate problem**: particleboard cannot bear loads as a span without significant deflection. A 900mm particleboard shelf with 20kg of books will sag noticeably over time. Plywood or solid wood shelves of the same thickness will not. IKEA's KALLAX shelf unit uses thick panels and box section to compensate for this limitation.

## Tubular Steel

### Manufacturing

```
STEEL TUBE PRODUCTION:
  Seamless tube: billet of solid steel drilled/extruded through mandrel.
    No weld; uniform wall thickness; high precision.
    More expensive; stronger.
    Used for: high-stress applications, chrome plating (no weld seam)

  Welded tube: flat strip of steel rolled into tube, seam welded.
    Weld seam visible (slightly) — important for chrome plating.
    Cheaper; adequate for most furniture.

BENDING:
  Cold bending (roll bending or mandrel bending):
    Tube passes through rollers or is bent over a mandrel.
    Inner radius must be at least 1.5–2× tube OD to avoid kinking.
    Used for: most furniture tube bending

  Heat bending (less common in furniture):
    For tighter radii; tube filled with sand to prevent collapse.

JOINING:
  Welding: MIG, TIG, or resistance welding.
    TIG gives cleanest weld (precise heat input, no spatter).
    Furniture tube joints typically TIG-welded.
  Hardware joints: bolts through tube, nut captured inside.
  Press-fit ferrules and feet.
```

### Chrome Plating

```
CHROME PLATING PROCESS:
  1. Polish steel to mirror finish
  2. Acid clean (removes oxide)
  3. Copper plate (adhesion layer)
  4. Nickel plate (corrosion barrier)
  5. Chrome plate (hard, decorative surface)

  Total plating thickness: ~0.025mm typically
  Trivalent chrome now standard (less toxic than hexavalent)

  POLISH GRADES:
  Satin: 240-grit brushed — understated
  Mirror: polished to <0.1mm Ra — very expensive, shows every scratch
  Bauhaus/Mies pieces typically mirror chrome — part of the luxury argument

POWDER COATING (alternative to chrome):
  Electrostatic polymer powder applied, oven-cured.
  Range of colors, textures.
  Cheaper than chrome; less durable than chrome at contact points.
  Good corrosion resistance.
  Standard for commercial/contract furniture (Herman Miller, Knoll).
```

### Cantilever Physics

```
CANTILEVER CHAIR MECHANICS:
  The U-shaped base of a cantilever chair (Breuer B32, etc.)
  acts as a spring in bending.

  LOAD PATH:
  Occupant weight
       |
       v
  Seat shell
       |
       v
  Back legs (the "vertical" part of U)
       |
       v (bending moment here)
  Curved connecting section (the "bottom" of U)
       |
       v
  Front legs (the "vertical" part of U, forward)

  The curved section is loaded in bending when you sit.
  Steel tube: modulus of elasticity ~200 GPa; good fatigue life.
  Typical deflection under 80kg person: 2–5mm at seat
  This is felt as "spring" — the chair yields slightly then returns.

  FAILURE MODE: metal fatigue at the bend radius after many cycles.
  Furniture-grade cantilever chairs rate for ~100,000–250,000 cycles.
  Commercial/contract chairs (office, school) rate for more.
```

## Fiberglass (GRP)

Fiberglass = Glass Reinforced Polymer (or Plastic). Glass fibers embedded in a polyester or epoxy resin matrix.

```
HAND LAYUP PROCESS (Eames shell method, ~1950):
  1. Mold is waxed (release agent)
  2. Gel coat (pigmented resin layer) applied — becomes the surface
  3. Glass mat or woven fabric laid in mold
  4. Resin applied with roller — wets out fiber
  5. More glass fiber layers as needed
  6. Cure at room temperature or low heat
  7. Part removed from mold; trimmed

  MOLD COST: High (several hundred to thousands of dollars)
  PART COST: Moderate; mostly labor
  MINIMUM PRODUCTION RUN to amortize mold: 50–500 units

EAMES SHELL PROPERTIES:
  Dense, slightly translucent in thin areas
  Very slightly flexible (more than polypropylene)
  Surface texture: slightly pebbled/orange-peel from original process
  Color integral throughout (dye in gel coat)
  Repairs possible: gel coat touch-up

UV DEGRADATION:
  Polyester resin yellows under UV.
  Original white shells from 1950s–60s are now cream/yellow.
  This is not a defect in the new sense — it's patina.
  Contemporary reissues use UV-stabilized resin or polypropylene.

POLYPROPYLENE REPLACEMENT (Vitra/Herman Miller, ~2000s):
  Environmentally less problematic (styrene emissions in fiberglass)
  Slightly different feel (more rigid, not translucent)
  Cheaper to produce at volume
  New shells are branded as such; originals clearly fiberglass by touch and translucency
```

## Polyurethane Foam

The replacement for traditional upholstery (springs, sinuous wire, horsehair, cotton batting):

```
TRADITIONAL UPHOLSTERY STACK (pre-1950s):
  Webbing (woven foundation strips)
  → Coil springs (individually hand-tied in 8-way hand-tied)
  → Burlap (keeps stuffing from falling into springs)
  → Horsehair or cotton batting (primary padding)
  → Final cotton batting (smooth surface layer)
  → Fabric or leather cover

  TIME: 4–8+ hours per chair
  SKILL: High (spring tying is complex)
  DURABILITY: Very high if done well (50+ years)
  REPAIRABILITY: High (each layer accessible)

POLYURETHANE FOAM (1950s–present):
  Petroleum-derived polymer, blown with gas to create cells
  Cut to shape by hot wire or CNC knife
  Glued to base (webbing or ply foundation)
  → Fabric or leather cover

  TIME: 30–60 minutes per chair
  SKILL: Low-medium
  DURABILITY: 10–20 years (foam breaks down, especially at edges)
  REPAIRABILITY: Moderate (foam replaceable; fabric requires re-upholstery)

FOAM SPECIFICATIONS:
  Density (kg/m³): heavier = more durable; 30–50kg/m³ for seating
  ILD (Indentation Load Deflection): measured in pounds to compress 1" by 25%
    Soft: 15–25 ILD (cushion-like)
    Medium: 25–35 ILD (standard seating)
    Firm: 35–45 ILD (car seats, firm contract seating)
  High-resilience (HR) foam: better recovery, better durability
  Memory foam (viscoelastic): slow recovery, pressure-distributing; expensive
```

## Die-Cast Aluminum

Used extensively in Eames furniture (670 base, Aluminum Group base):

```
DIE-CASTING PROCESS:
  Molten aluminum injected into steel die at ~400–700 bar
  Solidifies in seconds
  Die opened; part removed; trimmed, deburred
  CAN ACHIEVE: complex 3D forms, integral bosses/threads,
               surface texture from mold surface
  TOLERANCES: ±0.1–0.3mm typical for furniture parts
  MINIMUM RUN: 500–5,000 units to amortize die cost
  ALLOYS: AlSi9Mg, AlSi12, etc. — vary in strength vs. castability tradeoff

  EAMES 670 BASE:
  The five-arm star with integral swivel barrel is one die-cast piece.
  No post-processing welds; minimal assembly.
  The complexity that would require fabrication in steel is "free" in die-casting.
  Design constraint: no undercuts (parts that would prevent mold opening).

POLISHING / FINISHING:
  As-cast: rough, with mold parting line visible
  Tumbled: smoother, still has slight texture
  Polished: bright, mirror-like (most Eames authorized production)
  Anodized: electrochemical oxide layer — colors, wear resistance (not used for Eames)
  Powder coated: for commercial furniture bases (black or white typically)
```

## Injection-Molded Polypropylene

```
INJECTION MOLDING PROCESS:
  Plastic pellets heated to melt
  Injected into steel mold at ~500–1500 bar
  Mold closed; part forms; mold opened
  Cycle time: 15–60 seconds per part

  VERNER PANTON CHAIR (1960/1967):
  First single-material, single-piece cantilevered chair.
  The entire chair — legs, body, seat, back — is one injection-molded
  polypropylene piece. No joints; no fasteners; no separate parts.

  Why it took until 1967: the tooling cost and material science
  for a cantilevered PP chair required significant development.
  The cantilever in plastic depends on the stiffness of PP at
  exactly the right wall thickness — too thin = breaks; too thick = heavy.

KARTELL (Italian, 1950s–present):
  Specialized in plastic furniture from the beginning.
  Philippe Starck Louis Ghost Chair (2002): crystal-clear
  polycarbonate; transparent polycarbonate chair that references
  Louis XVI style in a completely ironic material.
  Available in 6 colors; ~$500 authorized.

POLYPROPYLENE PROPERTIES:
  Density: 900 kg/m³ (lightest structural thermoplastic)
  Flexural modulus: 1.5–2 GPa (stiff but flexible for thin sections)
  Impact strength: high (doesn't shatter like ABS or PC)
  UV resistance: moderate (degrades over years in sunlight without stabilizers)
  Recyclable: yes (#5 recycling code)
  Chemical resistance: excellent
```

## Contemporary CNC Applications

```
CNC ROUTING (flat materials):
  3-axis: X/Y cutting with vertical plunge (most furniture)
  5-axis: can tilt cutter to cut undercuts (rare in furniture; common in aerospace)
  Accuracy: ±0.1mm; repeatability: ±0.05mm
  Nesting software: optimizes part placement on sheet, minimizes waste
  Applications: all flat-pack furniture components, cabinet carcasses,
                parametric custom furniture

CNC BENDING (tube and sheet metal):
  CNC tube bender: pre-programmed bend sequences, multiple bend planes
  CNC press brake: sheet metal folded by CNC-positioned punch/die
  Applications: steel chair frames, cabinet hardware, light fixtures

CNC TURNING (lathework):
  Traditional turning + CNC creates: very precise, very consistent legs and components
  Also: live tooling allows cross-holes drilled during turning cycle

3D PRINTING IN FURNITURE:
  CURRENT STATE: large-format FDM for concept models; small parts
  LIMITATIONS:
    Structural anisotropy: FDM layer adhesion ~30–50% of in-layer strength
    Surface finish: layer lines visible at any standard resolution
    Size: most FDM printers limited to 300–600mm; furniture requires >1m
    Speed: a chair back would take 8–24 hours on a standard machine
    Cost at furniture scale: competitive with hand-made prototyping only
  FEASIBLE NOW: hardware components, handles, decorative elements
  5–10 years: large-format SLA or pellet extrusion may make structural printed furniture viable
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Material | Best For | Avoid For | Cost Index |
|----------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Solid wood | High-end furniture, longevity, repairs | Wide panels without movement allowance | High |
| Plywood | Load-bearing panels, drawer bottoms, backs | Fine visible surfaces (unless veneered) | Medium |
| MDF | Painted surfaces, CNC-profiled ornament | Load-bearing spans, moisture exposure | Low |
| Particleboard | Cabinet carcasses in dry interiors | Outdoor, high-load, high-quality applications | Lowest |
| Tubular steel | Frames, cantilevers, structural members | Where weight matters; uncoated in humid conditions | Medium |
| Fiberglass | One-piece organic shells, boat-like forms | High volume (too labor-intensive) | Medium-high |
| Polypropylene (injection) | High-volume simple chairs, outdoor | Complex 3D forms without expensive tooling | Low at volume |
| Polyurethane foam | Modern upholstery | Where long-term durability or repair matters | Low |
| Die-cast aluminum | Complex base castings, structural connection | Very low volume (die too expensive) | Medium at volume |

## Common Confusion Points

**Plywood does not warp**: this is approximately true for normal interior use. But plywood exposed to one-sided moisture (e.g., concrete floor moisture on one face) will bow toward the drier side. Always use moisture barrier under plywood in floor applications.

**MDF cannot hold screws in its edge**: the edge of MDF (perpendicular to the panel face) has very low screw-holding capacity. Use threaded inserts, dowel reinforcement, or redesign the joint. Face-screwing into MDF works acceptably.

**"Fiberglass" and "GRP" are the same thing**: Glass Reinforced Polymer/Plastic = fiberglass. The Eames shells are GRP; so are boat hulls. The matrix resin varies (polyester, vinylester, epoxy) but the generic term is the same.

**Polyurethane foam degrades from the inside out**: you can't tell a foam cushion is failing by pressing the edges. Sit in it — if the springs underneath are felt, the foam has lost density. Always check foam age (>15 years in a heavily used chair = likely needs replacement) when buying vintage.

**Injection molding tooling cost is inversely proportional to quantity**: a single injection mold can cost $20,000–$200,000 for a chair component. At 100,000 units, that's $0.20–$2.00 per part. At 100 units, it's $200–$2,000 per part. This is why plastic furniture is only economical at high volumes — exactly the same logic as semiconductor fabrication.
