# Labor Movement: Owen, Debs, Gompers, Chavez, Walesa

## The Big Picture

```
  THE LABOR MOVEMENT: 1810–1980
  ================================

  CORE PROBLEM:
  Industrial capitalism creates a power asymmetry:
  - Single employer (or colluding employers) negotiates with
    each individual worker separately
  - Individual worker: can be replaced; no leverage
  - Workers collectively: can shut down production
  → The labor movement is the mechanism for collective
    bargaining power

  THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ UTOPIAN SOCIALISM (Owen, Fourier, Saint-Simon)              │
  │ Goal: replace capitalism with cooperative communities        │
  │ Method: demonstrate the alternative; moral suasion           │
  │ Result: the communes failed; the ideas influenced later work │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ MARXIST SOCIALISM (Marx, Engels, Debs)                       │
  │ Goal: replace capitalism with worker ownership of production │
  │ Method: class struggle; general strike; revolution           │
  │ Result: USSR, China, Cuba (and their problems)              │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ TRADE UNIONISM (Gompers, AFL model)                          │
  │ Goal: better wages, hours, conditions within capitalism      │
  │ Method: collective bargaining; strikes; political pressure   │
  │ Result: US labor law; 8-hour day; weekend; OSHA             │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ INDUSTRIAL UNIONISM (IWW, CIO)                               │
  │ Goal: organize all workers in an industry (not just craft)   │
  │ Method: solidarity strikes; direct action                    │
  │ Result: CIO organized auto, steel, rubber (1930s)           │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY LEGISLATIVE MILESTONES (USA):
  1935  Wagner Act: right to organize; NLRB; collective bargaining
  1938  Fair Labor Standards Act: minimum wage; 40-hour week; overtime
  1947  Taft-Hartley Act: restricted union power (anti-communist provisions)
  1970  OSHA: workplace safety regulation
```

## Robert Owen (1771–1858)

### What He Did

Owen was a cotton mill owner who became the most important utopian socialist of the early 19th century. His experiment at New Lanark, Scotland — a cotton mill he managed from 1800 to 1825 — demonstrated that treating workers decently was compatible with, and he argued superior to, the prevailing factory model.

**New Lanark:**

```
  NEW LANARK: THE CONTROLLED EXPERIMENT
  ========================================

  PREVAILING NORM (1800):
  - 14-hour workdays
  - Child labor from age 5
  - Corporal punishment in factories
  - Degrading housing; alcoholism; poverty

  OWEN'S NEW LANARK:
  - 10-hour workday
  - No child labor under 10 (later raised to 12)
  - Schools for children and adults
  - Company store at cost (no profit gouging)
  - Housing inspected; moral improvement programs
  - No corporal punishment

  RESULTS:
  - Productivity: equal to or better than comparable mills
  - Profit: the mill was commercially successful
  - Education: children literate; capable of skilled work

  THE ARGUMENT THIS MADE:
  "Workers are not machines. Better conditions produce
  better workers. The fear of punishment and degradation
  is not the optimal motivational system."

  This was a direct empirical refutation of the
  subsistence wage theory (workers earn the minimum
  necessary; improving conditions = waste money)
  and of the "original sin" theory of poverty
  (poverty = moral failure; improvement = enabling vice)
```

**What Owen Tried Next (and Failed)**:

After New Lanark, Owen tried to scale his model:
- **New Harmony, Indiana (1825–27)**: A utopian community in the US. Attracted 900 settlers; collapsed within 2 years due to internal conflicts about the commune's rules.
- **Grand National Consolidated Trades Union (1834)**: Attempted to create a single union covering all British workers. Briefly had 500,000 members; collapsed within months when employers organized a counter-campaign and the government prosecuted union leaders (Tolpuddle Martyrs).

Owen's failures illustrate the limits of utopian socialism: demonstration experiments work at small scale with careful management; they don't automatically scale.

**Owen's Lasting Influence**: The cooperative movement (Rochdale Pioneers, 1844; still existing as the Co-op in the UK). The eight-hour workday demand (10 Hours Movement, which Owen supported). The idea that environment, not character, produces poverty and crime — which is the theoretical foundation of progressive social policy from his time to ours.

## Eugene V. Debs (1855–1926)

### What He Did

Debs was the most important American socialist of his era — five-time Socialist Party presidential candidate, founder of the American Railway Union (ARU), and organizer of the Pullman Strike.

**The Pullman Strike (1894)**:

```
  PULLMAN STRIKE: THE DYNAMICS
  ==============================

  George Pullman: owned the factory (sleeper cars) AND
  the town of Pullman, Illinois — company housing, company
  stores, company church.

  1893 depression: Pullman cut wages 25-40%; did NOT cut
  rents. Workers petitioned; company fired 3 leaders.

  May 1894: Workers walked out.
  Debs's ARU: 125,000 members refused to handle Pullman cars.
  This spread to 27 states; effectively shut down 1/3 of
  US railroad network.

  Attorney General Richard Olney (former railroad lawyer):
  obtained federal injunction against the strike.
  President Cleveland sent federal troops.
  Debs arrested for contempt of injunction.

  The injunction as union-busting tool:
  - Courts could issue injunctions against "restraint of trade"
  - Striking = restraint of trade
  - Disobeying injunction = contempt = prison
  - No jury required; no trial required for contempt
  - This was the primary anti-labor weapon 1890s-1930s
  - Norris-LaGuardia Act (1932): limited injunctions in labor disputes
  - Wagner Act (1935): ended the injunction era
```

**Debs in Prison**: Debs served 6 months for contempt. He read Marx in prison; emerged as a committed socialist. He ran for president on the Socialist Party ticket in 1900, 1904, 1908, 1912, and 1920. In 1920, he ran *from prison* — he had been convicted under the Espionage Act for a speech opposing WWI. He received 913,000 votes (3.4%) while incarcerated. Harding pardoned him in 1921.

**The Legacy**: Debs never won an election; he never built a durable institution. His legacy is as a moral force — he articulated the socialist critique of American capitalism for a mass audience, achieved the largest socialist vote in American history, and demonstrated the limits of political tolerance for dissent (jailed for a speech opposing a war).

## Samuel Gompers (1850–1924)

### What He Did

Gompers founded and led the American Federation of Labor (AFL) for nearly 40 years (1886–1924, with one interruption). He built the most durable labor federation in American history by making a specific strategic choice: *pure and simple trade unionism*.

```
  GOMPERS' "PURE AND SIMPLE" UNIONISM
  ======================================

  REJECTED:                      ACCEPTED:
  ----------                     ---------
  Socialist politics              Collective bargaining
  Class war rhetoric              Strike as last resort
  General strikes                 Dues + strike fund
  Industrial unionism             Craft/skill organizing
  (all workers in industry)       (skilled trades only)
  Third party politics            Work within 2-party system
  Utopian cooperatives            Contracts with employers
  "Abolish the wage system"       "More, more, more"

  THE LOGIC:
  Workers have immediate interests (wages, hours, safety).
  Political parties will betray them.
  Socialism will never be implemented through elections.
  The one leverage workers have: withholding labor.
  Build institutional capacity around that leverage.

  RESULTS:
  AFL membership: 1886: 138,000 → 1920: 4M
  Eight-hour day: AFL's primary campaign; won in many trades
  Child labor: AFL lobbied against it
  Working conditions: contract by contract, trade by trade
```

**The AFL's Limits**:

Gompers's model had structural exclusions:
- **Craft-only**: organized skilled workers (printers, cigar makers, carpenters); left unskilled industrial workers unorganized
- **Race**: AFL constitution permitted member unions to exclude Black workers; many did; Gompers was personally hostile to Black inclusion in most unions
- **Women**: Similar exclusions; women organized into separate "ladies' auxiliaries"
- **Immigrants**: Initially more inclusive; later Gompers supported immigration restriction

These exclusions were strategic (keep the organized pool small and skilled = more bargaining power) and reflected the racism and sexism of the era. The CIO (Congress of Industrial Organizations, 1935) was founded specifically to organize the workers the AFL wouldn't.

**AFL-CIO merger (1955)**: The AFL and CIO merged in 1955, creating a federation representing 16 million workers. The merged organization is still the primary US labor federation. Gompers's institutional design — with its pragmatic focus — is what survived.

## Cesar Chavez (1927–1993)

### What He Did

Chavez co-founded the United Farm Workers (UFW) union in 1962 with Dolores Huerta. Agricultural workers had been explicitly excluded from the Wagner Act's protections — no right to organize, no NLRB protection. Chavez organized a sector that had been deliberately left out of the New Deal labor framework.

```
  FARM WORKERS AND THE LEGAL GAP
  ================================

  Wagner Act (1935): protects workers' right to organize
                     EXCEPT agricultural workers
                     EXCEPT domestic workers
  (These exceptions were deliberate — Southern Democrats in
  Congress demanded them as condition of support, because both
  sectors were predominantly Black and Latino)

  By 1960s:
  California agricultural workers:
  - Largely Mexican American and Filipino American
  - No minimum wage protection
  - No right to organize
  - Subject to deportation threats during organizing
  - Housing in labor camps controlled by growers
  - Child labor still practiced
  - Pesticide exposure unregulated

  Chavez's approach:
  - Non-violence (explicitly Gandhian and influenced by
    MLK's SCLC organizing)
  - Community organizing: worker by worker, family by family
  - Fasting as moral witness (25-day fast, 1968)
  - Consumer boycott: grape boycott reaches 17M Americans
    at peak
  - Political coalition: unions, church, civil rights movement
  - Pilgrimage (300-mile march to Sacramento, 1966):
    combined religious pilgrimage imagery with labor march
```

**The Grape and Lettuce Boycotts**:

The grape boycott (1965–70) was the UFW's primary strategic tool. Since farm workers couldn't use NLRB processes (excluded from Wagner Act), they used consumer leverage instead. At peak in 1970, 17 million Americans were participating in the boycott. California growers signed the first UFW contracts in 1970.

```
  THE BOYCOTT MECHANISM:
  =======================

  Agricultural workers → strike → growers can hire
                                   replacement workers
                                   (no NLRA protection)
  ↓
  Cannot win through strike alone.
  ↓
  Consumer boycott → supermarkets feel pressure →
  growers can no longer move product →
  growers negotiate

  This is using supply chain leverage, not workplace leverage.
  Equivalent to a distributed denial-of-service attack
  on the distribution network rather than the production network.
```

**The California Agricultural Labor Relations Act (1975)**: Chavez lobbied Governor Jerry Brown; the CALRA passed, giving California farm workers the right to organize and collective bargaining under state law. First such protection in US history for agricultural workers.

**Later complications**: In his last decade, Chavez became increasingly autocratic within the UFW, purging organizers, cutting union membership (100,000 in 1973 → 5,000 in 1993), focusing on direct mail fundraising over worker organizing. The movement outlasted Chavez but his later management practices damaged the organization he built.

## Lech Walesa and Solidarity

(See also 09-CONTEMPORARY.md in political-reformers for the full political context)

### The Labor Movement Dimension

Solidarity is the largest and most politically consequential labor movement of the 20th century. Its significance in the labor movement context:

```
  SOLIDARITY AS LABOR INNOVATION
  ================================

  STANDARD UNION MODEL:
  Union represents workers in a specific workplace or industry
  in collective bargaining with employers
  Political activity: lobbying, campaign contributions

  SOLIDARITY'S INNOVATION:
  The union was the civil society — the only independent
  organization in a state where ALL organizations were
  controlled by the Party.

  Solidarity therefore simultaneously was:
  - Trade union (wages, hours, conditions)
  - Political opposition movement (competing with the party)
  - Cultural movement (banned literature, underground press)
  - Catholic identity movement (churches as protected space)
  - National independence movement (Polish vs. Soviet)

  10 million members = 55% of the Polish workforce
  This is the highest union density in any modern country
  for any union. It was possible because the union was
  not just a labor organization — it was the vessel for
  all organized civil society.
```

**Why Solidarity Won**: It operated in a system that had no mechanism for absorbing organized worker opposition without conceding the fundamental claim (that the party didn't represent the workers). The communist claim to represent the working class was fatally undermined by workers organizing independently *against* the communist government. The ideological contradiction was irreparable.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Unions are primarily about wages."**
Historically, labor organizing was about survival: 12–14-hour days, child labor, no safety protection, company towns where workers owed their wages back to the company store. The 40-hour week, child labor laws, workplace safety requirements (OSHA), and employer contributions to health and pension — these came from labor organizing. The "wages" framing misses the structural transformation.

**"Owen's cooperatives failed, therefore cooperative economics doesn't work."**
New Harmony failed (and Owen's other projects). The Rochdale Pioneers' cooperative (1844) succeeded and became the model for the global cooperative movement. The Mondragon cooperatives in the Basque Country have operated profitably for 70 years. Owen's specific experiments failed; the cooperative model did not.

**"Gompers excluded Black workers because he was personally racist."**
He was personally hostile to Black inclusion, yes. The AFL's structural exclusions reflected the era's racism, and Gompers used craft exclusivity as a rationalization. But the full story: some AFL member unions did include Black workers; the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters (A. Philip Randolph) was an AFL affiliate that organized Black workers. The exclusions were structural and ideological, not monolithic.

**"Chavez was only about farm workers."**
He was a major figure in the broader civil rights movement, allied with the Black civil rights movement (SNCC, SCLC), and addressed immigration, pesticide regulation, and environmental justice. The UFW was a Mexican American civil rights organization as much as a labor union.
