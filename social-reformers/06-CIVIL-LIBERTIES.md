# Civil Liberties: ACLU, Frankfurter, Thurgood Marshall, Ruth Bader Ginsburg

## The Big Picture

```
  AMERICAN CIVIL LIBERTIES: THE LEGAL ARCHITECTURE
  ==================================================

  CONSTITUTIONAL FOUNDATION:
  1st Amendment:  speech, press, religion, assembly, petition
  4th Amendment:  unreasonable searches and seizures
  5th Amendment:  due process; self-incrimination
  6th Amendment:  speedy trial; counsel; jury
  8th Amendment:  cruel and unusual punishment
  14th Amendment: equal protection; due process (applied to states)

  But rights written in a constitution are not self-enforcing.
  They require:
  (a) Courts willing to apply them
  (b) Attorneys willing to litigate them
  (c) Organizations with the resources and strategy
      to bring cases systematically

  TIMELINE OF CIVIL LIBERTIES EXPANSION:
  1919  ACLU founded (from National Civil Liberties Bureau)
  1925  Gitlow v. New York: 1st Amendment incorporated to states
        (14th Amendment applies Bill of Rights to states)
  1931  Near v. Minnesota: prior restraint unconstitutional
  1937  Court-packing failure → "switch in time"; expansion begins
  1954  Brown v. Board: Thurgood Marshall; equal protection
  1961  Mapp v. Ohio: 4th Amendment exclusionary rule → states
  1963  Gideon v. Wainwright: right to counsel → states
  1965  Griswold v. Connecticut: privacy (contraception)
  1966  Miranda v. Arizona: 5th Amendment warnings
  1971  Pentagon Papers: prior restraint unconstitutional
  1972  Furman v. Georgia: death penalty (temporary halt)
  1973  Roe v. Wade: abortion rights (1st trimester)
  1996  Romer v. Evans: Ginsburg; gay rights
  2003  Lawrence v. Texas: sodomy laws unconstitutional
  2013  Shelby County v. Holder: VRA Section 4 gutted
  2015  Obergefell v. Hodges: same-sex marriage
  2022  Dobbs v. Jackson: Roe overturned
```

## The ACLU — Founders and History

### Origin (1920)

The American Civil Liberties Union was founded in 1920 by Roger Baldwin, Crystal Eastman, and others, growing directly out of the National Civil Liberties Bureau (1917) — formed to defend conscientious objectors during WWI. The context: the Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918) criminalized criticism of the war; Eugene Debs was jailed for a speech; hundreds of socialists, anarchists, and labor organizers were prosecuted.

```
  ACLU FOUNDING CONTEXT
  ======================

  1919-20: The "Red Scare"
  Palmer Raids (A. Mitchell Palmer, Attorney General):
  - November 1919: ~250 arrested
  - January 1920: ~3,000 arrested in 23 cities
  - Deportations without trial
  - Emma Goldman, Alexander Berkman deported

  Felix Frankfurter (then Harvard Law professor):
  wrote "The Case for the Deportees" (1920) for
  *New Republic* — documented due process violations
  Frankfurter + Zechariah Chafee: The Report Upon the
  Illegal Practices of the United States Department of
  Justice — 67 lawyers signed; documented Palmer's abuses

  ACLU Mission: Defend civil liberties regardless of
  the political views of the party being suppressed.
  This principle produces the most controversial ACLU cases:
  - Defending Klan members' right to march (1978, Skokie)
  - Defending far-right speech
  - Defending Communists during McCarthyism
  → Principle: if only popular speech is protected, the
    protection is meaningless.
```

**ACLU's Inconsistency**: The 1940 "resolution" excluding Communists from ACLU leadership (passed during McCarthyism's early phase) — explicitly anti-Communist. The ACLU abandoned its stated principle under political pressure and expelled Elizabeth Gurley Flynn from its board because she was a Communist. This was reversed by formal resolution in 1968. It illustrates that organizations, like individuals, can act contrary to their stated principles under pressure.

## Felix Frankfurter (1882–1965)

### Who He Was

Frankfurter was born in Vienna, immigrated to the US at 12, graduated Harvard Law first in his class, served in multiple government roles, co-founded the ACLU (1920), wrote for *The New Republic*, and was appointed to the Supreme Court by FDR in 1939.

**The Sacco-Vanzetti Case (1927)**:

```
  SACCO AND VANZETTI (1920-27)
  ==============================

  Context: 1920 Red Scare; Italian immigrant anarchists
  Charge: Murder during a 1920 payroll robbery in
          South Braintree, Massachusetts
  Evidence: Disputed; conflicting eyewitness testimony;
            ballistics evidence later contested
  Defense: No alibi that satisfied the jury; radical
           politics prejudiced the proceedings

  Frankfurter in 1927: Published a case brief in
  *Atlantic Monthly* arguing the trial was fatally flawed.
  Response: Death threats; bomb sent to his home.
  He maintained his position.

  RESULT: Both executed August 23, 1927.
  Posthumous: Governor Michael Dukakis declared a
  memorial day in 1977 declaring the trial was
  "permeated with unfairness." Massachusetts officially
  exonerated them in 1977.

  Frankfurter's contribution: demonstrated that judicial
  review of criminal proceedings was legitimate public
  discourse — not just a function of defense attorneys.
```

**On the Supreme Court (1939–1962)**:

Frankfurter is one of the most complex Supreme Court justices. He was:
- A liberal when appointed (FDR wanted a progressive)
- Evolved into a defender of *judicial restraint*: courts should defer to democratic processes rather than constitutionalizing policy preferences

His famous dissent in *West Virginia State Board of Education v. Barnette* (1943):
- Majority: compulsory flag salute for Jehovah's Witnesses students is unconstitutional (First Amendment)
- Frankfurter dissent: the legislature made this decision; courts should not substitute their judgment for democratic processes
- His position was intellectually consistent with his philosophy; his personal history (Jewish immigrant who had experienced persecution) made it personally painful

**Frankfurter's Judicial Philosophy**:

```
  FRANKFURTER: JUDICIAL RESTRAINT vs. ACTIVISM
  ==============================================

  FRANKFURTER'S POSITION:
  Courts are not a super-legislature.
  When courts strike down democratic choices, they
  impose their own preferences under constitutional guise.
  This is anti-democratic.
  Courts should defer except for clear constitutional
  violations.

  THE PROBLEM:
  If courts defer to democratic majorities, minorities
  have no protection from majority oppression.
  The 14th Amendment exists specifically to protect
  minorities from state democratic processes.

  WARREN COURT RESPONSE (Brennan, Warren, Douglas):
  The Constitution's rights provisions are the
  democratic MANDATE to protect minorities.
  Enforcing them is democratic, not anti-democratic.

  THE ONGOING TENSION:
  This debate (judicial restraint vs. judicial activism)
  runs through every major constitutional controversy.
  Originalism (Scalia, Thomas) is a version of Frankfurter's
  position. Living constitutionalism (Brennan, Marshall) is
  the Warren Court position. Neither has won decisively.
```

## Thurgood Marshall (1908–1993)

### The NAACP Legal Defense Fund Strategy

Marshall served as the NAACP's chief counsel and then as director-counsel of the NAACP Legal Defense Fund (LDF) from 1940 to 1961. In those 21 years, he argued 32 cases before the Supreme Court and won 29 of them. The strategy that produced *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) was designed over 17 years.

**The Dismantling of Plessy v. Ferguson**:

```
  PLESSY v. FERGUSON (1896): "separate but equal"
  - States may segregate races as long as facilities are equal
  - This was the constitutional foundation of Jim Crow

  THE NAACP STRATEGY (1930s-1954):
  Charles Houston (Marshall's mentor) designed it;
  Marshall executed it.

  APPROACH: Don't attack Plessy directly (not yet).
  Attack the "equal" part of "separate but equal."
  Make states demonstrate their separate facilities
  are actually equal. They can't.

  STEP 1: GRADUATE EDUCATION (1936-50)
  Murray v. Pearson (1935): Maryland must admit Black
    students to its law school or build an equal one
    → Maryland admitted Murray
  Gaines v. Canada (1938): Missouri must provide equal
    legal education in-state
  Sweatt v. Painter (1950): Texas "Black law school"
    is NOT equal (no library, no faculty diversity,
    no alumni network, no reputation)
  McLaurin v. Oklahoma (1950): Black doctoral student
    admitted but forced to use separate desk, sit outside
    classroom, use separate library section → demeaning
    conditions prevent genuine equal education

  STEP 2: ATTACK THE DOCTRINE DIRECTLY (1950-54)
  After winning Sweatt and McLaurin: the Court agreed
  that intangible factors (reputation, network, interaction)
  are part of "equality" in education.
  → Plessy's "separate" cannot be "equal" by definition.
  → Time to argue Brown directly.

  BROWN v. BOARD OF EDUCATION (May 17, 1954):
  Marshall's argument:
  1. The doll test (Kenneth Clark): Black children chose
     white dolls as "nicer" — demonstrated psychological
     harm of segregation
  2. Sociological evidence: segregation produces inferiority
     stigma regardless of physical equality of facilities
  3. Legal argument: 14th Amendment equal protection
     requires equal access to educational opportunity
  RESULT: Unanimous. "Separate educational facilities
  are inherently unequal."
```

**Marshall's Later Career**:

Marshall became the first Black U.S. Solicitor General (1965) and the first Black Supreme Court Justice (1967, appointed by LBJ). On the Court until 1991, he became the consistent voice for the rights of the poor, the criminal defendant, and the minority — dissenting in case after case as the Burger and Rehnquist Courts narrowed the Warren Court's civil rights expansion.

**Marshall vs. Frankfurter**:

They represent the exact same judicial philosophy dispute:
- Frankfurter: judicial restraint; courts defer to democratic processes
- Marshall: the Constitution's rights provisions protect those whom democratic majorities would oppress; enforcing them IS the democratic mandate

Marshall's response to judicial restraint: "You do the best you can with what you've got." He was not constrained by abstract theoretical consistency; he argued from the facts and the harm.

## Ruth Bader Ginsburg (1933–2020)

### The ACLU Women's Rights Project Strategy

Ginsburg developed and executed the legal strategy for women's equality under the 14th Amendment's equal protection clause in the 1970s. Her model was explicitly borrowed from Marshall's NAACP playbook.

```
  THE GINSBURG STRATEGY (ACLU Women's Rights Project, 1972-80)
  ==============================================================

  THE PROBLEM: Courts traditionally upheld sex-based
  classifications under "rational basis" review —
  any conceivable rational reason sufficed.
  Women's protection under the 14th Amendment was minimal.

  GINSBURG'S APPROACH:
  1. Choose cases strategically: facts must be sympathetic;
     plaintiff must be compelling; lower court record must be clean
  2. Build precedent incrementally — don't leap to the conclusion
  3. Use male plaintiffs where possible:
     - Courts more sympathetic to men challenging "protective"
       laws that restrict men's options
     - Shows sex discrimination cuts both ways
     - Harder to dismiss as "special interest"

  THE CASES:
  Reed v. Reed (1971):
  - Idaho: fathers preferred over mothers as estate administrator
  - First time Supreme Court struck down a sex-based classification
  - Unanimous; "arbitrary legislative choices" not allowed

  Frontiero v. Richardson (1973):
  - Military: male servicemembers' wives automatic dependents;
    female servicemembers must prove husband's dependency
  - Unconstitutional; 8-1
  - Ginsburg argued: sex = "a characteristic determined solely
    by the accident of birth"

  Weinberger v. Wiesenfeld (1975):
  - Stephen Wiesenfeld: wife died in childbirth; Social Security
    gave survivor benefits to widows but not widowers raising children
  - Unanimous: unconstitutional
  - (A man challenging a law designed to protect women —
    Ginsburg's strategy of finding male plaintiffs)

  Califano v. Goldfarb (1977): same structure; expanded

  By 1980: the Court recognized "intermediate scrutiny"
  for sex-based classifications — harder standard to meet
  than rational basis; the foundation for all subsequent
  sex discrimination law.
```

**On the Supreme Court (1993–2020)**:

Ginsburg served 27 years on the Court. Her strategic contribution as a justice shifted from litigation to institutional:

- **US v. Virginia (1996)**: VMI cannot exclude women; wrote the majority opinion herself; "exceedingly persuasive justification" = the new standard for sex-based classifications
- **Dissents as public communication**: Her dissents — especially in *Ledbetter v. Goodyear* (pay discrimination) and *Shelby County v. Holder* (VRA) — became public documents that mobilized legislative and political responses
- *Ledbetter* dissent: Congress passed the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act (2009) specifically in response to her dissent's call for legislative action

**The "Notorious RBG" Problem**: Ginsburg's cultural icon status (the Notorious RBG meme, the opera about her, the biography films) reflects genuine admiration for her legal work but also created a political distortion. Her refusal to retire under Obama (2009–2016) when a Democratic president could have appointed her successor — she died September 18, 2020; Trump appointed Amy Coney Barrett — has been criticized sharply. She reportedly did not believe Trump would be elected in 2016. The decision to wait cannot be assessed separately from its consequence: the Court that overturned Roe v. Wade in *Dobbs* (2022) included her replacement.

## Common Confusion Points

**"The ACLU always defends the good guys."**
The ACLU's stated principle is: defend civil liberties regardless of the political views of the party being suppressed. In practice: they have defended Nazis (Skokie), the KKK, and far-right groups. They have also been inconsistent (1940 Communist exclusion). The principle is correct even when application is difficult.

**"Brown v. Board was about moral progress."**
It was also about strategic litigation executed over 17 years: choosing the right cases, in the right order, building toward the doctrine you want to establish. Marshall's plan was a systems engineer's approach to dismantling a legal precedent — identify the weakest supporting structure (the "equal" in "separate but equal"), attack it case by case, build precedents, then attack the core doctrine with accumulated wins. This is technical mastery, not just righteous argument.

**"Ginsburg was a consistent liberal."**
Her decisions do not map neatly onto the liberal/conservative binary. Her VMI decision was a rights-expansion decision; her *Employment Division v. Smith* vote (upholding a neutral law of general applicability against a religious free exercise challenge) sided with Scalia's majority — a "liberal" justice writing a rule that could restrict minority religious exercise. She was a lawyer's lawyer with a coherent doctrinal framework, not a political actor in robes.

**"Judicial activism is bad; judicial restraint is good."**
This is a tribal framing. Judicial activists and judicial restraintists use the same tools; the question is when courts should intervene in democratic processes. The answer depends on what you think democracy is for. If democracy can legitimately suppress minorities, restraint is appropriate. If the Constitution is specifically designed to protect minorities from majority oppression, then enforcement of those protections IS the democratic mandate.
