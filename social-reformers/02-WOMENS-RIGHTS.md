# Women's Rights: Wollstonecraft, Stanton, Anthony, Pankhurst

## The Big Picture

```
  WOMEN'S RIGHTS: THREE CENTURIES, THREE PHASES
  ================================================

  PHASE 1: THEORETICAL FOUNDATION (1792–1848)
  Wollstonecraft: A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792)
  Problem: "Women are inferior because they lack reason"
  Argument: Women lack reason because they are denied education
  → If education = equal, results = equal
  → No innate inferiority; only structural disadvantage

  PHASE 2: ORGANIZING + LEGAL DEMANDS (1848–1920)
  Seneca Falls (1848): Declaration of Sentiments
  "All men AND WOMEN are created equal"
  Demands: voting rights, property rights, educational access,
           professional access, divorce reform

  Stanton + Anthony (USA): theoretical framing → political organizing
  Pankhurst (UK): escalation to militancy when persuasion failed
  → UK women's suffrage (partial 1918; full 1928)
  → US 19th Amendment (1920)

  PHASE 3: SECOND WAVE + GLOBAL (1960s–present)
  Friedan (The Feminine Mystique, 1963)
  de Beauvoir (The Second Sex, 1949)
  Title IX (1972), Roe v. Wade (1973), Equal Credit Opportunity Act
  Global: Beijing Platform for Action (1995)
  Ongoing: gender pay gap, reproductive rights, workplace protection

  TIMELINE:
  1792  Wollstonecraft: Vindication
  1840  World Anti-Slavery Convention: women barred from speaking
        → Stanton + Mott decide to organize

  1890  NAWSA: merger

  1913  Emily Wilding Davison: dies under the King's horse at Epsom

```

## Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–1797)

### What She Argued

*A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792) is the foundational text of feminist political philosophy. Its argument:

```
  WOLLSTONECRAFT'S CORE ARGUMENT
  ================================

  CLAIM: Women are inferior to men.
  This was the standard view in 1792.

  THE STANDARD JUSTIFICATION:
  Women are governed by feeling, not reason.
  Reason is the faculty that makes humans capable
  of morality, citizenship, and intellectual achievement.
  Women lack this faculty; therefore women need guidance,
  not rights.

  WOLLSTONECRAFT'S COUNTER:

  Premise 1: Women appear to lack reason.
  Premise 2: What appears to be natural may be cultivated.
  Observation: Girls are raised to please, not to think.
               They are praised for beauty and compliance,
               not for analysis and argument.
               They are denied systematic education.
  Conclusion: The "inferiority" is the product of
              socialization and denied education,
              NOT innate incapacity.

  IMPLICATION:
  Equal education → equal development of reason →
  women are capable of moral agency and citizenship.

  This is essentially an empirical claim (nature vs. nurture)
  wrapped in Enlightenment political theory (reason as the
  basis of rights). It is still the foundational argument.
```

**Context**: Wollstonecraft wrote the *Vindication* in response to Edmund Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) and more directly to Rousseau's *Emile* — Rousseau had written extensively about education yet explicitly excluded girls from rigorous intellectual education (Sophie, in *Emile*, should be trained to please, not to reason). Wollstonecraft saw this as a fundamental contradiction in Enlightenment liberalism.

**Her Life as Contradiction**: Wollstonecraft had an illegitimate child with Gilbert Imlay (who abandoned her), attempted suicide twice, and died at 38 of puerperal fever after the birth of her second daughter (the future Mary Shelley, author of *Frankenstein*). Her husband William Godwin published a memoir that revealed all of this. For decades, the memoir was used to discredit her work — her "immoral" life supposedly invalidating her arguments about women's rational capacity. This is the ad hominem fallacy deployed systematically.

## Elizabeth Cady Stanton (1815–1902) and Susan B. Anthony (1820–1906)

### Seneca Falls and the Declaration of Sentiments (1848)

The 1840 World Anti-Slavery Convention in London barred women from the floor — they had to sit in the gallery behind a curtain. Elizabeth Cady Stanton (newly married to abolitionist Henry Stanton) and Lucretia Mott (Quaker abolitionist) decided on the spot to hold a women's rights convention. Eight years later, they organized one.

Seneca Falls (July 19–20, 1848): About 300 people attended. The Declaration of Sentiments was drafted by Stanton, modeled structurally on the Declaration of Independence:

```
  DECLARATION OF SENTIMENTS (1848) — KEY DEMANDS
  =================================================

  "We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men
   and women are created equal..."

  Grievances listed (parallel to Jefferson's list against the King):
  - Denied the franchise
  - Denied right to education and professional access
  - Required to submit to laws without voice in making them
  - If married: legally "civilly dead" (husband controls
    property, wages, person)
  - Divorce: courts favor husbands
  - Divorce: mother can lose children
  - Double standard of morality

  Resolutions adopted unanimously EXCEPT:
  The suffrage resolution — passed only after Frederick
  Douglass (the only notable man present) spoke in support.

  100 signatures collected (including Douglass, 32 men, 68 women).
  Of the 100 signatories, 68 were subsequently pressured to
  remove their names.
```

### The Stanton-Anthony Partnership

Stanton was the intellectual — she did the writing, the theoretical framing, the speeches. Anthony was the organizer — she ran the logistics, managed the campaigns, did the fundraising. Their partnership for 50 years was explicitly divided on these lines.

**Anthony's arrest (1872)**: Anthony voted in the Rochester, NY federal election and was arrested for "knowingly, wrongfully and unlawfully vot[ing]." She was convicted (the judge directed the verdict; wouldn't let the jury decide). Her defense: the 14th Amendment's "all persons" language included women; the judge rejected this reading. She refused to pay the fine; the government never collected it.

**The Split (1869)**:

```
  THE STANTON-ANTHONY vs. STONE SPLIT
  ======================================

  ISSUE: 15th Amendment gives voting rights to Black men
         but not women.

  Should women support the 15th Amendment?

  STANTON + ANTHONY (NWSA):
  NO. The 15th Amendment is a betrayal.
  Demand universal suffrage or nothing.
  Both use racist language in arguments
  (arguing that "educated white women" should vote
   before "ignorant Black men") — real and documented.

  LUCY STONE + HENRY WARD BEECHER (AWSA):
  Yes. Support the 15th Amendment.
  Black men's suffrage and women's suffrage
  are compatible struggles; advance both.

  THE HONEST ACCOUNT:
  Stanton and Anthony's position on the 15th Amendment
  was racist. They used explicitly racist arguments.
  This does not erase their contributions to suffrage
  but is part of the historical record.
  The women's suffrage movement and the civil rights
  movement were connected and in tension throughout.
  The 19th Amendment itself was partially won by
  appealing to Southern white racism (Southern states
  could suppress Black women's votes as they suppressed
  Black men's votes; so they could get "white woman
  suffrage" without giving Black women the vote).
```

**Stanton's later radicalism**: In her 70s and 80s, Stanton moved to increasingly radical positions — *The Woman's Bible* (1895) attacked organized religion's role in women's oppression; the NAWSA disavowed it. She died in 1902, 18 years before the 19th Amendment passed.

## Emmeline Pankhurst (1858–1928) and the WSPU

### The British Context

British women had been organizing for suffrage since the 1860s. By 1903, after decades of petitions, marches, and parliamentary lobbying, they had nothing. Emmeline Pankhurst concluded that the constitutional approach had failed. She founded the Women's Social and Political Union (WSPU) with her daughters Christabel and Sylvia.

**The escalation:**

```
  WSPU TACTICAL ESCALATION (1906–1914)
  =======================================

  1906  Disrupting political meetings; heckling; arrests
  1907  First major march: 3,000 women to Parliament
  1908  Mass demonstrations: 250,000 in Hyde Park
        Window-smashing begins (Downing Street)
  1909  First hunger strikes by imprisoned suffragettes
        Government: force-feeding
  1910  "Black Friday": 300 women march to Parliament;
        police violence against marchers; 2 deaths
        WSPU: truce
  1912  Truce ends: coordinated window-smashing across
        London's West End; £5,000 damage in one day
  1913  Arson campaign: post boxes, golf courses, timber yards
        Emily Wilding Davison: throws herself in front of
        King's horse at Epsom Derby; dies four days later
        Cat and Mouse Act: suffragettes released when near
        death; rearrested when recovered
  1913  Pankhurst imprisoned and released 11 times in one year
  1914  WWI: WSPU suspends campaign; Pankhurst supports war

  WWII: Women doing war work → political case for suffrage
        becomes unanswerable
  1918: Women 30+ with property vote
  1928: Equal Franchise Act: women on same terms as men
```

**The Debate on Militancy**:

```
  DID MILITANCY HELP OR HURT?
  ============================

  HELPED:
  - Kept issue in press (every window broken = front page)
  - Demonstrated that women would not be silenced
  - The "problem" of suffragettes couldn't be ignored
  - Forced politicians to take a position

  HURT:
  - Gave opponents a respectable reason to oppose
  - Some supporters withdrew over property destruction
  - Violence split the movement (Sylvia Pankhurst disagreed
    with escalation; eventually broke with Emmeline)
  - Postponed the vote? Or accelerated it?

  The historical counterfactual is unresolvable.
  The vote happened in 1918; WWI was the proximate cause
  (women doing war work); the WSPU campaign had kept the
  issue alive and raised costs of continued inaction.
```

**Pankhurst's later positions**: Pankhurst became increasingly conservative — supported WWI; opposed the Russian Revolution; ran (successfully) as a Conservative parliamentary candidate in 1928; died before taking her seat. Her politics after suffrage were the opposite of her daughters' (Sylvia became a committed socialist and Pan-Africanist).

## Common Confusion Points

**"Women were granted the vote."**
Women won the vote through 50–75 years of sustained organizing, marching, petitioning, agitating, being imprisoned, hunger-striking, and (in the WSPU case) property destruction. Passive construction erases the agents.

**"The suffragettes won through militancy."**
The WSPU suspended its campaign during WWI (1914–18). The immediate proximate cause of the 1918 partial reform was women's war work making the exclusion politically untenable, not the pre-war militant campaign. The campaign set the agenda; the war created the political opening.

**"Wollstonecraft founded feminism."**
Earlier women wrote on the topic: Christine de Pizan (15th century), Margaret Cavendish (17th century). Wollstonecraft synthesized the arguments within Enlightenment political theory in a form that made them available to subsequent political organizing. She systematized the argument, not invented it.

**"Stanton and Anthony were racist."**
Accurately: they used racist arguments in the specific context of the 15th Amendment debate (1869). This is historically documented, not exaggerated. It reflects the reality that the women's suffrage movement was predominantly white, often excluded Black women from organizing, and made tactical accommodations to Southern white racism in the campaign for the 19th Amendment. Ida B. Wells (civil rights activist) was explicitly marginalized in suffrage organizing. The movement's racial history is complicated and must not be elided.
