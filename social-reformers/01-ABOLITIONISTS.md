# Abolitionists: Wilberforce, Clarkson, Equiano, Tubman, Douglass, Garrison

## The Big Picture

```
  THE ABOLITION CAMPAIGNS: TWO PATHS, DIFFERENT TIMELINES
  =========================================================

  BRITISH ABOLITION (1787–1833):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1787: Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade│
  │         Clarkson (research+organizing) + Wilberforce (Parliament)
  │  1792: House of Commons passes gradual abolition by 230–85   │
  │         → House of Lords kills it                            │
  │  1807: Slave Trade Act — trade abolished (not ownership)     │
  │  1823: Anti-Slavery Society founded                          │
  │  1833: Slavery Abolition Act — ownership abolished           │
  │         Slaveowners compensated £20M; slaves NOT compensated │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  Duration: 46 years from Society founding to abolition
  Method: Parliamentary lobbying + mass petition + economic argument
  Key figures: Clarkson (organizer), Wilberforce (Parliament),
               Equiano (testimony), Quakers (funding + conscience)

  AMERICAN ABOLITION (1831–1865):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1831: Garrison founds The Liberator (newspaper)             │
  │         "I will not equivocate, and I will not excuse"       │
  │  1833: American Anti-Slavery Society founded                 │
  │  1845: Douglass publishes Narrative                          │
  │  1848: Seneca Falls; women's rights links to abolition       │

  │         → radicalizes Northern opinion                       │

  │  1863: Emancipation Proclamation (military/diplomatic)       │
  │  1865: 13th Amendment — slavery abolished constitutionally   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  Duration: 34 years from Liberator to 13th Amendment
  Method: Press + moral suasion + Underground Railroad +
          political organization + civil war
  Key figures: Garrison (publisher), Douglass (testimony+politics),
               Tubman (direct action), Lincoln (coalition + amendment)
```

## Thomas Clarkson (1760–1846)

### What He Did

Clarkson is the organizer that Wilberforce needed. His contribution: the *evidence base* for abolition.

**The Research**: In 1786, Clarkson's Cambridge essay prize-winning essay on slavery sparked his conversion. He then spent years:
- Collecting physical evidence: shackles, thumbscrews, branding irons, the "Middle Passage" mortality records
- Interviewing sailors in Bristol and Liverpool who had worked the slave ships
- Documenting mortality rates (sailors died at higher rates than slaves on the Middle Passage due to disease and violence from captains)
- Building a network of 500 correspondents across Britain

**The "chest"**: Clarkson kept a physical display case of instruments of the slave trade. He showed them to meetings, to Parliament, to anyone who would look. Visual evidence of the instruments of torture, opened in drawing rooms across England.

**The coalition building**: Clarkson organized the network of local abolitionist societies across Britain — by 1792, 519 petition campaigns had gathered 390,000 signatures. He rode 35,000 miles on horseback in two years organizing.

```
  CLARKSON'S INNOVATION: PETITION AS POLITICAL INSTRUMENT
  =========================================================

  Pre-1788: Petitions were rare and individually presented
  1788: Clarkson organizes simultaneous mass petitions
  1792: 519 petitions; 390,000 signatures
  → Parliament could not ignore this as "special interest"
  → This is the first modern mass petition campaign

  The mechanism: local societies + coordinated timing +
  pre-printed forms → political pressure at scale

  Direct descendant: every modern advocacy petition campaign
  (from ACLU mailings to online petitions) runs through
  Clarkson's organizational innovation
```

## William Wilberforce (1759–1833)

### What He Did

Wilberforce provided the parliamentary voice that Clarkson's organizing needed. He introduced abolition bills in Parliament every year from 1789 to 1807 — losing repeatedly — and finally succeeded.

**The relationship**: Clarkson found the evidence; Wilberforce made the speeches. Clarkson said he could not have done it without Wilberforce; Wilberforce's biographers have sometimes obscured Clarkson's contribution. The coalition was:
- Wilberforce (parliamentary access, social connections, evangelical Christianity)
- Clarkson (research, organizing, travel)
- Quakers (funding, initial organizing, moral commitment)
- Equiano and other formerly enslaved people (testimony)
- Josiah Wedgwood (the "Am I not a Man and a Brother?" medallion — mass produced propaganda)

**The Clapham Sect**: Wilberforce was part of a group of evangelical Anglican reformers (the "Clapham Sect") who combined personal piety with political action on multiple fronts — abolition, prison reform, Sunday schools, and foreign mission work. This was not incidental: their theological conviction that slavery was sin gave them staying power for a 20-year campaign.

**The 1807 Slave Trade Act**: Prohibited the slave *trade* — the transport of enslaved people from Africa to the colonies. It did not free anyone already enslaved. Enforcement was the next problem: the Royal Navy's West Africa Squadron was tasked with intercepting slave ships; by 1850, it had liberated 150,000 enslaved people. The total traffic by then had also increased, as traders raced to supply before enforcement tightened.

**The 1833 Slavery Abolition Act**: Passed three days after Wilberforce's death. He knew it was going to pass. It freed ~800,000 enslaved people in British colonies. The British government compensated slaveowners £20 million (40% of annual government revenue). The enslaved people received no compensation. This fact — paid in full to the perpetrators, nothing to the victims — is routinely omitted in celebratory accounts.

## Olaudah Equiano (c. 1745–1797)

### What He Did

Equiano was born in what is now Nigeria, enslaved as a child, transported to Barbados and then Virginia, eventually purchased his own freedom in 1766. He settled in England, joined the abolitionist movement, and published *The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano* (1789).

**The Narrative's function**:

```
  THE SLAVE NARRATIVE AS POLITICAL INSTRUMENT
  =============================================

  Pre-Equiano: abolitionists argued on moral/religious grounds
               ("slavery is wrong")
               against economic arguments
               ("slavery is necessary")

  Equiano's Narrative:
  - First-person account of specific horrors
  - Documented the Middle Passage with precision
  - Demonstrated the humanity of the enslaved person
    → The argument "Africans don't have inner lives"
      meets a sophisticated memoir that demonstrates otherwise
  - Made 9 editions in his lifetime (enormous for 1789)
  - Equiano himself sold it, touring Britain and Ireland

  The mechanism: changing what audiences imagined
  when they heard "slavery"
  from abstract economic category
  to specific human experience
```

**The scholarship dispute**: Some historians have questioned whether Equiano was born in Africa or in South Carolina (based on a baptism record). The question matters for the Middle Passage sections — were they autobiographical or synthesized from other sources? Most historians accept his African birth; the dispute remains live.

## William Lloyd Garrison (1805–1879)

### What He Did

Garrison founded *The Liberator* newspaper (1831–65) — 34 years of weekly publication. His position was radical and deliberately provocative: the US Constitution was a "covenant with death" (it protected slavery). He burned copies of it at public meetings. He wanted immediate, unconditional abolition — not gradual, not compensated.

```
  GARRISON'S STRATEGIC CHOICE: MORAL SUASION
  =============================================

  GARRISON'S THEORY:          PRACTICAL EFFECT:
  ==================          ================
  Slavery is sin               Raised the moral stakes
  Only total, immediate         — no "acceptable" middle
  abolition is acceptable       ground
  Political compromise is       Made Garrison unpopular
  itself sinful                 with moderates; isolated
  The Constitution must         the radical fringe;
  be rewritten or rejected      made Lincoln look moderate

  The Garrison / Douglass split (1847):
  Garrison: Constitution = pro-slavery; reject it
  Douglass: Constitution = potentially anti-slavery;
            use it
  → Douglass founded North Star; broke from Garrison's orbit

  In retrospect: Douglass was operationally correct —
  the 13th Amendment used the Constitution Garrison
  wanted to burn.

  But: Garrison's radicalism created the political space
  that made Lincoln "the moderate" — same mechanism as
  Malcolm X / MLK.
```

**The American Anti-Slavery Society (1833)**: Founded by Garrison with Arthur and Lewis Tappan (wealthy New York merchants who funded the movement). Women included from the start — unusual and controversial. The question of whether women should be allowed to speak at abolitionist meetings eventually split the movement.

## Harriet Tubman (c. 1822–1913)

### What She Did

Tubman escaped from slavery in Maryland in 1849, alone, using the North Star as navigation guide. She then made 13 return trips to the South over 10 years, leading approximately 70 enslaved people to freedom on the Underground Railroad — a network of safe houses, sympathetic conductors, and hidden routes from the slaveholding South to the free North (and eventually Canada after the 1850 Fugitive Slave Act).

```
  THE UNDERGROUND RAILROAD: WHAT IT ACTUALLY WAS
  ================================================

  NOT a literal railroad or physical structure
  NOT centrally organized or managed
  NOT primarily organized by white abolitionists

  WAS: a loose network of:
  - Safe houses (homes of free Black Americans, Quakers,
    other sympathetic white abolitionists)
  - Routes (generally north, using geographical features)
  - Conductors (like Tubman — people who knew the routes)
  - Communication (church networks, coded signals)
  - Funding (Anti-Slavery Society, individual donors)

  Tubman's specific contribution:
  - Conducted 13 missions, ~70 people
  - Never lost a passenger ("I never ran my train off
    the track and I never lost a passenger")
  - Used natural winter periods (longer nights; harder
    to track; fugitives could travel further)
  - Rewarded information: $40,000 bounty on her head
    by the late 1850s (~$1M in 2024 dollars)
  - Survived: through absolute operational security,
    local knowledge, network, and personal courage
```

**Civil War role**: During the Civil War, Tubman served the Union Army as a spy and scout, using her knowledge of the Maryland and South Carolina terrain. She led the Combahee River Raid (June 1863) that liberated 700+ enslaved people in South Carolina — the first military operation in American history planned and led by a woman.

**Post-war**: Tubman spent 30 years trying to get the Union Army to pay her wages for her service. Congress finally passed a bill in 1899 (granting her widow's pension); she died in 1913 with no direct compensation for her espionage work.

## Frederick Douglass (1818–1895)

(See also 06-CIVIL-RIGHTS.md for political context)

### The Abolitionist Work Specifically

**Narrative (1845)**: The first edition was 144 pages. Douglass named names, dates, and places — unusual and dangerous (it proved he was who he said he was; it also identified people who might seek revenge). It went through 9 editions in 3 years; translated into French and Dutch. Douglass subsequently fled to Britain to avoid re-enslavement under the Fugitive Slave Act (his former enslaver could have legally reclaimed him).

**British tour (1845–47)**: Douglass lectured across Britain; British supporters raised £150 to purchase his freedom from his enslaver. He returned to the US as a legally free man.

**The North Star (1847)**: Douglass founded his own newspaper against Garrison's advice (Garrison wanted him to stay as a speaker/exhibit for the society, not develop independent standing). The break was the right call — it gave Douglass an independent platform that he used for 17 years.

**The Lincoln strategy**: Douglass met with Lincoln three times during the Civil War. He pushed Lincoln toward faster emancipation, more rigorous arming of Black troops, and equal pay (Black soldiers initially paid less). He influenced the recruitment of 180,000 Black soldiers into the Union Army. Lincoln reportedly said to Douglass at his second inauguration: "I know who you are... I saw you in the crowd today... There is no man in the country whose opinion I value more than yours."

## Common Confusion Points

**"Wilberforce abolished slavery."**
He abolished the slave *trade* (1807). The actual abolition of slavery in British colonies (ownership, not just transport) was the 1833 Abolition Act. Wilberforce died three days after it passed. Clarkson lived another 13 years.

**"The Underground Railroad was a white abolitionist project."**
Free Black Americans, Black churches, and the Black community in Northern cities were the primary infrastructure. White abolitionists (Quakers especially) provided safe houses and funding; the organizational network was predominantly Black.

**"Garrison's radicalism hurt the cause."**
It probably helped by defining the outer boundary of the acceptable, making Lincoln's position look moderate. The mechanism is identical to the Malcolm X effect in the 1960s. Whether you call this "helping the cause" or "occupying a necessary niche in a political ecosystem" is a semantic question.

**"Abolition was primarily a moral/religious movement."**
Moral and religious arguments were essential. So were economic arguments (slavery's inefficiency; free labor economics), geopolitical arguments (US slavery weakening its standing), and legal arguments (natural law; constitutional interpretation). The eventual success required all of them operating simultaneously.
