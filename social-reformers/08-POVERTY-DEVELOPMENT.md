# Poverty and Development: Addams, Mother Teresa, Yunus, Sachs vs. Easterly

## The Big Picture

```
  APPROACHES TO POVERTY: A SPECTRUM
  ====================================

  INDIVIDUAL SERVICE                STRUCTURAL REFORM
  (treat symptoms)                  (change conditions)
  ────────────────                  ─────────────────
  Mother Teresa:                    Addams:
  Serve the dying poor              Settlement house = community
  Do not change structures          building + political reform

  MICROFINANCE                      MACRO DEVELOPMENT
  (bottom-up)                       (top-down)
  ────────────────                  ─────────────────
  Yunus:                            Sachs:
  Small loans to poor women         Massive aid investment
  They build enterprises            to end extreme poverty
  Self-sustaining

  VERSUS:                           VERSUS:
  Easterly:                         Easterly:
  Aid creates dependency            Planners fail; searchers succeed
  Markets work better               Local knowledge > expert plan
  Accountability matters

  THE CORE DISPUTE:
  Is poverty primarily:
  (a) A lack of resources that can be supplied from outside?
  (b) A result of institutional failures that outside
      resources cannot fix?
  (c) A problem of local knowledge and incentives that
      require bottom-up solutions?
  (d) All of the above in different proportions in
      different contexts?

  The empirical evidence favors (d).
  The political economy of aid favors (a) and (b)
  because they justify expert-managed large programs.
```

## Jane Addams (1860–1935)

### What She Did

Addams co-founded Hull House in Chicago in 1889 with Ellen Gates Starr — the first "settlement house" in the United States. The concept came from Toynbee Hall in London: educated middle-class volunteers *live* in poor neighborhoods, building community institutions and advocating for reform.

**Hull House in Operation**:

```
  HULL HOUSE, CHICAGO (1889-1935)
  ==================================

  LOCATION: Chicago's Near West Side — Italian, Greek,
  Russian Jewish, German, Irish, Polish immigrants
  in a dense, poor, industrial neighborhood

  WHAT IT OFFERED:
  - Kindergarten (50 children first year)
  - Adult education: English classes, art, music
  - Library (5,000 volumes by 1893)
  - Gymnasium + public bath (before indoor plumbing)
  - Labor museum (demonstrating immigrant textile arts)
  - Meeting space for labor unions
  - Daycare for working mothers
  - Job placement services
  - Legal advice

  RESIDENTS (at peak, 70 residents, 2,000 weekly visitors):
  - Not permanent charity workers
  - Educated professionals and volunteers who lived there
  - Florence Kelley (labor reform); Alice Hamilton (industrial
    medicine); Edith Abbott (social work as profession)

  THE RESEARCH FUNCTION:
  Hull House residents documented neighborhood conditions
  systematically:
  - Hull House Maps and Papers (1895): first systematic
    social mapping of a US neighborhood
  - Led directly to political reform campaigns
```

**The Reform Work**:

Addams did not just run services; she organized politically. The Hull House network's direct policy results:

| Reform | Year | What Changed |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Illinois Factory and Workshop Inspection Act | 1893 | First state factory inspection law; Florence Kelley as inspector |
| Juvenile court system | 1899 | Cook County Juvenile Court — first in US |
| Illinois Child Labor Law | 1903 | Restricted working hours and ages |
| Workers' Compensation | 1910 | Illinois pioneers the model |
| Federal Children's Bureau | 1912 | Julia Lathrop (Hull House) as first chief |

**Peace work**: Addams was a committed pacifist; she opposed WWI (unusual and unpopular). She was expelled from the Daughters of the American Revolution, lost speaking invitations, was publicly condemned as "the most dangerous woman in America." She co-founded the Women's International League for Peace and Freedom (WILPF, 1919). She won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1931.

**The Settlement House as Systems Thinking**:

Hull House was not charity in the traditional sense — it was community infrastructure. The insight: poor communities lack not moral character but resources, institutions, and connections to power. Supply the institutions; develop political capacity; change conditions. This is community development theory in its first systematic American form.

## Mother Teresa (1910–1997)

### What She Did

Teresa Bojaxhiu was born in Albania, joined the Sisters of Loreto (Irish missionary order), was sent to India, and spent 17 years as a conventional teaching nun in Calcutta before her "call within a call" (1946): leave the convent, live among the poorest of the poor.

She founded the Missionaries of Charity in 1950. Their work: die with dignity. Homes for the dying (Nirmal Hriday, Kalighat); homes for abandoned children; leper colonies; AIDS hospices. By her death, the Missionaries of Charity had 4,500 sisters in 133 countries running 610 missions.

**What She Provided**:

```
  MISSIONARIES OF CHARITY: THE ACTUAL WORK
  ==========================================

  Kalighat (Nirmal Hriday, "Pure Heart"):
  Home for the dying destitute in Calcutta.
  People brought from the street by volunteers.
  Given a place to die with care.
  Religious rites of their own tradition (Hindu, Muslim,
  Catholic) performed at death.

  What was provided: clean bed; food; care; dignity in dying
  What was NOT provided: medical treatment (antibiotics, surgery)
  because: Teresa believed suffering = sharing in Christ's
  passion; medical intervention was not her mission

  SCOPE: 4,500 sisters; 610 missions; 133 countries
  FUNDING: $50M+/year at her death; undocumented sources
```

**The Critiques**:

Christopher Hitchens (*The Missionary Position*, 1995) and medical journal criticisms were systematic:
- **Medical care**: Substandard by any clinical measure. Reused needles; poor nutrition; inadequate pain management. Terminal patients received care but not treatment that could have helped them.
- **Transparency**: No published accounts of the Missionaries of Charity's finances; $50M+ per year in donations with no accounting.
- **Theology of suffering**: Teresa publicly stated that suffering brought people closer to God; that poverty was a gift. "I think it is very beautiful for the poor to accept their lot, to share it with the passion of Christ."
- **Political associations**: Accepted awards from and associated with Jean-Claude Duvalier ("Baby Doc") of Haiti and Charles Keating (S&L fraud convicted).

**The Honest Assessment**:

```
  WHAT TERESA WAS                  WHAT SHE WAS NOT
  ───────────────                  ────────────────
  Genuine personal holiness        An effective poverty reformer
  Devoted to the dying poor        A systemic change agent
  Globally mobilizing for          An efficient charity
  compassion                       administrator
  A symbol of individual           A public health worker
  service
                                   She never argued for systemic
  Her mission: NOT to              change; she provided
  end poverty                      individual service

  THE COMPARISON:
  Addams: serves the poor → also organizes politically
           → structures change
  Teresa: serves the dying → does not organize politically
           → structures unchanged
  Both are genuine. They are different projects.
```

She was canonized by Pope Francis in 2016. Regardless of the critiques of her medical practices, the canonization reflects the Catholic Church's judgment about her sanctity — a different question from her effectiveness as a poverty reformer.

## Muhammad Yunus (b. 1940) and Grameen Bank

### What He Did

Yunus is a Bangladeshi economist who developed *microfinance* — small loans to poor people (especially women) without collateral — as a poverty alleviation tool. He founded Grameen Bank in 1983; it now has 10 million borrowers (97% women) in Bangladesh.

**The Origin (1976)**:

```
  YUNUS'S ORIGIN STORY
  ======================

  1974 Bangladesh famine: Yunus (economics professor at
  Chittagong University) is appalled that textbook theory
  and visible suffering are so disconnected.

  1976: Visits a village near the university.
  Meets a woman who makes bamboo stools:
  - Borrows 22 cents from a moneylender at 10%/week
  - Buys bamboo; makes stool; sells stool
  - After repaying loan: profit of 2 cents
  The moneylender captures essentially all the value.

  Yunus lends $27 from his own pocket to 42 women.
  They all repay.

  HYPOTHESIS: Poor people are creditworthy.
  They cannot get credit because:
  (1) No collateral
  (2) Banks won't process tiny loans (too expensive)
  (3) Moneylenders have monopoly

  GRAMEEN BANK MODEL:
  - Loans to women in groups of 5
  - Group members are not guarantors but social pressure
    ensures repayment
  - No collateral required
  - Progressive lending: small first loan; larger if repaid
  - Weekly repayment meetings (social accountability + education)
  - Current repayment rate: ~97%
```

**The Impact and Evidence**:

```
  GRAMEEN BANK RESULTS (2024):
  =============================
  Borrowers: 10 million (97% women)
  Loans disbursed total: $38+ billion since founding
  Repayment rate: ~97%
  Interest rates: 16-27% (lower than moneylenders; higher
                  than commercial banks — reflects processing cost)

  DEVELOPMENT EFFECTS (empirical evidence mixed):
  Positive:
  - Women's autonomy increased
  - Girls' education increased among borrowers
  - Household consumption increases
  - Asset accumulation
  Contested:
  - Poverty reduction: does microcredit reduce poverty?
    RCTs (randomized controlled trials) show modest effects
    on some outcomes; no consistent poverty graduation
  - Debt trap: some borrowers take new loans to repay old
  - Income effects smaller than originally claimed

  THE HONEST ASSESSMENT:
  Microcredit is better than moneylenders.
  It is not sufficient to end poverty on its own.
  The early claims (Yunus: can eliminate poverty globally)
  were inflated.
  The RCT evidence (Banerjee, Duflo, etc.): modest but
  positive effects; not the transformational tool claimed.
```

**Yunus's Political Troubles**: He was removed from Grameen Bank's leadership by the Bangladesh government in 2011; the government alleged he had transferred assets improperly. Human rights groups saw this as political persecution (he had briefly considered running for political office). He won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2006.

## Jeffrey Sachs vs. William Easterly — The Development Debate

This is the central methodological debate in international development:

```
  JEFFREY SACHS                   WILLIAM EASTERLY
  (The End of Poverty, 2005)      (The White Man's Burden, 2006)

  "PLANNERS"                      "SEARCHERS"
  ──────────                      ───────────
  Poverty is a poverty trap:      Poverty persists because of
  too poor to invest in           institutional failure:
  health/education/infrastructure corruption, bad governance,
  that would produce growth       perverse incentives from aid

  SOLUTION:                       AID FAILURE:
  Enough aid ($195B/yr) to        Planners don't know enough
  cover all investment needs      to plan; local knowledge
  → escape from poverty trap      matters; accountability absent

  EVIDENCE:                       EVIDENCE:
  Millennium Villages Project     Africa received $600B in
  (2005-2015): intensive          aid over 50 years; little
  aid packages to pilot           to show for it; same problems
  villages in Africa              persist

  VERDICT ON MILLENNIUM VILLAGES:
  External evaluators: mixed results; no evidence of
  sustainable improvement vs. control villages;
  ended in 2015

  SACHS'S UPDATED POSITION:       EASTERLY'S POSITION:
  SDGs (Sustainable Development   Governments that are
  Goals) need massive finance      accountable to their own
  from rich countries; Paris       citizens solve problems;
  Agreement needs funding         foreign planners don't
```

**What the Evidence Actually Shows**:

```
  THE RANDOMIZED CONTROLLED TRIAL REVOLUTION IN DEVELOPMENT
  ============================================================

  J-PAL (Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab):
  Abhijit Banerjee, Esther Duflo, Michael Kremer
  (Nobel Prize in Economics, 2019)

  Approach: Run RCTs on specific interventions.
  Don't argue about broad ideological positions.
  Test whether specific programs work.

  FINDINGS (examples):
  ✓ Deworming programs: huge educational attendance gains
    for tiny cost
  ✓ Conditional cash transfers (Bolsa Família model):
    significant poverty reduction; long-run effects
  ✓ Bed nets for malaria: effective; free distribution
    is better than subsidized distribution
  ✓ Specific vaccines: effective
  ✗ Microcredit: modest effects; not transformational
  ✗ Generic "aid packages": mixed evidence
  ✗ School computer programs (in Kenya): no learning gains

  THE SACHS vs. EASTERLY DEBATE IN THIS CONTEXT:
  Both overgeneralize. The question is not
  "does aid work?" but "which specific interventions
  work in which contexts?"

  J-PAL's approach supersedes both in operational terms.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Mother Teresa saved countless lives."**
She comforted many in their dying hours. She did not provide medical care that could have saved lives — that was not her mission. The distinction matters. She was a saint by vocation, not a development worker.

**"Microfinance ended poverty in Bangladesh."**
Bangladesh's poverty reduction over 40 years involved multiple factors: garment industry growth, NGO education programs (BRAC), agricultural green revolution, remittances from overseas workers, and microfinance. Microfinance was one factor; RCTs show it has positive but limited effects on its own.

**"Sachs is right because billions have escaped poverty."**
The decline in global extreme poverty from ~36% in 1990 to ~10% in 2015 was driven primarily by China's economic growth — which was not a product of the aid model Sachs advocates. India's growth also contributed substantially. Sub-Saharan Africa's poverty reduction was more modest and more varied.

**"Easterly's critique means we shouldn't give foreign aid."**
Easterly criticizes poorly designed, unaccountable aid — not all aid. He supports specific programs that have demonstrated effectiveness and accountability. His critique of "planners" is not a case against development assistance; it is a case against planning without feedback and accountability.
