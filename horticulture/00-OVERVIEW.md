# Horticulture — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Horticulture is the science and practice of growing plants for human use and enjoyment — at a scale smaller and more diverse than agriculture, but more intensive and technically demanding per unit area.

```
HORTICULTURE vs AGRICULTURE DISTINCTION:

  AGRICULTURE                          HORTICULTURE
  +----------------------------------+ +----------------------------------+
  | Field crops: grain, oilseed, hay | | Fruits, vegetables, flowers,     |
  | Large-scale, extensive            | | ornamentals, nursery stock       |
  | Commodity production              | | Intensive, high-value per acre   |
  | Mechanized, low labor/acre        | | High labor/acre, skilled work    |
  | Few species per farm              | | Many species per operation       |
  | Long shelf life (grain)           | | Perishable (hours to weeks)      |
  | Price-taker (commodity market)    | | Price-maker (direct market)      |
  +----------------------------------+ +----------------------------------+
                        |
                        v
               SHARED BIOLOGY:
               Both are applied plant science.
               Photosynthesis, transpiration, nutrient cycling,
               soil microbiology — same mechanisms.
               Horticulture applies them at finer precision,
               higher inputs, higher outputs per area.
```

## Plant Biology Fundamentals

These are the physical processes that all horticultural practice is managing:

### Photosynthesis

```
LIGHT REACTIONS (thylakoid membranes):
  Light energy + H₂O → ATP + NADPH + O₂
  Chlorophyll absorbs: red (660nm) and blue (450nm)
  Reflects: green (550nm) → that's why plants look green

CALVIN CYCLE (stroma):
  CO₂ + ATP + NADPH → glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)
  RuBisCO: the enzyme that fixes CO₂ (most abundant enzyme on Earth)

  C3 plants: standard Calvin cycle
    (wheat, most vegetables, most trees)
    Photorespiration at high temp reduces efficiency
  C4 plants: CO₂ concentrating mechanism before Calvin cycle
    (corn, sugarcane, many tropical grasses)
    More efficient at high temp and light
  CAM plants: open stomata at night to fix CO₂
    (cacti, succulents, pineapple)
    Extreme water efficiency; slow growth

LIGHT SATURATION POINT:
  Most C3 crops saturate at ~1/3 of full sunlight.
  More light does not equal more photosynthesis beyond saturation.
  This is why shade crops exist and why full-sun specification matters.
```

### Transpiration

```
TRANSPIRATION (water movement through plant):
  Soil water → root uptake → xylem transport → leaf stomata → atmosphere
  Driven by: water potential gradient (soil > plant > air)

  FUNCTIONS:
  1. Nutrient delivery: minerals dissolved in water travel with it
  2. Cooling: evaporative cooling prevents leaf overheating
  3. Turgor: water pressure maintains cell rigidity (wilting = turgor loss)

  STOMATA: Guard cells open/close based on:
  - Light (open in light, close in dark)
  - CO₂ concentration (high CO₂ = close)
  - ABA (abscisic acid) — drought hormone triggers closure
  - Temperature and humidity

  VAPOR PRESSURE DEFICIT (VPD):
  VPD = pressure difference between air's water vapor content
        and the air's saturation point.
  High VPD (dry air) = high transpiration demand = stress if roots can't supply.
  Low VPD (humid air) = low transpiration = good for some crops, bad for others.
  Controlled environment horticulture (greenhouses, vertical farms) manages
  VPD as a primary control parameter.
```

### Meristems

```
MERISTEMS (growth tissue):
  Meristematic cells are undifferentiated; capable of cell division.
  All plant growth originates from meristems.

  APICAL MERISTEM: tip of shoot and root — extends length
  LATERAL MERISTEM (cambium): adds girth to stems/roots
  INTERCALARY MERISTEM: in grass blades — re-grows after mowing
  AXILLARY BUD: in leaf axils — develops into lateral shoot when apical
                dominance is removed (pruning triggers this)

  APICAL DOMINANCE:
  The apical meristem suppresses lateral buds via auxin (IAA) production.
  Remove the apex (by pruning or damage) → auxin production removed →
  lateral buds activate → bushy growth.
  This is why pruning a hedge makes it dense; pinching annuals makes them branchy.
```

### Dormancy

```
DORMANCY TYPES:
  Endodormancy (true dormancy): internal blocks to growth; requires specific
    conditions to break. Chilling requirement: accumulate hours below ~7°C.
    Peaches: 600–1100 chilling hours. Apple: 400–1700 hours (variety dependent).
    Climate change reducing chilling hours: significant problem for temperate fruit.

  Ecodormancy: external conditions too harsh (cold, dry) but plant would
    grow if conditions improved. Hardiness limits are ecodormancy in extreme.

  Seed dormancy: prevents germination at wrong time/place.
    Physical: seed coat impermeable (scarification required)
    Physiological: inhibitor compounds must be leached (stratification)
    Morphological: embryo must complete development (orchid seeds)
```

## Horticulture's Economic Scale

```
GLOBAL HORTICULTURE (approximate 2023):
  Vegetable production: ~1.1 billion tonnes/year
  Fruit production: ~950 million tonnes/year
  Ornamental horticulture: ~$60 billion global market
  Cut flowers: ~$10 billion global trade (Kenya, Netherlands, Colombia)

  US HORTICULTURE:
  Farm gate value: ~$50 billion/year (2022)
  Largest sectors: vegetables ($20B), fruits ($18B), nursery/greenhouse ($12B)
  Employment: ~1.5 million farm workers; 3+ million if supply chain included

  THE VALUE-PER-ACRE ADVANTAGE:
  Field corn: ~$700-1,000/acre gross revenue
  Processing tomatoes: ~$3,000-5,000/acre
  Fresh market strawberries: ~$15,000-25,000/acre
  Greenhouse cucumbers: ~$50,000-200,000+/acre
  The intensity gradient reflects labor, inputs, and perishability.
```

## Connection to Other Fields

```
HORTICULTURE CONNECTS TO:

  BOTANY (taxonomy, physiology, morphology)
    Horticulture = applied botany
    Understanding plant families predicts cultural requirements

  ECOLOGY (community dynamics, ecosystem services)
    Landscape design = managed ecology
    Native plant horticulture = ecological restoration

  GENETICS (plant breeding, biotechnology)
    New varieties: yield, disease resistance, quality
    GMO applications: Bt resistance, herbicide tolerance
    Marker-assisted selection: much faster than traditional breeding

  FOOD SCIENCE (post-harvest, nutrition)
    Post-harvest physiology: ethylene, respiration, cell wall degradation
    Nutritional quality: antioxidants, vitamins — affected by variety,
    production method, harvest timing, storage

  CHEMISTRY (pesticides, fertilizers, soil chemistry)
    IPM requires understanding pesticide MOA, resistance mechanisms
    Fertigation requires solution chemistry calculation

  ENGINEERING (greenhouse design, irrigation systems, machinery)
    Controlled environment agriculture = HVAC + lighting + nutrient delivery
    as an engineered system with feedback control
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Horticulture includes both food and ornamentals**: the common assumption is that horticulture = vegetables. It also includes floriculture (flowers), arboriculture (trees), and turfgrass — all applied plant science.

**"Organic" is a production certification, not a biological category**: organic horticulture uses different input lists (no synthetic pesticides/fertilizers) but the underlying plant biology is identical. Many organic inputs are chemically complex (copper fungicides, pyrethrin) — "organic" does not mean simple or non-toxic.

**Post-harvest is as important as production**: fresh strawberries at 15°C deteriorate in hours; at 0°C they last 7–10 days. Temperature management from harvest to retail is responsible for 30–40% of fresh produce losses globally. Post-harvest physiology is a discipline with as much economic impact as growing practice.

**C3 vs. C4 matters for controlled environment design**: C4 plants (corn) have higher light saturation points and are less responsive to supplemental CO₂ enrichment. Most greenhouse crops are C3 and benefit significantly from elevated CO₂ (up to ~1,000ppm) in the growing environment.
