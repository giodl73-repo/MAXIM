# Landscape Design Principles

## The Big Picture

Landscape design is the art and science of designing outdoor space to serve human needs while working within ecological constraints. It combines aesthetics, ecology, horticulture, civil engineering, and cultural context.

```
LANDSCAPE DESIGN PROCESS:

  SITE ANALYSIS          PROGRAM              DESIGN
  +------------------+   +------------------+  +------------------+
  | Sun/shade mapping |   | Who uses it?     |  | Concept (parti)  |
  | Soil assessment  |   | What activities? |  | Master plan      |
  | Drainage/water   |   | What budget?     |  | Planting plan    |

  | Views (keep/hide)|   | Style preference |  | Lighting plan    |

                     irrigation drawings, details)
                                 |
                                 v
                    IMPLEMENTATION + MANAGEMENT
                    (establishment + ongoing care)
```

## Design Elements vs. Design Principles

These are different hierarchical categories that are often confused:

```
DESIGN ELEMENTS (vocabulary — the tools of composition):
  LINE: edges, paths, garden beds, hedges — direct movement and gaze.
  FORM: shape of plants and structures — round, columnar, spreading, weeping.
  TEXTURE: surface quality — fine (grass, ferns), medium (most shrubs), coarse (Fatsia, banana).
  COLOR: flower, foliage, bark — hue, value, saturation.
  SPACE: the void defined by solid elements — the garden room concept.

DESIGN PRINCIPLES (grammar — how elements relate):
  UNITY: the garden reads as a coherent whole.
         Achieved through: repetition, limited palette, consistent style.
         Overuse of different species = jumbled; no unity.

  BALANCE: visual equilibrium.
           Symmetrical: formal garden; same on both sides.
           Asymmetrical: Japanese/naturalistic; different elements
                         of equivalent visual weight on each side.

  RHYTHM: repetition with variation.
          Repeating an element (same plant, same material) creates rhythm.
          Variation prevents monotony.
          Example: alternating columnar yews and low rounded boxwood.

  EMPHASIS: focal points draw the eye.
            Primary focal point: the first place the eye goes.
            Secondary: where the eye goes next.
            Without emphasis: the eye doesn't know where to go — boring.

  PROPORTION: correct sizing relationships.
              Human scale matters: paths, seats, doors.
              Plant scale: at maturity, not at purchase.
              THE MATURITY ERROR: most common mistake in planting design.

  SIMPLICITY: restraint; less is often more.
              Sophisticated gardens often use very few species.
              20 species done well > 100 species in confusion.
```

## Plant Selection Criteria

```
SELECTION HIERARCHY (apply in order):

  1. CLIMATE ZONE HARDINESS:
     USDA Hardiness Zones (US): based on average minimum winter temperature.
     Zone 5 = -28 to -23°C average minimum. Zone 7 = -18 to -12°C. Etc.
     A Zone 7 plant in Zone 5 will die in winter.
     RHS hardiness (UK): H1–H7 system.
     Do not compromise on hardiness — plants that are marginally hardy
     die in average winters and certainly in cold winters.

  2. MICROCLIMATE:
     South-facing wall: zone warmer than average.
     North slope, valley bottom: zone colder.
     Coastal: moderated; less extreme.
     Urban heat island: effective zone 1–2 warmer than USDA zone for trees.
     Know your actual microclimate; base selection on that.

  3. MATURE SIZE:
     The most commonly ignored criterion.
     "Dwarf" Alberta spruce: 8m at 30 years. This is dwarf.
     'Forest Pansy' redbud: 9m at maturity. Planted 1.5m from foundation.
     The plant you buy is not the plant you will have.
     Always check mature dimensions. Design for mature size.

  4. SITE CONDITIONS:
     Sun exposure (full sun, part shade, shade).
     Soil moisture (wet, moist, dry, xeric).
     Soil pH (acid, neutral, alkaline).
     Soil texture (heavy clay, loam, sandy, rocky).
     Match plant requirements to site conditions.
     "The right plant for the right place" is not a cliche — it's the rule.

  5. SEASONAL INTEREST:
     Design for 4-season or 12-month interest.
     Spring: bulbs, early flowering trees/shrubs.
     Summer: herbaceous perennials, annuals.
     Autumn: fall color, berries, seed heads.
     Winter: bark, structure, berries, evergreen.
     A garden that peaks in June and is nothing else is a missed opportunity.

  6. MAINTENANCE REQUIREMENTS:
     Match to maintenance budget and skill.
     High-maintenance plants (roses, formal hedges, exotic annuals) in a
     low-maintenance design = failure.
     Low-maintenance ≠ no maintenance. "No-maintenance" does not exist.

  7. INVASIVE POTENTIAL:
     Check regional invasive species lists before specifying.
     Japanese Knotweed (Fallopia japonica): illegal to plant in UK; invasive everywhere.
     Butterfly bush (Buddleja davidii): invasive in Pacific Northwest.
     Many ornamentals popular in horticulture are ecologically problematic.
```

## Site Analysis

```
SUN/SHADE MAPPING:
  Map shadows at: 9am, 12pm, 3pm on June 21 (summer solstice — least shadow)
                  and December 21 (winter solstice — most shadow).
  Tools: Sunseeker app, SunCalc, or physical observation over a season.

  SHADE CATEGORIES:
  Full sun: >6 hours direct sun (especially midday).
  Part sun/part shade: 4–6 hours direct sun.
  Full shade: <4 hours direct sun (direct); bright indirect throughout day.
  Deep shade: <2 hours; little indirect light.

DRAINAGE:
  Observe after heavy rain:
  Where does water pool? (low spots, compacted areas, clay).
  Where does water run? (swales, berms, impervious surfaces).
  Where is soil excessively dry? (slopes, sandy areas, near buildings).
  This determines: what plants will survive; where drainage intervention needed.

SOIL ASSESSMENT:
  Take cores at multiple locations; note:
  Texture (jar test or ribbon test).
  Depth to hardpan or rock.
  Organic matter (dark color vs. light; smell).
  pH test (test kit or meter).
  Compaction test: screwdriver pushed by hand; should penetrate 15cm easily.
  If it stops at 5cm: severe compaction; aeration and organic matter needed.

EXISTING VEGETATION:
  Identify and map trees and significant shrubs.
  Tree root zones: typically 1.5–3× crown radius; often larger.
  Root zone protection during construction: fencing at drip line minimum.
  Estimate tree health and longevity.
  Root conflict with proposed hardscape.
```

## Design Styles

### Formal

```
FORMAL GARDEN:
  Geometry: symmetry, straight lines, geometric shapes.
  Hedges: clipped to precise forms (topiary, parterres).
  Axes: visual lines terminated by focal points (obelisk, fountain, statue).
  Historical: Italian Renaissance (Villa d'Este), French Baroque (Versailles).
  Contemporary formal: more simplified geometry; less topiary; same spatial logic.

  VERSAILLES (Le Notre, 1661–1700):
  The supreme example. Key elements:
    Grand axial perspective (2.5km parterres → horizon).
    Parterres de broderie: low-trimmed shrubs in intricate patterns.
    Bosquets: clipped woodland rooms (outdoor rooms).
    Fountains as termination points of axes.
    Scale: overwhelming human. Designed for absolutist spectacle.

PARTERRE:
  Formal pattern bed. Types:
    Parterre de broderie: intricate embroidery-like knot patterns.
    Parterre de pieces coupees: separate geometric beds separated by gravel.
    Simple geometric beds with central feature.
```

### Japanese Garden Aesthetics

```
KEY JAPANESE PRINCIPLES:

  MA (間) — Negative space:
    Emptiness is as important as fullness.
    The gravel raked in a karesansui (dry rock garden) is "water"
    not because it looks like water but because the empty space
    creates the experience of water.
    In design: don't fill every space. Let voids define solids.

  WABI-SABI (侘び寂び) — Transience and imperfection:
    Beauty in impermanence; beauty in weathering.
    The moss-covered stone; the asymmetric bent pine.
    Handmade irregularity preferred over machine perfection.
    Aged materials preferred over new.

  ASYMMETRY:
    Nature is not symmetrical. Japanese gardens are asymmetric.
    Balance achieved through asymmetric composition (different weights).
    Tripartite composition: tall/medium/small elements.

  SHAKKEI (借景) — Borrowed scenery:
    Deliberately include distant views as part of the composition.
    A mountain framed by garden trees; a distant grove included in the view.
    The garden extends beyond its boundaries into the landscape.

  SCALE:
    Japanese gardens often imply large landscapes in small spaces.
    A large stone represents a mountain. A small pool represents a lake.
    The visitor's mind completes the illusion.
    Scale illusion: use fine-textured plants at the back (they appear smaller
    and more distant); coarse-textured in front (appear larger, nearer).
```

### English Landscape Garden

```
ENGLISH LANDSCAPE GARDEN (Lancelot "Capability" Brown, 1716–1783):
  Reaction against formal French gardens.
  Principles:
    Naturalistic curves (ha-ha — sunken wall — removes visible boundary;
    landscape flows into park).
    Rolling lawn (not hedged parterres).
    Clumped tree plantings (not allees).
    Serpentine lakes (not geometric ponds).
    Views framed by trees.
  Brown's improvements: remove geometric gardens; smooth rolling grass;
  plant irregular belts and clumps; dam streams for serpentine lakes.

  Historical significance: Brown transformed hundreds of British country estates.
  His work defines our visual idea of "natural" landscape — but his
  "naturalistic" landscapes were entirely constructed.
  Nature was a concept being designed, not left alone.
```

### Contemporary Styles

```
NATURALISTIC/NEW PERENNIAL MOVEMENT:
  Primary figures: Piet Oudolf (Netherlands), James van Sweden (US).
  Principles:
    Plant in drifts and masses rather than individual specimens.
    Use grasses extensively.
    Celebrate all seasons including winter structure (seedheads, dried stems).
    Matrix planting: plants woven together as groundcover, not set apart.
    Ecological function: attracts pollinators and birds.
  Key projects: High Line (New York), Millennium Park (Chicago),
                Hauser & Wirth Somerset garden.

MEDITERRANEAN / XERIC:
  Appropriate for: dry climates; drought-prone regions; water-restricted.
  Plant palette: lavender, rosemary, Cistus, Phlomis, grasses, succulents.
  Hardscape: local stone, gravel, terracotta.
  Water: harvested, recycled, minimal supplemental.
  American equivalent: California native garden; xeriscape (water-wise landscaping).

CONTEMPORARY MINIMALIST:
  Clean geometry; limited plant palette; emphasis on materiality.
  Hardscape: concrete, weathered steel (corten), timber.
  Plants: grasses, simple groundcovers, single-species masses.
  Influence: Modernist architecture; Japanese aesthetics; urban context.
```

## Planting Design

### Layered Planting

```
CANOPY LAYER (large trees, 10–30m):
  + Structural; long-lived; shade and shelter providers.
  Examples: oak, maple, ash, beech, ginkgo.
  Site considerations: scale to space; root zone management.

UNDERSTORY LAYER (small trees, 5–10m):
  + Operates under canopy; flowers/fruit often prominent.
  Examples: Amelanchier, Cornus, Magnolia, Prunus.
  Best below canopy where root competition manageable.

SHRUB LAYER (1–4m):
  + Structure, year-round interest; wildlife habitat.
  Examples: Viburnum, Mahonia, Euonymus, Hydrangea, Rosa.
  Mix evergreen and deciduous for year-round interest.

PERENNIAL LAYER (0.3–1.5m):
  + Seasonal flower interest; seasonal change; wildlife food.
  Examples: Salvia, Geranium (hardy), Persicaria, Deschampsia.
  Oudolf-influenced: grasses woven among perennials.

GROUNDCOVER LAYER (<0.3m):
  + Suppresses weeds; covers soil; low maintenance when established.
  Examples: Geranium macrorrhizum, Epimedium, Vinca, Ajuga.
  Match to light conditions.

BULB LAYER (any height, ephemeral):
  + Spring interest before perennials emerge; fall interest.
  Examples: Narcissus, Tulipa, Allium, Colchicum.
  Plant under deciduous shrubs and perennials — space available when dormant.
```

### Drift Planting

```
PIET OUDOLF'S MATRIX PLANTING:
  Traditional planting: specimen plants set apart, mulch between.
  Matrix planting: grasses or low structural plants as ground matrix;
                   feature plants emerge from within the matrix.

  +--------+--------+--------+--------+
  |  g  g  |  g  F  |  g  g  |  g  g  |  g = grass matrix
  |  g  g  |  g  g  |  g  F  |  g  g  |  F = feature perennial
  |  g  F  |  g  g  |  g  g  |  g  F  |  Pattern: scattered, not rigidly spaced
  +--------+--------+--------+--------+

  Benefits:
    Plants support each other; grass matrix reduces weed pressure.
    More natural appearance.
    Seasonal evolution: grasses provide structure as perennials die back.
    More resilient: if one plant fails, matrix fills in.
```

## Ecological Landscape Design

### Native Plants

```
NATIVE PLANT VALUE:
  Evolutionary relationships: local wildlife evolved with local plants.
  Example: monarch butterfly larva eats only Asclepias (milkweed) — native.
  Douglas Tallamy research: native oaks support 500+ caterpillar species;
  ginkgo (non-native) supports 0.
  Insects are the base of the food web; without native plants,
  insects decline → bird decline → mammal decline.

  DEFINITION: Native to the specific region, not just the country.
    New England native ≠ California native.
    "Native to North America" = meaningless for most plants.

NATIVE PLANT CAVEATS:
  Not all native plants are appropriate for all urban sites.
  Cultivars of native plants vary in wildlife value (straight species
  usually better than ornamental cultivars with modified flowers).
  "Nativar" double-flowered forms: pollinators cannot access nectar.
  Choose straight species or single-flowered cultivars.
```

### Rain Gardens and Bioswales

```
RAIN GARDEN:
  Shallow, concave planting area designed to capture and infiltrate
  stormwater runoff. NOT a pond — drains within 24–48 hours.

  SITING:
    2–15m from foundation (avoid basement flooding).
    On flat or gentle slope below impervious surface (roof, driveway).
    >6m from trees (root protection).
    Soil must percolate: test with 15cm hole filled with water;
    should drain in 6–12 hours. Clay soils require amendment.

  PLANT SELECTION:
    Wet zone (center/low point): tolerates seasonal inundation.
      Iris versicolor, Carex, Lobelia cardinalis, Juncus.
    Transition zone (side slopes): tolerates wet and dry.
      Calamagrostis, Aster, Eutrochium (Joe pye weed).
    Edge zone (top): xeric to moderate.
      Echinacea, Agastache, Panicum.

BIOSWALE:
  Linear channel designed to slow, filter, and infiltrate stormwater.
  Shallower than rain garden; longer; designed for channelized flow.
  Vegetated with native grasses and wetland plants.
  Check dams: periodic low barriers to slow flow and promote infiltration.
```

### Edible Landscapes

```
EDIBLE LANDSCAPE PRINCIPLES:
  Integrate food-producing plants into ornamental design.
  Not "vegetable garden as focal point" but "ornamental plants
  that are also edible."

  EXAMPLES:
  Fruit trees as canopy (Malus, Prunus, Pyrus): ornamental spring flower + fruit.
  Blueberries as shrub layer: ornamental fall color + fruit.
  Herbs as groundcover (thyme, Ajuga): ornamental + edible.
  Edible flowers (Calendula, Nasturtium, Viola): color + culinary use.
  Kale, chard: ornamental leaf color + edible.
  Aronia (chokeberry): native shrub, fall color, wildlife value, edible.
  Goji (Lycium barbarum): shrub, orange/red berries.

  AESTHETIC INTEGRATION:
  Organize by color, texture, height — as with ornamentals.
  Conceal utilities (irrigation, pest netting) or make them architectural.
  The kitchen garden tradition (walled vegetable gardens) is the historic
  precedent — these were both productive and beautifully designed.
```

## Maintenance Planning

```
MAINTENANCE BUDGET LEVELS:
  LOW:      Mow lawn; pick up debris; weed 1×/month.
            Appropriate plants: native shrubs, groundcovers, established trees.
            NOT appropriate: annual beds, topiary, roses, perennial borders.

  MEDIUM:   Low + seasonal planting (annuals), perennial cutback, fertilizing.
            Appropriate: mixed border with perennials + some annuals.

  HIGH:     Medium + deadheading, pruning (roses, formal hedges, espalier).
            Appropriate: formal gardens, rose gardens, vegetable/herb gardens.

  VERY HIGH: High + daily watering, daily harvest, pest monitoring.
              Appropriate: intensive vegetable production; bonsai.

MAINTENANCE-DESIGN MATCHING (most common design error):
  Designing a high-maintenance garden for a low-maintenance client.
  The garden looks great in year 1; deteriorates in year 2–3 without care.
  The rule: design the maintenance DOWN to what the client will actually do.
  Overplanting and high-input selections = garden that fails its owner.

ESTABLISHMENT PERIOD:
  All plants require more maintenance in years 1–3 than at maturity.
  Irrigation: essential in establishment; most established plants need less.
  Weed competition: critical in establishment (weeds can smother new plants).
  Year 4+: maintenance drops significantly for appropriate, well-sited plants.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Design Goal | Primary Approach | Key Consideration |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Low-maintenance garden | Native plants + groundcovers | Eliminate lawn in shade areas |
| Formal garden | Symmetry, clipped hedges, axis | Choose hedge species appropriate for climate |
| Wildlife habitat | Native plants, layered structure | Include water; minimize pesticides |
| Small urban garden | Vertical elements, pots, wall plants | Every surface is a planting opportunity |
| Stormwater management | Rain garden, bioswale, permeable paving | Test soil percolation first |
| Edible landscape | Food plants selected for ornamental value | Maintenance required for production |
| Japanese aesthetic | Asymmetry, negative space, rocks, gravel | Restraint in plant selection |

## Common Confusion Points

**Design elements and design principles are not interchangeable**: "line" is an element (a tool); "rhythm" is a principle (how elements relate). Using them as synonyms causes confusion in design review discussions.

**USDA hardiness zones are minimum temperature only**: the zones tell you about cold hardiness, not about heat tolerance, humidity, soil drainage, or disease pressure. A plant might survive the winter in Zone 7 but die from summer heat and humidity that the zone number does not describe. Use the hardiness zone as a necessary condition, not a sufficient one.

**Native plants require maintenance too**: "native plants = no maintenance" is a common client misconception. Native plants require establishment irrigation, weed management during establishment, and occasionally cutback. The difference is: once established (2–4 years), native plants require significantly less supplemental water and fertilizer than non-native ornamentals adapted to different climates.

**Oudolf-style planting requires specific maintenance knowledge**: matrix planting with grasses and perennials looks right in summer and into winter, but requires leaving plant material standing through winter (the point — seedheads, dried stems are ornamental). Cut down in late winter/early spring. Clients accustomed to "tidy up in fall" will ruin the effect.

**"Right plant, right place" overrides all other design considerations**: a plant that is wrong for the site conditions will fail regardless of how perfectly it fits the design concept. Begin with site conditions; identify plants appropriate to those conditions; then design within that constrained palette. Not the reverse.
