# Soil Science for Horticulture

## The Big Picture

Soil is a three-phase system — solid (minerals + organic matter), liquid (soil water + dissolved nutrients), and gas (air) — inhabited by a community of organisms. Horticultural practice is fundamentally about managing these phases to optimize plant growth.

```
SOIL SYSTEM:

  SOLID PHASE (~50% by volume):
    +-----------------+
    | MINERAL PARTICLES|  Sand (2.0–0.05mm), Silt (0.05–0.002mm),
    | 45% of total     |  Clay (<0.002mm)
    | volume           |  Originate from rock weathering
    +-----------------+
    +-----------------+
    | ORGANIC MATTER  |  Fresh debris → humus → stabilized OM
    | 5% of total     |  Living: 1% (roots, organisms)
    | volume          |  Non-living: 4% (decomposed material)
    +-----------------+

  LIQUID PHASE (~25% of volume when at field capacity):
    Soil water contains dissolved ions: NO₃⁻, H₂PO₄⁻, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, etc.
    Water potential gradient drives movement root → plant.

  GAS PHASE (~25% of volume when at field capacity):
    N₂, O₂, CO₂ (higher than atmosphere), water vapor.
    O₂ availability critical for root respiration and microbial activity.
    Waterlogged soil: gas phase eliminated → anaerobic stress.
```

## Soil Texture

Texture = the proportion of sand, silt, and clay particles. It is a fixed characteristic — you cannot change texture without replacing the soil.

### The Soil Texture Triangle

```
         CLAY
         100%
          |\.
          | \.  CLAY
       60 |  \.____________
          |   |  SANDY  |  \
          |   |   CLAY  |  SILTY
          |  CLAY  |    CLAY\  |
       40 |  LOAM  |  CLAY   \|
          |________|   LOAM   |
          |   SANDY|__________|
       20 | CLAY   |LOAM|SILT |
          |  LOAM  |    |LOAM |
          |SANDY   |    |     |
        0 |LOAM  SAND         |
          +--------------------+
          0      40     80  SILT
         SAND 100%        100%

(Simplified — actual USDA triangle has precise class boundaries)

KEY TEXTURE CLASSES:
  Sandy: >70% sand — fast drainage, low water retention, low nutrients
  Loam: balanced mix — "ideal"; good structure, drainage, and retention
  Clay: >40% clay — slow drainage, high retention, shrink-swell, compacts
  Silt: smooth texture; good water retention but poor structure; crusts
```

### Particle Properties

```
PARTICLE SIZE COMPARISON:
  Sand: visible to naked eye (0.05–2mm). Like small ball bearings.
        High drainage; low surface area; low nutrient holding.
  Silt: not visible (0.002–0.05mm). Smooth, flour-like when dry.
        Moderate properties; crusts easily.
  Clay: microscopic platelets (<0.002mm). Flat, sheet-like.
        ENORMOUS surface area: 1g of clay = 5–800 m² surface area.
        This surface area is why clay holds water and nutrients.

CLAY MINERALS (two main types in temperate soils):
  Kaolinite: 1:1 structure (1 silica: 1 alumina layer).
             Low CEC, low shrink-swell. Dominant in tropical weathered soils.
  Smectite (Montmorillonite): 2:1 structure.
             High CEC, high shrink-swell. Dominant in many temperate soils.
             This is why black clay soils crack in summer and swell in winter.
```

## Soil Structure

Structure = the arrangement of particles into aggregates (peds). Unlike texture, structure can be managed.

```
AGGREGATE FORMATION:
  Clay particles + organic matter + biological activity
  → particles bound into aggregates
  → aggregates create a pore system

  MACROPORES (>0.075mm): drainage, gas exchange, root penetration
  MESOPORES (0.0002–0.075mm): water storage (held against gravity)
  MICROPORES (<0.0002mm): water held too tightly for plants (unavailable)

STRUCTURE QUALITY INDICATORS:
  Visual: crumbly, granular aggregates (good)
          massive, compacted (bad)
          slaking: aggregates that collapse in water (unstable)

  IMPLICATIONS FOR MANAGEMENT:
  Tillage: breaks up compaction but destroys aggregates.
           No-till: aggregates develop over time; but compaction risk.
  Organic matter additions: cements aggregates; improves stability.
  Foot traffic / machinery: compacts macro pore space.
  Wetting/drying cycles: can help or harm structure depending on clay type.
```

## Soil Organic Matter

SOM is the most important modifiable soil property for horticulture:

```
WHAT ORGANIC MATTER DOES:

  WATER RETENTION:
    SOM holds 5–20× its weight in water.
    Each 1% increase in OM → ~0.3% increase in plant-available water.
    A loam soil with 3% OM holds significantly more water than 1% OM.

  CATION EXCHANGE CAPACITY (CEC):
    SOM has very high CEC: 100–300 cmol(+)/kg
    (compared to kaolinite: 2–6; smectite: 80–150)
    More SOM = more nutrient holding capacity.

  BIOLOGICAL ACTIVITY:
    SOM is food for soil organisms.
    More SOM = more bacteria, fungi, earthworms.
    More biological activity = faster nutrient cycling.
    Mycorrhizal networks require organic substrate.

  TILTH:
    SOM binds soil particles into aggregates.
    Good aggregation → good tilth → easy tillage + root penetration.

ORGANIC MATTER DYNAMICS:
  Additions (compost, manure, crop residues) are balanced by decomposition.
  Decomposition rate: fast in warm, wet, aerated soils.
                      slow in cold, dry, or waterlogged soils.
  Equilibrium OM content: determined by input rate vs. decomposition rate.
  Increasing OM in a warm climate takes sustained additions — decomposition
  fights every addition. Building OM in cool climates is easier.
```

## Soil pH and Nutrient Availability

pH is the single most important factor controlling nutrient availability:

```
pH-NUTRIENT AVAILABILITY CHART (simplified):

  pH:    4.0   5.0   6.0   7.0   7.5   8.0
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  N:     [--NARROW--][====WIDE====][---NARROW---]
  P:     [NARROW][=====WIDE=====][---NARROW---]
  K:     [NARROW][=========WIDE==========]
  Ca:    [NARROW][=======WIDE=======]
  Mg:    [NARROW][=======WIDE=======]
  Fe:    [=====WIDE====][--NARROW--][VERY NARROW]
  Mn:    [=====WIDE====][--NARROW--][VERY NARROW]
  B:     [NARROW][=======WIDE=======]
  Mo:    [NARROW][NARROW][===WIDE===]
  Zn:    [===WIDE===][NARROW]
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Optimal for most crops: pH 6.0–6.8

KEY pH EFFECTS:
  pH <5.5: Fe and Mn become soluble → potential toxicity to sensitive crops
            Al³⁺ soluble → toxic to most plants; inhibits P uptake
            Ca, Mg, P, Mo availability reduced

  pH >7.5: Fe, Mn, Zn, B, Cu become insoluble (hydroxides, carbonates)
            Iron chlorosis is the characteristic symptom in calcareous soils
            (yellow leaves with green veins — iron unavailable despite being present)
            P ties up as calcium phosphate

  pH 6.0–7.0: "Sweet spot" where most nutrients simultaneously available
```

### pH Adjustment

```
LOWERING pH (acidifying):
  Elemental sulfur: sulfur-oxidizing bacteria convert to H₂SO₄.
    Works slowly (weeks-months); persists.
    Acidifying fertilizers: ammonium sulfate, ammonium nitrate
    (nitrification releases H⁺).

RAISING pH (liming):
  Agricultural lime (CaCO₃): slow; takes 3–6 months to react fully.
  Dolomite lime (CaMg(CO₃)₂): also adds Mg; preferred where Mg low.
  Hydrated lime (Ca(OH)₂): fast-acting; can burn plants; use carefully.

  BUFFERING:
  The amount of lime required to raise pH depends on CEC.
  High-CEC soils (clay, high OM): require more lime to change pH.
  Sandy soils: require much less. This is "buffer pH" — the test
  that tells you how much lime to apply for a target pH change.
```

## Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)

CEC is the soil's ability to hold positively charged ions (cations) against leaching:

```
CEC EXPLAINED:
  Clay and SOM surfaces are negatively charged.
  Cations (K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, NH₄⁺, H⁺, Na⁺) are attracted to these surfaces.
  CEC = total negative charge available.
  Units: cmol(+)/kg (or me/100g — older unit; same numerically)

  TYPICAL CEC VALUES:
  Sand: 1–5 cmol(+)/kg
  Sandy loam: 5–10
  Loam: 10–20
  Clay loam: 20–30
  Heavy clay: 30–50+
  Organic soil (peat): 50–150

  LOW CEC IMPLICATIONS:
  Nutrients leach easily (esp. with rain or irrigation).
  Frequent small applications better than large infrequent applications.
  Sandy soils in humid climates: apply N in split applications.

BASE SATURATION:
  What % of CEC is occupied by Ca, Mg, K (base cations) vs. H, Al (acidic).
  High base saturation (>80%) = high pH, fertile.
  Low base saturation (<50%) = acid soil, less fertile.
  The % saturation of each base affects crop performance:
  Ca should be 60–80% of CEC; Mg 10–20%; K 2–5%.
```

## Container Media

Field soil fails in containers. Understanding why explains why commercial container mixes use completely different components:

```
THE PERCHED WATER TABLE PROBLEM:

  CONTAINER:
  +-------------------+
  |                   |
  |   SOIL/MEDIA      |  <- Here, water drains by gravity
  |                   |
  +-------------------+
  |  DRAINAGE HOLE    |

  In a container, a column of saturated soil sits at the bottom.
  The height of this saturated zone = "perched water table."
  Height is constant regardless of container size.
  In a large container (30cm height): 5cm at bottom = saturated.
  In a small container (10cm height): 5cm at bottom = 50% saturated.
  Most pots are less tall than the perched water table → roots in anaerobic zone.

CONTAINER MEDIA REQUIREMENTS:
  1. High air-filled porosity (AFP): >15% by volume at field capacity.
     Compare field soil: AFP = 2–10% in fine-textured soils.
  2. Adequate water retention: available water at least 25–35% by volume.
  3. Low density: ease of handling, reduced compaction risk.
  4. pH buffering and good CEC (for fertigation programs).
  5. Disease suppression (steam-pasteurized or biologically active).

TYPICAL COMPONENTS:
  Peat moss: high water retention; acidic; low density; poor structure alone.
  Coir (coconut coir): similar to peat; more sustainable; higher pH.
  Perlite (volcanic glass): large pores; air drainage; adds AFP; no nutrients.
  Vermiculite: micaceous mineral; holds water; adds CEC; absorbs compaction.
  Bark (composted pine bark): structure; drainage; longevity; slight pH buffering.
  Sand: adds weight and drainage; use coarse (not fine — fine worsens drainage).

COMMON MIXES:
  Standard nursery mix: 50–70% bark + 30–50% perlite
  Seedling mix: 50% peat/coir + 25% perlite + 25% vermiculite
  Cactus/succulent: 50% bark + 50% coarse sand or grit
```

## Compost Science

```
COMPOSTING MECHANISM:
  Organic materials + microbes + O₂ + water → CO₂ + H₂O + stabilized humus

C:N RATIO (Carbon:Nitrogen):
  Microbes need both C (energy) and N (proteins).
  Optimal C:N: 25:1 to 30:1 for rapid decomposition.
  High C:N (straw, sawdust ~80:1): decompose slowly; tie up N.
  Low C:N (grass clippings, food scraps ~15:1): decompose fast; lose N as NH₃.
  Mix materials to approach 25–30:1.

THERMAL STAGES:
  Mesophilic (20–40°C, days 1–3): initial colonization by mesophiles.
  Thermophilic (40–65°C, weeks 1–6): rapid decomposition; heat kills pathogens.
    Critical temperature: >55°C for 3 days (or >50°C for 7 days) required
    for USDA pathogen reduction standards.
    E. coli O157:H7 and Salmonella: killed at these temperatures.
    Weed seeds: killed at 50°C for 40 minutes.
  Cooling (weeks 6+): thermophiles depleted; temperature drops.
  Maturation (months): humus formation; mesophiles complete decomposition.

MATURITY TESTING:
  Temperature: mature compost does not reheat when remixed.
  CO₂ respiration: low in mature compost (no more active decomposition).
  Ammonium/Nitrate ratio: mature compost has low NH₄⁺, higher NO₃⁻
    (nitrification has occurred).
  Phytotoxicity test: germinate radish or lettuce seeds in 50:50 compost:sand.
    Germination >90% of control = mature.
    Immature compost inhibits germination (acetic acid, ammonia).
```

## Mycorrhizal Fungi

```
MYCORRHIZAL TYPES:
  Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF):
    Colonize root cortex; penetrate cells; form arbuscules (tree-like structures).
    ~80% of land plant species form AMF (most vegetables, most trees, grasses).
    Hyphae extend 10–15cm into soil beyond root surface.

  Ectomycorrhizal fungi:
    Form mantle around root; grow between cortex cells (Hartig net).
    ~3% of plant species but important: forest trees (pine, oak, beech, birch).
    Many are edible (chanterelle, truffle, porcini, fly agaric).

AMF BENEFITS:
  Phosphorus: AMF hyphae explore 100–1,000× more soil volume than roots.
    Critical for P uptake (P is immobile in soil; diffusion rate slow).
    AMF provide 20–80% of plant P requirement.
  Water: hyphae access micropores unavailable to roots.
  Disease suppression: competitive exclusion of pathogens.
  Aggregate formation: glomalin (AMF protein) cements soil aggregates.

INOCULATION IN HORTICULTURE:
  Commercial AMF inoculants: Glomus intraradices, Rhizophagus irregularis.
  Applied to transplant roots or mixed into container media.
  Evidence: strong benefit in low-P situations and in soils with
  damaged native AMF populations (fumigated, sterilized soils).
  Limited benefit in high-P soils (plant down-regulates AMF when P sufficient).
  Container media: usually AMF-poor (steam-pasteurized kills AMF).
  Field soil: native AMF usually adequate unless severely disturbed.
```

## Soil Testing

```
STANDARD SOIL TEST:
  pH (electrode): most important single measurement.
  Buffer pH: determines lime requirement.
  Mehlich-3 extraction: extractant dissolves available Ca, Mg, K, P,
    Zn, Mn, B, Cu. Standard in eastern US and many labs globally.
  Results: ppm or lb/acre of each element.
  Interpretation: comparison to calibrated sufficiency ranges.

  MEHLICH-3 CAVEATS:
  Extraction efficiency varies by element.
  Calibration developed for specific crops and soil types.
  "Optimal range" for P in vegetable production differs from turf.

ORGANIC MATTER TEST:
  Loss-on-ignition: burn sample at 360°C; weight loss = OM.
    Overestimates slightly (includes some mineral water).
  Walkley-Black (wet oxidation): more accurate; uses Cr²O₇²⁻.
    Being phased out for health reasons.
  Reported as %OM or %organic carbon (× 1.72 to convert OC to OM).

PLANT TISSUE TESTING:
  Foliar test: sample recently matured leaves; digest; analyze.
  Identifies hidden hunger (deficiency before visual symptoms).
  Used to calibrate fertilizer programs for perennial crops.
  Petiole sap testing: rapid; used in-season for tomato, potato.
    Indicates current N, K status within hours.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Soil Problem | Diagnosis | Solution |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Iron chlorosis (yellow leaves, green veins) | pH >7.0 | Acidify soil; apply chelated iron; use acid-tolerant rootstock |
| Slow drainage, waterlogging | Clay texture or compaction | Raised beds; drainage tile; organic matter additions over time |
| Nutrient leaching after rain | Sandy texture, low CEC | Split fertigation applications; increase SOM |
| Container media drying too fast | Low water retention | Add coir or vermiculite; increase irrigation frequency |
| Seedling damping-off in containers | Soilborne pathogens | Pasteurize media; reduce overwatering; improve drainage |
| Low pH (<5.5), poor growth | Acid soil | Apply dolomite lime to target pH |
| High pH (>7.5), iron chlorosis | Calcareous soil | Acidify; use chelated micronutrients; use tolerant rootstocks |

**Soil as an engineered system**: the three-phase model (solid mineral + organic, liquid soil solution, gas pore space) maps directly to a resource-bounded system with typed storage. The solid phase is the persistent store — high latency, high capacity, slow to change. The liquid phase is the active working set — nutrients in solution are immediately available to roots but have limited capacity (CEC is the buffer size). The gas phase is the O₂ supply for root and microbial respiration — oxygen depletion in waterlogged soils is equivalent to thread starvation in a saturated thread pool.

CEC (cation exchange capacity) is the soil's charge-based buffer cache for cations. High CEC (clay-rich or high-SOM soils) holds more nutrient cations against leaching — high cache capacity. Low CEC (sandy soils) lets nutrients leach rapidly — small cache, frequent misses. pH is a global configuration parameter: it controls which nutrients are in soluble (available) vs. insoluble (locked) form. At pH 7.5+, iron and manganese precipitate as hydroxides — the equivalent of a feature flag that disables a subsystem. At pH 4.5–, aluminum solubilizes to toxic levels — the equivalent of a misconfiguration that causes a previously disabled subsystem to re-enable destructively.

## Common Confusion Points

**Adding sand to clay soil makes it worse, not better**: adding a small amount of sand to clay creates a weak concrete-like mix. To improve clay drainage with sand, you need to add ~50% of the soil volume in coarse sand — an impractical quantity. The correct approach for clay: add organic matter continuously; use raised beds.

**CEC is not directly visible in a soil test result**: standard soil tests report nutrient concentrations (ppm) and pH. CEC is usually reported separately or inferred from the soil's cation base saturation. Check that the lab reports CEC if you need it for lime calculation.

**"pH balanced" fertilizer is meaningless**: fertilizer pH in the bag has almost no effect on soil pH. What matters is whether the fertilizer's chemistry releases or consumes H⁺ in the soil — this is the acidifying or alkalinizing effect. Ammonium-based N fertilizers acidify; nitrate-based N fertilizers are approximately neutral; calcium nitrate slightly alkalinizes.

**Container media and field soil require entirely different approaches**: a recipe that builds excellent field soil tilth (deep organic matter additions, cover crops, reduced tillage) is irrelevant for containers. Container media is an engineering problem: specify the required AFP, water retention, CEC, and pH, then build the mix from components that deliver those specs.

**SOM percentage looks small but represents large mass**: 3% OM in the top 15cm of a hectare of soil is approximately 60 tonnes of organic matter. Each 1% increase requires adding substantial organic material, accounting for decomposition. Building SOM is a multi-year project, not a one-season intervention.
