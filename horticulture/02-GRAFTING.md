# Grafting and Budding Techniques

## The Big Picture

Grafting is the union of two plants so that they grow as one. It is the foundational technique for fruit tree production and an increasingly standard practice in vegetables. The underlying biology is cellular: the graft works only when vascular tissues from both partners reconnect.

```
GRAFTING COMPONENTS AND TERMINOLOGY:

  SCION: The top portion; provides the cultivar (fruit variety, etc.)
         Shoot tip that will grow and bear fruit.

  ROOTSTOCK (stock): The bottom portion; provides the root system.
         Controls: tree size, precocity, soil adaptation, disease resistance.

  GRAFT UNION: The interface where scion and rootstock tissues unite.

           [SCION - Honeycrisp apple variety]
                    |
           +--------+--------+
           | GRAFT UNION     |  <-- vascular reconnection zone
           +--------+--------+
                    |
           [ROOTSTOCK - M.9 dwarfing rootstock]
                    |
               [ROOT SYSTEM]

  FUNDAMENTAL REQUIREMENT:
  Cambium layers of scion and rootstock must be brought into
  contact and held motionless while they grow together.
  No cambium alignment → no union → graft fails.
```

## The Graft Union: Biology

### Cambium and Vascular Reconnection

```
ANATOMY OF A WOODY STEM (cross-section):
  Bark (outermost)
  Phloem (inner bark) — carries sugars down from leaves
  CAMBIUM (1–2 cell layer thick) — produces new phloem and xylem
  Xylem (wood) — carries water + minerals up from roots
  Pith (center)

GRAFT UNION FORMATION (3–6 weeks):
  Step 1 (Days 1–5): Cut surfaces dry; cells die at margins.
  Step 2 (Days 3–10): Surviving cambial cells produce callus
    (undifferentiated parenchyma cells) that fill the graft interface.
  Step 3 (Days 7–21): Callus cells from both scion and rootstock
    intermingle and differentiate → new vascular connections form.
  Step 4 (Days 21–42): Continuous vascular tissue crosses the union.
    Phloem reconnects first (sugars can now flow).
    Xylem reconnects (water and minerals can now flow).
  Step 5: Scion begins independent growth.

REQUIREMENTS FOR SUCCESS:
  1. Cambium alignment: the key. Cells that are not in contact cannot unite.
  2. Wound excluded from air and moisture loss (wax, grafting tape, parafilm).
  3. Stable connection: any movement breaks forming callus bridges → failure.
  4. Compatible species pairing (see incompatibility below).
  5. Temperature: 15–25°C optimal for callus formation.
```

## Rootstocks: What They Control

The rootstock's root system connects to the soil; its vascular system feeds the canopy. This gives rootstocks profound influence over the whole tree:

```
ROOTSTOCK EFFECTS:
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | SIZE CONTROL     | Dwarfing rootstocks produce smaller trees |
  |                  | by restricting water/nutrient delivery    |
  |                  | and through graft union restriction.      |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | PRECOCITY        | Dwarfing rootstocks induce earlier       |
  |                  | flowering: typically 1–2 years earlier.  |
  |                  | Important commercially: capital costs    |
  |                  | until first harvest are minimized.       |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | SOIL ADAPTATION  | Different rootstocks tolerate: wet soils,|
  |                  | dry soils, heavy clay, calcareous soils, |
  |                  | cold soils (varies by species).          |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | DISEASE RESIST.  | Phytophthora crown rot resistance,      |
  |                  | Replant disease tolerance, Fire blight  |
  |                  | resistance (some apple rootstocks).      |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | FRUIT QUALITY    | Indirect: tree vigor affects fruit size, |
  |                  | color development, sugar content.        |
  |                  | High-vigor rootstocks → large trees →   |
  |                  | excess shade → poor color development.   |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
```

### Apple Rootstock Example (M Series)

```
EAST MALLING ROOTSTOCK SERIES (UK, 1900s–present):

  Rootstock  | Tree size       | Precocity  | Support needed | Commercial use
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  M.27       | 20% of seedling | Very early | Yes (stake)    | Ultra-dwarf; research
  M.9        | 25–30%          | Early      | Yes (stake)    | Most commercial orchards
  M.26       | 35–45%          | Early      | Yes/sometimes  | Home/semi-commercial
  MM.106     | 50–65%          | Moderate   | Sometimes      | Larger operations
  MM.111     | 80–90%          | Late       | No             | Dry/poor soils
  Seedling   | 100% (full)     | Very late  | No             | Cider, traditional orchards

M.9 NOTE: M.9 is the backbone of modern intensive apple orchards worldwide.
  High-density planting: 800–1,200 trees/acre (vs. 40–100 on seedling).
  Each tree smaller; total yield per acre higher.
  But: requires stake/wire trellis system (roots too weak to anchor alone).
  The economics work because: precocity (bear in year 2–3 vs. year 7–10),
  ease of harvest, fruit quality (better light penetration), labor efficiency.
```

## Grafting Methods

### Whip-and-Tongue (Splice Graft)

```
WHIP-AND-TONGUE GRAFT:
  Best for: scion and rootstock of same or similar diameter (6–12mm)
  Used for: bench grafting (done off the tree, indoors in winter)
            grapes, ornamental trees, rootstock production

  CUT PROFILE:
  1. Make 45° cut through both scion and rootstock
  2. Make second cut to create interlocking tongue:
     From midpoint of first cut, cut parallel to grain direction
     halfway into the wood.

      Scion:           Rootstock:
      |\               /|
      | \             / |
      |  \====       |  |
      |   |   \===== |  |
      |   /   /      |  |
      |  / ==|        \ |
      | /    \         \|
      |/      |

  3. Interlock the tongues: the T-shape of scion fits into rootstock's slot.
     Cambium layers must align on at least one side (preferably both).
  4. Bind with grafting tape or parafilm.
  5. Seal with wax or leave parafilm as sealer.

  CAMBIUM ALIGNMENT IS CRITICAL:
  If scion diameter ≠ rootstock diameter: align ONE side only.
  The cambium is only 1–2 cells thick just inside the bark.
  "Line up the bark" is the practical instruction.
```

### Cleft Graft (for Top-working)

```
CLEFT GRAFT:
  Best for: topworking mature trees (replacing variety in old tree),
            large rootstock much bigger than scion
  Used for: re-grafting existing fruit trees to new varieties

  PROCEDURE:
  1. Saw off branch cleanly (any size, typically 20–80mm diameter).
  2. Split the stub through the center with cleaving tool.
  3. Wedge open the split with tool left in place.
  4. Prepare two scions: 6–8cm with 2–3 buds; base cut as
     thin wedge (outer side slightly thicker than inner).
  5. Insert one scion each side of split, cambium aligned.
  6. Remove wedge from split; clamping action holds scions.
  7. Wax all cut surfaces.

  RESULT: Typically both scions take initially.
  After 1 season: remove the weaker; leave strongest.

  TIMING: Late dormant season (buds just swelling).
          Rootstock must be actively beginning to grow;
          scion must still be fully dormant.
          "Scion dormant, rootstock active" = optimal.
```

### T-Budding (Shield Budding)

```
T-BUDDING (SHIELD BUDDING):
  The most widely used grafting method globally.
  Best for: roses, most stone fruits (peach, plum, cherry), citrus
  Timing: midsummer when bark "slips" (cambium actively growing,
          bark separates cleanly from wood)

  PROCEDURE:
  1. On rootstock: make T-shaped cut — vertical 2.5cm + horizontal cross-cut.
     Lift corners of T with budding knife handle.

  2. On scion wood: cut a single bud with shield of bark and thin
     wood slice attached. Cut from 1cm above bud, arc under,
     to 1cm below bud. Result: shield-shaped piece with bud.
     Remove wood slice from inside shield (optional; retained if thin).

  3. Insert shield into T: slide under bark flaps.
     Trim any protruding shield top flush with cross-cut.

  4. Wrap with budding rubber or parafilm below and above bud,
     leaving bud exposed.

  5. After 2–3 weeks: check for union.
     Living bud: plump, fresh green.
     Failed bud: shriveled, brown.

  6. After union confirmed: remove wrapping material.
     Cut rootstock above bud the following spring.
     The bud grows out to become the new canopy.
```

### Chip Budding

```
CHIP BUDDING:
  Used when bark does not slip (spring, fall).
  Similar to T-budding but removes a chip of both bark and wood.

  Rootstock: remove a chip (30–45° downward cut, then second cut
             meeting first → chip removed).
  Scion: take matching chip with bud included.
  Fit chip of scion into chip void of rootstock.
  Cambium alignment: at least one side.
  Wrap entirely (including bud — it can be wrapped; will push through).

  ADVANTAGE: Season-independent (doesn't require slipping bark).
  Used widely in: apple, pear, oak nursery production.
```

## Graft Incompatibility

Not all combinations of scion and rootstock are compatible. The mechanisms:

```
COMPATIBILITY RULES:
  1. Same species grafts are almost always compatible.
  2. Same genus, different species: usually compatible.
  3. Different genera: usually incompatible (exceptions exist).
  4. Different families: almost never compatible.

  Apple (Malus) onto pear (Pyrus) rootstock: incompatible.
  Pear (Pyrus) onto quince (Cydonia) rootstock: compatible — important
    commercially (quince provides dwarfing effect for pears).
  Tomato onto tomato rootstock: compatible.
  Tomato onto interspecific hybrid rootstock: compatible.

INCOMPATIBILITY SYMPTOMS:
  Immediate: union fails to form; scion dies.
  Delayed: union forms but is weak.
    Years later: shoot above union grows poorly; leaves yellow.
    Trees suddenly collapse at the union ("nursery run").
    Union examination: brown/necrotic tissue at junction.

MECHANISM:
  Protein-protein recognition: each plant's phloem proteins
  are species-specific. Incompatible combinations: phloem proteins
  precipitate at the union → vascular blockage.

INTERSTOCK SOLUTION:
  When A and C are incompatible but B is compatible with both:
  A (rootstock) → B (interstock, 10–20cm) → C (scion)
  A + B unite; B + C unite; C grows on double-grafted system.
  Used for: pear (C) on quince (A) when direct incompatibility exists.
    e.g., 'Conference' pear is incompatible with quince directly.
    Interstock of compatible pear variety solves this.
```

## Grafted Vegetables

Vegetable grafting has become standard practice in Europe and Asia and is growing in North America:

```
WHY GRAFT VEGETABLES:
  Problem: soilborne diseases (Fusarium, Verticillium, nematodes)
           accumulate in field soil after years of production.
           Soilborne pathogen fumigation (methyl bromide) phased out.

  Solution: graft susceptible scion (flavorful variety) onto
            vigorous rootstock with disease resistance.

TARGET CROPS:
  Tomato: most important grafted vegetable crop globally.
    Rootstocks: 'Arnold', 'Maxifort', 'Big Power', etc. (interspecific hybrids
    Solanum lycopersicum × S. habrochaites or S. peruvianum).
    Benefits: Fusarium/Verticillium resistance, nematode resistance,
    vigor increase (50–100% yield increase in infested soils),
    water/nutrient uptake efficiency.

  Eggplant (aubergine): similar rootstock logic.
  Pepper: less common but used where soilborne disease is severe.
  Watermelon: squash or gourd rootstocks for crown rot and nematode resistance.
    Major in East Asia; spreading to Europe/US.
  Cucumber: cucurbit rootstocks.

CLEFT GRAFT METHOD FOR TOMATO (typical commercial method):
  At cotyledon stage (before first true leaves):
  Rootstock: decapitated at cotyledon, 30–40° cleft cut.
  Scion: small wedge cut at base.
  Insert scion into cleft; hold with grafting clip.
  High humidity chamber 5–7 days (prevents wilting while union forms).
  Then transition to normal conditions.

SCALE: European Union: >200 million grafted vegetable transplants/year.
  Japan: >400 million. Cost: $0.50–$1.50 per plant (vs. $0.10–0.20 ungrafted).
  Economically justified when soilborne disease is severe.
```

---

Graft compatibility follows interface compatibility rules: same genus usually works (intra-genus grafting — apple on apple, tomato on tomato); same family is hit-or-miss (inter-genus within family — some Rosaceae cross-genus grafts work, most don't); different family almost never works. The analogy to API contract compatibility is tight: shared interface definition (same genus = shared biochemical signaling vocabulary) permits integration; divergent interfaces (different family = incompatible phytohormone and nutrient transport signals) produces graft failure even if both components are individually healthy. Incompatibility can be immediate (union never forms) or delayed (union forms, then breaks down after 2–5 years as the biochemical conflict accumulates) — exactly like an API integration that passes unit tests but fails under production load when implicit contract assumptions are violated.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Grafting Method | Why |
|-----------|----------------|-----|
| Bench grafting rootstock (similar diameter) | Whip-and-tongue | Best cambium contact, interlocking |
| Top-working mature tree (large stub) | Cleft graft | Handles large diameter; two scions inserted |
| Summer budding roses and stone fruits | T-budding (shield budding) | Fast; works when bark slips |
| Budding when bark is not slipping | Chip budding | Season-independent |
| Patching bark damage from rodents | Bridge graft | Bypass the damaged zone |
| Tomato in soilborne-disease-infested soil | Cleft graft onto vigorous rootstock | Disease resistance + vigor |

## Common Confusion Points

**Rootstock controls the roots; scion controls the fruit**: the fruit variety's genetics determine flavor, color, and other fruit characteristics. The rootstock controls tree size, soil adaptation, and disease resistance — but not flavor. A Honeycrisp apple on M.9 and Honeycrisp on MM.111 taste the same; the trees are very different sizes.

**Cambium is invisible but everything**: the cambium is one to two cells thick, just inside the bark. You can't see it; you align it by aligning the bark. Any cut that goes deeply into wood has gone past the cambium. The practical instruction is: "cut thinly" and "align the edges of the bark, not the wood."

**Grafting cannot cross the family barrier**: tomato can be grafted onto related Solanum species. It cannot be grafted onto pepper or cucumber (different genera within Solanaceae) reliably, and certainly not onto a completely unrelated species. The family boundary is real.

**Delayed incompatibility is a clinical diagnostic problem**: a graft union that looks fine for 3–5 years before failing represents a significant economic loss for an orchard grower. Testing combinations for delayed incompatibility requires long-term field trials — exactly the kind of expensive, slow, difficult experiment that the industry often skips. Many incompatibility problems are discovered by growers rather than researchers.

**Virus transmission through grafting is guaranteed**: grafting two plants creates a shared vascular system. Any systemic virus present in either partner will spread to both within weeks. This is why certified virus-free rootstock and scion wood is required in commercial tree fruit production, and why tissue culture virus elimination precedes the certified stock production system.
