# Pruning, Training, and Espalier

## The Big Picture

Pruning and training are the management of plant architecture to achieve specific goals: light distribution, fruit quality, structural safety, space limitation, or aesthetic form. The physiology is predictable once you understand the mechanisms.

```
PRUNING OBJECTIVES AND METHODS:

  OBJECTIVE              METHOD                      MECHANISM
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Increase branching     Heading cut (shorten)        Remove apical dominance
  Reduce branching       Thinning cut (remove)        Redirect growth to fewer shoots
  Restrict size          Both + root pruning           Reduce photosynthetic + absorptive area
  Improve fruit quality  Thinning cuts + fruit thin    Improve light; reduce competition
  Remove dead/diseased   Thinning cuts below wound    Compartmentalization (CODIT model)
  Shape espalier         Selective heading + tying     Redirect growth by orientation
  Rejuvenate old tree    Heavy thinning + watersprout  Remove old wood; stimulate renewal
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE:
  Every pruning cut removes stored carbohydrates and
  redirects the plant's growth response.
  The art is in predicting and directing that response.
```

## Physiology of Pruning

### Apical Dominance

```
APICAL DOMINANCE MECHANISM:
  The apical meristem (shoot tip) produces auxin (IAA).
  Auxin moves basipetally (downward) through the stem.
  High auxin concentration in upper stem suppresses lateral buds below.
  Lower in the plant: auxin concentration lower → lateral buds less suppressed.

  RESULT: A vertical shoot grows upward with lateral branches
          only lower on the stem where auxin concentration is lower.
          Strong apical dominance: narrow, columnar growth (conifers).
          Weak apical dominance: spreading growth (many shrubs).

REMOVE THE APEX:
  Cut the shoot tip → auxin source removed.
  Lateral buds below the cut: no longer suppressed.
  Multiple laterals grow → bushy growth response.

EFFECT OF CUT POSITION:
  Cut just above a bud: that bud becomes the new apical shoot.
  Cut partway between buds: the nearest bud below becomes dominant.
  Cut entirely removes the shoot: growth redirected to laterals lower down.
```

### Heading vs. Thinning Cuts

```
HEADING CUT:
  Remove only part of a branch or shoot — cut it shorter.
  Effect: removes apical dominance of that branch.
          Stimulates lateral branches below the cut.
          Makes the plant bushier in that zone.
  Use for: hedges, shaping, encouraging lower branching,
           controlling height in the current season.

      Before:          After heading:
      |                |
      |                +--> new lateral
      |                |
      |                +--> new lateral
      v                |
  (cut here)           (cut point)

THINNING CUT:
  Remove the entire branch back to its origin point (parent branch or trunk).
  Effect: redirects growth to remaining shoots.
          Does not stimulate new laterals (no lateral buds left to respond).
          Opens the plant up (more light inside canopy).
  Use for: opening center, removing crossing branches, removing diseased wood.

      Before:    After thinning:
      |  \       |
      |   \      |  (entire branch removed to origin)
      |    \     |
      |     v    |
  (cut here)
```

## Pruning Timing

### Dormant vs. Summer Pruning

```
DORMANT PRUNING (late winter, before bud break):
  Why winter: no leaves, so full architecture is visible.
               Plant is at lowest energy demand (dormant).
               Disease exposure is minimal (dormant season pathogens less active).

  GROWTH RESPONSE: large (deinvigorating).
  Reason: all carbohydrate stored in roots is available for the
          remaining buds. Fewer buds → each gets more resources.
          Heavy dormant pruning → vigorous regrowth.

  In fruit trees: dormant pruning is "deinvigorating" — but if you
  over-prune a mature tree, you stimulate vegetative growth
  at the expense of fruiting. The response is vigorous new shoots
  (waterspouts), which are unproductive for years.

SUMMER PRUNING (June–August for Northern Hemisphere):
  Effect: REDUCES vigor (inverts the normal effect).
  Reason: removes active leaves = removes current photosynthesis.
          Reduces carbohydrate production during the season.
          Reduces energy available for next year's growth.

  USES:
    Size control in vigorous trees (summer pruning reduces
    regrowth the following year more than dormant pruning).
    Color development in apple/grape: removing summer shade leaves
    allows light to red-pigment fruit surface.
    Espalier maintenance: remove shoots growing out of the plane.

PRUNING WOUND TIMING AND PATHOGENS:
  Avoid pruning in wet weather (disease spores spread in water).
  Prunus (cherry, plum): do NOT prune in fall (bacterial canker risk).
    Best in midsummer (rapid wound closure + dry conditions).
  Oak: do NOT prune March–July in oak wilt zones (Ceratocystis quercus risk).
    Best in winter.
  Most other species: dormant season is standard and safe.
```

## CODIT Model: How Trees Respond to Wounds

Alex Shigo's **Compartmentalization Of Decay In Trees** (CODIT) model, 1970s–80s, transformed understanding of pruning wounds:

```
CODIT PRINCIPLE:
  Trees do not "heal" wounds. They COMPARTMENTALIZE them.
  The tree walls off the wound zone and grows new wood around it.
  The old infected wood remains inside; it is walled off, not replaced.

FOUR WALLS OF COMPARTMENTALIZATION:
  Wall 1: Plugs vascular tissue above and below wound (xylem).
           Strongest wall. Stops vertical spread.

  Wall 2: Rings of wood formed before the wound. Dense heartwood boundary.
           Stops spread radially inward.

  Wall 3: Growth rings (annual ring boundaries).
           Stops lateral spread within a growth ring.

  Wall 4: The BARRIER ZONE — new wood formed after wounding.
           Most important wall. Formed outward from the wound.
           Strong; resists both fungal penetration and chemical breakdown.

IMPLICATION FOR PRUNING:
  Correct cut: at the "branch collar" (the swelling where branch meets trunk).
  The collar contains the barrier zone tissue.
  Flush cut: removes the collar → no barrier zone tissue remains.
             Decay enters trunk and cannot be walled off.
             FLUSH CUTS ARE WRONG.

  CORRECT CUT ANATOMY:
                    branch collar
                   /
  TRUNK  ---------( )-----  BRANCH
                   ^
                   Cut here: outside the collar, not flush.
                   Collar tissue remains; can form barrier zone.
```

### Wound Sealants

```
WOUND PAINT / PRUNING SEAL:
  Historical advice: paint wounds with tar, creosote, or latex paint.
  Shigo's research showed: wound paints do NOT speed healing.
                           Some paints actually inhibit callus formation.
                           Trees compartmentalize perfectly well without paint.
  CURRENT CONSENSUS: Do not use wound paint.
  Exception: pruning on susceptible species during high disease pressure
  (e.g., Cytospora canker on spruce, fire blight on apple) may benefit
  from specific fungicide/bactericide wound treatment. Not cosmetic sealant.
```

## Fruit Tree Training Systems

Different training systems optimize for different goals:

```
CENTRAL LEADER:
  Main trunk (leader) grows vertically through tree.
  Scaffold branches horizontal at regular intervals.
  Tree has Christmas-tree shape.
  Light: good (branches angled horizontally, light intercepts from above).
  Size: medium to large.
  Used for: Christmas trees, traditional apple/pear, pine.

  ------  scaffold 1 (widest)
     |
  ----  scaffold 2
     |
  --  scaffold 3 (narrowest, highest)
     |
     | (central leader continues)
     |

MODIFIED CENTRAL LEADER:
  As central leader, but top is headed at desired height.
  Scaffolds continue at apex; central leader not dominant.
  Compromise between central leader and open center.
  Used for: most modern apple orchards before intensive systems.

OPEN CENTER (VASE):
  No central leader. Three to five scaffold branches diverge
  from a central point, creating an open bowl shape.
  Advantages: excellent light to center of tree; easy to harvest.
  Disadvantages: heavy crops can split the crotch; structural risk.
  Used for: peach, plum, nectarine, almond (stone fruits).
             Stone fruits have strong apical dominance → open center
             works well because each scaffold becomes its own leader.

      /  \
     /    \
    /      \
   |  (open)|
   |  center|

SPINDLE (TALL SPINDLE):
  Very high-density planting (>1,000 trees/acre) on dwarfing rootstocks.
  Tree: narrow (1–1.5m wide); tall (3–4m).
  Fruiting branches: lateral branches allowed to hang; removed after 3-5 years.
  Renewal: continuous removal of old branches; replaced by young productive wood.
  Light interception: maximum (trees planted in rows, sunlight hits entire row).
  Used for: commercial apple, pear (M.9 rootstock).
  Requires: expensive support system (pole and wire trellis per tree or per row).
```

## Espalier

Espalier = training a plant against a flat plane (wall, trellis, fence). The word comes from Italian *spalliera* (from *spalla* = shoulder, the structure supported against a wall).

```
ESPALIER FORMS:

  HORIZONTAL CORDON (formal):
  |         |         |    ← branches trained horizontally
  |         |         |
  +---------+---------+    ← main stem
  |
  trunk vertical

  Used for: apple, pear. Spacing: 40–50cm between tiers.
  Training: tie developing branches to wire, maintaining horizontal angle.
            Vertical shoots on horizontal branches: remove.

  SINGLE CORDON:
  Just the vertical stem, trained flat.
  Fruiting spurs develop along the vertical.
  Used for: gooseberry, currant, apples in very limited space.

  FAN (FAN ESPALIER):
  Main stem short; branches radiate at ~30° angles.
    /||||||\
   / || || \
  /  ||  |  \
  Used for: peach, cherry, fig — crops that do better against warm wall.
            Wall provides radiated heat; fan maximizes exposure.

  PALMETTE (formal):
  Like horizontal cordon but branches angled at ~45°.
  More decorative than horizontal cordon.

  BELGIAN FENCE (multiple trees):
  Each tree trained as diagonal cordon.
  Multiple trees in a row: form a diamond lattice pattern.
  Living geometry; impressive visual effect.
```

### Wall Microclimates

```
WALL MICROCLIMATE BENEFITS:
  Radiant heat: stone/brick wall absorbs solar energy during day;
                radiates at night. Can extend season 2–4 weeks.
  Frost protection: thermal mass buffers temperature drops.
  Drier: walls shed rain; good for crops susceptible to fungal disease.
  Light reflection: south-facing walls (N. hemisphere) redirect additional light.

  This is why in Britain, walls were built specifically to train
  peaches, nectarines, apricots, and figs — species that are
  marginally hardy or require more warmth to ripen fruit than
  the ambient climate provides.
  Walled kitchen gardens of English country houses: the wall
  is the primary production technology, not just a support.
```

## Grape Vine Training

Grapevines require careful training to balance vegetative and reproductive growth:

```
GUYOT TRAINING:
  Single Guyot: one cane + one spur.
  Double Guyot: two canes + two spurs (most common in Bordeaux).

  SINGLE GUYOT ANATOMY:
    +--- CANE (this year's fruiting wood, 6–12 buds)
    |
  --+
    |
    +--- SPUR (2 buds — will produce next year's cane)

  DORMANT SEASON (each winter):
    Previous cane: remove entirely (it has fruited; done).
    One shoot from the spur: becomes new cane; tie down horizontal.
    Other shoot from the spur: head back to 2 buds → new spur.
    Repeat annually. The vine framework is permanent;
    the fruiting wood is renewed every year.

VIGOR BALANCE:
  Strong rootstock: many shoots, excessive leaf area.
  Weak rootstock: poor growth, low yield.
  The training system must match the rootstock vigor.
  "Vigor balance": canopy size matched to root system capacity.
  Balanced vines: dark green (not yellow = N deficient; not yellow-green
  = too vigorous), moderate shoot growth (15–25 nodes per shoot).

LEAF AREA INDEX (LAI):
  Total leaf area / ground area covered.
  Optimal LAI for wine grapes: 1.5–3.
  Too high LAI: shade inside canopy → poor fruit quality.
  Too low LAI: insufficient photosynthesis → poor ripening.
  Training system, shoot density, and leaf removal all manage LAI.
```

## Rose Pruning

Different rose classes require different approaches:

```
HYBRID TEA ROSES:
  Objective: maximum large blooms on long stems.
  When: late winter (just before bud break); after threat of hard frost.
  Method: Remove dead wood; remove crossing branches; remove thin canes.
          Head remaining canes to 30–40cm from ground.
          3–5 healthy outward-facing canes is the target framework.
          Extreme: remove all canes to 15cm → maximum rejuvenation.

SHRUB ROSES:
  Objective: natural form, continuous flowering.
  Method: Remove dead wood; light shaping; "one-third" rule:
          remove up to one-third of oldest canes annually.
          No heavy heading; maintain natural arching habit.

CLIMBING ROSES:
  Objective: maximize lateral flowering along long canes.
  Training: tie long canes horizontally (reduces apical dominance
            on the cane → lateral flowering shoots develop all along).
  Pruning: remove spent laterals; remove old canes after 3–4 years.

DEADHEADING (all types):
  Remove spent flowers to prevent energy going to seed development.
  Stimulates next flush of flowers.
  Method: cut back to a strong leaf node (not to base of flower stem).
  Not required on: once-blooming roses (only one flush regardless);
  species roses (hips are ornamental/food value).
```

## Hedge Trimming and Topiary

```
HEDGE TIMING:
  Most formal hedges: trim twice per year (late spring and late summer).
  Spring trim: removes new growth; defines the formal shape.
  Summer trim: removes regrowth; maintains shape through fall.

  NOT IN LATE FALL: new growth will not harden before frost.

BATTER (angled face):
  Formal hedges should be wider at base than top.
  "Batter" = 5–15° inward slope from base to top.
  Reason: allows light to reach lower leaves (prevents bottom die-out).
  Vertical face: lower portion shaded by upper → bottom becomes bare.

TOPIARY:
  Shaping into geometric or figurative forms.
  Traditional subjects: cones, spheres, spirals, animal forms.
  Species requirement: small-leafed, dense, tolerates repeated clipping.
    Best: Buxus (boxwood), Taxus (yew), Ilex (holly), Cupressus, Ligustrum.
  Timing: same as hedges; more frequent for precise forms.
  Tools: hand shears + template (wire form to guide cuts) for complex shapes.
  CAUTION: Buxus blight (Calonectria pseudonaviculata): devastating in Europe/N. America.
    Fungicide program required; consider Ilex or Taxus alternatives.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Cut Type | Timing | Result |
|------|---------|--------|--------|
| Make plant bushier | Heading cut (shorten shoot) | Any time; most effective spring | New laterals below cut |
| Open plant interior | Thinning cut (remove to origin) | Dormant season | Better light; no new lateral flush |
| Control tree size | Summer pruning (remove shoots) | June–August | Reduces growth NEXT season |
| Increase fruit color | Remove shading leaves around fruit | 3–4 weeks before harvest | Allows light to fruit surface |
| Train espalier tier | Heading + horizontal tying | After terminal bud set | New lateral extends horizontal arm |
| Remove diseased branch | Thinning cut below infection | Dry weather; appropriate season for species | Remove infection; collar preserved |
| Rejuvenate old hedge | Heavy cutting back (renovation) | Early spring | Stimulates from old wood (species dependent) |

---

**Resource budget framing**: a mature tree in late winter holds a fixed carbon reserve (stored carbohydrates in roots, trunk, and scaffold branches). Every dormant bud is a potential consumer of that reserve. Pruning removes consumers — each removed bud means more reserve per remaining bud, driving more vigorous growth from the survivors. This is identical to capacity planning: reduce the number of active consumers and per-consumer throughput increases, proportionally. The whole-tree response to heavy pruning (invigoration) follows directly from this model. Conversely, a tree carrying excessive crop load (too many fruit) is an oversubscribed resource pool — each fruit is undersized because the fixed carbohydrate supply is divided too many ways. Thinning fruit is load shedding.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Flush cuts" are taught in old references and wrong**: many older pruning guides (pre-1980s) recommend cutting flush with the trunk. Shigo's research definitively showed this destroys the collar and prevents compartmentalization. Cut outside the collar.

**Summer pruning reduces vigor; dormant pruning often increases it**: this is counterintuitive. Dormant pruning of vigorous trees may make them MORE vigorous (fewer buds, more resource per bud). Summer pruning, by removing active leaves, reduces photosynthate available for next year's growth.

**Rose pruning timing varies by climate**: "prune roses in late February" is UK/Pacific Northwest advice. In cold climates (Minnesota), you wait until after the last frost. In warm climates (Texas, California), you may prune in January. The rule is: prune when buds are beginning to swell but frost risk has passed.

**Espalier is high-maintenance**: the visual payoff is high; so is the labor input. A formally trained horizontal cordon apple requires 3–4 pruning/training operations per year (dormant, and two to three summer sessions) to maintain the form. Plan accordingly before committing a wall to espalier.

**Topiary with boxwood carries disease risk**: Buxus blight (Calonectria pseudonaviculata) spread across the UK and Europe from the 2000s onward; in North America from the 2010s. Traditional boxwood topiary is now high-risk. Consider Ilex crenata (Japanese holly) or Taxus (yew) as alternatives with similar appearance and cut-tolerance.
