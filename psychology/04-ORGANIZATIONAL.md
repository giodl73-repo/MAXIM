# Organizational Psychology

## The Big Picture

Industrial-Organizational (I-O) psychology applies psychological science to work
contexts. It has the most rigorous empirical tradition in applied psychology —
Schmidt and Hunter's meta-analytic work on selection validity is among the
most policy-relevant research in social science. If you hire people, lead teams,
or design work systems, this field's findings directly apply.

## Section 1: Selection Validity — What Predicts Job Performance?

Schmidt and Hunter's 1998 and 2016 meta-analyses are the foundational evidence base.
These represent ~100 years of research on thousands of studies.

### Predictor Validity Summary

```
  SCHMIDT & HUNTER META-ANALYSIS (validity coefficients, operational validity):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┐
  │ Predictor                            │ Validity (r with job perf) │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ GMA (General Mental Ability)         │ 0.51 (single best predictor│
  │   — cognitive ability tests          │ across all jobs)            │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Structured interviews                │ 0.58 (situational/behavioral│
  │   — behavioral + situational         │ with standardized scoring)  │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Work sample tests                    │ 0.54 (actual task performance│
  │   — test actual job tasks            │ high face validity)         │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Job knowledge tests                  │ 0.48 (for experienced)      │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Integrity tests                      │ 0.41                        │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Conscientiousness (Big Five)         │ 0.24                        │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Reference checks                     │ 0.26 (employment reference) │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Unstructured interviews              │ 0.20                        │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Years of education                   │ 0.10                        │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Years of experience                  │ 0.18 (quickly diminishing   │
  │                                      │ returns after 5 years)      │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ Graphology (handwriting analysis)    │ ~0.00 (no validity)         │
  └──────────────────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────┘

  OPTIMAL BATTERY (additive validity):
  GMA + Structured Interview ≈ r~0.65 with job performance
  GMA + Work Sample ≈ r~0.64
  Adding more predictors provides diminishing returns
```

### Why GMA Is So Predictive

```
  GMA MECHANISM:
  More complex jobs → more training required → GMA predicts more
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Job complexity:       Low    Medium    High                    │
  │ GMA validity:         0.23   0.40      0.58                   │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  GMA → Job Knowledge Acquisition → Job Performance
  GMA works through faster learning and more flexible problem-solving
  Not about test-taking; it's about the cognitive work of the job itself

  LEGAL/ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS:
  GMA tests have adverse impact (group differences in scores)
  → Disparate impact concern under Title VII
  → But the test is valid (job-related and consistent with business necessity)
  → Courts have upheld valid selection tests (Griggs, Watson, Ricci)
  Typical tradeoff: higher validity tests often have higher adverse impact
  → Structured interviews reduce adverse impact somewhat while maintaining validity
```

## Section 2: Structured Interviews

```
  WHY STRUCTURE MATTERS:
  Unstructured interview validity: r~0.20
  Structured interview validity: r~0.58 (nearly 3× more predictive!)

  Unstructured interview fails because:
  - Questions vary per candidate → incomparable responses
  - Halo effects (attractive candidate gets easier questions)
  - Confirmation bias (form impression early → seek confirming info)
  - Social similarity bias (like people similar to interviewer)
  - Contrast effects (following a weak candidate makes you look better)

  STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS THAT INCREASE VALIDITY:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Same questions for all candidates (standardized)               │
  │ Questions derived from job analysis                            │
  │ Anchored rating scales (BARS — Behaviorally Anchored)         │
  │ Multiple interviewers (panel vs single)                        │
  │ No prompting or follow-up beyond standardized probes           │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  TWO QUESTION TYPES:
  BEHAVIORAL: "Tell me about a time when..."
  → Past behavior predicts future behavior
  → STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
  → Hard to fake (requires actual experience)

  SITUATIONAL: "What would you do if..."
  → Hypothetical scenarios; tests judgment
  → Good for less experienced candidates (no track record)
  → Based on critical incidents from job analysis

  OPTIMAL: COMBINATION of behavioral + situational
```

## Section 3: Job Performance Models

### Campbell's Model (1990)

```
  EIGHT JOB PERFORMANCE DIMENSIONS:
  1. Task-specific performance (core technical work)
  2. Non-task-specific performance (cross-team, organization-wide)
  3. Written/oral communication performance
  4. Demonstrating effort (persistence, working hard)
  5. Maintaining personal discipline (non-counterproductive)
  6. Facilitating peer/team performance
  7. Supervision/leadership (for supervisors)
  8. Management/administration (for managers)

  DETERMINANTS OF PERFORMANCE (Campbell's model):
  Knowledge (K) × Skill (S) × Motivation (M) = Performance
  → Selection affects K and S
  → Training affects K and S
  → Job design, management affect M
  → Can't compensate poor ability with high motivation beyond a point
```

### OCB — Organizational Citizenship Behavior

```
  ORGAN (1988): Behaviors beyond formal role that contribute to effectiveness
  Not required; not formally rewarded; voluntarily given

  DIMENSIONS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Altruism: helping coworkers with work-related problems         │
  │ Conscientiousness: going beyond minimum requirements           │
  │ Sportsmanship: tolerating inconveniences without complaining   │
  │ Courtesy: preventing work problems; giving advance notice      │
  │ Civic virtue: participating in and concerning oneself with     │
  │               the organization's governance and functions      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  OCB PREDICTORS: Job satisfaction, organizational commitment,
  leader-member exchange (LMX), personality (agreeableness, conscientiousness)

  OCB OUTCOMES: Team performance, customer satisfaction, unit productivity
  → OCB contributes ~50-75% as much as task performance to unit effectiveness

  COUNTERPRODUCTIVE WORK BEHAVIOR (CWB):
  Opposite: theft, sabotage, incivility, presenteeism, withdrawal
  Best predictors: low agreeableness, low conscientiousness, high neuroticism,
  low organizational justice perceptions (unfairness triggers CWB)
```

## Section 4: Burnout

### Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI)

```
  MASLACH (1981): Burnout = THREE DIMENSIONS (not just tiredness)

  EXHAUSTION:
  Emotional and physical depletion; "I feel emotionally drained by my work"
  → The energy dimension of burnout; most central

  CYNICISM / DEPERSONALIZATION:
  Psychological distance from the work, job, or recipients
  Detached, indifferent, "going through the motions"
  → Protective mechanism against exhaustion (but maladaptive)

  REDUCED PROFESSIONAL EFFICACY:
  Feelings of incompetence and lack of achievement
  "I feel like I'm not making a difference"

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ BURNOUT (negative pole)      ENGAGEMENT (positive pole)        │
  │ Exhaustion ←────────────────→ Vigor (energy, resilience)      │
  │ Cynicism ←──────────────────→ Dedication (significance, pride) │
  │ Reduced efficacy ←──────────→ Absorption (fully immersed)     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  Engagement is not just "not burned out" — it's the positive state

  BURNOUT PREDICTORS:
  - Work overload (demands exceed capacity)
  - Lack of control/autonomy
  - Insufficient reward (financial, social, intrinsic)
  - Breakdown in community (isolation, conflict)
  - Absence of fairness
  - Values mismatch (personal values vs organizational)
  (Maslach's six areas of work life)
```

## Section 5: Job Demands-Resources Model (JD-R)

```
  BAKKER & DEMEROUTI (2007):

  TWO PROCESSES OPERATING SIMULTANEOUSLY:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ HEALTH IMPAIRMENT PROCESS (negative):                          │
  │ Job Demands → Depletion of resources → Exhaustion → Health    │
  │                                                                │
  │ Job Demands: Physical (manual labor), Cognitive (complexity),  │
  │ Emotional (patient care, customer service), Interpersonal     │
  │ (conflict), Work Pace, Uncertainty                             │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ MOTIVATIONAL PROCESS (positive):                               │
  │ Job Resources → Intrinsic Motivation → Engagement → Performance│
  │                                                                │
  │ Job Resources: Autonomy, Social support (supervisor/peer),     │
  │ Performance feedback, Skill variety, Task significance,        │
  │ Developmental opportunities, Role clarity                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY INSIGHT: Resources BUFFER the effect of demands on strain
  High demands + high resources: engaged (challenge demands)
  High demands + low resources: burned out (hindrance demands)

  JOB CRAFTING:
  Employees proactively shape their own jobs to align with strengths/interests
  Three forms: increase resources (seek feedback, develop skills)
               increase challenge demands (take on stretch assignments)
               decrease hindrance demands (reduce draining tasks)
  Evidence: job crafting → engagement → performance
  Managerial implication: encourage bottom-up job redesign
```

## Section 6: Performance Management

```
  RATING BIASES (systematic errors in performance ratings):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ HALO EFFECT: One positive attribute colors all ratings        │
  │ → Technically excellent → rated high on teamwork too          │
  │                                                                │
  │ LENIENCY/SEVERITY: Rater consistently rates all high or low   │
  │ → Distribution of ratings compressed; poor differentiation    │
  │                                                                │
  │ CENTRAL TENDENCY: Rater avoids extremes                       │
  │ → All ratings near the middle; top performers underdistinguished│
  │                                                                │
  │ RECENCY: Recent events dominate over full period              │
  │ → Last 3 months = "entire year" in rater's mind               │
  │                                                                │
  │ SIMILAR-TO-ME: Rate higher those who resemble rater           │
  │ → Demographic bias pathway                                     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  360-DEGREE FEEDBACK:
  Multiple rater sources: self, supervisor, peers, direct reports, customers
  Research findings: self-ratings least accurate; peer ratings most valid for
  interpersonal skills; supervisor most valid for task performance
  360 for development ≠ 360 for performance evaluation (mixing purposes ≠ good)

  CALIBRATION SESSIONS:
  Cross-manager discussion to align rating standards
  Reduces leniency, central tendency, and within-manager inconsistency
  Increases differentiation; surfaces high performers + low performers
  Essential for any merit-based system

  CONTINUOUS FEEDBACK vs ANNUAL REVIEW:
  Research: continuous check-ins → higher engagement, faster development
  Annual reviews: correlation with engagement negative in some studies
  Practice has moved toward more frequent feedback (quarterly or ongoing)
  BUT: annual structured calibration still necessary for fair differentiation
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Selection Challenge | Evidence-Based Approach |
|---|---|
| Most accurate predictor overall | GMA cognitive test + structured behavioral interview |
| Low-experience candidates | Situational interview + work sample (no behavioral history) |
| Reduce adverse impact while maintaining validity | Structured interviews (less adverse impact than GMA alone) |
| Assess leadership potential | GMA + structured interview with leadership scenarios + Conscientiousness + Extraversion |
| Reduce burnout | JD-R model: increase job resources (autonomy, support, feedback) |
| Improve engagement | Job crafting + resources + challenging work + clear goals |
| More accurate performance ratings | Training on errors + behavioral anchors + calibration |
| Reference checks that are valid | Structured reference check (same questions, rating scales) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Structured interviews are not rigid scripts**: the questions are standardized, but
the interviewer still builds rapport, listens carefully, and probes naturally within
the standard structure. "Structured" means consistent, not robotic.

**Years of experience has diminishing returns fast**: experience validity of r~0.18
already starts declining for jobs requiring 5+ years. Someone with 10 years vs 5 years
of experience is not predictably better. What matters is deliberate practice and
knowledge acquisition within that experience, not the years themselves.

**OCB can become expected**: initially voluntary OCB behaviors can be incorporated
into formal job requirements by organizations over time. The motivational properties
change once it's required. Burnout risk increases if OCBs become unacknowledged burdens.

**Burnout and depression overlap but are different**: both involve exhaustion and
reduced interest. But burnout is situational (work-specific, improves on vacation),
while depression is pervasive (all life domains, doesn't improve reliably with rest).
The distinction has treatment implications — job change may resolve burnout; clinical
depression requires clinical intervention.
