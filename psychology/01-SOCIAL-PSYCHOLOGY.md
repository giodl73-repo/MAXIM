# Social Psychology

## The Big Picture

Social psychology studies how the presence of others — real, imagined, or implied —
affects individual behavior and mental processes. Its most famous findings demonstrate
that situational factors overwhelm dispositional ones far more than people expect.
The core lesson: never underestimate context.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    SOCIAL INFLUENCE LANDSCAPE                        |
|                                                                      |
|  CONFORMITY              OBEDIENCE             ATTITUDE CHANGE       |
```

## Section 1: Conformity

### Asch's Line Studies (1951)

```
  SETUP:
  Participant in group of "confederates" (actors)
  Task: match line to one of three comparison lines (OBVIOUS correct answer)
  Confederates unanimously give WRONG answer

  RESULTS: 37% of critical trials: naive participant conformed to wrong answer
  25% never conformed; ~75% conformed at least once

  TWO MECHANISMS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ INFORMATIONAL SOCIAL INFLUENCE (ISI):                          │
  │ Others' behavior is informative about reality                  │
  │ "If everyone says C, maybe I'm wrong"                          │
  │ Stronger in: ambiguous situations, uncertainty, domain experts │
  │                                                                │
  │ NORMATIVE SOCIAL INFLUENCE (NSI):                              │
  │ Conform to fit in, avoid rejection, gain approval              │
  │ Operates even when you KNOW the group is wrong                 │
  │ Stronger in: public responses, close-knit groups               │
  │ Weaker in: anonymous responding                                │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MODERATING FACTORS:
  - Group unanimity: one ally → conformity drops dramatically
  - Group size: plateaus at ~3-4 people (adding more doesn't increase conformity)
  - Anonymity: written responses → lower conformity than spoken
  - Culture: collectivist societies show higher conformity (Bond/Smith meta-analysis)

  MINORITY INFLUENCE (Moscovici 1980):
  Minorities can change majorities — but requires CONSISTENCY
  A consistent minority (always gives same answer) → eventually shifts majority
  Mechanism: creates doubt, promotes systematic thinking about the position
```

## Section 2: Obedience to Authority

### Milgram's Obedience Studies (1963)

```
  SETUP:
  Teacher (naive participant) administers electric shocks to Learner (actor)
  for wrong answers; experimenter (authority) instructs to continue
  Shock generator: 15V (slight) to 450V (XXX - Danger Severe Shock)
  Learner protests, then screams, then silent at 300V+

  RESULT: 65% of participants delivered maximum 450V shock
  Replicated across variations; consistent cross-culturally

  SITUATIONAL FACTORS THAT EXPLAIN COMPLIANCE:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. LEGITIMATE AUTHORITY: Yale University, experimenter in lab  │
  │    coat; institutional legitimacy makes authority seem justified│
  │                                                                │
  │ 2. INCREMENTAL ESCALATION: Started at 15V; each step only 15V  │
  │    more; foot-in-the-door; commitment compounds                │
  │                                                                │
  │ 3. PROXIMITY TO AUTHORITY: Experimenter present → 65%          │
  │    Experimenter by phone → ~20%                                │
  │                                                                │
  │ 4. DISTANCE FROM VICTIM: Learner behind wall → 65%             │
  │    Learner in same room → ~40%                                 │
  │    Physical contact required → ~30%                            │
  │                                                                │
  │ 5. DIFFUSION OF RESPONSIBILITY:                                │
  │    "I'm just following orders"; experimenter takes moral burden│
  │                                                                │
  │ 6. AGENTIC STATE: Shift from autonomous agent to executing     │
  │    another's will; lower self-monitoring of moral implications │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  BURGER (2009) PARTIAL REPLICATION:
  Stopped at 150V (the point where learner first demands release)
  ~70% went to 150V (comparable to Milgram)
  Ethical stopping point; extrapolation to 450V uncertain

  REAL-WORLD ANALOGS: Abu Ghraib, Holocaust perpetrators (ordinary police,
  Browning "Ordinary Men"), corporate fraud under authority pressure
```

## Section 3: Bystander Effect

```
  KITTY GENOVESE MYTH:
  1964 New York murder; NYT reported 38 witnesses, none called police
  Later research: reporting was wrong; ~2 witnesses; some did call police
  BUT: Darley/Latané (1968) used the story to design real experiments

  DARLEY/LATANÉ (1968): "SEIZURE" EXPERIMENT
  Participant hears what sounds like another subject having a seizure
  Alone: 85% intervened within 2 minutes
  With 5 other "bystanders" (heard on intercom): only 31% intervened

  TWO MECHANISMS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ PLURALISTIC IGNORANCE:                                         │
  │ Each bystander looks to others for cues on how to react        │
  │ Others also appear calm (masking their own uncertainty)        │
  │ → Collective definition: "This must not be an emergency"       │
  │                                                                │
  │ DIFFUSION OF RESPONSIBILITY:                                   │
  │ With others present, individual responsibility decreases       │
  │ "Someone else will/has already called"                         │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CONDITIONS THAT INCREASE BYSTANDER HELPING:
  - Clear emergency (unambiguous need)
  - Only one bystander present
  - Knowing victim personally
  - Similarity to victim
  - Target explicitly requests help from specific person ("You! Call 911!")
  - No time pressure to intervene slowly building
```

## Section 4: Attribution

### Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)

```
  FAE (also called Correspondence Bias):
  We overattribute other people's behavior to DISPOSITIONAL factors
  and underestimate SITUATIONAL factors

  JONES/HARRIS (1967) — THE ESSAY STUDY:
  Participants read pro-Castro or anti-Castro essay
  Condition A: told the author freely chose their position
  Condition B: told the author was ASSIGNED that position
  → Both conditions: participants rated the essay as reflecting the author's
    true beliefs; situation had weak effect on attribution
  → Even knowing position was assigned, we infer underlying attitude

  ACTOR-OBSERVER ASYMMETRY:
  We commit FAE when judging others' behavior
  For OUR OWN behavior: we attribute more to situation
  "I was late because of traffic" (situation)
  "They were late because they're irresponsible" (disposition)

  CULTURAL MODULATION:
  FAE appears more strongly in Western (individualistic) cultures
  East Asian cultures (collectivistic) show more situational attribution
  → FAE is culturally influenced, not a simple cognitive universal
```

## Section 5: Cognitive Dissonance

### Festinger (1957)

```
  CORE THEORY:
  When two cognitions (beliefs, attitudes, behaviors) are inconsistent
  → Psychological discomfort (dissonance)
  → Motivation to reduce dissonance

  REDUCTION STRATEGIES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Change one cognition to be consistent with the other        │
  │ 2. Add a new cognition that bridges the inconsistency          │
  │ 3. Reduce the importance of one cognition                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FESTINGER-CARLSMITH (1959):
  Participants do a boring task, then tell the next subject it was interesting
  Paid $1 vs. $20 to lie
  Result: $1 group rated task as more enjoyable than $20 group

  Logic: $20 = sufficient external justification → no dissonance needed
  $1 = insufficient justification → must change belief ("I must have liked it")

  APPLICATIONS:
  Effort justification: harder initiation → stronger liking (fraternity hazing)
  Post-decision rationalization: once you choose, you see chosen option as better
  Belief perseverance: when predictions fail, believers often become MORE committed
  (Festinger's original research: doomsday cult — When Prophecy Fails)

  ALTERNATIVE THEORY (Bem's Self-Perception):
  We infer our own attitudes from our behavior, just as we infer others' attitudes
  "I told them it was interesting and didn't get much money, so I must have liked it"
  → Same behavioral predictions; different mechanism
  Current consensus: both operate; dissonance for attitudes we care about
```

## Section 6: Social Identity Theory

### Tajfel and Turner (1979, 1986)

```
  CORE FRAMEWORK:
  People don't just have personal identities; social group memberships
  are part of the self-concept ("social identity")

  THREE PROCESSES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. SOCIAL CATEGORIZATION                                       │
  │ We classify people into groups (including ourselves)           │
  │ Accentuates similarities within categories, differences between│
  │                                                                │
  │ 2. SOCIAL IDENTIFICATION                                       │
  │ We adopt the identity of groups we belong to                   │
  │ Group's fate = our fate; group's prestige = our self-esteem    │
  │                                                                │
  │ 3. SOCIAL COMPARISON                                           │
  │ We evaluate our group favorably relative to other groups       │
  │ → In-group favoritism + out-group derogation                   │
  │ → Serves self-esteem maintenance function                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MINIMAL GROUP PARADIGM:
  Groups assigned on arbitrary basis (coin flip, art preference)
  → Within minutes: in-group favoritism in resource allocation
  → Favored in-group even at cost of absolute gains (maximize difference)
  → Shows: group-based discrimination requires NO history, NO conflict, NO competition

  IMPLICATIONS:
  - National, racial, gender, sports team identities → predictable bias
  - Threat to group status → more extreme discrimination
  - Status differential between groups → higher social identity salience for low-status groups

  INTERGROUP CONTACT HYPOTHESIS (Allport 1954):
  Contact between groups reduces prejudice under conditions:
  1. Equal status between groups in contact
  2. Common goals
  3. Intergroup cooperation (not competition)
  4. Authority/institutional support
  Meta-analysis (Pettigrew/Tropp 2006): contact works, even without all four conditions
  → Effect size d~0.4; friendship is strongest mediator
```

## Section 7: Stereotypes, Prejudice, Implicit Bias

```
  STEREOTYPES:
  Cognitive schemas about group members — efficient but error-prone
  Accuracy varies: some stereotypes have kernel of truth; all overgeneralize
  Activation: automatic (fast, hard to control) vs application (can be suppressed)

  IMPLICIT BIAS (IAT — Implicit Association Test):
  Greenwald/Banaji (1998): measures automatic associations
  Black-White/Good-Bad IAT: widely studied; most show implicit bias toward White
  VALIDITY DEBATE:
  - IAT predicts micro-behaviors (blinking, warmth in interaction) in lab
  - Predictive validity for discrimination in field: weak (r~0.15)
  - Test-retest reliability: ~r=0.5 (low for something used in interventions)
  - Implicit bias training interventions: largely ineffective at changing behavior
  Current consensus: measures something real; doesn't predict individual discrimination well

  STEREOTYPE THREAT (Steele/Aronson 1995):
  Being aware that a stereotype applies to you in a domain → performance decrements
  Black students told test "diagnoses racial differences" → lower scores
  Women in STEM settings → lower performance
  Meta-analytic evidence: effect is real but smaller than original claims (d~0.3-0.4 not d=0.8)
  Moderators: domain identification, perceived controllability, stereotype salience
```

**Systems bridge — operational parallels:**

Bystander effect in incident response: when an alert fires to a shared on-call channel
without an assigned owner, every person assumes someone else is handling it. Pluralistic
ignorance ("everyone else looks calm; must not be critical") + diffusion of responsibility
("someone more senior will escalate") produces dangerous delay. Fix: PagerDuty/alerting
systems that assign a primary responder by name, not by group — directly implements the
social psychology countermeasure (assign explicit responsibility to specific individual).

Fundamental Attribution Error in post-mortems: engineers who cause incidents are blamed
dispositionally ("they should have been more careful") when the correct analysis is
situational (complex system, unclear runbook, inadequate review process). The FAE is
the root cause of blame-driven post-mortems. Blameless post-mortems are the formal
institutionalization of the attribution research finding.

Social proof in adoption curves: any new internal tool or process adoption exhibits
informational social influence — engineers look to peers to gauge whether the new system
is trustworthy. Early adopters function as confederates breaking the unanimity of the
status quo. The same mechanism that causes conformity to wrong answers (Asch) causes
conformity to established toolchains even when better options exist.

Minimal group paradigm in org design: team labels (frontend/backend, product/eng, core/platform)
create in-group/out-group dynamics without any prior history or conflict — exactly
the minimal group result. Cross-functional team structures directly address this
by collapsing the categorization that drives the bias.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Finding | Robustness | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Conformity (Asch) | Robust (replicated) | Unanimity breaks it; one ally helps |
| Milgram obedience | Partially replicated | Proximity + authority + incremental = compliance |
| Bystander effect | Robust | Assign responsibility explicitly to get help |
| FAE | Robust (cross-cultural variation) | Attribute behavior to situation, not character |
| Cognitive dissonance | Robust | Post-commitment behavior change; foot-in-door |
| Minimal group discrimination | Very robust | Any categorization → bias; not just race/politics |
| IAT predicting discrimination | Weak | Individual IAT score is not a reliable predictor |

## Common Confusion Points

**The Milgram study isn't about sadism**: participants were distressed; many asked to stop;
many followed up showed lasting discomfort. The finding is that ordinary people, in the
presence of legitimate authority and under incremental pressure, can do terrible things.
The lesson is situational, not about moral deficiency.

**Social identity ≠ prejudice from hatred**: the minimal group paradigm shows that
in-group favoritism emerges without any negative affect toward the out-group, without
competition, without history. Pure categorization is sufficient. This matters for
interventions — eliminating hatred doesn't eliminate bias.

**Pluralistic ignorance is contagious**: in an emergency, everyone looks calm because
everyone is looking at others for cues. The collective signal is "no emergency" even
though privately everyone is unsure. The fix: one person breaking the script (saying
"does anyone know CPR?") dissolves the false consensus.

**FAE operates on ourselves too — prospectively**: we assume we would behave better
than Milgram participants. Research on moral forecasting consistently shows people
overpredict how well they'll resist situational pressures. The FAE applies to
self-predictions as well as judgments of others.
