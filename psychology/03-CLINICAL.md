# Clinical & Abnormal Psychology

## The Big Picture

Clinical psychology sits at the intersection of science and practice. Diagnostic
systems (DSM-5, ICD-11) provide a common vocabulary that is largely atheoretical
and category-based — which is convenient for clinical communication but increasingly
disconnected from neuroscience and dimensional reality. Evidence-based treatments exist
for most major disorders, with CBT having the broadest support.

## Section 1: Diagnostic Systems

```
  DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, 5th ed., 2013):
  American Psychiatric Association
  Purpose: communication, insurance coding, research standardization
  Approach: CATEGORICAL + SYMPTOM-BASED
  - Diagnosis = meeting threshold number of symptoms from checklist
  - Largely atheoretical (doesn't commit to etiology)
  - High comorbidity: ~50% of patients meet criteria for 2+ disorders
  - Heterogeneity within categories: two patients with MDD may share <1 symptom

  ICD-11 (International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision, 2019):
  WHO; global use; broader coding system (all medical conditions)
  More dimensional elements than DSM-5
  Better integrated with primary care globally

  RDoC (Research Domain Criteria):
  NIMH initiative (2010, Insel et al.)
  Framework for future classification based on:
  - Neural circuits (fear/threat, reward learning, social processes)
  - Dimensions from genes → behavior (not categories)
  - Not yet a clinical diagnostic system; guides research funding

  HiTOP (Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology):
  Empirically-derived dimensional model
  Two broad spectra: Internalizing (mood/anxiety) vs Externalizing (substance/conduct)
  More predictive validity than DSM categories for many outcomes
```

## Section 2: Mood Disorders

### Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

```
  DSM-5 CRITERIA: 5 or more of 9 symptoms for ≥2 weeks, including depressed mood
  or anhedonia (loss of pleasure):

  SYMPTOMS:
  1. Depressed mood most of the day
  2. Markedly diminished interest/pleasure (anhedonia)
  3. Significant weight/appetite change
  4. Insomnia or hypersomnia
  5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation
  6. Fatigue or loss of energy
  7. Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  8. Diminished ability to think or concentrate
  9. Recurrent thoughts of death/suicidal ideation

  EPIDEMIOLOGY: Lifetime prevalence ~17%; 12-month prevalence ~8%
  Women ~2× more likely than men (across cultures)
  Average age of onset: mid-20s; earlier onset → worse prognosis

  NEUROBIOLOGY:
  Monoamine hypothesis (low serotonin → depression) is too simple
  → SSRIs work in ~50% of patients; 2-3 week delay suggests secondary changes
  HPA axis dysregulation: elevated cortisol; blunted feedback suppression
  Neuroplasticity hypothesis: reduced BDNF → hippocampal atrophy
  → Antidepressants restore neuroplasticity (more consistent mechanism)
  Inflammatory hypothesis: high-CRP subgroup; anti-inflammatory treatments?
```

### Bipolar Disorder

```
  BIPOLAR I: At least one manic episode (may or may not have depressive episodes)
  Mania: elevated or irritable mood + 3+ symptoms: grandiosity, decreased sleep need,
  pressured speech, racing thoughts, distractibility, goal-directed activity increase,
  risky behavior; ≥1 week; significant impairment

  BIPOLAR II: Hypomania (same but ≤4 days; no impairment) + MDD episodes

  DISTINCTION: Mania = serious functional impairment + possible psychosis
               Hypomania = productive, elevated state, not necessarily impairing

  TREATMENT: Lithium (gold standard for 60 years; reduces suicide risk 80%),
  valproate, lamotrigine, atypical antipsychotics; psychoeducation, rhythm regulation
  Antidepressants alone → manic switch risk; always with mood stabilizer
```

## Section 4: Trauma and OCD

### PTSD

```
  PTSD (DSM-5 — its own category, no longer anxiety disorder):
  Exposure to death, serious injury, sexual violence (directly, witnessed, or heard of)
  + symptoms in four clusters for ≥1 month:

  CLUSTER A: Intrusion (flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories)
  CLUSTER B: Avoidance (trauma-related thoughts, places, people)
  CLUSTER C: Negative alterations in cognition/mood (distorted blame, shame, anhedonia)
  CLUSTER D: Hyperarousal/reactivity (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, sleep problems)

  MECHANISMS:
  Fear network model: trauma creates overgeneralized fear memory
  Extinction deficit: fear memories formed during trauma hard to extinguish
  Amygdala hyperactivity + prefrontal cortex hypoactivation
  Dissociation in some: depersonalization/derealization symptom profile (PTSD-DS)

  COMPLEX PTSD (ICD-11):
  Repeated/prolonged trauma (child abuse, war, trafficking)
  + PTSD symptoms + "disturbances in self-organization"
  (emotion dysregulation, negative self-concept, relationship problems)
  Not in DSM-5 (awaits more empirical work)

  TREATMENT: PE (Prolonged Exposure), CPT (Cognitive Processing Therapy),
  EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing — mechanism debated,
  efficacy ~equivalent to exposure)
```

### OCD

```
  OCD: Obsessions (intrusive, unwanted thoughts/images/urges) + Compulsions
  (repetitive behaviors to reduce distress or prevent feared outcome)

  COGNITIVE-BEHAVIORAL LOOP MODEL:
  Intrusive thought → catastrophic misappraisal → anxiety → compulsion
  → temporary relief → intrusive thought returns stronger

  KEY: Everyone has intrusive thoughts; OCD = the MEANING attached to them
  and the neutralizing response that makes them persist

  TREATMENT: ERP (Exposure with Response Prevention) — gold standard
  Deliberately trigger obsession; prevent compulsive response; let anxiety habituate
  SSRI augmentation: OCD requires higher SSRI doses than depression
```

## Section 5: Psychotic Disorders

### Schizophrenia

```
  PREVALENCE: ~1% worldwide; onset typically 18-25 (men earlier than women)

  SYMPTOMS — THREE DIMENSIONS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ POSITIVE (added/excess symptoms):                              │
  │ Hallucinations: perception without stimulus (auditory most     │
  │   common; commanding voices; second-person commentary)         │
  │ Delusions: fixed false beliefs (paranoid, referential, grandiose│
  │ Disorganized thinking: loose associations, word salad          │
  │                                                                │
  │ NEGATIVE (diminished function):                                │
  │ Flat/blunted affect; alogia (poverty of speech);               │
  │ avolition (lack of motivation); anhedonia; asociality          │
  │ → Often more disabling than positive symptoms                  │
  │ → Less responsive to antipsychotics                            │
  │                                                                │
  │ COGNITIVE:                                                     │
  │ Working memory, processing speed, executive function deficits  │
  │ Present before psychosis onset; don't respond to medication    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  NEUROBIOLOGICAL MODELS:
  DOPAMINE HYPOTHESIS: excess dopaminergic activity → positive symptoms
  → Supported by: antipsychotics block D2 receptors; dopamine agonists cause psychosis
  → Too simple: doesn't explain negative/cognitive symptoms

  GLUTAMATE HYPOTHESIS: NMDA receptor hypofunction
  → Ketamine (NMDA antagonist) produces schizophrenia-like symptoms including negative
  → Explains why dopamine model is incomplete

  NEURODEVELOPMENTAL MODEL: Prenatal/early developmental disruption
  → Prenatal infection, maternal stress, obstetric complications
  → Structural brain changes (enlarged ventricles, cortical thinning)

  TREATMENT: Antipsychotics (D2 blockers); clozapine for treatment-resistant
  (most effective but agranulocytosis risk → white blood cell monitoring)
  Psychosocial: CBT for psychosis, family therapy, supported employment
```

## Section 7: Evidence-Based Treatments

```
  PSYCHOTHERAPY EFFICACY:
  Meta-analyses show psychotherapy > control for most disorders
  Key effect sizes (Cohen's d vs control):

  ┌────────────────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┐
  │ Disorder + Treatment               │ d (vs control)           │
  ├────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ Depression: CBT                    │ 0.7–1.0                  │
  │ Depression: Medication (SSRI)      │ 0.3–0.5 (vs placebo)     │
  │ Anxiety: CBT/Exposure              │ 0.9–1.5                  │
  │ PTSD: Prolonged Exposure           │ 1.0–1.5                  │
  │ PTSD: CPT                          │ ~1.0                     │
  │ BPD: DBT                           │ 0.5–1.0                  │
  │ OCD: ERP                           │ 1.5–2.0 (largest effects)│
  │ Schizophrenia: Antipsychotics      │ 0.8–1.5 (positive sx)    │
  └────────────────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┘

  CBT (Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy):
  Developed: Beck (depression), Ellis (REBT), Wolpe (desensitization)
  Techniques: cognitive restructuring (identify and challenge distorted thoughts),
  behavioral activation, exposure, homework, skills training
  Gold standard for: depression, all anxiety disorders, eating disorders, insomnia

  ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy):
  "Third wave": mindfulness + values-based action + defusion from thoughts
  Accept unpleasant thoughts/feelings rather than fighting them
  Evidence base growing; comparable to CBT for most conditions

  PSYCHOPHARMACOLOGY PLACEBO RESPONSE:
  Placebo response in depression RCTs: ~35% (response), ~25% (remission)
  Active drug: ~50% response, ~35% remission
  Drug-placebo difference: ~15 percentage points (smaller than perceived)
  Implication: placebo effects are large; setting, expectation, therapeutic relationship matter

  LONG-TERM OUTCOMES DEBATE:
  Psychotherapy: relapse rates after discontinuation lower than medication
  Medication: relapse on discontinuation higher
  → Psychotherapy teaches skills that persist; medication suppresses symptoms
```

**Formal bridge — categorical vs dimensional classification:**

The DSM → RDoC/HiTOP transition is the exact same move as replacing discrete enum
types with continuous feature vectors in a classification system. DSM-5 uses categorical
diagnosis: you meet or don't meet a threshold (5 of 9 MDD criteria). This creates the
same problems as hard-thresholding a continuous signal: information loss, arbitrary
boundaries, high comorbidity (categories overlap because they're projections of a
continuous high-dimensional space onto a small set of discrete bins).

The 50–70% comorbidity rate in clinical populations is not a coincidence — it's the
expected consequence of carving a continuous multivariate space with poorly-placed
category boundaries. Two disorders that share 3 of their 5 diagnostic features will
co-occur in the same patients at high rates. This is exactly the problem you encounter
when you use naive Bayes with overlapping feature distributions, or when you try to
classify a data point that sits between two cluster centroids.

RDoC addresses this by specifying the underlying continuous dimensions (neural circuits,
behavioral constructs, dimensional measurement) rather than categories. HiTOP gives you
a hierarchical taxonomy with spectra at multiple granularities — internalizing/externalizing
as broad factors, then more specific subdimensions, then narrow syndromes — analogous to
a hierarchical clustering tree rather than a flat categorization. The approach lets you
represent a patient as a vector of dimensional scores rather than a set of category
memberships, which has better predictive validity and maps better onto treatment targets.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Condition | First-Line Treatment |
|---|---|
| Major depression | CBT or SSRI; combined for severe; CBT preferred for prevention of relapse |
| Anxiety disorders (all types) | CBT with exposure; SSRI/SNRI for pharmacotherapy |
| PTSD | Prolonged Exposure or CPT; EMDR is equivalent alternative |
| OCD | ERP (Exposure + Response Prevention); SSRI at higher doses |
| Bipolar I | Lithium (gold standard); valproate; atypical antipsychotics |
| Schizophrenia | Antipsychotics (lowest effective dose); clozapine for TRS |
| BPD | DBT (only evidence-based specific treatment) |
| Insomnia | CBT-I (cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia) > medication for chronic |

## Common Confusion Points

**DSM categories are not natural kinds**: the diagnostic thresholds (5 of 9 symptoms
for MDD) are clinically useful conventions, not natural boundaries. Someone with 4
symptoms is not categorically different from someone with 5. Subthreshold conditions
matter clinically.

**Comorbidity is the norm, not the exception**: in clinical populations, ~50-70% of
patients meet criteria for more than one DSM diagnosis. This suggests the categories
are carving nature at the wrong joints — disorders share underlying mechanisms.

**Antidepressants work, but modestly**: the drug-placebo difference in depression trials
is ~15 percentage points. This is clinically meaningful but much less dramatic than
often portrayed. The cases with most absolute benefit are severe depression; mild-
moderate cases show smaller drug-placebo differences.

**ADHD is a real disorder with real impairments**: the validity of ADHD as a construct
is sometimes challenged. The evidence is clear: ADHD has substantial heritability (~75%),
consistent neurobiological correlates (prefrontal cortex/dopamine/executive function),
and stimulant treatment has strong efficacy (effect sizes among the largest in
psychiatry). The diagnostic challenges are about thresholds and overdiagnosis, not
whether the core syndrome exists.
