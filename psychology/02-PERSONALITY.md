# Personality Psychology

## The Big Picture

Personality refers to consistent patterns of thought, feeling, and behavior that
differ between individuals and are relatively stable over time and contexts. The Big
Five is the dominant empirical model — derived from factor analysis of language,
replicated across cultures, and predictively valid. MBTI is not this.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    PERSONALITY MODEL LANDSCAPE                        |
|                                                                      |
|  EMPIRICAL (research-based)     vs    POPULAR (untested)             |
|  ─────────────────────────            ──────────────────────         |
|  Big Five (OCEAN)                     MBTI (Myers-Briggs)            |
|  Factor-analytically derived          Jungian typology               |
|  Replicated cross-culturally          50% different type in 5 weeks  |
|  Predicts job performance,            Weak predictive validity        |
|  relationships, health, politics      Not used by researchers         |
|                                                                      |
|  DARK TRIAD (applied)               HEXACO (extending Big Five)      |
|  Narcissism + Machiavellianism      Adds Honesty-Humility dimension  |
|  + Subclinical psychopathy          Better predicts dark behaviors   |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Section 1: Big Five Origins

### The Lexical Hypothesis

```
  FOUNDATION: All important personality differences between people will eventually
  be encoded in language — if a trait matters, it gets a word

  PROCESS:
  Allport & Odbert (1936): 17,953 trait-describing words in English dictionary
  Cattell (1943): reduced to ~170 clusters → 16PF (16 personality factors)
  Fiske (1949): factor-analyzed Cattell's work → 5 factors
  Tupes & Christal (1961): replicated 5 factors
  Goldberg (1981): coined "Big Five" label; expanded lexical studies
  Costa & McCrae (1980s-90s): developed NEO-PI (Neuroticism-Extraversion-Openness)
  → NEO-PI-R with 240 items; still the standard research instrument

  CROSS-LINGUISTIC REPLICATION:
  Big Five factors emerge in studies of 50+ languages
  Including non-Indo-European languages (Chinese, Korean, Tagalog)
  Five factors are not an artifact of English structure
```

## Section 3: Predictive Validity

```
  BIG FIVE VALIDITY SUMMARY (r values; meta-analytic):

  OUTCOME               C      E      A      N      O
  Job performance      .24    .15    .10   -.14    .08
  Academic achievement .28   .07    .06   -.12    .16
  Leadership emergence .22    .31    .00   -.17    .24
  Relationship quality .22    .17    .26   -.27    .12
  Life satisfaction    .23    .36    .18   -.41    .16
  Mental health        .22    .25    .17   -.58    .11
  Physical health      .26    .12    .09   -.20    .09

  Note: N is negative for these outcomes (stability = health/performance)
  Conscientiousness is the "good trait" for life outcomes
  Neuroticism is the "bad trait" for mental/physical health
  Extraversion matters most for social/affective outcomes

  INCREMENTAL VALIDITY OVER IQ:
  Big Five adds prediction even after controlling for GMA
  C predicts job performance beyond IQ (especially for complex jobs)
  N predicts mental health beyond life events
```

## Section 4: Heritability

```
  BEHAVIORAL GENETICS FINDINGS:
  Each Big Five dimension: h² ≈ 40–60%
  Heritability of personality does NOT decline after adolescence (unlike some traits)

  TWIN STUDY DECOMPOSITION (ACE model):
  A = Additive genetic (h²): ~40-60% for each Big Five trait
  C = Shared environment (growing up in same family): ~5-10% (surprisingly small!)
  E = Non-shared environment (unique experiences): ~30-50%

  SURPRISING FINDING (Loehlin 1992; Plomin et al.):
  Shared family environment has MINIMAL effect on adult personality
  Twins raised apart have similar personalities as twins raised together
  → Raises profound questions about parenting effects on personality traits
  → Parenting matters for many other outcomes — just not much for Big Five

  GENE-ENVIRONMENT INTERACTION:
  Serotonin transporter polymorphism (5-HTTLPR) + stressful life events → depression
  → Several large meta-analyses FAILED to replicate this specific interaction
  → General lesson: specific candidate gene effects are mostly too small to detect
  → GWAS: many thousands of tiny variants; rare large-effect variants

  GxE THAT IS ROBUST:
  Maltreatment × MAOA: violence (partially replicated but effect modest)
  Warm parenting × child genetic risk for anxiety: modulates expression
  General principle: genes set sensitivity ranges; environment triggers expression
```

## Section 5: Stability Across the Lifespan

```
  TWO DISTINCT QUESTIONS:
  1. Rank-order stability: does my rank in the population stay the same?
  2. Mean-level change: does the average level of a trait change with age?

  RANK-ORDER STABILITY:
  Increases with age; peaks in middle adulthood
  4-year interval: r~0.55 in adolescence → r~0.75 in adulthood
  40-year interval: r~0.55 (considerable stability despite long span)
  Implication: relative personality is quite stable after ~30

  MEAN-LEVEL CHANGES (cross-sectional and longitudinal):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Conscientiousness: INCREASES into middle age ("maturation")      │
  │ Agreeableness: INCREASES with age                                │
  │ Neuroticism: DECREASES with age (esp. women)                     │
  │ Openness: slight decrease in old age                             │
  │ Extraversion: slight decrease overall; positive affect component │
  │               more stable                                        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PERSONALITY CHANGE IS POSSIBLE:
  Significant life events → personality change (moderately)
  Psychotherapy → modest Big Five changes (especially N → ↓)
  Intentional change: deliberate practice of behaviors → trait shifts
  But change is slow; stable attractors exist
```

## Section 6: The MBTI Critique

```
  MYERS-BRIGGS TYPE INDICATOR:
  Based on Carl Jung's psychological type theory (1920s)
  16 types from 4 dichotomies: E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P

  PROBLEMS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ RELIABILITY:                                                   │
  │ Test-retest after 5 weeks: ~50% receive different type        │
  │ Forced dichotomies (E vs I) when underlying distributions     │
  │ are continuous → arbitrary cutpoints create artificial types   │
  │                                                                │
  │ VALIDITY:                                                      │
  │ Weak prediction of job performance                             │
  │ Doesn't predict outcomes better than Big Five                  │
  │ S/N dimension correlates with Big Five O (~r=0.70)             │
  │ → MBTI ≈ poor-reliability, forced-dichotomy version of Big Five│
  │                                                                │
  │ SCIENTIFIC STATUS:                                             │
  │ Not used in personality research literature                    │
  │ ~2,000 peer-reviewed publications use Big Five per year       │
  │ ~10 use MBTI for trait prediction                              │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHY IT PERSISTS:
  - Positive (non-threatening) framing: no "bad" types
  - Simple categories feel satisfying; narrative identity
  - Multi-billion dollar consulting industry
  - Organizational inertia
```

## Section 7: The Dark Triad

```
  PAULHUS & WILLIAMS (2002):

  Three "socially aversive" personality traits that correlate with each other
  but have distinct mechanisms and behavioral predictions:

  NARCISSISM (subclinical):
  Core: grandiosity, entitlement, exploitativeness, lack of empathy
  Not the same as clinical NPD (personality disorder)
  Correlates with: leadership emergence (positive short-term impression),
                   aggression when ego is threatened, romantic short-term mating success
  Measurement: NPI (Narcissistic Personality Inventory)
  Meta-analytic note: entitlement/exploitativeness facet most toxic behaviorally

  MACHIAVELLIANISM:
  Core: strategic cynicism, willingness to manipulate, emotional detachment
  Named after Machiavelli's pragmatic political philosophy
  Correlates with: financial crime, workplace deception, long-term manipulation
  Differs from narcissism: lower impulsivity; more deliberate

  SUBCLINICAL PSYCHOPATHY:
  Core: callousness, impulsivity, antisocial tendencies, risk-seeking
  Not the same as clinical psychopathy (lacks some severe features)
  Highest correlation with antisocial outcomes, aggression, manipulation
  Low anxiety component distinguishes from other dark traits

  DARK TETRAD (extending):
  Everyday sadism added (Buckels 2013): pleasure from others' suffering
  Strongest predictor of online trolling behavior

  MEASUREMENT:
  Short Dark Triad (SD3) — 27 items; widely used
  Dirty Dozen — 12 items for screening
  Separate measures for each trait exist (NPI for narcissism, etc.)

  ORGANIZATIONAL RELEVANCE:
  Dark Triad scores predict: workplace deviance, counterproductive work behavior
  Narcissism predicts leadership derailment but initially positive assessments
  Psychopathy predicts "successful psychopath" pattern in some high-stakes roles
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Best personality model for prediction? | Big Five (OCEAN) — factor-analytically derived, replicated |
| Most predictive single trait for job performance? | Conscientiousness (r~0.24) |
| Predicts mental health best (negatively)? | Neuroticism |
| Does personality change? | Yes, slowly — mean-level maturation; possible through deliberate effort |
| Should I use MBTI for hiring? | No — poor reliability, weak validity |
| What is the Dark Triad? | Narcissism + Machiavellianism + Psychopathy; distinct but correlated |
| Does parenting determine personality? | Little effect on Big Five in adulthood (genetic evidence) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Introversion ≠ shyness**: introversion is preference for lower stimulation environments
and need for solitude to recharge. Shyness is anxiety in social situations (closer to
Neuroticism). An introvert can be socially skilled and enjoy interaction — just in smaller
doses. Mislabeling introverts as shy is both incorrect and often pejorative.

**Neuroticism is not emotional expressiveness**: it's emotional instability and tendency
toward negative affect. A very expressive, emotionally demonstrative person can be
low in Neuroticism if their emotional expression is not distress-driven.

**Big Five traits are continuous, not types**: you don't "have" Openness or "lack" it.
You're at some level in a continuous distribution. The trait labels describe the extreme
ends of that distribution. "High Conscientiousness" means in the upper quartile.

**Personality predicts, but weakly**: the validity coefficients (r~0.2-0.4) mean personality
explains 4-16% of variance in outcomes. Life outcomes have many causes; personality is
one. Never predict an individual's behavior from personality alone — it's a probabilistic
statement about populations.
