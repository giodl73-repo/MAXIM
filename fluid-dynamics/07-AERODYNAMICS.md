# Aerodynamics: Lift, Drag, and Wings

## The Big Picture

Aerodynamics is the application of fluid dynamics to bodies moving through air. Lift and drag are the two fundamental forces. Lift is generated by circulation (Kutta-Joukowski theorem) — a consequence of the pressure difference between upper and lower surfaces. Drag has multiple sources: skin friction (viscous), pressure drag (separation), induced drag (3D wing tip effects), and wave drag (shocks). Wing theory connects complex analysis, boundary layer theory, and the governing equations into a unified framework for designing aircraft.

```
AERODYNAMIC FORCES — CLASSIFICATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LIFT L = ½ρV²SC_L    (perpendicular to velocity)
  DRAG D = ½ρV²SC_D    (parallel to velocity, opposing motion)
  MOMENT M = ½ρV²ScC_M  (about some reference point)

  q = ½ρV² = dynamic pressure,  S = reference area (usually planform area)

  DRAG SOURCES:
  ┌───────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Skin friction     │ Viscous shear at wall; C_Df ~ Re^{-1/5} (turb)│
  │ Pressure drag     │ Separation → wake; depends on body shape       │
  │ Induced drag      │ 3D wing tip vortices; C_Di = C_L²/(πeAR)     │
  │ Wave drag         │ Shocks at M > 0.8; rises rapidly near M = 1    │
  └───────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  LIFT MECHANISM (thin airfoil):
  Upper surface: faster flow → lower pressure (Bernoulli)
  Lower surface: slower flow → higher pressure
  Net upward force = ∫ (p_lower - p_upper) dA = ΔpA
```

## Airfoil Geometry and Notation

```
AIRFOIL GEOMETRY:
     chord line ──────────────────────────────────────────
             LE (leading edge)    TE (trailing edge)
     camber line (mean camber line: midpoint between surfaces)
     thickness t(x) = distance between upper/lower surface

  KEY PARAMETERS:
  c = chord length (LE to TE)
  t/c = thickness ratio (e.g., 0.12 for NACA 0012)
  max camber / chord position (NACA 4-digit code encodes these)
  α = angle of attack (angle between chord line and freestream velocity)

  NACA AIRFOIL NAMING:
  NACA 2412:
    2  = max camber 2% of chord
    4  = max camber at 40% chord
    12 = max thickness 12% of chord
  NACA 0012: symmetric (no camber), 12% thick
```

## Thin Airfoil Theory

For thin airfoils (t/c << 1) at small angle of attack, the lift coefficient is:

    C_L = 2π(α + β)    (thin airfoil theory)

where α is angle of attack and β is the camber-dependent term.

For symmetric airfoils: C_L = 2πα (lift slope = 2π per radian)

**Physical meaning**: Each additional degree of angle of attack increases C_L by 2π/57.3 ≈ 0.11 per degree.

**Kutta condition**: At the trailing edge, the velocity must be finite. This determines the circulation Γ and hence the lift. Without the Kutta condition, there would be infinite velocity at the sharp trailing edge — physically impossible.

```
KUTTA CONDITION MECHANISM:
  At sharp TE, two possibilities:
  1) Flow rounds the TE: velocity → ∞  (unphysical)
  2) Flow leaves smoothly from TE: finite velocity (Kutta condition)

  The Kutta condition selects the physical solution.
  Mathematically: it determines the circulation Γ.

  From Kutta-Joukowski: L = ρV∞Γ
  From thin airfoil theory: Γ = πcV∞(α + β)
  ⟹ C_L = 2πα (for symmetric airfoil)
```

## Lift Generation — Multiple Perspectives

### Perspective 1: Bernoulli (pressure difference)

Upper surface: flow accelerates → pressure decreases (Bernoulli: p + ½ρv² = const)
Lower surface: flow decelerates → pressure increases

Net upward pressure force = lift.

*This is correct but says nothing about WHY the flow is faster above.*

### Perspective 2: Circulation (Kutta-Joukowski)

The wing develops circulation Γ (due to Kutta condition).

    L = ρV∞Γ    (per unit span)

Γ is generated by the geometry and angle of attack, quantified via conformal mapping (Joukowski, see 04-CONFORMAL-MAPS in complex-analysis/).

### Perspective 3: Momentum (Newton's 3rd Law)

Wing deflects air downward (downwash) → reaction force = lift upward.

All three perspectives are correct — they are different ways of viewing the same physics.

## Pressure Distribution and Center of Pressure

The pressure coefficient:

    C_p = (p − p∞) / (½ρV∞²) = 1 − (v/V∞)²  (Bernoulli)

**Typical airfoil C_p distribution**:

```
C_p
  1  ─────────────────────────────  stagnation (C_p = 1 at LE)
  0  ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─
     ─ upper surface (negative C_p = suction)
-1
-2  ─ peak suction (max lift; near LE)
     ─ lower surface (positive C_p = pressure)
  0  ─────────────────────────────  at TE both surfaces meet
```

The **center of pressure** is where the resultant pressure force acts. For cambered airfoils, it moves as α changes. The **aerodynamic center** (at ¼ chord for thin airfoils) is the point where pitching moment does NOT change with α — used as reference in stability analysis.

## Stall — Loss of Lift

Stall occurs when the boundary layer separates from the upper surface, collapsing the suction peak and losing lift.

```
ANGLE OF ATTACK EVOLUTION:
  α = 0°:    C_L ≈ 0 (symmetric) or C_L₀ (cambered)
  α = 10°:   C_L ≈ 1.1 (rising linearly)
  α = 15°:   C_L ≈ 1.4 (near maximum)
  α = 16°:   STALL — boundary layer separates from leading edge
  α > 16°:   C_L drops sharply (separated flow)

  C_Lmax ≈ 1.2–1.8 (clean airfoil)
  with flaps: C_Lmax ≈ 2–3 (flaps increase camber)
```

**Stall types**:
- **Trailing edge stall** (thick airfoils): gradual, progressive
- **Leading edge stall** (thin airfoils): abrupt, sudden
- **Combined**: for medium-thick airfoils

## 3D Wing Theory — Prandtl's Lifting Line

For a finite wing (span b, not infinite), the spanwise variation of lift creates tip vortices.

**Circulation distribution**: Each section has circulation Γ(y) varying from root to tip.

**Biot-Savart law** (vortex induction): The trailing vortex sheet (shed from the wing) induces a downward velocity (downwash) w(y) at the wing:

    w(y) = −(1/4π) ∫_{-b/2}^{b/2} (dΓ/dη)/(y−η) dη

This downwash reduces the effective angle of attack:

    α_eff(y) = α − w(y)/V∞

**Prandtl's integro-differential equation** (lifting line theory):

    Γ(y) = πcV∞ α_eff(y) = πcV∞[α − 1/(4πV∞) ∫ (dΓ/dη)/(y−η) dη]

**Elliptic lift distribution** (optimal solution, achieved by elliptic planform or twist):

    Γ(y) = Γ₀ √(1 − (2y/b)²)

**Key results for elliptic distribution**:

    Induced drag:  C_Di = C_L²/(πAR)    where AR = b²/S (aspect ratio)
    Downwash:      w = constant = V∞C_L/(πAR)

```
ASPECT RATIO EFFECT ON INDUCED DRAG:
  AR = b²/S  (span² / planform area)

  AR = 5  (typical fighter jet):   C_Di/C_L² = 1/(5π) ≈ 0.064
  AR = 10 (typical airliner):      C_Di/C_L² = 1/(10π) ≈ 0.032
  AR = 20 (glider / albatross):    C_Di/C_L² = 1/(20π) ≈ 0.016
  AR = ∞  (2D wing):              C_Di = 0  (no tip vortices)

  ⟹ High-aspect-ratio wings are more efficient (less induced drag)
     for the same lift (same cruise C_L)
  ⟹ Gliders have very high AR (~25), fighters have low AR (~3)
```

## Drag Polar and Lift-to-Drag Ratio

**Total drag coefficient**:

    C_D = C_D0 + C_L²/(πeAR)

where C_D0 = zero-lift drag (skin friction + pressure), e = Oswald efficiency (≤ 1).

**Drag polar**: C_D vs C_L curve (parabolic for subsonic flight).

**Maximum L/D** (best glide ratio):

    (L/D)_max = 1/(2√(C_D0 πeAR))    at C_L = √(πeAR C_D0)

For a commercial airliner: L/D_max ≈ 18–20, meaning the aircraft glides ~18 km horizontally per km of altitude in engine-off flight.

For a high-performance glider: L/D_max ≈ 50–60.

## High-Lift Devices

At takeoff and landing, additional lift is needed at low speed (V is low, need high C_L to generate same force).

    L = ½ρV²S C_L

**Flaps** (trailing edge): increase camber → increase C_L at given α. Adds drag too.
**Slats** (leading edge): delay stall by energizing flow near the leading edge.
**Fowler flaps**: move aft and down → increase both camber AND area S.

```
C_Lmax COMPARISON:
  Clean airfoil:      C_Lmax ≈ 1.5
  Single-slot flap:   C_Lmax ≈ 2.0
  Double-slot flap:   C_Lmax ≈ 2.5
  Triple-slot flap:   C_Lmax ≈ 3.0
  (plus leading edge slats add ~0.5 more)
```

## Wave Drag and Transonic Effects

As M increases above ~0.8, local flow over the upper wing surface becomes supersonic (even though freestream M < 1). Shocks form and wave drag appears.

**Critical Mach number** M_cr: the freestream M at which local flow first reaches M = 1.

**Drag divergence Mach number** M_dd > M_cr: sharp increase in drag begins (shock-induced separation).

**Transonic area rule** (Whitcomb): To minimize wave drag at transonic speeds, the total cross-sectional area distribution should be as smooth as possible (like a Sears-Haack body). This is why fuselages near wings are "waisted" (Coke-bottle shape).

**Supercritical airfoils**: Designed to have gentler pressure recovery → weaker shock → higher M_dd. Used on all modern airliners. Characterized by flatter top surface and blunt trailing edge.

## Common Confusion Points

**Lift is not simply from Bernoulli's equation alone**: Bernoulli is correct for the pressure difference, but it doesn't explain WHY the flow is faster on the upper surface. The complete explanation requires the circulation (Kutta-Joukowski) framework — Γ is determined by the Kutta condition at the trailing edge.

**Induced drag is zero in 2D (infinite span)**: Induced drag arises from the finite span — tip vortices — which create downwash. A hypothetical infinite-span wing has no tip vortices and no induced drag. That's why 2D airfoil theory ignores induced drag.

**Stall is controlled by angle of attack, not speed per se**: An airfoil stalls at α_stall regardless of speed. The stall SPEED is low because at low speed you need high α to generate enough lift. High-speed stall is possible in banked turns (high load factor increases effective weight, requiring higher C_L, which requires higher α).

**Aspect ratio of common planforms**: AR = b^2/S. For an elliptical planform with semi-span a and semi-chord b: S = pi*a*b, AR = (2a)^2/(pi*a*b) = 4a/(pi*b). For a circular disk (a = b): AR = 4/pi = 1.27 — the lowest practical AR. High AR wings (gliders, AR > 20) are elongated and minimize induced drag. Fighter jets use low AR (~3-4) to maximize roll rate at the cost of higher induced drag.
