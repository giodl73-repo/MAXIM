# Industrial Ceramics — Technical Ceramics, Refractories, Bioceramics

## The Big Picture

The ceramics industry by revenue is dominated not by tableware or tiles but by technical ceramics — engineered materials for electronics, aerospace, medicine, and energy. Every semiconductor device, every gas turbine, every implanted hip joint, and every automotive exhaust system depends on ceramic engineering.

**Engineering entry point:** Technical ceramics are the enabling material layer underneath the systems you already know. If you know MOSFET fabrication: the gate dielectric (SiO₂, HfO₂), the shallow trench isolation (SiO₂), and the interlayer dielectrics are all ceramics. The packaging substrate (AlN or Al₂O₃) is a ceramic. If you know gas turbine engineering: the thermal barrier coating on turbine blades (yttria-stabilized zirconia, 100–500 µm) is the ceramic layer that allows combustion temperatures 100–200°C above the nickel superalloy's melting point. If you know EV power electronics: SiC MOSFETs and GaN-on-SiC are semiconductor-on-ceramic systems — the SiC substrate is the enabling thermal and electrical material. The pattern is consistent: wherever operating conditions exceed the limits of metals and polymers (temperature, hardness, dielectric strength, biocompatibility), ceramics are the solution layer.

```
INDUSTRIAL CERAMICS LANDSCAPE
==============================

TECHNICAL CERAMICS (engineered properties)
 ├── Structural ceramics (load-bearing)
 │   ├── Alumina (Al₂O₃): cutting tools, armor, hip joints
 │   ├── Zirconia (ZrO₂): dental crowns, O₂ sensors, TBC coatings
 │   ├── Silicon carbide (SiC): abrasives, armor, power electronics
 │   ├── Silicon nitride (Si₃N₄): bearings, turbocharger rotors
 │   └── Boron carbide (B₄C): armor, neutron absorption
 ├── Electronic ceramics
 │   ├── Piezoelectrics (BaTiO₃, PZT): sensors, actuators, ultrasound
 │   ├── MLCCs (BaTiO₃): capacitors (1-5 trillion/year)
 │   ├── Ferrites: transformer cores, magnets
 │   └── Superconductors (YBa₂Cu₃O₇): HTS systems
 ├── Thermal ceramics
 │   ├── Refractories: steel/glass/cement manufacturing
 │   └── Thermal barrier coatings: gas turbine blades
 └── Bioceramics
     ├── Bioinert (Al₂O₃, ZrO₂): hip joints, dental crowns
     ├── Bioactive (hydroxyapatite, bioglass): bone bonding
     └── Bioresorbable (TCP): dissolves in body, replaced by bone

TRADITIONAL CERAMICS (by volume)
 ├── Whitewares: tableware, sanitaryware (toilets, sinks)
 ├── Tiles: floor/wall; global construction market
 ├── Bricks and refractories: construction, industrial
 └── Abrasives: grinding wheels, sandpaper (SiC, alumina)
```

## Alumina (Al₂O₃) — The Workhorse

The most widely used technical ceramic. Combines high hardness, thermal stability, chemical inertness, and electrical insulation.

```
ALUMINA PROPERTIES
==================
Chemical:      Al₂O₃ (corundum structure — same as ruby/sapphire)
Purity grades: 90%, 96%, 99.5%, 99.9% — higher purity = better properties
               but higher cost

PROPERTY         96% Al₂O₃    99.5% Al₂O₃
============     =========    ===========
Hardness         ~8.5 Mohs    ~9 Mohs
Density          3.72 g/cm³   3.90 g/cm³
Melting point    2,040°C      2,072°C
Flexural strength 350 MPa     400 MPa
Thermal cond.    20 W/mK      28-35 W/mK
Dielectric str.  10 kV/mm     14 kV/mm
```

### Key Alumina Applications

```
SPARK PLUG INSULATOR:
 The white ceramic in every automotive/aircraft spark plug
 Al₂O₃ around central electrode
 Requirements: high dielectric strength (resist high voltage arc),
               thermal stability (-40°C to 900°C cycling),
               excellent thermal conductivity (conduct heat away)
 ~2 billion spark plugs produced annually globally

HIP AND KNEE REPLACEMENT (FEMORAL HEADS):
 Al₂O₃ alumina or Y-TZP zirconia femoral heads
 Articulate against polyethylene or ceramic acetabular cup
 Requirements: hardness (resist scratching), biocompatibility,
               polish quality (low friction), corrosion resistance
 Al₂O₃ wear rate in body: ~0.1 mm³/million cycles
 Steel wear rate: ~2-5 mm³/million cycles → 20-50× less wear
 Consequence: alumina joint lasts longer → fewer revisions

CUTTING TOOL INSERTS:
 Al₂O₃ ceramic inserts for metal machining
 Hard enough to cut hardened steel at high speed
 No lubrication needed (unlike carbide inserts)
 Brittle (cannot handle interrupted cuts) → specific applications
 Grade: 96-99% Al₂O₃; SiC whisker-reinforced alumina composites

CERAMIC ARMOR TILES:
 Al₂O₃ tiles (0.3-1 cm thick) backed by composite
 Bullet deformation: ceramic is harder than steel projectile;
                     ceramic shatters projectile; backing stops fragments
 Vehicle armor (Bradley IFV, Stryker), body armor composite plates
```

## Zirconia (ZrO₂) — Transformation Toughening

Zirconia is unusual among ceramics: phase transformation can absorb crack energy, making it tougher than most oxide ceramics.

```
ZIRCONIA PHASE TRANSITIONS
===========================
Temperature     Phase               Crystal structure
===========     =====               ================
>2,370°C        Cubic               Isotropic; high symmetry
1,170–2,370°C   Tetragonal          Slight distortion
<1,170°C        Monoclinic          Low symmetry; ~3-5% volume expansion
                                    ON COOLING: monoclinic = larger
                                    ON HEATING: tetragonal = smaller

PROBLEM: Phase change at 1,170°C causes 3-5% volume change
         → pure zirconia shatters during cooling from sintering
         (the same issue as quartz inversion but much worse)

SOLUTION: Stabilize the tetragonal phase at room temperature with additives:
 YSZ (yttria-stabilized zirconia): 3-8 mol% Y₂O₃
  → tetragonal phase retained at room temperature
  → metastable tetragonal phase
  → crack tip stress → phase transformation → volume expansion at crack tip
  → CLOSES the crack → "transformation toughening"

 RESULT: ZrO₂ fracture toughness 6-10 MPa√m vs Al₂O₃'s 3-5 MPa√m
          2× tougher → suitable for thinner, more delicate components
```

### Key Zirconia Applications

```
DENTAL CROWNS (Y-TZP — yttria-stabilized tetragonal polycrystalline):
 Most dramatic growth application (2005-present)
 Process: CAD/CAM (scan tooth → mill from pre-sintered zirconia blank)
           Shrinkage: blank is ~25% oversize; mills to shape → sinters to final
           Final sintering: 1,450°C → fully dense, ~60 HRC equivalent hardness
 Colors: yttria-stabilized blocks with gradient coloring (gingival to incisal)
 Translucency: modern "multilayer" zirconia → high translucency for anterior teeth
 Market: >90% of posterior ceramic crowns in US/EU are now zirconia (2024)

AUTOMOTIVE LAMBDA SENSOR (OXYGEN SENSOR):
 YSZ solid electrolyte: conducts O²⁻ ions at operating temperature (~300-800°C)
 Electrochemical cell: reference side (air) vs exhaust side
 Output voltage indicates stoichiometry of combustion (rich/lean)
 Zirconia unique: O²⁻ ion conductor at operating temperature (no other material)
 Every modern car has 2-4 lambda sensors; 200M+ produced annually

THERMAL BARRIER COATINGS (TBC) ON GAS TURBINES:
 YSZ (7wt% Y₂O₃) plasma-sprayed onto nickel superalloy turbine blades
 Thermal conductivity: YSZ ~2.5 W/mK vs nickel superalloy ~12 W/mK
 → insulates blade; allows combustion gas temperature 100-200°C higher
 → higher temperatures = higher Carnot efficiency = fuel savings
 Critical enabler of modern gas turbine technology
 Coating thickness: 100-500 µm; replaced after ~25,000 flight hours
```

## Silicon Carbide (SiC)

```
SILICON CARBIDE PROPERTIES
===========================
Crystal:       Cubic β-SiC (3C) or hexagonal α-SiC (many polytypes)
Hardness:      9.2-9.5 Mohs (just below diamond, above corundum)
Thermal cond.: 120-200 W/mK (high — comparable to many metals)
               Excellent thermal shock resistance
Chemical:      Inert to most acids; resists oxidation to ~1,400°C
Density:       3.21 g/cm³

APPLICATIONS:

Abrasives: SiC grinding wheels, sandpaper, lapping compounds
 SiC sharper and faster-cutting than Al₂O₃ for non-ferrous materials
 Standard for: carbide, cast iron, ceramics, stone grinding
 Garnet > SiC > Al₂O₃ in some woodworking applications

Kiln furniture and heating elements:
 SiC kiln shelves: lightweight alternative to cordierite
 SiC heating elements (Globar): used in laboratory/industrial kilns
 Kanthal vs SiC elements: SiC handles higher temperatures (to 1,600°C)

Ballistic armor:
 SiC B-4 (dense sintered SiC): very hard; lighter than alumina
 SAPI plates (Small Arms Protective Insert): SiC or Al₂O₃ + polyethylene
 US military standard ballistic plates

Semiconductor power electronics (SiC MOSFET):
 Wide bandgap: 3.26 eV vs silicon's 1.12 eV
 → handles higher voltage, higher temperature
 → lower on-resistance at same voltage rating
 → faster switching
 Applications: EV inverters, solar inverters, industrial motor drives
 Tesla Model 3 inverter: uses SiC MOSFETs (SiCR SiC modules from ST Micro)
 → 30% reduction in inverter energy loss vs silicon equivalent

LED substrate history:
 SiC was the first viable substrate for blue LEDs before GaN-on-sapphire
 dominated. Cree's early blue and blue-green LEDs (1989–1993) used 6H-SiC
 substrates — the wide bandgap (3.26 eV) and hexagonal lattice were a
 reasonable match for GaN epitaxy. The SiC substrate path was commercially
 superseded by sapphire (Al₂O₃) + GaN buffer layers after Nakamura's
 breakthroughs at Nichia (1993–1994), which offered lower substrate cost and
 better GaN lattice match. However, SiC-on-SiC (GaN-on-SiC) is now standard
 for RF power transistors (5G base stations) because SiC's thermal
 conductivity (120–200 W/mK vs sapphire's 25 W/mK) handles the power density.
 The evolutionary path: SiC substrate (Cree blue LEDs) → sapphire substrate
 (Nichia, dominant LEDs) → SiC substrate returns for GaN RF power (Wolfspeed,
 MACOM). Ceramics substrate choice determines thermal management in all cases.

EV charging infrastructure:
 SiC MOSFETs are now the standard in Level 3 DC fast chargers (150–350 kW).
 The operating voltage (800V bus in latest EVs — Porsche Taycan, Hyundai
 Ioniq 5) requires switching transistors rated 1,200V, which is achievable
 with SiC at reasonable on-resistance. Silicon IGBTs at 800V bus are
 significantly less efficient. The ceramics (SiC) content of a modern EV
 fast charger is structurally similar to its power electronics.
```

## Silicon Nitride (Si₃N₄)

```
SILICON NITRIDE PROPERTIES
===========================
Best combination of: strength + toughness + thermal shock resistance
 of any ceramic
Flexural strength: 700-1000 MPa (very high for ceramic)
Fracture toughness: 5-7 MPa√m
Hardness: ~9 Mohs
Thermal expansion: 3.2 × 10⁻⁶/°C (very low → excellent thermal shock)
Thermal conductivity: 20-90 W/mK (depending on grade)

KEY APPLICATIONS:

Ceramic ball bearings (hybrid bearings):
 Si₃N₄ balls in steel races (or full ceramic bearings)
 Advantages over steel balls:
  - Lighter (40% density of steel) → lower centrifugal forces at speed
  - Harder → longer life; less Hertzian contact stress
  - Lower thermal expansion → less preload change with temperature
  - Electrically insulating → no current passage (important for VFD motors)
  - Less lubricant needed
 Used: high-speed machine tool spindles, bicycle bottom brackets,
       wind turbine main bearings, electric vehicle motors

Cutting tool inserts:
 Si₃N₄ inserts for cast iron machining (not suitable for steel)
 Better thermal shock resistance than Al₂O₃ → interrupted cutting

Turbocharger rotors (experimental/limited production):
 Ceramic turbocharger wheel: lower moment of inertia (lighter)
 → faster spool-up (better response)
 → can operate at higher temperatures (no cooling requirement)
 Limited by: ceramic brittleness under thermal shock at intake surge
```

## Refractories

```
REFRACTORY CERAMICS
====================
Definition: materials maintaining structural integrity above 538°C
            (industrial definition varies; typically >1,000°C service)

REFRACTORY TYPES:
 Fireclay:    Al₂O₃ 25-45% + SiO₂; 1,480-1,760°C service
              Most common; kiln linings, furnace walls
 High alumina: >45% Al₂O₃; higher temperature service
               Steel ladle linings, furnace roofs, rotary kiln ends
 Silica:      >93% SiO₂; excellent stability at constant high temperature
              Poor thermal cycling resistance (quartz inversion)
              Coke ovens, glass tank crowns
 Basic:       MgO, chrome-MgO, dolomite
              Alkaline steel-making conditions (basic oxygen furnace)
              Resist lime and iron oxide slag corrosion
 Monolithic:  Castable refractories (refractory aggregate + calcium
              aluminate cement); pour or gunite into place; no joints
              More uniform performance than brick; faster installation

STEEL PRODUCTION DEPENDENCE:
 Iron ore → blast furnace → pig iron: fireclay/high alumina linings
 Electric arc furnace (steel scrap melting): MgO lining
 Ladle metallurgy (refining): high alumina + magnesia slide gate bricks
 Continuous casting: zirconia nozzles (withstand steel temperatures)
 Global steel production (1.9 billion tonnes/yr) consumes ~30 kg
 refractories per tonne of steel → ~50 million tonnes refractories/year

GLASS MANUFACTURING:
 Glass tank ("glass melting furnace"): 1,200-1,600°C continuous operation
 Fireclay + silica + fused cast AZS (alumina-zirconia-silica) blocks
 AZS: high resistance to molten glass corrosion
 Furnace campaigns: 10-15 years before shutdown for rebuild
```

## Bioceramics

```
BIOCERAMIC CLASSIFICATION BY BODY INTERACTION
===============================================

BIOINERT (no reaction with body tissues)
 Materials: Al₂O₃, ZrO₂ (Y-TZP)
 Properties: hard, wear-resistant, non-reactive
 Body response: thin fibrous tissue capsule forms (normal foreign body response)
 Applications: femoral heads (hip replacement), acetabular cups,
               dental crowns and implant abutments, femoral knee condyles

BIOACTIVE (bonds to bone/soft tissue)
 Materials: Hydroxyapatite (HA), Bioglass (45S5)

 HYDROXYAPATITE: Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂
   Natural bone mineral (60-70% of bone by weight)
   Synthetic HA: identical composition; promotes bone ingrowth
   Applications:
    - Coating on Ti implants: HA plasma-sprayed on Ti alloy implants
                              → bone grows into HA layer → strong fixation
    - Bone graft substitute: porous HA blocks/granules for bone void filling
    - Drug delivery: porous HA loaded with antibiotics/growth factors

 BIOGLASS (45S5, Larry Hench 1969, University of Florida):
   Composition: 45% SiO₂, 24.5% Na₂O, 24.5% CaO, 6% P₂O₅
   Mechanism: in body fluids, Ca/P-rich layer forms on surface
              → crystallizes as hydroxycarbonate apatite
              → chemically bonds to bone collagen matrix
              First material discovered to actually bond to bone
   Applications: ossicular replacement (hearing loss surgery),
                 periodontal bone void fill, bone graft

BIORESORBABLE (gradually dissolves in body)
 Materials: Tricalcium phosphate (TCP), calcium sulfate
 Applications: bone void filler (replaced by new bone over 6-18 months),
               drug delivery matrix (controlled release as TCP dissolves)
```

## Electronic Ceramics

```
MULTILAYER CERAMIC CAPACITORS (MLCCs)
======================================
Material: Barium titanate (BaTiO₃) with additives
Annual production: 3-5 TRILLION units per year globally
 (the highest-volume manufactured ceramic product by count)

One smartphone: ~1,000 MLCCs
One automotive ECU: ~1,000-3,000 MLCCs
MLCC function: capacitor (stores and releases electrical charge)
               Filtering, decoupling, timing circuits

Layer thickness in modern MLCC: 0.4-1.0 µm per ceramic layer
 → 100-1000 layers per capacitor in a 1mm × 0.5mm package
 → total ceramic area per capacitor = the active storage volume

PIEZOELECTRIC CERAMICS
=======================
BaTiO₃ and PZT (lead zirconate titanate Pb(Zr,Ti)O₃):
 Piezoelectric effect: deformation → voltage; voltage → deformation
 Applications:
  Ultrasound: PZT transducers in medical imaging (5-50 MHz range)
  Fuel injectors: piezo actuators in common-rail diesel injectors
                   (Bosch, Siemens) — µs response time
  SONAR: naval; underwater acoustic systems
  Accelerometers: vibration sensing in industrial equipment
  Speakers: piezoelectric buzzers (basic audible alarms)
  Inkjet printer heads: piezo-actuated nozzles (not thermal)

PZT environmental concern: contains lead (40-60% by weight)
 RoHS exemption currently maintained for critical applications
 Research: lead-free piezoelectrics (BaTiO₃ composites, KNN — potassium
            sodium niobate) — not yet matching PZT performance

FERRITE CERAMICS
================
Iron oxide-based magnetic ceramics:
 Soft ferrites (MnZn, NiZn ferrites): low coercivity, high permeability
  → transformer cores, inductor cores in power supplies, EMI suppression
  Every switching power supply, every phone charger = ferrite core
 Hard ferrites (BaFe₁₂O₁₉, SrFe₁₂O₁₉): permanent magnets
  → cheap; lower energy density than NdFeB
  → loudspeaker magnets, motors, refrigerator magnets
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Technical ceramics ≠ pottery chemistry**:
Traditional ceramic chemistry is silicate-based (SiO₂ networks). Technical ceramics include oxides (Al₂O₃, ZrO₂), carbides (SiC, B₄C), nitrides (Si₃N₄), and borides — completely different chemistries and processing. The common thread is: inorganic, non-metallic, and fabricated via powder processing + sintering.

**Zirconia in dentistry ≠ CZ in jewelry**:
Cubic zirconia (CZ) in jewelry is single-crystal ZrO₂ grown by skull melting — optically clear, cubic phase. Dental zirconia is polycrystalline Y-TZP — opaque to translucent depending on Y₂O₃ content, engineered for strength not appearance.

**"Refractory" ≠ "hard"**:
Refractories are defined by high-temperature stability, not hardness. Fireclay bricks (soft, easily scratched) are excellent refractories. Diamond is hard but burns in oxygen at high temperature — not a good refractory.

**SiC as semiconductor ≠ SiC as abrasive**:
Same molecule; dramatically different form. Abrasive SiC: macro-grain (coarse grinding), polycrystalline, shape irrelevant. Power semiconductor SiC: ultra-pure single-crystal wafers, grown by Lely process, doped precisely.

|-----------------|---------------|
| Hip/knee joint bearing surface | Zirconia (Y-TZP) or alumina |
| Dental crown (posterior) | Zirconia (CAD-milled) |
