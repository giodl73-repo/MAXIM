# 08 — Industrial Ceramics

## Technical Ceramics, Refractories, Bioceramics — Engineering Applications

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Technical ceramics vs traditional ceramics**: traditional ceramics = silicate-based (clay minerals + quartz + feldspar); technical ceramics (advanced ceramics, engineering ceramics) = chemically pure, precisely controlled compositions; manufactured to specific tolerances; engineered properties rather than aesthetically pleasing properties; applications in electronics, aerospace, medical, cutting tools, armor; the trillion-dollar ceramics industry is dominated by technical ceramics, not tableware
- **Alumina (Al₂O₃)**: most widely used technical ceramic; 96% and 99.5% purity grades; properties: hardness ~9 Mohs, melting point 2,072°C, high dielectric strength, chemically inert; applications: spark plug insulators (the white part — standard automotive); semiconductor substrates; hip and knee joint replacements (femoral heads and acetabular cups — excellent biocompatibility and wear resistance); cutting tool inserts; alumina armor tiles; wear-resistant pump components; cost: moderate (abundant raw material)
- **Zirconia (ZrO₂)**: unusual ceramic — can be ductile at high temperature; "transformation toughening" (phase transformation from tetragonal to monoclinic under stress absorbs crack energy → dramatically higher toughness than other ceramics); stabilized with Y₂O₃ (yttria-stabilized zirconia, YSZ); applications: dental crowns (translucent zirconia, milled from CAD files by dental labs — majority of modern ceramic crowns); oxygen sensors in automotive exhaust (lambda sensor — YSZ conducts O²⁻ ions at operating temperature → electrochemical cell measures oxygen partial pressure); thermal barrier coatings (TBC) on gas turbine blades (YSZ sprayed on nickel superalloy — allows 100-200°C higher operating temperature); femoral heads (hip replacement)
- **Silicon carbide (SiC)**: extreme hardness (Mohs ~9.5); very high thermal conductivity; chemically inert; applications: abrasives (most sandpaper, grinding wheels — SiC or aluminum oxide); kiln furniture and heating elements (SiC tubes for kiln elements); ballistic armor plates; brake discs for high-performance vehicles (carbon-fiber-reinforced SiC); semiconductor material for power electronics (SiC MOSFETs for EVs — handle higher voltage and temperature than silicon); nuclear fuel cladding (accident-tolerant fuel program)
- **Silicon nitride (Si₃N₄)**: excellent combination of strength, toughness, thermal shock resistance; applications: ball bearings for high-speed/corrosive environments (Si₃N₄ balls in hybrid bearings — lighter, harder, less thermally expansive than steel; used in high-performance bicycles, machine tools, wind turbines); cutting tool inserts (replaces carbide in some operations); turbocharger rotors (ceramic rotors = lighter, better response but thermal shock sensitivity managed)
- **Boron carbide (B₄C)**: third hardest material known (after diamond and cubic BN); very low density (2.52 g/cm³); lightweight armor (body armor plates, vehicle armor); neutron absorption (nuclear reactor control); wear-resistant coatings
- **Refractories**: ceramics designed to maintain structural integrity at very high temperatures (above 538°C industrial standard, often 1,000-1,800°C+); essential in steel/aluminum/glass/cement manufacturing; types:
  - Fireclay refractories: alumina-silica; 1,480-1,760°C service; most common; kiln liners
  - High-alumina: >45% Al₂O₃; higher temperatures; steel ladles, furnace roofs
  - Silica: >93% SiO₂; coke ovens, glass furnaces; excellent at constant high temperature but poor thermal cycling
  - Basic (magnesia, chrome-magnesia): alkaline conditions; steel basic oxygen furnaces; cement kilns
  - Castable refractories: refractory aggregate + calcium aluminate cement; pour-in-place lining
- **Bioceramics**: ceramics used in medical applications; biocompatibility is primary requirement; types:
  - **Bioinert** (alumina, zirconia): stable in body, no chemical interaction; load-bearing applications (femoral heads, dental crowns)
  - **Bioactive** (hydroxyapatite, bioglass): bonds to bone; hydroxyapatite (Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂) = natural bone mineral → synthetic HA used as coating on titanium implants to promote bone ingrowth; bioglass (45S5 — Larry Hench, 1969) = specific composition that forms hydroxyapatite layer in body fluid → bonds to bone; used in bone graft substitutes, hearing aid ossicles
  - **Bioresorbable** (tricalcium phosphate, TCP): gradually dissolves in body, replaced by natural bone; bone void fillers, drug delivery
- **Electronic and electrical ceramics**: barium titanate (BaTiO₃) = piezoelectric (produces voltage when deformed, deforms when voltage applied) → sonar transducers, microphones, actuators, fuel injectors, ultrasound probes; PZT (lead zirconate titanate — higher piezoelectric coefficient, used in most piezoelectric devices despite lead content); ferroelectrics and multilayer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs — the ubiquitous component in all electronics: 1-5 trillion MLCCs manufactured annually); ferrites (magnetic ceramics — soft ferrites in transformer cores, hard ferrites in permanent magnets)
