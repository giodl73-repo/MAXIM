# Glazes — Chemistry, Colorants, Surface Types, Firing Atmosphere

## The Big Picture

A glaze is a layer of glass fused to a ceramic surface. The chemistry is silicate glass chemistry — the same family as window glass, optical fiber, and Pyrex — but formulated to melt at pottery temperatures and bond to the clay body.

```
GLAZE STRUCTURE
===============

FIRED GLAZE CROSS-SECTION:
                   ┌───────────────────────────────┐
                   │ ATMOSPHERE / AIR              │
                   ├───────────────────────────────┤ ← surface (gloss/matte/texture)
                   │                               │
                   │    GLASS LAYER                │
                   │    (fused silicate + flux      │
                   │     + colorants)               │
                   │                               │
                   ├───────────────────────────────┤ ← clay-glaze interface
                   │                               │   (interdiffusion zone)
                   │    CLAY BODY                  │
                   │                               │
                   └───────────────────────────────┘

THREE REQUIRED COMPONENTS (Unity Molecular Formula / Seger formula):
 R₂O/RO (flux oxides): lower silica's melting point
   Na₂O, K₂O (from feldspar) — warm, fluid
   CaO (from whiting) — cool, hard surface
   MgO, BaO, ZnO — specialty effects

 Al₂O₃ (alumina): stiffens the melt; prevents flow; clarity
   Too little: glaze too fluid (runs off)
   Too much: underfired look; matte

 SiO₂ (silica): the glass network
   Most of the glaze by weight
   Sources: quartz, feldspar, clay itself
```

## Glaze Chemistry: The Seger / Unity Molecular Formula

```
UNITY MOLECULAR FORMULA (UMF)
==============================
Normalize ALL flux oxides to sum = 1.00
Then express alumina and silica relative to that unity

EXAMPLE: typical Cone 10 stoneware glaze
 RO/R₂O group (fluxes) = 1.00 total:
   0.30 K₂O  (potassium — warm, bright)
   0.30 CaO  (calcium — cool, hard)
   0.40 MgO  (magnesium — satin to matte)
            ──────
            1.00

 Al₂O₃: 0.30 mol  (stiffener — medium value)
 SiO₂:  3.50 mol  (glass network — medium-high)

RATIOS THAT MATTER:
 SiO₂ : Al₂O₃  =  3.50 : 0.30 ≈ 11.7  (typical: 8–13)
 Silica too low: matte, underfired appearance, possible crazing
 Silica too high: difficulty melting; may devitrify (crystallize)

 Al₂O₃ range: 0.25–0.50 typical for studio glazes
 High Al₂O₃ (0.40+): matte surfaces; "refractory matte"
 Low Al₂O₃ (0.15–): very glossy, fluid; crystalline potential

TOOLS:
 Insight (software): commercial glaze calculator; industry standard
 Glazy.org: open-source glaze database + UMF calculator
             10,000+ recipes shared; searchable by temperature/type
 Digitalfire (Tony Hansen): extensive reference; Insight integration
```

## Flux Oxides and Their Effects

```
FLUX              SOURCE                EFFECT AT MATURATION
=====             ======                ====================
K₂O + Na₂O       Feldspar, Cornwall    Warm, fluid, slightly glossy
(combined as      stone, soda ash       Primary flux in most traditional
"KNaO")           frits                 glazes; feldspar provides both
                                        + brings silica and alumina

CaO               Whiting (CaCO₃),      High-fire flux (above 1,100°C);
(calcium)         wollastonite          cool, bright, hard surface;
                                        promotes crystalline textures
                                        at high percentages; most common
                                        high-fire flux

                  talc                  smooth and pleasant; popular in
                                        studio ceramics

ZnO               Zinc oxide            Variable: small amounts promote

                                        or crystalline; sensitive to
                                        atmosphere (can crawl in reduction)

                                        (do not breathe dust; fired = stable)
```

## Glaze Surface Types

```
GLOSS
======
Smooth, reflective; light bounces off uniformly
High silica relative to alumina
Fully melted; surface tension smoothed
Kitchen-tile look; commercial standard for functional ware

MATTE
======
Non-reflective; several mechanisms:
 Alumina matte: excess Al₂O₃ raises melting point → incomplete melt
                Dry, sometimes rough; unpleasant texture
 Calcium matte: high CaO → micro-crystals form at surface during cooling
                Smooth and velvety; highly prized in studio ceramics
 Barium matte: similar to calcium; distinctive
 Zinc matte:   high ZnO; variable results by atmosphere
 Underfired:   partial melt = matte (also = pinholing, crawling risk)

SATIN / VELVET
==============
Between gloss and matte; most sought in studio aesthetics
Very smooth under touch; subtle low reflectivity
Achieved: balanced CaO+MgO; careful firing; mid-range silica

CRYSTALLINE
============
Macro-crystals (1–5 cm) grown within glaze during controlled cooling
```

### Crystalline Glazes

```
CRYSTALLINE GLAZE PROCESS
==========================

Chemistry: high ZnO (25–35%); no alumina (alumina prevents crystal growth);
           high silica; very fluid

Firing schedule (critical):
 1. Rise to peak temperature (~1,285°C)
 2. Crash cool rapidly to crystallization temperature (1,050–1,100°C)
    → zinc silicate (willemite, ZnSiO₄) nucleates
 3. Hold at crystal growth temperature for 2–4 hours
    → crystals grow; longer hold = larger crystals
 4. Cool normally to completion

Requirements:
 Catch plate: glaze is extremely fluid; piece must stand over a
              catch plate (glazed separately) to collect runoff
 Perfect temperature control: electric kiln with digital controller
 Each piece unique: crystal placement random and unpredictable

Japanese tradition: yukizome (snowflake glaze); also German studio ceramics
Contemporary masters: Kelley Breslin, Karl Borgeson, others
```

## Colorant Oxides in Detail

```
IRON (Fe₂O₃ / FeO) — The most versatile
==========================================
%     Oxidation         Reduction        Notes
===   =========         =========        =====
1-2%  Light amber/tan   Celadon green    Classic celadon: 1-3% iron,
                                          reduction, Cone 10
3-5%  Amber/honey       Blue-green       Tenmoku starts developing
5-8%  Rich tan/brown    Gray-green       Partial tenmoku
8-12% Dark brown/black  Tenmoku (black)  Classic Song Dynasty tenmoku
15%+  Saturated glaze   Metallic black   "Persimmon" in reduction;
                         (iron red)       oil-spot (bubbles burst)

TENMOKU: Jian ware (Song dynasty) — black glaze with oil-spot
         hare's fur, rust spot variations; tea bowl aesthetic

COBALT (CoO) — Most powerful
============================
0.25-0.5%: Strong, clean blue
0.5-1%:    Vivid to deep blue
1-2%:      Deep to very deep blue
2-5%:      Nearly black
Not atmosphere-sensitive (stays blue in oxidation and reduction)
Historical: Yuan/Ming blue-and-white = cobalt imported from Persia

COPPER (CuO) — Most atmosphere-sensitive
=========================================
Oxidation:    1-3%: turquoise; 3-5%: green; +CaO → bright
Reduction:    0.5-2%: copper red (sang de boeuf) — spectacular
              "Flambe": red + purple/blue streaks
              "Peach bloom": pale pink + green areas
Requirement:  precise reduction — too much → metallic gray; too little → green
History:      Song Dynasty Jun ware blue-purple; flambe from copper

MANGANESE (MnO₂)
=================
2-5%: purple-brown
5-10%: warm black
+ Cobalt: deep rich black
+ Cobalt + iron: warm black with surface texture
In clay body: Manganese dioxide in stoneware clay = "manganese speckling"

CHROME (Cr₂O₃)
===============
1-3% alone: stable green (all atmospheres, all temperatures)
+ Tin: pink / salmon / coral (chrome-tin reaction)
+ Lead (historical): brilliant red-orange (now restricted; toxic)
Do NOT use chrome + tin in same kiln load: chrome vapors contaminate
tin-glazed ware → pink/gray contamination on supposedly white pieces

TITANIUM (TiO₂)
===============
2-5% alone: creamy white opacifier; slight warmth
+ Iron (rutile): promotes crystal growth; warm tans/oranges/golds;
                  "rutile" texture (swirling, breaking color)
                  Rutile (TiO₂ + FeO mix) = same effect from natural mineral

TIN (SnO₂) — THE OPACIFIER
============================
8-12%: fully opaque white
Traditional opacifier for white background (majolica/delftware)
Expensive: replaced in modern industrial by zircon (cheaper)
Tin gives different quality of white: warmer, livelier than zirconia
```

## Glaze Application

```
APPLICATION METHODS
====================

DIPPING (best):
 Bisqueware submerged in bucket of glaze slip
 Even coat throughout; fast; professional
 Requirement: uniform glaze specific gravity (1.40–1.50 g/cm³ typical)
 Thickness: 1–2mm wet coat; ~0.5–0.8mm fired

POURING:
 Pour over and into piece
 Used for large pieces; complex forms
 Combination: pour interior; dip exterior

BRUSHING:
 Single-stroke brushwork; deliberate texture
 Japanese tradition (hakeme = brush marks as decoration)
 Risk: brush marks visible; multiple coats for coverage

SPRAYING:
 Compressed air gun + spray booth
 Even thin coat on large/complex forms
 Silica dust hazard: essential ventilation + respirator
 Industrial standard for large production

WAX RESIST:
 Hot wax (beeswax) or commercial cold wax resist
 Brush on surfaces where glaze should NOT adhere
 → design lines, bands, masked areas; wax burns off in firing

THICKNESS CONTROL:
 Too thick: crawling (glaze shrinks, pulls back, bare spots)
 Too thin: dry looking, pinholing, pale color
 Test: scratch test with fingernail; 1mm wet impression = correct
 Specific gravity: hydrometer measurement of glaze suspension density
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Lead-glazed maiolica isn't toxic when fired**:
Raw lead oxide in a glaze is toxic (handle with full precautions). Fired lead glaze is stable at normal cooking temperatures. However, acid foods (vinegar, citrus) can leach lead from underfired or craized lead glazes — hence the modern food-safety regulations that effectively eliminated lead-glazed production.

**"Food-safe glaze" ≠ archival glaze**:
A food-safe fired glaze doesn't leach metals at detectable levels from normal use. Whether a studio-mixed glaze is food-safe requires testing — you can't determine it visually. Commercial food-safe certified glazes are tested; self-formulated glazes may not be.

**Glaze color in the bucket ≠ fired color**:
Most unfired glaze materials are gray, brown, or white. The color only develops in firing. Cobalt carbonate (blue) fires blue; iron (red-brown powder) fires tan-to-black depending on atmosphere. This is the most common surprise for beginners.

**Crazing is not always a defect**:
In Guan ware (Song Dynasty), crazing is a deliberate and celebrated aesthetic. In a functional cup intended for hot liquids, crazing risks leaking and harboring bacteria.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Glaze Approach |
|------|---------------|
| Food-safe white opaque earthenware | Tin glaze (8-12% SnO₂) + lead-free flux |
| Blue decoration on white | Cobalt oxide underglaze + clear glaze over |
| Celadon (jade green) | 1-2% iron, high-fire reduction Cone 10 |
| Copper red (sang de boeuf) | 0.5-1.5% copper, gas reduction Cone 10 |
| Matte satin surface | CaO + MgO flux; medium Al₂O₃ |
| Crystalline surface | High ZnO, no Al₂O₃, precise controlled cooling |
| Maximum visual surface interest | Rutile (TiO₂ + Fe) = breaking, unpredictable |
| Classical tenmoku | 8-12% iron, gas reduction Cone 10-12 |
| Orange-peel texture without salt kiln | Crystalline or specific crawl glaze |
| Bright commercial colors | Low-fire commercial stains (Cone 06 range) |
