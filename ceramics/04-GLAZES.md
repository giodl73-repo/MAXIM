# 04 — Glazes

## Chemistry, Colorants, Surface Types, Firing Atmosphere

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **What a glaze is**: a layer of glass fused to the ceramic surface; primary function = make porous earthenware liquid-tight; secondary = decoration and protection; chemistry: silica (glass former, SiO₂) + flux oxides (lower melting point) + alumina (Al₂O₃, stiffens melt, controls flow, prevents crystallization) = basic glaze; colorants and opacifiers added to base
- **Glaze chemistry (Seger formula / Unity Molecular Formula)**: the standard system for expressing glaze chemistry; all oxides normalized so that total flux oxides (RO/R₂O group) = 1.00; then alumina (Al₂O₃) and silica (SiO₂) ratios relative to that unity; typical stoneware glaze at Cone 10: 0.3 K₂O + 0.3 CaO + 0.4 MgO : 0.3 Al₂O₃ : 3.5 SiO₂; Silica:Alumina ratio critical (SiO₂:Al₂O₃ = 8:1 to 13:1 typical); too low silica = matte, underfire look; too high silica = hard to melt, may crystallize; glaze calculation software (Insight, Glazy.org — open source glaze database + calculator)
- **Flux oxides and their effects**:
  - CaO (calcium oxide, from whiting/wollastonite): most common high-fire flux; cool, bright, hard surface; promotes crystalline texture in some glazes
  - KNaO (potassium + sodium, from feldspar): warm, fluid; primary source in most traditional glazes; K₂O slightly higher gloss than Na₂O
  - MgO (magnesium, from dolomite/talc): high-temperature flux only; matte surfaces at high percentage; satiny; "magnesia matte" glazes
  - BaO (barium carbonate): toxic raw; creates distinctive satiny matte; use carefully
  - ZnO (zinc oxide): variable — small amounts promote gloss; larger amounts → matte crystalline or opaque; sensitive to atmosphere
  - PbO (lead oxide): historically the universal low-fire flux (brilliant, fluid, flexible — accommodates many colorants beautifully); now banned/restricted for food ware due to leaching toxicity; historical majolica, tin-glaze colors depended on lead; modern replacements less satisfactory for some color effects
- **Silica sources**: flint, quartz, silica sand; raw silica (in glazes) vs fired quartz; free silica in raw glaze materials is a significant health hazard (silicosis) — wet processing, dust masks essential
- **Glaze surface types**:
  - **Gloss**: smooth, reflective; high silica relative to alumina; fully melted
  - **Matte**: non-reflective, smooth or slightly rough; achieved by: high alumina (refractory matte), calcium matte (excess calcium), barium matte, zinc matte, underfiring, or crawling; can be silky-smooth or dry and rough
  - **Satin/velvet**: between gloss and matte; highly valued in studio ceramics
  - **Crystalline**: zinc silicate crystals grown in glaze during slow, controlled cooling; spectacular macro-crystals (cm scale); requires very precise firing schedule (rapid rise to peak, specific cooling rate to maintain crystallization temperature 1,050-1,100°C for zinc silicate); pure glaze (no alumina — lets crystals grow); catch plate essential (glaze very fluid, drips); master crystalline glazers = separate specialty; Japanese yukizome tradition
  - **Crawling**: glaze pulls back from surface during firing, exposing bare clay; defect in most contexts; used deliberately for texture; caused by high shrinkage differential, dust/oil contamination, excessively thick application
- **Colorant oxides and their effects**:
  - Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃/FeO): most important; oxidation = tans, ambers, browns, black (high %); reduction = greens (celadon), blue-green, black (tenmoku), tea-dust; 1-15% range covers vast spectrum
  - Cobalt oxide (CoO): most powerful colorant; 0.2-1% = strong blue; 2-5% = deep to black blue; very stable in oxidation and reduction; historical Chinese blue-and-white porcelain = cobalt oxide
  - Copper oxide/carbonate (CuO/CuCO₃): oxidation = turquoise, green; reduction = copper red (sang de boeuf — "ox blood"), flambe, peach bloom; legendary Song Dynasty Jun and Flambe glazes; very sensitive to small atmosphere changes
  - Manganese dioxide (MnO₂): purple, brown, warm black; with cobalt = rich dark
  - Chrome oxide (Cr₂O₃): stable green at most temperatures; with tin → pink/red (chrome-tin pink/coral); with lead → brilliant red (now restricted)
  - Titanium dioxide (TiO₂): opacifier; promotes crystals; warm ivory when combined with iron (rutile)
  - Tin oxide (SnO₂): primary traditional opacifier; 8-12% = white opaque (tin glaze basis for majolica/delftware/faience); expensive; alternatives = zirconia/zircon opacifiers (cheaper, slightly different quality)
- **Glaze application**: dipping (best for uniform coating), pouring (for large/complex shapes), brushing (deliberate texture, single coats), spraying (even thin coat, requires spray booth); glaze thickness critical (1-2mm wet = ~0.5-0.8mm fired); too thick = crawling; too thin = dry look, bare spots; wax resist (beeswax or commercial) prevents glaze adhesion for design effects
