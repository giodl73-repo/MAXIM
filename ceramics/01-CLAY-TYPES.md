# Clay Types — Earthenware, Stoneware, Porcelain, Kaolin

## The Big Picture

Clay is not a single mineral — it's a family of hydrated phyllosilicate minerals that share one critical property: plasticity when wet. Understanding what clay is at the atomic level explains all its working properties and why certain clays make good porcelain while others make good bricks.

```
CLAY MINERAL TAXONOMY
======================

PHYLLOSILICATES (sheet silicates) — the clay mineral family
 SiO₄ tetrahedra: silicon surrounded by 4 oxygen atoms
 Al octahedral layers: aluminum surrounded by 6 oxygen/hydroxyl
 Layers stack with hydroxyl groups (-OH) between them → plasticity

KEY MINERALS IN CERAMIC CLAYS
 Kaolinite     Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄   1:1 structure  Low plasticity, fires white
 Illite        (similar to mica) 2:1 structure  Medium plasticity
 Smectite      2:1 structure     High plasticity (montmorillonite = bentonite)
 Halloysite    1:1 tubular       Medium; some porcelain use
 Chlorite      2:1:1 structure   Low; contamination in some clays

WHY PLASTICITY WORKS:
 Clay platelets (hexagonal, 0.5–10µm)
 Water film between platelets
 ↓
 Platelets slide over each other (no friction — water lubricates)
 ↓
 Clay deforms under pressure without breaking
 ↓
 Remove water (dry) → van der Waals forces between platelets → rigid
 ↓
 Fire → structural hydroxyl groups leave → permanent transformation
```

## Kaolin (China Clay)

The defining ingredient of porcelain. The word "kaolin" derives from "Gaoling" (高嶺 = High Ridge) — a hill near Jingdezhen, China.

```
KAOLIN PROPERTIES
==================
Formula:       Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄ (kaolinite mineral)
Crystal:       1:1 phyllosilicate, triclinic
Platelet size: 1–5 µm (coarser than ball clay → less plastic)
Fired color:   White to off-white (low Fe content)
Plasticity:    Low (must blend with plastic clays)
Melting range: 1,750–1,800°C (refractory on its own)

FORMATION:
 Hydrothermal alteration of feldspar in granite:
 KAlSi₃O₈ (feldspar) + H₂O + CO₂ → Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄ (kaolin)
                                      + K₂CO₃ + 4SiO₂
 "Rotted granite" — kaolin deposits follow granite intrusion zones

WORLD DEPOSITS:
 Cornwall, UK:     St Austell; Europe's largest; supplied Wedgwood
                    and global ceramics for 200+ years; "china clay"
 Limoges, France:  Exceptional kaolin; Limoges porcelain center
 Saxony, Germany:  Colditz; Meissen's source (1710 "discovery")
 Georgia, USA:     Highest-purity kaolin for paper/industrial use
 Brazil:           Large reserves; growing supplier
 Jingdezhen, China: Exhausted but historically the defining source

PRIMARY GRADES:
 Calcined kaolin:    Pre-fired; reduces shrinkage in glazes/bodies
 Water-washed kaolin: Removed impurities; brighter white
 Air-floated kaolin: Particle size graded by air classification
```

## Ball Clay

The plasticity agent. Named for the balls (30–50 kg lumps) originally cut by hand from quarries in Devon and Dorset, UK.

```
BALL CLAY PROPERTIES
=====================
Formula:       Kaolinite-dominant but with smectite/illite;
               + organic material (humic acids) → dark gray when wet
Fired color:   Off-white to cream (slight organic staining possible)
Plasticity:    Very high (fine particle size, organic content)
               Too plastic alone: dries slowly, cracks, high shrinkage
               Used as 10-25% of porcelain/stoneware body

WORKING WITH BALL CLAY:
 Wet: gray to black (organic matter)
 Dry: pale gray
 Fired: off-white (organics burn out; iron content ~1-2%)

Key UK ball clay deposits: Devon, Dorset (still primary global source)
Also: Germany (Westerwald), USA, Australia, Turkey
```

## Fire Clay

High-alumina refractory clay. Withstands temperatures that would melt standard pottery clays.

```
FIRE CLAY PROPERTIES
=====================
Al₂O₃ content:  30–45% (vs ~20% for standard earthenware clay)
Service temp:   1,500–1,750°C
Plasticity:     Low to medium
Fired color:    Gray, buff, off-white
Texture:        Often coarse; used with grog additions

APPLICATIONS:
 Kiln furniture: Shelves, posts, props (cordierite often preferred)
 Saggers: Protective containers in salt/wood kilns
 Kiln liners: Firebrick construction
 Industrial furnaces: Steel ladle liners, boiler linings
 Grog: Crushed pre-fired fire clay added to clay bodies to:
   - Reduce shrinkage (pre-fired particles don't shrink)
   - Increase thermal shock resistance (open texture)
   - Add texture to surface
   - Reduce warping risk in thick pieces
```

## Stoneware Clay

The workhorse of functional ceramics. Fires dense and strong at 1,200–1,300°C.

```
STONEWARE CLAY PROPERTIES
==========================
Fe₂O₃ content:   1–5% (gives gray/brown/speckled body after firing)
Plasticity:       Good (medium particle size, some organic content)
Firing range:     Cone 4–10 (1,180–1,285°C)
Vitrification:    Yes (glass phase develops; body becomes dense)
Fired absorption: <2% (vs 10–15% for earthenware)
Color:            Gray, buff, tan, brown (depending on iron + atmosphere)

KEY REGIONAL STONEWARE CLAYS:
 Westerwald (Germany): historically the Rhineland salt-glaze stoneware
 St Austell, UK: blended stoneware bodies
 Kentucky/Tennessee: Laguna, Sheffield bodies in US market
 Jingdezhen-region: traditional Chinese stoneware
 Korean: Buncheong (gray-blue stoneware) and celadon traditions
```

## Porcelain Body

The pinnacle. Requires the right combination of materials — kaolin (whiteness + structure), feldspar (glass/flux), and silica (glass network).

```
STANDARD PORCELAIN BODY (approximate)
======================================
Kaolin:     25% → whiteness, fired structure
Ball clay:  25% → plasticity for workability
Feldspar:   25% → glass phase at temperature (K₂O, Na₂O flux)
Silica/quartz: 25% → glass network former; controls thermal expansion

Fires at: 1,260–1,320°C (Cone 6–10)
Color:    White; slightly translucent in thin section
Absorption: <0.5% (essentially non-porous)
Strength:  High (mullite + glass matrix)

WORKING CHALLENGES:
 Low plasticity: requires skill; wheel-throwing porcelain harder
                  than stoneware (less "forgiveness")
 High shrinkage: 12–18% total (wet to fired); must compensate
 Warping tendency: dense body; even walls essential
 Cost: higher raw material cost vs stoneware

TRANSLUCENCY PHYSICS:
 Thin porcelain walls (~1–2mm): light passes through
 Glass phase = transparent; mullite crystals = opaque
 More glass phase → more translucency → higher quality porcelain
 Bone china: highest translucency (bone ash addition increases
              glass phase substantially)
```

### Bone China

```
BONE CHINA (English soft-paste variant)
=========================================
Formula:    ~50% calcined bone ash (Ca₃(PO₄)₂)
            + ~25% Cornish stone (feldspathic rock)
            + ~25% kaolin
Firing:     ~1,200–1,250°C (lower than hard-paste porcelain)
Appearance: Very white, high translucency, lightweight

BONE ASH EFFECT:
 Tricalcium phosphate Ca₃(PO₄)₂ forms glass phase at lower temp
 Creates more translucent, whiter body than feldspathic porcelain
 Softer than hard-paste → can be dented with metal cutlery

HISTORY:
 Josiah Spode II (~1800): standardized the formula
 Standard for English fine tableware since 19th century:
 Wedgwood bone china, Royal Doulton, Minton, Spode, Royal Worcester

HARD-PASTE vs SOFT-PASTE vs BONE CHINA:
 Hard-paste: kaolin + petuntse; 1,260–1,400°C; true Chinese porcelain
             Meissen, Sèvres (after 1768), Worcester (some)
 Soft-paste: ground glass + clay; 1,100–1,200°C; pre-hard-paste European
             Chelsea, early Worcester, Vincennes
 Bone china:  bone ash + kaolin + Cornish stone; ~1,250°C; English standard
```

## Clay Body Preparation

```
PREPARATION STEPS
==================

1. WET MIXING (BLUNGING)
   Clay materials + water → blunger (paddle mixer)
   → homogeneous slip (liquid clay suspension)
   Deflocculant added for slip casting (sodium silicate + Na₂CO₃)
   → reduces water needed for fluid slip

2. FILTER PRESSING
   Slip pumped between filter cloth plates under pressure
   Water forced out → "filter cakes" (~20% moisture)
   Industrial standard; consistent moisture content

3. PUGMILL EXTRUSION
   De-airing pugmill: vacuum chamber removes air bubbles
   → extrudes uniform clay column
   Air in clay = weakness; air pockets → pinholes in fired ware

4. WEDGING (studio/hand method)
   Two types:
   Ram's head: roll clay over itself; kneads from top
   Spiral: spiral pattern; more thorough air removal
   Goal: uniform consistency, no air pockets, aligned platelets

5. AGING (optional)
   Store wedged clay in sealed bags, moist
   Bacterial activity releases organic acids → increases plasticity
   Traditional Japanese potters: age clay 1–5 years
   Improvement: noticeable even after 2–4 weeks
```

## Shrinkage

The unavoidable dimension-change that potters must account for:

```
SHRINKAGE STAGES
=================

WET CLAY → LEATHER HARD: ~5–10% linear shrinkage
 Free water leaves; platelets compact

LEATHER HARD → BONE DRY: further 2–5%
 Remaining free water leaves

BONE DRY → FIRED: 5–15% linear (depending on body type)
 Water of crystallization leaves; glass phase forms; body densifies

TOTAL (wet to fired): 10–25% linear shrinkage
 A bowl thrown at 25cm diameter → fired at ~20cm if 20% shrinkage

FACTORS AFFECTING SHRINKAGE:
 High plasticity clay: more shrinkage (fine particles pack closer)
 Grog additions: reduce shrinkage (pre-fired particles don't shrink)
 Firing temperature: higher temp → more glass phase → more densification
 Wall thickness: uneven walls dry and fire unevenly → warping/cracking

TESTING:
 Standard: throw or roll a test tile; measure wet, dry, fired dimensions
 Calculate linear shrinkage % = (wet - fired) / wet × 100
```

## Special Clays and Materials

| Material | Description | Use |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| **Bentonite** | Sodium smectite; extremely fine platelets; swells in water | 1–3% addition dramatically increases plasticity |
| **Paper clay** | Clay + cellulose paper fiber | Reduces cracking; joins wet-to-dry; sculpture |
| **Terracotta** | Iron-rich earthenware; red/orange in oxidation | Pots, figures, tiles, bricks |
| **Raku body** | High-grog content | Thermal shock resistance (rapid heating/cooling) |
| **Colored slips** | Clay + oxides (slip) | Surface coloration; slip trailing; sgraffito |
| **Terra sigillata** | Extremely fine deflocculated slip; burnished | Roman red/black gloss ware technique |
| **Cordierite** | Magnesium iron silicate mineral (not clay) | Kiln furniture; exceptional thermal shock resistance |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Kaolin" and "china clay" are the same thing**:
Kaolin = the mineral kaolinite. China clay = the industrial mined product which is largely kaolinite. Same material, different register (technical vs commercial).

**More plasticity ≠ better for porcelain**:
Ball clay is extremely plastic but gives a cream/off-white body. Porcelain requires whiteness (kaolin) balanced with workability (ball clay + feldspar). Too much ball clay → cream color, excessive shrinkage.

**Porcelain is not intrinsically fragile**:
Dense, fully vitrified porcelain is actually harder and more abrasion-resistant than earthenware. Its perceived fragility is about the thin wall thicknesses typical in fine tableware — not the material itself.

**Grog = pre-fired clay**:
Grog is simply fired clay ground to particles. Adding grog to a clay body introduces particles that don't shrink, don't absorb water, and add thermal mass. Essential for sculpture, raku, large-scale work.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Clay Choice |
|------|------------|
| First throwing lessons | Commercial stoneware body (Cone 6; forgiving) |
| Fine translucent tableware | Porcelain body or bone china |
| Outdoor/frost-resistant pots | Dense stoneware (vitrified) |
| Large sculpture | Grogged stoneware or paper clay |
| Raku firing | High-grog body (thermal shock) |
| Slip casting complex forms | Slip body: kaolin + ball clay + deflocculant |
| Maximum whiteness | Porcelain or bone china |
| Natural earth tones | Terracotta or natural stoneware |
| Most plastic throwing body | Ball clay-heavy blend (accepts bentonite) |
