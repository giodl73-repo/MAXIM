# Decoration — Underglaze, Overglaze, Reduction, Luster, Majolica

## The Big Picture

Ceramic decoration is fundamentally about the relationship between temperature and permanence. Decorations fired at high temperature are permanent but limit your palette. Decorations fired at low temperature have full palette options but are more vulnerable. The choices cascade through the entire production process.

**Process scheduling bridge:** The temperature/permanence trade-off in ceramic decoration is the same commit/flexibility tension that governs process scheduling in manufacturing and software. Underglaze decisions are made early and fired into glass at full temperature — they are immutable once the glaze fires. Overglaze decisions are made after the main fire — more palette freedom, lower commitment temperature, but less durability. Luster is the last step, lowest temperature, maximum palette freedom, minimum permanence. This is exactly the architecture of any staged manufacturing process: decisions made early in the pipeline are cheaper to execute and harder to undo; decisions deferred to late stages retain flexibility but add cost and fragility. The tradeoff is identical whether you're scheduling a fabrication run or decorating a porcelain plate.

## Slip Decoration

Applied to wet or leather-hard clay — the oldest ceramic surface technique.

```
SLIP = liquid clay suspension
 Clay + water; sometimes colorant oxides added
 Applied to clay body at similar moisture level

SLIP TRAILING
=============
Equipment: rubber bulb, bottle with pin tool in nozzle
           or slip trailer (specialized tool)
Technique: trail lines of slip in patterns on clay surface
           Applied to soft or leather-hard clay
           Allows: linear designs, text, botanical patterns
Result:    Raised lines (slight relief if thick application)
           English North Devon slipware; Staffordshire slipware

FEATHERING
==========
Technique: trail parallel lines of contrasting slip →
           drag fine tool (pin, feather) across lines at 90°
           → feathered or combed pattern in wet slip
Classic: English lead-glazed slipware trays; combed terracotta

SGRAFFITO
=========
Technique: leather-hard piece coated with slip of contrasting color
           Scratch through top slip layer with pointed tool
           → reveals underlying body or different slip
Result:    Lines, patterns, pictorial in negative space
Used by:   Bernard Leach; Korean celadon (reverse sgraffito);
           contemporary studio potters
```

## Underglaze

High-temperature-stable colorants applied before glazing. The cobalt connection: cobalt was the only reliably stable blue at high fire for centuries.

```
UNDERGLAZE PAINTING
====================

COBALT BLUE TRADITION:
 Cobalt oxide (CoO) or cobalt carbonate (CoCO₃)
 Mixed with clay + water + gum arabic (binder)
 Applied by brush to bisqueware
 Clear glaze applied over; fired to maturation
 Result: vivid blue design under glass surface; durable

HISTORY OF BLUE-AND-WHITE:
 Persian/Islamic potters (9th century): began cobalt underglaze work
 Yuan Dynasty China (14th century): Jingdezhen perfects technique
                                     Large-scale cobalt import from Persia
 Ming Dynasty: canonical blue-and-white; global export
 Ottoman Iznik (16th century): added green (copper), red (Armenian bole)
 Delft (17th century): imitates Chinese blue-and-white
 Wedgwood (18th century): transfer-printed blue-and-white

COMMERCIAL UNDERGLAZE COLORS:
 Factory-prepared: metal oxide colorant + clay + gum binder
 Fired stable to high temperatures (Cone 06 to Cone 10 depending on brand)
 Full palette: not limited to cobalt blue anymore
 Applied: brush, sponge, stamp, stencil
 Must be covered by clear glaze for full color development
 Some fire brighter with satin vs gloss overglaze
```

## Majolica Painting (In-Glaze Technique)

Painting on the surface of raw (unfired) tin glaze. The paint sinks into the wet glaze during firing — one integrated layer, not paint on top.

```
MAJOLICA PROCESS
=================

1. Bisque fire the earthenware piece
2. Dip in raw (unfired) tin glaze (thick white suspension)
3. Allow to dry (surface feels like chalk)
4. PAINT directly on dried tin glaze surface with metal oxide colors
   The surface absorbs color immediately (like watercolor on dry paper)
   NO corrections possible — each stroke permanent
5. Fire to glaze maturation (typically Cone 04–02, ~1,063–1,120°C)
   → colors flux into glaze; surface becomes glass
   → "in-glaze" decoration (between the two layers)

COLORS (traditional, lead-based historically):
 Cobalt:    blue
 Copper:    green (turquoise in oxidation)
 Manganese: purple/brown
 Antimony:  yellow (lead antimonate — bright yellow; requires lead)
 Iron:      orange/brown/black
 Modern lead-free: commercial in-glaze colors replace historical palette

REGIONAL TRADITIONS:
 Faenza (Italy): gave the name "faïence"; white tin-glaze, blue + purple
 Deruta (Umbria): luster AND majolica; wide palette; still a major center
 Gubbio (Umbria): gold and ruby luster achieved (Maestro Giorgio Andreoli)
 Urbino: istoriato — narrative scenes covering entire surface;
          Raphael-inspired; workshops worked from printed engravings
 Cafaggiolo, Castel Durante, Siena: other major Italian centers

ISTORIATO WARE (story-painted):
 Complete narrative scenes on large dishes and albarelli (apothecary jars)
 Religious stories, mythological scenes (derived from Raphael prints)
 Every centimeter painted; blue + yellow + orange + green + purple
 The highest status Italian maiolica form (15th-16th c.)
```

## Transfer Printing

Mechanical reproduction enables consistent, fine-detail decoration at industrial scale.

```
TRANSFER PRINTING PROCESS
==========================
1. Design engraved on copper plate
2. Ceramic ink applied to plate; wiped clean (ink stays in engraved lines)
3. Tissue paper pressed onto plate → picks up ink
4. Tissue pressed onto bisqueware
5. Piece soaked in water; tissue floats off; ink sticks to clay
6. Glaze applied over top
7. Fired → glaze melts; ink fuses permanently

FLOW BLUE (Victorian fashion):
 Cobalt blue transfer printing fired in humid/heavy atmosphere
 → cobalt "flows" slightly beyond print lines = soft, blurry appearance
 Fashionable 1820s–1900; collectible antique category

WILLOW PATTERN (c.1790, Thomas Minton):
 Most famous transfer print ever; adapted from Chinese scenes
 Copied endlessly by all English manufacturers
 Blue on white; pagoda, willow tree, bridge, dove, boat
 Mass-produced but became iconic "Chinese" pattern
 (Actually an English design — no authentic Chinese original)
```

## Decals (Ceramic Decals)

```
CERAMIC DECAL PROCESS
======================
1. Design printed with ceramic ink on water-slide paper (transfer paper)
2. Paper coated with covercoat (temporary holding layer)
3. Application: soak in water; slide decal off backing; position on
               already-fired glazed surface
4. Smooth out air bubbles with squeegee
5. Dry completely
6. Fire at 750–820°C (third fire)
   Covercoat burns off; ceramic inks fuse to glaze surface

RESULT: permanent, washable decoration
        Can achieve photographic quality
        Commercial ware standard (restaurant china, souvenir mugs)

STUDIO USE: fine art limited-edition ceramic works use custom-printed decals
            Edmund de Waal: conceptual use of text decals on porcelain
```

## Luster Decoration

The most technically demanding surface decoration. Islamic potters invented it in 9th-century Iraq; the technique spread to Egypt, Spain, Italy.

**Thin-film optics:** Luster produces color by thin-film interference — the same physics as anti-reflective coatings on camera lenses, oil films on water, and silicon nitride passivation layers on semiconductor wafers. A metal film 50–500 nm thick causes partial reflection at both the air-film and film-glaze interfaces. The two reflected beams interfere constructively or destructively depending on film thickness and wavelength, producing the characteristic iridescent color shift. Thicker films shift the interference peak toward longer wavelengths (red/gold); thinner films toward shorter wavelengths (silver/blue). Islamic potters empirically controlled this by varying metal salt concentration and reduction atmosphere; modern materials engineers do the same with PVD deposition time and pressure.

```
LUSTER TECHNIQUE
================

Chemistry:
 Metal salts (silver nitrate AgNO₃, copper sulfate CuSO₄,
              gold chloride, platinum compounds)
 Mixed in organic medium (vinegar, ochre, bismuth)
 Painted on already-fired glaze surface
 Fired at 600–700°C in REDUCTION atmosphere
 → organic medium burns; metal ions REDUCED to metallic state
 → thin metallic film (20–500 nm) forms at glaze surface
 → light interference → iridescent luster

COLORS:
 Silver:    yellow-gold (AgNO₃)
 Copper:    red-gold, green-gold (different atmospheres)
 Gold:      deep gold (gold chloride — most expensive)
 Platinum:  mother-of-pearl, gray

ATMOSPHERE CONTROL (critical):
 Must reduce during luster firing
 Too much reduction: dull gray metallic (not iridescent)
 Too little: color doesn't develop
 Saggar with organic material or specialized reduction kiln

HISTORICAL TRADITION:
 Abbasid Iraq (9th c.): first tin-glazed + luster ware; Baghdad/Samarra
 Fatimid Egypt (10th-12th c.): finest early examples; Cairo
 Moorish Spain (11th-15th c.): Málaga → Valencia (Manises);
                                 Hispano-Moresque luster;
                                 Alhambra tile borders
 Italian Renaissance (15th-16th c.): Gubbio (Maestro Giorgio);
                                       gold and ruby luster on maiolica
 Contemporary: Alan Caiger-Smith (Aldermaston Pottery, UK);
               revived Islamic techniques; extensive research

IRIDESCENCE PHYSICS:
 Thin film (20–500nm) on glaze surface
 Light reflects from both metal film surface AND glaze surface below
 Path length difference → constructive/destructive interference
 → different wavelengths reinforce or cancel → iridescent color shift
 Same physics as: oil film on water, butterfly wings, peacock feathers
 → but controlled by metal reduction
```

## Reduction and Black/Black Ware

```
MARIA MARTINEZ BLACK-ON-BLACK WARE
====================================

Origin: San Ildefonso Pueblo, New Mexico; ancient tradition revived c.1918
Potters: Maria Martinez + Julian Martinez (slip/design division)

TECHNIQUE:
1. Coil-build vessel (all hand-built — no wheel)
2. Allow to leather-hard stage
3. Burnish surface thoroughly with smooth stone or bone
   (compresses clay surface to high gloss)
4. Apply slip decoration in matte areas (contrast motif)
   OR burnish design areas vs leave matte background
5. Fire in outdoor open fire (oxidation) → piece fires red-orange first
6. At peak, smother fire with fine powdery ash or cow dung
   → heavy reduction + smothering → carbon penetrates into clay
7. Cool; unwrap; polish with cloth

RESULT:
 Burnished areas: high-gloss black (carbon + compressed surface)
 Matte slip areas: matte black (open surface, different carbon density)
 Difference between gloss and matte = the design

NOT A GLAZE: entirely clay-based surface technique; no glass layer
             The "gloss" is from compression + carbon, not glass
```

## Western Raku Post-Firing Decoration

```
WESTERN RAKU EFFECTS
=====================
(different from Japanese raku tradition — see 09-STUDIO-CERAMICS)

Process:
 Piece fired to maturation (Cone 06–04)
 Removed from kiln while glowing red-hot (~900°C) with metal tongs
 Placed in metal can with combustibles (newspaper, sawdust, straw)
 Can covered → combustibles ignite → heavy reduction atmosphere

EFFECTS:
 Metallic luster: carbon reduces metal oxides in glaze → metallic surface
 Crackle: rapid cooling → glaze crazes → carbon deposits in cracks
 Bare clay (carbon flash): unglazed areas absorb carbon → black
 Horse-hair raku: horse hair (or feathers, grass) laid on red-hot piece
                   immediately out of kiln → carbon traces where hair touches

NAKED RAKU (no glaze):
 Apply slip over bisqued piece; apply resist (paper, tape)
 Fire to low temperature; remove; post-fire reduction
 Slip peels off → exposes carbon-flashed bare clay
 Organic, primitive aesthetic
```

## Screen Printing and Modern Methods

```
INDUSTRIAL SURFACE DECORATION
==============================

Screen printing:
 Ceramic ink forced through fine mesh screen (squeegee method)
 Used for: tiles, large production runs, commercial plates
 Allows precise repeatable design; multi-color registration

Digital inkjet printing:
 Ceramic ink through modified inkjet printer heads
 Fired to fix; full photographic reproduction
 Used for: photo-realistic tile decoration, custom products
 Resolution: near-photographic; limited by ceramic ink particle size
 Industrial tile decoration standard since 2010s

Pad printing:
 Transfer from engraved plate via soft silicone pad
 Used for: logos, small marks on curved surfaces

Spray stenciling:
 Stencil held; airbrush sprays ceramic underglaze through openings
 Studio technique; produces soft-edge designs
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Underglaze vs in-glaze vs overglaze**:
- Underglaze: painted ON bisqueware, COVERED by transparent glaze, fired together → permanent, very durable
- In-glaze (majolica): painted ON raw (unfired) glaze surface, fuses into glaze during single firing → permanent, durable
- Overglaze: painted ON already-fired glaze, fired at lower temperature separately → permanent but slightly more vulnerable to abrasion than fired-under glass

**Blue-and-white ≠ always Chinese**:
Delft (Dutch), Wedgwood (English), Meissen (German), Iznik (Turkish) all made blue-and-white using the same cobalt underglaze technique in imitation of Chinese originals. The design vocabulary tells them apart.

**Luster ≠ metallic paint**:
Luster is a chemical reduction process that creates a true thin metallic film fused to the glaze. Commercial "gold luster" or "platinum luster" from pottery suppliers is a suspension of precious metal in organic medium — it produces similar visual effects but via a different mechanism (simpler, less dramatic).

**Western raku ≠ Japanese raku**:
Japanese raku: low-fire, lead-glazed, hand-built tea bowls; wabi aesthetic; subtle; 400-year tradition.
Western raku (Soldner's adaptation): post-firing reduction; dramatic carbon effects; metallic; theatrical. Different goals, different results, same name.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Technique |
|------|-----------|
| Permanent blue on white | Cobalt underglaze + clear glaze, high fire |
| Painterly freedom, rich palette | Overglaze enamels (third fire) |
| Traditional Italian look | Majolica (tin glaze + in-glaze painting) |
| Iridescent metallic surface | Luster (third fire, reduction) |
| Dramatic post-firing effects | Western raku |
| Industrial production decoration | Transfer print or digital inkjet |
| Handmade texture aesthetic | Slip trailing, sgraffito, feathering |
| Native American pottery look | Burnished coil build + carbon reduction |
| Photo-quality design on tile | Digital ceramic inkjet print |
| Narrative scenes on vessel | Istoriato majolica technique |
