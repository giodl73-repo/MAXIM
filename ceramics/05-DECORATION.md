# 05 — Decoration

## Underglaze, Overglaze, Reduction, Luster, Majolica

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Decoration timing and position in firing sequence**: ceramic decoration can be applied at multiple stages — each with different chemical and visual outcomes; the sequence determines what's possible:
  - Pre-glaze, on raw clay: carved/incised, stamped, textured, slip decoration
  - Underglaze: applied after bisque, before glaze; colors flux into the glaze during glaze firing; limited color palette (must withstand glaze firing temperature)
  - In-glaze: painted on unfired glaze surface, fires into glaze during single firing
  - Overglaze (on-glaze): applied to fired, glazed surface; requires third firing at lower temperature (750-900°C); broader color palette since lower temp; somewhat vulnerable to abrasion
- **Slip decoration**: liquid clay (slip) of different colors applied to raw or leather-hard clay; slip trailing (pouring from bulb or bottle — raised linear decoration, like icing); feathering (combing wet trailed lines with fine tool); sgraffito (scratching through top slip layer to reveal contrasting slip or clay body below); slip casting above uses slip for forming; colored slips contain oxides; terra sig (terra sigillata — extremely fine deflocculated slip, burnished — classical Roman red/black ware technique)
- **Underglaze**: cobalt oxide = primary historical underglaze (stable blue to high temperatures — only blue available in high-fire for centuries); Chinese blue-and-white porcelain underglaze cobalt painting (Yuan dynasty, 14th century onward → Ming ware — the defining Chinese ceramic) → imitated by Delft, Meissen, Wedgwood; Turkish Iznik ware combines cobalt with green (copper) and red (Armenian bole — an iron-rich red slip, not an underglaze in the same sense); commercial underglaze colors (factory-prepared, stable stain+clay slip mixture) in wide range; applied by brush; detailed painting technique
- **Majolica (maiolica) painting**: painting on raw tin-glazed earthenware surface with metal oxide colors; origins in Islamic lustre ware → Moorish Spain → Italian Renaissance maiolica (Faenza, Deruta, Gubbio, Urbino — the great Italian maiolica centers); the wet tin glaze surface absorbs colors immediately like watercolor on wet paper — no corrections possible; "istoriato" ware (story-painted — narrative scenes covering entire vessel surface); Raphael-style maiolica; later transferred to Northern Europe as "faience" (Faenza → faïence, French) and "Delftware" (Dutch)
- **Transfer printing**: mechanical reproduction of decoration; method: pattern engraved on copper plate → ink transferred to tissue paper → tissue pressed onto bisqued ware → tissue burned off in kiln, ink fuses to clay → glaze over top; "flow blue" = early blue transfer printing where cobalt ran slightly during firing → soft, blurry effect (Victorian fashion); Willow Pattern = most famous transfer print design (Thomas Minton, adapted Chinese scenes, c.1790); industrial-scale decoration
- **Decals (ceramic decals)**: glass-ceramic color decals on paper backing; soaked in water, slid onto glazed ware, fired at 750-820°C; mass market tableware decoration (commercial ware); can achieve photographic quality; permanent when fired (unlike cold paint)
- **Screen printing on ceramics**: ceramic ink pressed through screen onto flat tiles or curved ware; industrial tile decoration
- **Luster**: metallic surface decoration creating iridescent, metallic sheen; Islamic pottery tradition (9th century CE Abbasid Iraq → Fatimid Cairo → Moorish Spain — Alhambra lustreware → Italian Renaissance gubbio luster); chemistry: metal salts (silver, copper, gold, platinum) painted on fired glaze surface → reduction firing at 600-700°C → metal ions reduced to metallic state at glaze surface → thin metallic film → interference luster; silver = yellow/amber; copper = red/green; gold = rich gold; different atmospheres produce different colors from same compounds; contemporary studio potters reviving Islamic luster techniques (Caiger-Smith)
- **Reduction carbonization (black-on-black)**: Maria Martinez and Julian Martinez (San Ildefonso Pueblo, New Mexico) revived traditional burnished red-on-red and black-on-black ware; technique: burnish clay surface when leather-hard → apply red slip decoration → fire in oxidation → black-on-black: fire in reduction → smoke deposits in glossy slip surface while matte areas resist; the distinction between matte (unpainted clay) and polished slip surface creates the design; not a glaze — all clay-based surface decoration
- **Raku decoration**: Western raku post-firing reduction produces metallic lusters from carbon; horse-hair raku (horse hair laid on red-hot piece just out of kiln → carbon flashing lines where hair touches); feather and grass marks; no glaze required for carbon effects on bare clay (naked raku)
