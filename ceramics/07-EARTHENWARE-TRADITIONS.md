# 07 — Earthenware Traditions

## Greek/Roman Pottery, Majolica, Faience, Delftware

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Ancient Near East and Egypt**: fired clay vessels from Neolithic period; Samarra culture (6th millennium BCE Iraq) painted geometric pottery; Halaf culture (5th millennium) — polychrome geometric; Egyptian faience (not clay — see below); Mesopotamian terracotta figures and storage jars; clay tablets (cuneiform) = same material as pottery
- **Greek pottery** (~900-300 BCE): arguably the most culturally significant ceramic tradition; primary vessel types: amphora (storage/transport), krater (wine mixing), hydria (water), kylix (drinking cup), lekythos (oil, funerary), aryballos (perfume); decorative techniques in sequence of development:
  - Geometric (900-700 BCE): rectilinear patterns, meander bands, stylized figures
  - Black-figure (700-480 BCE): figures painted in iron-rich slip (black after firing) on terracotta ground; incised lines cut through to show terracotta below; Exekias (Ajax and Achilles playing dice — finest example); Athens/Attica as center
  - Red-figure (530 BCE-300 BCE): reversed technique — background painted black, figures remain terracotta, internal details painted with fine brush rather than incised; allows more naturalistic anatomy; Euphronios, Douris as great painters; the "slip" = Attic clay slip with high iron, carefully controlled 3-phase firing (oxidation → reduction → oxidation) produces the black/red contrast
  - White-ground: cream slip ground for more delicate painting; used for lekythoi (funerary)
  - Attic clay body: distinctive red-orange because of high iron content in Attic soil (Laurion/Marathon region)
- **Roman ceramics**: terra sigillata (Arretine ware → Samian ware) — molded red-gloss earthenware; made in molds impressed with relief decoration; fine red slip burnished (the "sigillata" surface); mass production throughout Roman Empire; "Samian ware" = common name in Britain (though not actually from Samos); amphorae = industrial packaging (wine, olive oil, fish sauce — tituli picti labels survive); late Roman: coarser wares, regional production
- **Egyptian faience** (≠ modern faience): the first synthetic material; silica (sand/quartz) + alkali flux (natron) + copper/cobalt colorant; turquoise-blue typical; fired at ~900°C; actually more similar to glass than clay ceramics; used for amulets, tiles, vessels, figurines from ~3500 BCE; the blue-green color = sacred (associated with fertility, rebirth); not to be confused with Italian/French faience (tin-glazed earthenware)
- **Tin-glazed earthenware traditions**: a family of related ceramics using white tin oxide opacified glaze over earthenware body; enables painted decoration on stark white background; the white tin glaze allowed imitation of Chinese white porcelain with available earthenware clays; spread from Islamic potters → Moorish Spain → Italy → Northern Europe:
  - **Islamic (9th-10th c. CE)**: Mesopotamian (Iraq) potters developed tin-glazed ceramics to imitate Tang white porcelain; Abbasid Caliphate pottery
  - **Hispano-Moresque** (11th-15th c.): Spain under Moorish rule; Málaga and later Valencia (Paterna, Manises) as centers; luster decoration (metallic sheen — unique achievement); exported throughout Europe; the Alhambra tile work tradition
  - **Maiolica (Italian, 15th-17th c.)**: tin-glazed earthenware from Italian workshops; Faenza (center) gave name to faïence; Deruta (Umbria — still major center); Gubbio (gold and ruby luster); Urbino (narrative istoriato painting); decorated with cobalt, copper green, manganese purple, antimony yellow, iron orange; Renaissance narrative scenes derived from engravings
  - **Faïence (French/German)**: equivalent of Italian maiolica in Northern Europe; Rouen (17th c. — blue lambrequin patterns); Strasbourg (15th-18th c.); Delft (see below); German Habaner faience; Nevers (French)
  - **Delftware** (Dutch, 17th-18th c.): Dutch East India Company imports Chinese blue-and-white porcelain → massive demand; Chinese trade disrupted by Ming-Qing transition wars (1644) → Dutch delftware industry fills gap; Delft (Netherlands) as center; 32 factories at peak; cobalt blue on white tin glaze imitating Chinese decoration; "Delftware" in England = English equivalents (London and Bristol); Tin-glaze tiles for house interiors
- **Wedgwood and the industrial transition**: Josiah Wedgwood (1730-1795) = transformation of English pottery from craft to industry; creamware (refined lead-glazed earthenware — cream color; "Queen's Ware" after royal patronage); Jasperware (unglazed stoneware tinted with metal oxides — white reliefs on colored ground; blue Wedgwood = the iconic look); Basalt (black stoneware — unglazed); division of labor; quality control; mass market; export orientation; partner Thomas Bentley handled marketing; early adopter of steam power and canal transport; Etruria factory (Stoke-on-Trent); Wedgwood factory model = template for industrial revolution applied to ceramics
