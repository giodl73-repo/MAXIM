# Earthenware Traditions — Greek/Roman, Majolica, Faience, Delftware

## The Big Picture

Earthenware — the lowest-fire, most porous ceramic family — is also the oldest. Every culture on earth developed earthenware independently before they developed stoneware or porcelain. The extraordinary range of decorative traditions built on this humble porous clay body spans 20,000 years.

```
EARTHENWARE TRADITIONS TIMELINE
================================

~18,000 BCE  Xianrendong Cave, China — oldest fired pottery
~14,000 BCE  Jōmon pottery, Japan
~7,000 BCE   Neolithic Near East — widespread pottery production
~6,000 BCE   Halaf culture polychrome geometric pottery (Mesopotamia)
~3,500 BCE   Egyptian faience (not clay — see below)
~900 BCE     Greek geometric and black-figure traditions emerge
~500 BCE     Athenian red-figure (peak of Greek ceramic art)
~100 CE      Roman terra sigillata — mass production; empire-wide
~9th c.      Islamic tin-glazed earthenware (imitates Chinese white)
~11th c.     Hispano-Moresque luster ware (Islamic Spain)
~15th c.     Italian maiolica — Renaissance narrative tradition
~17th c.     Dutch Delftware — imitates Chinese blue-and-white
~1730s       Wedgwood creamware — industrial transformation
```

## Ancient Near East and Egypt

```
NEOLITHIC NEAR EAST (7,000–4,000 BCE)
======================================
Pottery spreads along agricultural settlements
Halaf culture (5th millennium BCE, Syria/Mesopotamia):
 Polychrome geometric patterns: red + black on cream slip
 Sophisticated decoration; cultural identity marker

MESOPOTAMIAN TRADITIONS:
 Ubaid culture: painted pottery with geometric/animal motifs
 Uruk period: mass-produced undecorated pottery → wheel introduced
 Cuneiform tablets: fired clay with pressed symbols — same material as pots
                     Clay as information storage medium

EGYPTIAN FAIENCE (≠ Italian/French faience — completely different)
==================================================================
NOT clay-based — important distinction:
 Material: silica (sand/quartz, ~90%) + alkali flux (natron = sodium
           carbonate + sodium bicarbonate) + colorant (copper → turquoise)
 Process: modeling or molding; core formed from sandy paste
          Surface coating: copper-rich glaze layer added;
          fired at ~900°C → glaze melts and fuses
 Result: turquoise/green/blue surface; vitreous; not ceramic

WHY FAIENCE:
 Turquoise-blue = sacred color (fertility, rebirth, renewal)
 Egypt had no high-quality colored gemstones locally
 Faience = artificial turquoise; democratized sacred blue-green
 Used for: shabtis, amulets, tiles, vessels, decorative objects

THE FIRST SYNTHETIC MATERIAL ARGUMENT:
 Faience: composite material made from scratch by humans (~3,500 BCE)
 Predates bronze (~3,300 BCE) as an intentionally engineered material
 "Proto-glass" — the same flux+silica chemistry leads eventually to glass
```

The most culturally analyzed pottery in history. Classical archaeology is largely built on Greek vase chronology.

```

                olive oil, grain              (rests in sand); neck

                water + wine)
```

### The Three Great Attic Techniques

```
ATTIC CLAY BODY:
 High iron content (Fe₂O₃) from Attica's red-orange soil
 Fires: red-orange body in oxidation
 The signature visual quality of Greek pottery

GEOMETRIC (900–700 BCE):
 Band decoration: meander, zigzag, checkerboard, stippled areas
 Stylized figures (horses, deer, mourning scenes)
 Dipylon Master (Athens): finest large funeral amphoras
 Decorating the full vessel surface systematically

BLACK-FIGURE (700–480 BCE):
 Figures painted in iron-rich slip (fires black after the 3-phase firing)
 Incised lines cut THROUGH black slip → reveal orange body beneath
 Shows internal anatomy and clothing folds
 Exekias: master painter; "Ajax and Achilles Playing Dice" on amphora
          "Achilles and Ajax Dice" (Vatican Museums) = finest example
 Technique requires: precise brush painting + incising tool skill

RED-FIGURE (530–300 BCE): invented ~530 BCE
 Reversed: background painted black; FIGURES left as terracotta
 Internal details PAINTED with fine brush (no incising needed)
 → more naturalistic anatomy; foreshortening; expressive faces
 Three great stages: Archaic (530-480 BCE), Classical (480-400), Late

THE 3-PHASE FIRING (for black/red effect):
 Phase 1: Oxidation (enough O₂) → body turns red-orange; slip turns red
 Phase 2: Reduction (reduce air) → body turns black; slip also black
          (iron-rich slip with added flux → vitrifies/sinters black in reduction)
 Phase 3: Reoxidation (reopen kiln slightly) → body reoxidizes red-orange;
           slip is now vitrified/sintered → doesn't reoxidize → stays black
 RESULT: orange body, black figures (or vice versa for red-figure)
 The slip is not "paint" — it's iron-rich clay that responds differently
 to the firing atmosphere cycle than the base clay body
```

## Roman Ceramics

```
TERRA SIGILLATA (SAMIAN WARE)
==============================
Also called: Arretine ware (from Arretium = Arezzo, Italy — original center)
             Samian ware (Britain — though not from Samos)

Description: molded relief-decorated red-gloss earthenware
 Body: fine orange-red (terra rosa) earthenware
 Decoration: impressed designs in molds (mythological scenes, borders)
 Surface: "sigillata" gloss = fine red slip, burnished; fired to ~950°C

Sigillata = Latin: "sealed" → used as verb for mold-impressed; not gloss

Production system:
 Master potter: makes original decorated molds from stamps/punches
 Workers: press clay into molds → slip apply → fire → distribute

Centers of production (shift over time):
 Arretium (Arezzo, Italy): 1st century BCE – early 1st CE
 Gaul (La Graufesenque, Lezoux): 1st-2nd century CE; massive production
 Rheinzabern (Germany): 2nd-3rd century CE; supplying Rhine frontier

AMPHORAE (mass industrial container)
======================================
Amphorae = Roman industrial packaging
 Wine (Dressel 1, 2-4 types): from Italy → provinces
 Olive oil (Dressel 20): vast production in Spain (Baetican)
 Fish sauce (garum): Dressel 7-11; from Spain, North Africa
 Contents labeled with tituli picti (painted inscriptions on neck)
 Form = identity: shape alone identifies content type

Volume: hundreds of millions of amphorae over 500 years
Monte Testaccio (Rome): artificial hill made entirely of discarded
                          amphora sherds (~25 million Dressel 20s)
                          = world's largest man-made archaeological deposit
```

## Egyptian Faience vs Islamic/Italian Faience

**Critical disambiguation** — two completely different things sharing a name:

```
EGYPTIAN FAIENCE (≠ ceramic):
 Silica paste + alkali + copper colorant; ~3,500 BCE–Roman period
 NOT clay-based; amorphous silica + glaze layer
 Color: turquoise/green/blue (copper); white (tin/antimony); red (iron)
 Function: amulets, tiles, vessels, figurines
 Related to: glass (same chemistry, different form)

ITALIAN/FRENCH "FAIENCE" (completely different):
 Tin-glazed EARTHENWARE; clay body + tin-opacified glaze
 Named: Faenza (Italy) → Italian maiolica → French faïence
 Function: tableware, tiles, decorative objects
 Same as: maiolica (Italian name), Delftware (Dutch name)
```

## Tin-Glazed Earthenware: The Family

One technological lineage: Islamic potters applying tin oxide to create a white opaque glaze, spreading through trade and conquest across the Mediterranean.

```
TIN-GLAZED EARTHENWARE TRANSMISSION
=====================================

Mesopotamia (9th-10th c. CE)
         |
         | Islamic caliphate trade/culture
         v
Hispano-Moresque Spain (11th-15th c.)
         |
         | Italian merchants trade with Moorish Spain
         v
Italian Maiolica (15th-17th c.)
         |
         | Trade; artisan migration
         v
French Faïence (16th-17th c.)
         |
         | Dutch merchants; direct from Italy
         v
Dutch Delftware (17th-18th c.)
         |
         | Dutch trade + immigration
         v
English Delftware (17th c.)

All use: tin oxide opacified white glaze over earthenware body
```

### Islamic Foundation (9th–12th c. CE)

```
ABBASID IRAQ (MESOPOTAMIA):
 Baghdad/Samarra workshops
 Problem: Chinese Tang white porcelain arriving via trade → coveted
 Solution: opacify a glaze with tin oxide → white surface mimicking porcelain
 Innovation: luster decoration added → iridescent metallic surface
             (see 05-DECORATION)

Key centers: Basra, Raqqa (Syria), Fustat (Cairo)

FATIMID EGYPT (10th-12th c.):
 Cairo workshops; finest Islamic luster ware
 Figural representation (unusual in Islamic art — Fatimids more permissive)
 Bowl with swimming fish; drinking figures; hunters

SELJUK AND SUBSEQUENT (12th-15th c.):
 Kashan (Iran): finest fritware (silica-body, not clay — related to faience)
                Painted with cobalt, turquoise, black; spectacular
 Iznik (Turkish, 15th-17th c.): peak of Islamic ceramic art technically
                                  See below
```

### Hispano-Moresque (11th–15th c.)

```
HISPANO-MORESQUE LUSTER WARE
=============================
Moorish Spain: Islamic potters under various rulers
              Málaga → Valencia (Paterna, Manises) as centers

Characteristics:
 Tin glaze: white background
 Cobalt blue: geometric/floral patterns
 LUSTER: gold and copper luster decoration — the unique achievement
          Spread to Italian Renaissance from this tradition

Hispano-Moresque exports to Italy: documented from 13th century
The Alhambra (Granada): tile work combining geometric patterns + luster

AFTER RECONQUISTA (1492):
 Christian rulers kept the potters (too valuable)
 "Mudéjar" art: Islamic artistic tradition under Christian rule
 Valencia becomes European luster center; exports to Italy, Flanders
```

### Italian Maiolica (15th–17th c.)

```
ITALIAN MAIOLICA CENTERS
=========================

Faenza (Emilia-Romagna):
 Gave the name "faïence" to all tin-glazed pottery (through French)
 14th-16th c. production; berettino (blue-gray ground)
 Conservative style; iconic blue; exported widely

Deruta (Umbria):
 Early luster production; 15th c. onward
 STILL ACTIVE as major maiolica production center today
 Characteristic: deep blue + golden luster on white

Gubbio (Umbria):
 Maestro Giorgio Andreoli: master of gold and ruby luster
 ~1498-1541; extraordinary achievement of copper + silver luster
 Gubbio luster = finest Italian luster tradition

Urbino (Marche):
 Istoriato tradition (narrative scene painting)
 Workshops of Nicola da Urbino, Francesco Xanto Avelli
 Designs derived from Raphael prints and Greco-Roman mythology
 Entire vessel covered in complex narrative scenes

Castel Durante; Cafaggiolo (Florence — Medici patronage); Siena:
 Other important production centers; different regional styles
```

### Dutch Delftware (17th–18th c.)

```
DUTCH DELFTWARE
================
Background:
 VOC (Dutch East India Company) had cornered Chinese porcelain trade
 1644: Ming-Qing transition; civil war; Jingdezhen production disrupted
 → Dutch porcelain imports collapse
 → Dutch potters ALREADY making tin-glazed earthenware
 → Suddenly Delft fills the luxury demand gap

Scale:
 32 factories operating in Delft at peak (late 17th century)
 Factory names (Delft factories named after symbols/animals):
  "The Three Bells," "The Peacock," "The Greek A," "The Rose"

Characteristics:
 Cobalt blue on white tin glaze: imitating Chinese blue-and-white exactly
 Chinese (Wanli era) decorative motifs: peacocks, bamboo, willow
 Also: Dutch landscapes, Protestant religious subjects
 Fine quality: thin potting; crisp painting

MARKS:
 Each factory had a mark; forgeries common (factories stamped competitors' marks)
 "De Porceleyne Fles" = only surviving original Delft factory still operating
                         (Royal Delft, from 1653)

Decline:
 Late 18th century: hard-paste porcelain factories + Wedgwood creamware
 compete directly at lower price → Delft industry collapses
 Most factories close ~1780-1830

English Delftware:
 London (Southwark, Lambeth) and Bristol production
 Punch bowls, tiles, chargers in same tradition
 Slightly inferior quality to Dutch; important British ceramic history
```

## Wedgwood and the Industrial Transformation

```
JOSIAH WEDGWOOD (1730-1795)
============================
Born: Burslem, Staffordshire
Family: six generations of Staffordshire potters
Disability: childhood smallpox damaged his knee → couldn't work the wheel
             → focused on design, chemistry, business

CREAMWARE (Queen's Ware, 1760s):
 Refined cream-colored lead-glazed earthenware
 More consistent and higher quality than existing English ware
 Queen Charlotte commissioned a 952-piece service (1765)
  → renamed "Queen's Ware" → royal endorsement → prestige
 Affordable luxury; exported to France (put Rouen faience out of business)
 The first truly mass-market fine tableware

JASPERWARE (~1774):
 Unglazed fine stoneware (not earthenware — slightly higher fire)
 Tinted with metal oxides: blue (cobalt), green, yellow, lilac, black
 White relief decoration applied separately (sprig molding) → white on color
 "Blue Wedgwood" = the iconic look; globally recognized
 Cameos, medallions, vase forms
 Still in continuous production

BASALT WARE:
 Black unglazed stoneware
 Made from local clay + manganese + iron oxide
 Classical shapes; sold as "Etruscan" ware; archaeological revival

BUSINESS INNOVATIONS:
 Division of labor: thrower, handler, decorator, kiln — separate roles
 Quality control: rigorous standards; smash defective work in front of workers
 Marketing: "Empress" services for Russian court; showroom in London
 Distribution: canals; Trent & Mersey canal (Wedgwood invested; trunk route)
 The factory as marketing object: visitors welcome; tours organized
 Partner Thomas Bentley: handled London marketing and taste direction

ETHOS: "all useful things" → democratize good design
        Before Wedgwood: fine tableware = aristocratic luxury only
        After Wedgwood: middle-class aspirational goods = market created
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Red-figure inverted vs black-figure**:
Black-figure: figures painted BLACK on orange; lines incised through. Red-figure: figures LEFT as orange on BLACK background; details painted in. Red-figure is later and more naturalistic — painting lines is easier than incising them for expressive anatomy.

**The three "faiences" in ceramic history**:
1. Egyptian faience: silica paste, not clay, ~3500 BCE
2. Italian maiolica = French faïence = Dutch Delftware: all tin-glazed earthenware
3. German Hafnerware/Fayence: same family; regional name

**Delftware is earthenware, not porcelain**:
Despite imitating Chinese porcelain so convincingly that it fooled casual observers, Delftware is earthenware under that tin glaze. Knock it: it sounds dull. Chinese porcelain rings clear. The bodies are completely different.

**Wedgwood jasperware ≠ earthenware**:
Wedgwood's jasperware is technically a fine stoneware (fires denser, slightly higher than earthenware). Not porcelain either — it's a unique formulation Wedgwood developed specifically (contains barium sulfate as well as clay+flint).
