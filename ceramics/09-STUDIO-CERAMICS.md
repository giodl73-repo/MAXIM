# Studio Ceramics — Bernard Leach, Mingei, Raku, Contemporary Practice

## The Big Picture

Studio ceramics is the intersection of craft, design, and fine art. The 20th century transformed ceramics from a pure production trade into a medium for individual artistic expression — a shift driven by a handful of key figures and a series of philosophical arguments about what ceramics is for.

```
STUDIO CERAMICS GENEALOGY
==========================

JAPANESE TRADITION (functional, wabi aesthetics)
 Chōjirō (16th c.) → Raku family (tea ceremony ware)
 Kenzan VI (19th c.) → Leach influence
 Hamada Shōji, Kawai Kanjirō, Yanagi Sōetsu (Mingei, 1920s)
          |
          v
ENGLISH TRADITION
 Bernard Leach (St Ives, 1920) — synthesizes East + West
 Lucie Rie, Hans Coper (Vienna/London; austere modernism)
          |
          v
AMERICAN TRADITION
 Bauhaus influence + GI Bill → university ceramics programs
 Peter Voulkos (1950s) — ceramics as Abstract Expressionism
 Paul Soldner (western raku) — his student
 Studio Art programs (Otis, Berkeley, Alfred) — American ceramic art

CONTEMPORARY (global, post-2000)
 Social media democratizes visibility
 Ceramics boom (2015+): mindfulness, tactile reaction to digital
 Direct-to-consumer (Etsy, Instagram sales)
 Ceramics as investment category (Lucie Rie at auction: £50K+)
```

## Bernard Leach (1887–1979)

The most influential figure in Western studio ceramics. Built a bridge between East Asian craft philosophy and Western studio practice.

```
BIOGRAPHY AND CONTEXT
=====================
Born: Hong Kong, 1887 (British family)
Raised: Singapore, Japan (partly), England
Education: London School of Art; first western student to learn
           raku firing from Kenzan VI (6th-generation pottery master)
           in Tokyo, 1911

KEY RELATIONSHIPS:
 Yanagi Sōetsu: Japanese philosopher-critic; proposed Mingei movement;
                collaborated with Leach for decades
 Hamada Shōji: master potter; co-founded Leach Pottery;
               later returned to Japan at Mashiko (his base)
 Shoji Hamada + Yanagi visited US/UK; shaped Western understanding
 of Japanese folk pottery philosophy

LEACH POTTERY (St Ives, Cornwall, 1920):
 Co-founded with Hamada
 East-West synthesis as explicit program:
  Eastern: Japanese folk pottery, Korean, Chinese Song influence
  Western: English medieval slip ware, salt-glaze tradition
 Aesthetics: earth tones; functional ware; visible hand marks;
              textured surfaces; iron-brown + cream slips
 Technical standard: oriental stoneware firing at Cone 10;
                     reduction firing (unusual in English tradition then)

"A POTTER'S BOOK" (1940):
 Defining manifesto; covers technique + philosophy + aesthetics
 Still the most widely read book in studio ceramics
 Argued for: functional beauty; integration of making and thinking;
              the pot as embodiment of cultural values
 Influence: shaped ceramics education in UK/US for 30+ years

LEGACY AND CRITICISM:
 Established studio ceramics as a legitimate artistic pursuit
 Created the "Leach aesthetic" (which also became a constraint):
  Earth tones, East-West, functional = "real" ceramics
 Lucie Rie explicitly rejected this framework → important correction
 The Leach Pottery in St Ives still operates; foundation courses
```

## Mingei Movement (Japan, 1920s–)

```
MINGEI (民芸) = "people's arts"
=================================
民 (min) = people; 芸 (gei) = art/craft

FOUNDERS:
 Yanagi Sōetsu (柳宗悦, 1889-1961): philosopher-critic; the theorist
 Hamada Shōji (濱田庄司, 1894-1978): master potter; the practitioner
 Kawai Kanjirō (河井寛次郎, 1890-1966): potter; later independent

PHILOSOPHY:
 Beauty in anonymous functional objects made without self-consciousness
 The anonymous craftsman's pots (not signed, not individual) = truest art
 "Beauty in use" (用の美, yō no bi): functional objects have inherent beauty
 Opposed to: elitist art objects made for display; industrially identical goods
 Influenced by: William Morris / Arts and Crafts but distinctly Japanese
              (less nostalgic; more philosophically developed)

YANG'S ARGUMENT:
 The great folk pottery traditions (Ōtani, Tamba, Karatsu, Onggi) =
 made by anonymous craftspeople = without ego or self-expression
 → paradoxically more beautiful than "art" ceramics with named makers
 Western studio art = opposite ideal: named artist, unique expression
 Leach tried to synthesize both → awkward compromise
 Hamada: became "National Living Treasure" Japan 1955 while claiming
          to embody Mingei values (tension with anonymity principle)

MASHIKO:
 Hamada's base from 1924; small Tochigi prefecture village
 Local Mashiko stoneware clay; traditional forms
 Hamada worked alongside local potters; "lived the philosophy"
 Mashiko = pilgrimage site for studio potters globally

ŌTANI:
 Korean-origin pottery tradition in Shikoku, Japan
 Large storage jars (onggi tradition); coil built; wood fired
 Cited by Yanagi as authentic Mingei
```

## Raku — Two Traditions

```
JAPANESE RAKU (original)
=========================
Origin: Chōjirō (長次郎, d. 1592)
Context: Chōjirō made tea bowls for Sen no Rikyū
         (千利休, tea master; codified wabi tea ceremony 1585)
         Rikyū sought bowls embodying wabi-sabi philosophy:
         imperfection, impermanence, incompleteness as beauty

Technique:
 Hand-shaped (NOT thrown — crucial; wheel = mechanical perfection)
 Low-fire lead glaze (~800-1,000°C) — lower than most pottery
 Black raku: lead glaze in reduction → black
 Red raku: fired in oxidation → red-orange

The seal "Raku":
 Toyotomi Hideyoshi (Rikyū's patron) awarded a gold seal inscribed "Raku"
 to Chōjirō's son; "Raku" = kanji meaning "pleasure/enjoyment"
 The Raku family has maintained the name and tradition for 16 generations
 15th Raku (Kichizaemon, b. 1949): current head; continues tradition

AESTHETIC:
 Each bowl is unique; asymmetric; imperfect
 The irregularities ARE the aesthetic (not defects to minimize)
 Embodies: wabi (austere simplicity), sabi (beauty of impermanence),
           ma (negative space, silence between notes)
 Small scale: fits in two hands for tea drinking ritual
 Price: important historical Raku pieces at auction: millions USD

WESTERN RAKU (completely different)
=====================================
Origin: Paul Soldner (1921-2011) at Scripps College, California, 1960s
Inspiration: Leach's description of Japanese raku → Western adaptation
Key difference: POST-FIRING REDUCTION (not in Japanese tradition)

Technique:
 Thrown or hand-built (wheel acceptable)
 High-fire bisque first or fire once
 Fire to Cone 06–04 (~950-1,050°C)
 Remove glowing-hot piece with tongs (protective gear required)
 Place in metal garbage can with combustibles (newspaper, sawdust, straw)
 Cover can → combustibles ignite → oxygen depleted → heavy reduction
 Let cool slowly in can (or quench in water for more crazing)

Effects:
 Carbon flashing: bare clay surfaces absorb carbon → black marks
 Metallic luster: CO reduces metal oxides in glaze → metallic sheen
 Crackle: rapid thermal shock → crazing → carbon in cracks
 Horse-hair raku: drag horse hair on glowing hot piece immediately
                  out of kiln → carbon trace where hair touches

Philosophy: celebration of chance and process; communal event;
             antithesis of careful control; pedagogically dramatic
             (students fire multiple pieces in a day; learn quickly)
```

## Lucie Rie (1902–1995)

```
BIOGRAPHY
==========
Born: Vienna, 1902 (Jewish professional family)
Education: Wiener Werkstätte (Vienna, 1925)
           Trained under Michael Powolny; design-influenced
Emigrated: 1938 (fled Nazi Austria); London, Albion Mews studio
           (same studio for 50+ years — a key fact about her practice)

TECHNIQUE:
 Wheel-thrown; porcelain and stoneware
 Very thin walls (extraordinary throwing skill)
 Sgraffito (scratched linear decoration through slip)
 Flattened oval and squared forms (not round)
 Inlaid color: different colored clay inlaid into carved lines
 Dark glazes: manganese-rich, sometimes volcanic in texture

SIGNATURE AESTHETICS:
 Pitted/volcanic surfaces: over-fired manganese →
                            bubbles in glaze → pitted matte surface
 Dark glaze with inlaid white lines: austere, modern
 Banded rims: thin bands of color at rim or shoulder
 Flattened forms: bowls stretched to ovals; not circular

POSITION vs LEACH:
 Rie explicitly rejected the Leach East-West synthesis framework
 Her work: purely modernist; Wiener Werkstätte design sensibility
 No "folk pottery" influence; no Japanese reference
 Showed porcelain could be high-fired and austere (not pretty/decorative)
 Leach initially dismissive → eventually recognized her importance

COLLABORATION WITH HANS COPER:
 Hans Coper worked in Rie's studio 1946-1958
 The two defining voices of mid-century British studio ceramics
 Together: shaped what British studio ceramics meant to post-WWII generation
```

## Hans Coper (1920–1981)

```
BIOGRAPHY:
 Born: Chemnitz, Germany, 1920 (Jewish family)
 Came to England 1939 as Jewish refugee
 Met Lucie Rie through sculptor friend; worked in her studio 1946

WORK:
 Sculptural vessels — not functional in any way
 Forms: disc on drum; cycladic shapes; bobbin; spade
 All-ceramic; no mechanical reference
 Surfaces: matte, dark (manganese + heavy reduction), textured
 "Volcanic" surface: heavily grogged clay + over-fired manganese
 Oxide application: applied and scraped back repeatedly → layered depth

KEY FORMS:
 Thistle cup: tall flaring cup on narrow waist
 Spade form: broad flat disk narrowing to point
 Cycladic: inspired by Cycladic (Greek Bronze Age) female figures
 Composite forms: two thrown elements assembled (disc + cylinder)

SIGNIFICANCE:
 First major ceramicist to completely abandon function
 Pushed ceramics into the fine art conversation alongside sculpture
 Shows the same concerns as contemporaneous abstract sculptors (Brancusi lineage)
 Coper prices at auction: £50,000–£300,000+ (Sotheby's, 2023)
```

## Peter Voulkos (1924–2002) and the American Break

```
THE TRANSFORMATION
==================
Peter Voulkos trained as a functional ceramics production potter
1953: Black Mountain College meeting with Charles Olson + Robert Rauschenberg
      Encounter with Abstract Expressionism (de Kooning, Kline painting)
      → complete transformation of his approach

PRE-BREAK: functional stoneware; plates, bowls; Korean influence; Leach influence
POST-BREAK: tearing, puncturing, stacking thrown forms; anti-functional;
            gesture as meaning; process as art

Otis Art Institute (Los Angeles, 1954-1959):
 Students: Paul Soldner (western raku), Ken Price, Billy Al Bengston
 The "Otis Group" transformed American ceramics in 5 years

University of California, Berkeley (1959-2002):
 The second wave of students
 "Ice buckets": large stacked, torn, and reassembled cylinder forms
               ceramic expression of action painting

SIGNIFICANCE:
 Ceramics enters the gallery alongside painting and sculpture
 The functional/art distinction becomes academic
 University art departments install ceramics = fine art, not craft
 American ceramics market: gallery representation, museum collection,
                             fine art auction prices follow
```

## Contemporary Ceramics Landscape

```
THE CERAMICS BOOM (2015+)
==========================
Ceramics popularity surge driven by:
 - Reaction to digital life: tactile, slow, analog
 - Mindfulness culture: wheel-throwing as meditation
 - Instagram visibility: beautiful pottery photographs well
 - Gentrification of craft: ceramics in restaurant design

Studio potter economics:
 The "three-legged stool": making + selling + teaching
 Most studio potters cannot survive on sales alone
 Common patterns:
  Make + sell (Etsy, Instagram, studio sales) + teach classes
  Residencies: artist-in-residence programs (university, non-profit)
  Grant funding: arts councils (UK Arts Council, NEA)

HIGH-END STUDIO MARKET:
 Lucie Rie: £50,000–£100,000+ per piece at Sotheby's/Christie's
 Hans Coper: £50,000–£300,000+
 Bernard Leach (good pieces): £3,000–£20,000
 Contemporary established names: £500–£10,000
 Instagram-discovered makers: £50–£500 (wide range)

NOTABLE CONTEMPORARY FIGURES:
 Magdalene Odundo (b. 1950, Kenya/UK):
   Burnished, coil-built vessels; synthesis of African + Greek + Japanese
   Thermal glass enclosure technique (Odundo vessels in cases)
   Major retrospective: Fitzwilliam Museum Cambridge (2019)
   Prices: £30,000–£200,000+

 Edmund de Waal (b. 1964, UK):
   Also author of "The Hare With Amber Eyes" (2010) — bestseller
   Porcelain vessels in installations; text/language on surfaces
   Conceptual use of ceramics in literary/memory context

 Grayson Perry (b. 1960, UK):
   Turner Prize 2003 (in a dress); ceramic vases with dark narrative
   Autobiographical imagery; ceramic as canvas for complex storytelling
   Major retrospective: British Museum (2011)

 Ai Weiwei (b. 1957, China):
   "Dropping a Han Dynasty Urn" (1995): photographic triptych
   "Sunflower Seeds" (Tate Modern 2010): 100 million porcelain seeds
   (made by Jingdezhen artisans)
   Uses ceramics conceptually, not as primary medium
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Japanese raku vs western raku**:
They share a name and historical vague inspiration, but that's it. Japanese: low-fire, hand-built, lead-glazed, wabi aesthetics, 16-generation family tradition. Western: thrown or hand-built, post-firing reduction, carbon effects, Paul Soldner 1960s, American educational context. Don't confuse them.

**"Leach tradition" as the definition of studio ceramics**:
Leach was enormously influential but his framework — East-West synthesis, earth tones, functional ware — became constraining. Rie, Coper, Voulkos, and many others defined studio ceramics in entirely different ways. "Leach tradition" is one branch, not the tree.

**Studio pottery ≠ cottage industry**:
Some studio ceramic operations run as sophisticated direct-to-consumer businesses. East Fork Pottery (Asheville, NC) has $10M+ revenue from Instagram-driven sales and scaled production. The artisan/industrial spectrum applies within studio ceramics itself.

**A ceramics degree ≠ pottery instruction**:
University ceramics MFA programs are art programs where ceramics is the medium. The emphasis is concept, critique, and exhibition — not technical instruction. Technical instruction comes from apprenticeship or dedicated ceramic schools (Leach Pottery, Northern Potters).
