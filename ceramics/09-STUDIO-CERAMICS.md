# 09 — Studio Ceramics

## Bernard Leach, Mingei, Raku, Contemporary Practice

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Bernard Leach** (1887-1979): the father of studio ceramics in the West; English, raised partly in Japan; met Shoji Hamada and Yanagi Sōetsu in Tokyo 1918; learned Japanese folk pottery and raku techniques from Kenzan VI; returned to England and founded Leach Pottery in St Ives, Cornwall (1920) with Hamada; synthesized Eastern (Japanese, Korean, Chinese) and Western (English slipware, medieval) traditions; his 1940 *A Potter's Book* = defining studio ceramics manifesto for decades; Leach aesthetic: earth tones, salt glaze, functional vessels, visible handwork, East-West synthesis; critics later said he over-romanticized Eastern traditions and constrained what was considered valid
- **Mingei movement** (Japan, 1920s): "people's crafts" (民芸, mingei = folk craft); Yanagi Sōetsu (philosopher-critic), Hamada Shōji (potter), Kawai Kanjirō (potter) as founders; philosophy: beauty in anonymous, functional folk craft made without self-consciousness; pottery, textiles, lacquerware of common people as the truest art; anti-elitist; influenced by William Morris and Arts and Crafts but distinctly Japanese; Ōtani Village (Korean-origin pottery in Japan), Mashiko (Hamada's base), Tamba, Karatsu as celebrated mingei pottery centers; reaction against Western industrialism and Japanese adoption of Western aesthetics
- **Raku tradition (Japanese)**: Chōjirō (d.1592) created tea bowls for Sen no Rikyū (tea master) following wabi aesthetic (rustic simplicity, imperfection as beauty); first pieces awarded seal "Raku" by Toyotomi Hideyoshi; Raku family continuous tradition — 16 generations, each taking the Raku name; hand-shaped (not thrown), black or red lead glaze, low firing; the object embodies tea ceremony values: quiet, humble, unique; opposite aesthetic from porcelain's perfection
- **Western raku** (distinct from Japanese): Paul Soldner adapted raku for American ceramics education in 1960s; key difference: pieces removed from kiln while glowing red-hot, placed in metal cans with combustible materials (newspaper, sawdust), covered to create heavy reduction atmosphere; carbon flashing, metallic lusters, crackle effects; dramatic, unpredictable; not related to Japanese wabi aesthetics; became popular in college ceramics education for exciting results and community firing events
- **Lucie Rie** (1902-1995): Vienna-born, London-based; trained at Wiener Werkstätte; fled Nazi Austria 1938; studio in Albion Mews, London; ultra-refined porcelain and stoneware; sgraffito surfaces, manganese-rich dark glazes, volcanic pitted surfaces; thin-walled thrown forms of extraordinary precision; inlaid lines; flattened oval bowls; worked with Hans Coper; rejected Leach's East-West framework; showed porcelain could be high-fired, austere, modern — not merely decorative
- **Hans Coper** (1920-1981): German-born, came to England as refugee 1939; met Lucie Rie and worked in her studio; distinctive sculptural vessels — disc forms, cycladic shapes, bobbin shapes; matte dark surfaces (manganese and volcanic); pushed ceramics toward sculpture; no functional aspect; Coper and Rie together define mid-century British studio ceramics
- **Peter Voulkos** (1924-2002): transformed American ceramics from craft to fine art; trained as a functional potter → encounter with Abstract Expressionism at Black Mountain College 1953 → began tearing, puncturing, stacking thrown forms; Otis Art Institute, later Berkeley; students included Paul Soldner, Ken Price, Billy Al Bengston; the "breakthrough" that moved ceramics into gallery space alongside painting and sculpture; massive stacked vessels, ice buckets (large stacked cylinder forms); ceramics as action painting
- **Contemporary studio ceramics landscape**: global market; social media (Instagram) transformed visibility and sales for studio potters; Etsy, direct sales; collectors (major auction prices: Lucie Rie bowls £50,000+; Hans Coper £100,000+; Bernard Leach standards); galleries specializing in ceramics (Marsden Woo, Joanna Bird in London); ceramic art fairs (Collect, SOFA Chicago); tensions between: functional/decorative divide; hand-made authenticity vs production studio (multiples); sustainability (wood firing vs electric); the "ceramics boom" — pottery as mindfulness activity → surge in wheel-throwing classes and home studios post-2015
- **Notable contemporary potters**: Magdalene Odundo (Kenyan-British; burnished, coil-built vessels drawing on African + Greek + Japanese tradition); Edmund de Waal (also author of *The Hare With Amber Eyes* — porcelain vessels in installations); Ai Weiwei (dropped Han urn, sunflower seeds — uses ceramics conceptually); Grayson Perry (Turner Prize 2003 — narrative vases with dark autobiographical imagery); Katie Bunnell (digital/CAD ceramics)
