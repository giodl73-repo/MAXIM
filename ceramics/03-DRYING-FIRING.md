# 03 — Drying & Firing

## Greenware, Bisque, Cone System, Kiln Types — The Transformation

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Firing stages and chemistry**: three sequential transformations in kiln:
  1. **Water smoking / evaporation** (room temp → 120°C): remaining free water driven off; must be slow — steam can shatter wet or thick pieces; pre-heat at 100-120°C for thick work
  2. **Dehydroxylation** (~500-600°C): chemically combined hydroxyl groups driven off from clay minerals → amorphous metakaolinite; clay becomes irreversibly transformed (cannot re-slake in water)
  3. **Quartz inversion** (573°C): α-quartz → β-quartz crystal structure change; volume increase 2%; rapid cooling through this point can crack ware (must cool slowly through 573°C on the way down too)
  4. **Organic burnout** (~200-600°C): organic materials (paper clay additions, organic impurities) burn away; must have oxygen available; flambe reduction before organics are fully burned = problematic
  5. **Sintering and vitrification** (above 900°C): glass phase begins to form (from feldspar/flux melting); glass fills pores and bonds particles; extent of vitrification increases with temperature; earthenware = partial vitrification; stoneware/porcelain = extensive vitrification
  6. **Mullite formation** (above 1,050°C): needlelike mullite crystals (3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂) grow in the glass matrix; primary strengthening phase; more mullite = stronger ceramic
- **The cone system (Orton cones)**: the temperature measurement system for ceramics; pyrometric cones are small triangular pyramids of ceramic material that melt and bend at specific conditions; cone number = the equivalent temperature (varies with heating rate); Cone 06 (low-fire earthenware ~1,000°C) through Cone 10 (high-fire stoneware ~1,285°C) to Cone 14 (porcelain, some technical ceramics ~1,400°C); "cone 10" = standard high-fire stoneware firing; witness cones (sitter + guard + guide) placed in kiln to observe progress; Orton Foundation (Ohio) maintains the standard; temperature-equivalent tables vary with firing rate — same temperature held longer = "soaking" = same effect as higher peak
  - Low-fire (Cone 06-2, ~1,000-1,200°C): earthenware; terra cotta; majolica; bright commercial glaze colors (since most colorants remain stable at lower temps)
  - Mid-fire (Cone 4-6, ~1,180-1,240°C): studio stoneware; commercial functional pottery; compromise between low and high fire
  - High-fire (Cone 8-10+, ~1,260-1,400°C): stoneware and porcelain; most wood/salt/soda firing; maximum vitrification; fewer color options (some commercial stains needed for high-temp stability)
- **Bisque firing**: first firing of bone-dry greenware; typically lower temperature (Cone 06-04, ~1,000°C for low-fire; Cone 08-06 for high-fire bodies); converts raw clay to bisqueware — fragile but porous, absorbs glaze well; not vitrified; bisqueware handles better than greenware during glazing; traditional Chinese single-fire (no bisque) vs standard two-fire process; raw glazing (glazing greenware) possible with skill — eliminates bisque firing but risk of warping/cracking
- **Glaze firing (glost firing)**: second firing to mature glaze; glaze melts, flows to fill surface defects, bonds to body; temperature must match glaze and body maturation; kiln wash on shelves (alumina/silica mix) prevents pieces from fusing to shelves if glaze drips
- **Kiln types**:
  - **Electric kiln**: coils of resistance wire (kanthal — iron-chromium-aluminum alloy); excellent temperature uniformity; oxidizing atmosphere (no combustion); easy to use, clean, no fossil fuel; most common for studio potters, schools, commercial production; no flame effects (reduction not possible without special reduction firing techniques); firing to Cone 10+ requires high-element kilns (typically only Cone 6 with standard elements)
  - **Gas kiln**: natural gas or propane; allows reduction atmosphere (fuel-rich = low oxygen → carbon monoxide → reduces metal oxides in glaze and clay body → blue/green celadons from iron, copper reds, carbon trapping); downdraft vs updraft designs; more variable, requires greater skill; traditional for high-fire stoneware
  - **Wood kiln**: traditional (anagama — single-chamber tunnel kiln with Japanese origin; noborigama — multi-chamber climbing kiln); wood ash falls on ware → ash glaze forms naturally on surfaces; long firings (3-5 days); distinctive "flashing" and texture from fly ash; labor-intensive; community firings; Japanese folk kiln tradition
  - **Salt/soda kiln**: rock salt (NaCl) or sodium bicarbonate thrown into kiln at peak temperature → sodium vapors react with silica in clay body surface → orange-peel textured sodium silicate glaze (only on exterior surfaces); distinctive texture; traditional German salt-glaze stoneware (Rhineland, 16th c.); Raku firing: individual pieces removed from kiln at peak temperature with tongs, placed in combustibles (newspaper, straw) → post-firing reduction; Japanese origin (Chōjirō, 16th c., Kyoto — low-fire, black-glazed tea bowls); Western raku = different (more extreme effects, not same as Japanese tradition)
- **Firing atmosphere**: oxidation (sufficient oxygen — most electric, some gas firings; iron stays Fe³⁺ = red/tan/brown); reduction (insufficient oxygen — carbon monoxide environment; Fe³⁺→Fe²⁺ = greens, blues, grays; copper → copper reds — spectacular but unpredictable); neutral; atmosphere affects every iron- and copper-bearing glaze dramatically
- **Kiln furniture**: shelves (cordierite — magnesium iron silicate, refractory), posts, pins, stilts (for low-fire glazed ware); saggers (protective containers in wood/salt kilns); kiln furniture is consumable — gradually degrades with thermal cycling
