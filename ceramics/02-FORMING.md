# 02 — Forming

## Hand Building, Wheel Throwing, Slip Casting, Industrial Methods

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Hand building — pinch**: oldest technique; roll clay into ball, push thumb into center, pinch walls between thumb and fingers while rotating; direct, intimate connection with material; irregular surfaces; used for small vessels, sculpture; Korean onggi jars still partially pinch-formed for thick bottoms; Yixing teapots (Chinese purple clay — zisha) use a combination of slab and paddle; pinching + coiling combined for larger work
- **Hand building — coil**: roll clay into ropes (coils), stack in rings to build walls; can build any size and shape; smooth interior and exterior by blending coils; ancient technique; Pueblo pottery (Southwestern US — Maria Martinez black-on-black San Ildefonso ware); Nigerian traditional pottery; coils vs ribbon coils (thin slab strips); coil-to-coil joining: scoring and slipping (roughen surface + add clay slip before joining — prevents cracking at join as clay dries at different rates)
- **Hand building — slab**: roll clay to even thickness with rolling pin or slab roller; cut shapes; assemble like a box; leatherhard stage (partially dried, stiff but not dry) is best joining consistency; soft slab = draping over hump molds or into press molds; hard slab = straight-walled vessels; texture added with stamps, rollers, texture mats before assembly; slab-built architectural tiles; jiggering in industry = automated slab pressing
- **Wheel throwing**: the potter's wheel (kick wheel, electric wheel); centering is the first skill (clay must run true on the wheel without wobble — requires significant practice); opening (pushing down into centered clay); pulling up (compressing walls upward — centrifugal force helps); collar (narrowing at top for neck); trimming when leatherhard (inverted, trim foot ring with loop tool); throwing in sections (large vases thrown in two or three pieces, joined at leatherhard); cylindrical vs spherical throwing paths
  - History: potter's wheel invented ~3500-3000 BCE in Mesopotamia and South Asia independently (evidence from Ur); early wheels = slow (tournette, turned by hand); fast wheel appeared ~2400 BCE; spread throughout Old World; not independently invented in pre-Columbian Americas (Aztec, Inca pottery = hand-built); Chinese porcelain throwing on high-speed kick wheel = extraordinary technical achievement
- **Slip casting**: liquid clay (slip — clay + water + deflocculant) poured into plaster molds; plaster is hygroscopic — absorbs water from slip → layer of clay builds up on mold surface; when thick enough, excess slip poured out (hollow casting) or fills entirely (solid casting); mold opened after partial drying; seams cleaned; industrial casting = multi-part molds, automated pouring/emptying; allows complex forms impossible on wheel; consistent reproduction; used for sanitary ware (toilets, sinks), bone china teapots, figures; deflocculants (sodium silicate + sodium carbonate) → small amount creates highly fluid slip without excess water
- **Jiggering and jollying**: industrial process; rotating plaster mold (or batt) + template (profile tool); jiggering = making flatware (plate profile against rotating hump mold — mold forms one face, tool forms other); jollying = making hollowware (bowl profile against rotating mold — mold forms outside, arm forms inside); profile arm precisely machined to final desired shape; RAM pressing (die pressing) = industrial equivalent using rigid male/female dies pressing clay between them under hydraulic pressure; dry pressing uses powder clay, very precise
- **Extrusion**: clay forced through a die (pug mill with die plate); produces uniform cross-sections — tubes, tiles, handles, decorative strips; architectural terra cotta historically extruded; hollow extrusions for handles (more resistant to warping); coil extruder (hand tool for studio use)
- **CAD/CAM in ceramics**: digital design (Rhinoceros, Fusion 360) → CNC routing of mold in foam or plaster; 3D printing molds (photopolymer or plaster powder); direct 3D clay printing (paste extrusion printing) — emerging technology for custom architectural and art work; robotic arm clay printing (Jonathan Keep, others)
- **Leather-hard stage**: critical intermediate stage — clay dried to the consistency of leather; still contains ~15-20% moisture; firm enough to handle without deformation; best time for: joining pieces (soft slab to leather hard), trimming (foot ring), carving, faceting, adding handles, sprigging (applying decorative pressed clay appliqués); if clay dries beyond leather-hard, joining is unreliable; studio timing is crucial
- **Drying control**: uneven drying = warping and cracking; controlled drying (slow, covered with plastic to allow even moisture loss); air circulation important to prevent mold growth; ware racks with fans for industrial drying; bone dry = all free water removed; still contains structural hydroxyl groups (driven off only by firing)
