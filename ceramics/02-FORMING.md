# Forming — Hand Building, Wheel Throwing, Slip Casting, Industrial

## The Big Picture

Forming methods span from a person's bare hands to industrial hydraulic presses that produce 10,000 identical tiles per day. The physics is the same — deforming plastic clay to a desired shape before it dries — but the scale, skill, and equipment differ radically.

```
FORMING METHOD SPECTRUM
========================

HAND              WHEEL            SLIP CAST        INDUSTRIAL
BUILDING          THROWING         (plaster mold)   FORMING
========          ========         ==============   =========
Pinch             Kick wheel       Liquid slip       Jiggering/jollying
Coil              Electric wheel   poured into       RAM pressing
Slab              + trimming       multi-part        Dry pressing
                                   molds             Extrusion
                                                     Isostatic press

No tools needed   Centrifugal      Capillary         Machine tolerances
Human hands       force + hands    action (plaster   to 0.1mm
                                   absorbs water)

Unique pieces     Repeatable but   Identical         Millions of
Full control      variable         reproduction      identical pieces
Slow              Medium speed     Medium speed      High volume

HISTORICAL SEQUENCE:
 Pinch (before 20,000 BCE) → Coil (Neolithic) → Wheel (~3,500 BCE)
 → Mold casting (antiquity) → Industrial jiggering (18th c. Wedgwood)
 → RAM press (20th c.) → 3D clay printing (emerging)
```

## Hand Building: Pinch

The oldest ceramic technique. Direct. Immediate. Every mark is a human fingerprint.

```
PINCH FORMING
==============

1. Roll clay into smooth ball (palm-sized for beginners)
2. Push thumb into center (not all the way through)
3. Pinch walls between thumb (inside) and fingers (outside)
4. Rotate slowly while pinching; keep wall thickness even
5. Work from base upward; open mouth last

WALL THICKNESS: aim for even 5–8mm throughout
               Thin base + thick walls = uneven drying → cracking

COMBINED TECHNIQUES:
 Two pinch pots joined at rim → sphere
 Pinch base + coils built up for larger vessel
 Korean onggi jars: pinch base + coil body

TYPICAL SCALE: best for vessels up to 15cm height
               Larger work = coils over pinch base
               Pinching porcelain (more plastic, stronger) → very thin walls
```

## Hand Building: Coil

Can build any scale. Pre-Columbian Americas, West Africa, Pueblo pottery — coil building enabled monumental ceramic work without the wheel.

```
COIL BUILDING
==============

MAKING COILS:
 Roll clay sausage on flat surface with palms
 Even pressure → even diameter throughout
 Thin coils: 1–2cm diameter for fine work
 Thick coils: 3–5cm for structural mass

BUILDING PROCESS:
  [Pinch or slab base]
         |
  [Score + slip join area]     ← scoring: roughen surface with fork/tool
         |                        slipping: apply wet clay to scored area
  [Place coil in ring]         ← score and slip between each coil
         |
  [Blend interior join]        ← compress coils together from inside
         |
  [Let stiffen slightly]       ← prevents walls collapsing under weight
         |
  [Repeat: score, slip, add]
         |
  [Smooth/texture exterior]    ← blend or leave coil texture visible

JOINING RULE: score and slip every join while leather-hard assembly is wrong;
              best to join coil-to-coil while both are still soft/plastic;
              drying rate matching is crucial to prevent crack at joins
```

## Hand Building: Slab

Sheet metal for clay. Structural, geometric, enables architectural scale.

```
SLAB BUILDING
==============

MAKING SLABS:
 Rolling pin method: clay between two sticks of equal height;
                     roll to even thickness (~1cm typical)
 Slab roller (studio equipment): adjustable-gap rollers;
                                  consistent thickness; faster
 Slab thickness: 6–12mm typical; architectural tile: 10–15mm+

SLAB STATES FOR DIFFERENT PURPOSES:
 Soft slab (just rolled):
   Draped over hump molds (mound) → concave bowl/form
   Pressed into press molds → detailed impressions
   Too soft for vertical walls without support

 Leather-hard slab (partially dried):
   Best for slab construction (box forms, cylinder)
   Can be bent without cracking (still plastic)
   Score + slip joins → clean seams

 Stiff slab (firm but not dry):
   Architectural tiles: cut to size, dry flat
   Not suitable for joining

SLAB CONSTRUCTION SEQUENCE:
 Cut all pieces → let stiffen to leather-hard
 Score + slip all joining edges
 Assemble; reinforce interior joins with coil snake
 Smooth joins; refine
 Support undercuts until stiff enough to hold shape
```

## Wheel Throwing

The defining image of the potter's trade. Developed ~3,500 BCE in Mesopotamia. The potter's wheel enables speed, symmetry, and consistent wall thickness at a scale impossible by hand.

```
THROWING STEPS
==============

SETUP:
 Wheel: kick wheel (foot-powered, traditional) or electric (rheostat speed)
 Clay: wedged, room temperature, plastic
 Water: sponge and bowl

1. CENTER
   Place clay on center of wheel
   Wheel spinning; wet hands
   CONE: push clay up into cone with cupped hands
   FLATTEN: push cone down with heel of hand
   Repeat until clay "runs true" (no wobble)
   ↓
   CRITICAL SKILL: centering is 80% of throwing difficulty
   Off-center clay = uneven walls = wasted effort

2. OPEN
   Make a fist; push thumb into center of spinning clay
   Leave 1cm base (don't push through)
   Open to desired base diameter
   Compress base with finger (compresses clay particles → stronger)

3. PULL UP
   Both hands: inside fingers pressing out; outside fingers compressing
   Move both hands upward slowly (3–5 pulls for typical bowl)
   Each pull: compress wall and lift clay higher
   → walls thin and tall

4. SHAPE
   Cylinder → curve outward = bowl
   Cylinder → continue straight = vase cylinder
   Collar (narrow) at top = bottle neck

5. TRIM (at leather-hard stage)
   Invert bowl on wheel
   Use loop tool to cut foot ring
   Remove excess clay, shape foot

TYPICAL PRODUCTION SPEEDS:
 Beginner: 30–60 minutes per bowl (while learning)
 Competent amateur: 5–15 minutes per bowl
 Production thrower: 1–3 minutes per bowl (100–200/day)
```

### Wheel History

```
POTTER'S WHEEL EVOLUTION
=========================
~3,500 BCE: Slow wheel (tournette) — Mesopotamia; hand-pushed
            flat disc, rotated intermittently; not fast enough for throwing
~2,400 BCE: Fast wheel — continuous rotation; enables true throwing
            Spread: Mesopotamia → Egypt → Greece → South Asia → China
Pre-1400 CE: Not in Americas — all Pre-Columbian pottery = hand-built
             (independently confirms diffusional spread vs independent invention)

Kick wheel (traditional): heavy flywheel momentum stores energy;
                           foot kicks to maintain speed; frees hands
Electric wheel: 1940s+ ; variable speed rheostat; consistent
                 speed; lower physical effort; now dominant in studios
```

## Slip Casting

Enables complex 3D forms impossible on the wheel. Industrial production standard for sanitary ware, figurines, teapots.

```
SLIP CASTING PROCESS
=====================

1. SLIP PREPARATION
   Clay + water + deflocculant (sodium silicate + Na₂CO₃)
   Deflocculant: negative charges on clay particles repel each other
                 → highly fluid slip with less water than normal
                 Specific gravity: 1.65–1.80 g/cm³ typical

2. PLASTER MOLD
   Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O): sets hard; highly porous
   Multi-part mold poured around original model
   PLASTER ABSORBS WATER from slip → clay layer builds on mold surface
   Mold must dry completely between uses (30 min to hours)
   Mold life: 20–100 casts before plaster saturates and stops absorbing

3. POURING
   Fill mold to top; wait 5–20 min (thickness builds with time)
   Drain excess slip when wall thick enough (hollow casting)
   OR fill entirely and let fully absorb (solid casting — figurines)

4. DEMOLD
   Wait until clay shrinks slightly from mold surface (partial drying)
   Open mold; remove casting
   Clean seam lines (mold joint lines visible)
   Attach any slip-cast handles/components

5. FINISHING
   Trim, refine; dry; fire as normal

INDUSTRIAL CASTING SCALE:
 Toilet: 5+ part molds; automated filling/emptying
         Multiple molds in cycle; 50–100 cast/day per battery
 Bone china teapots: 6-8 part molds; hand-finished seams
 Figurines: complex molds; arms/legs/heads cast separately, assembled
```

## Industrial Forming Methods

```
JIGGERING AND JOLLYING
=======================
Rotating plaster mold (hump or hollow)
+ Template arm (profile tool) machined to precise shape

JIGGERING: flat ware (plates)
 Soft clay pat placed on rotating hump mold
 Profile arm descends: shapes top face of plate
 Mold forms bottom face
 Plate lifted off mold when leather-hard

JOLLYING: hollowware (cups, bowls)
 Mold spins; profile arm shapes inside
 Clay pressed onto mold forms outside
 Profile arm lifts; mold carries piece to dryer

ADVANTAGES: high consistency; 300-400 pieces/hour
            Wedgwood adopted jiggering ~1780 (early industrial automation)

RAM PRESSING (die pressing)
===========================
 Hydraulic press: 50–500 tonnes
 Clay bat or plastic clay placed between male+female dies
 Press closes → clay forced into die shape
 High precision (±0.5mm); no wet-dry shrinkage issues
 Used for: tiles, electrical insulators, spark plug bodies

DRY PRESSING (isostatic)
========================
 Granulated clay powder (spray-dried to free-flowing)
 Filled into die; pressed at 100–400 MPa
 Very high dimensional precision; low moisture = low shrinkage
 Used for: porcelain floor tiles, advanced ceramics, dental zirconia blanks

EXTRUSION
=========
 Stiff clay forced through die (pug mill with die plate)
 Continuous profile output
 Used for: architectural terra cotta, ceiling/floor tile profiles,
           roofing tiles, sewer pipes, handles (hollow extrusion)
```

## Leather-Hard Stage

The Goldilocks zone of ceramic work — firm enough to handle, soft enough to join, carve, and alter.

```
LEATHER-HARD PROPERTIES
========================
Moisture content:   ~15–20% (from ~25–30% plastic)
Color:              Lighter than plastic (surface drying)
Feel:               Like leather — firm, slightly cool, no deformation
                    under gentle pressure

BEST TIME FOR:
 Trimming (turning) foot ring on wheel-thrown pieces
 Joining: handle to mug, spout to teapot, lid to box
 Carving: faceting, incised decoration
 Sprigging: pressing clay appliqués onto surface
 Wax resist application
 Slip trailing
 Smoothing and burnishing

JOINING RULE: always join leather-hard to same moisture level
              Soft-to-leather-hard join → differential shrinkage → crack
              Exception: paper clay allows wet-to-dry joining
              Standard: score (scratch surface with fork), apply slip
              (liquid clay), press together, smooth join from inside

TIMING:
 Environmental: dry air → fast; humid/plastic bag → slow
 Thickness: thin walls → 30 minutes to leather-hard
            Thick sculpture → hours to days
```

## Contemporary Developments

```
3D CLAY PRINTING (paste extrusion)
===================================
Software: Rhinoceros, Grasshopper, Fusion 360 → G-code
Printer:  Extruder head; clay in syringe/cartridge; CNC-controlled
          Lulzbot modified, Delta robots, purpose-built ceramic printers
Layer by layer deposition of clay paste
Post-processing: dry; fire as normal

Current capabilities:
 Layer resolution: 1–3mm (coarser than FDM plastic, cleaner than manual coil)
 Forms: impossible by hand (complex geometry, algorithmic texture)
 Scale: small to architectural scale (robotic arm extrusion)

Limitations:
 Anisotropic strength (layer boundaries = weakness)
 Shrinkage and warping same as manual work
 Not yet competitive with hand-throwing for traditional forms
 Production speed slower than industrial methods

NOTABLE PRACTITIONERS:
 Jonathan Keep: algorithmic natural forms
 Unfold (Belgium): early 3D ceramic printing studio
 WASP (Italy): large-scale architectural printing
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Thrown" is not "thrown away"**:
In ceramics, "throwing" means wheel-forming. The etymology is from an old English word for twisting/turning. A "thrown bowl" = a wheel-made bowl. Nothing was discarded.

**Centering is the hard part, not pulling**:
Beginning throwers focus on pulling up walls, but the key skill is centered clay. Off-center clay cannot produce even walls regardless of pulling skill. Centering typically requires 3–6 months to develop reliably.

**Plaster molds must be completely dry**:
A damp plaster mold will not absorb water from slip efficiently — the slip won't build up properly and the cast will be thin and uneven. Molds should feel warm and completely dry before reuse. Production studios rotate large mold banks.

**Jiggering ≠ jigsawing**:
Jiggering and jollying are the specific industrial rotating-mold processes for plates and cups respectively. "Jigger" = flatware (jig + profile arm); "jolly" = hollowware.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Form | Best Forming Method |
|------|-------------------|
| Round, symmetrical bowl/vase | Wheel throwing |
| Teapot (complex, multiple parts) | Combination: wheel body + slab spout + pulled handle |
| Industrial tiles | Dry pressing (RAM) or extrusion |
| Bone china teacups (mass production) | Slip casting (jiggering for cups) |
| Organic sculptural form | Coil building or slab |
| Thin walls, precise interior shape | Slip casting |
| Traditional tribal vessel | Coil (many traditions never used wheel) |
| Architectural terra cotta profiles | Extrusion |
| Small decorative figurine | Slip casting (complex multi-part mold) |
| Custom one-off complex geometry | Hand building or 3D printing |
