# Ceramics — Overview: Clay to Ceramic: Earth, Fire, and Form

## The Big Picture

Ceramics is the oldest synthetic material. The transformation is irreversible: clay minerals + heat → ceramic. Unlike metals, you cannot remelt and recast. This permanence makes ceramic the primary evidence of human civilization for the last 20,000 years.

## The Key Chemistry

```
CLAY MINERAL → CERAMIC: PHASE TRANSFORMATIONS
===============================================

Starting material: Kaolinite Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄

STAGE 1 (~100–200°C): Free water evaporates
 Piece shrinks; clay particles compress

STAGE 2 (~500–600°C): Dehydroxylation
 Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄ → Al₂O₃·2SiO₂ (metakaolinite) + 2H₂O
 Structural hydroxyl groups driven off as water vapor
 IRREVERSIBLE — cannot re-wet and rework the clay
 Clay body permanently set

STAGE 3 (573°C): Quartz inversion
 α-quartz ⇌ β-quartz: 2% volume change (both heating and cooling)
 Rapid cooling through 573°C can crack ware (thermal shock)
 Must control cooling rate through this point

STAGE 4 (~900–1,000°C): Sintering begins
 Flux oxides (feldspar → K₂O, Na₂O, CaO) begin to melt
 Glass phase forms, starts filling pores

STAGE 5 (above 1,050°C): Mullite formation
 3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂: needle-like crystals grow in glass matrix
 Primary strengthening phase of fired ceramics
 More mullite + glass → stronger, denser ceramic

STAGE 6 (full maturation): Vitrification
 Glass phase fills remaining pores
 Body becomes dense, non-porous (stoneware / porcelain)
 Earthenware fired below this point → remains porous
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Fired ceramic can't be recycled like metals**:
When you melt steel, you get steel. When you melt fired ceramic, you get a glassy slag with different properties. Ceramic is irreversible — which is why fired sherds survive millennia in archaeological deposits.

**Earthenware ≠ low quality**:
Greek amphora, terracotta sculpture, Wedgwood jasperware, and fine Italian maiolica are all earthenware. The firing range is lower but the craft can be extremely refined.

**"Faience" means two different things**:
Egyptian faience = ancient synthetic silicate material (not clay). French/Italian faience = tin-glazed earthenware (majolica). They have nothing in common except a name that migrated confusingly through history.

**Porcelain = translucent**:
Only in thin cross-section. A standard dinner plate of porcelain is not visibly translucent — but a porcelain eggshell cup or GIA-quality bone china cup held to light shows translucency.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Use Case | Ceramic Type |
|----------|-------------|
| Outdoor pots, frost resistance | Stoneware (dense, vitrified) |
| Traditional Italian hand-painted tableware | Majolica (earthenware + tin glaze) |
| Fine white tableware | Porcelain or bone china |
| Industrial high-temp component | Technical ceramic (alumina, SiC, zirconia) |
| Dental crown | Zirconia (CAD-milled) |
| Hip joint | Alumina or zirconia femoral head |
| Decorative art piece | Any type depending on aesthetic |
| Wood-fired natural ash glaze | Stoneware or porcelain in anagama |
| High fire studio bowl | Stoneware Cone 10 (1,285°C) |
