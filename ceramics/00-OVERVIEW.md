# 00 — Overview

## Clay to Ceramic: Earth, Fire, and Form

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **The fundamental transformation**: clay (hydrated aluminum silicate minerals) + heat → ceramic; the key reaction: at ~573°C quartz inversion (α→β SiO₂); at ~600-900°C clay dehydroxylation (Al₂O₃·2SiO₂·2H₂O loses structural water → metakaolinite); at ~1,000-1,100°C mullite (3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂) begins to form → the primary ceramic strengthening phase; fired ceramic is irreversible — unlike metals, you cannot re-melt and recast clay into new forms; this permanence makes ceramic uniquely suited to archaeological dating (fired pottery survives for 50,000+ years essentially unchanged)
- **Three ceramic families** (by firing temperature and clay body):
  1. **Earthenware**: porous, fired 1,000-1,200°C; red/orange/buff clay body; does not vitrify (remains porous) → must be glazed for liquid use; terracotta, majolica, faience, delftware; oldest ceramic type
  2. **Stoneware**: denser, vitrified at 1,200-1,300°C; gray/brown/tan body; very low porosity even unglazed; more chip-resistant; the workhorse of functional pottery
  3. **Porcelain**: vitrified, translucent (in thin sections), white body; fired 1,260-1,400°C; requires specific clay (kaolin) + feldspar + silica; smooth, dense, non-porous; the pinnacle of ceramic development — achieved in China centuries before anywhere else
- **Ceramic supply chain**: kaolin / ball clay mining → clay body preparation (mixing, aging, de-airing) → forming → drying (greenware) → bisque firing → glazing → glaze firing → decoration (overglaze) → third firing (if enamels/lusters)
- **Cultural significance**: pottery = one of the earliest human-made materials (Xianrendong Cave, China, fired pottery ~20,000-18,000 BCE; Japanese Jōmon pottery ~16,500 BCE); ceramic vessels enabled food storage and cooking (boiling in clay vs stone-boiling); pottery shards (potsherds) primary archaeological dating evidence; ceramic typology (pot shapes and decoration) used to identify cultures, date strata, trace trade routes; Silk Road porcelain trade shaped European history and economics (see 06-PORCELAIN-HISTORY)
- **Trade roles overview** (detailed in 10-CERAMIC-TRADES): potter (basic forming and firing), kiln master (manages industrial firing), thrower (wheel specialist), hand builder, tile maker, porcelain painter (enamel decoration specialist), glaze chemist, industrial ceramics engineer
- **Industrial vs studio vs art**: industrial = millions of identical pieces (Wedgwood, Meissen, Royal Doulton production); studio = individual or small-batch handmade work; art ceramics = unique sculpture/vessels at exhibition level (Peter Voulkos, Lucie Rie, Hans Coper); these three categories have very different economics and skill profiles
- **Key chemistry bridges**: silicate chemistry (SiO₄ tetrahedra — same chemistry as glass and many geological minerals); flux oxides (Na₂O, K₂O, CaO, MgO lower melting point of silica by disrupting network — same principle as glass modifiers); colorant transition metal oxides (iron → amber/brown/green/blue depending on atmosphere; copper → green/red; cobalt → blue; manganese → purple; titanium → rutile textures); reduction vs oxidation firing atmosphere changes valence states (Fe³⁺ = iron red/orange; Fe²⁺ = celadon/green)
