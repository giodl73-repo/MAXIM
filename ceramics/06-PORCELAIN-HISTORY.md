# Porcelain History — Chinese Origins to Meissen 1708

## The Big Picture

Porcelain is the most coveted manufactured object in human history for roughly 1,200 years. European monarchs traded soldiers for Chinese vases. Alchemists spent careers trying to decode the formula. The word "porcelain" itself (from Portuguese "porcellana" — cowrie shell) reveals that Europeans had no better analogy for its translucent, smooth surface than a seashell.

```
PORCELAIN HISTORY TIMELINE
===========================

~3rd c. CE    Chinese proto-porcelain (Zhejiang) — first vitrified white ware
~7th c. CE    True high-fire white porcelain (Tang dynasty, Hebei province)
~960-1279     Song Dynasty — five great wares; peak ceramic aesthetic
~1271-1368    Yuan Dynasty — blue-and-white develops; global export begins
~1368-1644    Ming Dynasty — canonical export ware; European obsession grows
1603          First VOC (Dutch East India) auction of Chinese porcelain in Europe
~1620s        Chinese production disrupted → Dutch pivot to Delftware
1644          Ming-Qing transition; production disruption; Delft boom
1708-1710     Meissen: Böttger discovers European hard-paste porcelain
1718          Vienna (Du Paquier) — formula spread via Meissen defector
1745          Chelsea (soft-paste) — England's first porcelain factory
1756          Vincennes moves to Sèvres (royal patronage, Louis XV)
1768          French discover Limousin kaolin → hard-paste at Sèvres
1768          William Cookworthy discovers Cornish kaolin → hard-paste in England
~1800         Josiah Spode II standardizes bone china formula
1891          Meissen factory nationalizes production modernizes
2024          Meissen still producing; Jingdezhen studio revival active
```

## Chinese Origins

```
PROTO-PORCELAIN → TRUE PORCELAIN
==================================

~3rd c. CE: Zhejiang province, Eastern China
 "Yue ware" — green-glazed (celadon) stoneware approaching porcelain
 Vitrified body, no translucency, gray body color
 Bridge between stoneware and true porcelain

Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE):
 Hebei province: high-quality white porcelain with true white body
 "Ding ware" (early Ding) emerges
 White, hard, non-porous — the first true porcelain
 Key materials: high-kaolin local clay + feldspar "petuntse"
 Kilns reaching 1,280-1,350°C — unprecedented in the world
 No other civilization remotely approaching this temperature or material

KEY MATERIALS (Chinese porcelain):
 Kaolin (白瓷土, báicítǔ): pure white-firing clay
                            Named for Gaoling Hill near Jingdezhen
 Petuntse (白不子, bái bù zǐ, china stone):
           feldspathic rock; provides flux and glass phase
           Partially decomposed granite with feldspar + quartz
           Ground fine, mixed with kaolin → the porcelain body

FORMULA: ~50% kaolin + ~50% petuntse (traditional)
          Modern porcelain bodies modified but same principles
```

## Jingdezhen: The Porcelain Capital

```
JINGDEZHEN (景德鎮)
====================
Location:   Jiangxi province, southeastern China
River:      Chang (allows transport of goods, fuel wood incoming)
Fuel:       Vast pine forests → abundant wood for high-temperature kilns
Water:      Required for clay processing; local streams ideal

WHY JINGDEZHEN:
 Local geology: exceptional kaolin (Gaoling deposit — "kaolin" derives from this)
 Local petuntse: quality china stone from nearby mountains
 Water transport: goods shipped via river to coast → world markets
 Accumulated knowledge: ceramic tradition from 6th century CE minimum
 Imperial patronage: officially designated imperial kilns from ~1004 CE (Song)

IMPERIAL KILN HISTORY:
 Song Dynasty (~960-1279): first imperial kilns
 Ming Dynasty Yongle (1403 CE): Grand Imperial Kiln Bureau established
   → dedicated imperial ware production; best potters, materials
 Peak: 100,000+ workers in and around city at Qing dynasty peak
 The greatest manufacturing complex in the pre-industrial world

JINGDEZHEN TODAY:
 Major studio ceramics center (students from global art schools study here)
 Still commercial porcelain production
 "Taobao Village" — online sales to Chinese domestic market
 Some traditional techniques preserved; tourist/educational infrastructure
```

## Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE): The Five Great Wares

The Song Dynasty represents the pinnacle of Chinese ceramic achievement. Aesthetic refinement over spectacle.

```
THE FIVE GREAT SONG WARES
==========================

RU WARE (汝窑) — rarest; most prized
 Production: ~1086–1106 CE only (~20 years)
 Location: Ruzhou, Henan province (imperial ware only)
 Glaze: sky-blue/celadon; subtle; "sky-blue after rain"
 Body: dense; fine; pinkish in oblique light
 Surviving pieces: fewer than 100 (ca. 87–92 documented)
 Most expensive at auction: Ru brushwasher — HK$294M (2017)
 The scarcity is entirely from short production period + fragility

DING WARE (定窑)
 Location: Dingzhou, Hebei province (one of two northern kilns)
 Body: white; thin; fine
 Decoration: incised floral and bird designs (carved into body)
 Innovation: "inverted firing" — pieces fired upside down to prevent
              rim warping under high heat; result = unglazed rim
              → bound with gold/silver/copper metal lip
 Commercial scale: not just imperial; exported; common type in Song

GUAN WARE (官窑, "official ware")
 Two periods: Northern Guan (Bianjing); Southern Guan (Hangzhou after 1127)
 Body: dark gray to black (high iron body)
 Glaze: thick; pale blue-green; deliberately develops crazing
 Crackle pattern: controlled (fired quickly so glaze cools faster than body
                   → glaze in tension → controlled cracking = aesthetic)
 Paradox: the "defect" of crazing = the prized aesthetic
 Dark body shows at crackle lines (brown-black "iron wire")

JUN WARE (钧窑)
 Location: Yuxian, Henan province
 Glaze: thick, opalescent; primarily blue/blue-gray
 Effect: purple-red splashes (copper reduction in small areas)
 Large quantities survived; debated whether truly Song or Yuan-Ming
 Distinctive milky-blue glaze from rutile + bubbles + calcium

GE WARE (哥窑, "elder brother ware")
 Origin: disputed (possibly Longquan area)
 Crackle: two-scale crackle network ("iron wire and golden thread")
           Large dark cracks + small golden cracks filling spaces
 Body: gray-black; similar to Guan
 Attribution: contested; no known kiln site definitively identified
 Highly prized; small surviving group
```

## Yuan Dynasty Blue-and-White

The transformation that defined Chinese export ceramics for 500 years:

```
YUAN DYNASTY BLUE-AND-WHITE (~1271-1368)
=========================================
Innovation: cobalt (from Persia) applied as underglaze;
            clear glaze over; fired to 1,280°C+
            → brilliant blue on white; vivid, durable

COBALT SOURCE:
 "Smalt" = cobalt ore imported from Persia (Kashan)
 Persian cobalt = high manganese → deep blue with slight purple
 Local Chinese cobalt = different impurity profile; later blends
 Blue-and-white = the product of Persian-Chinese trade

PRODUCTION SCALE:
 Jingdezhen → massive plates and vases for Middle East market
 Ottoman court, Persian court, Egyptian mamluk courts = primary customers
 Large-format pieces (60+ cm diameter) technically unprecedented
 "Kraak" ware: export porcelain loaded onto Dutch/Portuguese carracks

IMPACT:
 Yuan blue-and-white establishes the template copied for 400 years:
 China → Iznik → Delft → Meissen → all of European porcelain

KEY PIECES:
 "David Vases" (1351 CE, British Museum): inscribed dated pieces proving
  Yuan blue-and-white production timeline; absolute reference
```

## Ming Dynasty

```
MING DYNASTY CERAMICS (~1368-1644)
====================================
Multiple reign periods each have characteristic styles:

Yongle (1403-1424): pure white (tianbai — "sweet white");
                     thin walls; elegant forms; sweet white porcelain
                     = one of the finest ever made

Xuande (1426-1435): classic blue-and-white; slightly orange-peel surface;
                     rich cobalt with "heaping and piling" effect from
                     cobalt impurities pooling in thick areas

Chenghua (1465-1487): DOUCAI (contrasting colors)
                       Underglaze blue outlines + overglaze polychrome enamels
                       Chicken cups (斗彩鸡缸杯): tiny cups with chickens;
                       most expensive Chinese ceramics at auction
                       Record: $36 million (Sotheby's Hong Kong, 2014)

Jiajing (1522-1566): vivid color; iron-red; yellow; wucai (five colors)

Wanli (1573-1620): largest production; massive export
                    Kraak ware: open-well plates with radiating panels
                    Dutch VOC imports primarily Wanli era
```

## European Porcelain Obsession

```
THE EUROPEAN PROBLEM (1600-1710)
==================================
Chinese porcelain arrived in Europe via Portuguese (from 1513) and
Dutch (VOC from 1602) trade. Volume: hundreds of thousands of pieces.

WHY SO COVETED:
 White, translucent, ring-clear — unlike any European earthenware or stoneware
 Superior to European faience at holding heat; non-porous; functional
 Luxury goods value: a Ming jar = same value as a ship's cargo of spices
 Aesthetic: utterly unlike European aesthetic tradition

EUROPEAN RESPONSE:
 Delftware: copy the surface decoration (blue-and-white) on earthenware
             → not porcelain; doesn't have the translucency/hardness
 "Soft-paste" experiments: add bone, glass, alabaster, alabaster...
             → never achieved true porcelain properties

THE ALCHEMICAL SEARCH:
 Augustus the Strong (Friedrich August I, Elector of Saxony 1694-1733):
  Most obsessive European porcelain collector of the period
  His "Japanisches Palais" in Dresden = entire palace for display
  The Dragon Vases: traded 600 Saxon dragoon soldiers to King
  of Prussia for 151 pieces of Chinese blue-and-white vases (1717)
  = 1 dragoon per vase (dragoons = elite cavalry = very expensive soldiers)

 Assigned Johann Friedrich Böttger (1682-1719) to find formula
 Böttger: trained as apothecary → became "alchemist" → imprisoned
           (Augustus didn't want him leaving with knowledge or without gold)
```

## Meissen: The European Discovery (1708–1710)

```
THE DISCOVERY
==============
Johann Friedrich Böttger (alchemist)
Ehrenfried Walther von Tschirnhaus (mathematician/scientist)
Working together 1706-1708 at Dresden:

Key breakthrough sequence:
 Tschirnhaus's lens array: could focus sunlight to melt diverse materials
                             at unprecedented temperatures → systematic testing

 1707: Red stoneware (Böttgersteinzeug)
   Colditz clay (local red iron-bearing clay)
   + calcined alabaster (CaSO₄ → CaO at high temperature)
   → dense red stoneware; hard enough to cut glass
   → marketed as "red porcelain" (Meissen's first product)
   → Later: Unglazed or lacquer-decorated; fashionable in Europe

 1708-1710 (precise date disputed): True white porcelain
   Böttger + Tschirnhaus add local alabaster (CaSO₄) to local kaolin
   → achieves white, translucent, vitrified porcelain
   → Tschirnhaus dies October 1708; Böttger continues
   → Augustus tests results; declares success
   Meissen Manufaktur established January 1710

 LOCATION: Albrechtsburg castle, Meissen (above Dresden on Elbe)
           Deliberately chosen: locked rooms; workers couldn't leave;
           secrets protected by royal decree (capital punishment for
           espionage or disclosure)
```

### Meissen Factory History

```
MEISSEN PRODUCTION HIGHLIGHTS
==============================
First decade (1710-1720):
 Böttgersteinzeug (red stoneware): first commercial product
 First white porcelain: simple forms; no successful colored decoration yet
 Painted with cold lacquer (peels off — not fired on)
 Struggle: no stable overglaze enamel colors initially

Johann Gregorius Höroldt (1696-1775):
 Joined Meissen 1720 as decorator
 Developed stable overglaze enamel colors for Meissen porcelain
 Chinoiserie style (imaginary "Chinese" scenes in European style)
 Harbour scenes; European bouquets; the definitive Meissen painted style

Johann Joachim Kändler (1706-1775):
 Master modeler at Meissen from 1731
 Redefined porcelain as sculpture medium
 Swan service (1737-1741, 2,200 pieces); life-size animal figures
 Meissen figurines (Harlequin, Pantaloon, Columbine — commedia dell'arte)
 The model for all European porcelain figure production

MEISSEN MARKS:
 "KPM" (Königliche Porzellan-Manufaktur) — earliest mark
 Crossed swords: established ~1720; still the Meissen mark today
 "AR" in monogram: pieces from Augustus the Strong's personal collection
```

## European Spread of Porcelain

```
FACTORY LINEAGE
===============

MEISSEN (1710) → secret leaked via escaped workers
         |
         ├── VIENNA (Du Paquier, 1718)
         |    Christoph Konrad Hunger (Meissen gilder) → Vienna
         |    Samuel Stölzel (Meissen workman) → Vienna kiln secrets
         |    Vienna Manufaktur (Du Paquier Porzellan 1718–1744)
         |    Later: Kaiserliche Porzellanmanufaktur (imperial, 1744)
         |    Characteristic: Vienna rococo; decorative painting
         |
         ├── FRANCE:
         |    Vincennes (1740, royal patronage, soft-paste)
         |    → Sèvres (1756, moved; royal monopoly)
         |    Hard-paste: 1768 (Limousin kaolin discovered)
         |    Sèvres biscuit figures, bleu céleste, rose Pompadour
         |    Royal direction: Madame de Pompadour; Louis XV sponsorship
         |
         ├── ENGLAND (soft-paste tradition):
         |    Chelsea (1745, Nicholas Sprimont, soft-paste)
         |    Bow (1748); Derby (1750); Worcester (1751, soft-paste)
         |    None achieved hard-paste until Cookworthy 1768
         |    Bone china: Spode II (~1800) = English porcelain standard
         |
         └── REST OF EUROPE:
              Berlin KPM (1763, Frederick the Great); Nymphenburg (1747);
              Copenhagen (1775); Wedgwood Jasperware (not porcelain per se)

SOFT-PASTE vs HARD-PASTE in Europe:
 Hard-paste: kaolin + petuntse; 1,260-1,400°C; Meissen, Sèvres (post-1768)
 Soft-paste: ground glass + clay (or kaolin + frit); 1,100-1,200°C
             Easier to fire; softer; many early European factories
             Each factory had its own formula (secrecy rampant)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"China" = porcelain historically**:
The English word "china" for tableware derives from the country of origin. "Fine china" = porcelain. The confusion is that Europeans called the material by its source before they understood it chemically.

**Bone china is not hard-paste porcelain**:
Chinese and Meissen porcelain = hard-paste (kaolin + petuntse, high-fire). English bone china = bone ash + china stone + kaolin, lower-fire. Both are vitrified, translucent, and called "porcelain" but different formulas and different fired properties. Bone china is softer.

**Meissen's secrecy failed**:
Despite imprisoning workers and placing guards on the castle, the formula leaked within 10 years. Vienna got it by 1718. By 1750, factories across Europe were producing hard-paste. The moat strategy is always temporary.

**"Kraak" ware = specific Ming export style**:
Kraak (from "carrack" ships) is a specific type of late Ming blue-and-white with characteristic radiating panel decoration. Not all Ming blue-and-white is Kraak — only the export style with open-well center + radiating borders.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| When You See... | Likely Identification |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Sky-blue glaze, Song dynasty | Ru ware |
| Two-scale crackle, gray body | Ge ware |
| Blue-and-white, pre-1644 China | Yuan or Ming dynasty |
| Crossed swords mark | Meissen (18th century) |
| Soft-paste, English, pre-1800 | Chelsea, Worcester, Derby, Bow |
| Bone ash formula | English bone china (Spode/Wedgwood/Royal Doulton) |
| Bleu céleste ground color | Sèvres |
| Chicken cup design | Ming Chenghua doucai |
| Orange-peel surface, heaping/piling | Xuande blue-and-white |
| Dragon Vase style | Wanli kraak or earlier Yuan |
