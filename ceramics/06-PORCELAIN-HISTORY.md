# 06 — Porcelain History

## Chinese Origins to Meissen 1708 — The Most Coveted Ceramic

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Chinese invention**: porcelain = China's greatest technological achievement in materials; proto-porcelain (green-glazed stoneware, Zhejiang) ~3rd century CE; true high-fire white porcelain (Tang dynasty, 618-907 CE) from Hebei province; the critical innovation = high-kaolin local clays ("china clay") + feldspar-rich petuntse (china stone) + high-temperature kilns; Chinese potters working at 1,280-1,380°C centuries before any other civilization
- **Jingdezhen**: the porcelain capital of China; located in Jiangxi province; combination of exceptional local materials (Gaoling kaolin deposit — "Gaoling Hill" = origin of the word "kaolin"), abundant wood fuel (pine), and river transport; Imperial Kilns established Song dynasty, formalized Ming dynasty (1403 CE); at peak, 100,000+ workers; the greatest factory complex in the pre-industrial world; produced imperial ware and export ware; still operating today (now a studio ceramic center)
- **Song Dynasty** (960-1279 CE): golden age of Chinese ceramics; five great wares:
  - **Ding ware** (Hebei): white porcelain, incised floral decoration, "inverted firing" (upside down to prevent rim warping → bare rim, bound with metal lip)
  - **Ru ware** (Henan): imperial sky-blue glaze (celadon); tiny, exquisite; perhaps the rarest — fewer than 100 pieces survive; fired only ~20 years (1086-1106 CE)
  - **Guan ware** ("official ware"): imperial use; characteristic crackle pattern (controlled crazing — glaze cools faster than body → tensile stress → controlled cracking; deliberately cultivated)
  - **Jun ware** (Henan): opalescent blue glazes; purple-red splashes from copper reduction; thick fluid glazes; most common of the five; debated whether truly Song or later
  - **Ge ware** (disputed origin): dense crackle network (two sizes of crackling, one dark brown, one yellow → "iron wire and golden thread"); attributions contested
- **Yuan Dynasty blue-and-white** (1271-1368 CE): cobalt imported from Persia (smalt) used for underglaze painting → the defining Chinese export ceramic; large scale production for Islamic world (Middle East courts); Jingdezhen perfects high-quality cobalt blue painting; massive export trade; "kraak" ware (Portuguese, Dutch East India Company import — named from "carrack" ships); this establishes the template that all European pottery tries to imitate for 400 years
- **Ming Dynasty** (1368-1644 CE): Yongle, Xuande, Chenghua, Wanli periods all have distinctive wares; chicken cups (Chenghua doucai — contrasting color — tiny cups, the most expensive Chinese ceramics at auction); wucai (five colors — underglaze blue + four overglaze enamels); Swatow ware (export, bold decoration, looser execution)
- **European obsession and the alchemical search**: by 1600, Chinese porcelain (brought by Portuguese and Dutch traders) was the most coveted luxury material in Europe; European royalty collected it obsessively; Augustus the Strong (Elector of Saxony, later King of Poland) traded 600 Saxon dragoon soldiers to the King of Prussia for 151 pieces of Chinese blue-and-white vases (1717) — the "dragon vases"; alchemists tried to decode the porcelain formula; the search for "the white gold" was genuinely obsessive; attempts using ground eggshells, bone, glass, alabaster all failed
- **Meissen — the discovery** (1708-1710): Johann Friedrich Böttger (1682-1719), alchemist working for Augustus the Strong (initially assigned to make actual gold — classic royal alchemist fate); working with physicist Ehrenfried Walther von Tschirnhaus; Tschirnhaus's high-temperature lens array melts diverse materials; local kaolin from Colditz (Germany) + alabaster (calcium sulfate) → red stoneware by 1707; adjustment to feldspathic additions → true white porcelain by 1708 (possibly 1709, date disputed); Augustus establishes Meissen porcelain factory 1710 in the former Albrechtsburg castle (secrets protected by not allowing workers to leave); first true European hard-paste porcelain
- **European porcelain spread**: despite Meissen's attempts at secrecy, formula leaked via workers; Vienna (Du Paquier, 1718 — via runaway Meissen worker Stolzel); Venice; France (Vincennes → Sèvres, 1756 — soft-paste porcelain initially, hard-paste from 1768 when Limousin kaolin discovered); England: William Cookworthy discovers Cornish kaolin 1768, hard-paste; but England becomes famous for soft-paste porcelain (bone china — Spode, c.1800) which becomes the English standard
- **Soft-paste vs hard-paste**: hard-paste = "true porcelain" = China's formula (kaolin + petuntse, 1,260-1,400°C); soft-paste = European compromise (ground glass + clay + soapstone or other materials, 1,100-1,200°C); soft-paste easier to fire but less durable, softer; many early European factories (Vincennes/Sèvres, Chelsea, Worcester) used soft-paste; only Meissen, Vienna, and later Sèvres + English factories produced hard-paste
