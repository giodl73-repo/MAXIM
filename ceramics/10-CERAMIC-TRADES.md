# Ceramic Trades — Potter, Kiln Master, Tile Maker, Porcelain Painter

## The Big Picture

Ceramics is one of humanity's oldest continuous industries. The trades within it span from the industrial production worker doing one narrow task 500 times a day to the studio artist who controls the entire process from clay to sale. Understanding the trade structure illuminates why certain ceramic traditions look the way they do.

```
CERAMIC TRADE HIERARCHY
========================

FINE ART / AUCTION                    INDUSTRIAL / MASS PRODUCTION
      |                                          |
Porcelain painter (Meissen, Sèvres)     Float glass / press line operator
Artist ceramicist                        IS machine operator
Studio potter                            Spray-glazer
      |                                  Production thrower (400+/day)
      |                                          |
   INDIVIDUAL ←————————————————————————→  FACTORY DIVISION
   (full process)                        (narrow specialization)

CORE TRADES BY PROCESS STAGE:
  Clay preparation  → Pug mill operator, clay mixer
  Forming           → Thrower, hand builder, caster, jiggerer
  Decoration        → Decorator, transfer printer, screen printer
  Firing            → Kiln master, fireman, kiln loader
  Glazing           → Glaze chemist, glaze technician, dipping/spraying
  Finishing         → Sponger, fettler, luster/enamel painter
  Quality control   → Inspector, gauge setter

ADJACENT TRADES:
  Model maker → creates originals for mold production
  Mold maker  → casts plaster molds from models
  Tile maker  → specialized forming + installation
  Refractories worker → kiln furniture, saggar, kiln bricks
```

## The Potter — Generalist Clay Former

The oldest trade designation. "Potter" historically meant anyone who made clay vessels — a functional catch-all that now means different things in different contexts.

```
HISTORICAL CONTEXT
==================

Ancient division:
 Almost every settled community had potters — among the first specialists
 alongside farmers; clay vessels required for grain storage, water, cooking
 Storage vessel = essential technology before refrigeration or metal working

Gender dynamics (complex, varies by culture):
 HAND BUILDING TRADITIONS — women dominant:
  African pottery: West Africa (Yoruba, Akan), East Africa, southern Africa
  = women's craft in most traditions; sometimes restricted to specific clans
  Native American: women the primary potters in most Pueblo, Navajo traditions
  (Maria Martinez, Lucy Lewis, Margaret Tafoya = major 20th c. names)
  Pre-Columbian South America: mostly women's production

 WHEEL THROWING TRADITIONS — men dominant:
  Middle Eastern, South Asian, East Asian: wheel = men's trade
  European guild system: male-dominated; women excluded from guilds
  The wheel's introduction often (not always) shifted gender dynamics
  toward male dominance

 Exceptions everywhere:
  Thailand, India, Morocco: complex mixed traditions
  Contemporary: largely equalized in studio ceramics

STAFFORDSHIRE POTTERIES (English industrial tradition):
 Wedgwood family had been potters for 6+ generations before Josiah
 "The Potteries" = the five towns (Stoke-on-Trent, Burslem, Hanley,
  Longton, Fenton) — densest concentration of pottery industry in history
 Social structure: potters as skilled working class, not artisans
 Children worked: "mould runners" (carrying ware), "dippers" (glaze)
 Lung diseases: flint dust (silicosis), glaze lead dust → endemic in
 the trade until 20th c. regulation
```

## The Thrower — Wheel-Throwing Specialist

```
INDUSTRIAL THROWER
===================

In Wedgwood-era factories, division of labor fragmented the "potter" role:
  Thrower: forms on wheel to standard dimensions
  Handler: attaches handles (separate formed piece)
  Turner/Fettler: trims and cleans foot ring; removes seams
  Sponger: sponges surfaces smooth
  Dipping: immerses in glaze
  Decorator: painted or printed decoration
  Kiln placer: loads kiln furniture, sets ware

Industrial thrower skill set:
 Speed: 100-200 identical pieces per day
 Consistency: each piece to gauge (template) dimensions
 No creative expression: optimization for identical output
 Pay: piece-rate (paid per piece thrown, not per hour)
 Training: traditional 7-year apprenticeship in Staffordshire trades

Throwing etymology:
 "Throw" = from Old English "thrawan" — to twist, to turn
 Alternative theory: the act of "throwing" clay onto the wheelhead
 to center it — still done by modern throwers

STUDIO THROWER vs PRODUCTION THROWER:
 Studio: individual expression; variable forms; slower (1-20/day)
 Production: dimensional consistency; speed; predictability
 The studio movement rejected the production model as its explicit premise
 (Leach, Hamada → individual expression back into the pot)
```

## The Kiln Master

```
KILN MASTER / FIREMAN
======================

The most consequential role in pre-industrial ceramics.
The same clay and glaze could produce different results
entirely based on the firing — kiln master's knowledge was proprietary.

TRADITIONAL WOOD KILN FIRING:
 Pre-industrial: all kilns were wood or coal
 Kiln master knowledge:
  - Loading the kiln (affects heat distribution and atmosphere)
  - Reading flame color (1,000°C = dull red; 1,100°C = orange;
    1,200°C = yellow; 1,280°C+ = white-yellow)
  - Damper management (controls atmosphere — reduction vs oxidation)
  - Cooling rate judgement (too fast → quartz inversion cracking)
  - Experience of specific kiln's "personality" (each kiln unique)
 Traditional guild: kiln master often a separate trade from potter
 The fireman at a 18th-c. English bottle kiln had enormous responsibility
 (one botched firing could destroy £1,000s of work)

ANAGAMA AND WOOD FIRING TODAY:
 Traditional Japanese anagama (single-chamber climbing kiln):
  3-5 day firing (continuous stoking)
  2-3 person stoking rotation (physical, hot, around-the-clock)
  Kiln master reads flame and draws test rings (draw trials)
  through small holes in kiln wall to assess progress
  The firing = an event; community of potters often fire together
  Outcome uncertainty = aesthetically central (fly ash, flame marks)

ELECTRIC KILN FIRING (modern studio):
 Much more controllable — thermocouple, kiln controller, ramp/soak programs
 Still requires knowledge of:
  - Glaze chemistry (compatible thermal expansion)
  - Loading and kiln furniture (no touching pieces)
  - Cooling rate through 573°C quartz inversion
  - Vent timing (off-gassing in bisque and early glaze fire)
 Kiln firing remains a skill even with automation

INDUSTRIAL KILN OPERATIONS:
 Tunnel kilns (continuous — ware moves through on kiln cars)
 Roller hearth kilns (ware moves on ceramic rollers)
 Kiln operators monitor temperature profile, atmosphere, speed
 Automated but failure modes require skilled intervention
```

## The Glaze Chemist and Glaze Technician

```
GLAZE CHEMISTRY AS SPECIALTY TRADE
=====================================

INDUSTRIAL GLAZE TECHNICIAN:
 Maintains glaze consistency across production batches
 Responsibilities:
  - Test firing: small test tiles to verify color/surface before full batch
  - Specific gravity monitoring (hydrometer — controls glaze application weight)
  - Raw materials testing (each new material delivery needs verification)
  - Color matching: customers want identical color lot after lot
  - Defect diagnosis (crawling, crazing, pinholing — see 04-GLAZES)
  - Lead-free reformulation: post-1970s regulatory wave required
    replacing traditional lead-flux glazes with safer alternatives
    (food-contact law in US; EU REACH; UK regulations)
  - Firing schedule development (temperature ramps for new glazes)

GLAZE CALCULATION SOFTWARE:
 Insight (Tony Hansen): industry standard for many production shops
 Glazy.org: open-source glaze recipe database + calculation
 Calculation produces: UMF (Unity Molecular Formula) —
  normalizes recipe to molar ratios; allows comparison of glazes
  independent of ingredient choices (see 04-GLAZES)
 A good glaze chemist understands the UMF intuitively —
  can predict how a glaze will behave from its formula

STUDIO CERAMICS APPROACH:
 Many studio potters develop glazes empirically:
  Line blends (vary one material in steps across test tiles)
  Triaxial blends (vary three materials simultaneously)
  100 test tiles → identify successful areas → refine
 Some studio potters work from calculated formulas; most from recipe books
  plus systematic testing
 The empiricist vs chemist divide is a real cultural split in studio ceramics
```

## The Porcelain Painter (Enamellist)

```
PORCELAIN PAINTING AS SPECIALIZED TRADE
=========================================

MEISSEN (GERMANY):
 Founded 1710; specialist painting trades established from ~1720
 Johann Gregorius Höroldt (1696-1775): developed the Meissen overglaze
  enamel color system; the first stable palette for fine porcelain painting
 Specialist divisions at large factories:
  Flower painters (blumenmalerei)
  Bird painters
  Landscape painters
  Figure/scene painters
  Portrait miniaturists
  Gilders (separate: application of gold and polishing)
 Each specialty required separate training; painters did not cross specialties
 Training: 3-year apprenticeship today at Meissen factory
 Product: hand-painted Meissen pieces command 10-100× premium over
  transfer-printed equivalents
 AR mark: "AR" (Augustus Rex) stamped on pieces reserved for
  Augustus the Strong's personal collection → highest Meissen tier

SÈVRES (FRANCE):
 Royal manufactory; Madame de Pompadour as major patron (from Vincennes)
 Painting divisions similarly specialized
 Bleu céleste ground (turquoise), rose Pompadour (later rose du Barry):
  ground colors applied first, fired; then reserved white panels
  painted by specialist painters
 Court painters: paid at court rates; some pieces painted by named artists

CHINESE EXPORT PORCELAIN — TWO-CITY SYSTEM:
 Jingdezhen: forms all porcelain (throwing, casting, bisque firing)
 Guangzhou (Canton): decorates export ware in European taste
  Canton workshops: received white porcelain from Jingdezhen →
  painted in famille rose, famille verte for export → refired for enamel
 "Chinese export porcelain" decorated in Guangzhou, not Jingdezhen
 Armorial porcelain (family coat of arms): European merchants
  commissioned sets; Canton painters copied heraldic designs from
  sent drawings or painted paper
 Canton workshops still exist; now tourist market + collector market

CONTEMPORARY PORCELAIN PAINTER:
 Meissen apprenticeship still 3 years
 Royal Copenhagen, Wedgwood: mostly transfer print now with hand touch-up
 Studio enamel painters: independent artists working on purchased bisque
  or fired pieces; niche market
 China painting as hobby craft: large community (American China Painters
  Association); uses commercial overglaze enamels; kiln firing at home
```

## The Tile Maker

```
TILE MAKING — ANCIENT AND CONTINUOUS TRADE
===========================================

HISTORICAL TILE TRADITIONS:
 Roman: hypocaust floor tiles (flat terracotta floor slabs for
  underfloor heating system); pantiles and imbrex roof tiles
 Islamic geometric tile work:
  Mosaic tiles (cut and set in plaster — zellige, Morocco)
  Tile panels (Iznik; Alhambra lustre tiles)
  Muqarnas (stalactite vault — small ceramic units)
 Dutch Delftware tiles (17th c.): 13cm square white tin glaze tiles
  with blue cobalt paintings; shipped throughout Europe and
  Americas for interior decoration
 Victorian encaustic tiles: inlaid clay patterns (different color clays
  pressed into incised surface → filled, leveled, fired → pattern
  runs through full depth); Minton as major maker; parliamentary
  buildings, churches, railroad stations
 William de Morgan (Arts & Crafts, UK, 1870s-90s):
  Tiles + pottery; Islamic-inspired luster tiles; hand-painted flora/fauna
 Grueby Pottery (Boston, 1890s-1910s): matte green art tiles; Arts & Crafts

INDUSTRIAL TILE PRODUCTION:
 Dry pressing: clay powder (3-7% moisture) pressed in dies
  under 200-400 tonnes pressure
  → very precise dimensions
  → minimal forming shrinkage (only firing shrinkage)
  → high production rates (1,000s/hour)
  → limited to simple geometries
 Extrusion: for shaped/profiled tiles and bullnose profiles
 Large format: recent development (3m × 1.5m full-body porcelain slabs)
  challenges transport, cutting, installation; requires suction cup handling

TILE SETTER vs TILE MAKER:
 The production of tiles and the installation are separate trades
 Tile setter (tiler) installs; uses bed setting methods:
  Traditional: sand-cement bedding (thick bed)
  Modern: thin-set (cement-based adhesive, much thinner bed)
  Grouting: Portland cement or epoxy grout
  Large format requires specialized large-format setting skills
 Historic tile restoration: specialist subset of conservation

PORCELAIN TILE vs CERAMIC TILE:
 Industry classification: "porcelain tile" = water absorption <0.5%
  (fully vitrified at firing temp); suitable outdoor/wet areas
 "Ceramic tile" = earthenware body, higher absorption;
  interior only in freeze climates
 Consumer confusion: all ceramic = "tiles"; but water resistance differs
```

## The Model Maker and Mold Maker

```
MODEL MAKER / MOLD MAKER
=========================

Separate from the forming workers — the people who make the tools
that enable forming.

MODEL MAKER (original sculpture):
 At major porcelain factories: the sculptor who creates the original
  from which production molds are taken
 Meissen: Johann Joachim Kändler (1706-1775)
  — most influential porcelain modeler in history
  — created the entire Meissen figurine vocabulary:
    Swan service (1737-1741): 2,200 pieces, entire dinner service modeled
    Commedia dell'arte figures (Harlequin, Columbine, Pantaloon)
    Animal figures: life-size animals for Japanese Palace, Augustus
  — his models still in production today (300+ years later)
 Contemporary: modelers at Meissen, Royal Copenhagen, Lladró
  work in traditional sculpting clay before conversion to production molds

MOLD MAKER (plaster molds):
 Slip casting requires plaster molds (plaster absorbs water from slip)
 Production plaster mold: white plaster of Paris (calcium sulfate hemihydrate)
  cast around master form → porous → absorbs water from clay slip
 Master → case mold (reinforced) → working molds (worn out after ~50 casts)
 Industrial mold shop: team produces hundreds of identical working molds
  which are regularly replaced as they wear
 Jiggering molds: different from casting molds; must withstand pressure
 Mold making is a precision trade — mold dimensions include
  allowance for clay shrinkage during drying and firing

CAD/CAM AND DIGITAL PROTOTYPING:
 Contemporary: 3D scanning of models + CNC machining of plaster
  or resin blocks → faster iteration on design
 3D printing (FDM, resin SLA): printing wax or resin masters
  directly → eliminating hand sculpting for geometric forms
 The traditional model maker skill is being disrupted but
  not eliminated — organic forms (figurines, decorative relief)
  still benefit from skilled hand sculpting
```

## Studio Ceramics as a Career

```
STUDIO CERAMICS ECONOMICS
===========================

The "three-legged stool" (industry term):
  1. MAKING — production of work for sale
  2. SELLING — direct and wholesale sales channels
  3. TEACHING — income from classes, workshops, courses

Why the stool metaphor: any two legs alone won't support a career;
 most studio potters need all three in some combination.

INCOME STREAM BREAKDOWN (typical studio potter):
 Making + selling direct:        20-40% of income
 Making + selling at craft fairs: 15-25%
 Teaching (studio classes):      30-50%
 Residencies/grants:             5-15% if obtained
 Online (Etsy, Instagram sales): 10-30% increasingly

PRICING MODELS:
 Formula approach: (materials + labor at $X/hr + overhead) × markup
  Studio potters undercharge for labor systematically
 Market approach: what collectors/buyers pay in your market
 Value approach: what the piece is worth as an art object
 The tension: pottery buyers expect functional ware to be cheap;
  the work per piece is often not reflected in market prices

PRODUCTION VOLUME HIERARCHY:
 One-of-a-kind studio artist:
  - 20-100 pieces/year; high prices ($200-$5,000+/piece)
  - Gallery representation; studio sales; collectors
 Limited production studio:
  - 500-2,000 pieces/year; consistent forms with variations
  - Craft fair + direct mail + studio sales
 Volume production studio:
  - 2,000-10,000+ pieces/year; standardized forms
  - Wholesale to shops, restaurants, online
 Scale example: East Fork Pottery (Asheville, NC)
  - Instagram-built brand; moved to volume production
  - $10M+ annual revenue; employs multiple production throwers
  - Raised prices substantially as brand established

MAKING + SELLING PLATFORMS:
 Craft fairs: American Craft Council (high-end); local/regional fairs
  (lower barrier to entry); booth fees $300-$3,000/weekend
 Instagram: portfolio + direct sales + workshop promotion
  Several potters built 100K+ followings → waitlists for work
 Etsy: volume market; fierce price competition; better for production
 Studio sales: one or two/year at home studio; loyal collector base
 Gallery representation: 50% commission; prestige; reduced sales effort
  (rare for functional pottery; more common for sculptural work)

RESIDENCIES:
 Artist-in-residence programs: university departments, arts centers,
  non-profit studios; provide studio space, kiln access, stipend
 Duration: 3-12 months typically
 Notable: Archie Bray Foundation (Montana); Pottery Northwest;
  Watershed Center (Maine); European ceramic centers
 Value: time to develop work without financial pressure;
  community of peers
```

## Ceramic Education

```
EDUCATION PATHWAYS
===================

UNIVERSITY (BFA/MFA):
 Art schools: ceramics as one of several studio art media
 Program type: fine art program; emphasis is concept, critique, exhibition
  NOT primarily technical instruction
 Degree: BFA (4 years); MFA (2-3 years, terminal degree in US)
 MFA typically required for university teaching position
 Major US programs: Alfred University (Alfred, NY) — the most ceramics-
  focused university in US; RISD; Cranbrook; Ohio State; Arizona State
 UK: Royal College of Art; Staffordshire University; Cardiff
 The gap: MFA produces artists + academics; not production potters

VOCATIONAL TRAINING:
 UK NVQ/apprenticeship: declining; Industry Skills Council (CABWI)
  has ceramic industry pathway; Glass and Ceramics Apprenticeship
 German Keramiker/Keramikerin apprenticeship:
  3-year formal apprenticeship; covers all technical areas;
  leads to Gesellenbrief (journeyman certificate)
  Further: Meister qualification (master craftsperson) = can run a business
  The German system → highest technical ceramic training in world
 Japan: traditional pottery schools and apprenticeship with masters;
  Mashiko (Hamada's village) has attached school;
  Tokyo University of the Arts: ceramics department
 US: no formal apprenticeship infrastructure for studio ceramics

CRAFT SCHOOL COURSES:
 Leach Pottery (St Ives, UK):
  Foundation course: 3 months; covers full ceramic process
  Focus: the Leach aesthetic tradition; Japanese and English influences
 Haystack Mountain School (Maine): intensive summer workshops
 Penland School of Crafts (NC): week-long and year-long residencies
 Pottery Northwest (Seattle): short courses + residencies
 Value: intensive immersion; direct instruction from experienced potters;
  community; faster than self-teaching

SELF-TEACHING:
 Large portion of practicing studio potters are primarily self-taught
 Resources: books (Leach "A Potter's Book"; Robin Hopper "Functional Pottery")
  Tony Hansen's Digitalfire website (most technical ceramic resource online)
  Glazy.org (glaze database, calculation, community)
  YouTube: large community of ceramics tutorial channels
 Community studio membership: access kiln + clay room + peer learning

PROFESSIONAL PUBLICATIONS:
 Ceramics: Art and Perception (Australian): premier studio ceramics journal
 Ceramics Technical (Australian: paired with above): technical focus
 Pottery Making Illustrated (US, American Ceramics Society affiliate)
 Ceramic Arts Network (web-based; digitalfire.com)
 Ceramic Review (UK): longest-running English-language ceramics magazine
 American Ceramics Society (technical/industrial side): JACS (Journal of the
  American Ceramic Society) — scientific, not studio focused

## Common Confusion Points

**Potter vs ceramist vs ceramic artist**:
"Potter" = functional ware tradition; often implies wheel-throwing. "Ceramist" = broader (functional or sculptural). "Ceramic artist" = fine art positioning. The words signal aesthetic and market positioning as much as medium.

**Studio ceramics MFA ≠ pottery instruction**:
An MFA in ceramics from an art school is a fine art degree. The emphasis is concept, critique, and professional practice. Technical instruction is minimal or entirely absent. Someone who wants to learn to throw well should attend a craft school or study with a working potter — not get an MFA.

**The kiln master's knowledge was proprietary**:
Pre-industrial, kiln firing knowledge was a trade secret. Kiln masters were paid for their knowledge, not just their labor. The industrial introduction of temperature gauges, electric kilns, and programmable controllers transferred the kiln master's accumulated wisdom to a machine — a major trade disruption.

**Industrial division of labor obscures the full craft**:
In a large production pottery (Wedgwood model), no single worker sees the full process. The thrower never glazes; the glaze dipper never decorates; the decorator never fires. This is efficiency maximization at the cost of craft integration. The studio movement explicitly reversed this — one potter, full process.

**Tile maker ≠ tile setter**:
The person who produces tiles (in a factory or studio) and the person who installs them are different trades with different skills. Tile setting (thin-set mortar, grout, layout geometry, waterproofing) is a construction trade. Tile making is a ceramic manufacturing trade.
