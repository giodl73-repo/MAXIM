# 10 — Ceramic Trades

## Potter, Kiln Master, Tile Maker, Porcelain Painter — The Trade Hierarchy

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Potter**: the generalist clay-forming trade; encompasses all hand and wheel forming; in traditional societies a skilled specialist (not everyone could throw); pottery as gendered trade varied by culture (women potters dominant in many African and Native American traditions; men dominant in Middle Eastern and East Asian wheel-throwing traditions; complex gender dynamics); professional potters distinguished from agricultural communities who also made basic storage vessels; Josiah Wedgwood's family had been potters in Staffordshire for generations before his industrial transformation; contemporary studio potter = artist + craftsperson + marketer
- **Thrower (wheel-throwing specialist)**: in industrial potteries, division of labor separated the "thrower" as the primary forming specialist from "handlers" (who attached handles), "finishers" (who trimmed foot rings), "decorators," etc.; Wedgwood explicitly divided these roles; skill of an industrial thrower = speed and consistency (100-200 bowls per day to identical dimensions); studio thrower emphasis on individual expression; "throwing" = technical term for wheel forming (etymology disputed — possibly from "thrawen" = to twist or turn)
- **Kiln master / fireman**: responsible for the firing — the most consequential variable in ceramic production; in traditional wood/coal kilns, kiln masters held specialized knowledge of firing schedules, atmosphere management, loading optimization; loading a kiln is itself a skill (proper separation of pieces, saggar use, kiln furniture placement); in pre-industrial England, "fireman" was a distinct trade from potter; modern electric kiln firing is more controllable but glaze chemists and production managers still oversee firing cycles; traditional wood kiln firing (anagama) requires experienced stokers and watchers throughout 3-5 day firing
- **Glaze chemist / glaze technician**: industrial potteries employ glaze technicians to maintain consistency; glaze chemistry calculations (Insight, Digitalfire), raw materials testing (specific gravity of slip, fired tests), color matching; lead-free reformulation (post-1970s regulatory changes requiring lead-free food-safe glazes); industrial colorist who develops new glaze colors for product lines; studio potters increasingly use glaze calculation software but many still work empirically
- **Porcelain painter (enamellist)**: at Meissen, Sèvres, and other major factories, enamel painting was a specialized trade separate from forming; apprentices trained specifically to paint one type of subject (flowers, birds, portraits, landscapes — separate specialties at large factories); miniaturist precision required; signed pieces at major factories command premium (Meissen "AR" mark for pieces from Augustus the Strong's collection); Chinese export porcelain often painted in Guangzhou (Canton) separately from manufacturing at Jingdezhen — two-city system; famille rose, famille verte enamel styles; contemporary porcelain painters at Meissen still undergo 3-year apprenticeships
- **Tile maker**: ceramic tiles = ancient and continuous trade; Roman hypocaust floor tiles; Islamic geometric tile work (Alhambra, Süleymaniye); Dutch delftware tiles (interior decoration, 17th c.); Victorian encaustic tiles (inlaid clay patterns — Minton); arts and crafts tiles (William de Morgan, Grueby); contemporary architectural tile; modern industrial tile production: dust pressing (dry or semi-dry clay powder pressed in dies under 200-400 tonnes → very precise dimensions, no shrinkage from forming, small shrinkage in firing); extrusion for some profiles; glazed vs unglazed porcelain tile; large format tile (3m × 1.5m slabs — recent industrial development)
- **Model maker / mold maker**: at porcelain factories, the sculptor/model maker creates the original model in clay or wax from which production molds are taken; distinct from the casting and forming workers; Meissen modeler Johann Joachim Kändler (1706-1775) created the Meissen animal, figure, and tableware models that defined the factory's identity; contemporary mold makers create plaster molds for slip casting and jiggering
- **Studio ceramics as career**: combination of making, selling, teaching; most studio potters cannot survive solely on sales; common pattern: make + sell at craft fairs + sell online + teach classes + artist residencies; large production studios (more consistent income) vs artist-potters (high-end, gallery-represented, lower volume, higher prices); notable sustainable studio models: Erin Hupp (Missouri), East Fork Pottery (North Carolina — successful direct-to-consumer brand built via social media, then moved to volume production)
- **Ceramic education**: university-based (BFA/MFA in ceramics at most art schools); vocational (Pottery apprenticeships in UK, German Keramikerin qualification — 3-year apprenticeship); short courses (Leach Pottery foundation courses, Northern Potters Association workshops); self-taught tradition (many successful potters — the nature of the materials rewards patient self-teaching); Cone 10 Press, Ceramics: Art and Perception (Australian journal) as primary professional publications
