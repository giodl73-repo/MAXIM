# Gender and Development: Boserup, Women in Development, Care Economy

## The Big Picture

Gender was invisible in development theory and policy for its first four decades. The correction — from Boserup (1970) through the WID/GAD debate, to evidence-based gender mainstreaming — has been substantial but incomplete. The care economy remains systematically unmeasured and undervalued.

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    GENDER AND DEVELOPMENT LANDSCAPE                      |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                          |
|  PARADIGM TIMELINE:                                                      |
|  1970 Boserup: named the problem (colonialism disrupted women's roles)  |
|    ↓                                                                     |
|  1975 WID (Women in Development): integrate women into existing programs|
|    "Add women and stir" — leave structures unchanged                    |
|    ↓ critique: insufficient; doesn't address structural power           |
|  1985 WAD (Women and Development): women's relationship to capitalism   |
|    ↓                                                                     |
|  1990s GAD (Gender and Development): transform structures, not add women|
|    Mainstreaming gender into all development activities                 |
|    ↓                                                                     |
|  2000s Evidence turn: RCTs on gender-targeted interventions             |
|    CCTs to women; education returns; microfinance + empowerment         |
|    ↓                                                                     |
|  2010s Care economy mainstreaming: time-use surveys, unpaid care data   |
|    World Bank: "gender equality is smart economics"                     |
|    ↓                                                                     |
|  Now: Backlash, "gender ideology" politics, implementation gaps         |
|                                                                          |
|  KEY MEASUREMENT TOOLS:                                                  |
|  GDI (Gender Development Index): HDI disaggregated by sex               |
|  GII (Gender Inequality Index): reproductive health + empowerment + LFP |
|  Time-use surveys: unpaid care economy quantification                   |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## 1. Ester Boserup: The Founding Text

*Woman's Role in Economic Development* (Ester Boserup, 1970) is the foundational text of gender and development. Written at 57, a Danish agricultural economist who had spent her career in India, she documented what no development economist had previously noticed:

**The African farming system disruption**:
```
PRE-COLONIAL AFRICAN AGRICULTURE:

  WOMEN'S ROLE:                    MEN'S ROLE:
  Primary cultivators               Clearing land
  Crop selection                    Hunting
  Soil preparation                  Livestock (in pastoral areas)
  Planting, weeding, harvesting     Political/ritual functions
  Food processing
  Water and fuel collection

  Women had recognized roles, property-like rights over crops,
  some autonomy in production decisions

COLONIAL DISRUPTION:

  Colonial authorities introduced:
    1. New "improved" farming techniques → taught to men
    2. Cash crop cultivation → men's domain
    3. Land tenure reform → registered to male "heads of household"
    4. Agricultural extension → male agents visiting male farmers

  RESULT:
    Green revolution technologies (fertilizer, HYV seeds) → men
    Cash crop profits → men
    Women's subsistence farming → unpaid, undervalued, invisible
    Women's workload ↑ (more subsistence work for unchanged household)
    Women's status ↓ (income and technology access lost)
```

**The Green Revolution gender bias**:
- High-yielding variety seeds required: credit (inaccessible to women), fertilizer (cash, men's domain), irrigation (land rights, men)
- Rice: women dominant in processing, seedbed preparation → mechanization displaced women
- Africa vs. Asia: different patterns, but consistent female exclusion from technical change benefits

## 2. WID → WAD → GAD

**Women in Development (WID)**: emerged from 1975 UN Women's Conference in Mexico City:

```
WID FRAMEWORK:
  Problem: women excluded from development process
  Solution: integrate women into existing programs
    - Women's project components
    - Women-targeted credit programs
    - Women's income-generating activities (IGAs)
    - Count women's economic contributions

CRITIQUE — "Add Women and Stir":
  Doesn't question why women were excluded
  Assumes development itself is fine; just add women
  Instrumentalizes women: "investment in women → growth"
  Doesn't address underlying patriarchal structures
  Women added to workload without changing distribution:
  market work + domestic work = double burden
```

**Women and Development (WAD)**: emerged from socialist feminist critique:
```
WAD FRAMEWORK:
  Women always contributed to development — they just weren't counted
  The problem is capitalism's systematic exploitation of women's labor
  → domestic labor subsidizes wages
  → women's low-paid work subsidizes profit
  → Solution: transform capitalist structures, not add women to them
```

**Gender and Development (GAD)**: emerged 1980s–90s, now mainstream:
```
GAD FRAMEWORK:
  NOT "women" as target category → "gender" as structural analysis
  Gender = socially constructed roles, relations, power differences
  Men and women both have gender; institutions have gender

  Problem: unequal gender power relations embedded in institutions
  Solution: transform the institutions
    - Analyze how policies affect men and women differently
    - Challenge patriarchal norms, not just offer women programs
    - Address unpaid care work distribution
    - Gender mainstreaming: incorporate gender analysis into ALL programs
    - Men's engagement: change masculinity norms

  Gender mainstreaming (Beijing Platform 1995): all development policies
  must analyze differential gender impacts before implementation
```

## 3. The Care Economy

The care economy — unpaid care and domestic work performed primarily by women — is the central structural issue:

```
CARE ECONOMY FACTS:

Time-use surveys (OECD, World Bank compilation):
  Women: 3-6 hours/day unpaid care (developed countries)
         4-8 hours/day unpaid care (developing countries)
  Men:   1-2 hours/day unpaid care
  Ratio: women do 2-10× more unpaid care than men

VALUE:
  If valued at replacement cost (market wages for care services):
  Unpaid care = 10-39% of GDP (varies by country, method)
  Globally: ~$10.8 trillion/year (Oxfam estimate)
  → Largest "sector" in most economies, not in national accounts

TYPES OF UNPAID CARE:
  Direct care: childcare, elder care, care for sick/disabled
  Domestic work: cooking, cleaning, laundry, shopping
  Indirect care: water/fuel collection in poor contexts
                 (can be 4+ hours/day in rural Africa)

CARE POVERTY:
  Care burden concentrates in poor households:
    - Cannot buy market substitutes (cook/clean services)
    - Public services (childcare, elder care) absent or unaffordable
    - Infrastructure deficits (no running water → hours of collection)
```

**Policy implications of care economy recognition**:

```
THREE Rs FRAMEWORK (Elson):

  RECOGNIZE: Count unpaid care in national accounts
             Time-use surveys as policy tool
             Include care in development indicators

  REDUCE: Technology investment that reduces care burden
          Running water, LPG cooking fuel, labor-saving devices
          Infrastructure that disproportionately benefits women

  REDISTRIBUTE: Within households (men take on more care)
               To state (public childcare, elder care)
               To employers (parental leave, flexible work)
               Social protection for unpaid carers
```

## 4. Women's Education: Returns and the Demographic Dividend

T. Paul Schultz's influential findings on girls' education:

```
EDUCATION RETURNS FOR GIRLS:

  Average returns to education (wage premium per year of schooling):
  Men: 7-8% in developing countries
  Women: 8-10% (often higher than men)

  BUT: Women's returns often unrealized because:
    - Labor market discrimination → lower wages regardless of education
    - Marriage market: education → later marriage → fewer children
    - Social norms: educated women still excluded from skilled work

  DEMOGRAPHIC EFFECTS (more important than direct wage effect):

  Girls' education → later age at first marriage
                  → later first birth
                  → fewer total births
                  → more investment per child (fewer children)
                  → better child nutrition/health

  This creates a DEMOGRAPHIC DIVIDEND:
  Fewer children per woman → lower dependency ratio
  → More women in labor force
  → National savings increase
  → Growth acceleration

  East Asia's demographic dividend ~1970–2010 estimated to explain
  25-40% of economic growth (Bloom/Williamson 1998)
  Triggered by: girls' education + family planning + economic opportunity
```

## 5. Conditional Cash Transfers to Women

Why CCTs target women (discussed in 05-AID-EFFECTIVENESS.md in detail):

**Evidence on women vs. men as recipients**:
```
MEXICO PROGRESA / OPORTUNIDADES:

  CCT paid to female head of household (mother)
  vs. early pilot comparisons with male recipient

  FINDINGS:
    Mothers: 80-90 cents per dollar spent on family consumption
             (food, children's clothing, health)
    Fathers: smaller share on family; more on adult goods (alcohol, tobacco)

  MECHANISM (Duncan Thomas 1990, basis for this targeting):
    Women's income → child health, nutrition, education
    Men's income → adult goods
    At the margin, women's income more productive for child development

  CAUTION: This is average effect; individual variation large.
           Targeting mothers can be infantilizing; can increase
           women's burden (now responsible for outcomes).
```

**Empowerment effects of CCTs**:
- Limited direct effects on women's autonomy, physical mobility, voice within household
- Income effect: some evidence of reduced intimate partner violence (financial independence reduces leverage)
- Positive: recognition as formal program recipient, outside household contact

## 6. Land Rights and Titling

Land is the key productive asset in agricultural economies. Who holds title has large effects:

```
LAND RIGHTS AND GENDER:

  TRADITIONAL/CUSTOMARY SYSTEMS:
    Often usufruct rights (right to use) for women
    Not ownership rights → cannot sell, mortgage, bequeath
    Dependent on husband's/family's permission
    Divorce or widowhood → loss of access

  FORMAL TITLING REFORM:
    Expected: secure title → investment → productivity

  THE JOINT TITLING vs. INDIVIDUAL TITLING DEBATE:

  Individual titling to women:
    + Full security, can transact independently
    + Strong investment incentives
    + Protection in case of divorce/widowhood
    - May violate social norms (backlash risk)
    - May not be accepted in local land markets

  Joint titling (husband + wife):
    + Socially acceptable
    + Protects women against unilateral sale
    + Recognized in legal system
    - Still requires husband's consent
    - Less protection if relationship dissolves

  DOSS ET AL. (2015):
    In Ghana, Ethiopia, Bangladesh: most women claim
    they have rights to household land — but "rights"
    depend on household relationship, not formal law
    Formal title doesn't guarantee women's control
    if social norms don't support it
```

**Joint titling in practice**: Ethiopia's land certification program (joint title with photo of both spouses) found improvements in women's investment and tenure security. Context-specific designs matter more than universal prescriptions.

## 7. Gender-Based Violence as Development Constraint

GBV (intimate partner violence, sexual violence) is both a human rights issue and a development constraint:

```
DEVELOPMENT COSTS OF GBV:

  HEALTH:
    Injuries, chronic pain, reproductive health damage
    Mental health: depression, PTSD, substance use
    HIV/AIDS risk: coercive sex, reduced negotiation of condom use

  ECONOMIC:
    Lost productivity: absenteeism, job loss
    Medical costs
    Criminal justice costs
    Perpetrators lose productivity when incarcerated (where prosecuted)

  INTERGENERATIONAL:
    Children witnessing violence: higher probability of abuse
    Reduced school attendance for children in violent households
    Cycle of violence across generations

  MEASUREMENT:
    WHO multi-country study: 15-71% of women report lifetime
    physical/sexual partner violence (varies enormously by context)
    Lower income countries generally higher rates
    Survey measurement complex: underreporting common

INTERVENTIONS WITH EVIDENCE:
  Income interventions + GBV training (IMAGE, South Africa)
  Social norm change programs (IMAGES Brazil)
  Economic empowerment + safety messaging
  Cash transfers reduce food insecurity → reduce GBV trigger
```

## 8. Microfinance and Women's Empowerment

Microfinance programs target women partly on the premise of empowerment. The evidence is mixed:

```
GRAMEEN BANK: 97%+ women borrowers
Arguments for women targeting:
  1. Better repayment (empirical)
  2. Children benefit more from women's income (discussed above)
  3. Women have less access to other capital
  4. Empowerment: economic independence → voice

RCT EVIDENCE ON EMPOWERMENT:
  Banerjee et al. (2015) six-country evaluation:
    No significant effects on women's empowerment measures
    (decision-making, physical mobility, freedom from violence)

EXPLANATION:
  Financial access is necessary but not sufficient for empowerment
  Social norms constrain what financial access translates to
  Group lending can reinforce existing hierarchies
  Debt can increase burden (husband controls income; wife has debt liability)

THE "BURDEN DOUBLING" CRITIQUE:
  Microfinance assigns women market responsibility
  Without reducing domestic responsibility
  → Women work more; empowerment unclear
  Goetz and Gupta (1996): in Bangladesh, men controlled
  a substantial portion of women's microfinance loans
  ("funnel effect" — loan in woman's name, use by male household head)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**WID is not GAD**: WID adds women to programs; GAD analyzes gender as a structural power relationship and seeks to transform institutions. "Gender mainstreaming" is a GAD approach. Many programs nominally claim GAD while actually implementing WID (adding women's components without structural analysis).

**Gender ≠ women**: GAD framework analyzes gender as a relationship between men and women, both of which have gender. Development programs for men (e.g., changing masculinity norms to reduce GBV or increase paternal care) are gender programs.

**The demographic dividend has conditions**: Girls' education → lower fertility → dividend requires also that: economic opportunities exist for women (otherwise educated women just marry later without entering labor force), and child mortality is low (parents need to trust fewer children will survive).

**The "women invest in children" finding is average, not universal**: The targeting of CCTs to women is based on average effects from multiple studies. Individual households vary. Framing all women as more virtuous investors than all men is a stereotype. The policy is justified by average population-level evidence, not as a claim about individual behavior.

**Colonialism disrupted systems that were already patriarchal**: Boserup showed colonialism worsened women's position, but pre-colonial African systems were not egalitarian. Women had recognized roles, not equal status. The historical baseline was unequal; colonialism made it worse in specific ways.
