# Microfinance and Poverty: Grameen Bank, Impact Evidence, Mobile Money

## The Big Picture

Microfinance promised to solve poverty through credit market access. The evidence reveals a more complicated story: credit alone doesn't escape poverty traps; savings and insurance may matter more; and the largest poverty-reduction story from financial inclusion is mobile money, not Grameen-style lending.

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    MICROFINANCE LANDSCAPE                                 |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                          |
|  THEORY:                                                                 |
|  Credit market failure → poor excluded → business formation blocked     |
|  Group lending solves information asymmetry                             |
|  → Microcredit → microenterprise → poverty escape                       |
|                                                                          |
|  EVIDENCE:                                                               |
|  Banerjee et al. 2015 (six countries, six RCTs):                        |
|    Average effect: modest (some new businesses; consumption smoothing)  |
|    No transformative poverty escape on average                          |
|    Some households benefited substantially; most did not                |
|                                                                          |
|  THE CYCLE OF HYPE AND CRISIS:                                          |
|  Grameen 1983 → Nobel 2006 → Commercialization 2000s →                 |
|  Compartamos IPO 2007 → AP crisis 2010 → mission drift debate          |
|                                                                          |
|  WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS BETTER:                                            |
|  Savings: VSLA village savings groups (stronger evidence)               |
|  Insurance: index insurance (works when well-designed)                  |
|  Mobile money: M-Pesa Kenya (largest financial inclusion impact)        |
|  Unconditional cash transfers: GiveDirectly (strong RCT evidence)      |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## 1. The Credit Market Failure Model

Why do poor households lack access to formal credit?

```
CREDIT MARKET FAILURE MECHANISMS:

1. ADVERSE SELECTION (hidden information):
   Bank cannot distinguish risky from safe borrowers at application
   → Sets interest rate reflecting average risk
   → Safe borrowers (low risk tolerance) find rate too high and exit
   → Pool becomes riskier → bank raises rate further
   → Death spiral: credit rationing (Stiglitz-Weiss 1981)

2. MORAL HAZARD (hidden action):
   After receiving loan, borrower can reduce effort or take risky actions
   Bank cannot monitor borrower behavior
   → Borrower under-invests effort (debt = insurance against failure)
   → Default rates higher than expected

3. LACK OF COLLATERAL:
   Formal banks require collateral (property, assets)
   Poor households have no formal collateral
   → Excluded from formal credit market

4. HIGH TRANSACTION COSTS FOR SMALL LOANS:
   Processing costs for a $100 loan ≈ processing costs for $10,000 loan
   → Cost per dollar lent is high for small loans
   → Formal banks unprofitable → don't serve poor

TRADITIONAL "SOLUTION": moneylenders
   Local information advantage → can assess risk
   Social proximity → enforcement mechanism
   BUT: monopoly position → exploitative rates (100-400% APR)
```

## 2. Grameen Bank and Group Lending

Muhammad Yunus founded Grameen Bank in Bangladesh in 1983 (formal charter). Nobel Peace Prize 2006.

**Group lending mechanism**:
```
GRAMEEN GROUP LENDING STRUCTURE:

  5 borrowers form a group (self-selected → information screening)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Borrower 1  │  Borrower 2  │  Borrower 3 │
  │  Borrower 4  │  Borrower 5              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────┘
                    │
              JOINT LIABILITY:
              If any member defaults,
              group loses access to future loans
                    │
              ┌─────────────────────┐
              │ INCENTIVE EFFECTS:  │
              │ 1. Select good peers│
              │    (adverse sel. ↓) │
              │ 2. Monitor peers    │
              │    (moral hazard ↓) │
              │ 3. Bail out peers   │
              │    (default ↓)      │
              └─────────────────────┘

  WEEKLY MEETINGS:
  Groups meet weekly with field officer
  Repayment in small installments (discipline)
  Social pressure for repayment
  Information sharing about business opportunities

  WOMEN BORROWERS:
  Grameen: 97%+ women borrowers
  Theory: women have higher social capital (more constrained, more embedded
         in community), thus better group lending candidates
  AND: women more likely to invest in household/children
```

**The original Grameen model's features**:
- Loan starts small (~$50–200), grows with repayment history
- Above-market interest rates (~20-25% nominal, ~30-40% effective annual)
- No collateral — group guarantee instead
- Field officers visit villages (no branch infrastructure needed)
- Savings required alongside credit

## 3. The Theory of Group Lending: Formal Models

**Stiglitz (1990)** — group lending as mechanism design:
```
KEY RESULT: Under joint liability with group self-selection,
equilibrium has safer borrowers forming groups together
(homogeneous risk pooling) → adverse selection solved.

BUT: Requires:
  1. Homogeneous risk groups can form (not always possible)
  2. Joint liability is enforceable (requires social capital)
  3. Monitoring costs within group are low (requires dense social networks)

EMPIRICAL VALIDATION: Ghatak (1999, 2000) formalized this.
Works best in dense, stable communities. Fails in:
  - Mobile populations
  - Urban settings (weak social capital)
  - Heterogeneous communities
```

## 4. Impact Evidence: The RCT Results

The critical RCTs on microfinance came in the 2000s–2010s:

**Banerjee, Duflo, Glennerster, Kinnan (2015)** — Hyderabad, India:
```
DESIGN: Random assignment of Spandana microcredit entry into
        half of 104 areas in Hyderabad slums
        15-18 months follow-up, then 3-year endline

RESULTS:
  Borrowing: ↑ 13% took up microfinance (treatment vs. control)

  Business formation: ↑ New (less profitable) businesses started
  Durable goods: ↑ Households bought more durables

  Consumption (average): No significant increase
  Education: No significant effect
  Women's empowerment: No significant effect
  Income: No significant effect for average household

  HETEROGENEITY:
  "Business owners" (already running business) saw significant gains
  Average household: no transformative effect
```

**The six-country synthesis** (Banerjee et al., *American Economic Review: Applied* 2015):
Six simultaneous RCTs across India, Ethiopia, Morocco, Mexico, Bosnia-Herzegovina, Mongolia:
- Consistent: more credit → more self-employment investment; consumption smoothing
- Consistent: no average effect on consumption, income, or women's empowerment
- Some borrowers benefited substantially; most did not
- Conclusion: credit is useful, but not the transformative poverty escape the hype promised

## 5. The Over-Indebtedness Crisis

**Andhra Pradesh, India, 2010**:
```
TIMELINE OF THE AP MICROFINANCE CRISIS:

2005–2010: Explosive growth of MFIs in AP
  SKS Microfinance: millions of borrowers
  Multiple lenders entering same villages
  Field officers competing for borrowers

Problem 1: MULTIPLE BORROWING
  Same household borrows from 3-4 MFIs simultaneously
  Uses loan 2 to repay loan 1 (debt cycling)
  Total debt > realistic repayment capacity

Problem 2: AGGRESSIVE COLLECTION PRACTICES
  Field officers incentivized by repayment rates
  Harassment, public shaming of defaulters
  Several reported suicides linked to collection pressure

October 2010: AP government issues ordinance
  MFIs cannot collect repayments without government approval
  Borrowers told by politicians to stop repaying
  MFI repayments collapse → credit crisis → SKS near-bankruptcy

SYSTEMIC FAILURE:
  Poor regulation: no credit bureaus, no over-indebtedness limits
  Incentive misalignment: field officer bonuses → loan pushing
  Mission drift: growth metrics displaced client welfare
```

## 6. Commercialization and Mission Drift

**Compartamos Banco, Mexico** (IPO 2007):
- Founded as NGO in 1990 with donor funding
- Grew to 840,000 borrowers
- IPO raised $467 million; founders became wealthy
- Interest rates: 86-100% APR (including fees/taxes) at IPO

**SKS Microfinance, India** (IPO 2010):
- Yunus strongly opposed: "a crystal clear picture of loan sharks"
- Argued commercial microfinance abandons the poor for profits
- IPO proceeds went to founders/investors, not to expanding financial access

**The double bottom line dilemma**:
```
SOCIAL MISSION vs. FINANCIAL SUSTAINABILITY:

  NGO microfinance:
    ┌─ Social mission primary
    ├─ Subsidized interest rates
    ├─ Outreach to poorest
    └─ Donor dependent (unsustainable scale)

  Commercial microfinance:
    ┌─ Profitability required
    ├─ Higher interest rates
    ├─ "Mission creep" to less-poor borrowers (easier to manage)
    ├─ Investor returns primary
    └─ Can scale without donors (but social mission at risk)

  MISSION DRIFT EVIDENCE:
  Cull, Demirgüç-Kunt, Morduch (2009): as MFIs commercialize,
  they serve fewer very poor clients; average loan size grows
  (larger loans = less poor clients = easier operations)
```

## 7. What Works Better: Savings and Insurance

**Village Savings and Loan Associations (VSLAs)**:
```
VSLA MODEL:
  15–25 members, self-organized, no external institution
  Weekly meetings: members buy shares ($0.50–2.00 each)
  Accumulated savings lent to members at moderate interest
  Interest accrues to the group (internal return)
  Annual "share out": total pot divided among members

ADVANTAGES:
  No external financial institution needed
  Members own and manage their own savings
  Returns stay within community
  Simpler to understand than liability-tied credit groups
  More robust: no MFI failure risk

EVIDENCE (multiple evaluations):
  VSLAs show stronger effects on savings, asset accumulation,
  and women's economic empowerment than microcredit programs
  Less evidence of over-indebtedness risk
```

**Weather / index insurance**:
- Poor households face covariate risks (drought, flood, pest) that devastate all at once
- Traditional microinsurance: moral hazard, adverse selection
- Index insurance: payouts tied to observable index (rainfall station, satellite vegetation index) not individual loss
- Evidence: take-up low at market prices (basis risk: index may not match actual loss); works well in pilots, struggling at scale

## 8. Mobile Money: M-Pesa

The largest financial inclusion impact comes not from credit but from mobile payments.

**M-Pesa, Kenya (2007)**:
```
M-PESA MECHANISM:
  Customer deposits cash at agent (shopkeeper)
  Agent credits mobile "e-wallet"
  Customer sends e-money to any phone number
  Recipient withdraws cash from any agent

  Safaricom launched 2007; by 2012: 15 million users (70% adults)
  Agent network: 40,000+ (more than all bank branches in sub-Saharan Africa)
```

**Jack and Suri (2011, 2014) — quasi-natural experiment**:
```
RESEARCH DESIGN:
  M-Pesa rollout was staggered across Kenya (agent availability varied)
  Comparison: early access vs. late access areas
  Panel data: 2008 and 2010 household surveys

FINDINGS:
  - M-Pesa reduces transaction costs of sending money (remittances)
  - Households better able to smooth consumption against shocks
  - Better risk-sharing: households with M-Pesa access cope better
    with health or rainfall shocks
  - Suri and Jack (2016 Science): ~22% reduction in extreme poverty
    for rural women over 2008–2014 period (attributing to M-Pesa)
    → 196,000 women moved from farming to business/retail

MECHANISMS:
  1. Internal remittances: urban migrants can send money to rural families
     much more efficiently (M-Pesa vs. bus driver carrying cash)
  2. Risk sharing: ability to call on wider network in emergency
  3. Savings: safe store of value (under-mattress cash has theft risk)
  4. Business: easier payment collection, supplier payments
```

**Global spread**: M-Pesa model spread to Tanzania, Rwanda, Ghana, India, Eastern Europe. Ghana's MTN Mobile Money, Bangladesh's bKash. "Mobile money" now the dominant financial inclusion story — larger impact than 40 years of microfinance development.

## 9. Grameen Model Assessment

**What the Grameen model genuinely achieved**:
- Proved that poor households are creditworthy (repayment rates ~97%)
- Reached scale (9M+ borrowers in Bangladesh)
- Empowered women as financial agents (household bargaining power)
- Built a replicable institutional model (self-selection, group pressure, progressive loans)
- Shifted narrative: poor can be trusted with money

**What it didn't achieve**:
- Mass poverty escape (evidence doesn't support)
- Microenterprise growth (most loan uses: consumption smoothing, inventory for petty trade, not capital investment in new enterprises)
- Women's empowerment beyond financial sphere (RCTs show limited effects on physical mobility, voice)

**The correct framing** (Morduch, Armendariz, Collins et al. *Portfolios of the Poor*):
```
Poor households manage complex financial lives:
  - Multiple income streams (daily labor, petty trade, seasonal farming)
  - Lumpy expenses (school fees, medical emergencies, funerals, weddings)
  - Need financial instruments to SMOOTH consumption and ACCUMULATE for lump sums

Microfinance = useful cash management tool, not poverty escape mechanism
The "poverty trap → credit → escape" model was wrong
Better model: "income variability management → credit/savings/insurance"
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Microfinance ≠ microcredit**: Microfinance includes credit, savings, insurance, money transfers. Microcredit (small loans) was the first dominant product but is not the whole field.

**Grameen repayment rates don't prove impact**: 97% repayment sounds like success. But repayment comes from multiple sources — not necessarily business income. Households borrow from family, other MFIs, moneylenders to repay. High repayment does not imply business creation or poverty reduction.

**The AP crisis was not Grameen**: Grameen's original model involves careful selection, progressive loans, and field officers who know borrowers. The AP crisis involved commercial MFIs with rapid expansion, field officers rewarded on repayment volume, and no cross-MFI coordination. Different institutional incentives.

**The Suri-Jack 22% estimate is contested**: Later work has questioned whether the poverty reduction attributable specifically to M-Pesa is as large as the original estimates. The finding of positive effects on consumption smoothing and risk sharing is robust; the magnitude of the poverty effect is debated.
