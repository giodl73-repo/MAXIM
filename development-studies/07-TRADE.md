# Trade and Industrialization: Export-Led Growth, Industrial Policy, China Model

## The Big Picture

The central debate in development trade policy is whether countries should industrialize behind tariff walls (ISI) or compete in world markets from the start (export-led growth). The East Asian miracle largely settled this empirically in favor of export orientation — but the *mechanism* is contested: was it free markets or industrial policy?

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    TRADE AND INDUSTRIALIZATION LANDSCAPE                 |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                          |
|  THE DEBATE:                                                             |
|  World Bank (1993): East Asia succeeded through markets                 |
|  vs.                                                                     |
|  Amsden (1989), Wade (1990), Chang (2002): State directed               |
|  industrial policy was essential                                         |
|  vs.                                                                     |
|  Rodrik synthesis: industrial policy works when disciplined             |
|  by export performance requirements                                      |
|                                                                          |
|  THEORY FRAMEWORK:                                                       |
|  Ricardo → comparative advantage → free trade always gains              |
|  Hamilton/List → infant industry → temporary protection justified       |
|  Lewis → structural transformation → move labor to manufacturing        |
|  Akamatsu → flying geese → regional technology upgrading chain          |
|  Rodrik trilemma → globalization/democracy/sovereignty tradeoff         |
|                                                                          |
|  THE EVIDENCE:                                                           |
|  Four Tigers: Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong → spectacular         |
|  China 2001–2015: WTO integration → largest export surge in history     |
|  "China shock": US manufacturing job losses (Autor/Dorn/Hanson)         |
|  Continued question: can Vietnam, Bangladesh, Ethiopia repeat it?       |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## 1. Comparative Advantage and Its Limits

**Ricardo (1817)**: Even if one country is absolutely better at everything, both countries gain from specializing in what they have comparative advantage in.

```
RICARDIAN EXAMPLE:

           Labor hours to produce 1 unit:
           Cloth    Wine
  England:   1        2      (England comparatively better at cloth)
  Portugal:  3        4      (Portugal comparatively better at wine)
                              (absolute advantage in both: Portugal, debatable
                               but comparative: England has lower opp. cost in cloth)

FREE TRADE EQUILIBRIUM:
  England specializes in cloth, Portugal in wine
  Both gain: more total production, prices converge internationally

HECKSCHER-OHLIN EXTENSION:
  Countries export goods intensive in their abundant factors
  Labor-abundant → export labor-intensive goods
  Capital-abundant → export capital-intensive goods
  → Poor countries should export labor-intensive manufactures
```

**The infant industry argument** (Hamilton 1791, List 1841):
```
WHY DYNAMIC COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGE MATTERS:

  Today's comparative advantage: cheap unskilled labor
  → export labor-intensive low-tech manufactures
  → stay stuck in low-value activities

  INFANT INDUSTRY LOGIC:
  If you protect industry X while it learns:
    Learning by doing reduces costs over time
    Economies of scale need domestic market to achieve
    Knowledge externalities: innovations benefit entire sector
    → After "growing up," industry can compete internationally

  HAMILTON'S PRESCRIPTION: temporary tariffs + subsidies
  to build infant industries

  LIST'S NATIONALIST ECONOMICS:
  A nation's productive powers (knowledge, skills, institutions)
  matter more than immediate consumption gains from free trade
  Britain used protection for centuries, then preached free trade
  (Chang: "kicking away the ladder")
```

## 2. Import Substitution Industrialization — Revisited

Already covered in 01-HISTORY.md from a historical perspective; the theoretical summary:

**Why ISI failed in most cases**:
```
ISI FAILURE MECHANISMS:

1. No export discipline:
   Protected firm has no international benchmark → inefficiency not punished
   Contrast: Korean chaebol protected but required to meet export targets
   If you fail export test → protection removed → bankruptcy

2. Fiscal unsustainability:
   Subsidies + SOE losses → fiscal deficit → inflation → currency crisis
   Argentina, Brazil 1980s hyperinflation; Mexico 1982 debt crisis

3. Urban bias:
   Cheap food for urban industrial workers → low agricultural prices
   → rural poverty worsens
   → political economy: urban coalitions entrench policy

4. Complexity ceiling:
   Easy to make textiles domestically; very hard to make semiconductors
   ISI could substitute simple goods; failed at capital/tech goods
   Continued dependence on imported capital goods → perpetual trade deficit
```

## 3. The East Asian Miracle

World Bank's *The East Asian Miracle* report (1993) documented the extraordinary growth of eight East Asian economies 1960–1990: Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong, Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand.

**Four Tigers: Key statistics**:

| Economy | GDP per capita 1960 | GDP per capita 1990 | Annual growth |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| South Korea | ~$1,500 (PPP) | ~$12,000 | ~7% |
| Taiwan | ~$1,600 | ~$13,000 | ~7% |
| Singapore | ~$3,000 | ~$24,000 | ~8% |
| Hong Kong | ~$4,300 | ~$22,000 | ~6% |

```
EAST ASIAN GROWTH MODEL:

Phase 1: LABOR-INTENSIVE EXPORTS
  Cheap labor + export processing zones
  Textiles, shoes, basic electronics assembly
  Accumulate capital + skills

Phase 2: TECHNOLOGY UPGRADING
  Move up value chain: steel → ships → chemicals → semiconductors
  State-directed credit to strategic sectors
  Mandatory investment in R&D + worker training

Phase 3: TECHNOLOGY FRONTIER
  Korea: Samsung, DRAM semiconductors, OLED displays
  Taiwan: TSMC (most advanced semiconductor fabrication in world)
  Singapore: financial hub + biomedical manufacturing

WHAT THEY HAD IN COMMON:
  High savings rates (30%+)
  Massive education investment (universal literacy → higher education)
  Export orientation (compete globally, not just domestically)
  Strong states (Weberian bureaucracy, meritocratic technocracy)
  Land reform (Korea, Taiwan) → broad-based agricultural foundation
```

**The Washington Consensus vs. East Asian model tension**:
- WC: liberalize, privatize, deregulate → markets do the work
- East Asia: state-directed credit, industrial policy, managed trade → *then* markets
- The World Bank 1993 report tried to attribute success to market fundamentals; Amsden, Wade, Rodrik argued the causation was the other way — policy managed the market

## 4. The Industrial Policy Debate

**Against industrial policy (orthodox view)**:
```
PROBLEMS WITH INDUSTRIAL POLICY:

Information problem: Government cannot know which sectors will
become competitive → resource misallocation

Political economy: Protected industries form lobbies → protection
becomes permanent regardless of performance ("infant" never grows up)

Rent-seeking: Firms spend resources lobbying for protection
rather than improving products

Competition effects: Protection reduces competition → less innovation

COUNTEREXAMPLE: For every Korea (successful), there are
multiple cases of failed industrial policy
(India steel, Brazil Embraer competitors, Argentina autos)
```

**For industrial policy (heterodox view)**:
```
MARKET FAILURES IN INDUSTRIALIZATION:

Coordination failures:
  Steel mill viable only if there's a car industry to buy steel
  Car industry viable only if there's steel
  Neither invests without the other → underinvestment equilibrium
  State coordinates simultaneous investment

Learning externalities:
  Firm A invests in learning technology X
  Engineers trained by Firm A leave to Firm B
  Firm A cannot capture returns → underinvests in learning
  State subsidy corrects for externality

Information externalities:
  Discovering that Bangladesh can make garments profitably
  is a costly experiment → first mover bears cost, sector benefits
  State insurance for pioneer investors (Hausmann-Rodrik 2003)
```

**Rodrik's synthesis** — industrial policy works when:
```
SUCCESSFUL INDUSTRIAL POLICY REQUIRES:

1. CARROTS + STICKS:
   Protection given only with performance requirements (export targets)
   Support removed if performance not met
   (Korea: chaebol had to meet export benchmarks)

2. COLLABORATION, NOT CAPTURE:
   Business-government dialogue without business capture
   State listens to industry; industry cannot dictate policy

3. BUILT-IN EXIT:
   Industrial policy is transitional
   No permanent protection
   Sunset clauses, automatic review

4. MONITORING + EVALUATION:
   Track whether supported industries are upgrading
   Information gathering to identify what's working

"Self-discovery" process: the state facilitates firms discovering
what the country can be good at — de-risking the exploration
```

## 5. Lewis Dual Sector Model

W. Arthur Lewis (*Economic Development with Unlimited Supplies of Labour*, 1954):

```
LEWIS MODEL:

  TRADITIONAL SECTOR            MODERN SECTOR
  (Agriculture/subsistence)     (Manufacturing/industry)
  ─────────────────────         ─────────────────────
  Surplus labor                 Capitalists invest profits
  (marginal product ≈ 0)        Labor demand grows
  Wages = subsistence wage      Wage = subsistence + premium
        │                               │
        └── workers transfer ──────────►│
                                        │
                              Profits → savings → investment
                              Investment → more labor demand
                              → More transfer from agriculture
                              → Growth

TURNING POINT:
  When surplus labor exhausted from agriculture:
  Wages begin to rise
  Labor cost advantage diminishes
  Economy must compete on productivity, not cheap labor
  (China hit Lewis turning point ~2012–2015)
```

**Structural transformation** (Kuznets): development = movement of labor from low-productivity agriculture to high-productivity manufacturing and services. Countries that have completed this transition are rich; those that haven't are not.

**The problem of premature deindustrialization** (Rodrik): manufacturing's employment share peaks earlier and lower in today's developing countries than it did in today's rich countries. Services don't offer the same productivity and learning benefits. This may make the classic Lewis/Kuznets pathway less available.

## 6. The Flying Geese Model

Kaname Akamatsu (1932, 1962); refined by Ozawa (1990s):

```
FLYING GEESE TECHNOLOGY LADDER:

1940s–50s: Japan produces basic manufactures (textiles, steel)
     ↓ Japan's wages rise; comparative advantage shifts
1960s–70s: Japan → Korea/Taiwan (textiles, steel, ships)
     ↓ Korea/Taiwan wages rise
1970s–80s: Korea/Taiwan → Thailand/Malaysia (labor-intensive assembly)
     ↓ ASEAN wages rise
1980s–90s: Thailand/Malaysia → China, Vietnam, Bangladesh
     ↓ China wages rising
2010s–:    China → Vietnam, Cambodia, Myanmar, Ethiopia?

MECHANISM:
  Lead country (Japan) moves up value chain
  → vacates lower-value activities
  → follower countries absorb vacated industries
  → technological knowledge transfers through supply chains,
    joint ventures, migrating engineers

POLICY IMPLICATION:
  Position yourself to receive the "geese" — attract FDI,
  build infrastructure, educate workers for next-tier industries
```

**China's disruption of the flying geese**: China's scale (1.4B people) allowed it to occupy multiple tiers simultaneously for much longer than the model predicts — not clearing out lower-value activities as fast as the model suggests, crowding out other followers.

## 7. China Shock: Trade Liberalization's Distributional Effects

China's WTO accession (December 2001): immediate reduction in tariffs on Chinese imports worldwide.

**Autor, Dorn, Hanson (2013, 2016)** — "The China Syndrome":
```
RESEARCH DESIGN:
  US commuting zones (local labor markets) vary in exposure to
  Chinese import competition (by which industries they host)
  Instrument: China's export growth to other rich countries
  (controls for US-specific demand shocks)

FINDINGS:
  High-exposure commuting zones (where Chinese import competition
  grew most) saw:
    - Significant manufacturing job losses (not offset by other sectors)
    - Wage declines (especially for workers without college)
    - Increased disability claims, lower labor force participation
    - Higher political polarization (trade and vote share for populist)

MAGNITUDE:
  ~2-2.4 million manufacturing jobs displaced in US 1999–2011
  Greater than standard models predicted (assumed smooth adjustment)

MECHANISM BREAKDOWN:
  Standard model: labor reabsorbed quickly into other sectors
  Reality: workers in affected regions stayed unemployed or
  exited labor force (geographic immobility of displaced workers)
```

**Global welfare vs. local distributional effects**:
- Standard trade theory: China WTO = global welfare gain
- Autor et al.: nationally aggregate gains; locally devastating losses
- Implication: trade liberalization requires active adjustment assistance; economists underestimated transition costs

## 8. Rodrik's Trilemma

Dani Rodrik (*The Globalization Paradox*, 2011):

```
RODRIK'S TRILEMMA:
  You can have at most 2 of these 3:

         HYPER-GLOBALIZATION
        (fully integrated world economy)
         /              \
        /                \
  DEMOCRACY           NATIONAL
  (responsive to      SOVEREIGNTY
   citizen             (ability to
   preferences)        set own policies)
        \                /
         \              /
        (pick any two)

CASE 1: Deep globalization + democracy = world government
  (sacrifice national sovereignty, but global standards enforced democratically)

CASE 2: Deep globalization + national sovereignty = gold standard era
  (governments serve global markets, not domestic constituents)
  (The Bretton Woods "embedded liberalism" alternative)

CASE 3: Democracy + national sovereignty = Bretton Woods era
  (limited globalization with capital controls; domestic policy space)

RODRIK'S PRESCRIPTION: Preserve option 3.
  Some globalization is good; hyper-globalization is not worth the
  loss of democratic policy space.
  Trade agreements should create market access, not lock in
  specific regulatory regimes (intellectual property, investor protection)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Comparative advantage ≠ current comparative advantage is permanent**: Ricardo's model describes static efficiency. Dynamic comparative advantage (infant industry theory) says you should invest in changing your comparative advantage — which has empirical support in East Asian upgrading.

**World Bank 1993 report was politically influenced**: The report initially found large state intervention; the final text softened these findings under US Treasury and internal pressure. The "heterodox" reading (Amsden, Wade) is arguably more accurate to what actually happened in Korea/Taiwan.

**China WTO was not just a trade liberalization**: China's WTO accession required domestic reforms (intellectual property, investment rules, transparency) that changed the Chinese economy beyond tariff reduction. The "China shock" reflects the scale of the integration, not just the policy change.

**"Industrial policy" covers a huge range**: From targeted R&D subsidies (US NIH, DARPA — industrial policy no one calls industrial policy) to full state ownership of industries. The interesting question is not "yes or no" but "what kind, with what governance, and with what performance discipline."
