# Development Studies: Growth, Human Development, Institutions — Landscape

## The Big Picture

Development studies is the field that asks: *why are some countries rich and others poor, and what can be done about it?* It spans economics, political science, sociology, and history — and its answers have changed dramatically over 75 years of theory and practice.

## Measurement: GDP vs. HDI vs. MPI

Three fundamentally different answers to "what is development?"

```
+-------------------+---------------------+----------------------+
|       GDP/GNI     |        HDI          |         MPI          |
+-------------------+---------------------+----------------------+
| What:             | What:               | What:                |
| Market value of   | Geometric mean of   | Headcount × intensity|
| goods & services  | 3 normalized indices| of deprivation across|
| produced          | (health, education, | 10 indicators        |
|                   | income)             |                      |
| Unit: $/person    | Unit: 0–1 index     | Unit: % × avg share  |
|                   |                     | deprived             |
| Misses:           | Misses:             | Misses:              |
| Distribution,     | Distribution within | Subjective wellbeing,|
| unpaid work,      | dimensions, beyond- | voice, capabilities  |
| sustainability,   | income poverty,     | beyond 10 indicators |
| non-market goods  | sustainability      |                      |
+-------------------+---------------------+----------------------+
| Outliers (GDP ↑): | Outliers (HDI ↑):   | Key use:             |
| Oil states (UAE,  | Kerala, Cuba (high  | Identifying who is   |
| Qatar) — high     | HDI despite low GDP)| poor and in what     |
| income, poor      | Gulf states (high   | dimension — targeting|
| human development | GDP, HDI lags)      | policy precisely     |
+-------------------+---------------------+----------------------+
```

**The fundamental insight**: GDP measures income; HDI measures capabilities; MPI identifies who lacks what. They give different rankings and prescribe different policies.

## The Institutional Turn

The most influential empirical move in development economics of the 2000s–2010s: **institutions, not geography or culture, are the proximate cause of income differences.**

```
ACEMOGLU-JOHNSON-ROBINSON CHAIN (AJR 2001, 2002):

Colonial      →  Type of          →  Institutional   →  Long-run
disease          colonization         quality today       income
environment      (extractive vs.      (property rights,   (GDP/capita)
                  settlement)          rule of law)

Instrument: settler mortality (historical)
Outcome:    income today
Channel:    institutions (rule of law, property rights)

Key finding: Doubling institutional quality raises per capita income
             by 5–6× — larger effect than geography alone
```

**North's formal definition**: Institutions = formal rules (laws, constitutions) + informal norms (culture, conventions) + enforcement mechanisms. Not the same as organizations.

## The Gender Dimension

Development policy ignored women for its first four decades. The correction has been partial but substantial:

```
TIMELINE OF GENDER IN DEVELOPMENT:

1970 — Boserup: Women's Role in Economic Development
       Colonialism disrupted African women's farming → systematic marginalization
              ↓
1975 — WID: Women in Development approach
       "Add women and stir" — integrate women into existing programs
              ↓
1990s — GAD: Gender and Development
         Structural critique: transform the institutions, not just include women
              ↓
2000s — Gender mainstreaming + CCTs targeting women
         Evidence: women invest more in children's health/education
              ↓
2010s — Women's economic empowerment as growth strategy
         World Bank: "invest in women, transform economies"
```

**The care economy gap**: Time-use surveys globally show women do 2–10× more unpaid care work than men. This doesn't appear in GDP, isn't compensated, and constrains women's market participation. Development policy that ignores it perpetuates the constraint.

## The SDGs and Planetary Limits

The tension at the heart of 21st-century development:

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
|                    THE TENSION                             |
|                                                           |
|   DEVELOPMENT IMPERATIVE          PLANETARY LIMITS        |
|   "Lift 700M out of extreme  vs.  We are already above    |
|   poverty — requires growth,      several planetary       |
|   energy, industrialization"      boundaries (climate,    |
|                                   biodiversity, P/N)      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
              ↓                              ↓
     SDGs (2015–2030):              Planetary Boundaries
     17 goals, 169 targets          (Rockström 2009):
     "Comprehensiveness"            9 Earth system limits,
     vs. coherence                  4 already transgressed
              ↓                              ↓
         DOUGHNUT ECONOMICS (Raworth):
         Social foundation (SDG floor) + planetary ceiling
         The space between = safe and just space for humanity
```

The **degrowth challenge** (Latouche, Kallis): is steady-state or declining GDP compatible with poverty reduction? The standard assumption — growth is necessary for development — is contested by advocates of post-growth economics.

## Common Confusion Points

**GDP vs. GNI**: GDP measures output *in* the country; GNI measures income *of* residents (including abroad). For remittance-dependent economies (Haiti, Philippines), GNI > GDP.

**Development economics vs. growth economics**: Growth economics studies why aggregate output grows (Solow, endogenous growth). Development economics is normatively concerned with poverty reduction, distribution, capabilities — growth is one means, not the goal.

**Washington Consensus vs. Post-Washington Consensus**: The WC (Williamson 1989) was a list of 10 policy prescriptions for crisis countries in Latin America — not originally meant as a universal development recipe. The Post-WC adds institutional preconditions. Neither is "neoliberalism" in the loose pejorative sense, though both have been labeled that.

**Dependency theory is not Marxism**: It emerged partly from Marxist roots (Frank) but includes structuralists (Prebisch, ECLA) who were not Marxist. The policy implication of ECLA dependency was ISI (still within capitalism), not socialist revolution.

**MDGs vs. SDGs**: MDGs (2000–2015) were 8 goals focused on developing countries, poverty-oriented. SDGs (2015–2030) are 17 goals applying to all countries, much broader. The comprehensiveness of SDGs is both a strength (systemic) and weakness (no clear priority ordering).
