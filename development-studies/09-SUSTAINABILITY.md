# Sustainability and Development Goals: SDGs, Planetary Boundaries, Degrowth

## The Big Picture

The sustainability challenge in development is a genuine structural tension: the standard development model requires growth, growth requires energy and materials, and the planetary system is showing signs of critical stress at current resource use levels — let alone at the levels needed to bring 7 billion people to OECD living standards.

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    SUSTAINABILITY AND DEVELOPMENT LANDSCAPE              |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                          |
|  THE CORE TENSION:                                                       |

|  │ DEVELOPMENT NEEDS   │         │ PLANETARY SYSTEM STATE   │           |
|  │                     │         │                          │           |
|  │ ~700M in extreme    │  CLASH  │ 4 of 9 planetary         │           |
|  │ poverty → growth    │ ───────►│ boundaries already       │           |
```

## 1. Brundtland and the Sustainability Definition

World Commission on Environment and Development, *Our Common Future* (1987), chaired by Gro Harlem Brundtland:

**The canonical definition**:
> "Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

**The three pillars**:
```
SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT TRIANGLE:

         ECONOMIC
         (growth, income,
          jobs, trade)
               │
               │
     ┌─────────┴─────────┐
     │                   │
  SOCIAL               ENVIRONMENTAL
  (equity, health,     (ecosystems, climate,
   education,           biodiversity,
   poverty reduction)   resource stocks)
     │                   │
     └─────────┬─────────┘
               │
       SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT
       lives in the intersection

TENSIONS:
  Economic × Environmental: growth uses resources, produces waste
  Economic × Social: growth can increase inequality
  Social × Environmental: poor communities bear pollution burden;
                           environmental protection costs jobs
```

**What Brundtland got right**: Linked environment and development — not either/or. Made intergenerational equity a development principle.

**What Brundtland left unresolved**: What happens when "needs of the present" and "future capacity" are irreversibly in conflict? The definition allows growth as long as it doesn't compromise the future — but whether this is possible at scale is precisely the question.

## 2. Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) 2000–2015

Adopted at the UN Millennium Summit 2000. Eight goals with targets and indicators, focused on developing countries:

| Goal | Target | 2015 Assessment |
|------|--------|----------------|
| 1. Eradicate extreme poverty and hunger | Halve share in extreme poverty | MET: extreme poverty from 36% to 12% globally (but China drove most of it) |
| 2. Universal primary education | Universal primary schooling | Partially met: enrollment up, quality lagged |
| 3. Gender equality | Equal gender ratio in education | Partially met: primary yes; secondary/tertiary gaps remain |
| 4. Child mortality | Reduce under-5 mortality by 2/3 | Partially met: 53% reduction |
| 5. Maternal health | Reduce maternal mortality by 3/4 | Partially met: 44% reduction |
| 6. HIV/AIDS, malaria, tuberculosis | Halt and reverse spread | Partially met: HIV treatment scaled; malaria deaths fell 60% |
| 7. Environmental sustainability | Various | Largely not met: CO2 emissions up; deforestation continued |
| 8. Global partnership | Trade, debt, technology | Largely not met: Doha Round failed; inadequate climate finance |

**MDG lessons**:
```
WHAT WORKED:
  Clear, simple, measurable targets
  Political mobilization (world leaders committed)
  Focus on health interventions (vaccines, ORS, bed nets)
  Concentrated on poorest countries and most actionable issues

WHAT FAILED:
  Goal 7 (environment) weakest — vague, no binding mechanism
  Goal 8 (global partnership) weakest — no accountability for rich countries
  No mechanism to address inequality within countries
  Country-level averages masked intra-country variation
  China's poverty reduction counted globally but was independent of MDGs
```

## 3. Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 2015–2030

Adopted September 2015 at UN General Assembly. 17 goals, 169 targets, 232 indicators.

```
THE 17 SDGs:

1.  No Poverty                 10. Reduced Inequalities
2.  Zero Hunger                11. Sustainable Cities and Communities
3.  Good Health and Wellbeing  12. Responsible Consumption/Production
4.  Quality Education          13. Climate Action
5.  Gender Equality            14. Life Below Water
6.  Clean Water and Sanitation 15. Life on Land
7.  Affordable Clean Energy    16. Peace, Justice, Strong Institutions
8.  Decent Work/Economic Growth 17. Partnerships for the Goals
9.  Industry, Innovation,
    Infrastructure
```

**SDG architecture**: The SDG framework is deliberately comprehensive and universal — it applies to all countries (not just developing), covers all policy domains, and connects environment to social goals. This is both strength and weakness:

```
SDG STRENGTHS vs. WEAKNESSES:

STRENGTHS:                           WEAKNESSES:
  Universal applicability            169 targets = no priorities
  Systemic framing (all linked)      Unmeasurable targets mixed
  Environment + development          with measurable ones
  integration                        No binding mechanism
  Goal 17: requires global           Country "voluntary national
  means of implementation            reviews" — weak accountability
  Social foundation + ecological     Most goals pull in same
  goals in same framework            direction but some in tension
                                     (e.g., SDG 8 growth + SDG 13
                                     climate + SDG 14 oceans)
```

**SDG tensions**:
- SDG 8 (economic growth, full employment) vs. SDG 13 (climate) vs. SDG 15 (biodiversity)
- SDG 2 (zero hunger — including meat protein) vs. SDG 15 (life on land — land use)
- SDG 3 (health) vs. SDG 12 (consumption) — healthcare is resource-intensive
- SDG 17 (partnership) — requires rich countries to do things (open trade, climate finance) with no enforcement

## 4. Planetary Boundaries Framework

Johan Rockström et al. (*Nature*, 2009), updated by Will Steffen et al. (*Science*, 2015):

**The framework**: Nine Earth system processes have boundaries within which humanity can operate safely. Transgress them and risk non-linear, potentially catastrophic regime shifts.

```
PLANETARY BOUNDARIES (Steffen et al. 2015 update):

  BOUNDARY          CONTROL VARIABLE      CURRENT STATUS
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Climate change    Atmospheric CO2 (ppm) TRANSGRESSED (422 ppm; limit ~350)
                    Radiative forcing      TRANSGRESSED (2.3 W/m²; limit ~1.0)

  Biosphere         Extinction rate        TRANSGRESSED (>100/Myr; limit <10)
  integrity         Functional diversity   TRANSGRESSED

  Land-system       % forested land        TRANSGRESSED
  change            (especially tropics)

  Biogeochemical    P cycle: P to ocean    TRANSGRESSED (22 Tg P/yr; limit ~11)
  flows (P and N)   N cycle: N fixed       TRANSGRESSED (150 Tg N/yr; limit ~62)

  Freshwater use    Global freshwater use  Within boundary (currently)
                    Blue water (rivers)

  Ocean             CO3²- saturation       Approaching boundary
  acidification     (aragonite)

  Atmospheric       Optical depth aerosol  Uncertain
  aerosol loading   (regional variation)

  Stratospheric     Stratospheric O3       Recovering (Montreal Protocol)
  ozone depletion   concentration

  Novel entities    (new: synthetic chem., Unknown, possibly transgressed
  (originally:      plastics, radioactive
  chemical          material)
  pollution)
```

**What "transgressed" means**: Not "civilization ends tomorrow" — it means humanity is operating outside the Holocene stability envelope that allowed civilization to develop. Risk of non-linear tipping points increases with distance from boundary. Transgressing multiple boundaries simultaneously is particularly dangerous (system feedbacks).

**Biodiversity boundary**: The most poorly understood. The Holocene background extinction rate was ~1 per million species per year. Current rate: ~100–1,000 per million. Biodiversity loss has cascading ecosystem effects (pollination, nutrient cycling, disease regulation) that cannot be predicted precisely.

## 5. Doughnut Economics

Kate Raworth (*Doughnut Economics*, 2017):

```
DOUGHNUT ECONOMICS FRAMEWORK:

         ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
         │         ECOLOGICAL CEILING                  │
         │  (Planetary boundaries — 9 dimensions)      │
         │    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐      │
         │    │                                 │       │
         │    │     THE DOUGHNUT               │       │
         │    │   (Safe and just space          │       │
         │    │    for humanity)               │       │
         │    │                                 │       │
         │    │  ┌────────────────────────┐    │       │
         │    │  │  SOCIAL FOUNDATION     │    │       │
         │    │  │  (12 SDG dimensions:   │    │       │
         │    │  │  food, water, health,  │    │       │
         │    │  │  education, income,    │    │       │
         │    │  │  housing, energy,      │    │       │
         │    │  │  voice, equality,      │    │       │
         │    │  │  gender, social equity,│    │       │
         │    │  │  political voice)      │    │       │
         │    │  └────────────────────────┘    │       │
         │    └─────────────────────────────────┘       │
         └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  BELOW social foundation = deprivation (development shortfall)
  ABOVE ecological ceiling = environmental overshoot
  THE DOUGHNUT = the space between: safe and just

RAWORTH'S ARGUMENT:
  Abandon GDP growth as goal
  Ask: is the economy meeting everyone's needs
       within planetary boundaries?
  Redesign economics for this question, not for growth
```

**Amsterdam adopting doughnut**: City of Amsterdam commissioned a doughnut city portrait (2020). Analysis showed Amsterdam "overshooting" in carbon, materials, food, land; "falling short" in income, housing. Used to set priorities — not just GDP growth but directional change.

## 6. The Degrowth Position

Serge Latouche, Giorgos Kallis, Jason Hickel — arguing that steady-state or declining GDP in rich countries is necessary and possible:

```
DEGROWTH ARGUMENT:

1. EVIDENCE OF DECOUPLING FAILURE:
   "Relative decoupling": GDP grows while resource intensity per unit falls
   "Absolute decoupling": total resource/emissions use falls while GDP grows

   Evidence for absolute decoupling: very limited
   Studies of dematerialization find it at best relative
   Rebound effects: efficiency gains → more consumption (Jevons paradox)

   Therefore: cannot grow GDP in rich countries while meeting climate goals

2. WHAT DEGROWTH IS NOT:
   Not recession (uncontrolled GDP decline)
   Not austerity (public spending cuts)
   Not poverty

   IS:
   Deliberate reduction in throughput (resource/energy use)
   Increase in leisure, care, local production
   Redistribution from high-consumption to low-consumption
   Well-being indicators replacing GDP

3. POLITICAL ECONOMY:
   Who owns the economy determines who benefits from growth
   Rich → growth maintains their position
   Poor → growth was supposed to lift them (hasn't fully)
   Degrowth requires redistribution — politically very hard

4. GLOBAL DIMENSION:
   Degrowth advocates position: rich countries degrow
   while poor countries still develop (grow)
   Net global material throughput falls
   Justice question: who decides who gets to grow?
```

**The green growth counter**:
```
GREEN GROWTH POSITION (OECD, World Bank, most governments):

  GDP can decouple from emissions with:
  - Carbon pricing (put price on emissions)
  - Clean energy transition (solar/wind already cheaper)
  - Circular economy (reuse materials)
  - Service economy (less material than manufacturing)
  - Technological innovation (AI efficiency, precision agriculture)

  Evidence:
  Some OECD countries have achieved absolute decoupling of
  emissions from GDP for 2+ decades (Denmark, UK, Germany)
  BUT: consumption-based accounting shows this is often
  offshored (counting production emissions, not consumption)
  AND: even if energy/emissions decoupled, other planetary
  boundaries (biodiversity, land, materials) may not
```

## 7. The Decoupling Evidence

The empirical question: is absolute decoupling of GDP from environmental pressure happening at scale?

```
DECOUPLING EVIDENCE SUMMARY:

ENERGY/EMISSIONS:
  Production-based: yes, in several rich countries
  Consumption-based: less so (imports from China carry embedded emissions)
  Global total: NO — global CO2 still rising despite efficiency gains
  Renewable transition underway but net additions to energy system
  (cheap energy → more consumption, partially offsetting efficiency)

MATERIAL USE (metals, minerals, biomass):
  Relative decoupling: yes (more GDP per ton of material)
  Absolute decoupling: NO evidence at national level for most countries
  (Wiedmann et al. 2015: material footprint rose with income)

BIODIVERSITY:
  Absolute decoupling: no evidence
  Land use still expanding for agriculture
  Even with yield improvements, expansion continues

CONCLUSION (Hickel, Kallis 2020 review):
  Strong evidence for relative decoupling in some dimensions
  Very limited evidence for absolute decoupling across all
  planetary boundaries simultaneously
  Green growth is possible in principle; unproven at required scale
```

## 8. Post-2030 Sustainability Architecture

Where the global development agenda is heading:

```
CURRENT TRAJECTORY:
  SDGs: likely partially met for some goals; very unlikely met globally
  Climate: Paris Agreement (1.5°C target) requires net-zero by 2050;
           current pledges lead to ~2.7°C by 2100
  Biodiversity: Kunming-Montreal (2022): 30×30 goal (protect 30% of
                land and ocean by 2030) — major mobilization needed
  Finance: ~$4.3 trillion/year needed for SDGs in developing countries
           (UNCTAD estimate); currently ~$1 trillion
           → $3 trillion financing gap

REFORM PROPOSALS:
  Debt relief: ~65 countries in debt distress; can't invest in SDGs
               while servicing IMF/bilateral debt → calls for G20 reform
  Carbon border adjustment (EU CBAM): price on imports from
               non-carbon-priced jurisdictions → incentive for climate
  Loss and damage: compensation for poor countries hit by rich-country
               caused climate change (COP27 Warsaw fund operationalized)
  SDG 2.0: reform for post-2030 framework — fewer, clearer priorities
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Planetary boundaries are not absolute cliffs**: Transgressing a boundary increases risk of non-linear change — it doesn't mean catastrophe happens immediately. The concern is that risk accumulates non-linearly with distance from boundary, and some changes may be irreversible.

**SDG 8 (growth) and SDG 13 (climate) are in tension**: The SDG framework doesn't resolve this. Achieving 7% GDP growth for developing countries AND net-zero emissions requires either unprecedented clean energy deployment or decoupling assumptions that aren't proven. The framework presents both as goals; the means are contested.

**Degrowth is not the same as recession**: Recession is uncontrolled GDP decline with unemployment. Degrowth advocates propose deliberate downsizing of high-consumption sectors, compensated by redistribution and expansion of care/leisure. Whether this is politically and economically feasible at scale is genuinely unknown.

**The MDG poverty reduction was largely China**: Extreme poverty globally fell from 36% to 12% 1990–2015. Most of this was China. In Sub-Saharan Africa, absolute numbers in extreme poverty rose even as rates fell (population growth outpaced poverty reduction). MDG 1 was "met" globally but the mechanisms were endogenous to China's growth, not MDG programs.

**Carbon pricing alone is not sufficient**: Economic models suggest carbon pricing is the most efficient climate policy. But it faces: political economy opposition (Yellow Vest protests in France over fuel taxes), distributional concerns (regressive without rebates), and the evidence that even with carbon pricing, complementary policies (standards, R&D, public investment) are needed for the speed required.
