# Aid Effectiveness: Sachs vs. Easterly Debate, RCTs, Conditional Cash Transfers

## The Big Picture

Foreign aid has transferred over $5 trillion from rich to poor countries since World War II. Its effectiveness is vigorously debated. The debate resolves, partly, once you distinguish: aid for what? (emergency relief vs. long-run development), through what mechanism? (budget support vs. targeted projects), evaluated how? (macro correlations vs. RCTs).


## 1. Jeffrey Sachs: The Poverty Trap and Big Push

*The End of Poverty* (2005):

**The poverty trap mechanism**:
```
POVERTY TRAP LOGIC:

Very poor household:
  Income barely covers survival
     ↓
  Cannot save / invest
     ↓
  Cannot accumulate capital (physical or human)
     ↓
  Cannot escape subsistence
     ↓
  Stays poor → children stay poor

THE TRAP IS SELF-REINFORCING:
  Countries in trap: too poor to invest in infrastructure,
  health, education → productivity too low → income too low
  to invest → stay in trap

  UNLIKE: middle-income countries which CAN save and invest
          and eventually self-sustain growth
```

**The big push**: Aid must be large enough and coordinated across sectors to escape the trap. A small injection doesn't work:
```
INSUFFICIENT AID → WASTED:
  Build a road → no trade because lack of health workers
  Health workers → no transport to reach patients
  Education → graduates leave (brain drain) for lack of jobs
  Jobs → blocked by lack of infrastructure

COORDINATED BIG PUSH:
  Infrastructure + health + education + agriculture
  simultaneously → synergies → self-sustaining growth
```

**Millennium Villages Project (MVP)**: Sachs's empirical test. ~500,000 people across 10 African countries. ~$300/person/year in comprehensive aid. Mixed results — external evaluation found modest improvements, but weak causal identification (no control group).

## 2. William Easterly: Planners vs. Searchers

*The White Man's Burden* (2006):

**The planner/searcher distinction**:
```
PLANNERS (top-down)              SEARCHERS (bottom-up)
────────────────────             ────────────────────
Set comprehensive goals          Find what works locally
Design master plans              Experiment incrementally
Allocate resources centrally     Respond to feedback
Accountable to donors            Accountable to beneficiaries
Success = inputs delivered       Success = outputs achieved

EASTERLY'S CLAIM:
  Aid operates as "planner" — donors set goals (end poverty by 2025),
  send resources, face no feedback when they fail
  Recipients have no voice in what they receive
  No mechanism to eliminate what doesn't work
  → Planners waste $2.3 trillion over 50 years (Easterly's figure)
```

**Easterly's alternative**: Support markets, entrepreneurs, local institutions. Remove trade barriers that hurt poor countries. Reduce conditionality. Stop large-scale planning missions.

**The empirical argument**: Cross-country regressions find no robust positive correlation between aid/GDP and growth. Countries that received most aid per capita (Africa) grew least. Countries that grew fastest (East Asia) received little aid relative to GDP.

**Counter-counter**: Rajan and Subramanian (2008) found no effect of aid on growth using instrumental variables. But critics note: impossible to know the counterfactual (what would have happened without aid?).

## 3. Dambisa Moyo: Dead Aid

*Dead Aid* (2009) — strongest anti-aid position:

**Moyo's mechanism**:
```
HOW AID HARMS:

Aid → Government revenue not dependent on taxation
   → No need to be accountable to taxpayers
   → Corrupt governments can stay in power (donor money)
   → Democratic institutions weaken

Aid → Floods local markets with donated goods
   → Destroys domestic production incentives
      (Malaria nets example: African net manufacturers
       undercut by free donated nets → go out of business
       → dependency on future aid for nets)

Aid → Enables government to avoid hard reforms
   → Perpetuates patronage systems

Aid → Creates aid industry (NGOs, consultants)
   → Industry has incentive to perpetuate need
```

**Moyo's prescription**: Poor countries should access capital markets (issue bonds), attract FDI, develop trade. Bond markets impose discipline (must repay) that aid does not.

**Critique of Moyo**: Most aid-receiving countries could not access capital markets even if they wanted to. Bond financing is expensive for poor countries. Her evidence is anecdotal; the macro evidence on aid and institutions is mixed, not uniformly negative.

## 4. The RCT Revolution

Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo (J-PAL, MIT; Nobel Prize 2019) with Michael Kremer (Nobel 2019) introduced randomized controlled trials into development economics:

**Why RCTs?**
```
PROBLEM WITH OBSERVATIONAL EVIDENCE:

  Compare countries with high vs. low aid:
  High-aid countries are poorer → aid hurts growth?
  BUT: Aid is given to poorer countries (selection bias)
  You can't compare apples and oranges.

  SAME FOR INTERVENTIONS:
  Countries that adopt CCTs also do other things.
  Areas that build schools may already have demand for education.
  Omitted variable bias, selection bias, reverse causality.

RCT SOLUTION:
  Randomly assign treatment and control groups
  Treatment = villages/households that receive intervention
  Control = identical villages/households that don't
  Difference in outcomes = causal effect of intervention

VALIDITY LIMITS:
  External validity: does this effect generalize beyond this site?
  Ethical issues: withholding potentially beneficial intervention
  Expensive and slow
  Can't evaluate macro-scale or systemic interventions (can't RCT a trade policy)
```

**J-PAL's evidence synthesis**:

| Intervention | Evidence quality | Effect |
|-------------|-----------------|--------|
| Oral rehydration therapy (ORS) | Strong | Dramatic reduction in child mortality from diarrhea |
| Vaccines (routine immunization) | Strong | Large reduction in child mortality |
| Insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs) | Strong | Reduces malaria incidence 50%+ |
| Conditional cash transfers (CCTs) | Strong | Significant health + education outcomes |
| Deworming children | Contested | Miguel-Kremer 2004: strong effects; Cochrane reanalysis 2016: no effects; ongoing dispute |
| Microfinance (credit) | Moderate | Modest average effects; no transformative poverty escape |
| Job training programs | Weak/mixed | Few rigorous evaluations; mixed results |
| One Laptop Per Child | Negative | Laptops delivered, learning unchanged |
| Community-driven development | Mixed | Depends heavily on design and context |
| Unconditional cash transfers (UCTs) | Emerging strong | GiveDirectly evidence: recipients invest wisely |

## 5. Conditional Cash Transfers (CCTs)

CCTs are the most robustly supported large-scale development intervention:

**Mechanism**:
```
CCT PROGRAM STRUCTURE (Progresa/Oportunidades, Mexico 1997):

Target: Poorest households in rural Mexico
Transfer: Cash (bimonthly) to female household head
Amount: ~20-30% of household consumption
Conditions:
  Children must attend school (>85% attendance)
  Household members must attend health check-ups
  Pregnant women must attend prenatal care

WHY TO WOMAN:
  Evidence from multiple contexts: women spend more on children
  Men more likely to spend on alcohol, tobacco
  Gives women bargaining power within household

SELF-TARGETING:
  Designed so wealthy households wouldn't apply (stigma, administrative hassle)
  Poverty means-tested (consumption/asset proxy means test)
```

**Progresa/Oportunidades evidence** (one of the most-studied programs in development economics):

| Outcome | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| School enrollment | +10-20% (secondary school) |
| Child health (height) | +1-4 cm over program period |
| Maternal health visits | +8-9% |
| Consumption | +13% (transfer + multiplier) |
| Poverty reduction | Significant, largest in rural areas |
| Long-term effects | Participants earn more as adults, have better health outcomes |

**CCT spread**: From Mexico, CCTs spread globally — Brazil Bolsa Família (largest, ~50M beneficiaries), Colombia Familias en Acción, Bangladesh, Philippines, Ghana, Turkey, Indonesia. Most country-specific evaluations show positive effects on education and health.

**Criticisms**:
- Don't address underlying structural causes (land, labor market)
- Administrative burden for poorest households (travel to check-ups)
- Conditions may be unnecessary — UCTs (unconditional cash transfers) show similar effects in some contexts
- Stigma, exclusion of poorest-of-poor who can't comply with conditions

## 6. Aid Modalities

Not all aid is alike:

```
AID MODALITY COMPARISON:

                    Budget      Project      Technical
                    Support     Aid          Assistance
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Goes to:            Government  Specific     Advisors,
                    treasury    project       training

Recipient control:  High        Low          Low

Donor visibility:   Low         High         Medium

Fungibility:        High        Lower        N/A
                    (can shift
                     other spending)

Accountability:     Government  Project mgr  Unclear
                    must account

Best for:           Strong,     Weak,        Specific skill
                    accountable unaccountable gaps
                    governments governments

Risk of:            Corruption, White        Expensive,
                    misuse      elephants    dependency
```

**Paris Declaration (2005) principles**: 109 countries signed:
1. **Ownership**: partner countries lead development strategies
2. **Alignment**: donors align with partner country systems
3. **Harmonization**: donors coordinate, simplify procedures
4. **Results**: focus on development results, not inputs
5. **Mutual accountability**: donors and partners accountable for results

**Implementation gap**: Evaluations (Busan 2011, Paris Monitoring surveys) show compliance is weak. Donors continue parallel systems, tied aid, short-term cycles driven by donor political calendars.

## 7. Tied Aid and Fungibility

**Tied aid**: recipient must spend in donor country (USAID food aid must ship on US-flagged vessels; bilateral aid often requires procurement from donor country firms). Estimated cost: 25-40% of aid value is wasted in tied aid transactions vs. open procurement.

**Fungibility**: if aid covers sector X's budget, government shifts its own funds away from X. Net additional spending on X may be far less than the aid amount.

```
FUNGIBILITY EXAMPLE:

Country budget (pre-aid): health $100M
Donor gives $50M for health sector
Expected: health spending = $150M
Actual: country shifts $40M from health to other budget items
Net new health spending = $10M (not $50M)
Fungibility rate = 80%

Empirical estimates: aid is substantially fungible in practice,
especially for budget support. Project aid (off-budget) less fungible
but creates parallel systems.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer | Source |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Does aid increase growth? | No robust aggregate effect (Rajan-Subramanian) | Macro evidence |
| Do specific interventions work? | Yes — CCTs, vaccines, ORS, bed nets have strong evidence | RCT evidence |
| Sachs or Easterly? | Both partially right — specific interventions work; big push planning has weak evidence | Synthesis |
| What's the best aid modality? | Depends on recipient governance quality; budget support requires accountability | Paris Declaration |
| Why does CCT target women? | Women invest more in children; increases women's bargaining power | Progresa evidence |
| Is unconditional cash better than conditional? | GiveDirectly evidence: UCTs also work; conditions may be unnecessary overhead | UCT RCTs |
| What about aid and corruption? | Mixed evidence; aid to accountable governments more effective | IMF/World Bank research |

## Common Confusion Points

**The Sachs-Easterly debate is not about whether specific interventions work**: Both would agree that vaccines work. Sachs argues for coordinated scale-up across sectors; Easterly argues against centralized planning and top-down goals. The debate is about development strategy, not specific health interventions.

**Deworming controversy is ongoing**: The Miguel-Kremer (2004) paper found enormous spillover effects (externalities from treatment) justifying mass deworming. Cochrane reanalysis (2016) found no significant effects on school attendance or weight. The dispute involves how to analyze cluster trials with spillovers — it's methodological as much as substantive. J-PAL maintains deworming is cost-effective; Cochrane's independent reviewers disagree.

**RCTs don't answer the macro question**: You can RCT whether to distribute bed nets at subsidized prices or for free (Dupas 2014: free is fine, but people treat free items as less valuable — nuanced result). You cannot RCT whether aid to a country increases its long-run growth rate. Different questions requiring different methods.

**Aid is not monolithic**: $10B in emergency food aid after a famine is different from $10B in budget support to the same government for 20 years. Evaluating "aid" as a category is like evaluating "medicine" — some cures, some harms, most depends on diagnosis and dosage.
