# History of Development: Modernization Theory, Dependency, Post-Development

## The Big Picture

The history of development thought is a succession of paradigms, each responding to the failures of its predecessor. Every major school has a geopolitical context — development theory was never politically neutral.

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    DEVELOPMENT PARADIGM TIMELINE                         |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                          |
|  1944–1945  BRETTON WOODS: IMF + World Bank created. "Development"      |
|             = industrialization of war-damaged and colonial economies    |
|                    ↓                                                     |
|  1949       TRUMAN POINT 4: "underdeveloped" coin minted; modernization  |
|             as Cold War project — make poor countries capitalist fast    |
|                    ↓                                                     |
|  1950s–60s  MODERNIZATION THEORY: Rostow's Stages, 1960                 |
|             "A Non-Communist Manifesto" — linear stages model            |
|             Dominant paradigm in US academia + policy                    |
|                    ↓ ECLA / CEPAL challenge                              |
|  1950s–70s  ISI: Import Substitution Industrialization                  |
|             Prebisch, ECLA: terms of trade deterioration,                |
|             industrialize behind tariff walls                            |
|                    ↓ failure: debt, inefficiency, inflation              |
|  1960s–80s  DEPENDENCY THEORY: Frank, Cardoso, Dos Santos               |
|             Core extracts surplus from periphery; underdevelopment is    |
|             produced, not original                                       |
|                    ↓ Wallerstein generalizes to world-systems            |
|  1980s–90s  STRUCTURAL ADJUSTMENT: Washington Consensus (1989)          |
|             IMF + World Bank: 10 prescriptions — fiscal discipline,      |
|             trade liberalization, privatization, deregulation            |
|                    ↓ backlash: lost decade in Africa + Latin America     |
|  1990s–00s  POST-WASHINGTON CONSENSUS: institutions matter              |
|             Stiglitz critique; WB adds governance, social capital        |
|                    ↓ parallel: Sachs/Easterly debate; RCT turn          |
|  2000s–10s  INSTITUTIONAL TURN + CAPABILITIES SYNTHESIS                 |
|             Acemoglu/Robinson, Sen, endogenous growth                    |
|                    ↓                                                     |
|  2010s–     CHINA MODEL + POST-DEVELOPMENT + SUSTAINABILITY             |
|             State capitalism, degrowth, planetary limits debate          |
+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## 1. The Founding Moment: Truman Point 4 (1949)

Harry Truman's inaugural address January 20, 1949, Point 4:

> "We must embark on a bold new program for making the benefits of our scientific advances and industrial progress available for the improvement and growth of underdeveloped areas."

This speech **invented the word "underdeveloped"** as a policy category. Before Truman, there were poor countries, colonial territories, backward regions — but not a universal category called "underdeveloped" awaiting uplift.

```
COLD WAR LOGIC OF DEVELOPMENT:

  Soviet communism ──────────────────┐
  (appeals to peasants/workers)      │
                                     ▼
  Poor country at risk          ┌──────────┐
  of communist insurgency ─────►│ Western  │
  or Soviet alignment           │ develop- │
                                │ ment aid  │
                                └──────────┘
                                     │
                                     ▼
                         Capitalist modernization
                         = bulwark against communism
                         (Korea, Taiwan, Thailand...)
```

**Marshall Plan as template**: The Marshall Plan (1948) rebuilt war-damaged European economies. Policymakers assumed the same model would work for poor countries — capital injection → industrialization → prosperity. The crucial difference (ignored for decades): Europe had existing institutions, educated populations, and was *re*-building, not building from scratch.

## 2. Modernization Theory: Rostow's Stages

W.W. Rostow, *The Stages of Economic Growth: A Non-Communist Manifesto* (1960).

The subtitle was deliberate. Rostow was explicitly offering a capitalist alternative to Marxist historical materialism.

```
ROSTOW'S FIVE STAGES:

Stage 1: TRADITIONAL SOCIETY
  Subsistence agriculture, Newtonian science not applied
  Hierarchical, fatalistic, ceiling on productivity
  "Pre-modern" — almost all countries before ~1750

Stage 2: PRECONDITIONS FOR TAKE-OFF
  Enlightenment ideas + national identity emerge
  Banks, infrastructure investment begins
  External shock (colonialism, trade) disrupts traditional society
  Britain 1700s; others mid-19th century

Stage 3: TAKE-OFF  ← The critical transition
  Investment rate rises to 10%+ of national income
  One or two manufacturing sectors lead
  Agricultural transformation frees labor
  Britain 1783–1802; US 1843–1860; Japan 1878–1900

Stage 4: DRIVE TO MATURITY
  Growth self-sustaining, spreads to all sectors
  Economy demonstrates it can produce anything
  40–60 years after take-off

Stage 5: AGE OF HIGH MASS CONSUMPTION
  Leading sectors shift to consumer durables and services
  Welfare state emerges; income broadly distributed
  US 1920s; Western Europe 1950s–60s
```

**What Rostow got wrong**:
- Stages are not sequential or universal — South Korea compressed them; Sub-Saharan Africa did not follow them
- Ignores power relations: why some countries industrialized first (colonialism, resource extraction from others)
- Capital is not the only bottleneck: institutions, governance, geography matter
- Countries don't modernize in isolation — the world economy creates winners and losers

## 3. ECLA/CEPAL and Import Substitution Industrialization

Raul Prebisch at the UN Economic Commission for Latin America (ECLA/CEPAL, est. 1948) developed the **Prebisch-Singer hypothesis**:

```
TERMS OF TRADE DETERIORATION:

  Primary commodities          Manufactured goods
  (coffee, copper, bananas)    (cars, machinery, chemicals)
        ↓                               ↓
  Prices fall over time        Prices rise over time
  (productivity gains          (wages captured by labor
   passed to consumers)         unions; monopoly power)
        ↓                               ↓
  PERIPHERY earns less ◄───────────────────────────  CORE earns more
  from same export volume                            from same export volume

POLICY IMPLICATION: If you export primary goods, you run a structural
trade deficit. Solution: industrialize behind tariffs (ISI).
```

**ISI in practice**:

| Element | Policy | Countries |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Infant industry protection | High tariffs on manufactured imports | Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, India |
| State-owned enterprises | Government direct production of steel, autos, chemicals | Brazil (Petrobras), India (Steel Authority) |
| Managed exchange rates | Overvalued currency to cheapen capital good imports | Most ISI countries |
| Credit direction | State development banks finance priority sectors | BNDES Brazil, Nacional Financiera Mexico |

**Why ISI failed in most cases**:
- Protected industries never became competitive — no export pressure, no efficiency discipline
- Fiscal cost: subsidies + SOE losses → inflation (Argentina, Brazil hyperinflation 1980s)
- Urban bias: kept agricultural prices low to feed industrial workers → rural poverty
- Exception: South Korea used industrial policy with export discipline — must compete globally or lose support

## 4. Dependency Theory

Frank (1967), Cardoso and Faletto (1979), Dos Santos — Latin American response to Rostow:

```
FRANK'S METROPOLIS-SATELLITE STRUCTURE:

World metropole (US, Western Europe)
    │  extracts surplus via:
    │  - unequal exchange (terms of trade)
    │  - profit repatriation
    │  - technology patents
    │  - finance / debt
    ▼
National metropole (Sao Paulo, Mexico City)
    │  same extraction at national scale
    ▼
Regional metropole (provincial cities)
    │
    ▼
Rural satellite (peasants, miners)

KEY CLAIM: "Development of underdevelopment"
The periphery's poverty is not original — it is PRODUCED
by the same process that enriches the core.
Latin America was fully integrated into capitalism early
(colonial silver, sugar) and that integration made it poor.
```

**Cardoso-Faletto refinement** (*Dependency and Development in Latin America*, 1979):
- "Associated-dependent development" is possible — Brazil under military regime grew significantly within dependency structures
- Agency matters: domestic class coalitions shape how global structures are navigated
- More nuanced than Frank's mechanical surplus extraction

**Dos Santos (1970)** — three historical forms of dependency:
1. Colonial dependency (land and mine exploitation)
2. Industrial-financial dependency (19th–early 20th century)
3. Technological-industrial dependency (post-WWII: MNCs, technology transfer)

**Critique of dependency theory**:
- Overly structural — can't explain variation (Korea vs. Kenya, both periphery)
- Unfalsifiable: success (Korea) explained as exception; failure explained as rule
- Empirically weak: doesn't handle East Asian success, which was deeply integrated in world economy
- Policy implication (autonomous development) was tried (Tanzania under Nyerere) with poor results

## 5. World Systems Theory: Wallerstein

Immanuel Wallerstein (*The Modern World-System*, 1974): generalized dependency to a global historical framework:

```
WALLERSTEIN'S THREE-TIER STRUCTURE:

     CORE                 SEMI-PERIPHERY          PERIPHERY
  (US, W. Europe,         (Brazil, Mexico,        (Sub-Saharan
   Japan)                  S. Korea 1970s,         Africa, S. Asia,
                           China 1990s)             Central America)
  High-wage,              Intermediate:            Low-wage, raw
  capital-intensive,      some industry,           materials, export
  diversified             some primary             crops, cheap labor
  production              goods                    assembly
        └─────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────┘
                               │
                    Single capitalist world-system
                    since ~1450, not multiple national
                    economies interacting

MOBILITY: Semi-periphery can rise or fall.
Korea rose; Argentina fell (core in 1900, semi-periphery now).
```

The semi-periphery is politically key: it buffers core-periphery conflict by providing a middle tier that defends the system's legitimacy.

## 6. The Washington Consensus (1989)

John Williamson coined the term in 1989 to describe what Washington-based institutions (IMF, World Bank, US Treasury) *actually prescribed* to Latin American crisis countries. It was originally descriptive, not prescriptive.

**Williamson's 10 prescriptions**:

| # | Policy | Rationale |
|---|--------|-----------|
| 1 | Fiscal discipline | End deficits → stop inflationary money printing |
| 2 | Reorder public expenditure | Cut subsidies; spend on health/education/infrastructure |
| 3 | Tax reform | Broaden base, lower marginal rates |
| 4 | Liberalize interest rates | Market-determined, positive real rates |
| 5 | Competitive exchange rate | Not overvalued; support exports |
| 6 | Trade liberalization | Reduce tariffs, remove quotas |
| 7 | Liberalize FDI inflows | Remove barriers to foreign investment |
| 8 | Privatize SOEs | Sell state enterprises; reduce fiscal burden |
| 9 | Deregulate | Remove barriers to entry, competition |
| 10 | Secure property rights | Legal foundation for investment |

**Results — the mixed record**:
- Chile: privatization + export diversification worked (partially)
- Latin America "lost decade" (1980s): structural adjustment + debt crisis
- Sub-Saharan Africa (1980s–90s): SAPs required cuts to health/education → social regression
- Russia "shock therapy" 1990s: rapid privatization → oligarchy, not competitive markets
- East Asia grew fastest but did NOT follow WC prescriptions

**The core critique** (Rodrik, Stiglitz): The WC assumed markets work automatically once liberalized. It ignored that markets need institutional foundations. Liberalizing without institutions produces crony capitalism.

## 7. Post-Washington Consensus

Joseph Stiglitz (World Bank Chief Economist 1997–2000):

```
WASHINGTON CONSENSUS vs. POST-WASHINGTON CONSENSUS:

WC                                    PWC
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Get prices right (liberalize)         Get institutions right
Minimize state role                   State has legitimate role
One-size-fits-all prescriptions       Country-specific sequencing
Macro stability = inflation control   Social stability = inclusion
Growth → trickle down                 Distribution = growth precondition
```

Key additions in PWC:
- Financial regulation before financial liberalization
- Education and health are investments, not just expenditures
- Governance and anti-corruption as prerequisites
- Social safety nets to manage adjustment costs

## 8. Post-Development

Arturo Escobar (*Encountering Development*, 1995), Wolfgang Sachs (*Development Dictionary*, 1992):

```
POST-DEVELOPMENT CRITIQUE:

  "Development" discourse
          │
          ├── Constructs the Global South as deficient,
          │   backward, in need of expert guidance
          │
          ├── Legitimizes intervention and control
          │   by Western powers/institutions
          │
          ├── Marginalizes local knowledges and
          │   indigenous practices
          │
          └── Produces the very poverty it claims to solve
                        │
                        ▼
              Escobar: "Invention of the Third World"
              Sachs: Development = colonialism with consent
```

**Policy implication**: "De-link" from development discourse; support local alternatives (*buen vivir*, solidarity economy).

**Critique of post-development**: Romanticizes pre-modern conditions; denies people the choice of development benefits (clean water, vaccines, education). People in "underdeveloped" countries often want development — post-development theorists speak *for* them, not *with* them.

## 9. The China Model

China's post-1978 trajectory poses the most serious empirical challenge to Washington Consensus prescriptions:

```
CHINA'S PATH 1978–2020:

1978:  Deng Xiaoping reforms: household responsibility system,
       agricultural de-collectivization
         ↓
1980s: Special Economic Zones (SEZs) — Shenzhen, Guangzhou
       TVEs (Township-Village Enterprises) — quasi-private
         ↓
1990s: Coastal manufacturing export boom
       State-led heavy industry investment
         ↓
2001:  WTO accession → manufacturing export surge
         ↓
2000s–10s: Industrial policy in steel, solar, semiconductors, EVs
           Belt and Road Initiative (external development financing)
         ↓
RESULT: ~800 million lifted from extreme poverty 1978–2015
        Largest poverty reduction in human history

WHAT CHINA DID NOT DO:
  × Follow WC sequencing
  × Fully privatize (SOEs remain dominant)
  × Liberalize capital account
  × Establish independent rule of law
```

**Interpretations of the China model**:

| Position | Claim | Proponent |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| Industrial policy vindicated | State direction worked | Rodrik, Mazzucato |
| Succeeded despite institutions | Scale + authoritarian capacity not reproducible | Acemoglu critics |
| Not transferable | Requires CCP, scale, specific history | Most mainstream economists |
| Now in trouble | Middle-income trap, demographics, debt | IMF, many economists |

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Paradigm | Core claim | Policy implication | Key empirical failure |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| Modernization (Rostow) | Linear stages; capital → take-off | Foreign aid, technology transfer | Can't explain why stages don't follow |
| ISI (Prebisch/ECLA) | Terms of trade deteriorate → industrialize | Import protection, SOEs | Protected industries uncompetitive; fiscal unsustainable |
| Dependency (Frank) | Core extracts surplus from periphery | De-link from world economy | Can't explain East Asian success |
| World Systems (Wallerstein) | One world-system since 1450 | Structural transformation | Too macro for actionable policy |
| Washington Consensus | Liberalize; markets work if unimpeded | Privatize, deregulate, fiscal discipline | Social costs; ignored institutions |
| Post-Washington Consensus | Institutions precede markets | Governance reform | Which institutions? How to build? |
| Post-Development | Development = power discourse | Local alternatives | Romanticizes poverty |
| China Model | State-led industrial policy + export discipline | SEZs, industrial policy | Reproducibility; authoritarianism required |

## Common Confusion Points

**Prebisch-Singer evidence is mixed**: The long-run trend of primary commodity prices falling relative to manufactured goods holds for some commodities (coffee, cotton) over some periods — not universally. Oil reversed the hypothesis for decades. Rare earths now favor the periphery.

**Washington Consensus ≠ structural adjustment programs**: Williamson's list was 10 policy norms for Latin American crisis management. SAPs attached to IMF loans often went further, requiring social spending cuts Williamson explicitly opposed in his Prescription 2.

**Cardoso later implemented market reforms as President**: Brazil's Fernando Henrique Cardoso (1995–2003), one of the dependency theorists, implemented privatization and fiscal stabilization (Real Plan). He argued his academic work was never about rejecting capitalism, only understanding how it operated in Latin America.

**"Post-development" is academic, not policy**: No government has adopted post-development as policy framework. It remains influential in academic anthropology, NGO critique, and social movement discourse. Buen vivir was incorporated into Ecuador's and Bolivia's constitutions as rhetorical framing; actual policy remained growth-oriented.
