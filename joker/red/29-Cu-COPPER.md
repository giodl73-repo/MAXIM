## The Puzzle

**Type:** Element Identification -- identify 7 elements from material-property riddles
**References:** metalworking/, ceramics/, glassmaking/, pigments/, jewelry/, coatings/

Seven elements hide below. Each riddle describes an element through its role in Material Culture -- how craftsmen have shaped, fired, coated, and colored with it across millennia. Name each element. Take the first letter of each name. Read them in order.

Pay attention. Some riddles refer to each other.

### Riddle 1

I am the element that makes steel possible. Not by being the metal -- that distinction belongs to the answer to Riddle 5 -- but by dissolving into it at concentrations below two percent. At 0.77% of me, a smith reaches the eutectoid composition: cool it slowly from austenite and you get pearlite, alternating lamellae of ferrite and cementite. Quench it fast and you trap me in a body-centered tetragonal lattice -- martensite, hard and brittle, waiting for the temper.

But my oldest role in material culture predates metallurgy by ninety millennia. At Blombos Cave, craftsmen ground me into workshop tools -- not as charcoal for drawing, but as the basis of the earliest known pigment preparation. I am the element whose oxide, in the hands of a ceramicist, produces the richest blacks at high concentration in an iron-bearing glaze.

I am also consumed in the process that freed the answer to Riddle 2 from its oxide prison. In that electrolytic cell, I serve as the anode -- oxidized to CO&#x2082; as the target metal plates out at the cathode. Approximately 400 kilograms of me are sacrificed for every tonne produced.

### Riddle 2

Before 1886, I was rarer at the jeweler's bench than the answer to Riddle 7. The Washington Monument wears a 2.85 kg cap of me -- placed in 1884 as a prestige material, when I cost more per ounce than any precious metal. Two twenty-two-year-olds, working independently on opposite sides of the Atlantic, dissolved my oxide in molten cryolite and ran a current through it. The price dropped ninety-nine percent.

Today I am the most die-cast metal in the world. A die casting cycle injects me at approximately 350 degrees Celsius into a hardened steel mold at pressures up to 700 megapascals. I cannot be smelted by the blast furnace method that works for the answer to Riddle 5 -- the bond between me and oxygen is too strong for a carbon reductant at any practical temperature.

In a surface-finishing shop, I am the metal most commonly anodized. The process converts my own surface into a porous oxide layer -- not depositing a foreign metal, but transforming what is already there. Type III hard anodize pushes hardness to 400-600 HV, harder than most steels, at the cost of reduced fatigue life from tensile residual stress in the oxide.

### Riddle 3

Sterling is my most familiar alloy: 92.5 percent me, 7.5 percent copper. I tarnish -- sulfur compounds in the air darken me to black silver sulfide -- but a germanium addition (Argentium, 935 fineness) largely defeats this reaction.

In the electrorefining of copper, I am part of the reward. When impure copper anodes dissolve into the electrolyte, I do not dissolve with them. I settle to the bottom of the tank as anode slime, alongside gold and the platinum group metals. The slime can be worth more than the copper it accompanies.

I am not used as a ceramic colorant. In the glassmaker's furnace, I play no structural role. My domain is the jeweler's bench and the electrical contact -- but even there, I am softer than the answer to Riddle 7's chief competitor in hardness. On the Mohs scale, both of us sit near 2.5 in our pure form.

### Riddle 4

I am the element whose oxide makes a glaze opaque. In the ceramicist's colorant table, my dioxide (SnO&#x2082;) is the traditional white opacifier -- the one that gives tin-glazed earthenware its name. Islamic potters of the ninth century used me to create a white background for painted decoration, a technique that spread through North Africa and Spain to become majolica, faience, and Delftware.

I hold a nuclear distinction: I possess more stable isotopes than any other element -- ten. This is a consequence of my atomic number, Z=50, being a magic number in nuclear physics, conferring extra stability on my nucleus through closed proton shells.

Together with the answer to Riddle 5, I form one of the oldest known alloys. Add me to copper and you get bronze -- harder than either parent metal, the material that named an age. Together with the answer to Riddle 6, I form a different alloy: one that resists corrosion well enough to coat steel food containers. You have eaten from my alloys and drunk from my glazes without knowing my name.

### Riddle 5

I am the element of the blast furnace and the Bessemer converter. My ore -- hematite, Fe&#x2082;O&#x2083; -- reduces in a countercurrent tower where coke burns at two thousand degrees and carbon monoxide rises to strip my oxygen away. The product is pig iron: ninety-four percent me and four and a half percent the answer to Riddle 1.

In a ceramicist's kiln, my oxide is the most versatile colorant of all. Under oxidation firing, I produce tans, ambers, browns, the red-orange of terracotta. Under reduction firing, the same oxide shifts to celadon greens, blue-greens, and tenmoku blacks. No other colorant covers a wider spectrum from a single oxide, and no other is as sensitive to kiln atmosphere.

I am the only element in this puzzle whose alloys can be heat treated by the quench-and-temper cycle that exploits an allotropic transformation at 912 degrees Celsius. Above that temperature, my crystal structure is FCC (austenite). Below it, BCC (ferrite). How fast you cool through that boundary determines whether you get soft pearlite or hard martensite -- the entire logic of the heat-treatment module.

### Riddle 6

I am the element that survives where the answer to Riddle 5 cannot. At temperatures above one thousand degrees Celsius, in the corrosive exhaust of a gas turbine, my superalloys -- Inconel 718, Hastelloy -- hold their shape while lesser metals creep and oxidize. My secret is a coherent gamma-prime precipitate phase (Ni&#x2083;Al) that pins dislocations and resists softening at temperatures where steels would have long since yielded.

In the electroplating shop, I serve two masters. Decorative plating lays down a bright layer of me beneath a thin flash of chromium -- the classic automotive trim stack. Engineering applications use my electroless variant instead: an autocatalytic bath deposits me uniformly without current, incorporating 2-14 percent phosphorus into a Ni-P alloy whose properties depend entirely on that phosphorus content. High-phosphorus electroless deposits are amorphous, non-magnetic, and extraordinarily corrosion-resistant.

I am NOT the element whose oxide makes cobalt blue. That color comes from Co&#xB2;&#x207A; in a tetrahedral spinel. But my name is close enough to cobalt's that careless readers confuse us. We sit beside each other in the periodic table -- consecutive atomic numbers, 27 and 28 -- yet our roles in material culture barely overlap.

**Element:** __ __ __ __ __ __

### Riddle 7

I am the element you can hammer thin enough to transmit light -- leaf so fine that 10,000 sheets stacked measure one centimeter. I do not oxidize, do not tarnish, do not corrode. Sulfur, which blackens the answer to Riddle 3, leaves me untouched.

In electronics, I plate contact surfaces at thicknesses as low as 0.05 micrometers -- just enough to prevent oxidation at the junction. For durability, platers alloy me with cobalt or with the answer to Riddle 6, raising my hardness from 60 to 200-300 HV. The result is called hard gold, and it endures millions of contact cycles without degrading.

The jeweler measures my purity in karats -- a system unrelated to the carat that weighs gemstones, despite sharing an etymology. At 24k I am pure but too soft for daily wear; the Mohs scale reads approximately 2.5, the same as the answer to Riddle 3. Standard fine jewelry alloys me at 18k (750 parts per thousand) or 14k (585 parts per thousand). Add copper and I turn rose. Add the answer to Riddle 3 and copper in balance and I stay yellow. Add palladium and I go white.
