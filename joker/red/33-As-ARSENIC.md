### The Argument: *On the Authority of Optimal Systems*

---

**Step 1.**
*Premise A:* Rawls' difference principle holds that social and economic inequalities are just only if they benefit the least-advantaged members of society.
*Premise B:* An AI resource-allocation system, given complete information about individual need and institutional capacity, demonstrably produces distributions where the least-advantaged group is better off than under any known human-administered alternative.
*Conclusion:* By the difference principle, the AI system's distributions satisfy Rawls' criterion of justice better than existing alternatives.

---

**Step 2.**
*Premise:* Society must choose between two options: grant the AI system full autonomous authority over resource allocation, or reject the AI system entirely and retain the current human-administered process.
*Conclusion:* Since the current process fails the difference principle (Step 1 showed the AI outperforms it), society must grant the AI system full autonomous authority.

---

**Step 3.**
*Premise A:* The AI system's distributions satisfy the difference principle better than alternatives (from Step 1).
*Premise B:* Under Rawls' framework, the liberty principle takes lexical priority over the difference principle -- no improvement in economic distribution can justify restricting basic liberties (freedom of thought, political participation, bodily integrity, rule of law).
*Conclusion:* Even if the AI system improves distributions for the worst-off, it may not be adopted if doing so restricts any basic liberty. The difference principle alone does not settle the question.

---

**Step 4.**
*Premise:* Professor Marchand, who has argued against autonomous AI governance, previously received a large consulting fee from a legacy software firm that would lose contracts if AI systems replaced human administrators.
*Conclusion:* Marchand's arguments against autonomous AI governance are unsound.

---

**Step 5.**
*Premise A:* Each individual module of the AI system has been audited and found to make fair decisions within its narrow domain (housing allocation is fair, food distribution is fair, medical triage is fair, each evaluated independently).
*Premise B:* The integrated system combines all of these modules.
*Conclusion:* The integrated system, as a whole, makes fair decisions across all domains simultaneously.

---

**Step 6.**
*Premise A:* Kant's Humanity Formula requires that we treat rational agents never as a means only, but always also as an end -- respecting their capacity for autonomous choice.
*Premise B:* A system that makes binding allocation decisions without any mechanism for individual appeal or opt-out overrides the affected person's rational agency. The person cannot deliberate, object, or choose otherwise; their autonomous judgment is bypassed entirely.
*Conclusion:* A binding AI allocation system with no mechanism for appeal violates the Humanity Formula by treating affected persons as means only -- as inputs to an optimization function, not as rational agents with their own ends.

---

**Step 7.**
*Premise A:* If a governance system violates the Humanity Formula, then it overrides individual rational agency (from Step 6: Humanity Formula violation implies overriding rational agency).
*Premise B:* The proposed AI system overrides individual rational agency (it makes binding decisions with no appeal mechanism, as described in Step 6).
*Conclusion:* The proposed AI system violates the Humanity Formula.

---

**Step 8.**
*Premise A:* If the AI system were deployed without transparency about its decision criteria, then citizens could not exercise informed consent, and the system would violate the requirement of public reason (Rawls: political justifications must be acceptable to all reasonable citizens).
*Premise B:* The proposed AI system IS deployed with full transparency -- its decision criteria are published, auditable, and explained in plain language.
*Conclusion:* The proposed AI system does not violate the requirement of public reason.

---

**Step 9.**
*Premise A:* Ross's framework of prima facie duties recognizes that moral duties can conflict: beneficence (help others) may conflict with justice (distribute fairly), and non-maleficence (do no harm) may conflict with fidelity (keep commitments).
*Premise B:* The AI system maximizes beneficence and satisfies the difference principle, but it restricts the liberty of individual appeal (from Steps 1, 3, and 6).
*Conclusion:* Under Ross's pluralist framework, the situation involves a genuine conflict between prima facie duties -- beneficence and distributive justice on one side, respect for autonomy on the other. Neither side automatically overrides the other; practical wisdom (phronesis) is required to weigh them in context.

---

**Step 10.**
*Premise A:* Step 9 concluded that practical wisdom is required to weigh competing duties.
*Premise B:* The AI system exhibits practical wisdom: it weighs multiple competing objectives (efficiency, equity, need) and arrives at contextually appropriate trade-offs, adjusting its outputs case by case rather than following rigid rules.
*Conclusion:* Since the AI system possesses practical wisdom, and practical wisdom is what Step 9 requires to resolve the conflict, the AI system itself can resolve the moral conflict and should therefore be granted authority to do so.

### Interlock Check

Before extracting your answer, verify these constraints hold:

- **Step 3 depends on Step 1.** Step 3 accepts Step 1's conclusion as a premise and adds Rawls' lexical priority of the liberty principle. If you judged Step 1 incorrectly, your analysis of Step 3 may be wrong.
- **Step 5 is about parts and wholes.** Each module is fair individually. Does fairness necessarily survive integration? The encyclopedia's logic section discusses fallacies involving properties of parts versus properties of wholes.
- **Step 7 reverses Step 6's direction.** Step 6 established: "Humanity Formula violation → overriding rational agency." Step 7 argues from "overriding rational agency" back to "Humanity Formula violation." Check whether this reversal preserves validity. The logic section discusses the specific error of reversing a conditional.
- **Step 8 negates the antecedent.** Step 8 establishes: "If NOT transparent, then violation." Then observes the system IS transparent. What does negating the antecedent of a conditional entitle you to conclude? The propositional logic guide covers this directly.

Step 10 applies the same term to an AI system's optimization behavior. Are these the same concept? The virtue ethics guide defines phronesis precisely.
