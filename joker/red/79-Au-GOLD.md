### Exchange I

> Allow me to put a question to you which has a certain theoretical interest for me, but which I cannot answer myself. Is it possible to establish a one-to-one correspondence between the collection of positive integers and the collection of positive real numbers, so that to each individual of one collection corresponds one and only one individual of the other?

The sender would go on to prove that no such correspondence exists -- launching a new branch of mathematics that his contemporaries called a disease from which the discipline would one day recover. The addressee, who worked on the foundations of the real number line, initially dismissed the question. Within weeks, the sender had his proof.

### Exchange II

> I have been now ever since my return engaged in a very presumptuous work, and I know no one individual who would not say a very foolish one. I was so struck with the distribution of the organisms of the Galapagos and with the character of the American fossil mammals, that I determined to collect blindly every sort of fact which could bear any way on what are species. At last gleams of light have come, and I am almost convinced (quite contrary to the opinion I started with) that species are not (it is like confessing a murder) immutable.

The sender wrote this in 1844, fifteen years before publishing the theory sketched here. The addressee was a botanist -- the first person outside the sender's family to learn of this dangerous idea. The sender had also written to a second naturalist: "I can plainly see that we have thought much alike and to a certain extent have come to similar conclusions." That second man would independently arrive at the same theory, forcing both into joint publication.

### Exchange III

> I find myself in complete agreement with you on all essential points, particularly on the value of a Begriffsschrift. Your reasons for the introduction of a function into logic I regard as fully justified. There is just one point where I have encountered a difficulty. You state that a function can also constitute the indefinite element. This I formerly believed, but now this view seems doubtful to me because of the following contradiction: Let w be the predicate of being a predicate that cannot be predicated of itself. Can w be predicated of itself? From either answer follows its contradictory.

The sender wrote this in 1902. The addressee had spent his career building a formal system meant to ground all of arithmetic in pure logic. This letter destroyed it. The addressee replied within a week: "Your discovery of the contradiction has surprised me beyond words and, I should almost like to say, left me thunderstruck." The second volume of his masterwork was already in press. He added an appendix acknowledging the fatal flaw.

### Exchange IV

> I feel that all my deepest ideas have been very greatly and favourably influenced by the talks I have had with you, more than with anyone else.

The sender had been a frequent visitor to the addressee's institute in Copenhagen throughout the late 1920s. By 1933, when this was written, the sender had already predicted the existence of antimatter and unified quantum mechanics with special relativity in a single equation. He was famously laconic -- the addressee once called him "the strangest man" who ever visited the institute. The addressee himself had proposed the model of the atom that first quantized the electron's orbit.

### Exchange V

> These are the two great principles on which I ground all my reasoning: the principle of contradiction, by virtue of which we judge false that which involves a contradiction, and true that which is opposed to or contradicts the false; and the principle of sufficient reason, by virtue of which we consider that no fact can be found to be true or existent, no statement correct, without there being a sufficient reason why it is thus and not otherwise, although most often these reasons cannot be known to us.

The sender wrote this in 1715 to a theologian who served as philosophical proxy for a rival. The real debate was with the rival -- over whether space is absolute (a container that exists independent of its contents) or relational (defined only by the positions of bodies). Both the sender and the rival independently invented the calculus. The sender's notation -- dy/dx, the integral sign -- is what the world still uses. The priority dispute between them poisoned an entire century of mathematics.

### Exchange VI

> I am greatly amazed to see how much your memory has deceived you. Particularly with regard to a number of the points which were discussed at our meeting, where the conversations to a great extent concerned precisely the same questions that have since been the object of your publications. I also remember quite clearly our conversation in my room at the Institute, where in vague terms you spoke in a manner that could only give me the firm impression that, under your leadership, everything was being done in Germany to develop atomic weapons.

The sender won the Nobel Prize for his model of the atom and later introduced the complementarity principle. He drafted this letter after reading an account of their 1941 wartime meeting that he believed gravely distorted what had occurred. The addressee had visited the sender's occupied city during the war. This letter was found in the sender's papers after his death and released in 2002. It was never sent.

### Exchange VII

> I am working very hard for you; like the Devil in fact; (which perhaps I am). I want to put in something about Bernoulli's Numbers, in one of my Notes, as an example of how an implicit function may be worked out by the engine, without having been worked out by human head and hands first.

The sender wrote this in July 1843 to the designer of a machine that would not be built in either of their lifetimes. The "Notes" -- appended to a translation of an Italian engineer's description of the machine -- contained what is now recognized as the first published algorithm. The machine was to be programmed with punched cards borrowed from the Jacquard loom.

### Exchange VIII

> Quantum mechanics is certainly imposing. But an inner voice tells me that it is not yet the real thing. The theory says a lot, but does not really bring us any closer to the secret of the Old One. I, at any rate, am convinced that He is not playing at dice.

The sender wrote this in December 1926. The addressee had just proposed the statistical interpretation of the quantum wave function -- the radical idea that physics can predict only probabilities, never certainties. The sender could not accept this. The addressee would win the Nobel Prize for the very interpretation the sender spent his remaining decades rejecting. Their correspondence ran from 1916 to 1955.
