### Reading the Score

Six measures. Six sustained whole notes. Three chromatic grace notes woven between them (the Composer's "chrome" -- fitting, for Chromium).

The grace notes are accented passing tones -- they flash and vanish. They do NOT contribute to the answer. Only the six whole notes matter.

To identify each note, you need two things from the encyclopedia:

1. **Which clef is active** -- treble and bass clef assign different note names to the same staff positions. The Musical Notation reference lists the line and space names for both clefs. Measures 1-3 use treble clef. Measures 4-6 use bass clef. If you read the bass clef notes as though the treble clef were still active, you will get the wrong letters.

2. **Lines, spaces, and ledger lines** -- most notes sit on the five lines or in the four spaces of the staff. One note sits on a ledger line below the treble staff. The Musical Notation reference explains ledger lines and identifies which pitch sits on the first ledger line below treble clef.

The key signature (one flat: B&#9837;) affects which pitches are played, but it does NOT change the letter names. B&#9837; is still called B. The grace note in measure 3 carries a natural sign (&#9838;) that cancels the flat -- but the letter name is still B. Accidentals change the pitch, not the alphabet.

### Verification (for the musically literate)

If you read notes, you may notice a pattern in the sustained tones. The first three notes in treble clef form the tonic triad of the key -- root, third, fifth of the key implied by the key signature. That is not an accident. If your first three letters do not spell a common major chord, re-read the clef.
