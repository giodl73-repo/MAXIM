### The Traits

Nine branching events. Nine innovations. Each description below corresponds to one of the nine numbered nodes. The descriptions are shuffled -- they are not in node order.

Your task: match each description to the correct node on the tree by understanding the evolutionary sequence. The evolutionary biology section of the encyclopedia will tell you what defines each clade.

---

**Trait A.** The modification of the first and second pharyngeal arches into a hinged upper and lower structure capable of grasping prey. This innovation opened predatory niches previously inaccessible to filter-feeding ancestors. It is one of the most consequential morphological transitions in vertebrate history.

**Trait B.** A fluid-filled set of extraembryonic membranes -- the amnion, chorion, and allantois -- that enclose the developing embryo and permit reproduction on dry land without returning to water. The egg no longer requires an aquatic environment.

**Trait C.** The replacement of a cartilaginous endoskeleton with one mineralized by hydroxyapatite -- calcium phosphate crystals deposited in a collagen matrix. Dermal bones and endochondral ossification appear for the first time, producing a rigid internal framework far harder than cartilage.

**Trait D.** A single opening in the temporal region of the skull, behind the orbit, bounded by the postorbital and squamosal bones. This fenestra provides an attachment site for jaw muscles and characterizes the lineage that eventually gave rise to mammals.

**Trait E.** Two openings in the temporal region of the skull behind the orbit -- an upper and a lower fenestra. This arrangement accommodates expanded jaw adductor muscles and is the ancestral condition for the lineage containing all living reptiles and birds.

**Trait F.** A segmented column of vertebrae -- initially cartilaginous, later bony -- surrounding and protecting the dorsal nerve cord. This defines the most inclusive clade on the tree and separates these organisms from their invertebrate chordate relatives.

**Trait G.** An additional opening in the skull between the orbit and the external naris (the antorbital fenestra). This lightened the skull and is associated with the lineage that radiated into the dominant large-bodied terrestrial animals of the Mesozoic, including the ancestors of modern birds and crocodylians.

**Trait H.** Paired appendages transformed from fins into weight-bearing limbs with distinct digits. The transition from aquatic to terrestrial locomotion required modifications to the shoulder and hip girdles, and the evolution of a distinct wrist and ankle.

**Trait I.** Paired fins supported by a fleshy, muscular lobe containing an internal bony axis -- a single proximal element articulating with two distal elements, homologous to the humerus-radius-ulna pattern of the tetrapod limb. These fins contrast sharply with the ray-supported, fan-shaped fins of the other major bony fish lineage.
