### II. The Dramatist

When this painter arrived in Rome around 1592, he brought a new
technology of darkness. His method placed figures against near-total
black, illuminated by a single raking light source as if a window
had been cut into one wall of a sealed room. The technique has a
name derived from the Italian for "dark." Art historians use it
to describe any painting where deep shadow dominates and figures
emerge from blackness. It is NOT the same as chiaroscuro -- it is
the extreme form.

### III. The Athlete

The weight shifts to one leg. The opposite hip drops. The shoulders
counter-rotate. The body is at rest but carries the potential for
movement -- a coiled spring disguised as stillness. The ancient
Greeks discovered it. Donatello revived it. Michelangelo made it
heroic at 5.17 meters of Carrara marble. The Italian term describes
this weight-bearing pose that turns a rigid figure into a living one.

### IV. The Partisan

Florence said drawing was the father of all arts. Venice said color
was the art itself. One camp believed the intellectual act of line
preceded everything. The other believed you discovered the painting
in the act of making it, brush loaded with pigment, thinking with
your hands. The Florentine term for their side of this debate is the
Italian word for "drawing" -- but it meant far more than draftsmanship.
It meant the conceptual design that precedes all execution.

### VI. The Codifier

He was not the one who proved it with a mirror and a pinhole.
He was the one who wrote the book. *Della Pittura* (1435) gave
painters a construction method: establish the ground line, choose
the horizon, place the vanishing point, draw the orthogonals,
compute the transversals. He called the painting a *finestra
aperta* -- an open window. He also insisted that the painter
must know geometry, mathematics, and history.

### VII. The Plasterer

Before oil, before canvas, this was how walls were painted. Pigment
ground in water was applied directly to freshly laid lime plaster --
the *intonaco* -- while it was still wet. As the plaster dried and
carbonated, the pigment bonded permanently into the wall surface.
The artist worked in sections called *giornate* (a day's work per
section), because once the plaster dried, no more paint could bind.
Michelangelo used this technique on the Sistine ceiling. Leonardo,
characteristically, did NOT use it for the Last Supper -- he wanted
to revise, so he used the dry-wall variant, and it began to
deteriorate within decades.

### VIII. The Smoker

His term comes from the Italian word for smoke. He dissolved the
hard contour lines that bounded every figure in Florentine painting
and replaced them with gradual tonal transitions -- form emerging
from shadow rather than being enclosed by line. Under microscopy,
the Mona Lisa shows fingerprints where he blended the translucent
oil glazes by hand. The faces in his paintings do not end; they
fade.

### IX. The Colorist

He was Venice's answer to Florence's cult of drawing. While
Michelangelo built form through line and contour, this painter
built form through color itself -- hue, saturation, and temperature
doing the structural work that line did for the Florentines. His
career spanned nearly the entire 16th century. He is one of the
two poles in the *disegno* vs *colorito* debate: the champion of
color as thought, not decoration.

### X. The Giant-Killer

It stands in the Galleria dell'Accademia in Florence: 5.17 meters of
marble, carved from a single block that two previous sculptors had
already attempted and abandoned. The figure turns his head to the
right, sling over his left shoulder, body coiled in the moment before
action -- not after victory but before the throw. Heroic scale.
Psychological concentration. The civic symbol of a republic that
believed it could defeat larger powers.

### XI. The Pioneer

He carved the first free-standing nude bronze since antiquity --
an adolescent figure with one foot on a severed head, classical
contrapposto rendered almost languid. But his late work went
somewhere entirely different: a polychrome wood carving of
Mary Magdalene, emaciated, hollowed out, centuries ahead of its
time in emotional intensity. He also built the equestrian bronze
at Padua -- the first large-scale horse-and-rider since Marcus
Aurelius.

## Production Note (for book design team)

```
ANAMORPHIC OPTION — FLAG FOR PHYSICAL PRODUCTION
══════════════════════════════════════════════════

This puzzle was designed as a go/no-go gate for anamorphic
typography. The concept: eleven columns of vertically stretched
letterforms, each 15-20x its normal height, printed across a
full page. When the reader tilts the book to a severe angle
(sighting along the page surface from the bottom edge), the
stretched characters compress to normal proportions and the
eleven-letter word becomes readable.

WHY IT DOES NOT WORK IN MARKDOWN / MONOSPACE:

  1. Anamorphic compression requires precise aspect ratio
     control. A letter stretched 15x vertically and viewed
     at ~86° incidence compresses to 1x. Monospaced ASCII
     characters on screen have fixed aspect ratios that
     cannot be stretched.

  2. Even if we stack characters vertically to approximate
     stretch (e.g., a column of dots/slashes forming a
     "tall P"), the reader would need a printed page at
     exact scale to perform the tilt. Screen viewing
     distances and pixel densities vary too much.

  3. The minimum viable anamorphic effect requires vector
     typography (not raster, not monospace) with controlled
     stretch ratios, printed at known DPI on a physical
     page of known dimensions.

FOR THE PRINTED BOOK:

  The anamorphic page SHOULD be produced. It works beautifully
  in physical print. The art-history identification puzzle
  above serves as the Markdown/digital fallback and as a
  companion puzzle in the printed edition (perhaps on the
  facing page). Both yield the same answer.

  Recommended production approach:
  - Set the word in a clean sans-serif (Futura, Helvetica)
  - Stretch each glyph vertically by factor of 15-20x
  - Add cobalt blue pigment streaks (the element's color)
    weaving through the distorted letterforms
  - Print full-bleed on a single page
  - Facing page: the identification puzzle as confirmation

  The solver who tilts the page and reads the word gets the
  immediate aha. The solver who works the identification
  puzzle gets the same answer through art-historical knowledge.
  Both paths are valid. The best solvers do both and confirm.
```
