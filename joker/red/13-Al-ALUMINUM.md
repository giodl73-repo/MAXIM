## The Puzzle

**Type:** Dichotomous Key -- classify specimens through branching identification key
**References:** animal-phylogeny/, botany/, mycology/, entomology/

Five specimens sit on your desk. Each is described only by what you can observe -- habitat, body plan, nutrition, reproduction. No names. No labels.

A dichotomous key follows. At each numbered step, read both options (a and b). Only one will match your specimen. Follow the arrow to the next step or to a final identification.

Use the key to classify each specimen to its common name. Then extract five letters.

### The Specimens

Read each description. Run it through the key above. Write the common name in the blank.

**Specimen A.** This organism lives in the deep pelagic zone of the Pacific and Indian Oceans. It is a cephalopod -- a member of phylum Mollusca, class Cephalopoda -- but unlike its soft-bodied relatives (octopus, squid), it retains a heavy external shell divided into gas-filled chambers. It controls its buoyancy by adjusting the gas-to-liquid ratio in these chambers via a siphuncle. It has up to ninety tentacles, no ink sac, and pinhole eyes without a lens. A living fossil: its lineage predates the dinosaurs by 300 million years.

**Specimen B.** A deciduous angiosperm tree found across the boreal and temperate zones of the Northern Hemisphere. Its bark is its most distinctive feature -- thin, papery, peeling in horizontal strips, often white or silver. Leaves are simple, ovate to triangular, with doubly serrated margins and an alternate arrangement on the stem. It is monoecious: male and female flowers appear on the same tree as catkins. The fruit is a tiny winged nutlet dispersed by wind. Pioneer species -- one of the first trees to colonize disturbed ground. Ectomycorrhizal: its roots form a symbiotic sheath with fungal hyphae that extend the root's absorptive reach for phosphorus and water.

**Specimen C.** The largest family of flowering plants by species count: over 28,000 species. This specimen is an epiphyte -- it grows on the branches of tropical trees, anchored by aerial roots, drawing moisture from humid air rather than soil. It is a monocot: parallel leaf venation, flower parts in threes. Its flowers are bilaterally symmetric (zygomorphic), with one modified petal forming a showy lip (labellum) that guides pollinators. Reproduction involves one of the most extreme seed strategies in the plant kingdom -- each capsule produces hundreds of thousands of dust-like seeds with no endosperm, dependent on a mycorrhizal fungal partner for germination.

**Specimen D.** This organism is not a plant and not an animal. It belongs to Kingdom Fungi -- more closely related to animals than to plants, despite its sessile appearance. Its cell walls are made of chitin (the same polymer found in insect exoskeletons), not cellulose. It stores energy as glycogen, not starch. Its body is a mycelium -- a vast underground network of branching hyphae, typically invisible. What you see on the desk is the fruiting body: a short-lived spore-dispersal structure with a cap (pileus), gills (lamellae) on the underside, and a central stalk (stipe). The gills are lined with club-shaped cells called basidia, each of which produces four basidiospores. It is a saprotroph -- it decomposes dead organic matter by secreting extracellular enzymes and absorbing the digested nutrients. Without organisms like this, dead wood would accumulate indefinitely and the carbon cycle would stall.

**Specimen E.** A deciduous eudicot tree of the family Sapindaceae, found throughout temperate forests of the Northern Hemisphere. Leaves are simple, palmately lobed with 3 to 5 pointed lobes, arranged in opposite pairs on the stem -- one of few temperate trees with opposite leaf arrangement. Leaf veins radiate from the petiole in a palmate pattern. The tree produces small, greenish-yellow flowers in spring, pollinated by wind and insects. Its fruit is a samara -- but unlike the single-winged samara of an ash, this tree produces paired samaras: two winged seeds joined at the base, spinning like helicopter rotors as they fall. The sap contains sucrose at concentrations of 2-3%, harvested in late winter when freeze-thaw cycles drive sap flow.
