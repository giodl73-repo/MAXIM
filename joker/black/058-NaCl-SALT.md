### The Seven Words

**1. SAL** (chemistry)

Latin *sal* persists in chemical nomenclature: *sal ammoniac*, *sal soda*. The word still means "salt" --- but only barely. Centuries of scientific formalization stripped it of every connotation: no trade empires, no taxation, no soldiers' rations. What once carried the weight of civilizations is now a bare classifier, emptied of force. The meaning weakened.

The Microeconomics guide describes how repeated market exchange reduces a differentiated product to a standardized good competing on price alone. Smith's *Wealth of Nations* (1776) documented how specialization and the division of labor transform sacred commodities into bulk industrial inputs. The word faded alongside the product it named.

**2. SALAD**

Latin *sal* → *herba salata* ("salted herbs") → Old French *salade* → English *salad*. Originally: anything preserved in salt brine --- meat, fish, vegetables, all of it *salata*. By the 17th century: only a specific category of cold vegetable dish. The scope contracted.

The Political Economy guide explains why nations specialize: Ricardo's theory of *comparative advantage* demonstrates that even a country with absolute superiority in producing all goods gains from trade by focusing on what it produces relatively best. Salt-preserved foods traveled the Mediterranean because comparative advantage made local preservation cheaper than importing fresh. The word's contraction from "all things salted" to one dish mirrors the narrowing of trade to specialized goods.

**3. SALACIOUS**

Latin *sal* → *salax* (literally "fond of salt"; applied to animals in heat, because salt was believed to stimulate lust) → Late Latin *salaciosus* → English *salacious* (lewd, obscene). What began as a dietary preference became a moral accusation. The meaning degraded.

The Political Science guide defines the Weberian state as a human community that claims the *monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory*. That territorial authority extends to regulating public expression --- censorship laws, obscenity standards, broadcast regulations. States claim the sovereign right to define what counts as *salacious*, exercising power over language itself.

**4. SAUCE**

Latin *sal* → *salsa* ("salted thing") → Old French *sauce* → English *sauce*. Once it meant only salt brine used for preservation. Then any seasoned liquid. Then any condiment. Then (in slang) audacity, impudence. The meaning expanded at every stage, growing far beyond its origin.

The Political Economy guide traces how the Heckscher-Ohlin model predicts trade patterns from *factor endowments*: the East had the climate for growing spices, Europe had silver and sea power. The global sauce trade --- pepper, cloves, nutmeg dissolved into European sauces --- was trade theory made edible. Strategic trade policy (Krugman's *new trade theory*) explains how early movers locked in monopoly positions over spice routes.

**5. SALARY**

Latin *salarium* --- an allowance given to Roman soldiers for purchasing salt --- became the English word for any regular professional compensation. A subsistence ration became a mark of dignity. What was once a commodity handout is now the centerpiece of an employment contract. The meaning improved.

The Economics guide on information economics describes Spence's *signaling* model (Nobel 2001): agents with private information perform costly actions to credibly reveal their type. A salary offer is a signal --- firms offer more to attract and retain types they cannot directly observe. The mechanism design module explains how salary structures are *incentive-compatible* transfers that make truth-telling optimal in *principal-agent* relationships.

**6. SALTS** (chemical plural)

English *salt* (NaCl, one specific compound) → *salts* (the entire chemical class: any ionic compound formed from the reaction of an acid and a base). What named one mineral now names thousands of compounds --- potassium chloride, calcium carbonate, sodium bicarbonate, all of them "salts." The meaning expanded from one substance to an entire category.

The Game Theory guide defines *Nash equilibrium*: a strategy profile where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally deviating. In commodity markets, salt's expansion from luxury to bulk commodity illustrates the equilibrium concept --- once all producers enter and prices stabilize, no single producer benefits from changing strategy. The microeconomics module traces how *competitive equilibrium* emerges when supply equals demand and the market clears.

**7. SOLDIER**

Latin *sal* → *solidus* (a Roman gold coin, its value historically linked to salt) → Late Latin *soldarius* ("one who serves for pay") → Old French *soldier*. A mineral became a coin, a coin became a wage, and a wage became a person. At each stage the word gained stature --- from commodity to currency to a title of service and sacrifice. The meaning improved.

The Political Science guide presents Waltz's *levels of analysis* framework for explaining war: Level 1 (individual --- leader psychology), Level 2 (domestic --- regime type, interest groups), Level 3 (systemic --- anarchy, distribution of power). The soldier exists at all three levels simultaneously: an individual, an instrument of the state, and a unit in the systemic balance of power. The game theory module models military commitment as a *credibility* problem in extensive-form games --- a soldier's willingness to fight is the state's signal of resolve.
