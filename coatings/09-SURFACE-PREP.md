# 09 — Surface Preparation

## The Most Critical Step — Cleaning, Profiling, Why 80% of Failures Are Prep Failures

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- The prime directive of coatings: "The coating is only as good as the surface it's applied to." Industry data: ~80% of premature coating failures trace to surface preparation failures, not coating failures; prep is non-glamorous but determinative
- **Contamination removal**: oil, grease, silicone, mold release agents cause adhesion failure; solvent wiping (SSPC SP1 — Solvent Cleaning), TSP (trisodium phosphate, alkaline cleaner for grease/dirt), pressure washing; silicone contamination = "fisheye" in fresh paint (coating retracts from silicone spots); test with water (water beading = contamination present)
- **Mechanical surface preparation for metal** (SSPC/NACE standards):
  - SSPC SP2: Hand Tool Cleaning (wire brush, scraper) — removes loose rust/scale; lowest standard
  - SSPC SP3: Power Tool Cleaning (angle grinder) — removes loose material
  - SSPC SP6/NACE 3: Commercial Blast Cleaning — removes 66%+ of mill scale; minimum for most industrial primers
  - SSPC SP10/NACE 2: Near-White Metal Blast — removes 95%+ of contaminants; required for aggressive environments
  - SSPC SP5/NACE 1: White Metal Blast — bare metal, complete removal; highest standard for immersion service
  - Abrasive types: steel grit (sharp), steel shot (round, peens), coal slag, garnet, aluminum oxide; profile (anchor pattern) measured in mils by Testex Press-O-Film tape
- **Surface profile (anchor pattern)**: blasted metal has microscopic peaks and valleys; primer must fill the valleys AND have enough build to cover peaks; specified in mils (thousandths of inch); too little profile = poor adhesion; too much profile = peaks read through coating (apparent corrosion) and require excessive film build
- **Wood surface preparation**: sanding (80→120→180→220 grit progression); grain raising (water-based products raise grain — wet and resand before final coat); tack cloth removal of dust; knot sealer for resinous knots (shellac-based); sanding between coats (light scuff — mechanical adhesion for subsequent coats); old glossy surfaces: degloss with liquid deglosser or sand
- **Masonry preparation**: remove efflorescence (crystalline salt deposits, wire brush or acid wash); neutralize acid-etched concrete (rinse thoroughly, pH test); moisture content critical (wet concrete = trapped moisture bubbles under coating); cure time (concrete typically 28 days minimum before coating with non-breathable products)
- **Temperature and humidity conditions**: most coatings require substrate temperature >40-50°F, air temperature >50°F, relative humidity <85%, substrate temperature at least 5°F above dew point (otherwise condensation on surface); application in sun on hot surfaces causes rapid solvent flash → blisters; "hot weather" application adjustments (reduced-evaporation thinner, early morning application)
- **Intercoat adhesion**: surface between coats must be clean and receptive; mechanical scuff (320 grit) for hard/glossy intermediate coats; time window (too soon = solvent entrapment, too late = adhesion failure, smooth/hard surface); "recoat window" specified in product TDS (Technical Data Sheet)
- **Failure analysis**: blistering (moisture under film), peeling (adhesion failure at substrate or between coats), alligatoring/checking (hard topcoat over softer undercoat), sagging (too thick, applied too cold), fisheyes (silicone contamination), flash rusting (moisture on steel before primer applied), tannin bleed (inadequate stain blocking)
