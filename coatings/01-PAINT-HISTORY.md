## 40,000 Years of Surface Coating

```
COATINGS HISTORY TIMELINE
==========================

~75,000 BCE   Ochre processing, Blombos Cave (South Africa)
~40,000 BCE   Cave paintings begin (El Castillo, Spain)
~17,000 BCE   Lascaux (France), Altamira (Spain) — complex pigments
~3,000 BCE    Egyptian fresco, encaustic wax paintings
~100 BCE      Roman fresco, Pompeian wall painting traditions
~430 CE       Egg tempera icons (Byzantine tradition)
~1420s        Oil painting mastered (Flemish school, van Eyck)
~1700s        Commercial lead + linseed oil house paint sold
~1870s        Ready-mixed paint (previously mixed on-site from ingredients)
1916          Titanium white (TiO₂) — replaces lead white
1921–1923     Development of synthetic alkyd resin (Kienle, GE)

~1950s        Acrylic latex — superior to SBR latex
1971          Lead Paint Poisoning Prevention Act (US)
1978          Full US residential lead paint ban
1990s         VOC regulations (CARB, EPA Clean Air Act amendments)
2000s–present Zero-VOC, antimicrobial, self-priming formulations
```

## Prehistoric: First Pigments, First Binders

### Blombos Cave (75,000 BCE)

```
  Ochre (iron oxide pigments) found in abalone shells
  Ground with quartzite grinding stones
  Mixed with bone marrow (as binder) + ochre
  → pastier mixture than pure pigment

  Significance: purposeful pigment preparation 75,000 years ago
  Debates: was this body paint, symbolic marking, adhesive?
  Either way: BINDING a pigment to a carrier = the core of paint
```

### Paleolithic Cave Painting (40,000–12,000 BCE)

```
  PIGMENTS:
    Ochre (Fe₂O₃) → yellow-red-brown range
    Charcoal → black
    Manganese dioxide (MnO₂) → black
    Kaolinite (clay) → white
    Calcite (CaCO₃) → white

  BINDERS:
    Animal fat (marrow, tallow)
    Blood (hemoglobin denaturation)
    Urine (urea as fixative?)
    Calcium-rich water → calcite re-crystallization
      (Lascaux: natural calcite seals the paintings)

  APPLICATION:
    Fingers, moss, fur pads
    Hollow bone as spray gun (blow pigment through tube)
    Grease + pigment paste applied by stick

  DURABILITY SECRET:
    Many caves have constant humidity (~90%) and temperature
    → no freeze-thaw cycles → no moisture stress on paint film
    Lascaux opened to tourism 1948 → by 1955 algae + fungi
    destroying paintings from CO₂ and warmth of visitors → closed
    Now accessible only via Lascaux II/III (replicas)
```

## Classical Antiquity

### Egyptian and Greek Mineral Paints

```
  EGYPTIAN BLUE (calcium copper silicate, CaCuSi₂O₅):
    Earliest synthetic pigment (~3000 BCE)
    Manufactured: quartz + copper + calcium + natron → kiln firing
    Blue color; used on tomb paintings, funerary objects
    Fluorescent in near-IR — can be detected and imaged today

  LIME WASH (ancient → present):
    Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime) + water
    Applied to wall surfaces
    Carbonates in air: Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O
    White finish; antimicrobial (alkaline pH kills bacteria)
    "Whitewash" — ancient Israel/Egypt; still used in vernacular
    architecture worldwide; also Tom Sawyer

  ROMAN FRESCO:
    Pigments mixed into wet lime plaster
    As plaster carbonates, pigment is locked in CaCO₃ crystal matrix
    "Buon fresco" — applied while wet; very durable
    "Fresco secco" — applied to dry plaster with binder; less durable
    Pompeii (preserved by 79 CE eruption): color remarkably vibrant
```

### Encaustic (Hot Wax) Painting

```
  Pigment mixed into heated beeswax
  Applied with heated spatulas/brushes while liquid
  Fused to surface with heated metal tool
  Extremely durable — Fayum mummy portraits (1st–3rd century CE)
  still vivid after 1,800 years
  Revival attempts in 19th-20th century; niche contemporary use
```

## Medieval to Renaissance: Oil Painting

### Egg Tempera (Byzantine, ~400–1500 CE)

```
  BINDER: Egg yolk
    Yolk = oil-in-water emulsion with lecithin emulsifier
    Water phase evaporates first → oil remains → gradually oxidizes
    Extremely stable film: Byzantine icons from 500 CE still intact

  PIGMENTS: Lead white, vermilion, ultramarine (ground lapis lazuli),
            azurite, malachite, carbon black, yellow ochre

  CHARACTERISTICS:
    Fast drying (no reworking)
    Brilliant, matte surface
    Cannot be blended (wet-into-wet impossible)
    Requires hatching technique for gradients
    Cannot be applied thick (cracks)
    Still used by icon painters; revival in academic art

  GOLD LEAF:
    Byzantine icons: gold leaf on gesso (gypsum + rabbit skin glue)
    Applied over bole (red clay, adhesive) while still tacky
    Burnished with agate burnisher to reflective finish
```

### The Flemish Oil Painting Revolution (~1420s)

```
  MYTH: Jan van Eyck "invented" oil painting
  FACT: Oil paints known in Europe since ~1000 CE; van Eyck
        perfected their use (layering, glazing, drying time control)

  LINSEED OIL CHEMISTRY:
    Linseed oil = triglycerides of mainly linolenic acid (18:3 ω-3)
    Highly unsaturated (three double bonds per chain)
    Oxidative polymerization: O₂ attacks double bonds
    → peroxy radicals → crosslinking between chains
    → solid polymer film (not evaporation — a chemical reaction)

    Critical: linolenic acid oxidizes faster than other drying oils
    Siccatives (dryers): lead, manganese, cobalt compounds catalyze
    the oxidation → faster drying

  TECHNIQUE PERFECTED BY VAN EYCK (Ghent Altarpiece, 1432):
    Ground layers (gesso + oil primer)
    Underdrawing (transferred from prepared cartoon)
    Dead color layer (establish values)
    Color layers (oil paint with lead white)
    Glazes (transparent colored oil layers over opaque base)
    Final varnish (mastic or sandarac resin in turpentine)

  WHY IT MATTERED:
    Slow drying → blending, sfumato
    Translucent layers → depth and luminosity
    Odorless drying (vs solvent evaporation)
    Reworkable while wet
    Dominant painting medium: 1420s–1960s (500+ years)
```

## Industrial Era: House Paint

### Lead White + Linseed Oil (1700s–1978)

```
  LEAD CARBONATE (2PbCO₃·Pb(OH)₂ — "basic lead carbonate"):
    Excellent hiding power (high refractive index)
    Superior adhesion
    Self-healing (lead soaps form at surface edges)
    Antimicrobial
    FATAL FLAW: Pb²⁺ ions toxic to nervous system development

  COMMERCIAL HISTORY:
    Ready-made lead paint sold in cans from ~1870s
    (previously: painters bought lead carbonate paste and mixed with
    linseed oil on-site → "Dutch boy" brand = lead paste in linseed)
    Dutch Boy (National Lead Company) brand — iconic
    1916: TiO₂ (titanium white) introduced — better hiding, non-toxic
          but lead remained dominant due to cost and paint properties

  US REGULATION:
    1971: Lead Paint Poisoning Prevention Act — restricted in
          federally funded housing
    1973: HUD banned lead paint in federally assisted housing
    1978: Consumer Product Safety Commission — final residential ban
          (>0.06% lead by weight in consumer paint)

  LEGACY:
    ~38 million US homes still contain lead paint (pre-1978)
    Lead paint hazard rules (EPA RRP Rule) for renovation/repair
    $50,000+ remediation cost for full lead abatement
    Lead paint chips + dust → pediatric lead poisoning
    CDC: no safe blood lead level in children
```

### The Latex Revolution (1940s–1950s)

```
  ORIGIN: WWII synthetic rubber research
    Natural rubber (polyisoprene) supply disrupted (Japanese
    occupation of Malaya) → crash program for synthetic rubber
    Styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR) developed ~1942

  ADAPTATION TO PAINT:
    ~1948: SBR latex emulsion paint introduced (Glidden)
    "Spred" brand — water-based, fast dry, soap-and-water cleanup

  LIMITATIONS OF SBR LATEX:
    Poor color retention (yellows)
    Chalks on exterior
    Lower adhesion than alkyd/oil

  ACRYLIC LATEX (Rohm and Haas, early 1950s):
    Acrylic polymer emulsion (polyacrylate/polymethacrylate)
    Superior: color retention, flexibility, adhesion, UV stability
    By 1970: dominates interior paint market
    By 1990: dominates residential paint market overall (~80%)

  COALESCENCE CHEMISTRY:
    Polymer particles in water emulsion
    Water evaporates → particles crowd together
    At MFFT: particles deform and fuse into continuous film
    Below MFFT: particles don't fuse → chalky, powdery film
    → Don't apply latex below 50°F / 10°C (film formation fails)
```

## VOC Regulation Era (1990s–Present)

```
  PROBLEM: Solvent-based coatings (alkyd, lacquer, solvent-borne
           industrial) release VOCs that form ground-level ozone

  REGULATORY FRAMEWORK:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1990 Clean Air Act Amendments (US)                        │
  │  → EPA architectural coating VOC limits                   │
  │                                                            │
  │  California CARB (most stringent — sets de facto          │
  │  national standard for manufacturers selling in CA)        │
  │                                                            │
  │  EU Decopaint Directive (2004/42/EC)                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FORMULATION SHIFTS:
    Oil-based primer → latex primer (for most interior work)
    Alkyd trim paint → water-based alkyd (hybrid technology)
    Solvent-borne industrial → waterborne epoxy, waterborne urethane
    "Zero VOC" = <5 g/L (water-based acrylic base; tints add back)

  MODERN INNOVATIONS:
    Self-priming paints (primer + topcoat combined — 2000s)
    Antimicrobial paints (Ag⁺ or copper ions in binder)
    Elastomeric bridge coatings (bridges cracks as substrate moves)
    Cool roof coatings (high SRI — solar reflectance index)
    Intumescent fire-retardant (foam up in heat to insulate steel)
    Photocatalytic paint (TiO₂ breaks down organic compounds in UV)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Oil-based" and "alkyd" are synonyms in consumer paint**: Modern "oil-based" paint uses alkyd resin (synthetic) dissolved in mineral spirits — not raw linseed oil. The "oil" in "oil-based" refers to the production chemistry of the alkyd resin, not literally oil in the can.

**Lead paint is still present in millions of homes**: The 1978 ban didn't remove existing paint. Pre-1978 homes have lead paint unless it was professionally remediated. Intact lead paint is not an immediate hazard; disturbed lead paint (sanding, demolition) creates dust. The danger is to children (hand-to-mouth) and workers.

**Latex paint cannot be applied in cold weather**: Below MFFT (~50°F / 10°C for most formulas), polymer particles don't coalesce → chalky, peeling film. Check can for minimum application temperature. Oil-based/alkyd can be applied in cooler weather (but takes longer to dry).

**"Self-priming" doesn't replace all priming**: Self-priming paints contain more solids and can fill minor texture on previously painted surfaces. On raw drywall, bare wood, or problem surfaces (stains, bare metal), dedicated primer is still required for best results.
