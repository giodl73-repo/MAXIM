# 01 — Paint History

## Cave Walls to Latex — 40,000 Years of Coating Technology

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Prehistoric: ochre + animal fat/blood as binder; Blombos Cave (75,000 BCE) ochre processing; Lascaux/Altamira cave paintings (15,000-35,000 BCE); lime wash on walls (ancient Egypt, Greece, Rome — whitewash as disinfectant and reflector)
- Ancient formulations: Egyptian fresco (pigment in wet lime plaster), encaustic (pigment in hot beeswax — Fayum mummy portraits, 1st-3rd century CE), egg tempera (yolk emulsifies water and oil — Byzantine icons, early Renaissance panels), casein paint (milk protein binder — ancient and still used)
- Linseed oil revolution: discovery that linseed oil "dries" (oxidative polymerization) made grinding pigments in oil practical; Flemish painters (Jan van Eyck association — probably didn't "invent" oil painting but perfected the technique ~1420s); linseed oil remained the dominant binder in paint until the 1940s
- Industrial-era house paint: commercial lead and linseed oil paint sold from ~1700s onward; ready-mixed paint available by ~1870s (previously mixed on site from ingredients); zinc white replacing lead white from ~1840s; titanium white from 1916; professional painters' dominant material was oil-based through 1950s
- **Lead paint era**: lead carbonate in linseed oil — excellent coverage, durability, adhesion; widely used interior/exterior 1700s-1970s; Lead Paint Poisoning Prevention Act (1971) restricted residential use; HUD banned lead in federally funded housing (1973); full US residential ban effective January 1978; ~38M US homes still contain lead paint (legacy problem, Superfund and EPA RRP rule)
- **Latex paint revolution** (~1940s-1950s): synthetic polymer emulsions developed from WWII-era synthetic rubber research; styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR) latex paints introduced ~1948; acrylic latex (superior color retention, elasticity) from ~1950s; advantages: water cleanup, fast dry, low odor, flexible; by 1970s surpassed oil-based in interior use; today ~80% of consumer paint
- **VOC regulation era** (1990s-present): 1990 Clean Air Act amendments; CARB (California Air Resources Board) architectural coating rules setting strict VOC limits; formulation shift to water-based, co-solvents replaced; "zero-VOC" marketing (still typically 5g/L); EU Decopaint Directive
- Modern innovations: self-priming paints (primer + topcoat combined), antimicrobial paints (silver ion or copper), elastomeric coatings (flexible bridge coatings), cool roof coatings (high SRI — solar reflectance index), intumescent fire-retardant paints (foam up in heat to insulate steel)
