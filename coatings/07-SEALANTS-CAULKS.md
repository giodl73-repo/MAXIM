# 07 — Sealants & Caulks

## Silicone, Polyurethane, Acrylic Latex — Movement, Compatibility, Durability

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Sealant vs caulk: often used interchangeably; technically, caulk fills static gaps (no movement expected — trim to wall); sealant accommodates movement (expansion joints, building envelope); sealants specified with movement accommodation factor (±12.5%, ±25%, ±50%)
- **Silicone sealant**: polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) backbone; most flexible and durable sealant; extreme temperature range (-65°F to 400°F); UV and weathering resistant; excellent adhesion to glass, metal, ceramic; two types: acetoxy cure (acetic acid byproduct = vinegar smell; corrosive to metal — use neutral cure instead) vs neutral cure (oxime or alkoxy — no acidic byproduct; preferred for metal, concrete, electronics); limitations: cannot be painted (paint beads off cured silicone); hard to remove once cured; specialty formulations: mold-resistant (antimicrobial, bathroom), structural glazing (SSG — structural silicone for curtain walls), fire-rated
- **Polyurethane sealant** (Sikaflex, Tremco Dymeric): isocyanate-based, moisture cure; excellent adhesion to concrete/masonry/wood/metal; paintable after cure; higher modulus than silicone (stiffer but still accommodates movement); outstanding durability for horizontal applications (driveways, parking deck joints); abrasion resistant; remains slightly flexible permanently; common for construction joints, window/door perimeter on stucco/EIFS; 1-part vs 2-part (faster cure, higher movement)
- **Acrylic latex caulk**: most common residential caulk; water-based; water cleanup; paintable immediately; lowest cost; limited movement accommodation (~12.5%); shrinks on drying (water loss) → gaps may open; interior trim, crown molding, baseboards; not for wet areas; "painters caulk" = acrylic latex; "siliconized acrylic" = acrylic + small % silicone additive → slightly more flexible and water-resistant than plain acrylic
- **Butyl rubber caulk**: excellent adhesion to most substrates; very tacky; used in roofing (butyl tape seals), HVAC duct connections, glazing; remains flexible; hard to tool smooth; doesn't accept paint well; aluminum-backed butyl tape = standard HVAC duct sealing tape
- **Hybrid sealants** (SMP — silyl-modified polymer): newer chemistry combining silicone flexibility with polyurethane paintability; OSI Quad, GE Advanced, Gorilla Clear Silicone 2; moisture-cure; excellent adhesion to virtually all substrates; paintable; no isocyanate (safer than polyurethane); premium product for multi-substrate applications
- **EPDM tape and pre-formed gaskets**: non-cured rubber seals; roofing applications (EPDM roof membrane laps), pipe boot flashings, window pan flashing tape; self-adhering butyl-backed EPDM tape; compression-type seals
- **Fire-rated caulk/sealant**: intumescent (expands on heat, blocking fire/smoke penetration of through-penetrations in fire-rated assemblies); UL tested; required at all pipe/conduit/cable penetrations through fire-rated walls and floors; 3M Fire Barrier, Hilti CP products; firestop system must be UL classified as a system (specific assembly)
- **Application best practices**: joint design (width:depth ratio ~2:1 for movement joints; backer rod to control depth); surface prep (dry, clean, oil-free); masking tape for clean lines; proper tooling (smooth concave profile compresses into substrate); cure time before water exposure; typical service life: silicone 20+ years, polyurethane 10-15 years, acrylic 5-10 years
