# Varnish, Lacquer & Polyurethane

## Film-Forming Clear Finishes — Chemistry and Performance Hierarchy

## The Big Picture

```
CLEAR FINISH SELECTION MATRIX:

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    PERFORMANCE vs CONVENIENCE                      │
  │                                                                    │
  │  Highest        Conversion Varnish (2-component, catalyzed)       │
  │  performance    2K Polyurethane (isocyanate hardener)             │
  │       ↑         Oil-based 1-part polyurethane                     │
  │       │         Pre-cat / post-cat lacquer                        │
  │       │         Water-based polyurethane                          │
  │       │         Nitrocellulose lacquer                             │
  │       │         Alkyd varnish                                      │
  │       │         Oil varnish / danish oil                           │
  │  Lowest         Shellac (beautiful, not durable)                  │
  │  performance    Paste wax (reversible, non-film)                  │
  │                                                                    │
  │  ←── Most convenient ────────── Least convenient ──→              │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THREE HARDENING MECHANISMS:
  1. Evaporation only (re-soluble): shellac, nitrocellulose lacquer
  2. Oxidative polymerization (crosslinks with air): oil varnish, alkyd
  3. Catalyzed crosslinking: 2K polyurethane, conversion varnish

  The catalyzed finishes form true polymer networks (dense crosslinks)
  → superior hardness, chemical resistance, durability vs evaporative
```

## Oil Varnish (Traditional)

```
  FORMULATION:
    Drying oil: linseed oil, tung oil
    + Resin: natural (copal, dammar) or synthetic alkyd
    + Solvent: mineral spirits (thins for application)
    = Oil varnish

  FILM FORMATION: OXIDATIVE POLYMERIZATION
    Unsaturated fatty acids (linoleic, linolenic in linseed oil)
    React with atmospheric oxygen → chain-crosslinking
    Not evaporation — chemical reaction in the film
    Slow: 24-48 hours between coats
    Fully cured: weeks (oxidation continues after dry-to-touch)

  TUNG OIL vs LINSEED OIL:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Tung Oil           │ Linseed Oil                        │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Harder, better     │ More flexible                      │
  │  water resistance   │ More amber color                   │
  │  Less yellowing     │ Slower cure                        │
  │  More expensive     │ Less expensive                     │
  │  Traditional boat   │ Traditional furniture              │
  │  finish             │ and floor                          │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  "DANISH OIL" / "TEAK OIL" / "ANTIQUE OIL":
    Marketing names — NO regulatory definition
    Typically: diluted oil varnish (30-50% solids)
    Wipes on easily; penetrates somewhat
    Low build: many coats = minimal protection
    Popular for "easy" maintenance finish
    NOT the same as rubbing oil straight on wood

  RUBIO MONOCOAT / OSMO (modern hardwax oil):
    Wipe-on, one-coat systems (hence "monocoat")
    Plant-based oil + carnauba wax blend
    Better hardness than traditional danish oil
    No film build = no peeling; refinish by reapplication
    Popular for floors, furniture, wooden toys (food-safe options)
```

## Alkyd Varnish

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    "Alkyd" = oil-modified polyester
    Phthalic anhydride + glycerol + drying oil fatty acids
    → alkyd resin
    Solvent-thinned; oxidative cure + polymer chain structure

  ADVANTAGES OVER PURE OIL VARNISH:
    Better hardness (polymer backbone)
    Faster cure than pure oil (modified polymer)
    Improved gloss retention
    Better for both interior and exterior

  YELLOWING:
    Linseed-based alkyds yellow significantly over time
    Less than pure linseed oil varnish
    More than polyurethane
    → not for clear finishes over light-colored wood
      where long-term clarity matters

  HISTORICAL USE:
    Pre-polyurethane era: alkyd was the standard clear floor
    finish; replaced by polyurethane from 1970s onward
    Still used: traditional boat varnish (Epifanes, Interlux)
    Exterior furniture varnish (some formulations)
```

## Polyurethane: The Dominant Modern Clear Finish

```
  CHEMISTRY: urethane linkages (–NH–CO–O–) connect polymer chains
  Three commercial forms with very different properties:

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1-PART OIL-BASED POLYURETHANE
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    "Urethane-modified alkyd" (technically)
    Moisture in air initiates cure (like isocyanate-free crosslinking)
    Most common floor finish in residential use
    Properties:
      Amber color: warm tone over wood grain
      Excellent durability: hard, abrasion-resistant
      High gloss achievable; matte/satin versions with flatting agent
      DRY TIME: dust-free 4-8h; recoat 8-24h (read TDS)
      Vapor-emitting: flammable during application + drying
      Sanding between coats: required for adhesion
    Application:
      Thin first coat as sealer coat (mineral spirits 10-15%)
      Full coats 2-3 after sealer
      Final coat: do NOT sand (leaves scratches)
      Applicator: lambswool applicator or bristle brush
      (synthetic bristle = no control; use china bristle for oil-based)

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WATER-BASED POLYURETHANE
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Polyurethane particles in water emulsion + co-solvent
    True crosslinking occurs during cure
    Properties:
      Water-white (almost no amber): reveals true wood color
      Lower VOC; soap and water cleanup
      FAST DRY: recoat in 2-4 hours (allows multiple coats/day)
      Slightly softer when first applied; modern formulas competitive
      Grain raise: first coat raises wood grain (sand lightly with
        220 after first coat, before second)
      Bubbling risk: don't apply in heat, don't over-brush
    Application:
      Apply thinner coats (water-based builds faster to sheen)
      Synthetic bristle applicator or foam roller
      Avoid draughts during drying (even drying = good film)
      3+ coats for floors

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  2-PART (2K) POLYURETHANE
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Component A: polyol resin
    Component B: isocyanate hardener (MDI, HDI, TDI)
    Mix ratio critical (by volume or weight — check TDS exactly)
    Reaction: isocyanate (–N=C=O) + hydroxyl (–OH) → urethane linkage
    Exothermic: generates heat during cure
    POT LIFE: 4-8 hours after mixing (then gels and is wasted)
    Properties:
      SUBSTANTIALLY harder than 1-part (true crosslinked network)
      Excellent chemical resistance (acid, solvent, oil)
      Outstanding abrasion resistance
      Used: car refinishing clear coat, bar tops, commercial floors
    Warning: isocyanate is a respiratory sensitizer
      Full respirator required (not dust mask)
      After sensitization, trace exposure causes asthma
      Ventilation + PPE non-negotiable
```

## Shellac

```
  ORIGIN:
    Natural resin secreted by Kerria lacca (lac insect)
    Female lac insect deposits resin on tree branches in India/SE Asia
    Scraped from branches → "lac" → processed → flake shellac
    Same insect species as lac dye (used for red fabric dye)
    ~ 100,000 insects to produce 1 lb of lac resin

  CHEMISTRY:
    Dissolved in denatured alcohol (ethanol)
    SOLVENT RELEASE only: alcohol evaporates, shellac stays
    Does NOT polymerize or crosslink
    → Fully reversible: alcohol re-dissolves cured shellac

  PROPERTIES:
    Exceptionally fast dry: 30-45 minutes between coats
    Excellent adhesion to virtually any surface
    → "Universal primer/barrier coat"
    Warm amber color (standard) or "blonde" (less color)
    Dewaxed shellac: wax removed → topcoats bond to it
      (waxed shellac: topcoats may not adhere → dewaxed critical)

  EXCELLENT FOR:
    Stain blocking: blocks smoke, nicotine, pet urine, markers,
      tannins, water stains
    Sealing dye stains before topcoat (prevents bleed)
    Sealing knots in pine/cedar before painting
    Antiques and conservation (reversibility = essential)
    Sealing shellacked surfaces before any topcoat (compatibility)

  LIMITATIONS:
    Not waterproof: water softens; coasters leave rings
    Not heat-resistant: glass of hot water → white haze
    Not solvent-resistant: alcohol re-dissolves it
    NOT for kitchen counters, bar tops, wet surfaces

  "ZINSSER BIN" vs "ZINSSER BULLS EYE 1-2-3":
    BIN = shellac-based; the real stain blocker
    Bulls Eye 1-2-3 = latex primer; general use
    Do NOT confuse; BIN is the product for smoke/stain blocking
```

## Nitrocellulose Lacquer

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    Cellulose treated with nitric/sulfuric acid → nitrocellulose
    (similar chemistry to guncotton, but lower degree of nitration)
    Dissolved in fast-evaporating solvents (lacquer thinner)
    SOLVENT RELEASE: no crosslinking, no polymerization
    Film = suspended nitrocellulose + plasticizer in dried state

  PROPERTIES:
    Dries very fast (20-30 minutes, touch-dry)
    Thin, clear film; excellent clarity
    Builds quickly with many thin coats (spray application)
    Re-soluble: fresh lacquer thinner dissolves old coat
      → repairs melt into surrounding surface seamlessly
    Lower durability than polyurethane (no crosslinking)
    Brittle over time (cellulose chains degrade)

  "NITRO GUITAR FINISH":
    Gibson and Fender used nitrocellulose lacquer since 1950s
    Vintage instruments highly valued partly for this finish
    Modern manufacturers: still offer nitro (labor-intensive,
    high VOC, thin film builds, but: authentic vintage aging
    develops — "checking" crazing, worn areas, yellowing)
    Player claim: nitro allows wood to "breathe" (debated;
    very thin film offers negligible resistance to vapor)

  APPLICATIONS:
    Furniture lacquer (spray applied, professional shops)
    Musical instruments (guitars, pianos)
    Automotive touch-up (original formulation)

  SPRAY-ONLY:
    Nitrocellulose lacquer must be sprayed (HVLP, airless)
    Brushable lacquer = different formulation
    Spray requires exhaust ventilation: solvent fumes flammable
    and toxic; proper spray booth essential
```

## Catalyzed Finishes (Professional)

```
  THREE LEVELS OF CATALYSIS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  PRE-CATALYZED LACQUER ("pre-cat"):                         │
  │    Catalyst added by manufacturer at factory                │
  │    Limited shelf life: ~1 year after opening                │
  │    One-component application (no mixing required)           │
  │    More durable than nitro; some crosslinking               │
  │    Professional cabinet shops; spray application            │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  POST-CATALYZED LACQUER ("post-cat"):                       │
  │    Finisher adds catalyst just before use                   │
  │    Higher crosslink density → harder film                   │
  │    Mix ratio important: too much catalyst = brittle;        │
  │    too little = under-cured                                  │
  │    Pot life: 8-24 hours after catalyzing                    │
  │    Better than pre-cat; less labor than conversion varnish  │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  CONVERSION VARNISH (acid-catalyzed varnish):               │
  │    Full two-component system: urea-formaldehyde or          │
  │    melamine resin + acid catalyst                           │
  │    HIGHEST performance clear finish for wood:               │
  │      Exceeds polyurethane in hardness and chem. resistance  │
  │      Kitchen cabinets, commercial casework, hardwood floors  │
  │    Requires precise mix ratio (typically 5-10% catalyst)    │
  │    Pot life: 8-12 hours                                     │
  │    Temperature sensitivity: cold = slow cure, brittle;      │
  │    hot = fast cure                                          │
  │    PROFESSIONAL SHOP ONLY: spray equipment, spray booth,    │
  │    temperature/humidity control for consistent results      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Wax

```
  NOT a clear coat in the durable sense:
    Provides surface protection: none (thin mechanical layer)
    Provides look: yes (deep, rich glow; enhances grain)
    Provides feel: yes (silky hand)
    Provides reversibility: complete (dissolves in mineral spirits)
    Durability: very low (months on furniture, weeks on floor)

  WAXES USED:
    Carnauba wax: from palm leaves; hardest natural wax;
                  high gloss; durable among waxes
    Beeswax: softer; traditional; easy application
    Paraffin: cheapest; softest
    Microcrystalline wax: synthetic; more flexible than paraffin
    Paste wax: wax + solvent carrier (mineral spirits or naphtha)
    Water-based wax emulsion: lower VOC; used on floors

  APPLICATIONS:
    Antique furniture: reversible → conservators prefer
    Floors: as maintenance coat over existing finish
    Fine furniture: final step after lacquer for extra depth
    Turnings, carvings: easy application in complex forms

  CRITICAL RULE:
    Wax over any finish = fine
    ANYTHING OVER WAX = adhesion failure
    Wax contaminates surfaces for ALL subsequent coatings
    If wax applied to a surface, entire wax layer must be
    removed with mineral spirits before any new finish
    (or it will peel, fisheye, or delaminate)
```

## Application Sequence and Compatibility

```
  BETWEEN-COAT SANDING:
    Oil-based poly between coats: 220 grit light scuff
      → removes nibs, improves adhesion
    Water-based between coats: 220 grit (more critical —
      film is harder, less chemically soluble for adhesion)
    Conversion varnish: light scuff with Scotch-Brite or 320
    Final coat: do NOT sand (scratches visible, ruins gloss)

  COMPATIBLE SEQUENCES:
    Oil-based stain → water-based poly ✅ (24-48h dry first)
    Water-based stain → oil-based poly ⚠️ (test; oil may
      not penetrate over water-based film)
    Shellac (dewaxed) → ANY topcoat ✅
    Wax → NOTHING ✗ (remove wax first)
    Nitro lacquer → nitro lacquer ✅ (re-dissolves and bonds)
    Nitro → conversion varnish ⚠️ (adhesion sometimes poor)

  DRY TIME vs CURE TIME:
    Dry to touch ≠ cured
    Oil-based poly dry 4h; recoat at 8-24h; fully cured 30 days
    Applying loads (heat, chemical exposure) before cure completes
    → permanent damage to under-cured film
    Kitchens: wait 30 days after final coat before heavy use
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Application | Best choice |
|---|---|
| Hardwood floor, maximum durability | 1-part oil-based poly (3 coats) or conversion varnish |
| Floor, non-yellowing/fast job | Water-based poly (3-4 coats same day) |
| Cabinet doors, kitchen | Conversion varnish (sprayed, professional) or 2K poly |
| Guitar or vintage instrument | Nitrocellulose lacquer ("nitro") |
| Stain blocking, antique repair | Shellac (dewaxed; Zinsser SealCoat) |
| Teak/outdoor furniture | Tung oil varnish or alkyd spar varnish |
| Furniture, reversible/museum | Paste wax over existing finish |
| Bar top or boat interior | 2K polyurethane (chemical + water resistance) |
| Easy wipe-on for shop project | Danish oil/hardwax oil (Rubio, Osmo) |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Danish oil" is not a defined product**: It's a marketing label that can mean anything from diluted linseed oil to a 50% solids alkyd varnish mix. Two products with identical names from different manufacturers may be entirely different chemistries. Read the solids content on TDS and evaluate actual build/hardness.

**Shellac is not a finish for durable surfaces**: Shellac is exceptional as a primer, sealer, and stain blocker. It is not appropriate as a final finish on anything that will contact water, alcohol, or heat. Understand where it excels (stain blocking, barrier coats, antiques) vs where it fails (any wet or hot surface).

**Sanding between coats of water-based poly is more critical than oil-based**: Oil-based coats have some chemical intercoat adhesion (the solvents slightly soften the previous coat). Water-based coats form hard, non-soluble films. Without mechanical scuffing between coats, adhesion is purely mechanical → delamination risk under stress.

**Polyurethane DOES yellow — it just yellows less than alkyd**: Water-based poly over light maple or bleached wood will look radically different from oil-based poly. The "water-white" finish of water-based is a feature when it matters (blond/white/gray wood), not just a selling point.
