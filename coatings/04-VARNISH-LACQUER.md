# 04 — Varnish, Lacquer & Polyurethane

## Film-Forming Clear Finishes — Chemistry and Performance Hierarchy

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Oil varnish** (traditional): drying oil (linseed/tung) + resin (natural alkyd/copal/dammar) + solvent; oxidative polymerization cross-links film; slow dry (24-48h between coats); amber color (particularly linseed); warm, traditional appearance; penetrates slightly; tung oil = harder, water-resistant than linseed; "danish oil" / "teak oil" = diluted oil varnish (easy application, low build, many coats needed)
- **Alkyd varnish**: oil-modified polyester resin; solvent-based; improved hardness over pure oil; faster cure; interior and exterior applications; yellows over time (less than pure linseed); oil-based floor varnish historically alkyd-based
- **Polyurethane (urethane)** — the dominant modern clear finish:
  - Oil-based polyurethane (1-part): most common floor finish; urethane-modified alkyd; moisture in air initiates cure; amber color, excellent durability; dry time 8-24h between coats; vapors flammable; sanding between coats required
  - Water-based polyurethane: lower VOC, faster dry (2-4h between coats), clearer/non-yellowing; less amber — closer to "water-white"; film less hard than oil-based when first applied but modern formulations competitive; bubbling issue if applied too warm or with too much agitation; raises grain slightly
  - 2-part polyurethane (2K urethane): isocyanate hardener + polyol resin; pot life (work time after mixing); dramatically harder, more chemical resistant; professional/industrial applications; car refinishing clear coat; floor coatings
- **Shellac**: natural resin secreted by *Kerria lacca* (lac insect, same species as lac dye); dissolved in denatured alcohol; very fast dry (30 min between coats); excellent adhesion to almost anything — universal "barrier coat" sealer; Zinsser BIN = dewaxed shellac-based primer (CRITICAL: dewaxed — waxed shellac doesn't accept subsequent finishes well); downsides: not waterproof (alcohol will re-dissolve it), not heat-resistant (coaster rings), not good for surfaces exposed to water or solvents; museum standard for certain conservation uses
- **Nitrocellulose lacquer**: cellulose treated with nitric acid → nitrocellulose polymer; solvent-release (fast-drying, non-crosslinking); vintage guitar finishes (Gibson, Fender — "nitro" finishes); furniture lacquer; thin, builds fast, easy to rub out; re-soluble in lacquer thinner (melts into itself for repairs); not durable for floors; flammable, high VOC
- **Catalyzed finishes** (professional cabinet/furniture):
  - Pre-catalyzed lacquer: acid catalyst pre-added by manufacturer; limited shelf life (~1 year); crosslinks → tougher than nitro; one-component application
  - Post-catalyzed lacquer: separate catalyst added by finisher just before use; higher conversion = harder film; pot life consideration
  - Conversion varnish: full two-component crosslinked; hardest, most chemical-resistant clear finish available for wood; professional shops only; exceeds polyurethane in performance; requires accurate mix ratio
- **Danish oil, teak oil, antique oil** (marketing names): typically diluted oil varnish (30-50% solids); wipes on easily; minimal build per coat; many coats = minimal protection; popular for "easy" results; compare to "hard wax oil" (Rubio Monocoat, Osmo) — harder-wearing modern wipe-on alternatives
- **Wax** (furniture paste wax, floor wax): low durability, good for antiques (non-film-forming, reversible); carnuba wax (hard, from palm), beeswax (soft), paraffin; solvent-carried or water-emulsion; periodic reapplication; incompatible as topcoat over most finishes (nothing will bond over wax except wax)
