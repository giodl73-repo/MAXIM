# 08 — Primers

## Why Primer Matters — Adhesion, Stain Blocking, pH Equalization

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Why primer: (1) adhesion — bonds to difficult substrates where topcoat alone would fail; (2) stain blocking — prevents tannins/smoke/water stains from bleeding through; (3) uniform porosity — equalize absorption so topcoat goes on evenly; (4) pH neutralization — fresh concrete/plaster alkaline, will saponify (destroy) oil-based topcoats without primer; (5) corrosion inhibition — for metal; (6) fill/build — high-build primers for rough surfaces
- **Latex/water-based primer**: most common for drywall, previously-painted surfaces, general use; acrylic binder; low stain blocking; adequate for clean, properly prepared surfaces; fast dry, low odor; not ideal for smoke, severe staining, or raw wood high in tannins
- **Oil-based primer**: alkyd binder; excellent adhesion to bare wood, metal, previously-painted surfaces with adhesion issues; better stain blocking than latex; slower dry (24h); higher VOC; better at penetrating chalky or failing surfaces; required over existing oil-based paint if recoating with latex (provides adhesion bridge)
- **Shellac-based primer** (Zinsser BIN, B-I-N): the universal stain-blocker; dewaxed shellac in denatured alcohol; bonds to anything — "universal primer"; excellent for: smoke damage, nicotine stains, pet urine, markers, tannin bleed (redwood/cedar/pine knots), water stains; fast dry (45 min); not for exterior (moisture-sensitive); strong odor; lacquer thinner or denatured alcohol cleanup; product note: Zinsser BIN ≠ Zinsser 1-2-3 (latex) — different products for different purposes
- **Bonding primer**: aggressive adhesion to difficult surfaces; slick/glossy surfaces (tile, glass, metal, previously-painted glossy); Zinsser Bulls Eye 1-2-3, Valspar Multi-Surface; often water-based but modified for adhesion
- **Drywall primer (PVA primer)**: polyvinyl alcohol sealer; seals cut drywall paper and joint compound uniformly; critical — unprimed drywall joint compound absorbs topcoat differently than paper face → "flashing" (visible sheen differences even when dry); cheap, fast; only for new drywall
- **Metal primer (direct-to-metal, DTM)**: corrosion-inhibiting; zinc chromate (traditional, toxic, excellent), zinc phosphate, zinc-rich (>80% zinc by weight — provides cathodic/galvanic protection); epoxy primer for best adhesion to bare metal; product example: Rustoleum Professional primer; surface prep (SSPC SP1 solvent cleaning at minimum, SP6 commercial blast for severe exposure)
- **High-build primer/surfacer**: thick film (3-10x normal primer); fills surface irregularities; sanding primer for bodywork and furniture; lacquer-based surfacer for automotive; spray-applied; sanded before topcoat; used in marine applications (fairing compound)
- **Masonry/concrete primer**: alkali-resistant formulation; latex-based for most applications; epoxy for garage floors (requires acid etch prep first — opens pores, promotes adhesion); penetrating consolidants (silane/siloxane penetrate rather than film-forming)
- **Stain-blocking spectrum** (weakest to strongest): latex primer → oil-based primer → shellac-based primer; for serious stains always use shellac-based BIN; cost is minimal relative to re-painting after bleed-through
