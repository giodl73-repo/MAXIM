## 1. The Stain-Blocking Spectrum

Understanding when each primer tier is needed is the core skill.

```
  WEAKEST ─────────────────────────────────────────────── STRONGEST

  Latex/water-based ──→ Oil-based alkyd ──→ Shellac-based (BIN)

  Handles:              Handles:              Handles:
  ✓ Clean drywall       ✓ Bare wood           ✓ Smoke damage
  ✓ Previously painted  ✓ Tannin-rich wood    ✓ Nicotine stains
  ✓ Minor surface prep  ✓ Mild water stains   ✓ Pet urine
  ✗ Stains               ✓ Failing surfaces    ✓ Markers/ink
  ✗ Tannin bleed        ✗ Heavy smoke         ✓ Severe tannin bleed
  ✗ Smoke               ✗ Nicotine            ✓ Water stain halos
                        ✗ Pet urine           ✓ Odor encapsulation
                                              ✓ Knots (pine/redwood)

  The correct approach: use shellac-based for any stain you can't
  guarantee the source of. It's ~$25/qt and 45-minute dry time.
  A second topcoat costs more than the right primer.
```

## 2. Latex / Water-Based Primer

The default for standard interior applications on properly prepared, unstained surfaces.

### Chemistry

Acrylic or PVA (polyvinyl acetate) emulsion. Film forms by water evaporation followed
by particle coalescence — the latex spheres deform and fuse as water leaves.

```
  Wet primer:   [latex sphere][latex sphere][latex sphere]  in water
  Drying:        water evaporates → spheres pack together
  Cured film:   [──────continuous polymer film──────]
                 (no crosslinking, physical entanglement only)
```

### Properties and Use Cases

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Dry time | 1-2 hours (temp/humidity dependent) |
| Recoat time | 2-4 hours |
| VOC | Low (50-100 g/L typical) |
| Cleanup | Water |
| Stain blocking | Low |
| Grain raise on bare wood | Yes (raises grain — resand before topcoat) |
| Coverage | 350-400 sq ft/gallon |

**Best for:**
- New drywall (though PVA primer is better — see below)
- Previously painted walls in good condition
- Spot priming minor repairs on painted surfaces
- When stain blocking is not a concern

**Not for:**
- Bare, tannin-rich wood (cedar, redwood, pine knots — use oil or shellac)
- Any stain situation
- Glossy surfaces without surface prep

## 3. Oil-Based (Alkyd) Primer

The workhorse for bare wood and surfaces with adhesion challenges.

### Chemistry

Alkyd resin (oil-modified polyester) in mineral spirits. Cures by oxidative
crosslinking — oxygen from air reacts with unsaturated fatty acid chains in the oil
component, forming crosslinks over 24-48 hours.

```
  Alkyd = phthalic anhydride + polyol + fatty acid (from linseed/soya/tall oil)

  Cure:
  ─CH=CH─CH=CH─ + O₂ → ─CH─CH─CH─CH─
                              |     |
                             O─O (peroxide crosslinks)
                              ↓ (further reaction)
                             C─O─C (ether crosslinks) + H₂O
```

### Why Oil Beats Latex on Bare Wood

Water-based primers raise the grain AND don't penetrate wood as deeply. Oil primers:
- Penetrate the wood fibers — better mechanical bond
- Wet the surface thoroughly before building a film
- Better stain blocking for mild tannins (knots still need shellac)
- Better adhesion to weathered or chalky surfaces

### Properties and Use Cases

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Dry time | 4-8 hours (touch) |
| Full cure | 24 hours before topcoat |
| VOC | High (300-400 g/L typical) |
| Cleanup | Mineral spirits/paint thinner |
| Stain blocking | Moderate (mild tannins, water stains) |
| Grain raise on bare wood | Minimal |
| Adhesion to chalky/failing | Excellent |

**Best for:**
- Bare or stripped bare wood (especially exterior)
- Surfaces where latex primer has previously failed (poor adhesion)
- Recoating over existing oil-based paint before switching to latex
  (latex needs an oil primer as adhesion bridge over oil-based paint in poor condition)
- Mild tannin situations (lighter woods, not redwood/cedar heartwood)

**The latex-over-oil-over-latex question:**
- Oil-based topcoat over cured latex primer: generally fine
- Latex topcoat over oil-based primer: fine after primer is fully cured
- Latex topcoat directly over glossy oil-based paint: poor adhesion — use oil primer
  as adhesion bridge, or sand aggressively, or use bonding primer

## 4. Shellac-Based Primer

The universal stain blocker. When in doubt about staining, use this.

### Chemistry

Shellac = natural resin secreted by the lac bug (Kerria lacca), dissolved in denatured
alcohol (ethanol). Pre-formed polymer — no cure reaction. Film forms purely by solvent
evaporation. The resulting film is extremely dense, hard, and non-porous.

```
  Shellac chemistry (simplified):
  Aleuritic acid + shellolic acid polymers + minor components

  Key property: very dense, low-porosity film that physically prevents
  volatile compounds (tannins, odors, tobacco residue) from migrating
  through to topcoat

  Analogy: it's a vapor barrier for the wall, not just a primer
```

### The Zinsser BIN Product (Critical Product Knowledge)

```
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ ZINSSER BIN (B-I-N) ─── White Pigmented Shellac                 │
  │   Binder: dewaxed shellac in denatured alcohol                  │
  │   The "dewaxed" part matters — wax in shellac prevents          │
  │   topcoat adhesion; BIN is dewaxed = topcoats bond              │
  │   Dry time: 45 minutes                                          │
  │   Recoat: 45 minutes                                            │
  │   Topcoats: latex or oil — anything bonds to dewaxed shellac    │
  │   Cleanup: denatured alcohol (not water, not mineral spirits)   │
  │   Exterior: NOT recommended (shellac is moisture-sensitive)     │
  │   Price: ~$20-25/qt                                             │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ ZINSSER BULLS EYE 1-2-3 ─── Acrylic Latex Primer               │
  │   Completely different product — water-based, acrylic           │
  │   Multi-purpose interior/exterior primer                        │
  │   NOT a stain blocker for serious stains                        │
  │   The "1-2-3" name causes constant product confusion            │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Stains That Require Shellac-Based Primer

```
  SMOKE AND FIRE DAMAGE
  ├── Carbon soot: physically blocks even with multiple coats of latex
  ├── Volatile organic odor compounds: migrate through latex for months
  └── Shellac encapsulates both — after 2 coats BIN, odor sealed

  NICOTINE AND TOBACCO
  ├── Yellow/brown compounds penetrate deeply into drywall paper
  ├── Water activates them — latex paint = water carrier = activates stain
  └── Shellac must go on dry (no water), seals before topcoat

  TANNIN BLEED
  ├── Cedar/redwood: extremely high tannin content
  ├── Pine knots: sap + resin, not just tannins
  ├── Water-based primers activate and lift tannins
  └── Shellac or oil primer over knots before ANY topcoat system

  PET URINE
  ├── Uric acid crystals in subfloor/drywall
  ├── Humidity reactivates odor indefinitely
  └── Heavy coats BIN, may need enzymatic treatment first

  WATER STAIN HALOS
  ├── Water stain ring = mineral deposits + biological material
  ├── Latex over water stains: halo bleeds through after painting
  └── BIN seals; confirm source dried before sealing
```

### Using Shellac as Universal Barrier Coat (Beyond Priming)

Relevant to the varnish/lacquer system (see 04-VARNISH-LACQUER.md): dewaxed shellac
is chemically compatible with everything above and below it:
- Latex over shellac: yes
- Oil over shellac: yes
- Lacquer over shellac: yes
- Shellac over latex: yes
- Shellac over oil: yes

When you don't know what's on a surface: shellac first. It's the universal bridge.

## 5. Bonding Primer

For surfaces that normal primers won't adhere to: glossy tile, glass, metal, dense
smooth plastics, previously painted glossy surfaces.

### Mechanism

Bonding primers use aggressive binders (often acrylic with chemical adhesion
promoters) that mechanically bite into harder surfaces. Some use chlorinated rubber
or other specialty binders. The goal is to create a key that subsequent topcoats
can bond to.

**Common products:**
- Zinsser Bulls Eye 1-2-3: acrylic, multi-surface
- Insl-X Stix (Advance): aggressive adhesion to hard surfaces
- KILZ Adhesion: latex, good for slick surfaces
- Rustoleum Adhesion Primer: recommended over plastics, PVC

**Process note:** Even with bonding primer, the fundamental requirement is clean
surface. Bonding primer bonds to the substrate — not to contamination on the substrate.
Wipe with isopropyl alcohol before application.

## 6. PVA Drywall Primer

Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) sealer — specific to new, uncoated drywall.

### Why New Drywall Is Different

```
  New drywall surface has two materials:

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  DRYWALL PAPER FACE    │  Absorbs topcoat moderately     │
  │  (paper + clay filler) │  Consistent porosity            │
  ├────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────┤
  │  JOINT COMPOUND REPAIR │  Absorbs topcoat AGGRESSIVELY   │
  │  (gypsum + fillers)    │  High porosity = soaks up paint │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Result without PVA primer:
  Over paper → paint sheen X
  Over joint compound → same paint, less sheen, more flat
  = "flashing" — visible sheen variation under raking light
    even after topcoat is fully dry

  PVA primer seals the joint compound to match the paper absorption
  → uniform porosity → uniform topcoat sheen
```

**Rule:** New drywall always gets PVA drywall primer (or good quality latex primer),
never topcoat direct. The cost of properly priming new drywall is trivial; the
cost of "flashing" on a freshly painted room is re-priming and repainting.

**Products:** Sherwin-Williams ProMar Drywall Primer, Sherwin ProGreen 200,
Glidden PVA Primer, most manufacturer "drywall primer" designations.

## 7. Metal Primers (DTM — Direct-to-Metal)

Corrosion inhibition is the primary function. Three mechanisms:

```
  MECHANISM 1: BARRIER
  Dense film prevents oxygen and moisture from reaching metal
  All primers provide some barrier; quality = adhesion strength and film integrity

  MECHANISM 2: INHIBITIVE (galvanic/sacrificial)
  Zinc-rich primers: >80% metallic zinc by weight
  Zinc (more anodic than iron) corrodes preferentially
  Steel is protected as long as zinc is present
  = Same principle as hot-dip galvanizing, applied by brush/spray

  MECHANISM 3: PASSIVATING
  Traditional: zinc chromate (yellow — excellent but Cr⁶⁺ = carcinogen)
  Modern alternatives: zinc phosphate (reacts with iron surface, forms passive layer)
                       molybdate pigments
                       strontium chromate (lower toxicity than zinc chromate)
```

### Metal Primer Types

| Primer Type | Mechanism | Best For |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Zinc-rich (organic/inorganic binder) | Galvanic/sacrificial | Structural steel, bridges |
| Zinc phosphate | Passivating | General metal, HVAC equipment |
| Epoxy primer | Barrier (exceptional adhesion) | Marine, aggressive environments |
| DTM acrylic | Barrier (adequate) | Architectural, light metal |
| Rust converter primer | Chemical conversion | Existing light rust |
| Etch primer (wash primer) | Phosphoric acid etch + primer | Bare aluminum, galvanized |

**Epoxy primer detail:** Two-component (2K) — epoxy base + polyamine hardener. Pot
life 4-8 hours typically. Outstanding adhesion and chemical resistance. Required for
immersion service (fuel tanks, boat hulls below waterline, chemical exposure).

**Surface prep interaction:** Metal primer performance is dominated by surface prep
(see 09-SURFACE-PREP.md). A zinc-rich primer on SSPC SP10 (near-white blast) will
outperform any primer on SSPC SP2 (hand tool). Prep first, then primer selection.

## 8. High-Build Primer / Surfacer

Thick-film primer designed to fill surface irregularities and provide a sanding
base before topcoat.

**When used:**
- Automotive bodywork (lacquer surfacer, 2K urethane surfacer)
- Furniture refinishing over rough surfaces
- Marine fairing (filling tooling marks, weave texture in fiberglass)
- MDF edges (highly absorbent, raise-free priming + build)

**Process:** Apply multiple coats (3-5 typical), allow full dry, block sand flat
(typically 180-320 grit on a sanding block), inspect with raking light, apply
topcoat. The primer fills; sanding levels; topcoat reveals.

## 9. Masonry and Concrete Primers

Fresh concrete/plaster is highly alkaline (pH 12-13). Oil-based topcoats undergo
saponification — the alkali attacks the ester bonds in the alkyd, converting it to
soap. The topcoat disintegrates. Latex topcoats are more alkali-resistant but still
benefit from priming.

```
  ALKALI SAPONIFICATION (oil-based topcoat on fresh masonry):

  Alkyd + NaOH (from concrete) → soap (sodium salt of fatty acid)
                                   + glycerol
  = Topcoat failure from below, not from top

  Prevention:
  - Allow concrete to cure (typically 28 days before non-breathable coating)
  - Test pH (should be <9 before oil-based coating)
  - Use alkali-resistant primer formulated for masonry
  - Or use penetrating silane/siloxane sealers (breathable, won't trap moisture)
```

**Garage floor epoxy:** Requires acid etch prep (10% muriatic acid solution → opens
surface pores) followed by thorough rinse, pH neutralization (baking soda solution),
then moisture test (tape plastic sheet, check for condensation = 24h), then epoxy
primer + epoxy topcoat. Skip any of these steps and epoxy will delaminate.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Primer = first coat of paint diluted with water"**
Absolutely not. Primer is a different product formulated for adhesion and sealing.
Diluting topcoat does not replicate primer function. This shortcut causes more
repaint callbacks than almost anything else in residential painting.

**"One coat of BIN is enough"**
For serious stains (smoke, heavy nicotine), two coats minimum. Check by letting
first coat dry, wiping with damp cloth. If stain color comes back even slightly,
apply second coat. The stain will bleed through topcoat if it bleeds through primer.

**"Dewaxed shellac vs. regular shellac"**
SealCoat (Zinsser) and BIN are dewaxed. Amber shellac flakes dissolved in alcohol
contain wax → topcoats won't bond. For any application where topcoat follows: always
dewaxed. Waxed shellac is acceptable only as a final coat with no topcoat planned.

**"Oil-based primer + water-based topcoat = problem"**
No — this is a common and correct combination. Oil primer for adhesion/stain blocking,
latex topcoat for ease of use. The requirement is that the oil primer is fully cured
(24 hours minimum) before latex topcoat. The reverse — latex primer + oil topcoat —
is also fine. The chemistry issue is with glossy oil paint as a substrate for latex,
not with properly cured oil primer.

**"Primer tinted to match topcoat color"**
Valid practice — reduces topcoat coats needed for deep colors. Ask paint store to
add tint to primer matching approximately 50% of topcoat color. For dark topcoats,
gray-tinted primer outperforms white primer significantly.
