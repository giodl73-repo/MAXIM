# 03 — Wood Stains

## Penetrating vs Film-Forming, Chemistry, Grain Enhancement

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Why stain wood**: enhance grain figure, even out natural color variation, change color while preserving wood texture, provide UV protection; distinction from paint (which hides grain)
- **Penetrating stains** (no surface film): pigment or dye in oil/solvent carrier that soaks into wood; no film to peel/crack; maintenance requires reapplication penetrating into weathered wood; good for textured surfaces (rough-sawn, weathered decks); less UV protection than film-forming
  - Oil-based penetrating: linseed/tung/alkyd oil carries pigment into wood; slow dry; excellent penetration; traditional deck stain
  - Water-based penetrating: polymer-modified water carriers; faster dry, lower VOC; less penetration than oil
- **Film-forming stains** (surface film): semi-transparent (shows grain, adds some build) to solid (essentially paint); opaque/solid = maximum protection, no grain visible; semi-transparent = best of both; two-tone effect where end grain absorbs more pigment
- **Dye vs pigment stains**:
  - Dye stains: dye molecules dissolve in solvent, penetrate deep into wood fiber; even, transparent color; excellent for showing grain; susceptible to UV fading and bleeding (not for exterior without topcoat); water-based or NGR (non-grain-raising, alcohol-based — does not raise wood fibers)
  - Pigment stains: pigment particles sit in wood pores/valleys; more UV stable than dyes; slightly opaque quality; grain-raising when water-based; can clog tight-grained wood
  - Combination: most commercial stains use both
- **Gel stains**: very thick (thixotropic) stain that sits on surface without penetrating deeply; solves problem with blotch-prone woods (pine, cherry, birch — these have uneven grain density causing uneven dye/pigment absorption); gel stain "floats" on surface; more opaque, uniform appearance; less "in the wood" look; used with or without pre-conditioner
- **Wood conditioner/pre-conditioner**: thin, penetrating liquid applied before stain on blotch-prone species; partially fills pores to even absorption; reduces blotching effect; reduces overall stain color intensity
- **Deck stains**: exterior challenge is UV, water, foot traffic, mildew; water-repellency vs breathability balance; film-forming deck stains eventually peel, require stripping; penetrating deck stains don't peel but need more frequent reapplication; TWP (Total Wood Preservative), Defy, Armstrong Clark as common brands; mildewcide and UV inhibitor content key differentiators
- **Pickling/whitewashing**: diluted white paint wiped across wood, partially wiped off; pigment lodges in grain/pores; reveals grain in white; limed oak = traditional English finish; cerusing = wax-filled grain channels; Scandinavian/Nordic look with gray or white wash
- **Compatibility**: oil-based stains under oil or waterborne topcoat (with dry time); water-based stain under waterborne topcoat (water-based under oil = not recommended, oil may not penetrate over water-based film); test compatibility, especially with dye stains under film-forming topcoats (dye may bleed)
