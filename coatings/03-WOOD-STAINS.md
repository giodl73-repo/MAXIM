# Wood Stains

## Penetrating vs Film-Forming, Dyes vs Pigments, Blotch Control

## The Big Picture

```
PURPOSE OF STAINING WOOD:
  ENHANCE or CHANGE color while keeping wood texture visible
  (If hiding grain is acceptable: use paint)

STAIN SELECTION DEPENDS ON:
  1. Wood species (open-grain? blotch-prone?)
  2. Interior or exterior
  3. Penetrating (no film) vs film-forming (builds surface)
  4. Colorant type: dye vs pigment vs combination
  5. Whether clear topcoat will be applied

+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    STAIN DECISION TREE                           |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  Interior wood?             Exterior wood?                       |
|       ↓                           ↓                             |
|  Open grain?   Blotch-prone?   Deck/siding?   Fence/rough?      |
|  (oak/ash)     (pine/cherry)   ↓              ↓                 |
|       ↓             ↓        Film-forming   Penetrating          |
|  Dye works     Gel stain +   solid/semi    (oil-based,          |
|  well          conditioner   transparent   2-coat)              |
|                                                                  |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Penetrating Stains (No Surface Film)

### How They Work

```
  Oil carrier (linseed, tung, alkyd, mineral spirits, water)
  + pigment/dye
  Soaks into wood cell walls and pores
  Carrier evaporates/polymerizes in wood
  Pigment/dye trapped in wood fibers

  RESULT:
    Color: yes (from colorant)
    Film on surface: NO (no film to peel, crack, bubble)
    Texture: shows all wood texture, grain, and sanding scratches
    Protection: oil in wood = some UV and moisture resistance
    Maintenance: when worn, can apply more penetrating stain directly
                 (no film to remove or sand back through)

  WHY PENETRATING STAINS ARE USED:
    Rough-sawn wood (can't get film to adhere well to very rough surface)
    Log cabins, weathered decks (where film would trap moisture)
    When periodic maintenance by homeowner is expected
    Where film-forming stain has previously failed and peeled
```

### Oil-Based Penetrating

```
  FORMULATION:
    Linseed or tung oil + mineral spirits (petroleum solvent)
    Pigment ground into oil base
    Some: alkyd resin for better film-forming tendency (bridging
          toward semi-film)

  APPLICATION:
    Liberal wet coat → allow to penetrate (5-30 min)
    Wipe off excess (don't leave puddles — they form sticky residue)
    2nd coat after 24 hours if darker color desired
    Dry time: 24-48 hours before foot traffic or topcoat

  PROPERTIES:
    Deep penetration (oil penetrates before polymerizing)
    Rich, "wet" appearance
    Long dry time (vs water-based)
    Flammable during application (mineral spirits)
    High VOC

  BRANDS: TWP (Total Wood Preservative), Armstrong Clark,
          Messmer's, Penofin
```

### Water-Based Penetrating

```
  FORMULATION:
    Water + co-solvent (glycol ether)
    Acrylic resin (very small amount — doesn't form substantial film)
    Pigment dispersed in water

  APPLICATION:
    Apply, allow penetration, wipe off excess
    Faster dry time (1-2 hours)
    Lower VOC

  LIMITATIONS:
    Less penetration depth than oil-based (water doesn't penetrate
    as deeply as oil into wood cell walls)
    Raises grain slightly (first coat; sand before second)
    May need more frequent maintenance
```

## Film-Forming Stains

```
SEMI-TRANSPARENT STAIN:
  Shows grain and texture
  Thin film forms on surface
  Some hiding of imperfections
  Best of both: color + some protection
  Most popular choice for maintenance: when old film fails,
  requires stripping to solid/bare wood
  Deck stains at this level: 1-2 year reapplication cycle typical

SOLID STAIN:
  Essentially paint — full opacity
  No grain visible
  Maximum protection (thick film, UV block, water barrier)
  Peels like paint when film fails
  Hardest to maintain: stripping required before recoating
  Use when: wood condition is poor and you need to hide it
            + when longevity > appearance is priority

TRANSPARENT STAIN:
  Very thin film; maximum grain show
  Minimal protection
  Fastest to fail outdoors (UV hits wood directly)
  Best for: interior fine wood where showing grain is priority
            + topcoat with clear varnish for actual protection
```

## Dye Stains vs Pigment Stains

### Dye Stains: Into the Wood

```
  COLORANT: dye molecules dissolve molecularly in solvent
  Molecule size: ~0.5-2 nm (vs pigment particles: 100-1000 nm)

  PENETRATION:
    Dye molecules penetrate deep into wood cell walls
    (not just pores) → coloring within fiber

  VISUAL EFFECT:
    Very transparent — grain shows completely
    Even color tone across grain
    Excellent for exotic woods where grain is the feature
    End grain: absorbs dye deeper → darker (two-tone effect
    pronounced — can be managed with pre-conditioner or seal)

  TYPES:
    NGR (Non-Grain-Raising): alcohol/acetone-based solvent
      Does NOT raise wood fibers (unlike water-based)
      Very fast dry (minutes)
      Excellent clarity
      Less forgiving: lap marks appear if you go back over
      Professional woodworking standard for stain under topcoat

    Water-based dye:
      Raises grain (sand after first coat raises fibers; sand lightly
      with 220 before second application)
      More forgiving; lower VOC

    Oil-soluble dye:
      Dissolved in oil/mineral spirits
      Combined with oil stain products
      Moderate penetration

  LIMITATIONS:
    UV sensitivity: most dyes fade faster than pigments in sunlight
    → Always use UV-stable topcoat if sunlight exposure
    Bleeding: dye may bleed into topcoat if not fully bound
    Not for exterior without protective topcoat
```

### Pigment Stains: In the Pores

```
  COLORANT: pigment particles suspended in oil/water
  Particle size: 100-1000 nm (much larger than dye molecules)

  LOCATION:
    Pigment particles cannot enter cell walls (too large)
    → Sit in pores and surface valleys of wood
    → Larger pores collect more pigment → darker areas
    Open-grain wood (oak, ash, walnut): pores collect lots of pigment
    → beautiful effect (pores show darker, grain visible)

  VISUAL EFFECT:
    Slightly "obscuring" — not as crystal clear as dye
    Open-grain woods: excellent (pores collect pigment → figure shows)
    Tight-grain woods (maple, pine, cherry): pores are small/irregular
    → pigment collects where grain is soft (earlywood) but not where
       grain is hard (latewood) → uneven, blotchy result

  ADVANTAGES:
    More UV stable than dyes (pigment = opaque particles block UV)
    More exterior durability
    Does not bleed (particles don't migrate into topcoat)

  MOST COMMERCIAL STAINS:
    Combination of pigment + dye for:
    → Transparency of dye + UV stability of pigment
```

## Blotch-Prone Wood Species

```
  WHY BLOTCHING OCCURS:
    Some wood species have uneven density in grain pattern
    Earlywood (spring): soft, porous → absorbs more stain
    Latewood (summer): dense, hard → absorbs less stain
    CONTRAST: porous areas much darker than dense areas
    Result: splotchy, uneven, ugly

  BLOTCH-PRONE SPECIES:
    Pine, fir (dramatic earlywood/latewood contrast)
    Cherry (soft figure with dense hard grain adjacent)
    Birch, maple (tight uniform grain with occasional soft spots)
    Alder (similar to maple)
    Soft maple, poplar

  SOLUTIONS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1. WOOD CONDITIONER (Pre-conditioner)                     │
  │     Apply thin penetrating liquid before stain              │
  │     Partially fills pores → equalizes absorption           │
  │     Reduces blotching substantially                         │
  │     Reduces overall stain intensity (need stronger stain   │
  │     color to compensate)                                    │
  │     Minwax Pre-Stain Wood Conditioner, General Finishes    │
  │     Wait Time: apply stain within 15 min (oil) or 30 min   │
  │     (water-based) of conditioner — don't let dry fully     │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  2. GEL STAIN                                              │
  │     Very thick (thixotropic) stain — doesn't penetrate     │
  │     Sits on surface; uniform regardless of grain           │
  │     More opaque, less natural look                          │
  │     Excellent for: pine furniture, softwood cabinets       │
  │     Also: for cherry (natural blotching hidden)            │
  │     Brands: General Finishes Gel Stain, Old Masters        │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  3. WASHCOAT (THIN SHELLAC SEAL)                           │
  │     Apply 1-lb cut shellac or thin sealer before stain     │
  │     Partially seals pores → reduces penetration variation  │
  │     Then stain on top of washcoat                          │
  │     More even tone; reduced grain pop                      │
  ├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  4. PAINT (accept opacity)                                 │
  │     If blotching is unacceptable and transparency not      │
  │     required: paint hides everything                       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Exterior Deck and Siding Stains

```
  CHALLENGE STACK:
    UV radiation → degrades binders
    Moisture cycling → wood moves; film stains crack/peel
    Foot traffic (decks) → abrasion
    Mildew → colonizes surface

  PENETRATING VS FILM-FORMING FOR EXTERIOR:

  PENETRATING (best for horizontal, high-traffic decks):
    No film to peel
    Water vapor moves freely (breathes)
    Maintenance: apply more penetrating stain every 1-3 years
    Surface prep: clean + let dry (no stripping needed if same product)
    Best for: rough-sawn, weathered, older wood
    Key additives: UV inhibitors, mildewcide, water repellent

  SEMI-TRANSPARENT FILM-FORMING (siding, less-used decks):
    Thin film; shows grain
    More UV and weather protection than penetrating
    Fails by fading → re-apply over old (if cleaned well)
    1-3 year cycle for deck; 2-5 year for siding (vertical, less traffic)

  SOLID STAIN (problem wood, high protection):
    Full hiding; maximum protection
    Fails by peeling → stripping required before recoat
    Labor-intensive maintenance cycle
    Appropriate for: very weathered wood; large siding projects
                     where color consistency matters

  DECK STAIN BRANDS:
    TWP (Total Wood Preservative): penetrating; high oil content
    Armstrong Clark: penetrating; good UV stability; VOC-compliant
    Cabot Australian Timber Oil: semi-penetrating; rich look
    Defy Extreme: water-based semi-transparent; good modern formula
```

## Specialty Techniques

### Pickling and Whitewashing

```
  WHITEWASH:
    Original: diluted lime wash (Ca(OH)₂) applied to wood/masonry
    Modern interior: diluted white paint wiped across wood
    Wipe on → wipe off → white pigment lodges in pores/grain
    Result: pale wood with grain showing in white

  LIMING / CERUSING:
    Traditional English technique for oak
    Gray or white filler worked into grain of open-grain wood
    (oak, ash — requires open pores)
    Fill with wire brush (open/raise pores first)
    Apply liming wax or paste → work into grain → wipe off excess
    → White/gray in pores, natural wood color on surface
    Scandinavian/Nordic look; mid-century modern revival

  TECHNIQUE:
    Wire brush across grain → opens pores
    Work liming wax into pores
    Remove excess with burlap/rag
    Seal with clear wax or lacquer (prevents further contamination)
```

## Compatibility and Application Sequence

```
  KEY RULE: match solvent systems or allow complete drying

  SAFE SEQUENCES:
    Oil-based stain → oil-based varnish/polyurethane ✅
    Oil-based stain → water-based topcoat ✅ (after 24-48hr dry)
    Water-based dye stain → water-based topcoat ✅
    Water-based stain → oil-based topcoat ⚠️ (oil may not penetrate
                                              over water-based film;
                                              test adhesion first)
    Shellac → any stain or topcoat ✅ (shellac is universal sealer)

  DYE BLEED RISK:
    Some alcohol-soluble dyes can bleed into oil-based topcoats
    → Apply alcohol-based dye → seal with shellac (de-waxed)
       → then apply any topcoat
    The shellac locks the dye in place

  TANNIN REACTION:
    Oak and other tannin-rich woods: water-based products over fresh
    oak may cause gray/green discoloration from tannin reaction with
    iron in water
    Solution: use oil-based first coat; or seal with shellac

  WATER-GRAIN RAISE:
    First coat water-based stain or finish: wood fibers swell and raise
    Let dry completely → sand lightly with 220 grit (won't affect color much
    if stain is in wood) → smooth for next coat
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Best choice |
|---|---|
| Open-grain hardwood, rich color, interior | Oil-based wiping stain (pigment + dye combo) |
| Cherry or pine cabinet (blotch-prone) | Pre-conditioner + oil stain OR gel stain |
| Hardwood floor stain | Water-based NGR dye stain OR Rubio Monocoat type |
| Exterior deck (high traffic) | Oil-based penetrating (TWP, Armstrong Clark) |
| Exterior siding (vertical) | Semi-transparent oil-based or quality water-based |
| Rustic/weathered look | Solid or semi-solid for color + hide defects |
| Show all grain + deep rich color | NGR dye stain under clear topcoat |
| White/Scandi look | Liming wax or white wash + clear seal |

## Common Confusion Points

**Wiping off excess is essential, not optional**: Penetrating stains left on the surface don't dry properly — they become a sticky residue. The "penetrating" process means applying wet, letting penetrate, then wiping off ALL surface excess. The color that remains is in the wood, not on it.

**Gel stain is designed not to penetrate**: This sounds counterintuitive. Gel stain's high viscosity prevents deep penetration — it sits on the surface uniformly regardless of grain density. This is the feature, not a bug. Use it when you want even color on blotch-prone wood.

**Wood conditioner reduces color depth**: Pre-conditioner partially fills pores before stain goes on. This reduces blotching by evening absorption — but it also reduces how dark the stain gets. You may need to choose a darker stain color than usual to compensate for the conditioner's effect.

**"Oil-based stain" means the carrier is oil**: It doesn't mean the colorant is different from water-based stain. Pigments and dyes are similar; the carrier is what changes penetration, dry time, and solvent compatibility. Oil penetrates deeper and takes longer to dry.
