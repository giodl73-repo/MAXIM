# 05 — Industrial Coatings

## Powder Coating, Anodizing, Electroplating, Galvanizing, PVD

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Powder coating**: dry powder (thermoset or thermoplastic polymer + pigment + additives) applied electrostatically (part grounded, powder spray gun charges particles → electrostatic attraction), then cured in oven (typically 350-400°F, 20-30 min); crosslinking creates hard, durable film; no solvent → essentially zero VOC; uniform thickness, excellent edge coverage; limitations: requires conductive substrate, requires oven (no field application), minimum size/complexity; most exterior aluminum (windows, bicycle frames, fencing) is powder-coated; thermoset (epoxy, polyester, TGIC polyester, hybrid) vs thermoplastic (nylon, PVDF — more flexible, re-workable)
- **Anodizing** (aluminum only): electrochemical process — aluminum part = anode in sulfuric acid electrolyte; current causes controlled oxidation → aluminum oxide (Al₂O₃) layer grows from surface both outward and inward; hard, dense, porous structure; pores can be colored with dye then sealed; Type I (chromic acid, thin), Type II (sulfuric acid, standard — 0.0002"-0.001"), Type III (hard anodize — thicker, harder, abrasion resistant — piston rings, firearms); not a coating *on* aluminum — the aluminum *becomes* oxide; color options via dye absorption before sealing; architectural anodize (AAMA 611) standards for building products
- **Electroplating**: deposit metal onto substrate via electrolytic reduction; substrate = cathode, plating metal = anode (or inert anode with metal salt solution); current drives metal ions → reduced → deposit on part; applications: chrome (decorative + corrosion), nickel (hardness, corrosion), zinc (galvanic corrosion protection), gold (electronics, electrical contacts — non-corroding), silver (electronics, flatware), copper (conductive), tin (food cans, solder adhesion); adhesion requires proper preparation (clean, micro-etched surface)
- **Electroless plating**: autocatalytic deposition without electricity; chemistry controls reduction; uniform thickness over complex geometry (unlike electrolytic which builds more at high-current edges); electroless nickel critical for uniform coating of threads, interior holes; PTFE-composite electroless nickel for release properties (cookware)
- **Hot-dip galvanizing**: steel submerged in molten zinc (840°F); zinc metallurgically bonds — forms intermetallic layers (gamma/delta/zeta) plus outer pure zinc; coating thickness proportional to immersion time + steel composition; galvanic protection (zinc sacrificially corrodes to protect steel); much thicker and more durable than electrogalvanizing; structural steel, rebar, guardrails; inspection by magnetic thickness gauge; ASTM A123 standard
- **Electrogalvanizing**: thin zinc coating applied electrochemically; smooth, uniform; thinner than hot-dip; automotive steel (car bodies — then painted over for additional protection); ASTM A591
- **PVD (Physical Vapor Deposition)**: vacuum process; target material vaporized (by electron beam, sputtering, or thermal evaporation) → vapor deposits on substrate; decorative and functional; TiN (titanium nitride, gold color, hard — drill bits, cutting tools), TiAlN, CrN, DLC (diamond-like carbon); thin films (nanometers to microns); highest performance coatings for tooling; used for architectural hardware (PVD brass/gunmetal fixtures — scratch resistant), watchmaking
- **CVD (Chemical Vapor Deposition)**: reaction of gaseous precursors at surface; produces films not achievable with PVD; LPCVD, PECVD (plasma-enhanced); primarily semiconductor fabrication but also used for diamond coatings on cutting tools, thermal barrier coatings on turbine blades
- **Thermal spray**: molten/softened particles sprayed onto surface; flame spray, plasma spray, HVOF (high velocity oxy-fuel); ceramic thermal barrier coatings (TBC) on turbine blades (YSZ — yttria-stabilized zirconia); zinc/aluminum arc spray for corrosion protection on large structures; wear-resistant carbide coatings
- **Passivation**: forming protective oxide layer by chemical treatment; stainless steel passivation (nitric or citric acid removes free iron, promotes chromium oxide layer); titanium naturally passivates; important for medical devices and food processing equipment (ASTM A967)
