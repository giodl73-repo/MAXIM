# Adhesives

## CA Glue, Epoxy, Contact Cement, Hot Melt — Bond Mechanisms and Selection

## The Big Picture

```
ADHESIVE BOND MECHANISMS:

  ALL adhesives use one or more of:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  MECHANICAL INTERLOCKING:                                   │
  │    Adhesive flows into surface micro-pores and solidifies   │
  │    Physical anchor in substrate texture                      │
  │    Requires: wettable surface; porous enough for penetration│
  │                                                              │
  │  CHEMICAL BONDING:                                          │
  │    Covalent or ionic bonds with substrate surface atoms      │
  │    Reactive adhesives: epoxy, silane-based, some acrylics  │
  │    Strongest possible interface                              │
  │                                                              │
  │  PHYSICAL FORCES:                                           │
  │    van der Waals forces; electrostatic attraction           │
  │    Operate at molecular scale; act on all surfaces          │
  │    Maximized when: contact area maximized (flat, clean)     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  JOINT DESIGN DETERMINES FAILURE MODE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  LOADING          │ ADHESIVE STRENGTH                       │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Shear (parallel) │ STRONG — best loading mode              │
  │  Tension (pull)   │ Good — second best                      │
  │  Peel             │ WEAK — adhesive's worst mode            │
  │  Cleavage         │ Weak — opening wedge                    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Key design rule: design adhesive joints for shear loading;
  avoid peel. A flexible adhesive (polyurethane, PSA) handles
  peel better than a rigid one (epoxy, CA).
```

## Contact Cement

```
  CHEMISTRY: Polychloroprene (Neoprene) + solvent (typically
  naphtha or MEK for solvent-based; water for water-based)

  MECHANISM:
    BOTH surfaces coated with adhesive
    Solvent evaporates (or water evaporates for water-based)
    → Both surfaces become dry but reactive ("tacky")
    Surfaces brought into contact → INSTANT bond (on contact)
    Re-positioning after contact: IMPOSSIBLE
    No clamping required: bond forms at contact

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  APPLICATION SEQUENCE:                                      │
  │  1. Apply adhesive to BOTH surfaces                        │
  │  2. Allow solvent to flash off (~5-15 min)                  │
  │     Test: touch surface → no transfer to finger            │
  │  3. Align carefully BEFORE contact                          │
  │  4. Press together firmly                                   │
  │  5. Roll or hammer for full contact                         │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Laminate (Formica) bonding to countertop substrate:
      → Classic application; can't clamp → contact cement
    Rubber to metal bonding
    Leather to foam
    Large-area flexible substrate bonding

  SOLVENT vs WATER-BASED:
    Solvent-based (naphtha/MEK): stronger immediate tack;
    more aggressive bond; flammable during application;
    very high VOC; open flame hazard
    Water-based: lower VOC; safer; slightly less aggressive
    but adequate for most applications; longer flash time

  LIMITATIONS:
    Not for high-temperature exposure (softens)
    Not for continuous water immersion
    Not structural (peel resistance low)
```

## Cyanoacrylate (CA / Super Glue)

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    Monomer: ethyl cyanoacrylate (or methyl, butyl, octyl)
    Polymerization: anionic chain polymerization
    Initiator: trace moisture (water molecules on surface or in air)

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  REACTION:                                                  │
  │  Trace OH⁻ from surface moisture initiates chain           │
  │  → Monomer rapidly adds to growing chain                    │
  │  → Polymerizes to solid poly(ethyl cyanoacrylate)          │
  │  Time: seconds (smooth metal, glass) to minutes (wood)      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHY IT BONDS SKIN SO FAST:
    Skin: naturally slightly basic (pH 4.5-5.5 but higher
    moisture than wood)
    High moisture + amino acids on skin → rapid polymerization
    Medical tissue adhesive: octyl cyanoacrylate (Dermabond,
    Vetbond) — approved for skin closure (slower-setting,
    more flexible, less exothermic than ethyl CA)

  DEBONDING: acetone (dissolves the polymer)
  Or: patience (body oils break down skin bonds in hours)

  PRODUCT VARIANTS:
    Thin liquid: deeply penetrates micro-gaps; bonds tight joints
    Gel CA: thixotropic; stays on surface; gap-filling capability
    Rubber-toughened: modified for impact resistance;
    black color; less brittle than standard

  ACCELERATOR/ACTIVATOR:
    Spray on one surface → amine-based compound
    Immediately initiates polymerization on contact with CA
    on other surface → instant cure
    Useful for: gap-filling (polymerizes before sagging)
    or where moisture is inadequate

  LIMITATIONS:
    BRITTLE: poor impact resistance (the most important limitation)
    Rigid polymer with no flexibility
    Bond fractures under shock load, vibration, flex
    NOT UV stable (yellows; strength degrades in sunlight)
    NOT for sustained water immersion
    Not for polyethylene or polypropylene (non-polar; won't bond)
    Glass and metal: bonds well
    Wood: bonds well but brittle joint
```

## Two-Part Epoxy

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    Part A: epoxide resin (bisphenol-A diglycidyl ether typical)
    Part B: amine hardener (polyamine, polyamide, or cycloaliphatic)
    Mixed → exothermic crosslinking reaction

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CURE CHEMISTRY:                                            │
  │  Epoxide ring (strained 3-membered ring with oxygen)       │
  │  + Amine NH group → covalent bond (ring opens)             │
  │  Network grows → 3D crosslinked polymer matrix             │
  │  Exothermic: temperature rises during cure                 │
  │  More heat → faster cure (use for rapid cure)              │
  │  Cold: slows dramatically (use heat lamp to accelerate)    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MIX RATIO: CRITICAL
    Must be exact by weight or volume (depends on product)
    Off-ratio: one component in excess → unhardened sticky residue
    or under-hardened brittle joint
    "5-minute epoxy" in twin syringe: pre-metered equal volume
    (by design; always check if ratio is actually 1:1)
    Marine/structural: may be 2:1 or 5:1 — read TDS carefully

  CURE TIME vs STRENGTH:
    5-minute epoxy: handling strength fast; LOWER final strength
    than slow-cure formulations
    30-minute epoxy: intermediate
    Overnight cure: highest tensile/shear strength
    Full cure at room temp: ~7 days
    Post-cure at elevated temperature: higher Tg; better performance

  PROPERTIES (fully cured):
    Tensile strength: 3,000-7,000 PSI (varies by formulation)
    Shear strength: 2,000-4,000 PSI
    Excellent chemical resistance (after full cure)
    Bonds: metal, glass, stone, most plastics (not PE/PP)
    Gap filling: excellent (paste epoxy fills voids)

  FLEXIBLE/IMPACT-RESISTANT EPOXY:
    Rubber-toughened (CTBN = carboxyl-terminated butadiene nitrile
    added to epoxy) → reduced Tg; better impact resistance
    Trade-off: lower hardness, lower heat resistance
    Use for: vibrating structures, joints requiring flex

  LIMITATIONS:
    Not for PE, PP, PTFE (low surface energy → poor adhesion
    without special prep)
    Not for sustained UV exposure without UV-stabilized topcoat
    Two-component: pot life after mixing (4-60 minutes)
    Mixing and dispensing time required
```

## Polyurethane Adhesive (1-Part)

```
  CHEMISTRY: isocyanate-terminated prepolymer
  Cure: moisture-cure (reacts with water vapor in air and substrate)
  GORILLA GLUE = most recognizable brand (Gorilla Glue is PU)

  MECHANISM:
    Moisture activates cure; foam expansion
    Forms urethane linkages; exothermic
    Volume EXPANDS as it foams: 3-4× original volume

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  IMPORTANT: EXPANSION CAUSES PART MOVEMENT                 │
  │  Clamp or weight parts FIRMLY during cure                  │
  │  Foaming can shift unclamped parts out of alignment        │
  │  Pre-moisten one surface for better cure in dry conditions  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Wood to stone or concrete
    Mixed materials (wood + metal, wood + plastic)
    Gaps: foam fills voids (but see expansion warning)
    Outdoor applications: waterproof after cure

  LIMITATIONS:
    Foam expansion can be messy (overflows joint line)
    Brown color (uncured and excess foam)
    Lower shear strength than epoxy
    Foam can compress under load (not for rigid structural)

  REACTIVE HOT MELT (POLYURETHANE):
    PUR hot melt: applied hot (like regular hot melt) but
    also moisture-cures after cooling (crosslinks)
    Much stronger and waterproof than standard EVA hot melt
    Bond is permanent (not thermoplastic-reversible)
    Used in: furniture edge banding, construction assembly,
    bookbinding (premium), automotive assembly
```

## Hot Melt Adhesive (HMA)

```
  CHEMISTRY: Thermoplastic polymer (no crosslinking)
  Applied in molten state → instantly bonds as it cools
  Bond is reversible: reheat → adhesive re-melts → disassemble

  COMMON POLYMERS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  EVA (Ethylene Vinyl Acetate): most common "glue gun"       │
  │    Application temp: ~250-375°F; moderate bond strength    │
  │    Good: paper, cardboard, foam, wood, fabric               │
  │    Weakness: heat creep (joint softens in hot car, etc.)   │
  │                                                              │
  │  Polyamide (PA): higher temperature versions               │
  │    Bond strength: higher than EVA                           │
  │    Heat resistance: better than EVA                         │
  │    Used: automotive, electronics, industrial assembly       │
  │                                                              │
  │  Polyolefin (PO): bonds to polyolefin plastics (PE, PP)    │
  │    Specialty: bonds to notoriously difficult PE/PP substrates│
  │                                                              │
  │  Polyester (PET): highest temperature HMA                   │
  │    Industrial high-performance applications                 │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Consumer crafts, packaging (corrugated boxes, cartons)
    Bookbinding (spine adhesive on paperbacks)
    Furniture assembly (edge banding, back panel attachment)
    Electronics (component positioning before soldering)
    Foam bonding (upholstery, packaging inserts)

  INDUSTRIAL HOT MELT:
    Tank melter + pump + hose + applicator (not gun/stick)
    Precise temperature control; metered dispensing
    High speed: automotive assembly, packaging lines
```

## Anaerobic Adhesives (Loctite Family)

```
  CHEMISTRY: Single-component dimethacrylate monomer
  Cures ONLY when two conditions are SIMULTANEOUSLY met:
    1. Absence of oxygen (anaerobic environment)
    2. Contact with metal ions (Fe, Cu, etc.) as initiator

  MECHANISM:
    In the bottle: oxygen dissolves in monomer → inhibits cure
    → bottle stays liquid indefinitely
    In tight joint: oxygen purged → metal ions initiate
    → rapid free-radical polymerization → solid thermoset

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  LOCTITE COLOR CODE (THREADLOCKER):                         │
  │  Purple (222): low strength; for small screws; removable   │
  │  Blue (243):   medium strength; most common; hand-removable │
  │  Red (262/271): permanent; requires 500°F+ heat to break   │
  │  Green (290):   "wicking grade"; low viscosity; penetrates  │
  │                 assembled threads (post-assembly application)│
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Threadlocker: prevent fastener loosening from vibration
    (blue = removable for maintenance; red = permanent/never-loosen)
    Retaining compound (609, 638): secure bearings/bushings
    in housings; bearing OD in bore; fills gaps to 0.015"
    Gasket eliminator (518, 515): form-in-place gasket on
    flanged joints; replaces cut gasket
    Anaerobic on surfaces: thread locker only cures in
    contact zone; exposed film stays liquid → cannot be used
    as surface sealant on flat non-mating surfaces

  ACTIVATOR (7471):
    Primer applied to surfaces — provides metal ions for
    non-metallic applications (plastic, passive stainless)
    Without activator: anaerobics won't cure on plastic
    or passivated metal surfaces

  HEAT FOR REMOVAL (red Loctite):
    Apply heat (propane torch) to 500°F+ → polymer degrades
    → fastener can be turned
    Let cool before removing completely (hot seized thread)
```

## UV-Curable Adhesive

```
  CHEMISTRY: Acrylate or methacrylate monomer + photoinitiator
  Cure: UV or visible light breaks down photoinitiator
  → free radicals → polymerization → solid in seconds under UV

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  REQUIREMENT: at least one substrate must be UV-TRANSPARENT │
  │  Light must reach adhesive bondline to cure                │
  │  → Glass + anything: works (glass transmits UV)            │
  │  → Metal + metal: UV can't penetrate → won't cure          │
  │    (exception: UV cures from edges if joint is thin)       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Glass to glass: aquariums, glass furniture, display cases
    Glass to metal: optical instruments, display screens
    Clear plastic assemblies: electronics, optical devices
    Medical devices: UV-cured catheter bonding, tubing joins
    PCB conformal coating: UV-cured protective coating
    Dental: UV-cured composites, sealants, bonding agents

  ADVANTAGES:
    Instant cure on demand (only cure when you're ready)
    One-component (no mixing)
    Excellent precision dispensing (no pot life issues)
    Very fast: 5-30 seconds under UV lamp
    Consistent controlled cure (vs moisture/temperature-dependent)

  LIMITATIONS:
    Requires UV lamp (capital equipment)
    Not for opaque substrates (no path for UV)
    Possible yellowing over time (UV photoinitiators can degrade)
    Check "shadow cure" for areas UV can't reach:
    secondary moisture cure or dual-cure formulations exist
```

## Pressure-Sensitive Adhesives (PSA)

```
  VISCOELASTIC MATERIALS: always tacky; never dry

  BOND MECHANISM:
    Soft polymer (low Tg, below room temperature) flows and
    wets substrate under contact pressure alone
    No cure required — just press
    Bond strength increases with contact time and pressure

  PROPERTIES (unlike structural adhesives):
    Remains partially fluid at use temperature → creep over time
    Resistant to peel (some) but weak in sustained shear
    Removable (low-tack) or permanent (high-tack) depending
    on polymer composition and backing

  TYPES BY CHEMISTRY:
    Rubber-based PSA: SIS, SBS block copolymer + tackifier resin
      → Excellent initial tack; repositionable; ages faster (UV)
      → Masking tape, painter's tape, low-permanence labels
    Acrylic PSA: crosslinked polyacrylate
      → Higher temperature resistance; better UV stability
      → Permanent labels, mounting tape, medical tape, transfer tape
    Silicone PSA: for extreme temperature and chemical environments
      → Medical, industrial high-temp; expensive

  PRESSURE-SENSITIVE PRODUCTS:
    Masking tape: paper backing + rubber PSA (low tack, clean removal)
    Foam mounting tape (3M VHB = Very High Bond):
      Viscoelastic foam core + aggressive acrylic PSA both sides
      Outstanding gap-filling; high shear strength; vibration damping
      Replaces mechanical fasteners in automotive, signage, construction
    Transfer tape: PSA only, no backing; applied via liner
    Double-sided carpet tape: high tack rubber PSA
```

## Wood Glue (PVA)

```
  CHEMISTRY: Polyvinyl acetate emulsion (water-based)
  White liquid; water carrier evaporates → polymer chains contact

  REMARKABLE PROPERTY:
    On wood: glue line is STRONGER than the wood itself
    Joint failure = wood tear-out, not glue failure
    Requires: close-fitting joint (no gap filling)
    and adequate clamping pressure and time

  TYPES:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Type I (waterproof): ANSI/HPVA Type I — passes 3-cycle    │
  │  soak test; exterior use; Titebond III (most known)        │
  │                                                              │
  │  Type II (water resistant): ANSI Type II — passes cyclic   │
  │  moisture test; exterior with some protection; Titebond II │
  │                                                              │
  │  Type III (interior): Titebond Original (Titebond I);      │
  │  standard interior woodworking glue                        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CLAMP TIME vs CURE TIME:
    Clamp time (minimum to handle): 30-60 minutes
    Full cure: 24 hours
    Do not stress joint until full cure

  OPEN TIME:
    Standard PVA: 3-5 minutes (works in most joints)
    Extended open time versions: 10-15 minutes (for large glue-ups)
    If too much working time needed: polyurethane or hide glue
    Hide glue: traditional; reheatable; reversible (antique repair)

  GRAIN DIRECTION:
    Long grain to long grain: strongest; full fiber contact
    End grain: very weak (cross-grain gluing)
    End-grain gluing tip: first coat as sealer (let soak in),
    then apply second coat to glue → better bond on end grain
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Application | Best adhesive |
|---|---|
| Large area laminate (Formica to countertop) | Contact cement |
| Quick fix, rigid bond (metal, ceramic, glass) | CA gel (thicker) or 5-min epoxy |
| Structural joint, maximum strength | 2-part epoxy (30-min or overnight) |
| Mixed materials, gap-filling outdoors | 1-part polyurethane (Gorilla Glue) |
| Woodworking, close-fitting joint | PVA (Titebond II for moisture; III for waterproof) |
| Prevent bolt loosening, vibration | Loctite Blue 243 (removable) or Red (permanent) |
| Bearing pressed into housing | Loctite Retaining Compound (609 or 638) |
| Glass to glass display case | UV-curable (Loctite 349 or similar) |
| Components to PCB (temporary) | Hot melt EVA (low-temp) |
| Car trim panel to body (replaces screws) | 3M VHB foam mounting tape |

## Common Confusion Points

**CA glue is brittle — do not use for impact or flex joints**: The one thing cyanoacrylate cannot handle is shock loading. A well-bonded CA joint will fail suddenly under impact where a flexible adhesive would survive. Impact-resistant CA (toughened formulations) helps but doesn't fully solve this. Use epoxy or PU for anything subject to impact.

**Epoxy mix ratio is not always 1:1**: The classic twin-syringe "5-minute epoxy" is 1:1 by volume (by design of the syringe). But two-part marine, structural, and industrial epoxies may be 2:1, 4:1, or other ratios by weight. An off-ratio mix doesn't fully cure — one component remains excess and acts as a plasticizer, dramatically reducing strength.

**Contact cement must be dry before contact — this is the key step**: Apply to both surfaces, wait until no adhesive transfers to your finger (fully dried). If contacted while still wet: weak bond from solvent interference. The "open time" (window when tacky surfaces will bond) is 15-30 minutes after drying — not indefinitely.

**Anaerobic adhesives don't cure in open air or on non-metal**: Red Loctite on a flat aluminum surface does not cure on the air-exposed portions — only in the thread/joint gap. The exposed excess remains liquid and must be wiped off. Anaerobic = specifically designed for enclosed gaps between metal surfaces.

**PVA (wood glue) is not water-resistant unless specified**: Titebond Original (I) is interior only. Titebond II is water-resistant (passes Type II test). Titebond III is waterproof (Type I test). "White glue" (Elmer's School Glue) is PVA without woodworking-grade formulation — will dissolve in water. Specify the right type based on moisture exposure.
