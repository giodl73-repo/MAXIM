# 06 — Adhesives

## CA Glue, Epoxy, Contact Cement, Hot Melt — Bond Mechanisms and Selection

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Why adhesives bond: mechanical interlocking (adhesive flows into substrate micro-pores and solidifies), chemical bonding (covalent/ionic bonds with substrate surface), electrostatic/van der Waals forces; most adhesives use multiple mechanisms; joint design matters — adhesives are weakest in peel, strongest in shear/tension
- **Contact cement** (neoprene, solvent-based): both surfaces coated, solvent evaporates, surfaces pressed together → instant bond on contact; no clamping time needed; solvent flash-off critical; re-positioning impossible once contacted; classic for laminate (Formica) bonding; flammable solvent fumes; water-based alternatives (lower VOC, slightly less aggressive); good for large areas, flexible substrates
- **Cyanoacrylate (CA / super glue)**: ethyl/methyl cyanoacrylate monomer; bonds by anionic polymerization initiated by weak base — moisture on surface or in air; bonds skin (fingertips) faster than wood (wood slightly acidic); gap-filling variants (gel CA vs thin liquid); activators/accelerators speed cure; debonders (acetone); NOT impact resistant; brittle; used in forensics (fingerprint development — CA fuming); medical tissue adhesive (octyl cyanoacrylate — Dermabond); bond strength fastest to glass/metal, slower to wood
- **Two-part epoxy** (epoxide + amine hardener): mix ratio critical (by weight or volume, product-specific); exothermic cure; harder = slower cure, higher strength; flexible/impact-resistant formulations available; very high shear/tensile strength; excellent chemical resistance; bonds to nearly everything with surface prep; structural aerospace applications (composite layup); 5-minute epoxy vs 30-minute vs overnight — trade-off between convenience and strength; fully cured epoxy resists most solvents
- **Polyurethane adhesive** (Gorilla Glue type): 1-part, moisture-cure; moderate strength; expands as foams (fills gaps but can shift parts if not clamped); good for wood, stone, concrete; foam expansion requires weight/clamping; isocyanate chemistry; water activates cure
- **Hot melt adhesive** (HMA): thermoplastic polymer sticks (EVA — ethylene vinyl acetate most common, also polyolefin, polyamide, polyester); applied hot (~250°-375°F), instant bond on cooling; no mixing, no waiting; repositionable if reheated; lower strength than structural adhesives; excellent for packaging, crafts, light assembly; industrial hot melts for bookbinding, furniture edge banding (hot melt PUR = reactive hot melt — cross-links after cooling, much stronger, waterproof)
- **Anaerobic adhesives** (Loctite threadlocker, retaining compound, gasket maker): single-component; cure only in absence of oxygen AND in contact with metal ions (initiator); liquid stays in bottle indefinitely; cures in small, oxygen-deprived spaces (thread gaps, close-fitting parts); Loctite color code: 222 (purple, light) → 243 (blue, medium, removable) → 263 (red, permanent, requires heat to break); retaining compound for bearing fits, gasket eliminator for flanges
- **Structural acrylic**: two-component methacrylate; bonds metals, composites, plastics without surface prep (unlike epoxy); excellent impact resistance; used in automotive structural bonding (truck cab mounting, trailer assembly); MMA (methyl methacrylate adhesive); SEM-Lok and Lord Fusor brands
- **UV-curable adhesive**: liquid cures instantly under UV/visible light; used in electronics assembly, optics (glass bonding), medical devices; one substrate must be UV-transparent; precise application possible; Loctite 349 for glass bonding
- **Pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSA)**: viscoelastic — always tacky; Post-It notes (low tack), masking tape, duct tape, foam tape; acrylic (most permanent PSA) vs rubber (repositionable PSA); no cure, stays viscous; bond depends on pressure application
- **Wood glue (PVA)**: polyvinyl acetate emulsion; water-based; bonds wood fiber-to-fiber (glue line can be stronger than wood itself when properly applied); Type I (waterproof, ANSR), Type II (water resistant), Type III (interior); Titebond I/II/III; gap-filling not ideal (want close-fitting joints); clamp time vs full cure time
