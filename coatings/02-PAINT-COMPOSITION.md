# Paint Composition

## Binder, Pigment, Solvent, Additives — What's Actually in a Can

## The Big Picture: Four Components

```
PAINT = BINDER + PIGMENT + SOLVENT/CARRIER + ADDITIVES

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Component    │ Function              │ % by weight (typical)    │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Binder       │ Forms the film;       │ 20–40%                   │
│  (vehicle)    │ holds pigment         │                          │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Pigment      │ Color, opacity,       │ 10–30% (pigment only)    │
│               │ sometimes rust inhib. │ + extenders add ~20%     │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Solvent/     │ Enables application;  │ 30–60% (water-based)     │
│  Carrier      │ evaporates; sets VOC  │ 25–50% (oil-based)       │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Additives    │ Performance tuning:   │ <5% total, but essential │
│               │ thickening, biocides, │                          │
│               │ driers, coalescing    │                          │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

WHAT HAPPENS ON DRYING:
  1. Solvent evaporates (water or mineral spirits)
  2. Binder coalesces or crosslinks
  3. Result: solid polymer film containing pigment particles
```

## Binders: The Film Former

### Acrylic Latex (Water-Based)

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    Acrylic polymer: typically poly(methyl methacrylate) or
    poly(butyl acrylate) or copolymers thereof
    Emulsified in water as particles (~100-300 nm diameter)
    Coalescence mechanism on drying (see 00-OVERVIEW.md)

  "100% ACRYLIC" vs "VINYL-ACRYLIC":
    100% acrylic: all-acrylic emulsion binder
      → better UV stability, adhesion, flexibility, durability
      → required for quality exterior coatings
    Vinyl-acrylic (VAE): vinyl acetate copolymerized with acrylic
      → cheaper; adequate for interior; inferior for exterior
      → watch for in budget brands

  KEY PROPERTIES:
    MFFT (minimum film formation temperature): ~40-50°F for most latex
    Application below MFFT: chalky, non-coalesced, powdery film
    Dry time: 1-2 hours (vs 6-8 for alkyd)
    Recoat: 4 hours (dry to recoat) vs 16-24 hours (alkyd)
    Full cure: 2-4 weeks for maximum hardness and washability

  WATERBORNE ALKYD (hybrid):
    Alkyd resin emulsified in water
    Better leveling and penetration than straight latex
    Slower dry than latex, faster than oil alkyd
    Used for: premium trim paints marketed as "oil quality, water cleanup"
    Examples: Benjamin Moore Advance, Sherwin-Williams Emerald Urethane
```

### Alkyd (Oil-Based)

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    Polyester backbone + unsaturated fatty acids from drying oils
    "Alkyd" from "alcohol" + "acid" (polyol + dibasic acid + fatty acid)
    Linseed oil, soybean oil, tung oil, safflower oil
    Oil content: "long oil" (>60% oil) = more flexible, slower dry
                 "medium oil" (40-60%) = balanced
                 "short oil" (<40%) = faster dry, harder film

  OXIDATIVE CURE:
    Cures by oxygen absorption and crosslinking (hours to days)
    NOT by solvent evaporation (solvent just enables application)
    Continues crosslinking for months — film gets harder over time
    Oil-soaked rags: exothermic oxidation can cause spontaneous combustion
    → spread flat to dry or store in sealed metal container with water

  PROPERTIES:
    Superior leveling (self-levels from brush marks): smooth finish
    Better cold weather application (vs latex that fails to coalesce)
    Excellent adhesion to chalky surfaces, bare wood, old oil paint
    Yellows (especially in dark areas): oxygen-deprived areas don't cure
    Long dry time: 6-8 hours touch dry; 16-24 hours recoat
    Full cure: 30 days
    Solvent: mineral spirits → VOC; cleanup requires solvents

  WHERE ALKYD STILL WINS:
    Priming very old or chalky surfaces
    Painting in cold (below 50°F) when latex fails
    Kitchen cabinet finishes (traditional oil-based durability)
```

### Epoxy (Two-Component)

```
  CHEMISTRY:
    Part A: epoxy resin (bisphenol A diglycidyl ether, BADGE)
    Part B: amine or polyamide hardener
    Mix ratio CRITICAL: must be exact for proper crosslinking
    Pot life: 30 min to several hours after mixing

  MIXING:
    Read TDS (technical data sheet) for mix ratio by volume
    Mix THOROUGHLY: incomplete mixing = soft spots, tacky areas
    Start timer: pot life from first contact of components

  PROPERTIES:
    Chemical resistance: excellent (acids, bases, solvents)
    Hardness: very high
    Adhesion: excellent to metal, concrete
    UV stability: POOR — epoxy chalks rapidly outdoors
                  Top with polyurethane or aliphatic urethane
                  if UV exposure expected

  APPLICATIONS:
    Garage floor coating (epoxy floor paint)
    Tank lining, industrial process equipment
    Marine keel/hull coating (below waterline)
    Anti-corrosion primer on metal
    NOT for use outdoors as topcoat (UV chalking)
```

## Pigments: Color and Hiding Power

### Titanium Dioxide (TiO₂): The Dominant White

```
  WHY TiO₂ IS THE HIDING PIGMENT:
    Refractive index: rutile TiO₂ = 2.73 (vs glass = 1.5)
    High RI causes strong light scattering at particle surfaces
    Light scattering = "hiding" the substrate

  PARTICLE SIZE OPTIMIZATION:
    Maximum scattering at ~200-300 nm particle diameter
    (λ/2 rule — Mie scattering theory)
    Paint manufacturers optimize PSD (particle size distribution)
    for maximum hiding power per pound of TiO₂

  SUPPLY CHAIN SIGNIFICANCE:
    TiO₂ = most expensive raw material in most paints
    TiO₂ shortage (2010-2011, 2021): caused paint cost spikes
    Production: sulfate process (from ilmenite) or chloride process
    (from rutile) — chloride process = higher purity, cleaner
    Major producers: Chemours (formerly Du Pont), Tronox, Venator

  RUTILE vs ANATASE:
    Rutile: higher RI (2.73), better weathering, dominant form
    Anatase: RI 2.55, used in interior paint (photocatalytic activity
             in exterior → chalking)
```

### Color Pigments

```
  INORGANIC PIGMENTS:
    Iron oxides: Fe₂O₃ (red), Fe₃O₄ (black), FeO(OH) (yellow)
                 Excellent UV stability, opacity, lightfastness
                 Earth tones (red, yellow, brown, black)
    Chrome oxide: Cr₂O₃ → green; excellent lightfastness, heat-stable
    Cobalt blue, cobalt green: expensive; excellent stability
    Zinc white: ZnO; secondary white; fungicide property
    Zinc yellow (zinc chromate): anti-corrosion primer pigment
                                 (chromate = environmental concern)

  ORGANIC PIGMENTS:
    Phthalocyanine blue/green: brilliant blues and greens
                                Very good lightfastness; one of
                                most important modern pigments
    Quinacridone: red, violet; excellent lightfastness
    Diarylide yellow: transparent yellow; moderate lightfastness
    Carbon black: excellent opacity, tinting strength, lightfastness
    Perylene: high-performance red; automotive, industrial
```

### Extender Pigments (Not for Color or Hiding)

```
  CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate/chalk):
    Cheapest extender; imparts porosity to flat paints
    In high PVC: creates micro-voids → flat appearance
    Good for flat interior wall paint

  Talc (Mg₃Si₄O₁₀(OH)₂):
    Platelet structure → improves sag resistance on vertical surfaces
    Anti-settling in wet paint
    Some exterior durability benefit

  Kaolin clay:
    Flat paint; anti-settling; rheology modifier

  Barium sulfate (BaSO₄, blanc fixe):
    Dense; corrosion resistance; hard surfaces
    High-end industrial coatings

  Silica (SiO₂):
    Mar resistance; matting agent; anti-settling
    Engineered silica for controlled sheen in floor finishes
```

## Critical PVC Concept: Pigment Volume Concentration

```
  PVC = (Volume of pigment + extenders) / (Total dry film volume)
  CPVC = Critical PVC (the threshold)

  BELOW CPVC (PVC < CPVC):                ABOVE CPVC (PVC > CPVC):
  Binder fills all voids between          More pigment than binder can
  pigment particles                       fill; air voids in film
  ─────────────────────────────           ────────────────────────────
  Continuous binder matrix                Discontinuous binder; air voids
  Hard, impermeable film                  Porous film
  Washable/scrub resistant                Poor scrub resistance
  Higher gloss                            Flat (voids scatter light)
  Expensive (more binder)                 Cheaper (less binder)

  FLAT PAINT: PVC ≥ CPVC (~80-95% PVC)
    Microscopically porous → excellent hiding, matte
    Cannot scrub hard (voids = weak points)
  GLOSS PAINT: PVC << CPVC (~5-15% PVC)
    Dense binder matrix → hard, washable, glossy
```

## Sheen Levels: The Engineering Behind Appearance

```
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────┬──────────┬────────────────────────┐
  │  Sheen Level     │ 60° Gloss│ PVC %    │ Applications           │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────┼──────────┼────────────────────────┤
  │  Flat/Matte      │ 0–5      │ 85–95    │ Ceilings; hide texture │
  │  Matte           │ 6–14     │ 75–85    │ Low-traffic walls      │
  │  Eggshell        │ 15–30    │ 50–70    │ Living rooms, bedrooms │
  │  Satin           │ 30–50    │ 35–50    │ Hallways, main walls   │
  │  Semi-gloss      │ 50–70    │ 15–35    │ Kitchens, baths, trim  │
  │  Gloss           │ 70–85    │ 8–15     │ Doors, cabinets, trim  │
  │  High-gloss      │ 85+      │ 5–10     │ Furniture, marine      │
  └──────────────────┴──────────┴──────────┴────────────────────────┘

  PHYSICAL EXPLANATION OF GLOSS:
  Flat surface: light scattered in all directions → matte
  Smooth surface: light reflected at consistent angle → gloss
  Higher PVC: pigment particles protrude through surface → scatter
  Lower PVC: binder fills around pigment → smooth → gloss
```

## Additives: Small Amounts, Critical Functions

### Rheology Modifiers (Thickeners)

```
  PURPOSE: give paint pseudoplastic (shear-thinning) behavior
           Thick at rest → doesn't drip from brush/roller
           Thin when sheared → flows and levels under brush

  HEUR (Hydrophobically Modified Ethylene Oxide Urethane):
    Modern associative thickener
    Hydrophobic ends associate in solution → network → viscosity
    Applied shear breaks associations → thinning
    Better leveling than old cellulosic thickeners
    Less roller spattering

  HEC (Hydroxyethyl Cellulose):
    Cellulosic thickener; older technology
    Less pseudoplastic; more roller spatter
    Still used in lower-cost formulations
```

### Coalescing Agents

```
  PURPOSE: temporarily plasticize acrylic polymer particles
           → lower MFFT → allow film formation at lower temperature

  TEXANOL (2,2,4-trimethyl-1,3-pentanediol monoisobutyrate):
    Most common; VOC contributor (~150 g/L)
    Evaporates slowly after film forms
    "VOC exempt" alternatives: Eastman's DIDP, low-VOC coalescing agents

  WITHOUT COALESCENT: latex applied near MFFT → chalky film
  WITH EXCESS COALESCENT: prolonged soft film; tackiness
```

### Biocides

```
  IN-CAN BIOCIDES: prevent microbial growth in wet paint during storage
    Isothiazolinone compounds (MIT, BIT, OIT)
    Sensitization concerns → reducing use, reformulating
    "Zero preservative" paints: shelf life concerns → single season use

  DRY-FILM BIOCIDES (MILDEWCIDES): prevent mold/mildew growth
  on painted surface
    Critical for exterior and bathroom/kitchen applications
    IPBC (iodopropynyl butylcarbamate)
    Zinc pyrithione
    Triclosan (phasing out)
    Wet-room paints: higher biocide loading
```

### Driers (For Alkyd/Oil Paints)

```
  PURPOSE: catalyze oxidative crosslinking → faster cure

  Cobalt naphthenate: primary drier; surface drying
  Manganese naphthenate: through-dry
  Zirconium: through-dry; replacing lead driers
  Calcium: co-drier, improves penetration
  (Lead driers: historically used; now restricted due to toxicity)

  DRYER "COBALT BLUE" SMELL: characteristic odor of cobalt driers
  drying alkyd — harmless, temporary
  Skin formation in can: oxidation at surface; add film-preventing
  agent (anti-skinning agent) or press plastic wrap to surface
```

## Interior vs. Exterior Formulation

```
                      INTERIOR            EXTERIOR
                      ========            ========
  Primary failure     Wear, staining       UV degradation
  mode                                     Moisture cycling
                                           Temperature cycling

  UV stabilizers      Minimal              HALS (hindered amine
                                           light stabilizers)
                                           UV absorbers

  Flexibility         Lower               Higher (thermal expansion
                                          of substrate)

  Mildewcide          Moderate             High loading

  Blocking            Important            Less critical
  resistance          (stacked windows/
                      doors stick)

  Gloss retention     Less important       Critical for appearance
  (exterior)

  Dirt pickup         Less critical        Minimize: hydrophilic
  resistance                               polymers attract dirt
                                           hard binder resists it

  Example binder:     Soft acrylic latex   Rigid acrylic or
                      (flexibility for     hard acrylic for dirt
                      interior substrate)  resistance + UV stability
```

## Reading a Technical Data Sheet

```
  KEY FIELDS:
  Pot life:         For 2-part: working time after mixing
  Dry to touch:     ~30 min–2 hr (latex); 6–8 hr (alkyd)
  Dry to recoat:    ~4 hr (latex); 16–24 hr (alkyd)
  Full cure:        2–4 weeks (latex); 30 days (alkyd)
  Coverage:         Theoretical (sq ft/gal) at stated mil thickness
                    ACTUAL: ~75% of theoretical (rough surfaces, edges)
  Dry film thickness: Specified as "1 coat at X mils DFT"
                      1 mil = 0.001" = 25.4 μm
                      Interior wall: ~1.5 mils DFT typical
                      Anti-corrosion primer: 3–6 mils required
  VOC:              g/L (grams per liter of coating)
  ASTM tests cited: D2486 (scrub resistance), D3359 (adhesion cross-cut),
                    D4587 (QUV accelerated weathering)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Project | Binder | Sheen | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Interior wall | 100% acrylic latex | Eggshell/Satin | Self-priming on previously painted |
| Ceiling | Flat latex | Flat | Hide texture, no glare |
| Trim, doors | Waterborne alkyd or 100% acrylic | Semi-gloss or Gloss | Hard film, cleanable |
| Kitchen cabinets | Waterborne alkyd | Gloss/Semi-gloss | Benjamin Moore Advance, SW Emerald |
| Garage floor | 2-part epoxy | Floor coating | Roll-on 2-part; prime concrete first |
| Bare metal | Alkyd/epoxy primer | N/A | Primer only; topcoat separately |
| Exterior siding | 100% acrylic exterior | Satin/semi-gloss | HALS, mildewcide, UV stable |
| Bathroom | 100% acrylic | Semi-gloss | Mildewcide loading; anti-condensation |

## Common Confusion Points

**PVC (pigment volume concentration) ≠ PVC (polyvinyl chloride)**: These are two completely different things that share an acronym. In paint chemistry, PVC is the ratio of pigment particles to total dry film volume — a formula parameter. In plumbing/materials, PVC is a plastic polymer.

**"Self-priming" doesn't mean no prep**: Self-priming paints have higher solids and binder levels, suitable for previously-painted surfaces in good condition. On raw drywall, stained surfaces, bare wood, or metal, a dedicated primer is still required.

**More coats ≠ thicker film per coat**: Apply each coat at manufacturer-specified wet mil thickness. Two thin coats are better than one thick coat (thick coats sag, trap solvent, don't cure properly). Total DFT target = number of coats × single coat DFT.

**Full cure takes weeks, not hours**: Touch-dry (30 min) ≠ recoatable (4 hr) ≠ washable (1 week) ≠ fully cured (2-4 weeks). Don't scrub new paint before 2 weeks; don't wax floors before 30 days. The polymer network is still crosslinking.
