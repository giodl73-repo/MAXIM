**Why coatings exist**: Every exposed surface is under attack. Steel rusts (electrochemical oxidation). Wood rots (fungi + water). Concrete carbonates (CO₂ diffusion reaches rebar). Aluminum oxidizes (though its native oxide layer is actually protective). Coatings are engineered barriers sitting between substrate and environment.

## Film Formation: The Core Chemistry

All liquid coatings start wet and cure to solid film. The mechanism determines:
- Can you recoat after full cure?
- Is it reversible (strippable)?
- What are pot-life and recoat-time constraints?

```
FILM FORMATION MECHANISMS
┌─────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Mechanism       │ How it works                                      │
├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Evaporation     │ Solvent evaporates, polymer chains deposit        │
│                 │ Reversible: yes (re-dissolve with same solvent)   │
│                 │ Examples: lacquer, shellac, basic acrylics        │
├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Oxidative cure  │ O₂ crosslinks unsaturated fatty acid chains      │
│                 │ Reversible: no (crosslinked network)              │
│                 │ Examples: oil varnish, alkyd paint                │
│                 │ CAUTION: oil-soaked rags spontaneously combust    │
├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Coalescence     │ Latex particles fuse as water evaporates          │
│ (latex)         │ Requires T > MFFT (min film formation temp)       │
│                 │ Reversible: no                                    │
│                 │ Examples: latex/acrylic interior/exterior paint   │
├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Polymerization  │ Chemical reaction between mixed components        │
│ (2-part)        │ Irreversible covalent bonds; pot life constraint  │
│                 │ Examples: 2-part epoxy, 2-part polyurethane       │
├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Heat cure       │ Powder or liquid applied, then cured in oven      │
│                 │ Irreversible; highest performance                 │
│                 │ Examples: powder coating, e-coat (automotive)     │
└─────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Four Components of Paint

```
  PAINT = BINDER + PIGMENT + SOLVENT/CARRIER + ADDITIVES

  BINDER (film former):
    What holds the film together after solvent leaves
    Latex:   acrylic polymer emulsion (water-based)
    Alkyd:   synthetic resin from oil + acid + alcohol (oil-based)
    Epoxy:   2-component amine-cured (high performance)
    Urethane: isocyanate crosslinked (tough, flexible)

  PIGMENT (color + opacity):
    Hiding pigments: TiO₂ (titanium dioxide) — dominant white,
                     high refractive index, maximum hiding power
    Color pigments: inorganic (iron oxides, chromates) and
                    organic (phthalocyanines, quinacridones)
    Extender pigments: CaCO₃, talc, clay — reduce cost, affect
                       rheology, not hiding

  SOLVENT/CARRIER:
    Water (latex): evaporates; low VOC; slow dry in high humidity
    Mineral spirits (alkyd): evaporates; higher VOC; better
                             cold-weather flow
    Ketones/acetones (lacquer): fast evaporation; flammable

  ADDITIVES:
    Biocides (mildewcides), coalescing agents (lower MFFT),
    thickeners (rheology), UV stabilizers, driers (oxidative cure),
    defoamers, anti-skinning agents
```

## Sheen Levels: Function, Not Just Aesthetics

```
  FLAT (matte)     0–5°   gloss
    Hides surface imperfections best
    Lowest durability, hardest to wash
    Use: ceilings, low-traffic areas

  EGGSHELL        10–25°  gloss
    Slight sheen; more washable than flat
    Use: living rooms, bedrooms

  SATIN           25–35°  gloss
    Pearl-like sheen; good washability
    Use: most walls in active spaces

  SEMI-GLOSS      35–70°  gloss
    Notable sheen; hard, washable film
    Use: trim, doors, bathrooms, kitchens

  GLOSS/HI-GLOSS  70–85°  gloss
    Maximum sheen; hardest, most washable
    Highlights every surface imperfection
    Use: doors, cabinets, furniture

  Rule: Higher sheen = harder film = more washable = highlights defects
```

## Failure Mode Taxonomy

Most failures trace to **surface prep** (module 09), not product selection:

```
FAILURE                ROOT CAUSE                  PREVENTION
=======                ==========                  ==========
Peeling                Adhesion failure:           Proper prep, correct
                       contamination (oil/wax),    primer, dull glossy
                       wrong primer, over glossy   surfaces before painting

Blistering             Moisture under film;        Prime correctly; allow
                       painted in hot direct sun   substrate to dry; avoid
                                                   painting >90°F in sun

Cracking/checking      Film too rigid for          Use flexible topcoat on
                       substrate movement          flexible substrate; proper
                                                   film build (not too thick)

Rust bleed (steel)     Rust not fully removed;     SSPC-SP6 minimum; zinc
                       inadequate film build       primer on bare steel

Yellowing (alkyds)     Oil chemistry turns yellow  Use non-yellowing acrylics
                       especially in low-light     for white/light colors

Lap marks              Recoating over dried edge   Maintain wet edge; work
                                                   panel by panel; proper
                                                   temperature/humidity
```

## VOC Regulations

```
  VOC = volatile organic compounds — evaporate during application/cure
  React with NOₓ in atmosphere → ground-level ozone + smog
  Also: indoor air quality during application

  REGULATORY FRAMEWORK (US):
    EPA: national baseline limits by product category
    CARB: California Air Resources Board — most stringent, de facto
          national standard for manufacturers who want to sell in CA
    "CARB-compliant" paint: generally <50 g/L flat, <100 g/L gloss

  TREND: industry-wide shift to water-based (low-VOC)
  "Zero VOC" = typically <5 g/L
  "Low VOC" = typically <50 g/L for flat, <150 g/L for gloss
  Note: tints added at store counter can add 50–150 g/L — ask for low-VOC tint
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Primer is not diluted paint**: Primer is formulated for adhesion to substrate and chemical blocking (stains, tannins, pH). Topcoat is for durability and aesthetics. Using topcoat as primer or primer as a topcoat causes premature failure.

**"Dry to touch" ≠ "fully cured"**: An alkyd may be touch-dry in 4h, recoatable in 24h, but not reaching full hardness for 30 days. Full cure = polymer network fully crosslinked. Putting rugs on alkyd floors before full cure causes indent marks.

**Latex vs acrylic**: All acrylic paints are latex (water-based emulsion), but not all latex paints are 100% acrylic. "Vinyl-acrylic" is cheaper but less durable. "100% acrylic" or "pure acrylic" = higher quality binder, better adhesion and flexibility.

**Oil-based vs water-based**: "Oil-based" means alkyd binder, mineral spirits solvent. "Water-based" means acrylic latex, water carrier. Modern premium water-based formulations match or exceed oil-based in durability; they've largely replaced oil-based for interior use. Oil-based still dominates in some industrial applications.

**Spontaneous combustion is real**: Oil-based coatings are applied thin and cure by oxidation — the oxidation process generates heat. Oily rags (with linseed oil, teak oil, danish oil) bunched together can self-heat to ignition temperature within hours. Spread flat to dry, or store in metal container with water.
