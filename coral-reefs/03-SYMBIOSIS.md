# Symbiosis: Zooxanthellae, Photosynthesis, and the Bleaching Trigger

## The Big Picture

The coral-zooxanthellae partnership is one of the most consequential symbioses on Earth. It is a nutritional mutualism: the photosynthetic algae supply up to 90% of the coral's energy, and the coral provides a protected cell interior with CO₂ and dissolved inorganic nitrogen. The relationship is also catastrophically fragile — a few degrees of excess heat for a few weeks triggers its breakdown, which is the mechanism behind coral bleaching.

```
CORAL-ZOOXANTHELLAE ENERGY FLOW
=================================

SUNLIGHT
   |
   v
ZOOXANTHELLAE (in gastrodermal cells)
   |
   | Photosynthesis
   | 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
   |
   v
PHOTOSYNTHATE (mostly glycerol, glucose, amino acids)
   |
   | 90% translocated to coral host
   v
CORAL HOST
   |
   +→ Calcification (2–3× accelerated by light-enhanced rate)
   +→ Growth and reproduction
   +→ Mucus production
   +→ Respiration
   |
   | Returns to zooxanthellae:
   +→ CO₂ (photosynthesis substrate)
   +→ NH₄⁺ (nitrogen source from coral catabolism)
   +→ PO₄³⁻ (phosphorus source)
   +→ Protected intracellular environment

Result: Zooxanthellae are ~10× more productive than free-living
        algae of the same type (protected, nutrient-supplied)
```

## Symbiodiniaceae — The Zooxanthellae

"Zooxanthellae" is not a species — it's a colloquial term for a diverse guild of dinoflagellate algae in family Symbiodiniaceae, formerly classified as a single genus *Symbiodinium*, now split into multiple genera.

```
SYMBIODINIACEAE CLASSIFICATION
================================

Family: Symbiodiniaceae
Phylum: Dinoflagellata

GENUS (former clade)   CHARACTERISTICS              THERMAL TOLERANCE
---------------------  -------------------------    -----------------
Symbiodinium (A)       Opportunistic; early         Moderate-high
                       succession; stress-tolerant
Breviolum (B)          Caribbean dominant;          Lower
                       Gorgonian specialist
Cladocopium (C)        Most diverse; Indo-Pacific   Variable (C1 lower;
                       dominant; many subclades     D1a-like higher)
Durusdinium (D)        Thermotolerant; "stress      HIGH — key for
  (formerly D1a)       tolerant" clade; often       bleaching resistance
                       in post-bleaching corals
Effrenium (E)          Less common; open water      Unknown
Fugacium (F)           Less common; diverse         Unknown

Note: D (Durusdinium) was formerly "D1a" or "clade D" and is the
primary thermotolerant clade; corals bearing it bleach less severely
```

**Clade switching**: Under heat stress, some corals expel C and reacquire D (more thermotolerant). This "shuffling" offers a potential acclimation mechanism but is not universal and may come with fitness trade-offs (lower photosynthetic efficiency under normal conditions).

## The Photosynthesis Machinery

Zooxanthellae are functionally just photoautotrophs living inside animal cells. The unusual aspect is the intracellular location and the degree of metabolite export.

```
ZOOXANTHELLAE CELL BIOLOGY
===========================

  HOST GASTRODERMAL CELL
  +--------------------------------+
  | ZOOXANTHELLA (in vacuole)      |
  |  +----------------------------+|
  |  | Chloroplast (peridinin-    ||   ← Peridinin is the
  |  | chlorophyll a complex)     ||     dinoflagellate
  |  | captures 400–700nm light   ||     accessory pigment
  |  |                            ||     (gives brown color)
  |  | Carbon fixation via        ||
  |  | Calvin cycle               ||
  |  +----------------------------+|
  |                                |
  | Vacuole (symbiosome):          |
  |   - Isolates zooxanthella      |
  |   - Controls nutrient exchange |
  |   - Prevents lysosome fusion   |
  |     (zooxanthella evades       |
  |      normal digestion)         |
  +--------------------------------+
```

**Carbon export mechanism**: Zooxanthellae translocate photosynthate as:
- Glycerol (~50–60%)
- Glucose
- Alanine and other amino acids
- Lipids (under some conditions)

Host cells absorb these directly. This metabolic intimacy is why the symbiosis is so tightly coupled — and why its breakdown is so acute.

## Bleaching: Mechanism

The bleaching cascade is now well understood at the molecular level:

```
BLEACHING CASCADE
==================

STEP 1: THERMAL STRESS
  SST exceeds bleaching threshold (1°C above mean summer maximum)
  Duration matters as much as magnitude (see DHW in 05-BLEACHING.md)

STEP 2: PHOTOINHIBITION IN ZOOXANTHELLA
  Excess heat disrupts photosystem II (PSII)
  PSII damage → electron transport chain backs up
  Light energy cannot be safely processed
                |
                v
STEP 3: REACTIVE OXYGEN SPECIES (ROS) PRODUCTION
  Backed-up electrons react with O₂ → superoxide radical (O₂⁻)
  O₂⁻ → H₂O₂ (hydrogen peroxide) → OH• (hydroxyl radical)
  ROS diffuse from zooxanthella into host cell
                |
                v
STEP 4: HOST CELL OXIDATIVE DAMAGE
  ROS attack host cell membranes, proteins, DNA
  Host cell activates stress response
                |
                v
STEP 5: ZOOXANTHELLA EXPULSION
  Multiple mechanisms simultaneously:
  a) Host cell exocytosis (active ejection)
  b) Apoptosis (programmed cell death) — cell + symbiont lost
  c) Necrosis (uncontrolled death) — severe stress
  d) Zooxanthella mitotic arrest and gradual loss
                |
                v
RESULT: CORAL TURNS WHITE
  Without brown zooxanthellae, white aragonite skeleton visible
  through transparent tissue
  (NOTE: coral is still alive — just starving)
```

## The Color of Coral — and of Bleached Coral

```
COLOR SOURCES IN HEALTHY vs. BLEACHED CORAL
=============================================

HEALTHY CORAL:
  Color = f(zooxanthellae concentration, host pigments)

  Brown/tan: zooxanthellae (chlorophyll + peridinin) dominate
  Bright blue/green/purple/red: host fluorescent proteins (GFP-like)
  Yellow: some zooxanthellae strains + host pigments
  Pink/red: beta-carotene and host chromoproteins

BLEACHED CORAL:
  Zooxanthellae expelled → brown/yellow pigments gone
  White: reflected light from white aragonite skeleton
         through now-transparent tissue
  Pale: partial bleaching (some zooxanthellae remain)
  Fluorescent colors: HOST FLUORESCENT PROTEINS REMAIN
    → bleached coral sometimes fluoresces blue/green
    → fluorescent proteins may be photoprotective (ROS scavengers)

DEAD CORAL (post-bleaching mortality):
  Tissue sloughs off → bare white skeleton
  Rapidly colonized by turf algae → green/brown
  CCA colonization → pink/purple encrusting layer
```

## Nitrogen Cycling in the Symbiosis

Often overlooked but critical to understanding why reefs thrive in nutrient-poor "blue desert" ocean water:

```
NITROGEN CYCLE IN CORAL-ZOOXANTHELLAE SYSTEM
==============================================

Dissolved Inorganic Nitrogen (DIN) in oligotrophic ocean:
  <1 μmol/L NH₄⁺ in reef water — extremely low

ZOOXANTHELLAE:
  - Fix DIN from seawater (directly absorb NH₄⁺, NO₃⁻)
  - Incorporate N into amino acids, proteins
  - Transfer N-containing compounds to host

CORAL HOST:
  - Metabolizes proteins → generates NH₄⁺ waste
  - Normally would excrete NH₄⁺
  - INSTEAD: zooxanthellae capture host's metabolic waste N
  - "Nitrogen recycling loop" — host's waste is algae's input

Result: Tight nitrogen recycling allows high productivity on
        near-zero external nutrient inputs

DISRUPTION: High external N (sewage, runoff, fertilizer)
  → favors free-living algae over zooxanthellae
  → algae overgrow corals (phase shift to algal reef)
  → see 08-HUMAN-IMPACTS.md
```

## Clade Thermotolerance — Practical Consequences

```
THERMOTOLERANCE RANKING
=========================

LOWER TOLERANCE          HIGHER TOLERANCE
(bleach sooner)          (bleach later)
     |                        |
     v                        v
  Clade B                  Clade D
  (Breviolum)              (Durusdinium)
     |                        |
  Clade C                  Clade A
  (Cladocopium C1)         (Symbiodinium)
     |
  Clade C (most subclades)

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:
- Corals with D clade bleach at higher temperatures
- D clade photosynthetically less efficient at normal temps
  (trade-off: thermotolerance costs ~10% productivity)
- Some corals can shuffle from C to D during bleaching events
  (not all; genetic predisposition matters)
- D clade distribution has INCREASED on GBR since 1998 bleaching
  (shift toward more thermotolerant community — natural selection)
- Restoration programs: inoculating lab-reared corals with D clade
  before outplanting ("symbiont manipulation" — see 09-RESTORATION.md)
```

## Other Coral Symbionts

Zooxanthellae are the best-known but not the only coral symbionts:

| Symbiont | Location | Function |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Zooxanthellae (Symbiodiniaceae) | Gastrodermal cells | Photosynthesis; 90% of energy |
| Bacteria (coral microbiome) | Surface mucus, skeleton | N₂ fixation; antibiotic production; pathogen defense |
| Endolithic algae | Within skeleton | Secondary photosynthesis in dead skeletal zones |
| Archaea | Mucus layer | Nitrogen cycling |
| Fungi | Skeleton | Unknown; possibly pathogenic or neutral |
| Viruses | Tissue, zooxanthellae | Some pathogenic; some may be beneficial |

The "coral holobiont" concept: the coral + all its associated microorganisms function as a single ecological unit. Disruption of any component can destabilize the whole system.

## Common Confusion Points

**Zooxanthellae are not plants.** They are dinoflagellates — protists — that happen to be photosynthetic. They have two flagella, a complex nucleus, and retained evolutionary characteristics of their heterotrophic ancestors.

**Bleaching is not death, but it is a crisis.** A bleached coral is like a person who has lost their primary food source. They can survive for weeks to months on heterotrophic feeding (catching zooplankton), but at 1/10 of their normal energy budget. Prolonged bleaching (>4 weeks) without temperature relief leads to starvation-induced tissue loss and mortality.

**Clade D is not universally "better."** Thermotolerant does not mean optimal. D clade symbioses tend to be less efficient photosynthetically, which limits calcification under normal conditions. Coral communities that have shifted entirely to D clade after bleaching events show slower growth rates and reduced structural complexity.

**The ROS hypothesis is the dominant but not only bleaching mechanism.** Multiple pathways are active simultaneously: lipid peroxidation, protein denaturation, and direct damage to the zooxanthellae's photosynthetic membranes. The cascade as drawn above is a simplification of a genuinely complex cellular stress response.

**Symbiodiniaceae live free in seawater too.** The "free-living" Symbiodiniaceae in reef water serve as the inoculum for newly settled polyps acquiring their first symbionts. Larvae of many species are born without zooxanthellae (aposymbiotic) and must acquire them environmentally. How they select the right clade is a major research question.
