# Coral Biology: Polyp Anatomy, Skeleton, Spawning, Growth

## The Big Picture

Coral is an animal — phylum Cnidaria, class Anthozoa. Each coral "head" is a colony of genetically identical polyps sharing a basal tissue layer (coenosarc) and a communal skeleton. The polyp is a simple sac-like animal but its capacity to precision-engineer an aragonite skeleton, maintain an endosymbiosis, and coordinate colonial growth makes it the architect of the ocean's most structurally complex habitat.

```
CORAL ORGANIZATIONAL HIERARCHY
================================

  INDIVIDUAL POLYP (mm scale)
  +----------------------------------+
  | Tentacles (nematocyst-bearing)   |
  | Oral disc + mouth                |
  | Gastrovascular cavity            |
  | Mesenteries + mesenterial fil.   |
  | Calicoblastic epithelium         |
  | Aragonite corallite (cup)        |
  +----------------------------------+
    Calicoblastic epithelium secretes the aragonite skeleton.
           |  connected by coenosarc
           |  (shared body tissue)
           v
  COLONY (cm to m scale)
  +--------------------------------------+
  | Hundreds to millions of polyps       |
  | Interconnected gastrovascular system |
  | Shared aragonite skeleton (corallum) |
  | Common zooxanthellae population      |
  +--------------------------------------+
           |  reproduces by
           v
  POPULATION (reef scale)
  Multiple colonies; genetic variety through sexual reproduction
```

## Polyp Anatomy

```
CORAL POLYP — CROSS SECTION
=============================

  Top to bottom (apex to base of polyp):

    Oral disc with MOUTH (surrounded by tentacles in rings).
      Tentacles bear nematocysts (stinging cells).
    Pharynx descends into the body cavity.

    Body wall encloses the GASTROVASCULAR CAVITY.
      Mesenteries: vertical partitions inside the cavity.
      Mesenterial filaments hang from the mesenteries
        and digest prey extra-orally.

    Basal disc / coenosarc connects polyps in a colony.

    Calicoblastic epithelium (key: secretes the skeleton).

  Skeletal CUP (aragonite corallite) sits beneath the polyp:
    Septa: radial partitions.
    Columella: central pillar.
    Costae: external ridges.
```

**Nematocysts**: Cnidocytes containing coiled tubules that evert explosively (one of fastest cellular events in biology — microseconds) to inject venom into prey or predators. Triggered by chemical + mechanical stimuli. Primary prey capture mechanism; tentacles retract prey into mouth.

**Mesenterial filaments**: Extruded through the mouth or body wall; secrete digestive enzymes directly onto captured prey (extra-oral digestion), and also onto competing organisms (coral warfare — allelopathy).

**Coenosarc**: The thin tissue layer connecting all polyps in a colony across the skeleton surface. Allows nutrient sharing and chemical communication colony-wide. Zooxanthellae reside in the gastrodermal cells of both the polyp body and the coenosarc.

## The Skeleton

```
CORALLITE ANATOMY
==================

Top view (one polyp's cup):

         N
    ___________
   /    septa   \
  |  (12 primary |
  |   radiating  |   W ──── center ──── E
  |   partitions)|
   \___________/
         S
   + columella in center (may be solid or absent by genus)

Side cross-section showing growth:

  ___________  ← current living surface
  ___________  ← previous growth band (annual?)
  ___________
  ___________  ← older skeleton (may be many decades)
  (skeleton continues down)

Growth increments visible in X-radiograph:
  High-density band = winter/slow growth
  Low-density band  = summer/fast growth
  Analogous to tree rings for paleoclimate
```

**Aragonite microstructure**: Skeleton is not amorphous calcium carbonate. Organized into:
- **Rapid Accretion Deposits (RAD)**: fasciculi of aragonite needles; faster but less organized
- **Thickening deposits**: denser, slower; fill in between RAD framework

This two-component system was discovered by electron microscopy — the skeleton is a precision biological material, not geologic precipitation.

## Reproduction

Two reproductive modes, both critical to reef ecology:

```
ASEXUAL vs. SEXUAL REPRODUCTION
=================================

ASEXUAL (clonal growth and fragmentation)
+------------------------------------------+
| Budding         | New polyps bud from    |
|                 | parent; colony expands |
| Fragmentation   | Storm breaks branches; |
|                 | fragments settle and   |
|                 | recruit (fast recovery)|
| Bail-out        | Stressed polyps detach,|
| (rare)          | float, settle elsewhere|
+------------------------------------------+
→ Maintains genetic diversity within colony = 0
→ Reef resilience via rapid recolonization of
  disturbed substrate

SEXUAL (broadcast spawning or brooding)
+------------------------------------------+
| Broadcast       | Gametes released into  |
| Spawning        | water column; external |
|                 | fertilization          |
| Brooding        | Internal fertilization;|
|                 | planula larvae released|
+------------------------------------------+
→ Creates genetic diversity
→ Enables long-distance dispersal
→ Key for reef recovery after large disturbances
```

### Broadcast Spawning

The majority of Indo-Pacific reef-building corals are broadcast spawners. The most dramatic example:

```
GREAT BARRIER REEF MASS SPAWNING
==================================

Trigger:  Rising water temperature + full moon (October–December)
          Lunar cycle synchronizes spawning across 1,500 km of reef

Event:    Corals simultaneously release gamete bundles
          (eggs + sperm bound together, buoyant)
          Bundles rise to surface, disaggregate
          Cross-fertilization occurs in slick

Scale:    Hundreds of millions of bundles per km²
          "Blizzard in reverse" — visible from satellite
          Slicks extend for kilometers

After 24h: Fertilized eggs develop into ciliated planula larvae
After days–weeks: Larvae settle on CCA substrate
                  Metamorphose into primary polyp
                  Begin secreting skeleton
```

**Brooding corals** (e.g., *Pocillopora* species, many Caribbean corals): fertilize eggs internally; release competent planula larvae that settle quickly (hours to days). Less dispersal than broadcast spawners but higher survival rate of settled larvae.

**Age records**:
- Porites heads >5 m diameter on GBR estimated at 700–1,000 years
- Lophelia pertusa (cold-water ahermatypic): ~1 mm/yr; some mounds 8,000+ years old
- Colony age ≠ reef age; the limestone reef structure long outlasts individual colonies

**Size and colony age don't always track**: Fragmentation can produce large colonies that are genetically young (just a fragment that regrew). Molecular clock methods and skeletal banding together establish true age.

## Tissue and Cell Biology

```
TISSUE LAYERS
==============

EPIDERMIS (outer body wall)
  - Cnidocytes (nematocysts)
  - Mucus-secreting cells (key: mucus traps particles, excludes sediment)
  - Mucocytes produce copious mucus (up to 5g C m⁻² day⁻¹)

MESOGLEA (jelly layer, thin in corals)

GASTRODERMIS (inner lining of gastrovascular cavity)
  - ZOOXANTHELLAE RESIDE HERE (in gastrodermal cells = zooxanthellae)
  - Absorption cells for digested nutrients
  - Flagellated cells move water

CALICOBLASTIC EPITHELIUM (below basal disc)
  - Specialized secretory cells
  - Tight junctions isolate calcifying fluid from seawater
  - Ion pumps: Ca²⁺-ATPase concentrates Ca²⁺
  - Maintains calcifying fluid pH ~8.5–9 (vs. seawater ~8.1)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Coral is not a plant.** The green or brown color comes from zooxanthellae inside the coral's cells. The coral itself is an animal — a predator that catches zooplankton with stinging tentacles at night.

**A coral "head" is thousands of animals.** Each polyp is a separate individual. They are clonal (genetically identical), connected by coenosarc tissue, and share nutrients via the gastrovascular system. The head grows by polyp budding.

**Bleaching ≠ polyp death (immediately).** See 03-SYMBIOSIS.md. Bleaching = expulsion of zooxanthellae. Polyps remain alive and can re-acquire zooxanthellae if stress ends quickly.

**Mass spawning synchrony is astonishing.** Corals have no nervous system beyond their own polyp body. Yet millions of colonies across the GBR release gametes within the same 1–2 hour window on the same night. The synchronizing cue is lunar cycle + temperature + possibly light spectrum. The adaptive logic: simultaneous release overwhelms predators (predator satiation) and maximizes fertilization probability.

**Fragmentation both creates and destroys.** Storms fragment branching corals — this kills some individuals but the fragments that land on suitable substrate regrow into new colonies (clonal propagation at no genetic cost to the organism). Fragmentation drives rapid recovery of branching coral fields after disturbance. Restoration programs exploit this mechanism deliberately (see 09-RESTORATION.md).
