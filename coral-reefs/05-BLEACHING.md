# Bleaching: Thermal Stress, Global Events, Recovery and Mortality

## The Big Picture

Bleaching is the visible symptom of symbiosis breakdown under thermal stress. The Degree Heating Week (DHW) metric translates cumulative heat exposure into a predictive bleaching signal — the closest thing reef science has to a thermometer for coral stress.

```
BLEACHING SEVERITY vs. DHW
============================

DHW (°C-weeks)    Typical Response
--------------    --------------------------
0–1               No bleaching expected
1–2               Watch: occasional bleaching possible
2–4               ALERT 1: Some bleaching (20–30% of susceptible corals)
4–8               ALERT 2: Widespread bleaching, partial mortality expected
8+                Severe bleaching; significant mortality expected
12+               Mass mortality; structural reef framework collapse

DHW = cumulative sum of weekly thermal anomalies exceeding 1°C
      above Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM) SST

Example calculation:
  Week 1: SST = MMM + 1.5°C → contributes 1.5°C-week
  Week 2: SST = MMM + 2.0°C → contributes 2.0°C-week
  Week 3: SST = MMM + 0.5°C → contributes 0°C-week (below threshold)
  DHW after 3 weeks = 3.5°C-weeks → Alert 1 level
```

## The Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM) Baseline

```
DHW CALCULATION FRAMEWORK
===========================

NOAA Coral Reef Watch calculates MMM for every reef pixel globally:
  MMM = mean SST of the warmest month averaged over 1985–2012 baseline

BLEACHING THRESHOLD = MMM + 1°C
  (corals are adapted to local max; 1°C above that is the stress trigger)

KEY INSIGHT: A reef in cool water (e.g., 26°C MMM) bleaches at 27°C
             A reef in warm water (e.g., 30°C MMM) bleaches at 31°C
             The threshold is relative, not absolute

PRODUCTS:
  SST Anomaly:   current SST - climatological SST
  Hot Spots:     current SST - MMM (positive = thermal stress)
  DHW:           4-month rolling cumulative sum of positive hot spots ≥1°C

NOAA CRW updates daily at 5km resolution globally
```

## Major Bleaching Events

### 1997–1998: First Global Bleaching Event

The El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) event of 1997–98 was the most intense since record-keeping. Sea surface temperatures exceeded bleaching thresholds across the entire Indo-Pacific simultaneously.

```
1997–98 GLOBAL BLEACHING
==========================

Geographic extent:  Indian Ocean, Southeast Asia, Pacific, Caribbean
  - Indian Ocean: 90% of reefs bleached; 46% coral mortality in some areas
  - Maldives: >90% bleaching; up to 73% mortality
  - Kenya coast: 50–80% mortality
  - GBR: Affected but less severely than Indian Ocean
  - Caribbean: Partial; worst in Florida Keys (50% mortality in some areas)

Cause: 1997–98 El Niño (one of strongest on record)
       + background warming trend adding ~0.2°C to baseline

Significance: First recognized GLOBAL bleaching event
              Demonstrated interconnected nature of reef crisis

Recovery: Partial recovery on many reefs by 2002–2005
          Some Indian Ocean reefs took 10–15 years
          Indian Ocean reefs showed genetic selection for
          thermotolerance in post-bleaching coral populations
```

### 2010: Second Global Bleaching Event

Weaker than 1997–98 but still significant. Indian Ocean and Caribbean most affected. First widespread Caribbean bleaching affecting previously-healthy reefs.

### 2015–2017: Third (and Most Severe) Global Bleaching Event

```
2015–2017 GLOBAL BLEACHING
============================

Duration:   36 consecutive months of elevated bleaching alerts
Trigger:    2015–16 El Niño (second most intense on record)
            + accelerating anthropogenic warming baseline
            → SST anomalies unprecedented in instrument record

Geographic extent:
  - 75% of global reefs exposed to bleaching-level heat stress
  - 30% of reefs exposed to MORTALITY-level heat stress
  - First major bleaching in Hawaiian Islands (2015)
  - First mass bleaching of northern Great Barrier Reef

Great Barrier Reef — 2016–2017 specifically:
  2016: Northern GBR — 93% of survey sites bleached
         67% of corals on northern GBR died in 2016 alone
         (worst northern GBR bleaching in recorded history)
  2017: Central GBR — second bleaching before northern recovered
         "Back-to-back" bleaching — unprecedented

  2020: Fourth bleaching event; worst ever in EXTENT
        (but temperature slightly lower than 2016 peak;
         overall: 50% coral cover loss 1995–2021 on GBR)
```

## Recovery vs. Mortality — The Decision Tree

```
BLEACHING OUTCOME DECISION TREE
================================

BLEACHING BEGINS
      |
      v
Duration of thermal stress?
      |
      +---> SHORT (1–3 weeks): Temperatures return to normal
      |          |
      |          v
      |     RECOVERY LIKELY
      |     Corals re-acquire zooxanthellae
      |     Pale for weeks; some metabolic cost
      |     Full photosynthetic function: 1–6 months
      |
      +---> MODERATE (3–6 weeks): Partial mortality
      |          |
      |          v
      |     PARTIAL MORTALITY
      |     Susceptible colonies die; thermotolerant survive
      |     Tissue loss; algal colonization of dead areas
      |     Colony recovery on survivors: 1–3 years
      |
      +---> LONG (>6 weeks) or repeat event before recovery:
                 |
                 v
           MASS MORTALITY
           Tissue sloughs from skeleton
           Coral framework intact but bare
           Rapid algal colonization
           Sponge boring accelerates
           Structural collapse over 5–20 years
           Recovery: 10–50+ years IF no further events
```

## Recovery Timeline

```
REEF RECOVERY AFTER MASS BLEACHING
=====================================

YEAR 0:    Mass bleaching event
YEAR 0–1:  Dead coral skeleton colonized by turf algae (weeks)
           Surviving corals re-acquire zooxanthellae (1–6 months)
YEAR 1–3:  CCA begins colonizing dead framework
           Fast-growing branching Acropora may show small colonies
           from recruitment if larvae available
YEAR 2–5:  Pioneer coral recruitment and early growth
           Highly variable by location; depends on:
             - Larval supply from adjacent healthy reefs
             - Herbivory maintaining algae-free substrate
             - Water quality (low sediment + nutrients)
             - No further heat stress events
YEAR 5–10: Branching corals reach visible size; structural complexity
           begins returning; fish species returning
YEAR 10–25: Substantial coral cover possible on fast-recovering reefs
            Massive corals still small (decades to centuries to original size)
YEAR 25–50: Framework approaching pre-bleaching complexity
            ONLY IF no further bleaching events in interval

PROBLEM: With bleaching events now every 3–5 years on GBR
         (vs. 27-year return interval before 1980):
         Recovery cannot complete between events
         → NET LOSS trajectory; "ratchet" downward
```

## Mortality Thresholds and Variation

Not all corals bleach equally. The variation is partly zooxanthellae clade (see 03-SYMBIOSIS.md), partly host genetic resistance, partly local thermal history.

```
BLEACHING SUSCEPTIBILITY BY CORAL GENUS
=========================================

MORE SUSCEPTIBLE                    MORE RESISTANT
(bleach at lower DHW)               (withstand higher DHW)
-----------------------             -----------------------
Acropora (branching, table)         Porites (massive, encrusting)
Montipora (plating, encrusting)     Pavona
Pocillopora (branching)             Favia / Dipsastraea
Stylophora                          Turbinaria
Most fast-growing forms             Montastraea
                                    Coralline algae (not coral, but
                                    survives most bleaching events)

ALSO VARIES BY:
  - Water depth: deeper corals bleach less (cooler, less light)
  - Cloud cover: shading reduces light-enhanced ROS production
  - Local thermal history: "pre-conditioning" by previous mild stress
    can increase resistance (temperature acclimatization)
  - Water mixing: upwelling of cool water provides local refugia
  - Colony genetics: some individuals consistently bleach less
```

## Cool Water Refugia

```
REFUGIA TYPES
==============

DEEP REEFS (mesophotic, 30–150m):
  - Temperature 1–3°C cooler than surface
  - Light-limited; different community
  - Can serve as source populations if larval dispersal possible
  - Concern: same CO₂/acidification problem as shallow reefs

UPWELLING ZONES:
  - Cold, deep water brought to surface by coastal or equatorial upwelling
  - Reefs near upwelling: bleach less (cooler water) but higher CO₂
    (deep water is CO₂-rich → acidification problem)
  - Eastern Pacific reefs near upwelling zones: paradoxical refugia

HIGH-LATITUDE REEFS:
  - Further from equator = cooler water overall
  - But: as tropics warm, warm water expands poleward
  - Lord Howe Island (31°S), northern Japan: presently marginal reefs;
    will warm too eventually

SEASONAL COOLING:
  - Winter SST at some reefs drops well below MMM
  - Brief hot summers before seasonal cooling → some reefs regularly bleach
    but recover during winter cool
  - GBR southern reefs: more resilient due to seasonal cooling

LOCAL CIRCULATION:
  - Reef passages with enhanced flow = better mixed water = cooler
  - Specific reef structures may maintain locally cooler temps
  - "Thermal heterogeneity" within reefs → local refugia within reefs
```

## Future Projections

```
BLEACHING FREQUENCY PROJECTIONS BY SCENARIO
=============================================

Current trajectory (1.5°C rise scenarios):
  - Annual bleaching-level events on 70–90% of world's reefs
  - No recovery time between events
  - >90% of reef coral lost globally

2°C rise scenario:
  - >99% of reefs bleach annually
  - Functional collapse of coral reef ecosystems
  - "Coral rubble" reefs with <1% coral cover

Optimistic scenario (1.5°C maintained, Paris goals):
  - 70–90% of reefs still suffer severe bleaching
  - Some high-latitude / upwelling refugia persist
  - Thermotolerant strains and assisted evolution may extend survival
  - Still: massive biodiversity loss from pre-industrial state

Historical comparison:
  GBR bleaching return interval:
    Pre-1980: >27 years between events (rare in instrument record)
    1998–2016: ~5 years average
    2016–2024: ~2–3 years
    Projected 2050+: annual at business-as-usual
```

## Common Confusion Points

**DHW is cumulative, not instantaneous.** A reef that sits at MMM+1.5°C for 8 weeks accumulates 12 DHW (mass mortality level) even though the daily temperature anomaly is only 1.5°C. Duration of stress is as important as its magnitude. This is why brief heat events cause far less damage than persistent ones.

**The bleaching threshold is local, not global.** A reef in the Red Sea (MMM ~31°C) bleaches at ~32°C. A reef in the Coral Sea (MMM ~27°C) bleaches at ~28°C. Applying a universal temperature threshold misses this critical adaptation. NOAA's Coral Reef Watch correctly uses the local MMM baseline.

**2016 and 2020 GBR bleaching differed in important ways.** 2016 set the peak temperature record for the northern GBR and had the highest mortality. 2020 had lower peak temperatures but affected the widest geographic area (entire GBR for the first time). The pattern of "different each time" means there are few permanent refugia within the GBR system.

**Recovery numbers are misleading.** A reef with "30% coral cover" in 2005 and again in 2015 may have completely different species. Branching Acropora can recover fast in area coverage but represents less structural complexity and biodiversity than a mature mixed-species reef. Simple % cover metrics miss this.

**El Niño amplifies but doesn't cause the crisis.** El Niño years produce bleaching events. But the 2020 bleaching event (near-record GBR extent) occurred without a significant El Niño. The background warming trend is now high enough to produce bleaching without El Niño assistance. The system has crossed a threshold.
