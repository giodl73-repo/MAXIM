# Reef Formation: Carbonate Accretion, Atoll Development, Sea Level

## The Big Picture

A reef is a carbonate structure built by organisms faster than it is destroyed by physical and biological erosion. The key insight: reefs are not passive rock — they are dynamic accretion systems in near-equilibrium. Remove the animals and the reef erodes away within decades.

```
REEF BUDGET: ACCRETION vs. EROSION
====================================

ACCRETION SIDE                     EROSION SIDE
--------------                     ------------
Coral calcification (primary)       Bioerosion (sponges bore into skeleton)
Coralline algae (encrusting)        Parrotfish (scrape coral surface)
Mollusks, serpulid worms            Sea urchins (rasp substrate)
Foraminifera (sand-forming)         Chemical dissolution (low Ωarag)
                                    Storm breakage and abrasion

NET REEF GROWTH WHEN:  Accretion rate > Erosion rate

Healthy reef:      1–10 mm/yr net vertical accretion
Degraded reef:     Net erosion — reef loses structural height
After bleaching:   Often net erosion for 5–10 years during recovery

Production:  2–4 kg CaCO3 m⁻² yr⁻¹ on healthy reef
Erosion:     1–2 kg CaCO3 m⁻² yr⁻¹ typical
```

## Carbonate Accretion Chemistry

The fundamental reaction coral polyps drive to build their aragonite skeleton:

```
CALCIFICATION REACTION
======================

  Ca²⁺ (seawater) + 2HCO₃⁻ (seawater) → CaCO₃ (aragonite) + H₂O + CO₂
                                              ↑
                                    secreted by polyp into
                                    extracellular calcifying fluid

Aragonite vs. Calcite:
- Both are CaCO₃ polymorphs
- Aragonite: orthorhombic crystal structure, denser, more soluble
- Corals exclusively use aragonite
- Coralline algae use high-Mg calcite (slightly different properties)
- Aragonite more vulnerable to dissolution as pH drops (ocean acidification)

The polyp drives calcification by:
1. Pumping Ca²⁺ into calcifying fluid (active transport)
2. Pumping H⁺ OUT (raising pH at deposition site)
3. Concentrating CO₃²⁻ to exceed saturation threshold
4. Organic matrix proteins template crystal growth
```

**Role of zooxanthellae in calcification**: Symbiotic algae accelerate calcification 2–3× by consuming CO₂ (photosynthesis shifts equilibrium toward carbonate), providing ATP, and removing phosphate that would otherwise inhibit calcification. In darkness, corals still calcify but at 20–30% of light-enhanced rate.

## Darwin's Subsidence Theory — The Three Stages

Darwin aboard HMS Beagle (1835–1836) proposed the fringing → barrier → atoll progression based on volcanic island subsidence. Confirmed 100 years later by deep drilling on Bikini Atoll (1952) and Enewetak (1952), finding coral limestone thousands of meters thick over basalt.

```
DARWIN'S ATOLL FORMATION SEQUENCE
===================================

STAGE 1: FRINGING REEF
  Active volcano
      ___
     /   \
    | VOL |
    |CANO |____sea level
     \___/
  coral fringe
  adjacent to shore
  No lagoon (or very narrow)
  Examples: Hawaii, young Caribbean islands

STAGE 2: BARRIER REEF
  Island subsiding + reef tracks sea level
      ___
     /   \
    |     |__________sea level
     \___/ ~ lagoon ~
           |REEF|
  Lagoon forms between island and crest
  Reef keeps pace with subsidence by upward growth
  Examples: Great Barrier Reef (continental scale),
            Bora Bora (classic volcanic barrier)

STAGE 3: ATOLL
  Island fully submerged; reef ring remains
  ~~~~~~~~~~~sea level~~~~~~~~~~~
  ~ ~ ~  lagoon  ~ ~ ~
 |REEF|            |REEF|
  Ring of reef over submerged seamount
  May have sand cay (island) on windward side
  Examples: Bikini, Enewetak, Maldives, Tuamotu

TIMESCALE: thousands to millions of years
DRILLING:  Enewetak — 1,405 m of coral limestone over basalt
           Bikini    — ~800 m coral limestone
```

**Why reefs track sea level**: Zooxanthellae require light. As the island subsides or sea level rises, the reef grows upward to maintain position in the photic zone. This "keep-up" growth is the mechanism that traces Darwin's entire sequence.

```
===================================

                      SEA LEVEL RISE RATE
                   Slow (<2 mm/yr)    Fast (>10 mm/yr)

REEF GROWTH     +-------------+      +-------------+
RATE            |             |      |             |
High            |  KEEP UP    |      | KEEP UP or  |
(>10 mm/yr)     | reef at     |      | GIVE UP if  |
                | sea surface |      | extremely   |
                |             |      | fast        |
                +-------------+      +-------------+
Low             |             |      |             |
(<2 mm/yr)      | CATCH UP    |      | GIVE UP     |
                | drowned flat|      | submerged   |

                +-------------+      +-------------+

"Drowned reefs":  Submerged structures attesting to past rapid sea level rise
                  Found at 20–40m depth on GBR; Florida shelf edge reefs

                  Degraded reefs (low accretion) vulnerable to drowning
```

Note: "growth rate" is tip extension for branching; bulk CaCO₃ output matters more for reef accretion. A massive *Porites* head contributes more limestone per year than a fast-growing branching coral despite slower linear extension.

**Age and size**: Massive *Porites* heads can be 5–10 m diameter, 500–700 years old. Annual growth bands visible in X-radiograph of core slabs — analogous to tree rings. GBR *Porites* cores provide continuous paleoclimate records extending to the 1600s (SST, rainfall proxies).

## Common Confusion Points

**Accretion rate vs. individual coral growth rate**: Reef accretion is the net result of thousands of colonies growing, plus algal cement, minus bioerosion by sponges, urchins, and parrotfish, minus storm breakage. An individual colony's growth rate is one input among many.

**Atolls require both subsidence AND reef keeping pace**: If the island subsides but the reef can't keep pace (too turbid, too cold, too acidic), you get a guyot — a flat-topped seamount with no reef. Darwin's theory requires both conditions running simultaneously for millions of years.

**CCA is structural, not decorative**: Crustose coralline algae chemically cement loose coral rubble and bind the reef framework together. Reefs without adequate CCA are mechanically weaker and lose structural integrity faster post-storm. CCA abundance is itself a reef health indicator.

**Aragonite saturation state (Ωarag) is not pH**: Both respond to CO₂ inputs, but Ωarag directly governs whether CaCO₃ precipitates or dissolves. Reefs need Ωarag > ~2 to accrete faster than they dissolve. See 07-REEF-CHEMISTRY.md.

**Hermatypic does not mean tropical**: The defining characteristic is zooxanthellae presence, not geography. Some hermatypic species exist in subtropical environments (Bermuda, high-latitude Japan). However, constructing large carbonate reef structures is indeed restricted to warm, clear, shallow tropical/subtropical seas because that combination maximizes zooxanthellae photosynthesis and thus calcification rates.
