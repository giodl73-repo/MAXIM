# Coral Reefs: Biology, Ecology, and Crisis — Landscape

## The Big Picture

Coral reefs cover less than 0.1% of the ocean floor yet harbor an estimated 25–35% of all described marine species. They are the ocean's tropical rainforests — built by animals, sustained by symbiotic algae, and now being dismantled by a combination of local and global stressors at a rate that outpaces any natural reef-building process.

## Reef Types

Four structural categories, each reflecting a different relationship between carbonate accretion, sea level, and substrate:

```
FRINGING REEF          BARRIER REEF            ATOLL
                                            (oceanic)
 ~~~Sea~~~              ~~~Sea~~~           ~~~Sea~~~
+-------+             +--+ ~~~ +--+      +--+   +--+
| Reef  |             |R | Lag |R |      |R |   |R |
+-------+             +--+ oon +--+      +--+   +--+
| Shore |             |   Reef  |        |  Lagoon  |
+-------+             +---------+        +----------+

Directly adjacent   Separated from       Volcanic island
to coastline        shore by lagoon      subsided; ring
                    100m–10km wide       of reef remains
```

**Patch reefs**: isolated mounds within a lagoon or on a shallow shelf — not large enough to qualify as barrier or fringing.

**Bank/shelf reefs**: large platform structures on continental shelves, not fringing a shore — e.g., Flower Garden Banks (Gulf of Mexico), Abrolhos (Brazil).

## Geographic Distribution

```
REEF DISTRIBUTION — LATITUDINAL CONSTRAINTS
============================================

      North
  30°N ----+------------------------------------------- warm-water limit
           |   Mediterranean (no reefs — too cold/turbid)
           |
  15°N ----+--- Florida Keys    Red Sea    Arabian Sea
           |
  EQ  ----+--- Amazon plume (no reefs — turbid freshwater)
           |   Coral Triangle (peak diversity)
  15°S ----+--- Great Barrier Reef  |  Pacific atolls
           |
  30°S ----+------------------------------------------- warm-water limit
      South

Limiting factors:
  - Temperature: 18°C minimum (optimal 23–29°C)
  - Light: zooxanthellae require >1% surface irradiance
  - Salinity: 32–42 ppt
  - Aragonite saturation: Ωarag > 1 (typically >2 for growth)
  - Turbidity: sediment smothers polyps, blocks light
```

**Exceptions**: Mesophotic reefs (30–150 m depth) extend below the photic zone optimum and may serve as thermal refugia. High-latitude marginal reefs exist at Lord Howe Island (31°S, ~22°C winter), Bermuda (32°N, warmed by Gulf Stream).

```

======================================================

Mollusks                       ~5,000+ spp
Crustaceans                    ~4,000+ spp
Echinoderms                    ~600+ spp
Sponges                        ~5,000+ spp
Foraminifera                   ~1,000+ spp (key sand-forming organisms)
Macroalgae                     ~1,000+ spp
Seagrasses                     ~60 spp (adjacent systems, not reef proper)
Total estimate (all taxa)      ~800,000–1,000,000+ spp (most undescribed)
```

**Why so many species on so little area?** Structural complexity (calcium carbonate architecture creates fractal habitat), high light and productivity, year-round stable temperatures (relative to temperate), and 200–500 million years of evolutionary diversification in the Indo-Pacific.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Coral" is the animal, not the rock.** The calcium carbonate structure (skeleton) is secreted by the coral polyp — a soft-bodied animal in phylum Cnidaria. The reef is the accumulated skeleton of generations of polyps plus coralline algae.

**Bleaching is not death.** Bleached corals have expelled their zooxanthellae but are still alive. They can recover if temperatures drop quickly enough. Prolonged bleaching (weeks) leads to starvation and mortality.

**Not all corals build reefs.** Hermatypic (zooxanthellate) corals build reefs. Ahermatypic corals exist in deep cold water worldwide but do not accrete the massive structures that constitute reefs.

**The Indo-Pacific / Atlantic split is ancient.** The Central American Seaway closed ~3.5 million years ago, separating Atlantic and Indo-Pacific coral fauna. Caribbean reefs have been on a separate evolutionary trajectory ever since — hence the dramatic species count difference (600+ vs. 60–70 hard coral species).

**Ocean acidification and bleaching are separate stressors.** Bleaching is driven by temperature; acidification by CO2 dissolving to form carbonic acid. Both derive from fossil fuel emissions but operate through different mechanisms and timescales.
