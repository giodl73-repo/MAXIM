# Reef Ecology: Zones, Food Webs, Herbivory, Cryptic Fauna

## The Big Picture

A reef is not one ecosystem — it is a mosaic of overlapping communities shaped by water energy, depth, light, and substrate. Understanding reef ecology requires layering three frameworks: spatial zonation (where), trophic structure (who eats whom), and temporal dynamics (day vs. night, season, recruitment pulses).

## Zone-by-Zone Detail

### Reef Flat

Exposed at low tide on many reefs. Conditions: high temperature, low salinity (rain), desiccation stress. Dominated by encrusting coralline algae, massive Porites, hardy sea cucumbers, urchins, and small fish. Surge channels cut through the flat allow water circulation. Habitat: foraging ground for wrasses, goatfish, surgeonfish during tidal flooding.

### Reef Crest

The highest-energy zone — breaking wave energy dissipated here. Dominated by encrusting CCA (mechanically strongest), encrusting *Montipora*, *Pocillopora* (branchy but sturdy). Surge channels are cut by the wave energy itself. Very few fish tolerate this zone full-time, though some wrasses and surgeonfish forage at the margins.

### Fore-Reef Slope (2–20 m)

**Maximum coral cover and fish diversity.** Gradient of species by depth:
- 0–5 m: *Pocillopora*, *Stylophora*, table *Acropora*, massive corals
- 5–15 m: Peak diversity; *Acropora* thickets, massive corals, gorgonians beginning
- 15–25 m: Plating *Montipora*, *Turbinaria*, deep-adapted *Porites*

### Reef Wall / Deep Slope (>20 m)

Plating and foliose corals maximize surface area for light capture. Gorgonians, black corals (antipatharians), sponges increase in importance. Commercially important fish species (grouper, snapper spawning aggregations). At 30–150 m: mesophotic zone — distinct community, cooler, thermally buffered.

## Herbivory — The Critical Reef Process

Without herbivory, macroalgae overgrow coral and prevent recruitment. This is the most important regulatory process on reefs after coral growth itself.

```
HERBIVORY DYNAMICS
===================

ALGAE grow on any hard substrate vacated by coral
     ↓ if herbivory is intact
HERBIVORES remove algae and keep substrate open for coral larvae

KEY HERBIVORES:

PARROTFISH (Scaridae):
  - Bite coral substrate with fused beak (scraping + excavating)
  - Produce sand: 1 large parrotfish = ~90 kg sand/yr
  - Excavating species bite into live coral framework (bioerosion)
  - Critical for both algae removal AND creating settlement substrate
  - Loss → algal accumulation; reduced coral recruitment

SURGEONFISH (Acanthuridae):
  - Browsers and grazers of algal turfs
  - Less dramatic per-individual impact but enormous biomass
  - High site fidelity; important in back-reef / flat zones

URCHINS (primarily Diadema antillarum, Caribbean):
  - Spiny sea urchins graze bare rock continuously
  - Caribbean reefs: critical after parrotfish removal by fishing
  - 1983: Mass mortality of Diadema (disease) across entire Caribbean
  - Result: immediate macroalgal bloom → reef phase shift in many areas
  - D. antillarum now recovering in some areas; still at <20% of 1980s abundance

ALGAL PHASE SHIFT:
  Intact herbivory    →  Coral-dominated reef (10–50% coral cover)
  Reduced herbivory   →  Transitional ("rubble reef")
  No effective herbivy→  Algal-dominated reef (macroalgae >80% cover)
  Once shifted, extremely difficult to reverse without active intervention
```

**Acanthaster planci** (crown-of-thorns, CoTS) deserves special mention: it is native to Indo-Pacific reefs and normally present at low density (0.1–6 per km²). At outbreak densities (>15 per km²), each individual consumes up to 6 m² of live coral per year. A single outbreak on a small reef can remove >50% of live coral cover in 2 years.

## Cryptic Fauna

The reef is mostly invisible:

```
CRYPTOFAUNA — THE HIDDEN REEF
================================

Estimated 50–70% of reef species are cryptic
(living in crevices, rubble, under coral heads)

FRAMEWORK CRYPTOFAUNA
  - Sponges (boring into skeleton)
  - Polychaete worms (Christmas tree worms = Spirobranchus in Porites)
  - Crabs (Trapezia spp. in Acropora — obligate mutualists; defend coral)
  - Shrimps (Alpheid pistol shrimp; snapping mantis shrimp)
  - Brittle stars (Ophiuroidea) in crevices — emerge at night
  - Small blennies, gobies (hundreds of cryptic species)
  - Nudibranchs

RUBBLE ZONE
  - Juvenile fish (rubble = nursery habitat)
  - Heart urchins (infaunal)
  - Bivalves (Tridacna giant clams = framework)

CRYPTOFAUNA FUNCTIONS:
  - Bioerosion (sponges, urchins) = ~30% of CaCO₃ budget
  - Food web nodes (massive invertebrate biomass invisible to surveys)
  - Cleaning stations (cleaner shrimps, cleaner wrasse)
  - Coral defense (Trapezia crabs defend their Acropora host)
```

## Day vs. Night Communities

```
DAY/NIGHT SHIFT
================

DAY COMMUNITY:
  - Visual predators (barracuda, jacks) active
  - Planktivores (Chromis, Anthias) hover above coral feeding on zooplankton
  - Herbivores (parrotfish, surgeonfish) actively graze
  - Butterflyfishes forage on coral polyps
  - Colors vivid (many fish have UV vision)

NIGHT COMMUNITY:
  - Nocturnal predators emerge (lobster, octopus, cone snails)
  - Coral polyps EXTEND TENTACLES — active predation on zooplankton
  - Parrotfish sleep (mucus cocoon); surgeonfish retreat to crevices
  - Moray eels hunt
  - Cardinalfish (Apogonidae) emerge from daytime shelter
  - Biofluorescence visible: many corals and fish fluoresce red/green
    in blue-enriched light

NET EFFECT: Reef is productive 24 hours
  Day  = algal photosynthesis + fish foraging
  Night = coral carnivory + invertebrate activity
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Reef fish diversity is not evenly distributed.** The highest fish species richness is on the fore-reef slope (5–20 m). The reef flat is species-poor (few fish tolerate the extreme conditions). The lagoon/back-reef has a different but important community (including juveniles of many slope species).

**Coral reefs are not high-productivity systems in the conventional sense.** Primary productivity per unit area is moderate. The paradox — high animal diversity and biomass on low-nutrient "blue desert" water — is resolved by the tight nutrient recycling (see 03-SYMBIOSIS.md) and by the reef's architecture concentrating organisms.

**Herbivory is more important than nutrients for algae control.** Experiments and field observations consistently show that reducing herbivores (through overfishing) causes algal overgrowth even when nutrients are low, while high herbivory prevents algal overgrowth even at elevated nutrients. Fishing, not just nutrient runoff, is the primary driver of algal phase shifts.

**Crown-of-thorns outbreaks are partly natural.** Low-density CoTS populations are a normal component of Indo-Pacific reef food webs. The hypothesis is that nutrient-enriched waters (land runoff) boost phytoplankton, which increases larval survival of CoTS, enabling outbreaks. So "natural" outbreaks exist but human-enhanced nutrient inputs increase their frequency and intensity.

**Night visibility reveals the real reef.** Survey data collected during the day severely undercount cryptic species and underestimate coral feeding. Night snorkeling or SCUBA on a reef reveals a completely different functional community — and the corals themselves as active carnivores.
