# Human Impacts: Overfishing, Runoff, Physical Damage, Tourism

## The Big Picture

Coral reefs face simultaneous stressors operating at multiple scales — local (overfishing, runoff, physical damage) and global (warming, acidification). The local stressors compound the global ones: a reef with intact herbivore populations can recover from bleaching faster than a heavily fished reef. Management of local stressors is therefore one of the few levers available to buy time for reefs while global emissions are addressed.

## Overfishing — Trophic Cascade Mechanism

```
FISHING IMPACT CASCADE
========================

STAGE 1: Large predator removal (sharks, large grouper)
  - Shark finning, spearfishing, longline bycatch
  - Mesopredators released → eat more herbivores
  - Functional change but indirect; less visual

STAGE 2: Target fish removal (parrotfish, surgeonfish)
  - Direct spearfishing, net fishing for herbivores
  - MOST CRITICAL IMPACT
  - Herbivore biomass drops → algae not controlled
  - In Caribbean: now illegal to catch parrotfish in some countries
    (Jamaica 2009; Belize 2009; too late in many cases)

STAGE 3: Algal bloom
  - Without parrotfish + surgeonfish + urchins:
  - Macroalgae grow on any disturbed substrate
  - Coral recruitment blocked (larvae cannot settle on algae)
  - Phase shift to algal reef difficult to reverse

STAGE 4: "Fishing down the food web"
  - Large fish gone → medium fish targeted → small fish
  - Invertebrate fisheries expand (lobster, conch, sea cucumber)
  - Eventually: only low-value, small, fast-reproducing species remain

QUANTITATIVE:
  Unfished reef: 300–800 kg fish biomass per hectare
  Fished reef:   10–50 kg fish biomass per hectare
  Heavily fished: <10 kg — functionally collapsed food web
```

## Crown-of-Thorns Starfish (CoTS) — Nutrient-Fishing Nexus

```
CoTS OUTBREAK MECHANISM
=========================

Acanthaster planci — a coral predator; normal density: 0.1–6 per km²
OUTBREAK density: >15 per km² (devastates reef)

PRIMARY OUTBREAK HYPOTHESIS:
  Land-based nutrient runoff
       |
       v
  Phytoplankton bloom in nearshore waters (especially Dec–Feb)
       |
       v
  Increased larval CoTS food (phytoplankton)
       |
       v
  10× to 100× higher larval survival rate
       |
       v
  Massive settlement of juvenile CoTS
       |
       v
  Outbreak 3–5 years after nutrient pulse
       (juveniles feed on coralline algae; adults on coral)

SECONDARY FACTOR: Removal of CoTS predators by overfishing
  Natural predators: Napoleon wrasse (Cheilinus undulatus),
                     Titan triggerfish, giant triton snail (Charonia)
  All heavily targeted by fishing
  Their removal may allow outbreak persistence

GBR OUTBREAKS:
  Major outbreaks: ~1962, 1979, 1993, 2010, 2017–present
  Estimated loss: ~40% of GBR coral cover historically attributable to CoTS
  Active management: Crown-of-Thorns Starfish Control Program (GBRMPA)
                     Injection with bile salts or oxgall → kills CoTS
                     Requires millions of injections per year to have impact
```

## Runoff — Sediment and Nutrients

```
LAND-BASED POLLUTION PATHWAYS
================================

SOURCES          WHAT IT CARRIES      REEF IMPACT
-----------      ---------------      -----------
Agriculture      Nutrients (N, P)     Algal bloom
                 Pesticides           Coral toxicity
                 Sediment            Smothering

Urban runoff     Sediment            Smothering
                 Nutrients            Algal bloom
                 Heavy metals         Toxicity
                 Pathogens           Disease

Sewage           Nutrients (DIN, DIP) Algal bloom
(untreated)      Pathogens            Disease
                 Pharmaceuticals      Endocrine disruption

Dredging         Sediment plumes     Smothering; light reduction
                 Turbidity           Blocks photosynthesis

Deforestation    Massively increased  Turbidity spike; sediment
                 erosion + runoff     blanket on coral

SEDIMENT IMPACTS ON CORAL:
  1. Direct smothering (physically covers polyps)
  2. Light attenuation (turbid water = less photosynthesis)
  3. Increased mucus production (clogs filtration systems)
  4. Elevated organic matter → disease-promoting bacteria

NUTRIENT IMPACTS:
  1. Phytoplankton bloom → turbidity → less light
  2. Macroalgae fertilized → algal phase shift
  3. CoTS larval survival enhanced (see above)
  4. Bacterial growth → disease increase
  5. Bioerosion accelerated (boring sponges respond to nutrients)
```

## Physical Damage

```
PHYSICAL DAMAGE SOURCES
=========================

ANCHORING:
  Single anchor drop on Acropora thicket: destroys 1–10 m² immediately
  Anchor chain dragging: 50–200 m² path of destruction per anchor event
  Repeated anchoring: permanent clearing of coral in popular dive sites
  Solutions: Mooring buoys; no-anchor zones; GPS-guided anchoring

DESTRUCTIVE FISHING:
  Blast (dynamite) fishing:
    One blast: kills everything in 5–10 m radius; shatters skeleton
    Rubble piles persist for decades; cannot naturally revegetate
    Common in Southeast Asia; illegal but widespread
    A single fisherman can destroy millions of dollars of reef value

  Cyanide fishing (for live aquarium/restaurant fish):
    Squirted directly at fish refuge (coral crevice)
    Stuns fish for capture; also stuns/kills coral polyps
    Coral mortality 24–48 hours later
    Used across Indo-Pacific for ornamental fish + live reef fish trade

  Muro-ami (drive net + rock pounding):
    Illegal; drives fish toward nets by smashing coral
    Historical practice; now rare

  Bottom trawling:
    Rare on shallow reefs but destroys deep coral structures

VESSEL GROUNDINGS:
  Large ship groundings: catastrophic; remove 1,000+ m² immediately
  Notable: SS United Western (Florida Keys, 1989): decades of litigation
  Restoration: some groundings restored by breakage removal + transplant

RECREATIONAL:
  Diver contact: standing on coral, touching, fins brushing
  Individual impact low; aggregate impact at high-use sites significant
  Studies show 0.1–0.2 coral contacts per diver per dive
  At 1,000 dives/day: material ecosystem damage over years
  Dive briefings + certification requirements: effective mitigation
```

## Sunscreen and Chemical Pollution

```
SUNSCREEN CHEMISTRY AND REEF IMPACT
=====================================

Oxybenzone (benzophenone-3, BP-3):
  - Common UV filter in chemical sunscreens
  - Concentration in popular dive sites: 1–13 parts per TRILLION
  - Lab studies: causes coral bleaching, DNA damage, skeletal deformation
    at concentrations as low as 62 parts per trillion
  - Endocrine disruption in juvenile coral
  - Found in coral tissue in Caribbean and Pacific
  - Hawaii banned oxybenzone + octinoxate (effective 2021)
  - US Virgin Islands, Key West, Palau: similar bans

Octinoxate (ethylhexyl methoxycinnamate):
  - Second common UV filter; similar concerns

Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide — non-nano):
  - Less absorbable by marine organisms
  - Generally considered reef-safer
  - Nano-particles: may still be problematic; research ongoing

OTHER CHEMICAL INPUTS:
  Antifouling paint (tributyltin: banned; replacement compounds: various risks)
  Pharmaceuticals in wastewater
  Plasticizers (phthalates, BPA)
  Pesticides (chlorpyrifos, etc.)
  Heavy metals from industry
```

## Tourism Management Frameworks

```
REEF TOURISM IMPACT MANAGEMENT
================================

CARRYING CAPACITY:
  Concept: limit diver numbers per site per day
  Caribbean studies: 4,000–6,000 diver-contacts per site per year
                    before measurable structural damage
  Challenge: economic incentives favor unlimited access
  Solution: marine park fees + dive site rotation

ZONING SYSTEMS (example: Great Barrier Reef Marine Park):
  GREEN: No-take marine national park (fully protected)
         33% of GBR is no-take after 2004 rezoning
  BLUE:  Habitat protection zone (some activities; no trawling)
  YELLOW: Conservation park (recreational fishing allowed)
  ORANGE: Buffer zone (limited extractive use)
  RED:   General use (commercial fishing allowed)

  OUTCOME: No-take zones show 20–30% higher fish biomass
           Spillover effect benefits adjacent fished areas

BOAT MOORINGS:
  Replace anchoring with permanent mooring buoys
  Florida Keys: 500+ moorings installed
  Each mooring: saves 1–2 m² of coral per visitor from anchor damage

VISITOR MANAGEMENT:
  Pre-dive briefing
  No-touch policies
  Certified dive operator requirements
  Photographer guidelines (no touching for photo)

ECONOMIC JUSTIFICATION:
  GBR tourism: ~$6.4 billion AUD/year, 64,000 jobs
  Reef degradation: measurable impact on dive tourism revenue
  Economically rational to invest in reef protection
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Overfishing is more damaging per reef area than bleaching in many places.** In regions without severe bleaching events, chronic overfishing has caused the most coral cover loss over the past 50 years. Bleaching gets more attention because it's visible, dramatic, and global; overfishing kills reefs slowly and invisibly.

**Nutrient runoff effects are delayed.** The CoTS outbreak mechanism involves a 3–5 year lag between nutrient pulse and visible outbreak. This makes attribution difficult politically — the nutrient event responsible for a 2020 outbreak may have been the 2016–2017 flood season. This lag obscures the causal link for land managers.

**Diver impact is individually small but collectively significant.** A single diver contacting coral once per dive seems trivial. At high-use sites with 500+ divers/day, this becomes 500+ contacts/day × 365 days × years = measurable mechanical damage over the reef's recovery timescale. The problem is not individual behavior but the aggregate of millions of dives annually.

**Sunscreen bans are politically visible but physically marginal.** The concentrations of oxybenzone in reef water are in the parts-per-trillion range. Lab studies show effects but field attributable mortality from sunscreen is difficult to quantify. The bans are symbolically important and likely worth implementing, but the dominant drivers of reef decline are thermal bleaching and overfishing at far higher magnitude.

**No-take zones work but must be large and enforced.** A small marine reserve surrounded by heavily fished water provides limited benefit — fish simply move out. Effective no-take zones need to be >30–50% of total reef area to show significant spillover benefits. The GBR at 33% no-take is near the lower boundary of ecological effectiveness.
