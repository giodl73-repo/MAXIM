# Colonial Extraction — Economic History

## The Big Picture

European overseas expansion (1492-1900) created an extractive global economy that simultaneously funded European industrial development and suppressed development in colonized regions. The mechanisms were varied: Spanish bullion extraction, plantation slave labor, monopoly trading companies, and direct tax extraction. The historiographic debate centers on magnitudes — how much did colonial extraction contribute to European growth versus how much did it suppress colonized economies?

```
COLONIAL EXTRACTION TIMELINE
==============================

1492        Columbus; Caribbean colonization begins
1519-1521   Cortez conquers Aztec Empire
1532-1572   Pizarro conquers Inca Empire; Potosi silver 1545
1520-1650   Price revolution: Spanish silver floods Europe

1600        British East India Company chartered
1602        Dutch East India Company (VOC) chartered
1619        First enslaved Africans to British Virginia
1650-1800   Atlantic slave trade peak period

1757        Battle of Plassey: EIC begins conquest of Bengal
1765        Diwani rights: EIC gains Bengal tax collection authority
1770        Bengal Famine: EIC tax extraction amid crop failure; ~10M dead

1776        Adam Smith, Wealth of Nations: systematic critique of mercantilism
1807        Britain abolishes slave trade (not slavery)
1833        British Slavery Abolition Act

1850-1914   High imperialism: Africa, SE Asia partitioned

SCALE ESTIMATES:
  Potosi silver 1545-1800: ~41,000 metric tons exported to Europe
  Atlantic slave trade 1500-1900: ~12.5 million enslaved Africans transported
  Patnaik estimate for India 1765-1938: $45 trillion extracted (2018 estimate)
```

## Spanish Bullion Extraction

### Potosi: The Silver Mountain

Cerro Rico ("Rich Mountain") at Potosi in modern Bolivia was discovered in 1545. At 4,090 meters altitude, it became the largest silver producer in world history and the reason for the 16th-17th century price revolution.

```
POTOSI SILVER MINE
===================

SCALE:
  Peak production: ~4 million pesos (silver peso) per year circa 1600
  Total output 1545-1800: estimated 41,000 metric tons of silver
  At peak: Potosi was one of the largest cities in the world (~160,000 people)
  The hill itself: thought to be ~2 billion pesos total in silver

MERCURY AMALGAMATION PROCESS (introduced 1572):
  Old method (smelting): required charcoal; inefficient for lower-grade ore
  New method (patio process):
  1. Ore crushed in stamp mills
  2. Mixed with mercury, salt, and copper sulfate on patio (paved courtyard)
  3. Workers (workers called "jornaleros") stomped mixture with feet and mules
     for days to ensure mercury contact
  4. Mercury binds with silver; amalgam separated from waste rock
  5. Amalgam heated: mercury vaporizes (and was inhaled by workers),
     silver solidifies
  RESULT: could process lower-grade ore profitably
  COST:   mercury poisoning killed thousands; mercury sourced from Huancavelica
          (another forced labor mine)

MITA FORCED LABOR SYSTEM:
  Spanish adapted the Inca mit'a (rotational labor obligation to the state)
  Mandate: each village in a defined zone sent 1/7 of adult male population
           to Potosi for 18-month shifts
  Scale:   roughly 13,500 mitayos (conscripted workers) per year
  Wages:   nominally paid, but wages set below subsistence;
           workers had to bring food and supplies
           families often followed to maintain subsistence
  Conditions: underground tunnels, mercury exposure, cave-ins
              estimated 8 million deaths over the mine's history
              ("the mountain that eats men")
  Legal status: mita was a legal labor obligation, not chattel slavery;
                but the practical reality was coerced labor under deadly conditions
```

## The Atlantic Slave Trade

### The Economic Architecture

The Atlantic slave trade (1500-1900) transported approximately 12.5 million enslaved Africans to the Americas. It was not an incidental feature of colonial economics — it was the foundational labor system of tropical plantation agriculture.

```
TRIANGULAR TRADE STRUCTURE
============================

EUROPE
  Ships loaded with trade goods:
  textiles, guns, alcohol, ironware, cowrie shells
        |
        | LEG 1: to West Africa
        | (favorable winds: NE trades southward)
        v
WEST AFRICA
  Goods exchanged for enslaved people
  Source of enslaved people:
    - African rulers selling war captives
    - Raiding parties (sometimes Portuguese/British directly)
    - Debt bondage enforcement
  Major embarkation points:
    Elmina (Gold Coast, Ghana), Bonny (Nigeria), Loango (Congo)
        |
        | LEG 2: MIDDLE PASSAGE (to Americas)
        | 6-8 weeks; 1,000-mile Atlantic crossing
        v
AMERICAS
  Enslaved people sold at auction to plantation owners
  Sugar, tobacco, cotton, rice loaded as return cargo
        |
        | LEG 3: to Europe
        | (favorable winds: westerlies northward)
        v
EUROPE
  Plantation goods sold at enormous profit
  Cycle repeats

NOTE: The "triangular" framing oversimplifies.
  Many voyages were bilateral, not triangular.
  The key point is the three-commodity chain:
  manufactured goods -> enslaved people -> plantation commodities
```

### Middle Passage Mortality

```
MIDDLE PASSAGE CONDITIONS AND MORTALITY
=========================================

SHIP CONDITIONS:
  Enslaved people packed in "tight packing" configuration:
  5-6 feet long, 16-18 inches wide per person, lying flat
  Chained in pairs at ankle and wrist
  2 "decks" separated by shelving: ~24-30 inches vertical clearance
  ~300-500 people on a typical slaving vessel

MORTALITY RATES:
  Early period (1650-1750): ~20-25% mortality en route
  Late period (1750-1850): ~5-8% mortality (improved navigation,
    faster voyages, slightly better provisioning)
  Overall average (all voyages): ~12-15% mortality

  Of 12.5 million embarked, roughly 10.7 million arrived
  = approximately 1.8 million deaths in transit

  Causes: dehydration, dysentery, smallpox, depression/suicide,
          crew violence, ship wrecks

THE ZONG MASSACRE (1781):
  British slave ship Zong; Captain Luke Collingwood commanding
  Voyage behind schedule; water supplies low; disease spreading
  Collingwood ordered 133 sick/dying enslaved people thrown overboard
  Rationale: sick enslaved people dying = commercial loss;
             throwing overboard = insurance claim ("jettison of cargo")
  Insurance claim filed; insurers refused to pay; case went to court
  Lord Mansfield's ruling: could be tried as murder, not insurance claim
  SIGNIFICANCE:
    1. Legal case: first court challenge to treating enslaved people as cargo
    2. Abolition movement: Olaudah Equiano publicized the case;
       Granville Sharp used it to galvanize the abolition campaign
    3. Economic logic: reveals how completely commodified the enslaved were
       in insurance and commercial law
```

### Plantation Economics: Capital Returns

```
PLANTATION ECONOMICS
=====================

CASE: BARBADOS SUGAR PLANTATION, ~1750

Investment structure:
  Land:      expensive (cleared, developed)
  Buildings: mill, boiling house, curing house
  Equipment: sugar mill, pots, tools
  Labor:     enslaved people (largest capital item)
             Average enslaved person on sugar estate: ~25-35 pounds Sterling
             A 200-acre plantation: 200+ enslaved people
             Total labor capital: ~5,000-7,000 pounds

Returns:
  Sugar price 1750: ~30-35 shillings per hundredweight (cwt)
  Yield: well-run plantation: ~100-200 hogsheads/year
  One hogshead: ~1,000 lbs sugar
  Revenue: ~3,000-6,000 pounds/year
  Operating costs: maintenance of enslaved, overseers, insurance, shipping
  Net return: 8-15% per year on capital invested (before accounting for
              mortality and depreciation of enslaved labor force)

  COMPARISON: Bank of England paid ~3-5% in same period
              Plantation slavery was twice as profitable as bonds

  NOTE: The "capital" in enslaved people was unusual:
  - They produced income (labor output)
  - They depreciated (mortality, aging)
  - They could be "replenished" (purchase from slave market)
  - They were insurable (as livestock in early period)
  - They were mortgageable (plantations raised working capital by
    mortgaging their enslaved workforce)

BRITISH INSURANCE MARKETS:
  Lloyd's of London developed from coffee-house meetings of merchants
  and underwriters who insured slave ships and their "cargo"
  Slave ship insurance: standard Lloyd's product through ~1807
  Post-abolition (1807): insurance adapted to other maritime commerce
```

## Mercantilist Theory

### The Theory Behind the System

Mercantilism was the dominant economic theory from ~1550 to 1776, when Adam Smith systematically demolished it. It was never a single coherent theory — more a collection of policy presumptions and justifications.

```
MERCANTILIST CORE ASSUMPTIONS
===============================

1. BULLIONIST FALLACY:
   National wealth = stock of gold and silver
   Goal: accumulate maximum precious metals
   Policy: always export more than you import (trade surplus)
           surplus = net inflow of gold/silver payment

2. FIXED WORLD TRADE PIE:
   Total world trade is roughly constant
   One nation's gain = another's loss (zero-sum)
   Policy: protect domestic industry; exclude foreign goods
           Navigation Acts (England): must use English ships
           Colbert's France: high tariffs on foreign manufactures

3. COLONIES AS ECONOMIC TOOLS:
   Colonies exist to supply raw materials to the metropole
   Colonies exist to buy manufactured goods from the metropole
   Colonies must NOT manufacture for themselves (suppresses competition)
   Example: British laws forbade Irish and American woolen exports

4. POPULATION AS WEALTH:
   More people = more labor = more production = more power
   Policy: encourage birth rate; restrict emigration
   Immigration welcomed (except from competing nations)

COLBERT (Jean-Baptiste Colbert, 1619-1683):
  French finance minister under Louis XIV
  "Colbertism" = French mercantilism
  Built state-owned manufacturers (Gobelins tapestries, Sevres porcelain)
  High tariffs: Tariff of 1667 (triggered trade war with Dutch)
  State control of trade: monopoly companies for each colonial route
  Goal: make France self-sufficient and export-dominant
  LEGACY: Colbertism died as theory but French industrial policy
          tradition persists; see 09-CONTEMPORARY on industrial policy return

ADAM SMITH'S CRITIQUE (Wealth of Nations, 1776):
  1. Wealth is not gold but the annual product of land and labor
  2. Trade is not zero-sum: comparative advantage creates mutual gains
  3. Division of labor increases productivity (pin factory example)
  4. Colonies are not profitable: the military cost of maintaining
     them exceeds the trade benefits (especially for Britain)
  5. Navigation Acts made British goods more expensive; hurt consumers
  Smith was wrong about point 4 empirically (slave trade was profitable)
  but correct about the general theory
```

## British East India Company

### The EIC: A Chartered Monopoly as Conquest Engine

The English East India Company (chartered 1600) is the canonical example of how commercial organization became colonial domination. It moved from trading company to military power to territorial governor within 165 years.

```
EIC EVOLUTION
==============

1600: CHARTER PHASE
  Royal charter grants exclusive English rights to East India trade
  Joint-stock company: investors hold shares; limited liability
  Initial capital: 68,373 pounds (raised by ~218 subscribers)
  Aim: trade cloth for spices (nutmeg, pepper, cinnamon)
  Competition: Dutch VOC (already dominant); Portuguese (weakening)

1612-1757: TRADING PHASE
  Established "factories" (warehouses + offices) at:
    Surat (1612), Madras (1639), Bombay (1668), Calcutta (1690)
  Gradual military capacity: armed guards for factories
  Mughal Empire still dominant; EIC operates with Mughal permission
  Conflict with Nawabs (local Mughal governors) growing
  1744: EIC has 3,000 European troops + Indian sepoys

1757: BATTLE OF PLASSEY (INFLECTION POINT)
  Robert Clive defeats Nawab Siraj ud-Daulah at Plassey (Bengal)
  Battle was largely won through conspiracy: Mir Jafar (rival of Nawab)
  changed sides mid-battle after EIC bribe
  Mir Jafar installed as Nawab; pays enormous "tribute" to EIC
  SIGNIFICANCE: Company now has effective control of Bengal, richest
  province in India (~40% of India's revenue)

1765: DIWANI RIGHTS -- CONQUEST BY ACCOUNTING
  The Mughal Emperor grants EIC the "diwani" (revenue collection rights)
  for Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
  WHAT THIS MEANT:
    EIC could collect all taxes in these provinces
    Previously: revenue went to Mughal Emperor (partially)
                then to Nawab
                then to local administration
    Now: revenue goes to EIC
    But EIC still nominally subject to Mughal sovereignty
    (maintains the fiction of Mughal authority while extracting the substance)

  THIS IS: A commercial company operating as a sovereign taxing authority
           over 30 million people

  The phrase "conquest by accounting" is apt:
  - No declaration of war
  - No transfer of sovereignty in formal legal terms
  - Just... administrative rearrangement of who collects taxes

1770: BENGAL FAMINE
  Crop failure combined with EIC tax extraction at full rate
  EIC doubled land tax demands in 1769
  Grain merchants hoarded during shortage (EIC allowed this for profit)
  Estimated 10 million deaths (approximately 1/3 of Bengal's population)
  EIC dividend to shareholders: maintained during famine year
  Parliamentary inquiry later: "mismanagement and neglect"
  SIGNIFICANCE: First major critique of EIC governance practices
                led eventually to India Act 1784

1784-1858: CROWN OVERSIGHT
  Pitt's India Act (1784): Board of Control (Crown/Parliament supervision)
  EIC retains commercial operations; government controls military/political
  Ongoing military expansion: Mysore wars (1779-1799), Maratha wars
  Sepoy Mutiny (Indian Rebellion) 1857: triggered by multiple grievances
  1858: Crown takes direct control; EIC dissolved; British Raj begins
```

### The Drain of Wealth

Utsa Patnaik (Jawaharlal Nehru University) published an estimate in 2018: India transferred approximately $45 trillion (2018 dollars) to Britain over the period 1765-1938.

```
PATNAIK'S DRAIN OF WEALTH METHODOLOGY
========================================

MECHANISM:
  1. EIC (later British government) collected taxes in India
  2. Used tax revenue to purchase Indian goods for export
     (so exports were not paid for in silver, as normal trade would require)
  3. Indian exporters received payment in "Council Bills"
     (claims on the India Office in London)
  4. Indian exporters cashed bills in India (getting rupees)
     but the transaction did not require actual British goods
     flowing to India in exchange
  5. Result: India exported goods; received only paper claims;
             the real resources left India without equivalent return

  COMPARISON: Normal trade = export goods, receive imported goods or silver
              Drain mechanism = export goods, receive paper that is redeemed
              locally but backed by taxes extracted from Indians

PATNAIK'S ESTIMATE:
  Methodology: calculate volume of unrequited exports
               Value in 2018 dollars using compound interest at 5%
  Result: $44.6 trillion (roughly equivalent to 17x India's current annual GDP)

CRITIQUES:
  1. Compound interest assumption overstates the figure dramatically
     (any positive interest rate over 173 years compounds enormously)
  2. Counterfactual problem: what would India's trajectory have been
     without British rule? Population growth rate was actually similar
     to pre-colonial periods.
  3. Some investment flowed TO India: railways, irrigation, judiciary
  4. Dirk: argues Britain actually subsidized India with security
     (though the military costs were largely paid by Indian taxes)

LOWER-BOUND ESTIMATES:
  Angus Maddison's estimate: India's share of world GDP
    1700: ~24% (roughly proportional to its share of world population)
    1950: ~4% (at independence)
  Whether this decline was caused by extraction, by relative industrialization
  of Europe, or by policy differences is contested

WHAT IS NOT CONTESTED:
  1. The mechanism (unrequited exports via Council Bills) is documented
  2. India's per capita income was roughly stagnant during British rule
  3. Indian textile industry was deliberately suppressed (Manchester imports
     had lower tariffs in India than in Britain)
  4. British infrastructure investment (railways) was primarily built
     to serve British economic interests (export commodities outward),
     not Indian internal development needs
```

## Dutch East India Company (VOC)

### The VOC as First Multinational Corporation

The Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie (VOC, chartered 1602) was the world's first true multinational corporation with permanently invested capital, tradeable shares, and global operations.

```
VOC STRUCTURE AND INNOVATION
==============================

CAPITAL STRUCTURE (unprecedented in 1602):
  Raised: 6.4 million guilders from ~1,800 investors
  (Previous joint-stock: closed after each voyage; capital returned)
  VOC: capital was PERMANENT; shareholders could not demand return
  Could only exit by SELLING SHARES on the secondary market
  This forced the Amsterdam Exchange to develop as a secondary market
  = Birth of the modern stock market (Amsterdam Exchange, 1602)

CORPORATE GOVERNANCE (also new):
  Heeren XVII (17 Lords): governing board of 17 directors
  No shareholder votes on major decisions
  Dividends: irregular; sometimes paid in spice (when cash short)
  Information asymmetry: directors knew more than shareholders;
  early insider trading was rampant

OPERATIONS:
  At peak: 150 merchant ships, 40 warships
  50,000 employees (largest private employer in world at the time)
  Controlled spice trade from Maluku (Moluccas) Islands
  Key spices: nutmeg (Banda Islands), cloves (Ternate, Tidore), pepper

FORCED CULTIVATION (Cultuurstelsel, later):
  1619: Jan Pieterszoon Coen takes Batavia (Jakarta)
  Massacres Banda Islands population to establish nutmeg monopoly
  Replaced with Dutch-supervised "perken" (small plantations)
  Kept nutmeg available only from Dutch-controlled islands
  Enforced by destroying nutmeg trees elsewhere + militarily blocking trade

1830: CULTUURSTELSEL (Cultivation System)
  Dutch colonial governor Van den Bosch
  Java: peasants required to devote 20% of land (or 60 days/year) to
  export crops for Dutch government (coffee, sugar, indigo)
  These were delivered to Dutch trading company at below-market prices
  Essentially: forced labor to produce export commodities
  Results:
    Dutch government: enormous profits (funded Dutch infrastructure)
    Javanese peasants: famines when food crop areas converted to export crops

VOC DECLINE (1700s):
  Dividend payments (often funded by new debt, not profits)
  Corruption: Jan Compagnie (nickname) officials took personal cuts
  Naval competition: British East India Company; French; local rulers
  Cost of maintaining military presence exceeded trade profits
  Nationalized by Dutch government 1799; dissolved
```

## Counterfactual: What Would Indian/Chinese Per Capita Look Like Without Extraction?

This is the hardest question in colonial economic history — it requires a counterfactual that cannot be tested.

```
COUNTERFACTUAL FRAMEWORK
==========================

INDIA WITHOUT BRITISH RULE:
  Scenario: Mughal Empire continued, or successor Indian states
            maintained independence

  Arguments for higher per capita income:
  1. Indian textile industry was suppressed (Lancashire cotton preference);
     India's comparative advantage in cotton manufacturing was artificially
     eliminated by differential tariff policy
  2. Council Bill mechanism drained ~$200M/year (Maddison's lower estimate);
     if reinvested domestically, compound growth implications are large
  3. Land revenue policy: British maximized short-term extraction;
     this discouraged agricultural investment
  4. Railway network built for export, not internal development

  Arguments against higher per capita income:
  1. Mughal Empire was already fragmenting (Aurangzeb died 1707;
     Maratha, Sikh, Afghan challenges); political fragmentation
     = trade disruption regardless
  2. What caused Indian deindustrialization? Multiple candidates:
     British policy + the general cheapening of textiles globally
     (Lancashire industrialization made cotton cheap everywhere)
  3. Some institutional transfers: common law, property rights,
     unified currency, railways (even if suboptimally routed)

CHINA WITHOUT EUROPEAN INTERFERENCE:
  The Opium Wars (1839-42, 1856-60) forced opium imports on China;
  subsequent treaties imposed unequal tariff structures
  Chinese silver drained westward through opium trade
  The "century of humiliation" (1839-1949): Chinese GDP per capita
  remained roughly flat while the West industrialized

  Had China maintained its ~1750 trajectory:
  Allen's estimate: China in 1750 was at same living standards as
  European fringe countries; real wages in Yangtze Delta roughly equal
  to southern European wages
  The counterfactual: without foreign interference + without the
  coal-geography problem Allen identifies, could China have
  industrialized? Possibly yes, but probably more slowly.
  China's coal was in the northwest (Shanxi); the industrial
  centers were in the coastal east. The transport cost was prohibitive
  until railways arrived, and railways arrived in the context of
  semicolonial concessions.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Mercantilist theory was not stupid — it was wrong in specific ways.** The zero-sum assumption about trade was incorrect (comparative advantage shows trade creates mutual gains). But the concern about terms-of-trade (who captures gains from trade) was not wrong. Modern "unequal exchange" theories (Emmanuel, Wallerstein) are more sophisticated versions of similar intuitions.

**The VOC was not a "monopoly" in the modern sense.** It had a monopoly on *Dutch* trade in the East Indies. English, Portuguese, and local Asian traders all competed in the same markets. The VOC's advantage was scale and military force, not legal exclusion of all competition.

**Chattel slavery and mita were different systems with different economic logic.** Mita workers were not owned; they were legally free people with a labor obligation, nominally paid. Chattel slaves were property, bought, sold, and mortgaged. The economic effect on the workers was similar (coerced labor under deadly conditions), but the accounting treatment, legal status, and institutional structure were different.

**The "drain" debate conflates several different mechanisms.** There is: (1) direct extraction via taxes converted to export goods, (2) suppression of Indian industry through tariff policy, (3) the diversion of Indian savings to British infrastructure and military expenditures, and (4) the opportunity cost of foregone industrialization. Patnaik's $45 trillion is the biggest estimate; most historians accept a substantial but much smaller number for the direct drain mechanism.
