# Bretton Woods — Economic History

## The Big Picture

Bretton Woods (July 1944) was the negotiation to design the post-WWII international economic architecture while the war was still ongoing. The conference at the Mount Washington Hotel in New Hampshire produced the IMF, the World Bank, and the rules for a dollar-centered fixed exchange rate system. It also produced a clear winner (Harry Dexter White, representing the US) and a clear loser (John Maynard Keynes, representing a debt-exhausted UK).

```
BRETTON WOODS ARCHITECTURE
============================

        GOALS AT BRETTON WOODS (July 1944):
        1. Prevent the beggar-thy-neighbor competitive devaluations
           of the 1930s (which extended the Depression)
        2. Provide credit to countries with balance-of-payments problems
           (so they don't have to deflate sharply to adjust)
        3. Reduce trade barriers (no return to Smoot-Hawley)
        4. Provide financing for post-war reconstruction

        TWO PLANS:
        +------------------+      vs     +------------------+
        | WHITE PLAN (US)  |             | KEYNES PLAN (UK) |
        |                  |             |                  |
        | Dollar standard  |             | Bancor standard  |
        | US in control    |             | Symmetric adj.   |
        | Creditor favored |             | Debtor protected |
        | Smaller fund     |             | Larger fund      |
        | Status quo for   |             | New international|
        | reserve currency |             | reserve unit     |
        +------------------+             +------------------+
                |                                |
                v                                v
                       WHITE PLAN WINS
                       US was the creditor;
                       US had the leverage

        INSTITUTIONS CREATED:
        +------------------+------------------+------------------+
        | IMF              | World Bank       | GATT             |
        | Monetary fund    | (IBRD)           | Trade rules      |
        | Balance of       | Reconstruction   | Tariff reduction |
        | payments lending | and development  | Most-favored-    |
        | Conditionality   | lending          | nation principle |
        +------------------+------------------+------------------+

        EXCHANGE RATE REGIME:
        Dollar pegged to gold at $35/oz
        All other currencies pegged to dollar
        Adjustable pegs: country could change rate with IMF approval
        (This was the compromise: fixed enough to prevent speculation;
         flexible enough to allow adjustment)
```

## White Plan vs. Keynes Plan

### What Keynes Was Trying to Achieve

John Maynard Keynes had learned from the interwar experience: the gold standard forced deficit countries to deflate, which created unemployment and political instability. He wanted a system where the burden of adjustment fell symmetrically — on surplus countries as much as deficit countries.

```
KEYNES PLAN: THE BANCOR
========================

KEYNES'S DIAGNOSIS OF 1930s FAILURE:
  Under gold standard, all adjustment fell on deficit countries
  Surplus countries (US and France in 1920s) sterilized gold inflows
  (prevented gold from inflating their economies)
  This meant: deficit countries deflated; surplus countries didn't inflate
  = global deflationary bias

THE BANCOR PROPOSAL:
  Create a new international reserve unit: "bancor"
  Not issued by any single country
  Issued by an International Clearing Union (ICU)
  All countries hold accounts at ICU denominated in bancor
  Exchange rates fixed to bancor (but adjustable)

  PAYMENT MECHANISM:
  Country A imports more than it exports:
    A's bancor account is debited (goes negative = "overdraft")
  Country B exports more than it imports:
    B's bancor account is credited (goes positive = "surplus")

  SYMMETRIC PRESSURE ON BOTH:
    Overdraft countries: pay interest on debit balance
    SURPLUS COUNTRIES ALSO: pay interest on CREDIT balance
    (This was the key innovation: making surplus hoarding costly)

  MAXIMUM QUOTA: if overdraft exceeded half of quota, country must:
    devalue currency; or impose capital controls
  IF SURPLUS exceeded quota:
    Must REVALUE currency upward (making imports cheaper)
    Or pay interest penalty on excess surplus

BANCOR QUOTA SIZE:
  Keynes wanted: $25-35 billion total quota
  (Large enough to provide real liquidity cushion)

WHY KEYNES WANTED THIS:
  UK in 1944: exhausted creditor's balance; owed US enormous debts
  UK would be running deficits for years post-war
  Keynes needed: (1) large enough fund to borrow from;
                 (2) symmetric adjustment so US surplus would adjust too;
                 (3) enough flexibility to maintain full employment
```

### What White Wanted (and Got)

Harry Dexter White was the chief US economist on international monetary policy and the real architect of the Bretton Woods system.

```
WHITE PLAN: THE DOLLAR STANDARD
=================================

US NEGOTIATING POSITION (context):
  US held ~70% of world's monetary gold in 1944
  US was the world's largest creditor
  US was the world's largest economy (by far)
  Every country needed access to US markets and capital
  US held all the leverage

  US domestic political constraint:
  Congress would not accept obligations that limited US freedom to act
  A bancor system would force the US to adjust when running surpluses
  = politically unacceptable to US industry (could depress exports)

WHITE'S SYSTEM:
  Dollar pegged to gold at $35/oz (existing US peg)
  All other currencies pegged to dollar
  Convertibility: any country could convert dollars to gold at $35
  (This is the "gold-exchange standard" -- not every country holds gold;
   they hold dollars which are convertible to gold)

  IMF: much smaller than Keynes wanted
    Total resources: $8.8 billion (Keynes wanted $25-35B)
    US quota: $2.75B (largest; gives US effective veto)
    No symmetric pressure on surplus countries

  ADJUSTMENT MECHANISM: entirely on deficit countries
    IMF loans available to deficit countries
    BUT with conditionality (policy conditions)
    Surplus countries had no adjustment obligation

  THE SCARCE CURRENCY CLAUSE (token concession to Keynes):
    IF a currency was declared "scarce" (= that country running
    massive surplus), other countries could discriminate against it
    In practice: never invoked; dead letter
    Keynes claimed this as a win; it was not

RESULT: WHITE PLAN ADOPTED IN ALL ESSENTIALS
  Dollar as reserve currency = US "exorbitant privilege"
  (Barry Estaing's phrase: US could pay for imports with its
   own printed currency; others could not)
  No symmetric adjustment obligation on surplus countries
  This would create the Triffin dilemma within 20 years
```

## IMF: Institutional Mechanics

```
IMF STRUCTURE AND OPERATIONS
==============================

FOUNDING:
  44 countries signed Articles of Agreement at Bretton Woods
  IMF operational from 1947
  Current membership: 190 countries (nearly universal)

GOVERNANCE:
  Quota system:
  - Each country has a "quota" reflecting its economic size
  - Quota determines: (1) how much it can borrow, (2) how many votes it gets
  - US has ~17.4% of votes (as of recent years)
  - EU collectively has ~28% of votes
  - Developing countries chronically underrepresented
  - Decisions requiring 85% supermajority: US has effective veto

  Managing Director: by convention, always a European
  Chief Economist and Deputy MD: often American (informal agreement)

CORE FUNCTIONS:
  1. BALANCE OF PAYMENTS LENDING
     Country A has balance of payments crisis:
     Running out of foreign reserves; cannot pay for imports
     IMF lends foreign currency (Special Drawing Rights, SDRs, or dollars)
     Conditionality: must agree to policy reforms

  2. SURVEILLANCE
     Annual "Article IV" consultations with each member
     IMF monitors macroeconomic and financial stability
     Publishes World Economic Outlook twice yearly

  3. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE
     Advises central banks and finance ministries
     On monetary policy, exchange rate regimes, financial regulation

CONDITIONALITY (the controversial part):
  Standard package evolved over 1970s-1990s:
  - Fiscal austerity: reduce budget deficit
  - Monetary tightening: raise interest rates; control inflation
  - Currency devaluation: restore competitiveness
  - Trade liberalization: reduce tariffs and capital controls
  - Privatization: sell state-owned enterprises

  CRITIQUE (Stiglitz, "Globalization and Its Discontents", 2002):
  - One-size-fits-all; ignored local conditions
  - Contractionary policies in already-depressed economies
  - 1997-98 Asian crisis: IMF conditionality made crisis worse
    (forced interest rate increases worsened capital flight)
  - Policies served creditor interests (US banks holding bonds)
    more than debtor country interests

  IMF response: changed "structural adjustment" to "structural reform";
  softened some conditionality after 2000; learned from East Asian crisis

2010 QUOTA AND VOICE REFORM:
  After the 2008 crisis, emerging markets (particularly China, India,
  Brazil) argued that IMF governance still reflected 1944 power
  distribution, not the current global economy
  2010 reform (took effect 2016 after US Congressional delay):
  → China becomes 3rd largest shareholder (after US and Japan)
  → China, Brazil, India, Russia among top 10 shareholders
  → Two Executive Board seats shifted from European advanced economies
    to under-represented developing countries
  Significance: legitimacy question for a global institution
  An organization where voting power doesn't reflect economic weight
  loses legitimacy with the members it's asking to accept discipline
  The reform was partial: the US still holds ~17% of votes (near the
  15% threshold required to block major decisions — a de facto veto)
  and the IMF Managing Director is traditionally European
```

## World Bank: The IBRD

```
WORLD BANK STRUCTURE
=====================

OFFICIAL NAME: International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD)
Current World Bank Group: five institutions including IBRD, IDA, IFC, etc.

ORIGINAL MANDATE: post-WWII reconstruction
  In practice: France and UK got Marshall Plan (bilateral US aid)
  World Bank barely relevant for reconstruction
  Shifted to development lending quickly

EVOLUTION OF MANDATE:
  1944-1960s: infrastructure loans (dams, roads, power plants)
  1970s:      Robert McNamara era; focus shifted to poverty reduction
              "Basic needs" approach; loans for education, health, agriculture
  1980s:      Structural adjustment loans (conditionality like IMF)
              Washington Consensus era
  1990s-2000s: Poverty reduction again; Millennium Development Goals
               Environmental and social safeguards added
  2010s-now:  Climate change; fragile states; pandemic response

GOVERNANCE:
  Same quota/voting system as IMF
  President: by convention, always an American (informal agreement)
  Challenged by China and others in recent decades

LENDING MECHANICS:
  IBRD: loans to middle-income countries at slightly below-market rates
  IDA: concessional loans and grants to poorest countries; near-zero interest
  IFC: equity and loan investments in private sector of developing countries

CRITICISM:
  "White elephant" projects: large dams, highways that displaced populations
  Environmental damage: approved loans for projects that degraded ecosystems
  Limited effectiveness: Easterly ("The White Man's Burden"):
    $2.3 trillion in aid over 50 years; poverty persists
  Counter-evidence: electrification, clean water, vaccination programs worked
  Debate: is systemic aid effective, or do specific projects matter?
```

## GATT and Trade Liberalization

```
GATT AND WTO EVOLUTION
========================

GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, 1947):
  Not a formal institution initially: just an agreement
  23 founding contracting parties; eventually 128
  Core principle: MOST-FAVORED-NATION (MFN)
    If you give trade preference to one country, you must extend it to all
    Prevents bilateral discrimination; drives toward universal tariff parity

  TARIFF REDUCTION ROUNDS:
  Geneva 1947:          ~21% average tariff cut
  Annecy 1949
  Torquay 1950
  Dillon Round 1960-61
  Kennedy Round 1963-67: 35% average cut
  Tokyo Round 1973-79:   33% average cut
  Uruguay Round 1986-94: services, intellectual property, agriculture added
  Result of Uruguay Round: WTO created (1995)

  US TARIFF RATES:
  1932 (Smoot-Hawley): ~52% average
  1945:                25%
  1990:                 5%
  2020:                 3.5%

WTO (World Trade Organization, January 1, 1995):
  Replaced GATT as formal institution
  164 member countries
  Core innovation: DISPUTE SETTLEMENT BODY
    Binding arbitration for trade disputes
    Retaliatory measures must be proportionate and WTO-authorized
    Much stronger than GATT's voluntary compliance
  China accession: December 11, 2001 (see 09-CONTEMPORARY)
```

## The Marshall Plan

### What It Was and Why It Worked

The European Recovery Program (Marshall Plan, 1948-1952) is the canonical example of successful foreign aid. Why it worked is a matter of ongoing debate, but the aggregate results were clear.

```
MARSHALL PLAN FACTS
====================

ORIGIN:
  Secretary of State George Marshall, Harvard commencement address, June 5, 1947:
  "Europe's requirements are so much greater than her present ability
  to pay that she must have substantial additional help or face economic,
  social, and political deterioration of a very grave character"

  US motivation:
  1. Prevent Communist parties from winning elections in France, Italy
     (genuine concern; French and Italian Communist parties were large)
  2. Create markets for US exports (Europe must be solvent to buy US goods)
  3. Geopolitical: stable, prosperous allies better than unstable, poor ones
  4. Arguably: Keynesian logic at international scale (inject demand)

SOVIET RESPONSE:
  Marshall Plan was offered to ALL European countries including USSR
  Stalin refused; ordered Eastern European countries to refuse
  This was a defining Cold War moment:
  US: "we invited everyone"
  USSR: chose to exclude itself and satellites
  (Stalin suspicious that Marshall Plan would increase US influence)

AMOUNTS:
  Total 1948-1952: $13.2 billion (~$150 billion in 2022 dollars)
  Breakdown by major recipient:
  UK:       $3.3 billion (largest recipient; war debt)
  France:   $2.7 billion
  W.Germany: $1.5 billion
  Italy:    $1.5 billion
  Netherlands, Belgium, others: rest

  As % of US GDP: ~1.2% per year
  Compare: US foreign aid today: ~0.2% of GDP

FORM OF AID:
  ~90% grants (not loans); 10% loans
  Commodity imports: food, fuel, raw materials, industrial equipment
  "Counterpart funds": European governments deposited local currency
  equivalent of US aid; used for local investment projects

DID IT WORK?
  European GDP growth 1948-1952:
  W.Germany: ~35% GDP growth over 4 years
  France:    ~25%
  Italy:     ~30%
  UK:        ~20%

  But was this Marshall Plan or natural recovery?
  Counterfactual problem: Europe was rebuilding anyway;
  infrastructure and know-how were intact; workers were skilled
  Some argue: recovery would have happened anyway, just slower
  Bradford DeLong and Barry Eichengreen: Marshall Plan removed
  specific bottlenecks (raw materials, equipment) that were
  holding back recovery; European human capital was intact
  Marshall Plan made the difference between 5 years to recovery
  and perhaps 10-15 years to recovery
```

## Dollar-Gold Mechanics and the Triffin Dilemma

### How the Bretton Woods Dollar System Worked

```
DOLLAR-GOLD SYSTEM MECHANICS
==============================

BASIC RULE:
  1 oz gold = $35 US
  (US Treasury guaranteed to sell/buy gold at this price
   from foreign central banks)

  All other currencies pegged to dollar within +/-1% band
  Adjustment only with IMF approval

HOW IT FUNCTIONED:
  Japan needed international reserves (to back yen and cover
  balance of payments deficits if needed)
  Japan held: US dollars (earning interest, held in Treasury bills)
  NOT: gold (which earns no interest and is heavy)
  US dollars were "as good as gold" -- you could convert them

  Reserve creation:
  US ran current account deficits: paid out more dollars than earned
  These dollars accumulated as reserves in central banks worldwide
  World dollar reserves grew; world trade expanded; system worked

THE TRIFFIN DILEMMA (Robert Triffin, 1960):
  Written in "Gold and the Dollar Crisis: The Future of Convertibility"

  STATEMENT OF THE DILEMMA:
  For the system to work, the US must run deficits
  (the world needs dollars to use as reserves and to conduct trade)

  But if the US runs deficits:
  Dollar liabilities accumulate (foreigners hold more and more dollars)
  Gold stock stays roughly constant (US can't create gold)
  THEREFORE: the gold-backing ratio deteriorates over time
  EVENTUALLY: foreign central banks hold more dollars than the US
  has gold to back them
  At that point: the convertibility promise becomes a fiction
  IF everyone demanded gold simultaneously: US could not deliver

  EARLY WARNING SIGNS:
  1960: US gold reserves = $17.8 billion; dollar liabilities = $20 billion
       (Dollar liabilities already exceeded gold)
  1968: US gold = $10.9 billion; dollar liabilities = $37 billion

  RESPONSE:
  London Gold Pool (1961): major central banks agreed not to sell/buy
  gold except at $35 to prop up the fiction
  Two-tier gold market (1968): official rate ($35) vs private market
  Both were stalling tactics
```

## Nixon Shock (August 15, 1971)

### The Collapse of Bretton Woods

```
NIXON SHOCK: AUGUST 15, 1971
==============================

BACKGROUND:
  Vietnam War: enormous US government spending; inflation rising
  US trade balance: went negative for first time since 1893 (1971)
  Capital outflows: rising inflation made US assets less attractive
  European central banks (Germany, France especially):
  converting dollar reserves to gold at the $35 rate
  US gold reserves: falling toward critically low levels

  France particularly aggressive: de Gaulle complained about
  US "exorbitant privilege" since 1960s; wanted to force discipline
  Germany had already revalued the mark upward (May 1971);
  speculative flows continued

  August 1971: UK Treasury Department requested the US to provide
  $3 billion gold guarantee for UK dollar holdings
  This was the proximate trigger

CAMP DAVID MEETING (August 13-15, 1971):
  Nixon met with economic advisors: Treasury Secretary John Connally,
  Fed Chairman Arthur Burns, economist Paul Volcker
  Decision: suspend gold convertibility unilaterally
  No advance consultation with IMF or European allies

ANNOUNCEMENT (Sunday August 15, prime-time TV):
  Nixon's "new economic policy":
  1. GOLD WINDOW CLOSED: US will no longer convert dollars to gold
     at $35/oz
  2. Wage and price freeze (90 days): to combat inflation
  3. 10% surcharge on imports: to improve trade balance
  4. Investment tax credit: domestic stimulus

  The gold window closure was permanent.
  The rest was temporary (wage/price freeze abandoned 1974;
  import surcharge removed December 1971 at Smithsonian Agreement)

WHY "SHOCK":
  No advance notice to trading partners
  Connally told Europeans: "The dollar is our currency, but it's
  your problem"
  Exchange markets closed for several days; reopened with dollar
  depreciating sharply

AFTERMATH: SMITHSONIAN AGREEMENT (December 1971)
  Dollar devalued to $38/oz gold (then $42.22)
  Other currencies revalued upward
  Attempted to save fixed rates; failed within 15 months

BRETTON WOODS OFFICIALLY ENDED: March 1973
  Major currencies began floating
  IMF amended Articles of Agreement (1978): permitted floating

WHY DID THE DOLLAR SURVIVE AS RESERVE CURRENCY?
  (Despite losing gold backing)
  1. NETWORK EFFECTS: oil was priced in dollars (OPEC agreement with US);
     to buy oil, you needed dollars; everyone needed oil; everyone needed
     dollars; the demand for dollars was structural
  2. NO ALTERNATIVE: what would you hold instead? German marks (too small
     a market); yen (Japan didn't want reserve currency status);
     SDRs (artificial; no liquid market)
  3. US financial markets: deep, liquid, safe (relatively);
     US Treasury market remains largest, most liquid bond market in world
  4. US military power: dollar-denominated contracts ultimately backed
     by geopolitical power
  5. PATH DEPENDENCE: the entire infrastructure of international finance
     was built around the dollar; switching costs enormous
```

## Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)

```
SPECIAL DRAWING RIGHTS
=======================

CREATED: 1969 (before Nixon shock)
PURPOSE: Supplement US dollar as reserve asset
         (Intended solution to Triffin dilemma:
          create new reserve asset not dependent on US deficits)

WHAT THEY ARE:
  International reserve asset created by IMF
  Not a currency; not a loan; not redeemable for gold
  A claim on freely usable currencies of IMF members
  Value set by basket: currently US dollar, euro, yuan, yen, pound

ISSUES:
  Total SDRs ever created (~1969 to 2021): SDR 204 billion
  (~$293 billion; equivalent to world's reserve holdings in early hours)
  This is tiny compared to global reserves (~$12 trillion)
  SDRs were never made large enough to replace the dollar

COVID RESPONSE (2021):
  IMF approved $650 billion SDR allocation (largest ever)
  Distributed to all members proportional to quota
  Rich countries received most (proportional to quotas)
  Poor countries received less; but amount still meaningful for them
  Several rich countries "rechanneled" SDRs to poorer countries

KEYNES'S GHOST:
  SDRs are the closest thing ever created to Keynes's bancor
  They could theoretically provide global liquidity without
  requiring any country to run deficits
  In practice: underused; never achieved critical mass
  The dollar remains the dominant reserve currency
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Bretton Woods did not create the IMF and World Bank as partners.** The IMF is a monetary fund; the World Bank is a development lender. They have different mandates, different clients, and often different views. During the 1980s-90s, they sometimes gave conflicting advice to the same countries. The "Washington Consensus" was partly the result of their convergence in the 1980s.

**The Bretton Woods dollar-gold system (1944-1971) was different from the classical gold standard (1870-1914).** In the classical gold standard, all countries' currencies were directly convertible to gold. In Bretton Woods, only the US dollar was convertible to gold; other currencies were convertible to dollars. The dollar was the only direct gold anchor. This made the system vulnerable to US balance of payments deficits in a way the classical system was not.

**Nixon did not "take the dollar off the gold standard" domestically.** FDR did that in 1933 (Gold Reserve Act). Nixon closed the *international* gold window — foreign central banks could no longer convert dollars to gold. American citizens hadn't been able to own gold since 1933 and couldn't again until 1974.

**The Marshall Plan was not "just money."** The aid was specifically structured to overcome bottlenecks in European supply chains: raw materials, industrial equipment, fuel. The structure of the aid (counterpart funds deposited locally; OEEC coordination among Europeans) forced European economic cooperation and laid groundwork for the EEC (1957).
