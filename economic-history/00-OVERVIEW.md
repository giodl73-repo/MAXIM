# Economic History — Discipline Overview

## The Big Picture

Economic history sits at the intersection of economics (models, quantification) and history (context, narrative, contingency). The field answers questions that neither discipline can answer alone: *Why did sustained growth begin when and where it did? What caused the Great Divergence? How do institutions shape long-run outcomes?*

```
METHODOLOGICAL SPECTRUM
========================

CLIOMETRICS                                    NARRATIVE HISTORY
(New Economic History)                         (Traditional)
        |                                              |
        |                                              |
 Quantitative data                           Document analysis
 Econometric models                          Archival research
 Counterfactual testing                      Contextualization
 Theory-driven hypothesis                    Case-study depth
        |                                              |
        +----------------+---------------------------+
                         |
                   MODERN SYNTHESIS
              (Both required for strong work)
              Fogel: quantitative + archival
              North: institutions + formal models
              Allen: wage data + technology theory
              Acemoglu: regression + natural experiments


KEY JOURNALS
  Journal of Economic History (1941—)
  Economic History Review (1927—)
  Explorations in Economic History
  Journal of Economic Perspectives (synthesis pieces)
```

## Cliometrics vs. Narrative History

### Cliometrics — The Quantitative Turn

Coined ~1960 by Stan Reiter. Robert Fogel and Douglass North won the 1993 Nobel for pioneering it.

**Core methodology:**
- Construct historical national accounts, price series, wage data
- Apply econometric regression to historical data
- Build explicit counterfactual: "What *would* have happened without railroads / without slavery?"
- Use standard economic theory as interpretive framework

**Fogel's railroad counterfactual (1964):**
```
Standard claim: Railroads were indispensable to American economic growth

Fogel's method:
  Actual 1890 US GNP
  vs.
  Counterfactual 1890 US GNP assuming best-available canal/road network

Result: Railroads added ~5% to GNP — large, but not "indispensable"
         The American economy would have been roughly the same size by 1910

Controversy: Critics said the counterfactual was too clean.
             But the method forced rigor onto a sloppy debate.
```

**Fogel's slavery work (Time on the Cross, 1974):**
- Argued Southern plantation slavery was economically efficient (high TFP)
- Separated moral question from economic efficiency question
- Triggered massive controversy and methodological debate
- Later work (Without Consent or Contract, 1989): quantified mortality, brutality

**North's institutional approach:**
- Property rights and transaction costs as drivers of growth
- Why do some institutions persist even when inefficient? (Path dependence)
- Quantified transaction sector as % of US economy (grew from 25% in 1870 → 45% by 1970)

### Narrative History — What Cliometrics Misses

| Cliometrics Strength | Narrative Strength |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Causal identification | Mechanism detail |
| Comparability across time | Actor motivation |
| Testable hypotheses | Unique events / contingency |
| Aggregates | Local variation |
| Falsifiability | Context and meaning |

**The limits of counterfactuals:** Every counterfactual requires assumptions. "What if no Black Death?" depends on assuming everything else constant — which was never true in history. Narrative historians argue that complex systems don't permit clean isolation.

**Where narrative wins:** The origins of the Glorious Revolution 1688, the decision-making inside the Federal Reserve in 1931, the political economy of Versailles — these require document analysis, not regression.

## Key Datasets

### Maddison Project (GDP Estimates)

Angus Maddison (1926-2010) constructed GDP per capita estimates back to 1 CE. The Maddison Project (University of Groningen) has extended and revised this work.

```
MADDISON PROJECT — KEY FACTS
==============================

Unit: 2011 international dollars (purchasing power parity)
Coverage: 169 countries, 1 CE to present
Method: Mix of national accounts, wage/price series, benchmark years

KEY SERIES:
  gdppc  — GDP per capita (most cited)
  pop    — population estimates
  gdp    — total GDP

CAUTION FLAGS:
  Pre-1800 estimates: very uncertain, especially for Asia/Africa
  Comparability: PPP benchmarks change across revisions
  "GDP" before 1800 doesn't capture what GDP means today
     (subsistence agriculture, non-market production)

ACCESS: https://www.rug.nl/ggdc/historicaldevelopment/maddison/
```

**Key finding from Maddison data:**

THE GREAT FLATNESS: 0–1800 CE — almost no growth

### Clark's Real Wage Data (England 1200-2000)

Gregory Clark constructed real wage series for English laborers using probate records, farm accounts, institutional records.

**Key finding:** English real wages doubled between 1300 and 1800 — almost entirely due to Black Death population shock. Then industrialization raised wages further but slowly until 1850s.

### Long Run Productivity Database (Penn World Tables / Conference Board)

Total Factor Productivity (TFP) series. TFP is the residual after accounting for capital and labor inputs — the "technology" term. TFP growth is what drives sustained long-run living standards improvement.

### Historical Statistics of the United States (Cambridge)

The comprehensive US historical dataset: HSUS online. Covers 1790—present. Everything from wages to tariff rates to railroad mileage.

## Core Analytical Frameworks

### 1. The Malthusian Trap

Before ~1800, the dominant dynamic in human economies:

```
MALTHUSIAN EQUILIBRIUM
=======================

Good harvest / Technology improvement
         |
         v
  More food → population grows
         |
         v
  Per capita income returns to subsistence
         |
         v
  Living standards stagnate

KEY MECHANISM:
  Population adjusts to resources, not vice versa
  "Positive checks": famine, disease, war reduce population
  "Preventive checks": delayed marriage, celibacy reduce birth rate
  Long-run equilibrium: subsistence wages

EVIDENCE:
  England: Black Death (1347) killed ~40% of population
           → surviving workers got substantially higher wages
           → over next century, population recovered
           → wages fell back toward pre-plague level
  China: multiple cycles of expansion/contraction
  India: similar pattern

ESCAPE CONDITIONS (why Malthus became wrong post-1800):
  1. Technology outpaced population growth
  2. Demographic transition (lower birth rates as incomes rose)
  3. Fossil fuels unlocked effectively unlimited energy
  4. Germ theory → sanitation → lower infant mortality WITHOUT
     requiring higher birth rates to compensate
```

### 2. The Great Divergence

Why did Western Europe (and its settler colonies) pull so far ahead of Asia between 1800 and 1970?

```
GREAT DIVERGENCE — COMPETING EXPLANATIONS
==========================================

GEOGRAPHY (Diamond, Pomeranz in part)
  Lucky resource endowment: Atlantic coast for trade
  Coal in accessible locations (Britain)
  Disease environment favors European settler success
  Temperate climate correlates with institutions
  CRITIQUE: Geography is destiny? Hard to falsify.

INSTITUTIONS (North, Acemoglu-Robinson)
  Secure property rights → investment → growth
  Glorious Revolution 1688: Parliament constrains Crown
  → credible commitment to repay debt
  → capital flows to England
  → industrialization financed
  "Why Nations Fail": extractive vs inclusive institutions
  CRITIQUE: What causes good institutions? (Circular)

HIGH-WAGE HYPOTHESIS (Robert Allen)
  British wages were uniquely high (coal miners, artisans)
  → Labor-saving technology was PROFITABLE in Britain
  → Spinning jenny, power loom, steam engine all
     substituted expensive labor
  → Same technology wouldn't have been adopted in India
     where labor was cheap
  EVIDENCE: Detailed wage/energy cost comparisons across
             Britain, France, Netherlands, India, China

CULTURE / PROTESTANT WORK ETHIC (Weber)
  Protestantism → saving, rational profit-seeking → capitalism
  CRITIQUE: Catholics industrialized too; timing doesn't line up
             perfectly; correlation vs causation

COLONIAL EXPLOITATION (Frank, Wallerstein, Patnaik)
  Extraction of resources from colonies funded European growth
  AND suppressed development in colonized regions
  Patnaik: estimates £45 trillion extracted from India 1765-1938
  CRITIQUE: Hard to separate trade gains from extraction;
             counterfactuals contested; some colonies saw
             infrastructure investment

POMERANZ SYNTHESIS (The Great Divergence, 2000)
  Britain and China's Yangtze Delta were roughly equivalent
  in living standards circa 1750
  Two factors explain Britain's breakaway:
  1. Coal geography: Britain's coal was near industrial centers;
     China's coal was in the northwest, far from population
  2. Atlantic colonies: provided land/resources that relieved
     Malthusian pressure, especially in food and raw materials
```

### 3. Institutional Economics

Douglass North's framework: institutions are the "rules of the game" — formal (laws, property rights, contracts) and informal (norms, culture, trust).

```
INSTITUTIONAL ECONOMICS FRAMEWORK
===================================

INSTITUTIONS
    |
    |-- Formal rules: constitutions, laws, property rights
    |-- Informal norms: culture, trust, reciprocity
    |-- Enforcement mechanisms: courts, reputation, violence
    |
    v
TRANSACTION COSTS (Coase, North)
    |
    |-- Search and information costs
    |-- Bargaining and decision costs
    |-- Policing and enforcement costs
    |
    v
ECONOMIC PERFORMANCE
    |
    |-- High transaction costs → less specialization → poverty
    |-- Low transaction costs → markets expand → growth

PATH DEPENDENCE:
  Institutions persist even when inefficient
  QWERTY keyboard: suboptimal lock-in
  Southern US slave economy: persisted because institutional
    beneficiaries had political power to block reform
  North: "The death of inefficient institutions requires
          either external shock or internal contradiction"

NATURAL STATE vs OPEN ACCESS ORDER (North, Wallis, Weingast 2009):
  Natural State: elites limit competition to generate rents;
                 violence controlled through rent distribution
  Open Access: impersonal rule of law; Schumpeterian creative
               destruction permitted; broad political access
  Transition between them: very rare, hard, path-dependent
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Correlation vs causation in historical data.** Institutions, geography, and development all correlate. Acemoglu-Johnson-Robinson (2001) used settler mortality as an instrument for institutions — mortality determined settlement patterns, which determined institution type — to separate institutions from geography. This is the gold standard of identification in economic history. Many other results are more fragile.

**Maddison numbers are estimates, not measurements.** Pre-1800 GDP figures carry enormous uncertainty. The error bars on "China GDP 1500" could span a factor of 2. Use them for order-of-magnitude comparisons, not precise claims.

**The Malthusian framework doesn't mean nothing improved pre-1800.** Cities, trade, specialization all developed. But *sustained per capita income growth* was largely absent. A good harvest or technological improvement raised population, not wages, within a generation or two.

**Great Divergence ≠ "West was always ahead."** As late as 1750, Chinese and Indian economies were comparable in total size to European economies. The divergence is specifically an 1800-1970 phenomenon. In total GDP (not per capita), China was the world's largest economy for most of recorded history until ~1890.

**Cliometrics proved the South's slavery was economically efficient — not that it was desirable or inevitable.** Fogel was explicit on this separation. The efficiency finding made the slavery debate harder, not easier, to dismiss with simple economic arguments. That was the point.

## Decision Cheat Sheet — Which Framework When

| Question Type | Best Framework | Key Source |
|--------------|----------------|------------|
| Long-run growth comparisons | Maddison Project + growth accounting | Maddison Project database |
| Why industrialization happened where/when | Allen's high-wage hypothesis | Allen, *The British Industrial Revolution in Global Perspective* |
| Why some countries stayed poor | Acemoglu-Robinson institutions | *Why Nations Fail* (2012) |
| Role of geography | Diamond's lucky latitudes, Sachs on disease | *Guns Germs and Steel*; Sachs papers |
| Trade and globalization waves | Baldwin's stages + Maddison GDP | *The Great Convergence* (2016) |
| Financial crises: causes and responses | Kindleberger + Reinhart-Rogoff | *Manias Panics and Crashes*; *This Time Is Different* |

| Institutional persistence | North + path dependence | *Institutions, Institutional Change* (1990) |
| Convergence / divergence | Growth regressions + Solow decomposition | Barro-Sala-i-Martin |
