# World Wars and the Great Depression — Economic History

## The Big Picture

<!-- @editor[diagram/P2]: Opening diagram is a timeline, not a landscape — consider a system diagram showing how war finance, reparations, gold standard, monetary contraction, and policy responses structurally interconnect -->

The period 1914-1945 destroyed the first globalization, produced the worst economic catastrophe in recorded history, and rebuilt the foundation for a new global economic order. The gold standard propagated the Depression internationally. Misguided fiscal and monetary policy deepened it. And WWII ironically demonstrated Keynesian economics in the most definitive possible experiment.

```
THE INTERWAR PERIOD: SEQUENCE OF CRISES
=========================================

1914        WWI begins; gold standard suspended
            Capital flows collapse; tariffs rise
            First globalization ends

1914-1918   War finance: bonds, printing, borrowing
            UK: debt rose from 24% to 136% of GDP
            Germany: deficit financed by money printing
            US: remained creditor; entered 1917

1919        Versailles Treaty: German reparations
            Keynes resigns; publishes "Economic Consequences of the Peace"

1920-1921   Post-war depression (brief): deflation; US contraction
            US bounces back; European economies struggle

1921-1923   Weimar hyperinflation: Papiermark destroyed
            Reparations crisis; Ruhr occupation

1924-1928   "Golden Twenties": apparent stability
            Dawes Plan (1924): reparations restructured
            US capital flows to Germany; Germany to reparations creditors

1929        US stock market crash: October
            Credit tightens; margin calls cascade

1930        Smoot-Hawley Tariff: US raises tariffs
            Trading partners retaliate; trade collapses ~66%

1930-1933   Banking crisis: 9,000 US bank failures
            Deflation spiral: prices fall; real debt rises; more defaults
            Fed fails to act as lender of last resort

1931        UK abandons gold standard: September
            Countries that leave gold earliest recover earliest

1933        FDR inaugurated: bank holiday; New Deal begins
            US abandons gold domestically (not internationally yet)

1936        Keynes publishes "General Theory"

1939        WWII begins: government spending explodes
1942        US unemployment effectively zero
            WWII as Keynesian experiment: proven
```

## WWI War Finance

### The Fiscal Crisis of Total War

WWI was the first industrial-scale total war. The financial demands exceeded anything any government had previously financed.

```
WWI WAR FINANCE COMPARISON
============================

COSTS (current prices, very approximate):
  UK:       $177 billion  (1918 dollars)
  Germany:  $175 billion
  France:   $146 billion
  US:        $33 billion  (entered 1917, lower total costs)

UK DEBT TRAJECTORY:
  National debt / GDP:
  1913:  24%
  1918: 136%
  (Net debt, excluding inter-Allied loans)

FINANCING METHODS:
  1. TAXATION: raised rates; new taxes
     UK: income tax doubled; excess profits tax introduced
     Covered ~30% of UK war costs
  2. DOMESTIC BORROWING: war bonds
     UK "War Savings Certificates"; small denomination (accessible to all)
     US "Liberty Bonds": huge propaganda campaign; ~$17B raised
     Covered ~50-60% of UK costs; ~80% of US costs
  3. MONEY CREATION: Printed money
     Germany: largest reliance; printed to cover deficits
     UK: some; less than Germany
     This planted seeds of post-war inflation

  Germany's problem: blockaded by British Navy
  Could not import food or raw materials
  Had to maximize domestic war production; printed money to finance it
  Inflation already elevated during war (~50% 1914-1918)
  But the massive hyperinflation came AFTER the war (see below)

US EXPERIENCE (entered April 1917):
  Was the world's leading creditor by 1914 (had been debtor until 1890s)
  Lent to UK and France during 1914-1917 (while neutral)
  When entered war: raised taxes; Liberty Bonds; Federal Reserve expanded
  Came out of war as unambiguous financial hegemon
  UK and France owed US billions
```

### Versailles Reparations

The reparations settlement at Versailles (1919) was both an economic mistake and a political disaster. Keynes saw it clearly and said so publicly.

```
VERSAILLES REPARATIONS
========================

AMOUNT:
  132 billion gold marks (set 1921; London Schedule)
  In practice: "A Bonds" (50B marks) were to be issued immediately
               "B Bonds" (82B marks) were indefinitely deferred
               (effectively: immediate demand was ~50B marks)
  50 billion gold marks in 1921 = approximately $12.5 billion USD
  = roughly 100% of Germany's 1913 GDP

GERMANY'S PAYMENT PROBLEM:
  To pay reparations, Germany needed to:
  EITHER: export goods (and accept that creditors received German goods
           as payment -- which Allied industries opposed, as it
           competed with their own production)
  OR: borrow abroad (get dollars/pounds from foreign banks; pay creditors)
  OR: print marks and buy foreign currency (hyperinflation route)

  The political economy made option 1 impossible:
  French and British steel and textiles did not want German competition
  The transfer problem (Keynes vs Ohlin debate, 1929):
  Keynes: paying reparations requires running a trade surplus;
          running a surplus requires making goods cheaper (devaluation);
          creditors won't accept devaluation; therefore reparations
          cannot be paid without severe deflationary adjustment
  Ohlin: adjustment is possible through income effects
  Keynes was more right

KEYNES: "ECONOMIC CONSEQUENCES OF THE PEACE" (1919)
  Keynes was British Treasury representative at Versailles
  He resigned, returned to Cambridge, wrote the book in 2 months
  Key arguments:
  1. Reparations were unpayable at the amounts demanded
  2. The peace terms would destroy the German middle class
  3. Economic instability in Germany would destabilize all of Europe
  4. The victors were imposing a Carthaginian peace that would
     breed resentment and future conflict
  Historical verdict: largely correct on all points
  Hitler's rise was not inevitable from Versailles, but the economic
  grievances Keynes predicted were weaponized by demagogues
```

## Weimar Hyperinflation (1921-1923)

### The Mechanics

The Weimar hyperinflation was not caused by reparations alone — but reparations were the proximate trigger for the critical 1922-1923 episode.

```
WEIMAR HYPERINFLATION MECHANICS
=================================

BACKGROUND:
  Germany emerged from WWI with:
  - Inflation already elevated (~50% higher prices than 1914)
  - Government committed to full reparations payments
  - Economy weakened by defeat and Allied blockade
  - New republic (Weimar, est. 1919) politically fragile

SEQUENCE:
  1921: Germany pays first reparations installment; gold reserves depleted
  1922: Cannot pay; mark begins falling on exchange markets
  January 1923: Germany defaults on timber deliveries (reparations in kind)
  January 11, 1923: FRANCE AND BELGIUM OCCUPY THE RUHR
    (Germany's industrial heartland; coal, iron, steel)
    Legal justification: Germany in default on reparations
    Practical effect: Germany loses ~80% of coal output; 70% of iron/steel

  German government response: "PASSIVE RESISTANCE"
  Told German workers and civil servants in Ruhr: do not work for occupiers
  Paid for this passive resistance by: PRINTING MONEY

  The Reichsbank printed marks to pay workers who weren't working
  While simultaneously losing its main revenue-producing region

PRICE ESCALATION:
  Exchange rate (marks per dollar):
  July 1914:       4.2 marks/$
  January 1920:  105 marks/$
  January 1922:  192 marks/$
  November 1922: 7,400 marks/$  (fast but not yet hyperinflation)
  June 1923:     100,000 marks/$
  August 1923: 4,600,000 marks/$
  October 1923: 25,000,000,000 marks/$
  November 1923: 4,200,000,000,000 marks/$  (4.2 trillion per dollar)

  HYPERINFLATION DEFINITION: more than 50% per month inflation
  Germany's peak: prices roughly DOUBLING EVERY 3-4 DAYS

REAL EFFECTS:
  Who lost:
    Holders of fixed-income assets (bonds, bank deposits, pensions)
    Middle class savings wiped out
    Landlords with fixed-rate mortgages -- mortgages became worthless
    but so did their rents (fixed in nominal terms)
  Who gained (short-term):
    Debtors (repaid nominal debts with nearly worthless marks)
    Manufacturers with physical plant (real assets held value)
    Foreign currency holders (exchange rate arbitrage)
    The government (real value of domestic debt destroyed)

STABILIZATION (November 1923):
  Rentenbankmark (Rentenmark) introduced November 15, 1923
  Exchange rate: 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old Papiermarks
  Backed by: hypothetical mortgage on German land and industry
  (Not actually convertible; the "backing" was symbolic)
  Hjalmar Schacht (Reichsbank president): ended passive resistance;
  stopped Ruhr occupation payments; negotiated Dawes Plan (1924)

  WHY DID STABILIZATION WORK?
  Not because of the Rentenmark's "backing" (no gold; land mortgages
  were not really convertible)
  Because the German government COMMITTED CREDIBLY to stop printing
  Fiscal adjustment: government cut spending, raised taxes
  Expectation of future money supply fixed exchange rate expectations
  This is: the fiscal theory of the price level in action
```

**Political consequences:** The hyperinflation of 1923 wiped out the savings of Germany's middle class (professionals, merchants, civil servants). This created a class of economically desperate, politically angry citizens with no remaining stake in the existing financial system — fertile ground for extremist politics. The Nazi Party's early support came disproportionately from the same middle classes devastated by hyperinflation.

## The Great Depression

### The Crash of 1929

The stock market crash of October 1929 was the trigger but not the cause of the Depression. The deeper structural problems were overextension of credit, agricultural weakness, and international financial imbalances.

```
CRASH MECHANICS (OCTOBER 1929)
================================

BACKGROUND:
  1920s: US stock market tripled 1926-1929
  "The New Era": technology stocks (autos, radios, electricity)
  Rising on genuine productivity growth + speculative momentum
  Margin buying widespread: investors bought stocks with ~10% down,
  90% borrowed from brokers ("call money")
  Interest rates: Federal Reserve raised rates in 1928-1929
  to cool speculation (without deflating economy)

BLACK THURSDAY (October 24, 1929):
  12.9 million shares traded; prices fell
  Bankers pooled resources to stabilize (temporary)

BLACK TUESDAY (October 29, 1929):
  16.4 million shares traded; prices collapsed
  Margin calls: brokers demanded collateral from leveraged investors
  Margin calls forced selling; selling depressed prices; more calls
  Classic debt-deflation cascade

DOW JONES INDUSTRIAL AVERAGE:
  September 3, 1929:  381 (peak)
  November 13, 1929:  199 (-48%)
  July 8, 1932:        41 (-89% from peak)
  (Did not recover 1929 level until 1954)

WHAT THE CRASH DID:
  Destroyed ~$30 billion in paper wealth (immediately)
  Damaged consumer confidence
  Tightened credit (banks had lent to brokers; brokers' collateral collapsed)

WHAT THE CRASH DID NOT DO:
  The crash alone did not cause the Depression
  Previous crashes (1907, 1921) had not led to decade-long depression
  The Depression required additional policy failures to become the Depression
```

### Bank Failures and the Monetary Contraction

This is where Friedman and Schwartz (A Monetary History of the United States, 1963) make their central argument: the Fed caused the Depression by allowing the money supply to collapse.

```
BANKING CRISIS AND MONETARY CONTRACTION
=========================================

BANK FAILURES:
  1929: 659 bank failures
  1930: 1,350 bank failures  (First Banking Panic, Nov-Dec 1930)
  1931: 2,293 bank failures  (Second Banking Panic, spring 1931)
  1932: 1,453 bank failures
  1933: 4,000 bank failures  (Third Banking Panic, Feb-Mar 1933)
  TOTAL 1930-1933: ~9,000 bank failures; ~$7B in deposits lost

  NO DEPOSIT INSURANCE EXISTED:
  Deposits were completely unprotected
  When bank failed: depositors lost everything (or waited for liquidation)

MONEY SUPPLY COLLAPSE:
  M2 (broad money supply):
  August 1929:  $46.6 billion
  April 1933:   $30.8 billion
  = Decline of 34%

FRIEDMAN-SCHWARTZ ARGUMENT:
  The Fed had the tools to prevent this
  Federal Reserve could have:
  1. Lent to distressed banks (lender of last resort function)
  2. Conducted open market operations (buy government securities;
     inject reserves into banking system)
  3. Lowered discount rate more aggressively

  Instead:
  - Fed raised rates in 1931 (!) to defend the gold standard
    when UK left gold and investors feared US would follow
  - Failed to lend to Bank of United States (Dec 1930) -- a major
    bank; allowed it to fail; triggered first banking panic
  - Open market operations: inadequate size

  CONCLUSION: a monetary contraction of 1/3 was not a natural disaster;
  it was a policy failure that transformed a recession into a depression

BEN BERNANKE'S TRIBUTE (2002):
  Bernanke (then Fed Governor, later Chairman) at Friedman's 90th birthday:
  "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry. But thanks to you,
  we won't do it again."
  (In 2008, Bernanke's Fed acted precisely as Friedman prescribed:
  lent freely; expanded money supply; prevented monetary collapse)
```

### The Gold Standard as Transmission Mechanism

Barry Eichengreen (Golden Fetters, 1992) showed that the gold standard propagated the Depression internationally.

```
GOLD STANDARD AS DEPRESSION TRANSMISSION
==========================================

MECHANISM:
  1929: US tightens monetary policy (to stop stock speculation)
        Gold flows to US (higher returns)
        Other countries lose gold
        Must tighten monetary policy to defend gold peg
        Global deflation simultaneously

  1930: Smoot-Hawley tariffs (US): partners retaliate
        Trade collapses; countries all tightening simultaneously

  1931: Austria's Creditanstalt fails (May): biggest Austrian bank
        Panic spreads to Germany
        Germany imposes capital controls
        UK: runs on pound sterling; Bank of England gold depletes
        September 21, 1931: UK LEAVES GOLD STANDARD
        Immediate effect: pound depreciates; UK exports become cheaper
        UK recovery begins: 1932

EICHENGREEN'S KEY FINDING:
  "The date a country left the gold standard
   is the date its recovery began"

  Countries leaving gold:
  UK (1931): recovery begins 1932
  US (April 1933): recovery begins 1933
  France, Belgium, Netherlands: stayed on gold until 1936
  France's depression lasted 7 years; finally left gold 1936
  France recovered in 1937

  The gold standard was not just constraining -- it was transmitting
  deflation mechanically around the world

WHY COULDN'T COUNTRIES JUST LEAVE SOONER?
  1. Gold standard was not a policy choice -- it was a credibility anchor
     Leaving gold = admission of inability to manage finances
     = destroys country's international credit rating
  2. Political economy: central bankers and finance ministers were
     "gold bugs" ideologically; saw gold as the only sound money
  3. International coordination: if you leave first, you're the "cheater"
     Social pressure to maintain the system
  4. Creditors (banks, bondholders) wanted deflation -- debts worth more
     Labor wanted inflation -- wages maintained
     Political power determined outcome
```

## The New Deal

### What the New Deal Actually Was

Franklin Roosevelt's New Deal (1933-1939) has been mythologized in two directions: as the salvation of capitalism and as socialism. Neither is accurate.

```
NEW DEAL PROGRAMS
==================

EMERGENCY MEASURES (1933):
  Emergency Banking Act (March 9, 1933): bank holiday; reopened
  sound banks; closed insolvent ones; restored confidence
  FDR's first fireside chat: explained to Americans it was safer
  to keep money in banks than under mattresses
  Effect: bank runs stopped; deposits returned

  Glass-Steagall Act (1933):
  1. FDIC: Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation
     Insured deposits up to $2,500 (later raised)
     THIS WAS THE MOST IMPORTANT SINGLE REFORM
     Eliminated the bank-run incentive
     If deposits are insured, no reason to panic-withdraw
  2. Separated commercial and investment banking
     Commercial banks: deposits; could not underwrite securities
     Investment banks: could not take deposits
     (Repealed 1999 by Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act; this contributed to 2008)

  Gold standard domestically ended: April 1933
  Gold Reserve Act (January 1934): gold revalued $20.67 to $35/oz
  (Dollar devalued vs gold by 41%; roughly a 41% devaluation vs
  other currencies still on gold standard)
  Effect: US prices rose; deflation arrested; recovery began

ALPHABET AGENCIES:
  AAA (Agricultural Adjustment Act): paid farmers to reduce output
      Raised food prices; helped farmers; controversial (poor displaced)
  NRA (National Recovery Administration): industry codes; minimum wages;
      collective bargaining rights; price-fixing
      Struck down by Supreme Court (Schechter Poultry, 1935): unconstitutional
  CCC (Civilian Conservation Corps): 3 million young men; conservation work;
      $30/month; sent $25 home to families
  PWA (Public Works Administration): Ickes; major infrastructure
      $3.3 billion over 6 years; bridges, dams, schools
  WPA (Works Progress Administration): Hopkins; $11 billion; 8.5 million workers
      Federal Art Project; Federal Theatre Project; Federal Writers Project

SECOND NEW DEAL (1935):
  Social Security Act: old-age pensions; unemployment insurance
  Wagner Act: guaranteed right to unionize; collective bargaining
  Banking Act 1935: strengthened Fed's powers; Open Market Committee formalized

KEYNES AND THE NEW DEAL:
  Keynes wrote an open letter to FDR in New York Times (1933):
  "Dear Mr President, spending is the only way out"
  FDR met Keynes once (1934): said he found him too mathematical
  THE NEW DEAL WAS NOT SUFFICIENTLY KEYNESIAN:
  Federal spending increased, but not enough to close output gap
  FDR worried about deficits; reduced spending 1937-38
  1937-38 recession ("Roosevelt Recession"): deflationary fiscal shift
  caused sharp relapse
  This actually proved Keynesian theory: spending cuts during recovery
  cause recession; the cure was more spending, not less
```

## WWII as Keynesian Experiment

### The Definitive Test

If the New Deal was too small to fully test Keynesian theory, WWII resolved any doubts.

```
WWII AS KEYNESIAN STIMULUS
============================

US UNEMPLOYMENT:
  1933:  25%
  1936:  17%
  1938:  19% (Roosevelt recession)
  1940:  15%
  1941:  10%
  1942:   4.7% (effectively full employment)
  1944:   1.2% (below any definition of full employment)

  THE DEPRESSION THAT COULD NOT BE ENDED BY MODERATE NEW DEAL
  SPENDING WAS ENDED BY MASSIVE WARTIME SPENDING

SPENDING SCALE:
  Federal spending as % of GDP:
  1939:  10%
  1940:  10%
  1941:  22%
  1942:  44%
  1943:  52%  (PEAK)
  1944:  50%

  Total war spending 1941-1945: ~$4.1 trillion (2011 dollars)

  MULTIPLIER: Keynes argued government spending multiplied through economy
  For every $1 government spent, GDP increased more than $1
  (because each dollar of spending became income to workers;
  they spent more; that became income to others; etc.)
  WWII evidence is consistent with multiplier of 1.5-2.0

WAR FINANCE:
  Not all war spending was deficit financed
  Individual income tax: expanded massively
  1939: ~4 million taxpayers; 1945: ~43 million taxpayers
  Withholding introduced 1943 (Milton Friedman helped design it)
  War bonds: $185 billion sold
  Deficit: still ran; debt/GDP rose to ~120% by 1945

WARTIME ECONOMIC CONTROLS:
  Rationing: meat, butter, sugar, gasoline, tires, shoes
  Price controls: Office of Price Administration
  Wage controls: War Labor Board
  Renegotiation of contracts: war profits clawback
  These were administratively complex and partially ineffective;
  black markets emerged; but aggregate controls worked

  THE KEY LESSON: The economy was not self-correcting
  From 1929 to 1941 = 12 years of persistently high unemployment
  Only war spending -- directed government demand -- cleared the market
  This is the empirical foundation of Keynesian macroeconomics
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Weimar hyperinflation was not caused by the Versailles reparations alone.** The root cause was the fiscal and monetary policy response to the Ruhr occupation (1923). Reparations created the pressure; the government chose to finance passive resistance by printing money. Alternative: accept the occupation, allow French extraction, stabilize the currency (at political cost). Germany chose money printing; France (1926) chose fiscal austerity. Different choices; different outcomes.

**The Smoot-Hawley Tariff did not cause the Depression.** It deepened and lengthened it by collapsing trade. Trade was ~7% of US GDP; even full trade collapse could not cause a 30% GDP contraction. The monetary contraction was far more damaging. Smoot-Hawley's main harm: made international cooperation on gold standard adjustment impossible.

**The New Deal did not end the Depression.** GDP recovered to 1929 levels by about 1937. But unemployment was still 15-17%. It took WWII spending to eliminate unemployment. The New Deal reformed the financial system (FDIC, Securities Acts), provided relief, and restored confidence — but fell short of the spending needed for full recovery.

**Gold standard abandonment was not obviously the right policy at the time.** Sound-money advocates had a coherent argument: leaving gold destroys credibility; future borrowing becomes expensive; long-run costs exceed short-run benefits. They were wrong empirically (countries that left gold recovered), but the argument was not crazy. Eichengreen's data settled the debate retrospectively.

**WWII's economic "success" does not straightforwardly prove deficit spending is always effective.** WWII combined: massive spending + binding administrative controls + near-total-war national mobilization + specific demand shocks. Generalizing to peacetime fiscal stimulus requires care. The multiplier in WWII may have been higher than in normal recessions due to the specific circumstances.
