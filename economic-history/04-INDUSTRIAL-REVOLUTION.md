# The Industrial Revolution — Economic History

## The Big Picture

The Industrial Revolution (Britain, ~1760-1840; spreading globally ~1840-1913) was the first sustained escape from the Malthusian trap in human history. Real wages began rising persistently. Energy consumption per capita began rising. Urbanization accelerated. The key historical puzzle: why Britain first, and why then?

```
INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION OVERVIEW
================================

                    GDP PER CAPITA GROWTH (Britain)
                    =================================
  400  |
       |                                      *
  300  |                                   ***
       |                                ***
  200  |                  *** (plateau)***
       |         (slow rise 1650-1760) /
  100  |**************************
       |
     1700   1750   1760  1780  1800  1830  1860  1900
                     ^                 ^
                   START              TAKEOFF
                 (textiles)        (railways, steam)

KEY COMPETING EXPLANATIONS FOR "WHY BRITAIN FIRST":

  GEOGRAPHY          Coal near surface and near industry
  (Pomeranz, Allen)  Coastal geography for trade

  INSTITUTIONS       Glorious Revolution 1688:
  (North-Weingast)   Parliament constrains Crown
                     Secure property rights
                     Credible debt commitments

  HIGH WAGES         British wages uniquely high
  (Allen)            Labor-saving innovation profitable only where
                     labor expensive relative to energy (coal)

  CULTURE            Protestant work ethic (Weber)
  (Weber, Mokyr)     Enlightenment empiricism; "useful knowledge"
                     culture of improvement

  DEMAND             Colonial markets absorbed manufactured goods
  (Broadberry)       Atlantic trade enabled specialization

  MOST LIKELY:       All of the above are complementary
                     Geography + institutions + wages created a
                     system where innovation was profitable AND
                     financeable AND enforceable
```

## The "Why Britain First" Debate

### Allen's High-Wage Hypothesis

Robert Allen (Oxford, then NYU) constructed detailed wage and energy price series across Europe, India, and China for 1700-1850. His argument is the most data-driven explanation for why Britain industrialized first.

```
ALLEN'S HIGH-WAGE / CHEAP-ENERGY ARGUMENT
===========================================

KEY DATA (real wages, daily, in grams of silver, ~1750):
  London:       32 grams silver/day
  Amsterdam:    24
  Leipzig:      12
  Paris:        14
  Milan:        12
  Florence:     10
  Strasbourg:   11
  Delhi:         4
  Beijing:       6

British wages were roughly 3-4x higher than Asian wages
Dutch wages were also high (second most likely to industrialize)

ENERGY PRICES (cost of 1 million BTUs, 1750):
  Britain (coal):    1.5 shillings
  France (wood):     8 shillings
  Asia (wood):       variable but higher than Britain for coal equivalent

THE CRUCIAL RATIO:
  Cost of replacing one day's labor with energy input:
  London: labor expensive, coal cheap -- ratio FAVORS innovation
  India:  labor cheap, fuel moderately priced -- ratio DISCOURAGES innovation

EXAMPLE: The Spinning Jenny (James Hargreaves, 1764)
  Old method:  one spinner, one spindle, paid ~6d/day
  New machine: 8 spindles, one worker, costs money to build

  In England: pays off quickly because labor saved is expensive
  In India:   labor so cheap that machine cannot pay off its capital cost
              = no incentive to adopt

ALLEN'S PREDICTION:
  The technologies developed in Britain were specifically designed
  to substitute expensive British labor with cheap British energy
  These same technologies were NOT economically rational to adopt
  in India or China until wages rose (which is why Asia didn't
  industrialize until much later, or when they copied British
  technology that was now cheap to copy)

  IMPLICATION: Industrialization happened in Britain not because
  the British were smarter or culturally superior, but because the
  PRICE SIGNALS in Britain made labor-saving investment profitable
```

### North-Weingast: The Institutional Hypothesis

Douglass North and Barry Weingast (1989 paper: "Constitutions and Commitment") argued that the Glorious Revolution (1688) created the institutional precondition for industrialization by making property rights secure.

```
GLORIOUS REVOLUTION 1688 AND INSTITUTIONS
==========================================

BEFORE 1688:
  Stuart kings (Charles II, James II) could:
  - Arbitrarily tax merchants and landowners
  - Repudiate Crown debts (Charles II: Stop of the Exchequer, 1672:
    simply refused to repay ~1.3 million pounds owed to goldsmith banks)
  - Grant/revoke monopolies at will
  - Result: high risk for capital investment; capital demanded high returns;
             industrial investment was speculative and underfinanced

1688: WILLIAM OF ORANGE INVASION + BILL OF RIGHTS
  James II fled; William III invited by Parliament
  Bill of Rights 1689:
  - Parliament controls taxation
  - Parliament controls standing armies
  - Free elections; free speech in Parliament
  - No suspension of laws by Crown without Parliamentary consent

NORTH-WEINGAST ARGUMENT:
  After 1688:
  - Crown could not arbitrarily repudiate debt
  - Parliament controlled taxation = no arbitrary expropriation
  - Property rights became credible: investors could plan long-term

  IMMEDIATE EFFECT:
  Bank of England founded 1694: government now had credible mechanism
  to borrow at low interest rates
  Interest on government debt fell from ~14% (1680s) to ~3% (1730s)

  LOW INTEREST RATES = available capital for industrial investment

  CRITIQUE (Clark, Allen):
  - Dutch Republic had similar property rights but did NOT industrialize first
  - England already had reasonable property rights before 1688
  - The timing is imprecise: industrialization started 70 years after 1688
  - Wages and geography may matter more than institutions per se

RESOLUTION: Institutions are necessary but not sufficient.
  Britain needed: secure property rights (post-1688) AND
                  high wages (making innovation profitable) AND
                  cheap coal (making energy substitution cheap) AND
                  Atlantic trade (demand for manufactured goods)
```

### Why Not China?

China in 1750 was technologically sophisticated: printing, paper money, sophisticated ceramics, advanced agriculture, large-scale canal networks. The Song Dynasty (960-1279) had proto-industrialization in iron and steel production. Why didn't China industrialize first?

```
WHY NOT CHINA (Pomeranz, Allen)
=================================

ARGUMENT 1: COAL GEOGRAPHY (Allen)
  China has substantial coal deposits
  BUT: they are in the northwest (Shanxi, Shaanxi provinces)
  Industrial demand centers: coastal provinces (Jiangsu, Guangdong,
    Zhejiang) -- the dense population and commercial economy
  Distance between coal and industry: 1,000+ miles
  18th-century transport cost: prohibitively expensive by cart or barge

  In Britain:
  Coal deposits in Lancashire, Yorkshire, Staffordshire, S. Wales
  Industrial centers: same regions
  Distance: often under 20 miles
  Canal transport cheap; later railways

  RESULT: British coal was effectively ~5x cheaper per BTU for
  industrial use compared to the best available fuel in Chinese
  industrial centers (wood and charcoal)

ARGUMENT 2: HIGH-SKILL ARTISANAL EQUILIBRIUM (Pomeranz)
  China's Yangtze Delta by 1750: extremely high skill level in
  silk and cotton textile production
  Workers were producing high-quality goods using labor-intensive,
  skill-intensive techniques
  Demand: luxury silk (expensive, high-quality) not mass cotton

  British textile innovation (spinning jenny, power loom) was about
  producing CHEAP COTTON in BULK for mass markets
  Chinese textile economy was oriented toward quality, not volume
  The markets were different; the optimal technology was different

ARGUMENT 3: INSTITUTIONAL DIFFERENCES
  Imperial China: talented administrators ("mandarins") captured
  in examination system; commercial success did not translate to
  political power as directly as in Britain
  High-level equilibrium trap (Elvin): very efficient existing
  technology for existing resource/population ratio
  → little pressure to innovate

  Note: This is contested; China had sophisticated commercial law
  and merchant families with political connections

ARGUMENT 4: WHAT ACTUALLY DID INDUSTRIALIZE CHINA?
  Japan (Meiji Restoration 1868): conscious technological transfer
  China: coastal Treaty Ports, then 1950s Soviet-style forced
  industrialization, then export-led growth 1980s onward
  In all cases: imported technology, not indigenous development
```

## Textile Mechanization

The British textile industry was the proving ground for industrialization. The mechanization of spinning and weaving between 1764 and 1830 demonstrated that factories and machines could achieve productivity gains far beyond any artisanal workshop.

```
TEXTILE MECHANIZATION SEQUENCE
================================

STARTING POINT (~1750):
  Spinning: done by women at home, one spindle each
             "spinster" is the original term for an unmarried woman
             (because spinning was her primary activity)
  Weaving:  done by male weavers on hand looms, one person, one loom
  Bottleneck: weaving was faster than spinning; weavers sat idle
              waiting for yarn
  Technology: no steam; water mills used for fulling (finishing cloth)

1764: SPINNING JENNY (James Hargreaves)
  8 spindles on one machine, operated by one worker
  Could be used at home; no water power needed
  Increased spinning productivity ~8x
  Impact: flooded market with cheap yarn; weavers now had surplus

1769: WATER FRAME (Richard Arkwright)
  Powered by water wheel; could not be used at home
  Required factory location near river
  Produced stronger, coarser thread suitable for warp (structural thread)
  Key innovation: rollers moving at different speeds to draft the cotton
  Arkwright's factories: first industrial factories in modern sense
    - Cromford Mill (1771): 200 workers; shifts; discipline; no seasonal work
    - Children employed age 7+; 14-hour days
  Arkwright became enormously wealthy; many patent disputes

1779: SPINNING MULE (Samuel Crompton)
  Hybrid of jenny + water frame
  Produced fine, strong thread that jenny alone couldn't make
  Could produce all grades of cotton thread
  Became dominant spinning technology until 1800s
  Powered by hand initially; then water; then steam

1785: POWER LOOM (Edmund Cartwright)
  Mechanized the weaving step
  Early versions unreliable; improved through 1820s
  Widespread adoption: 1820s-1840s
  Impact: hand-loom weavers displaced; Luddite riots 1811-1816

PRODUCTIVITY NUMBERS:
  Spinning (twist per spindle per day):
    1780: hand spinning jenny: ~16 hanks/day
    1800: mule: ~120 hanks/day
    1860: power mule: ~2,000 hanks/day

  Labor required to spin 100 lb of cotton to yarn:
    1760: 50,000 hours (hand spinners)
    1790: 2,000 hours (mule)
    1825: 300 hours (power mule)
    = 99.4% labor reduction in 65 years

FACTORY SYSTEM EMERGENCE:
  Centralized production became necessary when machines were:
  1. Too expensive for individual ownership
  2. Powered by water or steam (fixed location)
  3. Interdependent (spinning + weaving synchronization needed)
  The factory was not just a building; it was a new way of organizing
  time, discipline, and social relations
```

## The Steam Engine

### Watt's Separate Condenser

James Watt did not invent the steam engine. Thomas Newcomen built the first practical atmospheric steam engine in 1712. Watt's 1769 invention was the separate condenser, which dramatically increased efficiency.

```
STEAM ENGINE EFFICIENCY IMPROVEMENTS
======================================

NEWCOMEN (1712): Atmospheric Engine
  Operation: steam fills cylinder; cold water injected into cylinder;
             steam condenses; atmospheric pressure pushes piston down
  Problem:   cylinder alternately heated and cooled = enormous heat loss
             Efficiency: ~0.5% thermal efficiency (Carnot limit: much higher)
  Use:       pumping water out of mines; too inefficient for most applications

WATT (1765 insight, 1769 patent): Separate Condenser
  Key idea:  do NOT cool the main cylinder
             Add a SEPARATE condenser vessel (always cold)
             Steam enters main cylinder (always hot) and expands
             Then is directed to separate condenser (always cold) to condense
  Result:    main cylinder stays hot; no energy wasted heating cylinder
             from cold to hot each stroke
  Efficiency: ~2.7% (5x better than Newcomen)
  Economic significance:
    Coal consumption per unit of work: dropped ~75%
    For a mine owner: Watt engine uses 1/4 the coal of Newcomen
    This justified the higher purchase price of the Watt engine

WATT + BOULTON PARTNERSHIP (1775):
  Matthew Boulton provided capital and commercial organization
  Watt provided engineering
  Revenue model: charged customers 1/3 of fuel savings vs Newcomen
                 This made the efficiency argument concrete and monetizable
  Watt's 1782 improvements:
    Double-acting engine (steam on both sides of piston)
    Rotative motion (crankshaft converts reciprocating to rotary)
    Centrifugal governor (automatic speed control)
    Parallel motion linkage
  These converted steam from "pump for mines" to "universal prime mover"

EFFICIENCY TIMELINE:
  Newcomen 1712:    ~0.5%
  Watt 1769:        ~2.7%
  Watt 1782:        ~4.5%
  Trevithick 1800:  ~8% (high-pressure steam)
  Triple-expansion  ~20-25%
   marine engine 1880s:
  Modern steam:     ~40%+ (combined cycle)
```

### Steam as Platform Technology

```
STEAM ENGINE APPLICATIONS
===========================

1712-1780: MINE PUMPING
  Only application for 70 years
  Kept coal mines dry; enabled deeper mining
  Irony: steam engine required coal to run; coal mining enabled more steam

1780-1820: TEXTILE FACTORIES
  Watt's rotative engine (1782) enabled factory siting away from rivers
  Previously: factories must be near rivers for water power
  With steam: factories could be in cities (near labor and markets)
  Manchester growth: from ~25,000 (1772) to ~300,000 (1850)

1804: STEAM LOCOMOTIVE (Richard Trevithick)
  First successful steam railway: Merthyr Tydfil ironworks, Wales
  Used high-pressure steam (Watt had avoided high pressure as "dangerous")
  Proved concept; Trevithick died poor despite priority

1825: STOCKTON AND DARLINGTON RAILWAY
  George Stephenson; first public steam railway
  Pulled ~90 tons at ~15 mph

1830: LIVERPOOL AND MANCHESTER RAILWAY
  First intercity steam railway with timetable service
  Rainhill Trials (1829): Stephenson's Rocket wins; 29 mph
  Opening day: William Huskisson MP killed by train (first railway fatality)

1830-1850: RAILWAY MANIA
  See separate section below
```

## Railways: Capital Mobilization and Secondary Effects

### The Railway as Platform Technology

Railways were not just faster transport. They restructured the entire economy.

```
RAILWAY ECONOMIC EFFECTS
===========================

DIRECT EFFECTS:
  1. Transport cost reduction:
     Pre-railway: freight by road: ~15d per ton-mile
     Railway:     ~0.75d per ton-mile (20x cheaper)
     Time: London to Edinburgh by coach (1830): 40 hours
           London to Edinburgh by train (1850): 9 hours

  2. Market integration:
     Price differences for identical goods between cities
     disappeared within months of railway connection
     National price convergence: wheat, coal, iron

  3. Perishable goods became marketable nationally:
     Fresh milk, fish, vegetables shipped to cities overnight
     Changed urban diets substantially

INDIRECT/SECONDARY EFFECTS:
  1. COAL demand: railways were the largest coal consumers
     Drove coal production growth; lowered coal price further

  2. IRON AND STEEL: massive demand for iron rails
     British iron production: 700,000 tons (1830) to 3.8M tons (1870)
     This demand drove steel industry scale

  3. ENGINEERING PROFESSION: bridges, tunnels, locomotives required
     a new profession: civil engineering; Institution of Civil Engineers 1818

  4. CAPITAL MARKETS: railways required unprecedented capital
     A single major railway line cost millions of pounds
     No existing institution could raise this
     Solution: joint-stock railway companies sold to public investors
     Thousands of investors bought small stakes
     This expanded capital markets; established stock exchange norms

  5. CORPORATE LAW: Limited Liability Act (1855) — partly driven by
     need for investor protection in railway investment

  6. STANDARDIZATION:
     Railway time: Britain had ~350 local times in 1840 (sun time differs
     by longitude)
     Railways required timetables; could not have 350 different times
     Railway time = Greenwich Mean Time imposed nationally ~1847
     (Clocks synchronization: proto-coordination technology)

FOGEL'S SOCIAL SAVINGS CALCULATION:
  "What would US 1890 GNP have been without railroads?"
  Counterfactual: best available canals + roads
  Result: US GNP would have been ~5% lower
  BUT: geography-specific; US has great rivers (Mississippi, Erie Canal)
  Britain's social savings were larger (more geographic obstacles)
```

### Railway Finance and Bubble

```
RAILWAY MANIA (BRITAIN, 1844-1847)
=====================================

BACKGROUND:
  1844: cheap money (low interest rates); investors seeking returns
        300 miles of railway built by 1845 proved profitable
        George Hudson "Railway King": promoted many lines;
        controlled ~1,000 miles of railway by 1844

BUBBLE MECHANICS:
  1844: Parliament received 248 railway bills; passed 66
  1845: railway share prices doubled; mania peak
        ~11,000 miles of new railway projected
        Investment: ~200 million pounds committed in 1845
        (~comparable to the entire national debt at the time)
  1846: interest rates rose; credit tightened
        Many companies had raised capital but not yet started building
  1847: crash; railway shares fell 50-70%
        Hudson exposed as fraudster: had paid dividends from capital,
        not profits; manipulated accounts
        Many investors lost everything

AFTERMATH:
  Most projected railways WERE actually built (1850-1870)
  The capital was largely sunk and the railways were productive
  The financial losses were from buying at peak prices
  LESSON: Asset bubbles can fund genuine productive infrastructure
  (Compare: dot-com bubble funded internet infrastructure)

  Railways were ultimately the most important capital investment
  in Victorian Britain; Mania was a pricing error, not a real-goods error
```

## Urbanization: Manchester and Its Consequences

### Manchester 1800-1850

Manchester's growth was the most visible human consequence of industrialization — and the most alarming to contemporaries.

```
MANCHESTER POPULATION GROWTH
==============================

  1773:   25,000
  1801:   75,000
  1831:  182,000
  1851:  303,000
  1901:  645,000

  Growth rate 1801-1851: 4x in 50 years
  No city had grown this fast in recorded history

  Infrastructure built FOR: none
  Houses: back-to-back workers' cottages; no sanitation
  Water: shared pumps; frequently contaminated
  Waste disposal: privy middens (piles of human waste in courts)
  Air: smoke from factories and domestic coal
  Life expectancy in Manchester slums (1840s): ~28 years
  Life expectancy in rural England: ~44 years
```

**Engels' analysis (1845):** Friedrich Engels worked in his family's Manchester cotton mill from 1842. "The Condition of the Working Class in England" (1845) is a systematic empirical survey of Manchester's slums. His observations directly shaped Marx's Capital. Engels documented:
- Cellar dwellings: families of 5-6 living underground with no light or ventilation
- Child labor: children working 14-hour days from age 7
- Wage trends: nominal wages rising but real wages unclear (high food prices)
- Social stratification: middle-class Manchester never had to see the workers' districts (streets designed for through-traffic of bourgeois; workers' areas in back courts)

**Public health revolution:** Manchester's conditions eventually triggered the sanitary reform movement:
- Chadwick's Sanitary Report (1842): documented correlation between slum conditions and disease mortality
- Public Health Act (1848): established local boards of health
- Metropolitan Board of Works (London, 1855): began systematic sewerage
- Joseph Bazalgette's London sewers (1858-1875): 1,300 miles of sewers; ended London cholera epidemics
- Germ theory (Pasteur, 1860s; Koch, 1880s): scientific basis for hygiene
- Life expectancy began rising in cities from ~1880 onward

## Why Not China: The Summary

```
CHINA vs BRITAIN: WHY INDUSTRIALIZATION DIVERGED
==================================================

BRITAIN (~1760)              CHINA (~1760)
-------------                -----
High wages: ~30g silver/day  Low wages: ~6g silver/day
Cheap coal: near industry    Expensive coal: far from industry
Secure property rights       Secure commercial rights within
(post-1688)                  customary merchant law, but
                             imperial arbitrary power present
Deep capital markets:        Merchant capital pooled in
Amsterdam + London           family/guild structures
Banking infrastructure:      Pawnshops; no joint-stock
Bank of England, commercial  companies; limited bill-of-
banking                      exchange equivalents
Atlantic trade demand        Domestic market largely
for cheap cotton goods       served by artisanal production

Song Dynasty precedent:      High-skill artisanal equilibrium
Iron production at           producing quality silk; wrong
Kaifeng: comparable to       cost structure for mass
early Industrial Revolution  mechanization

POMERANZ THESIS (2000):
"The Great Divergence" argues that by 1750, Yangtze Delta living
standards were roughly equal to England. Two contingent factors
tipped industrialization to Britain:
1. Coal geography (above)
2. Atlantic colonies (land windfall relieved Malthusian pressure;
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Watt did not invent the steam engine.** Newcomen built the first practical steam engine in 1712, 53 years before Watt's separate condenser patent. Watt made the existing engine dramatically more efficient. Trevithick (not Watt) made the first steam locomotive.

**The Luddites were not anti-technology in principle.** They were skilled handloom weavers who correctly identified that power looms would destroy their wages and livelihoods. They targeted specific machines that displaced their specific labor. The "Luddite" label (anti-technology) is historically inaccurate. They were engaged in a labor dispute using machine-breaking as negotiating leverage.

**"Industrial Revolution" does not mean a sudden change.** Textile mechanization proceeded over 80 years (1764-1840s). Real wages in England did not clearly start rising until the 1840s-1850s, 80 years after the first inventions. The "Engels pause" (wages stagnating or falling for workers even as national income grew) is empirically documented for 1780-1840.

**Railways were built by navvies (laborers), not by factories.** The physical construction of railways was done with hand tools, explosives, and horse-drawn carts by ~250,000 Irish and English "navigators" (navvies). The capital intensity of railways was in the materials (iron rails, locomotives) and the organizational complexity, not automated machinery.
