The period 1870-1913 was the first era of financial globalization. Capital moved across borders at rates not seen again until the 1990s. The gold standard coordinated the international monetary system. Investment banks organized the capital for railroads, steel, and electrification. Concentration produced oligopolies and trusts. And the institutional infrastructure that failed in 1929 was being built — and the seeds of its failure planted.

## Investment Banking: Organizing Capital at Scale

### The Rothschilds: Sovereign Bond Specialists

Nathan Mayer Rothschild built the London branch (established 1804) into the premier sovereign bond house of the 19th century. The Rothschild network — London, Frankfurt, Paris, Vienna, Naples — had a structural advantage: faster information through the family's private courier network.

```
ROTHSCHILD BUSINESS MODEL
===========================

CORE BUSINESS: Sovereign bond underwriting
  How it worked:
  1. Sovereign government (say, Prussia) needs to borrow 10M pounds
  2. Rothschilds negotiates terms: coupon rate, maturity, covenants
  3. Rothschilds underwrites: agrees to sell bonds to investors
  4. Places bonds with network of correspondents across Europe
     (aristocrats, merchants, other banks)
  5. Earns: underwriting spread (difference between issue price
     and price paid by investors)

INFORMATION ADVANTAGE:
  Private courier network (riders, carrier pigeons for short distances)
  Was faster than any government communication system until telegraph
  Rothschilds received news of Waterloo outcome (June 18, 1815)
  before official government courier reached London (June 20)
  MYTH: They shorted British bonds, knowing Wellington had won
  REALITY: The primary historical record is fragmentary. Ferguson's
           "The House of Rothschild" (1998) — the most careful scholarly
           treatment — finds the evidence for the specific trading story
           (whether short or long) is thin and largely apocryphal.
           What is documented: Nathan did receive early intelligence;
           what precisely he did with it is unclear.
  The information advantage was structural and real;
  the specific Waterloo trade story is probably legend

SCALE:
  By 1825: Rothschilds had underwritten bonds for:
  Austria, Prussia, Russia, England, France, Brazil, Mexico, others
  The LARGEST private creditor to European sovereigns
  Peer-level relationship with Metternich (Austria); Wellington (Britain)
  Refused to underwrite Russian debt when Russia massacred Polish Jews
  (economic coercion as political leverage -- prototype)

RAILWAY EXTENSION (1830s-1850s):
  N. European railways largely financed by Rothschild network
  French Northern Railway: Rothschild underwrote; built by Rothschild capital
  Austrian railways: same
  The railway transformed Rothschild from sovereign specialists to
  general infrastructure finance
```

### J.P. Morgan: Industrial Consolidation

John Pierpont Morgan (1837-1913) was the dominant American investment banker and financier of the Gilded Age. His significance was not just in specific deals but in creating the model of the investment bank as industrial organizer.

```
JP MORGAN'S ROLE
==================

KEY DIFFERENCE FROM ROTHSCHILDS:
  Rothschilds: placed bonds with aristocratic/wealthy investors
  Morgan: organized industrial consolidation; created corporations
          from competing companies; placed equity with wealthy clients

MORGAN'S OPERATING PRINCIPLE:
  Preferred stable oligopoly to destructive competition
  Competed companies were burning capital undercutting each other
  Morgan's solution: bring them together under one organization;
                     rationalize pricing; stabilize returns
  This is: industrial organization theory applied as business model
  Critics: monopoly creation; market manipulation
  Morgan: creating efficient enterprises; eliminating waste

KEY TRANSACTIONS:
  1879: reorganized New York Central Railroad (Vanderbilt)
  1893: reorganized Philadelphia and Reading Railroad (after bankruptcy)
  "Morganization": term for putting company on sound financial footing
    typically meant: cutting dividends; reducing debt; firing management;
    placing Morgan partner on board as monitor

  1895 + 1893: GOLD RESCUES
  US Treasury's gold reserve nearly depleted (silver legislation debate;
  people hoarding gold)
  Morgan organized syndicate of bankers to provide US Treasury with gold
  in exchange for bonds
  Saved the gold standard in the US; earned enormous political enmity
  (Bryan's "cross of gold" speech 1896 was partly response to this)

  1901: US STEEL CORPORATION
  Carnegie Steel Company: Andrew Carnegie wanted to retire; offered to sell
  Morgan: bought Carnegie Steel for $480 million (Carnegie personally got $225M)
  Combined it with: Federal Steel, National Steel, American Tin Plate,
                    American Steel and Wire, American Sheet Steel, others
  Created US Steel: capitalized at $1.4 billion
  Largest corporation in the world; 67% of US steel production
  This is modern merger and acquisition (M&A) mechanics

  1907: PANIC OF 1907
  Knickerbocker Trust Company (third-largest NYC bank) failed
  Bank run spreading to other trusts
  US had no central bank; no lender of last resort
  Morgan locked bankers in his library until 2 AM;
  organized pledges of support to stabilize specific banks
  Stopped the panic; prevented general bank collapse
  Congressional response: "this cannot depend on one private citizen"
  Led directly to Federal Reserve Act (1913)
```

## The Gold Standard

### Mechanics

The classical gold standard (1870-1914) was the international monetary regime that constrained governments, stabilized exchange rates, and coordinated global capital flows.

```
GOLD STANDARD MECHANICS
=========================

BASIC SETUP:
  Each country fixes domestic currency to gold at a specific price
  UK: 1 pound = 113 grains of fine gold (since 1717; Newton's mint)
  US: 1 dollar = 23.2 grains of fine gold
  Germany: joined 1871 (newly unified)

  CONSEQUENCE: Fixed exchange rates between countries
  1 pound = 4.86 dollars (because ratio of gold content)
  This is PERFECTLY FIXED; no band, no float

GOLD FLOWS AND ADJUSTMENT (Hume Price-Specie Flow Mechanism, 1752):
  Country A runs trade deficit (imports > exports):
  1. Country A pays for imports in gold
  2. Country A's gold stock falls
  3. Money supply falls (currency backed by gold)
  4. Prices in A fall
  5. A's goods become cheaper internationally
  6. A's exports increase; imports fall
  7. Trade balance corrects; gold flows back

  Country B runs surplus (mirror image):
  1. Receives gold
  2. Money supply rises
  3. Prices rise
  4. B's goods more expensive internationally
  5. B's exports fall; imports rise
  6. Trade balance corrects; gold outflows

  AUTOMATIC ADJUSTMENT: No policy intervention needed
  The gold standard was "self-regulating" in theory

REALITY vs THEORY:
  The adjustment mechanism worked slowly and imperfectly
  Countries often "sterilized" gold inflows (prevented them from
  expanding money supply -- keeping prices low to maintain export advantage)
  This violated the spirit of the gold standard
  France and US (1920s) major offenders in sterilizing gold inflows
```

### Gold Standard's Constraints

```
GOLD STANDARD CONSTRAINTS ON POLICY
======================================

1. MONETARY POLICY: NONE
   Cannot lower interest rates during recession if this threatens
   gold convertibility (capital would flee to higher-yield currencies)
   Cannot expand money supply without gold backing
   RESULT: monetary policy is hostage to external balance

2. FISCAL POLICY: LIMITED
   Cannot run persistent deficits if gold standard maintained
   War finance required suspending gold standard or borrowing abroad

3. DEFLATION BIAS:
   Gold supply grows slowly (mining limited)
   World output growing faster than gold supply
   RESULT: secular deflation tendency
   1873-1896: "Long Depression" partly a deflation episode
   Prices fell 30-40% in UK; 25-30% in US

4. WHO BEARS ADJUSTMENT COSTS?
   In theory: symmetric (surplus and deficit countries both adjust)
   In practice: deficit countries bore most adjustment
   (surplus countries sterilized)
   RESULT: deficit countries faced contractionary monetary conditions
           most of the time -- asymmetric burden

5. INCOMPATIBLE WITH DEMOCRACY (Polanyi's argument):
   Gold standard required wage flexibility (wages fall in recession)
   But organized labor resisted wage cuts
   Once workers could vote, maintaining gold at cost of mass unemployment
   became politically unsustainable
   1931: UK abandoned gold; 1933: US abandoned gold
   The gold standard died because it was incompatible with
   democratic management of unemployment
```

## The Long Depression (1873-1896)

### What Was It?

The "Long Depression" is a contested historiographic concept. In terms of GDP growth, the period 1873-1896 was not uniformly depressed — the US and Germany continued industrializing rapidly. But it was a period of price deflation, agricultural distress, and periodic financial crises.

```
LONG DEPRESSION PROFILE
=========================

PRICE DEFLATION:
  UK wholesale prices: fell ~40% from 1873-1896
  US wholesale prices: fell ~30%
  Cause: rapid industrial productivity growth; gold supply inadequate
         for expanding output; deflationary pressure persistent

AGRICULTURAL CRISIS:
  US and Australian wheat flooded European markets (railways + steamships)
  European farmers couldn't compete at lower prices
  English agricultural rents collapsed
  European political response: tariff protection
  Germany: Bismarck's "marriage of iron and rye" (1879 tariffs)
  France: Meline tariff (1892)
  UK: stayed free-trade; landed gentry suffered

SPECIFIC CRISES:
  1873 Vienna Panic: Austrian-German stock market crash
       US: failure of Jay Cooke & Company (had financed Northern Pacific Railway)
       Triggered bank runs, railroad failures across US
  1882 French banking crisis: Union Générale bank collapse
  1890 Baring Crisis: Barings Brothers nearly failed on Argentine bonds
       Morgan + Bank of England organized rescue; Barings saved
  1893 US: severe banking panic; Philadelphia and Reading Railroad failed

POLITICAL CONSEQUENCES:
  Bimetallism debate (US): should silver be added alongside gold?
  William Jennings Bryan, 1896 Democratic convention:
  "You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold"
  (Farmers in debt wanted inflation; creditors wanted deflation)
  Bryan lost; gold standard maintained
  Populist movement: agrarian resentment of eastern financial capital

RESOLUTION:
  1896: gold standard "won" partially because Klondike gold rush (1896)
  and South African gold discoveries increased gold supply
  Prices began rising; deflation ended; Long Depression controversy
  resolved by new gold supply
```

## Robber Barons and Trust Formation

### The Logic of Vertical Integration

The post-Civil War US economy created enormous opportunities for industrial consolidation. The key figures — Rockefeller (oil), Carnegie (steel), Vanderbilt (railroads), Gould (railroads), Morgan (finance) — built industrial empires using both legal and illegal means.

```
STANDARD OIL AND TRUST MECHANICS
===================================

ROCKEFELLER'S STRATEGY:
  1870: Standard Oil of Ohio incorporated
  Problem: Oil refining intensely competitive; prices volatile;
           margins thin
  Solution: Control the distribution bottleneck (pipelines + railroads)

STEP 1: SECRET RAILROAD REBATES
  Negotiated with Erie, New York Central, Pennsylvania railroads:
  Standard Oil guaranteed volume; railroads gave SECRET rebates
  Standard paid lower shipping rates than competitors
  Competitors also charged a "drawback" (payment to Standard on
  competitors' shipments)
  This was legal in 1870s; eventually outlawed
  Effect: Standard could undercut competitors on price; drive them out

STEP 2: HORIZONTAL INTEGRATION
  Bought out competitors at distress prices ("or be crushed")
  By 1879: Standard controlled ~90% of US oil refining capacity

STEP 3: THE TRUST MECHANISM (1882)
  Problem: cannot own companies in multiple states (corporate law then
  did not permit this; each state required separate incorporation)
  Solution: Shareholders in 40 different Standard Oil companies
  transferred their shares to a "board of trustees"
  Trustees issued "trust certificates" in return
  Trustees managed all companies as unified enterprise
  Legal fiction: separate companies; economic reality: one company

  THIS IS THE ORIGIN OF THE WORD "TRUST" IN ANTITRUST LAW

OTHER TRUSTS FORMED ON SAME MODEL:
  American Cotton Oil Trust (1884)
  National Linseed Oil Trust (1885)
  Distillers and Cattle Feeders Trust (1887) "Whiskey Trust"
  American Sugar Refining Company (essentially a trust)

VERTICAL INTEGRATION (Standard Oil model):
  Owned: oil wells, pipelines, refineries, railroads (partially),
         barrel manufacturing, tank cars, distribution networks
  Controlled every step from wellhead to customer
  Eliminated possibility of bottleneck competitors
```

### Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)

```
SHERMAN ANTITRUST ACT (1890)
==============================

TEXT (Section 1):
  "Every contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise,
  or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the
  several States, or with foreign nations, is hereby declared illegal"

TEXT (Section 2):
  "Every person who shall monopolize, or attempt to monopolize,
  or combine or conspire with any other person or persons,
  to monopolize any part of the trade or commerce among the
  several States, or with foreign nations, shall be deemed
  guilty of a misdemeanor"

INITIAL ENFORCEMENT: WEAK (1890-1901)
  Sherman was a criminal statute with misdemeanor penalties
  Courts interpreted narrowly (US v EC Knight 1895: manufacturing
  is not commerce; sugar trust allowed to stand)
  Only 18 cases brought in first decade; few convictions

THEODORE ROOSEVELT ERA (1901-1909):
  Northern Securities Company (1904): first major trust dissolved
  Broke up railroad combination (Hill + Morgan + Harriman)
  Standard Oil breakup (1911): Supreme Court ordered dissolution
  Standard Oil became: Esso (later ExxonMobil), Mobil, Chevron,
  BP (through Amoco), Conoco, and ~30 other companies
  American Tobacco Company dissolved (1911)

RULE OF REASON (Standard Oil case, 1911):
  Court said not all monopolies are illegal; only UNREASONABLE ones
  Introduced balancing test: market power + anticompetitive conduct
  This is still the basic framework of US antitrust

STRUCTURAL PARALLEL — RAILROAD REBATES → IE BUNDLING:
  Standard Oil's strategy: exclusive dealing with railroads;
  preferential rebates that competitors couldn't match;
  control the distribution bottleneck (railroads) to lock out
  competing refiners from the market
  US v. Microsoft (2001): same Sherman Act, same rule of reason
  framework, same "control the distribution bottleneck" strategy:
  IE bundling into Windows → locked out Netscape from the browser
  market → threatened Java (cross-platform runtime would have
  reduced Windows lock-in); OEM restrictions prevented pre-installing
  rival browsers. The DOJ's market definition (browsers as separate
  market within OS), the tying theory, and the conduct-plus-power
  analysis all track the Standard Oil precedent.
```

## The Federal Reserve: Creation

### Why the US Had No Central Bank in 1907

The US had eliminated its central bank (Second Bank of the United States) in 1836 when Andrew Jackson refused to renew its charter. For 77 years, the US had no lender of last resort.

```
PATH TO THE FEDERAL RESERVE
==============================

PROBLEM WITHOUT A CENTRAL BANK:
  In a banking panic:
  1. Depositors demand cash from banks
  2. Banks cannot meet all demands simultaneously
     (fractional reserve: hold only fraction of deposits in cash)
  3. Banks must call in loans; sell assets
  4. Asset sales depress prices; solvent banks become technically insolvent
  5. More panic; more runs; system collapse

  SOLUTION (Bagehot's 1873 dictum):
  "Lend freely at penalty rates against good collateral"
  A lender of last resort (central bank) provides cash to solvent banks
  facing liquidity crises; stops the cascade

1907 PANIC:
  October: Knickerbocker Trust Company run
  Morgan had to play central bank from his private library
  Secretary of Treasury parked $35M of government deposits in Morgan-
  selected banks to provide liquidity
  Worked; but exposed the structural fragility
  Congress was alarmed: private bank coordination was too slow and
  dependent on Morgan's personal authority

ALDRICH-VREELAND ACT (1908):
  Emergency currency provisions for panics
  Created National Monetary Commission to study the problem

JEKYLL ISLAND MEETING (November 1910):
  Senator Nelson Aldrich, Frank Vanderlip (National City Bank),
  Henry Davison (JP Morgan), Charles Norton (First National Bank),
  Paul Warburg (Kuhn Loeb), Benjamin Strong (later first NY Fed president)

  Met secretly at Jekyll Island Club (private club, coastal Georgia)
  for 10 days to design a central bank

  WHY SECRETLY?
  Any plan seen as Wall Street-designed would be politically toxic
  (Populists, Bryanites would kill it)
  Fiction maintained that participants met "for duck hunting"

  WHAT THEY DESIGNED:
  - Decentralized (regional banks, not one central bank)
  - Private ownership (banks own Federal Reserve stock)
  - Government presence (Federal Reserve Board in Washington)
  - Elastic currency (Fed could expand money supply in panics)

ALDRICH PLAN vs GLASS-OWEN ACT:
  Aldrich presented Warburg/Morgan plan; Democrats rejected it
  (too Wall Street)
  Carter Glass (Virginia, House) + Robert Owen (Oklahoma, Senate)
  rewrote it with more government control
  Federal Reserve Act: signed December 23, 1913
  Created: 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks + Federal Reserve Board
  Owned by member banks (private); governed with public oversight
  Mandated: bank supervision + monetary policy
  (Full employment mandate added in 1977 Humphrey-Hawkins Act)
```

## First Globalization (1870-1914)

### Capital and Trade Flows

The period 1870-1914 saw international economic integration on a scale not replicated until the 1990s.

```
FIRST GLOBALIZATION METRICS
=============================

TRADE:
  World exports / world GDP:
  1870:  5%
  1913:  12%  (peak until ~1970)

  What drove it:
  1. Transport costs: steamships (Suez Canal 1869);
     unit freight costs fell ~70% between 1870 and 1913
  2. Communications: transatlantic telegraph (1866);
     price arbitrage possible globally for first time
  3. Gold standard: exchange rate certainty reduced trade risk
  4. Low tariffs: UK free trade; Germany/France with protection
     but declining; US protected but still integrated

CAPITAL FLOWS:
  Britain invested ~40-50% of its annual savings abroad by 1913
  French: ~35% of savings abroad
  British foreign investment 1914: ~4 billion pounds
     (~170% of British annual GDP)
  Compare: US foreign investment/GDP now: ~50%
  The 1914 era was MORE globally financially integrated than today
  in several measures

WHERE BRITISH CAPITAL WENT:
  ~40% to British Empire (India, Australia, Canada, S.Africa)
  ~25% to Latin America (Argentina, Brazil: railways, infrastructure)
  ~20% to US (railways, bonds)
  ~15% rest of world

  Capital flowed from high-wage to low-wage countries
  (= from capital-abundant UK to capital-scarce Argentina)
  This is the theory; in practice, capital also flowed from
  UK to even-higher-wage US (network effects, institutions)

LABOR FLOWS:
  ~55 million people emigrated from Europe to Americas 1820-1913
  Mass migration: Irish (potato famine), Italian, Polish, Russian Jewish
  Labor convergence: wages in source countries rose; destination fell
  (until arrival of new immigrants pushed wages down again)

WHY 1914 ENDED IT:
  WWI: capital flows reversed; trade collapsed; gold standard suspended
  Countries used tariffs for war finance
  The first globalization never fully recovered in the interwar period
```

## Common Confusion Points

**The gold standard did not prevent inflation — it prevented *large* inflation.** Under gold, prices fluctuated with gold discoveries (1849 California gold rush inflated prices; 1873-1896 deflation from insufficient gold supply). The stability was in *exchange rates*, not price levels.

**J.P. Morgan was an investment banker, not a commercial banker.** He did not take deposits or make loans to consumers. He organized capital from wealthy investors and directed it to industrial corporations. The modern equivalents are Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley — not JPMorgan Chase's retail branches (though those carry his name).

**Sherman Act did not break up Standard Oil quickly.** Passed 1890; Standard Oil broken up 1911 — a 21-year gap. The initial decade of enforcement was almost entirely ineffective. Real antitrust enforcement required political will (Roosevelt's "trust-busting") plus workable legal doctrine (rule of reason).

**The Federal Reserve was not designed to stabilize the economy — it was designed to prevent bank panics.** The 1913 Act focused on: elastic currency (prevent panic-driven money shortages), bank supervision, and clearing of checks. Macroeconomic stabilization was not the concept. The Fed's modern macroeconomic mandate developed gradually through the 1930s-1970s.

**"Robber baron" is politically loaded.** The same men who used anti-competitive tactics (secret railroad rebates, predatory pricing) also built infrastructure, created jobs, and drove productivity growth. Carnegie's steel productivity was genuine. Rockefeller's oil prices fell consistently. The historical debate is about distribution of gains, not whether gains existed.
