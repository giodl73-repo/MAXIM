# Postwar Globalization — Economic History

## The Big Picture

The period 1945-1973 was the most rapid sustained economic growth in history — the "Golden Age of Capitalism." It was followed by the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979, which broke the Keynesian consensus and triggered the monetarist turn. The East Asian developmental states demonstrated that rapid industrialization was possible outside the Western path, using state-directed industrial policy in ways mainstream economists said shouldn't work — but did.

```
POSTWAR ECONOMIC TRAJECTORY
=============================

GDP PER CAPITA GROWTH RATES (annual average):
                    1950-73     1973-90     1990-2016
  Western Europe     4.4%        2.1%        1.5%
  Japan              8.1%        3.1%        0.8%
  South Korea        5.8%        7.6%        4.5%
  USA                2.5%        2.2%        1.8%
  China              2.9%        4.0%        7.2%
  India              1.4%        2.6%        4.5%
  World (avg)        2.9%        1.9%        2.3%

KEY PERIODS:
  1945-1973  Golden Age: reconstruction + Keynesian demand management
             + cheap oil + US hegemony + labor-capital bargain
  1973       First oil shock: OPEC embargo; stagflation begins
  1979       Second oil shock + Iranian Revolution + Volcker shock
  1980s      Monetarist turn: Thatcher, Reagan; financialization begins
  1986       Single European Act (EU integration accelerates)
  1990       Japan stagnation begins; "Lost Decade" starts
  1997-98    East Asian financial crisis
  2001       China joins WTO; hyperglobalization peaks
```

## The Golden Age (1945-1973)

### Why Growth Was So Fast

The postwar Golden Age combined several favorable factors that may never recur simultaneously:

```
GOLDEN AGE GROWTH FACTORS
===========================

1. RECONSTRUCTION DEMAND (Europe and Japan):
   Physical capital had been destroyed
   Re-equipping with the best available technology
   = rapid productivity catch-up
   "Catch-up growth": poor countries (or destroyed ones) grow faster
   because they can adopt frontier technology without R&D costs
   Solow model: capital-scarce economies have high marginal returns to
   capital investment; converge toward steady state rapidly

2. KEYNESIAN DEMAND MANAGEMENT:
   Government had learned (from 1930s) to use fiscal and monetary
   policy to smooth business cycles
   Automatic stabilizers: unemployment insurance, social security
   prevented income collapses
   Countercyclical fiscal policy: spend in recessions; reduce in booms
   Result: no major recession 1945-1973 in Western countries

3. CHEAP ENERGY:
   Oil price 1945-1973: roughly constant in real terms (~$2-3/barrel)
   Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq: vast reserves; low production cost
   Cheap energy = cheap transport, cheap production
   The entire postwar growth model assumed cheap energy

4. BRETTON WOODS STABILITY:
   Fixed exchange rates reduced international trade uncertainty
   Capital controls (under Bretton Woods) prevented speculative
   attacks on currencies
   Countries could maintain full employment without fear of
   currency crisis (as long as they didn't run too large deficits)

5. LABOR-CAPITAL BARGAIN:
   Strong unions (20-35% membership in most Western countries)
   Wages grew with productivity
   Mass consumption: workers earned enough to buy what they made
   Ford's logic applied nationally: pay workers enough to buy Ford cars
   Kuznets curve (contested): rising inequality first, then falling
   as education and redistribution increase; 1945-1973 was the
   falling-inequality phase in rich countries

6. US HEGEMONY AND MARSHALL PLAN:
   US provided security umbrella (NATO, Japan-US Security Treaty)
   Allowed Europe and Japan to spend less on defense
   Marshall Plan and other aid started the process
   US market open for allies' exports (geo-strategic decision)
```

### The Welfare State Expansion

The postwar period saw the construction of universal welfare states across Western Europe:

```
WELFARE STATE EXPANSION (WESTERN EUROPE)
==========================================

UK (1945-1948):
  Attlee government (Labour won 1945 landslide despite Churchill)
  National Health Service 1948: free universal healthcare
  National Insurance Act 1946: unemployment, sickness, retirement benefits
  Housing Act: massive public housing construction
  Nationalization: coal, steel, railroads, telecommunications

SWEDEN (Social Democratic model):
  High taxes (~50% GDP); universal services; active labor market policy
  "Solidaristic wage policy": compress wages; boost productivity
  Corporatist bargaining: government + unions + employers negotiate
  Rehn-Meidner model: tight fiscal policy + full employment via
  labor market programs, not demand stimulus
  Result: Sweden grew faster than most countries 1945-1970

GERMANY (Social Market Economy):
  "Ordoliberalism" (Freiburg school): market economy with strong
  social institutions and competition policy
  NOT Keynesianism; NOT laissez-faire
  Erhard's "economic miracle" (Wirtschaftswunder): currency reform 1948,
  free prices, competition policy
  Codetermination (Mitbestimmung): workers on company boards
  Strong unions but cooperative; export-led growth model

KEY METRICS AT GOLDEN AGE PEAK (1970):
  Western European countries: ~90% unionization in some sectors
  Income inequality (Gini): 0.25-0.35 (much lower than today)
  Unemployment: typically 2-4% (near "full employment")
  Trade as % of GDP: rising but still below later levels
```

## The Japanese Miracle

### The MITI Model

Japan's growth from 1950 to 1973 (8.1% per year) is the most dramatic peacetime economic expansion in history. The Ministry of International Trade and Industry (MITI) played a central coordinating role.

```
MITI AND JAPANESE INDUSTRIAL POLICY
=====================================

MITI'S TOOLS:
  1. CREDIT ALLOCATION:
     Japan Development Bank channeled low-interest loans
     to priority industries (steel, chemicals, electronics, autos)
     Private banks followed MITI guidance on lending

  2. IMPORT PROTECTION:
     High tariffs and quotas protected nascent Japanese industries
     until they achieved scale and efficiency
     Example: Toyota protected from GM/Ford competition in 1950s
     When Toyota was competitive: protection gradually removed

  3. TECHNOLOGY LICENSING:
     MITI brokered technology license deals with US companies
     Japanese firms paid royalties but got the technology cheaply
     (US companies preferred this to market access competition)
     Example: semiconductor technology licensed from US companies;
     Japan mastered it; then competed globally

  4. CARTELS AND RATIONALIZATION:
     MITI organized recession cartels: when industry overinvested,
     MITI coordinated production cuts to maintain prices
     This was anti-competitive but prevented destructive price wars
     that would have destroyed capital in young industries

  5. ADMINISTRATIVE GUIDANCE:
     MITI issued non-binding "guidance" to industries
     Companies generally followed it (credit allocation leverage)
     Could not be easily litigated; informal but effective

KEIRETSU SYSTEM:
  Interlocking ownership among suppliers, manufacturers, banks
  Toyota Group: Toyota Motor + Toyota Industries + Toyota Tsusho
              + Toyota Financial + suppliers + Tokai Bank
  Advantages: long-term relationships; just-in-time supply chains;
              patient capital; R&D investment without quarterly pressure
  Disadvantages: cross-subsidization of weak units; less competition

EXPORT-LED GROWTH:
  Japan's domestic market too small for scale; must export
  Key sequence:
  1. Protect domestic market (high tariffs, non-tariff barriers)
  2. Achieve scale in protected domestic market
  3. Use scale to compete internationally on price/quality
  4. Export revenues fund next round of investment

  "Flying geese" model (Akamatsu): Japan leads; Korea, Taiwan, etc.
  follow as Japan moves to higher-value-added products
```

**Was MITI the cause of the miracle?** This is contested. Arguments against MITI as cause:
- MITI predicted wrong in famous cases: told Honda to stay out of auto manufacturing; blocked Sony from buying transistor technology (overruled). Toyota's quality improvement (Ohno's lean production) was internally developed, not MITI-directed.
- The fundamental driver may have been: high savings rates, educated workforce, cheap imports of Western technology, and access to US markets.
- Arguments for MITI: without protection, Toyota may have been destroyed by GM/Ford competition in 1950s before it became competitive. The infant industry argument has empirical support here.

## The Asian Tigers

South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore (the "Four Tigers") achieved rapid industrialization from the 1960s onward using variations on the Japanese model.

```
ASIAN TIGER GROWTH COMPARISON
===============================

                    GDP/capita 1960   GDP/capita 2000   Growth
  South Korea         $1,105           $14,900           13x
  Taiwan              $1,300           $15,000           12x
  Singapore           $2,500           $27,000           11x
  Hong Kong           $3,900           $23,500            6x
  For reference:
  UK                  $8,600           $23,000            2.7x
  US                 $10,900           $34,000            3.1x

DEVELOPMENT MODELS:

SOUTH KOREA (Developmental State, most interventionist):
  Park Chung-hee regime (1961-1979): authoritarian; export-led growth
  Chaebol system: large family-owned conglomerates (Samsung, Hyundai, LG, Daewoo)
  State direction: 5-year plans; credit allocation; tariff protection
  Labor: suppressed unions; low wages early; education investment
  Export performance: steel, ships, electronics, autos by 1980s
  Debt-financed growth: high leverage; vulnerable to crisis (1997)

TAIWAN (Land reform + export promotion):
  1950s land reform: broke up large estates; created small farmers
  Small and medium enterprises (SMEs): not chaebol-scale
  ITRI (Industrial Technology Research Institute): state R&D
  Technology transfer: licensed US technology; TSMC founded 1987
  (TSMC is now the world's most important semiconductor manufacturer)

SINGAPORE (City-state; entrepot):
  Lee Kuan Yew (PM 1959-1990): authoritarian developmentalism
  Different model: attract multinational investment
  Low taxes; English law; strong property rights; anti-corruption
  Temasek Holdings: state investment company holds strategic sectors
  Port and financial services; education; housing programs (HDB)

HONG KONG (Laissez-faire):
  British colony; Common Law; minimal industrial policy
  Entrepot trade; then financial services; then re-export of China
  Highest income without industrial policy: but geography (China gateway)
  was unique advantage
```

### ISI vs. EOI: The Development Strategy Debate

```
IMPORT SUBSTITUTION INDUSTRIALIZATION (ISI)
 vs EXPORT-ORIENTED INDUSTRIALIZATION (EOI)
=============================================

ISI (Latin America, India, Africa, 1950s-1970s):
  Theory: can't industrialize by competing with advanced countries
          immediately; must protect and nurture infant industries
  Policy: high tariffs; state-owned enterprises; import licenses
  Results: some industrialization; but protected industries
           often uncompetitive; balance of payments crises
           (need foreign exchange for capital goods imports)
  Problem: protecting inefficient industries indefinitely;
           domestic consumers pay high prices
  Case: Brazil, Argentina, India until 1990s liberalization

EOI (East Asian tigers, from 1960s):
  Theory: export to world market; achieve scale; compete and learn
  Policy: some initial protection, but contingent on export performance
          (had to export to retain protection -- "performance standards")
          Low corporate taxes; special export zones; exchange rate
          management; active labor market policy
  Results: fastest growth in economic history; technology upgrading
  Key difference from simple free trade: not just opening up markets
  but STRATEGICALLY targeting industries and building capabilities

WORLD BANK CONSENSUS SHIFT:
  1993 "East Asian Miracle" report: acknowledged industrial policy role
  Previous consensus: markets; no industrial policy
  Post-1993: conceded that "market-friendly" intervention could work
  But: argued that East Asian industrial policy worked because of
  strong institutions, education, macro stability -- not because
  picking winners always works

LATIN AMERICA COMPARISON:
  Similar policies to East Asia in some respects
  But: ISI without export pressure = inefficient protected industries
  AND: less focus on education investment
  AND: weak financial systems
  AND: political instability and inequality → redistribution pressure
       that ISI rents were used for (not reinvestment)
  RESULT: "Lost decade" 1980s; debt crises; structural adjustment
```

## The Oil Shocks

### 1973: The First Shock

The 1973 oil embargo was both an economic disruption and a geopolitical turning point.

```
1973 OIL SHOCK
===============

CONTEXT:
  October War (Yom Kippur War): Egypt and Syria attacked Israel
  October 6, 1973
  US airlifted arms to Israel: Operation Nickel Grass

OPEC EMBARGO:
  October 17: Arab OPEC members announced oil embargo on US, Netherlands
  October-December: oil price quadrupled
  Oil price: $3/barrel (before) to $12/barrel (after)

  OPEC MECHANICS:
  Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (formed 1960)
  13 members (as of 1973): Algeria, Ecuador, Gabon, Indonesia,
  Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Libya, Nigeria, Qatar, Saudi Arabia,
  UAE, Venezuela
  Production quotas: each member assigned quota; cartel discipline
  Supply restriction = price increase

  WHY OPEC COULD DO THIS:
  1. Middle Eastern oil: low production cost (~$0.15/barrel);
     cartel profit enormous
  2. Western countries dependent: ~50% of oil was OPEC
  3. No quick substitutes: cannot quickly build nuclear plants;
     coal infrastructure different; natural gas infrastructure limited
  4. Strategic coordination: Arab states unified briefly after 1967
     defeat

STAGFLATION PUZZLE:
  Standard Keynesian model: inflation OR unemployment; Phillips curve tradeoff
  1970s: BOTH inflation AND unemployment simultaneously = stagflation

  WHY STAGFLATION BREAKS KEYNESIAN MODEL:
  Keynesian assumed demand-side shocks:
    Too much demand → inflation → raise rates → reduce demand → fix
    Too little demand → unemployment → lower rates → boost demand → fix

  Oil shock is a SUPPLY-SIDE shock:
    Oil price rise → production costs rise for EVERYTHING
    Higher costs → either raise prices (inflation) OR reduce output (unemployment)
    YOU GET BOTH

  Keynesian demand management response to stagflation:
    Raise rates (to fight inflation): worsens unemployment
    Lower rates (to fight unemployment): worsens inflation
    NO GOOD OPTION within Keynesian framework
```

### 1979: The Second Shock

```
1979 OIL SHOCK AND VOLCKER SHOCK
===================================

1979 SHOCK:
  Iranian Revolution (January-February 1979): Shah overthrown
  Iranian oil production collapsed: -4.8 million barrels/day
  (~7% of world supply)
  Oil price: ~$15/barrel (1978) to $39/barrel (1980)

  Second shock compounded first:
  US gasoline lines; rationing; "energy crisis" consciousness

  OPEC quota system: Saudi Arabia increased production to partially
  offset Iran; didn't fully compensate

VOLCKER SHOCK (1979-1981):
  Paul Volcker appointed Fed Chairman by Carter, August 1979
  Previous Fed policy: accommodating; monetary policy loosened
  whenever unemployment rose; "inflationary expectations" unanchored

  VOLCKER'S STRATEGY:
  October 6, 1979: "Saturday Night Special"
  Changed Fed operating target from interest rates to bank reserves
  = No longer targeting federal funds rate directly
  = Allowed interest rates to go where needed to control money supply

  RESULT:
  Federal Funds Rate:
  1979: 11.2%
  1980 peak: 20% (June 1981: 20.06%)
  1982: falling; recession ends

  RECESSION:
  1980: mild recession; Carter administration
  1981-82: severe recession; Reagan administration
  Unemployment peaked at 10.8% (December 1982)
  Worst unemployment since the Depression

  WHY ACCEPT THIS PAIN?
  Volcker/Reagan judgment: inflation must be broken before
  it became embedded in wage-setting expectations
  "Wage-price spiral": workers expected inflation → demanded higher wages
  → firms raised prices → inflation became self-fulfilling
  Break the expectation: accept deep recession; demonstrate commitment
  Result: inflation fell from ~13% (1979) to ~3.2% (1983)

  COST: ~3% of GDP permanently lost; unemployment scar; deindustrialization
  accelerated; Latin American debt crisis 1982 (US rates sucked
  capital from developing countries that had borrowed in dollars)
```

## Deindustrialization and Financialization

```
DEINDUSTRIALIZATION IN WESTERN ECONOMIES
==========================================

MANUFACTURING EMPLOYMENT (% of total employment):
             1960    1973    1990    2000    2016
  US          28%     25%     17%     14%      9%
  UK          38%     35%     23%     17%     10%
  Germany     40%     44%     33%     29%     24% (less deindustrialized)
  France      30%     28%     22%     18%     12%

CAUSES:
  1. IMPORT COMPETITION:
     Japan, then Korea, then China producing manufactures more cheaply
     US car imports from Japan: near zero (1960) to 25% (1980)
     Textile imports: developing world competitive
  2. PRODUCTIVITY GROWTH IN MANUFACTURING:
     Automation = same output with fewer workers
     Manufacturing output may stay the same but employment falls
     (Baumol's cost disease: services don't automate as easily;
     services employment grows as manufacturing shrinks)
  3. OFFSHORING:
     Corporate decision to move production to lower-wage locations
     Initially: textile/apparel to Caribbean, then East Asia
     Then: electronics assembly to China
     Then: services (call centers to India 1990s-2000s)

FINANCIALIZATION (1980s onward):
  Financial sector's share of corporate profits:
  1950s-60s: ~10-15% of S&P 500 profits
  2000s: ~30-40% of corporate profits
  (Before 2008 crisis: 40%+)

  Finance, insurance, real estate (FIRE) share of GDP:
  US 1950: ~10%; US 2007: ~21%

  What drove it:
  1. Deregulation: Glass-Steagall erosion; derivatives deregulation
  2. Mathematical finance: Black-Scholes (1973) → options; complex products
  3. Petrodollar recycling: oil exporters' surplus deposited in Western banks;
     banks needed to deploy it
  4. Reagan-Thatcher ideological shift: markets > regulation
  5. Shareholder value maximization doctrine (Jensen-Meckling, 1976;
     Friedman's "social responsibility of business" 1970):
     CEOs should maximize stock price; stock options as compensation
     Short-termism; share buybacks; financial engineering over real investment
```

## Why Did the Golden Age End?

```
GOLDEN AGE EXHAUSTION (1973 and after)
========================================

1. CATCH-UP GROWTH EXHAUSTED:
   Europe and Japan had caught up to US technology frontier by ~1970
   No more cheap catch-up gains; must now do original innovation
   Growth slowed as catch-up potential diminished

2. CHEAP ENERGY GONE:
   The entire postwar production system assumed $2-3/barrel oil
   All infrastructure: cars, suburbs, highways, long supply chains
   None of this was optimal for $30-40/barrel oil
   Restructuring required: took 10-15 years

3. BRETTON WOODS BROKE DOWN (1971-1973):
   Exchange rate uncertainty; capital flows volatility
   Countries lost ability to use monetary policy for domestic goals
   Impossible trinity: cannot simultaneously have fixed exchange rates,
   free capital flows, AND independent monetary policy
   Post-1971: chose free capital flows; gave up fixed rates

4. SUPPLY-SIDE SHOCK vs DEMAND-SIDE TOOLS:
   Oil was supply-side; Keynesian tools are demand-side
   No good demand-side response to a cost-push shock

5. LABOR POWER PEAKED:
   1970s: union power at peak in US and UK
   Labor demanded wage increases during inflation
   Cost-push wage-price spiral resulted
   From employer perspective: labor costs rose faster than productivity
   Response: mechanization; offshoring; union-busting (Reagan PATCO 1981)

6. PRODUCTIVITY SLOWDOWN (SOLOW PARADOX):
   Total factor productivity growth slowed after 1973
   Robert Solow (1987): "You can see the computer age everywhere
   except in the productivity statistics"
   ICT investment was not yet showing up in aggregate productivity
   (Shows up post-1995, somewhat; but never as large as 1945-1973
   growth rates)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**MITI did not run the Japanese economy as a command economy.** Japanese firms retained autonomy; MITI's guidance was often ignored (Honda and Sony are the famous cases). The mechanism was credit allocation and market access, not command. The relationship was more like a powerful regulator with implicit leverage than a Soviet-style planner.

**"Asian miracle" did not prove industrial policy always works.** The same governments that supported successful industries also supported many failures. The selection of which industries to support was often more lucky than systematic. Furthermore, the political conditions (authoritarian governments with bureaucratic capacity and constrained rent-seeking) are hard to replicate.

**The Volcker shock was not caused by Reagan.** Carter appointed Volcker and the October 1979 policy change occurred under Carter. Reagan inherited it and supported it. The political risk of 10.8% unemployment fell on Reagan, who accepted it deliberately.

**Stagflation did not "disprove" Keynesian economics.** It demonstrated that demand management alone cannot handle supply-side cost shocks. The Keynesian framework still applies to demand shocks. The lesson was: you need supply-side and demand-side tools. The monetarist conclusion (only money supply matters) was also too strong; it was useful for the specific problem of breaking inflationary expectations.

**Deindustrialization in rich countries is not pure loss.** Standard trade theory predicts workers move from manufacturing to higher-productivity services as comparative advantage shifts. The problem was the transition costs: laid-off steelworkers in Pittsburgh cannot easily become software engineers in San Francisco. Geographic and skill mismatch created "rust belt" left-behind communities. The aggregate gains were real; the distributional problem was severe and politically explosive.
