# Ancient and Medieval Trade — Economic History

## The Big Picture

Long-distance trade and credit instruments are older than money itself. The core financial technologies — interest-bearing loans, bills of exchange, partnership contracts — were invented not once but several times across disconnected civilizations. The medieval period ended not with barbarism but with the most sophisticated pre-modern commercial network in history.

```
TRADE AND CREDIT TIMELINE
===========================

3000 BCE        Mesopotamian temple/palace economy
                Grain receipts, silver weights, interest-bearing loans

2000 BCE        Bronze Age trade networks: Dilmun hub (Bahrain)
                Tin from Afghanistan + copper from Cyprus = bronze everywhere

600 BCE         Lydian coinage (electrum); Greek silver drachma follows
                Coinage as standardized exchange medium

500 BCE-500 CE  Roman Mediterranean system
                Grain from Egypt/N.Africa to Rome; manufactured goods back
                Silver coinage; banking (trapezitai, argentarii)

500-1000 CE     Islamic commercial expansion
                Baghdad-Basra-Aden-Gujarat-Malacca axis
                mudaraba, suftaja, hawala: financial infrastructure

1000-1300       European commercial revolution begins
                Champagne fairs: clearing house innovation
                Italian city-states: Venice, Genoa, Florence rise

1241-1350       Mongol Pax: Silk Road briefly open; plague vector

1347-1351       Black Death: 30-50% European mortality
                Labor shock: institutional change follows

1358-1450       Hanseatic League peak
                N. European commercial network, 170+ cities

1400-1500       Italian financial innovation peaks
                Bills of exchange, double-entry bookkeeping, banking houses
```

## Mesopotamian Credit: The World's First Financial System

### Temple and Palace Economy

Sumer (3000-2000 BCE) operated a mixed economy: temples and palaces controlled large inventories of grain and administered redistribution, but private merchants operated alongside.

```
MESOPOTAMIAN ECONOMIC STRUCTURE
=================================

TEMPLE/PALACE (redistributive sector)
    |
    |-- Collected agricultural surplus
    |-- Employed artisans, scribes
    |-- Distributed rations (proto-salary system)
    |-- Lent grain and silver to merchants
    |
    v
TAMKARUM (merchant, private sector)
    |
    |-- Received capital from temple/palace
    |-- Conducted long-distance trade
    |-- Shared profits: typically 2/3 merchant, 1/3 temple
    |-- Used written contracts (clay tablets) to document obligations
    |
    v
TRADE ROUTES
    |-- Dilmun (Bahrain): entrepot for copper, timber
    |-- Meluhha (Indus Valley): carnelian, ivory
    |-- Anatolia: silver, copper (Assyrian merchant colonies at Kanesh)
```

**Assyrian merchant colony at Kanesh (Karum Kanesh, ~1950-1750 BCE):**
23,000 clay tablets recovered from Kanesh (modern Kultepe, Turkey).
Assyrian merchants sent cloth and tin from Assur; returned with silver and gold.
Used partnership contracts (tappatum): investor plus traveling merchant.
Used sealed clay envelopes as tamper-evident containers for contracts.
Interest rates: 30% per year for silver loans; 50% for grain loans.
Letters between family members describe credit, disputes, shipping problems.

This is not primitive barter. This is sophisticated commercial finance.

### Interest-Bearing Loans and Hammurabi's Caps

Interest existed in Mesopotamia from at least 3000 BCE — predating coinage by 2,400 years.

```
MESOPOTAMIAN INTEREST RATE STRUCTURE
======================================

Standard rates:
  Silver loans: 20% per annum (shekel-weight silver)
  Grain loans:  33% per annum (volume measure)

Hammurabi Code (~1754 BCE) interest caps:
  Silver: maximum 20% per annum
  Grain:  maximum 33% per annum

Enforcement: Debts above the cap were unenforceable
             BUT cap was roughly equal to the market rate anyway
             Hammurabi was codifying practice, not restricting it

DEBT CANCELLATION (andurarum):
  Periodic royal proclamations cancelled agricultural debts
  Motivation: debt slavery was depleting the free farmer class
              (farmers indebted to debt slaves means army shrinks)
  NOT cancellation of commercial debts between merchants
  The distinction: subsistence debt vs commercial debt

DEBT SLAVERY:
  Inability to repay meant debtor and family became slaves
  Maximum term: 3 years
  Hammurabi Code Paragraph 117: after 3 years, debt slave freed
```

**The usury debate's origin:** Hammurabi set maximum rates. Aristotle (384-322 BCE) later argued money was "barren" and could not reproduce itself naturally, so interest was unnatural. This became the basis for both medieval Christian and Islamic prohibitions on interest. The practical solution in all traditions: find ways to embed a return without calling it interest.

### What "Money" Was in Early Mesopotamia

No coined money. "Money" was weighed commodities:
- Silver (most common for large transactions): weighed in shekels (8.3 grams)
- Grain (barley): measured by volume
- Wool, copper, tin: used in specific contexts

**Credit preceding coinage:** Many transactions were settled by credit recorded on tablet, not physical transfer of silver. A merchant might present a clay tablet recording a debt, which would be transferred or extinguished. The medium of exchange was often the record, not the commodity.

## Roman Trade Networks

### The Mediterranean Grain System

Rome's population grew to perhaps 1 million by 1 CE — unprecedented for an ancient city. Feeding it required an industrial-scale supply chain.

```
ROMAN MEDITERRANEAN GRAIN SYSTEM
===================================

GRAIN SURPLUS PROVINCES:
  Egypt:       ~5 million modii/year to Rome
  N. Africa:   ~30 million modii/year to Rome  (Carthage/Utica area)
  Sicily:      ~3 million modii/year to Rome
  Sardinia:    supplementary

         |
         v
    OSTIA (port of Rome, 15 miles southwest)
         |
         |-- Harbor rebuilt by Claudius, then Trajan
         |-- Grain warehouses (horrea): massive complexes
         |-- Grain traders (negotiatores frumentarii)
         |-- Banking and money-changing offices
         |
         v
    TIBER RIVER to ROME
         (barge traffic to city warehouses)
         |
         v
    ANNONA (state grain distribution)
         ~200,000 citizens received free grain
         Grain dole (frumentatio): political stability tool

RETURN FLOWS:
  Manufactured goods: ceramics, glass, metalwork to provinces
  Wine and olive oil: Italy to N.Africa; Spain to Britain
  The amphora distribution map reveals trade routes
```

**Amphora archaeology as trade data:** Broken amphora fragments provide cliometric evidence. Monte Testaccio in Rome is a hill made of roughly 53 million amphora fragments. The distribution and stamping of amphorae reveals origin, date, and merchant identity — trade data extracted from physical evidence.

### Roman Banking (Argentarii and Mensarii)

Roman banking was sophisticated but differed from modern banking in key ways:

| Feature | Roman Banking | Modern Banking |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Deposits | Held for safekeeping, interest occasionally paid | Interest-bearing standard |
| Loans | Yes, at 12% annual rate (legal maximum) | Yes |
| Money transfer | Via giro (transfer order between accounts) | Yes |
| Note issuance | No bank notes | Central bank notes |
| Fractional reserve | Limited; extent unclear | Yes, regulated |
| State backing | No deposit insurance; occasional state lending | FDIC/central bank |

The Roman *stipulatio* (verbal contract, witnessed) was legally enforceable. Banking contracts used this form. The *chirograph* (written document) also appeared in later Roman practice.

### Coinage Debasement and Inflation

The Currency Crisis of the Third Century (235-284 CE) illustrates how monetary policy failures destroy trade:

```
ROMAN SILVER DEBASEMENT
========================

Silver content of denarius:
  Nero (64 CE):         ~90% silver
  Commodus (192 CE):    ~75%
  Septimius Severus:    ~55%
  Caracalla (215 CE):   ~50%  (introduced the antoninianus coin)
  Gallienus (268 CE):   ~2-5% (effectively bronze with silver wash)
  Diocletian (301 CE):  reset with new coinage; edict on maximum prices

WHY IT HAPPENED:
  Army pay (donativa) was political necessity after each civil war
  Silver mines not expanding fast enough
  Solution: reduce silver content = seigniorage without taxation
  Result: quantity theory kicks in; inflation follows
          Soldiers demanded pay in kind, not coin
          Long-distance trade disrupted; localization followed

DIOCLETIAN'S RESPONSE (301 CE):
  Edict on Maximum Prices: wage and price controls
  Listed ~1,000 items with maximum legal prices
  Penalty: death for overcharging
  Result: goods disappeared from markets; black market emerged;
          edict eventually abandoned
  Lesson: price controls without supply management fail
```

## Medieval Commercial Infrastructure

### Champagne Fairs (1100-1300 CE)

The Champagne fairs were the clearing house of medieval international commerce. Four towns in the County of Champagne (Troyes, Provins, Lagny, Bar-sur-Aube) hosted six annual fairs that together ran almost year-round.

```
CHAMPAGNE FAIR STRUCTURE
=========================

WHY CHAMPAGNE?
  Geography: between Italy (Florence, Venice, Genoa) and
             N. Europe (Bruges, London, Cologne)
  Political: Counts of Champagne provided safety and legal enforcement
             (fair wardens = "gardes des foires")
  Timing: six fairs staggered through year = near-continuous operation

FAIR CALENDAR:
  Lagny-sur-Marne:    Jan-Feb
  Bar-sur-Aube:       Feb-Mar
  Troyes (hot fair):  May-Jun
  Provins (May fair): May-Jun
  Troyes (cold fair): Sep-Oct
  Provins (Nov fair): Nov-Dec

WHAT WAS TRADED:
  Cloth from Flanders and N. France: primary commodity
  Italian silk, spices from Levant: high value
  Wool from England: raw material
  Furs from N. Europe: luxury

SETTLEMENT MECHANISM (the key financial innovation):
  Week 1-2:    Cloth fair (physical goods exchange)
  Week 3-5:    Leather fair
  Final days:  "PAIEMENT" -- pure financial settlement

  Italian merchants arrived with letters of credit from buyers
  and sellers across Europe. In the final days:
  1. All debts and credits were tabulated
  2. Netting occurred: A owes B, B owes C, C owes A -- cancel
  3. Net balances only settled in coin or carried forward

  THIS IS MULTILATERAL NETTING -- same as modern clearinghouse
  It dramatically reduced the need to transport silver across Europe
```

**Bills of exchange origins:** The Champagne fairs saw early use of written payment orders. By 1300, this had developed into the full 4-party bill of exchange. See 02-COMMERCIAL-REVOLUTION.md for full mechanics.

**Decline of Champagne fairs (~1300):**
- War of succession for Champagne: political instability collapsed legal protection
- Galley routes opened (Genoa-Bruges by sea, 1277): bypassed overland routes
- Italian merchant colonies established permanently in Bruges: fairs unnecessary
- The Champagne clearing mechanism was internalized within Italian banking houses

### The Hanseatic League (1241-1669)

The Hansa was a merchant guild confederation controlling northern European trade. Not a state, not a company — something institutionally new.

```
HANSEATIC LEAGUE STRUCTURE
============================

CORE FEATURE: Collective action without formal government
  Not a state:   no territory, no standing army
  Not a company: no joint ownership, no shareholders
  Not a guild:   transcended single craft or city
  Actual form:   voluntary confederation of merchant cities
                 with collective security and trading privileges

GEOGRAPHY AT PEAK (~1400):
  170+ member cities
  Arc: London to Bruges to Hamburg to Lubeck to Danzig to Riga to Reval (Tallinn)
  Baltic and North Sea dominance
  Controlled: Baltic grain, Scandinavian fish, Russian furs/wax/honey,
              Flemish cloth, English wool

KONTOR SYSTEM (trading posts):
  Four major permanent trading posts with extraterritorial rights:
  1. Steelyard, London:     wool and cloth purchasing
  2. Brugse Kontor, Bruges: Flemish cloth, financial hub
  3. Bergen Kontor:         Norwegian dried fish (stockfish) monopoly
  4. Peterhof, Novgorod:    Russian goods

  The Kontors were embassies + warehouses + banks combined:
  - Hansa merchants lived inside, often forbidden to marry locally
  - Local law did not apply inside
  - Collective credit and mutual insurance among members
  - Strict quality control: expelled merchants who sold substandard goods

ENFORCEMENT MECHANISMS:
  1. Collective boycott (Verhansung): expelled cities cut off from trade
     = economic death for a Baltic port city
  2. Naval blockade: occasional use of hired fleets
  3. Trading privileges: political leverage for favorable terms
     (English merchants excluded from Baltic in return for
      Steelyard privileges in London)

KEY COMMODITY FLOWS:
  Baltic to West:  rye, wheat, timber, pitch, tar, furs, wax
  West to Baltic:  cloth (Flemish/English), salt, dried herring, wine

  CRITICAL: Baltic grain fed North European cities
             Without Hansa grain flow, Amsterdam/Antwerp could not grow
```

**Why the Hansa declined (1550-1650):**
- Dutch and English merchants built larger, cheaper vessels (Dutch fluyt, 1590s): undercut Hansa on shipping costs
- Nation-states formed: diplomatic protection no longer required from a guild
- Amsterdam rose as the new commercial center
- Thirty Years' War disrupted the Baltic (1618-1648)

**The Hansa as institutional template:** The Kontor system — extraterritorial merchant outpost, collective credit, mutual enforcement — influenced later institutions: the English factory system in India, the VOC trading posts, the British Levant Company.

## Islamic Commercial Law and Infrastructure

### The Problem: Trade Without Interest

Islamic law (fiqh) prohibits riba — usually translated as interest, more precisely "unjustified increase." A loan of 100 requiring repayment of 110 is riba, forbidden.

But long-distance trade requires financing. The Islamic world solved this with legal instruments that provided a return without technically charging interest.

```
ISLAMIC COMMERCIAL INSTRUMENTS
================================

1. MUDARABA (Commenda Partnership)
   +--------------------------------------------+
   | Investor (rabb al-mal)                     |
   |   Provides: capital (100 dinars)           |
   |   Provides: nothing else -- no labor       |
   |   Receives: agreed share of PROFIT (e.g. 40%)|
   |   Liability: loss of capital only          |
   +------------------+--------------------------+
                      |
   +------------------v--------------------------+
   | Working Partner (mudarib)                   |
   |   Provides: labor, skill, management        |
   |   Provides: nothing financial               |
   |   Receives: agreed share of PROFIT (60%)    |
   |   Liability: loss of time and effort only   |
   +--------------------------------------------+

   KEY: Return to investor is SHARE OF PROFIT, not interest
   Losses: investor loses capital; working partner loses effort
   Legal form: this is equity, not debt
   Identical to: Commenda in Mediterranean (Italian) law
   SIGNIFICANCE: conceptual ancestor of limited partnership

2. SUFTAJA (Bill of Exchange / Hawala predecessor)
   Merchant in Baghdad gives 100 dinars to banker A
   Banker A issues a letter (suftaja)
   Merchant carries letter to Basra
   Banker A's Basra correspondent B pays out 100 dinars
   NO INTEREST -- but exchange rate could embed a return
   FUNCTION: avoids physical transport of coin; reduces robbery risk

3. HAWALA (Value Transfer Without Physical Transfer)
   Sender gives money to agent A in city X
   Agent A contacts agent B in city Y (trust network)
   Agent B pays recipient in city Y
   A and B settle the net balance later or through return flows
   Self-enforcing through reputation: default = loss of network
   Modern survival: hawala networks still used for remittances
   in South Asia, Middle East, Africa

4. MUSHARAKA (Equity Partnership)
   All partners contribute capital
   Profits and losses shared proportionally

5. BAY AL-SALAM (Forward Contract for Agricultural Goods)
   Buyer pays NOW for delivery of agricultural goods LATER
   At a discount (buyer takes delivery risk)
```

**The hajj as economic infrastructure:** The annual pilgrimage to Mecca created one of the world's first regularly scheduled mass-movement events. Pilgrims from Spain to Indonesia traveled the same routes, carried goods, extended credit, formed partnerships. The hajj network was as important commercially as it was religiously.

**Islamic banking's geographic reach:** By 1000 CE, a merchant could travel from Toledo (Spain) to Guangzhou (China) using only Islamic financial instruments — letters of credit, partnership contracts — without once requiring physical coin transfer. This network had no equivalent in Christian Europe until the 14th-century Italian banking system.

## The Black Death: Labor Market Shock of the Century

### The Shock

Yersinia pestis arrived in Sicily from Crimea in 1347. By 1351, it had killed 30-50% of Europe's population:
- Florence: 50-60% mortality
- England: 40-50%
- Poland and parts of Eastern Europe: 20-30%
- Milan: ~15% (aggressive quarantine — first use of quarantine in Western history)

```
BLACK DEATH POPULATION SHOCK
==============================

Western Europe population (millions, approximate):
  1300:  75 million
  1351:  50 million (after first wave)
  1400:  45 million (multiple subsequent waves)
  1500:  55 million
  1600:  78 million (back to pre-plague levels after 250 years)

LABOR MARKET MECHANICS (supply and demand):

  Before plague:
  +--------------------------------------------------+
  | Population high: labor abundant                  |
  | Lords negotiate from strength                    |
  | Serfdom: labor tied to land                      |
  | Wages: near subsistence                          |
  +--------------------------------------------------+

  After plague:
  +--------------------------------------------------+
  | Population -40%: labor scarce                    |
  | Surviving workers negotiate from strength        |
  | Lords compete for labor                          |
  | Wages: real wages roughly double in 50 years     |
  | Technology: horse-drawn plow adopted faster      |
  |   (horse faster than ox; substitutes expensive   |
  |   labor when labor is scarce)                    |
  +--------------------------------------------------+
```

### The East-West Divergence in Serfdom Response

The most important institutional puzzle from the Black Death: in Western Europe (England, France, Low Countries), serfdom collapsed. In Eastern Europe (Poland, Russia, Prussia, Bohemia), serfdom was actually tightened. Why?

```
EAST-WEST DIVERGENCE IN SERFDOM RESPONSE
==========================================

WESTERN EUROPE (serfdom collapsed):
  Mechanisms:
  1. Peasant mobility: flee to towns; lord cannot easily retrieve
  2. Towns needed labor: offered legal freedom to serfs
  3. English common law: "stay in a royal borough a year and a day
     and you are free" -- jurisdictional competition
  4. Peasant revolts: Wat Tyler (1381), Jacquerie France (1358)
     Not immediately successful but demonstrated costs of coercion
  5. Commercial agriculture: English wool to Flemish cloth
     = wage labor more efficient than coerced labor for this
  Statute of Laborers (1351): Parliament tried to freeze wages at
    pre-plague levels; failed, widely evaded

EASTERN EUROPE (serfdom tightened = "Second Serfdom"):
  Timeline: 1400-1650 gradual reenserfment
  Mechanisms:
  1. Weak towns: less developed; serfs had nowhere to flee
  2. Noble political power: Polish, Prussian, Russian nobility
     stronger relative to crown; could legislate labor control
  3. Export grain to West: grain export via Baltic profitable;
     coerced labor on grain estates (Gutswirtschaft = demesne economy)
  4. No common law: no "borough freedom" doctrine in Eastern Europe
  5. Long distance from strong markets: lower opportunity cost
     of maintaining coercive arrangements

  Polish serf law tightened: 1496, then 1519, then 1573
  Russian serfdom formalized 1649 (Sobornoye Ulozheniye)
  Prussian Junker estate system

  CONSEQUENCE: Eastern Europe's development path diverged here.
  North-Wallis-Weingast thesis: W. Europe developed "open access
  orders" faster; E. Europe remained in "natural state"
  configurations much longer. This structural divergence explains
  much of the 20th-century income gap between East and West Europe.
```

**The Malthusian reading of the Black Death:**
- Before plague: high population, low wages, at Malthusian equilibrium
- Plague: massive population reduction
- Result: temporary increase in per capita resources
- Mechanism: same land, fewer people, more food per person, higher wages
- Clark's real wage data confirms: English wages roughly doubled 1350-1450
- Then: population recovered slowly; wages gradually fell back
- This is Malthusian dynamics working as predicted — triggered by disease rather than famine

## Common Confusion Points

**"Barter preceded money" is mostly a myth.** David Graeber's "Debt: The First 5000 Years" (2011) makes this case well. Anthropologists find no society that used barter as the primary exchange mechanism. Credit preceded coinage. Mesopotamian evidence shows loans recorded on clay tablets before standardized coinage existed. Money-of-account (abstract units like shekels) preceded money-as-object.

**The Hansa was not a cartel in the modern sense.** It did not fix prices or divide markets between members. It enforced quality standards and collectively maintained political privileges. The economic power came from exclusive access to certain trading routes and posts, enforced by collective diplomatic action.

**Islamic financial instruments were not just "avoiding interest on a technicality."** Mudaraba genuinely allocates risk differently from debt. In a debt relationship, the borrower bears all downside (must repay regardless). In mudaraba, both investor and working partner bear downside risk in their respective domains. This is a substantively different incentive structure — closer to equity than debt.

**The "Dark Ages" were not economically dark, globally.** While Western Europe contracted after Rome's fall, Islamic civilization (700-1200), Tang Dynasty China (618-907), Song Dynasty China (960-1279), and the Byzantine Empire all achieved economic sophistication surpassing contemporary Western Europe. The label "Dark Ages" is a parochial European frame.

**Second Serfdom was not simply reverting to early medieval conditions.** It was a new institutional form created in response to the specific opportunity of Baltic grain export markets — serfdom as export-oriented production system, qualitatively different from the personal bondage arrangements of early medieval Europe.
