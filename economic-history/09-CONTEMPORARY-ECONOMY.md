# The Contemporary Economy — Economic History

## The Big Picture

<!-- @editor[diagram/P1]: No landscape diagram — guide opens with a timeline but no system diagram showing how Washington Consensus, hyperglobalization, 2008 crisis, and deglobalization structurally relate as cause-and-effect -->

The period from 1989 to the present has been shaped by three massive economic experiments: the Washington Consensus (1989-2008), hyperglobalization via WTO and China's rise (1994-2016), and the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis (2008-present). The third experiment broke the policy consensus of the previous two. We are now in an era of industrial policy return, deglobalization pressures, and unresolved questions about secular stagnation.

```
CONTEMPORARY ECONOMIC TIMELINE
================================

1989        Washington Consensus formulated (Williamson)
            Berlin Wall falls; Soviet bloc transitions begin

1990-1998   Structural adjustment programs: IMF conditionality applied
            widely in Latin America, Africa, Eastern Europe

1991        Soviet Union collapses; "end of history" (Fukuyama)

1994        NAFTA: US-Canada-Mexico free trade
            WTO process completed (Uruguay Round signed)

1994-95     Mexican peso crisis: first "Washington Consensus crisis"
            IMF bailout; structural adjustment

1997-98     Asian financial crisis: Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea
            IMF conditionality criticized; "crony capitalism" blamed
            (Stiglitz resigns from World Bank in protest)

1999        Euro launched (11 countries initially)
            Glass-Steagall effectively repealed (Gramm-Leach-Bliley)

2001        China joins WTO: December 11
            "China shock" begins: manufacturing job displacement in West

2001        Dot-com bust: first major tech bubble since 1929

2001-2006   US housing bubble: low rates + deregulation + securitization

2007        Bear Stearns hedge funds fail: first warning
2008        Bear Stearns (March), Fannie/Freddie (September), Lehman (September 15)
            AIG bailout; TARP ($700B); global financial crisis

2008-2015   Zero lower bound: Fed funds rate near 0%; QE 1, 2, 3
            European debt crisis: Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, Italy

2009        China fiscal stimulus: ~$586 billion; largest in history
2012        Mario Draghi: "Whatever it takes" -- saves euro

2016        Brexit vote; Trump election: populist revolt begins
2017-2020   US-China trade war; tariffs; decoupling pressure

2020        COVID-19 pandemic: largest peacetime government spending ever
            US fiscal response: ~$5 trillion in 2020-21

2021-2022   Inflation surge: post-COVID supply chain disruption +
            Ukraine war + fiscal stimulus
2022-2023   Rate hiking cycle: fastest since Volcker era

2022        Inflation Reduction Act (US): $369 billion; industrial policy
            CHIPS Act ($52 billion): semiconductor industrial policy
            = Washington Consensus officially abandoned
```

## Washington Consensus

### What It Actually Said

John Williamson (Peterson Institute) coined "Washington Consensus" in a 1989 paper synthesizing what policy institutions in Washington (IMF, World Bank, US Treasury) were recommending to Latin American countries. It was descriptive first; became prescriptive.

```
WASHINGTON CONSENSUS: 10 POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS (1989)
========================================================

1. FISCAL DISCIPLINE:
   Eliminate large budget deficits
   Rationale: deficits cause inflation and crowd out private investment

2. REORIENTATION OF PUBLIC SPENDING:
   Away from subsidies (especially indiscriminate ones)
   Toward: education, health, infrastructure
   (This was less ideological than often assumed)

3. TAX REFORM:
   Broaden tax base; reduce marginal rates
   Improve enforcement

4. FINANCIAL LIBERALIZATION:
   Interest rates to be market-determined
   End directed credit programs
   Rationale: price of capital should reflect opportunity cost

5. UNIFIED AND COMPETITIVE EXCHANGE RATES:
   End multiple exchange rate systems
   Achieve competitive rate for exports
   (NOT necessarily free floating; competitive peg was acceptable)

6. TRADE LIBERALIZATION:
   Remove quantitative restrictions (import licenses, quotas)
   Reduce tariffs; phase-in periods for sensitive sectors

7. LIBERALIZATION OF FOREIGN DIRECT INVESTMENT:
   Remove barriers to FDI entry

8. PRIVATIZATION:
   Privatize state-owned enterprises
   Rationale: private firms more efficient; government should not be
   running airlines, phone companies, steel mills

9. DEREGULATION:
   Remove barriers to entry; prevent monopoly except where economically justified

10. PROPERTY RIGHTS:
    Extend property rights (de Soto's thesis: informal economy
    cannot access credit because assets not legally titled)

WILLIAMSON'S INTENT vs HOW IT WAS APPLIED:
  Williamson claimed: these were moderate, sensible reforms
  In practice: IMF structural adjustment programs often applied
  versions of these as conditions for crisis lending
  WITH EXCESSIVE SPEED: simultaneous capital market opening,
  fiscal austerity, and trade liberalization → destabilizing
  SEQUENCING PROBLEM: financial liberalization before regulatory
  capacity was in place → banking crises
```

### Structural Adjustment: The Reality

```
STRUCTURAL ADJUSTMENT PROGRAMS (SAPs)
=======================================

MECHANISM:
  Country has balance of payments crisis (cannot pay debts)
  Goes to IMF for emergency loans
  IMF requires policy changes (conditionality) in exchange
  Same from World Bank for development loans

STANDARD PACKAGE (1980s-90s):
  Fiscal austerity: cut government spending, raise taxes
  Monetary tightening: raise interest rates
  Currency devaluation
  Trade liberalization: reduce tariffs quickly
  Privatization: sell state enterprises
  Financial liberalization: remove capital controls
  Labor market "flexibilization": make it easier to fire workers

RESULTS (contested):

ARGENTINA (1990s):
  Dollar peg implemented 1991 (Convertibility Law)
  Worked initially: ended hyperinflation
  But: when Brazil devalued (1999), Argentina's exports uncompetitive
  IMF kept lending as Argentina's deficit grew
  2001: Argentina defaulted ($95B) and abandoned convertibility
  2002: peso fell 75%; severe depression; unemployment 20%+
  IMF conditionality: criticized for demanding austerity
  that prevented adjustment

ZAMBIA, GHANA, MALI (Africa):
  Trade liberalization: removed tariffs
  Domestic manufacturing: wiped out by imports
  Agricultural price support removed: farmers exposed to world prices
  Result: deindustrialization of already-thin manufacturing base
  Social spending cuts: schools, clinics closed
  "Adjustment with a human face" (UNICEF, 1987): documentation of
  health and education consequences of austerity

STIGLITZ CRITIQUE ("Globalization and Its Discontents", 2002):
  Stiglitz: Chief Economist World Bank 1997-2000; resigned in protest
  Main arguments:
  1. IMF prescription designed to serve creditor interests (US banks)
     more than debtor country interests
  2. One-size-fits-all: ignored local context
  3. Sequencing wrong: opening capital markets before building
     banking regulation caused crises (East Asia 1997)
  4. Austerity in recession: contractionary; worsens the crisis
  5. Privatization without regulation: created private monopolies
     that were worse than public ones
```

### East Asian Financial Crisis (1997-98)

The 1997 crisis was partly produced by the financial liberalization that Washington Consensus had promoted, and IMF's response deepened it.

```
EAST ASIAN FINANCIAL CRISIS 1997-98
=====================================

BUILDUP:
  1990s: East Asian countries pressured to open capital accounts
  (allow foreign capital to flow in and out freely)
  Short-term capital flowed in: attracted by high growth and
  interest rate differentials
  Fixed exchange rates: investors felt no currency risk
  Bank lending: local banks borrowed in dollars (cheap);
  lent domestically in local currencies to risky projects
  CURRENCY MISMATCH: borrowed dollars, lent Thai baht/Korean won
  MATURITY MISMATCH: borrowed short-term (foreign capital); lent long-term

  Asset price bubbles: real estate (Thailand, Indonesia)

TRIGGER (Thailand, July 1997):
  Speculators attacked Thai baht (knew it was overvalued)
  Thailand: tried to defend peg; burned through reserves
  July 2, 1997: Thailand abandoned peg; baht fell ~50%
  Contagion: Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, South Korea

CONTAGION MECHANICS:
  1. Currency fell → foreign debt cost rose (borrowed in dollars;
     now need more local currency to repay)
  2. Bank solvency collapse → credit freeze
  3. IMF called in; crisis deepened before IMF programs took effect

IMF RESPONSE AND CONTROVERSY:
  IMF demanded: fiscal austerity, high interest rates, rapid
  structural reforms (close insolvent banks; open capital markets further)
  CRITIQUE:
    High interest rates: defense against currency speculation; but
    worsened recession and bank insolvencies (loans defaulted faster)
    Fiscal austerity: unnecessary; these countries were not fiscally
    profligate; austerity deepened the recession
    Structural reforms (close banks quickly): created panic when
    which banks would be closed was unknown

  MALAYSIA (Mahathir response):
  Malaysia refused IMF; imposed capital controls (September 1998)
  Mainstream economists: "this will be a disaster"
  Result: Malaysia recovered faster than Thailand; capital controls
  allowed monetary and fiscal stimulus without currency collapse
  Dani Rodrik used this as evidence that capital account openness
  was oversold

AFTERMATH:
  "Self-insurance" lesson: Asian countries accumulated enormous
  dollar reserves ($1-3 trillion) as protection against future crises
  China: $3.1 trillion reserves; Korea: $400B; Japan: $1.3 trillion
  This reserve accumulation contributed to the "global savings glut"
  of 2000s (Bernanke) that helped fund the US housing bubble
```

## WTO and Hyperglobalization

### China's WTO Accession (2001)

```
CHINA WTO ACCESSION: DECEMBER 11, 2001
========================================

WHY IT MATTERED:
  China in 1978: GDP/capita ~$200; almost entirely agrarian
  China in 2001: GDP/capita ~$1,000; beginning manufacturing export surge
  China's comparative advantage: manufacturing at low labor cost
  WTO membership: guaranteed market access to all WTO members

CHINA'S TRADE TRAJECTORY:
  Chinese exports:
  1990: $62 billion
  2000: $249 billion
  2005: $762 billion
  2010: $1,578 billion
  2015: $2,274 billion (peak share; ~14% of world exports)

"CHINA SHOCK" (Autor, Dorn, Hanson 2013):
  Systematic study of US manufacturing job losses attributable to Chinese imports
  Methodology: compared US regions with different exposure to Chinese
  import competition; measured employment changes
  Finding: Chinese import competition eliminated ~2.4M US manufacturing jobs
  1999-2011
  Key result: affected regions did NOT reabsorb workers as trade theory predicted
              Workers stayed unemployed or left labor force
              Standard comparative advantage prediction: workers move to
              services; wages in other sectors rise
              Reality: manufacturing communities hollowed out; no reabsorption
  Political implication: the distributional costs of trade are concentrated
  in specific communities; benefits are diffuse (cheaper goods for all consumers)
  CHINA SHOCK contributed to: 2016 Trump election in affected districts

GLOBAL VALUE CHAINS:
  Pre-WTO: most manufacturing done in one country
  Post-WTO: production fragmented across countries
  CASE: iPhone supply chain (approximate, as of 2016):
    Design: Apple, USA
    Display: Samsung, South Korea; Japan Display, Japan
    Processor (A-series chip): designed by Apple; manufactured by TSMC, Taiwan
    Memory: SK Hynix (Korea), Micron (US)
    Camera modules: Sony, Japan
    Battery: Amperex, China
    Assembly: Foxconn, China (Shenzhen, Zhengzhou)
    Final value: USA (Apple margin ~35%)
    Chinese "value added" in iPhone: ~$8-10 out of ~$800 retail price
    But China gets credit for ~$800 in Chinese export statistics

  IMPLICATION: "made in China" statistics overstate Chinese content;
  trade deficits overstate bilateral trade imbalances in welfare terms
```

## China's Rise

```
CHINA'S ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT PATH
====================================

PHASE 1 (1949-1978): SOVIET MODEL
  Mao's industrialization: state planning; collectivization
  Great Leap Forward (1958-62): collectivize agriculture;
  mobilize for steel production; famine killed ~15-45M people
  Cultural Revolution (1966-76): educated class persecuted;
  universities closed; economic disruption
  Average growth: ~2-3% per capita; enormous human cost

PHASE 2 (1978-1992): REFORM AND OPENING UP (Deng Xiaoping)
  Household Responsibility System (agriculture, 1978):
  Farm households given long-term land use rights;
  could sell surplus above quota at market prices
  = de facto privatization of agricultural production
  Result: agricultural output surged; rural incomes rose; ~200M
  people moved out of extreme poverty in 1980s

  Township and Village Enterprises (TVEs):
  Hybrid: local government owned; market-oriented; competed for profit
  "Neither state nor private" -- institutional innovation
  Employed ~135M people by 1994; massive productivity growth

  Special Economic Zones (SEZs, 1980):
  Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shantou, Xiamen; later expanded
  Deferred to foreign investors; special rules; export processing
  Shenzhen: fishing village (1980) to ~13M population (2020)

  GRADUALISM vs BIG BANG (contrast with Soviet transition):
  Soviet and Eastern European transition: rapid comprehensive reform
  ("Big Bang" or "Shock Therapy"): mass privatization; immediate price
  liberalization; outcome: economic collapse and oligarchy formation
  China's gradualism: maintained state control; slowly introduced market
  mechanisms; allowed dual-track pricing; Result: sustained growth
  Debate: was China's success due to gradualism, or to initial conditions?

PHASE 3 (1992-2015): HIGH GROWTH
  "Southern Tour" (Deng, 1992): reignited market reform after
  Tiananmen (1989) had created reform hesitation
  SOE reform: many state enterprises privatized or restructured
  WTO accession (2001): export manufacturing boom
  Infrastructure investment: roads, railways, airports, ports
  at scale no other country has matched

  GDP growth rates:
  1990s: ~10%/year average
  2000s: ~10%/year average
  2010s: declining to ~6%

  URBANIZATION:
  Rural population share: 74% (1990) to 36% (2020)
  ~750 million people moved from rural to urban in 30 years
  Fastest urbanization in history (by absolute numbers)

PHASE 4 (2015-present): MIDDLE-INCOME TRAP QUESTION
  GDP/capita: ~$12,000 (2022); middle-income country
  The "middle-income trap": many countries stagnate between
  $8,000-20,000/capita; cannot transition from labor-cost advantage
  to innovation-led growth
  Challenges:
  - Demographics: aging population; one-child policy consequences
  - Debt: corporate debt ~170% of GDP; local government hidden debt
  - Property sector: Evergrande and sector-wide excess construction
  - Innovation: catching up; leading in some areas; falling behind in AI chips
  - Geopolitics: US-China decoupling; tech restrictions
  BELT AND ROAD INITIATIVE (BRI, 2013):
  Infrastructure lending to developing countries
  Critics: "debt trap diplomacy" -- overindebted countries cede assets
  (Hambantota Port, Sri Lanka)
  Defenders: fills genuine infrastructure financing gap
  Reality: both elements present; varies by project and country
```

## 2008 Financial Crisis Mechanics

### The Securitization Machine

The 2008 crisis was fundamentally a failure of risk distribution through structured finance. The instruments are worth understanding in detail because the crisis was not accidental — it was systematic.

```
2008 CRISIS: INSTRUMENT MECHANICS
=====================================

STARTING POINT: MORTGAGE ORIGINATION
  Traditional model: bank originates mortgage; holds it on balance sheet
  Until maturity; has skin in the game
  New model (OTD = Originate-To-Distribute):
  Bank originates mortgage; immediately sells it to securitizer
  Bank has NO ongoing exposure; no skin in the game
  Implication: bank cares about origination volume, not quality

SECURITIZATION: MBS (MORTGAGE-BACKED SECURITY)
  Securitizer (investment bank) buys 1,000 mortgages
  Pools them into a trust
  Issues securities backed by the pool's cash flows
  Tranches:
  +------------------+  Senior tranche: AAA rated
  |  SENIOR (70%)    |  First claim on cash flows; last to absorb losses
  +------------------+  Yield: Treasury + 0.5%
  |  MEZZANINE (20%) |  BBB-AA; higher yield; more loss exposure
  +------------------+
  |  EQUITY (10%)    |  "First loss" piece; no rating; very high yield
  +------------------+  Or held by originator as "skin in game"

  TRANCHING LOGIC:
  Pool of 1,000 mortgages; if 5% default:
  Equity tranche absorbs losses first; senior unaffected
  For senior tranche to lose money: need >30% of pool to default
  Ratings models said: impossible based on historical data
  (Historical data used: 1980-2000, a period of rising house prices
   and relatively conservative lending standards)

CDO: COLLATERALIZED DEBT OBLIGATION
  Problem: too many mezzanine tranches (BBB) being created
  Solution: pool BBB tranches from different MBS pools into a CDO
  CDO tranches:
  +------------------+  CDO Senior: AAA (again!)
  |  SENIOR (70%)    |  Rationale: even though each underlying is BBB,
  +------------------+  pooling diversifies; senior tranche should be safe
  |  MEZZANINE (20%) |  (This is mathematically valid IF defaults are
  +------------------+  uncorrelated. They were highly correlated.)
  |  EQUITY (10%)    |
  +------------------+

  CDO-SQUARED: pool CDO tranches into another CDO
  Distance from underlying assets: three layers removed
<!-- @editor[bridge/P3]: Natural bridge to software abstraction layers — CDO-squared is nested indirection where the abstraction leaks catastrophically; any engineer who has debugged a 3-layer-deep dependency failure will recognize this pattern -->

  THE CORRELATION PROBLEM:
  Gaussian copula model (Li, 2000): used to estimate correlation
  between mortgage defaults
  Parameter: "correlation" estimated from 10 years of data
  Real correlation in housing downturn: much higher (all mortgages
  correlated to local and national house prices)
  When house prices fell nationally: all mortgages correlated;
  diversification failed; AAA tranches took losses

CDS: CREDIT DEFAULT SWAP
  Insurance-like instrument: Buyer pays periodic premium to seller
  If reference entity defaults: seller pays buyer the face value
  Key: NOT insurance (no insurable interest required; no regulation
  as insurance; no reserve requirements)
  AIG Financial Products: sold CDS protection on CDOs
  Received premium; posted minimal collateral (didn't think defaults
  would correlate)
  Exposure: $400+ billion in CDS protection sold
  When CDOs fell in value: AIG owed collateral under CSAs
  (Credit Support Annexes to ISDA master agreements)
  AIG ran out of cash; government bailed out for $182 billion

THE RATINGS AGENCIES PROBLEM:
  Moody's, S&P, Fitch: gave AAA to CDO tranches
  Conflict of interest: issuers PAID the agencies to rate their products
  Agencies: competed for business by giving favorable ratings
  Agencies' models: used same historical data; same correlation assumptions
  Outcome: AAA ratings on instruments that were not AAA quality
  Regulatory consequence: pension funds, insurance companies required
  to hold AAA assets; this demand fed CDO machine
```

### Lehman and the Crisis Cascade

```
2008 CRISIS SEQUENCE
=====================

MARCH 2008: BEAR STEARNS
  Bear Stearns hedge funds (managed by Ralph Cioffi): failed July 2007
  (first signal)
  March 14, 2008: Bear Stearns approached Fed; nearly failed
  Fed arranged emergency sale to JPMorgan for $2/share
  ($2 vs $170/share 14 months earlier)
  Fed provided $29B non-recourse loan to facilitate deal
  Moral hazard signal: "too big to fail" established (or so it seemed)

SEPTEMBER 7, 2008: FANNIE AND FREDDIE
  Federal National Mortgage Association (Fannie Mae) and
  Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation (Freddie Mac):
  government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs)
  Had implicit (now explicit) government guarantee
  Insolvent: Treasury placed into conservatorship
  ~$5 trillion in mortgage exposure; government took it on

SEPTEMBER 15, 2008: LEHMAN BROTHERS
  Investment bank; massive exposure to residential MBS
  Treasury and Fed: decided NOT to bail out
  Rationale: Bear Stearns bailout had created moral hazard;
  letting Lehman fail would restore market discipline
  Result: overnight, counterparties froze
  Lehman bankruptcy: largest in US history ($691B in assets)

  WHAT HAPPENED NEXT (72 hours):
  Money Market Funds: Reserve Primary Fund "broke the buck"
  (NAV fell below $1.00 because of Lehman commercial paper losses)
  Money market fund RUNS: investors withdrew $300B in days
  Commercial paper market FROZE: companies could not roll short-term debt
  Global interbank market FROZE: banks refused to lend to each other

  The financial system came within days of complete seizure.

GOVERNMENT RESPONSE:
  September 19: Treasury guarantees money market funds
  October 3: TARP ($700B Troubled Asset Relief Program) signed
  October: Fed provides direct lending to money market funds, commercial
  paper market
  November: QE1 announced: Fed buys $600B in MBS and agency debt
  Citigroup, Bank of America: given capital injections and guarantees

REAL ECONOMY IMPACT:
  GDP: -8.9% annualized Q4 2008
  Unemployment: 5% (September 2008) to 10% (October 2009)
  Household wealth: fell ~$13 trillion
  Global: ~$19 trillion in global wealth destroyed

MORAL HAZARD RESOLUTION (or non-resolution):
  Bank executives largely not prosecuted
  Banks made whole by government
  Underwater homeowners: largely not helped
  (HAMP, the home modification program: reached ~1.2M of ~7M eligible)
  Tea Party response: "why bail out banks and irresponsible homeowners?"
  Left response: "why bail out banks but not homeowners?"
  Both critiques had merit
```

## Zero Lower Bound and Post-Crisis Policy

```
ZERO LOWER BOUND AND QE
========================

THE PROBLEM:
  Federal Funds Rate:
  September 2007: 5.25%
  December 2008:  0.00-0.25%  (Zero Lower Bound)

  Cannot cut below zero (people would hold cash instead of
  depositing at negative rates -- in theory)
  Traditional monetary policy exhausted; recession ongoing

QUANTITATIVE EASING (QE):
  Mechanism: Fed buys long-duration assets (Treasury bonds, MBS)
             from banks and investors
  Effect on balance sheet:
    Banks: exchange long-term bonds for reserves at Fed
           Reserves are liquid; bonds are not
    Fed: balance sheet expands (was $900B; grew to $4.5 trillion by 2015)

  INTENDED EFFECTS:
  1. Portfolio balance: banks and investors now hold more reserves;
     buy other assets (equities, corporate bonds); prices rise
  2. Wealth effect: rising asset prices increase consumer spending
  3. Lower long-term rates: Fed buying reduces yields on Treasuries;
     mortgage rates follow; housing investment stimulated
  4. Signal: Fed commitment to keep rates low reduces uncertainty

  WHAT QE DID AND DIDN'T DO:
  Did: prevented deeper financial collapse in 2008-09; asset prices
       recovered; avoided deflation
  Didn't: produce rapid recovery in employment; labor market slow;
          "recovery" felt like recession for most workers
  Side effect: asset price inflation widened inequality
              (asset owners recovered; non-owners did not)

SECULAR STAGNATION (Summers, 2013):
  Why was recovery so slow?
  Hypothesis: the neutral real interest rate (r*) has been falling for
  decades and may now be permanently negative
  Reasons for falling r*:
  1. Demographics: aging population saves more; invests less
  2. Technology: information technology is cheap; less capital investment
     per dollar of output than industrial age
  3. Inequality: wealthy save high fraction of income; poor spend more;
     as inequality rises, aggregate savings rise, investment returns fall
  4. Global savings glut (Bernanke): developing country reserve accumulation
     suppresses global interest rates

  IMPLICATION: monetary policy may be permanently impotent;
  fiscal policy (government spending) needed to maintain demand

MMT (MODERN MONETARY THEORY) DEBATE:
  Mosler, Wray, Kelton ("The Deficit Myth", 2020):
  Sovereign currency issuers (US, UK, Japan) cannot run out of money
  in their own currency
  Fiscal space is limited by real resources and inflation, not by
  "running out of money"
  Government deficit = private sector surplus (accounting identity)
  Implication: do not fear deficits; fear inflation; adjust fiscal
  stance based on inflation target, not deficit target

  MAINSTREAM CRITIQUE:
  MMT is accounting, not economics; tells you what you can do,
  not what is optimal; inflation risk understated; credibility
  effects on bond markets understated
  Japan: evidence for MMT? (Debt 260% of GDP; no inflation...until 2022)
  2022: inflation arrived in Japan; complicated the MMT story
<!-- @editor[content/P3]: MMT section is thin — Mosler-Wray-Kelton framework deserves a more structured treatment: the accounting identity argument vs the behavioral/institutional constraints argument; currently reads as "some people say X, others say not-X" -->
```

## Deglobalization Pressures

```
DEGLOBALIZATION TREND
======================

TRADE AS % OF WORLD GDP:
  1990: 39%
  2008: 61% (peak)
  2016: 56%
  2021: 58% (post-COVID recovery)

  The hyperglobalization wave (1990-2008) partially reversed

CAUSES OF PRESSURE:

1. POLITICAL BACKLASH (POPULIST REVOLT):
   "China shock" manufacturing job losses
   Wage stagnation for non-college workers in rich countries
   Immigration pressures in Europe
   2016: Trump (US) + Brexit (UK) + rise of nationalist parties (Europe)
   Result: tariffs, trade restrictions, skepticism of free trade agreements

2. COVID-19 SUPPLY CHAIN CRISIS (2020-2022):
   "Just-in-time" supply chains optimized for efficiency; not resilience
   Single-source dependencies (China for PPE; Taiwan for chips)
   Exposed vulnerability of global value chains to concentrated risk
   Response: reshoring, "friend-shoring" (onshore to allied countries),
   dual-sourcing, strategic stockpiles

3. US-CHINA DECOUPLING (2018-present):
   Trump tariffs (2018): 25% on $250B of Chinese goods
   Huawei restrictions: US banned selling chips to Huawei
   TSMC restricted from selling advanced chips to China
   Export controls on semiconductor manufacturing equipment (2022)
   China response: accelerating domestic semiconductor industry
   Strategic industries: AI, quantum, biotech -- both sides investing
   nationally; not relying on trade
   Decoupling is not total: trade continues; but strategic sectors separating

4. INDUSTRIAL POLICY RETURN:
   CHIPS AND SCIENCE ACT (2022): $52B for US semiconductor manufacturing
   Intel, TSMC, Samsung building US fabs
   INFLATION REDUCTION ACT (2022): $369B for clean energy
   EV credits require North American content; supply chain reshoring
   European Union: Net Zero Industry Act; European Chips Act
   Japan: $9B semiconductor subsidies (TSMC fab in Kumamoto)
   = Washington Consensus officially abandoned; industrial policy back

INDUSTRIAL POLICY RATIONALE (new consensus):
  National security: cannot depend on geopolitical rivals for critical tech
  Market failure: private markets underinvest in externalities
  (clean energy, basic research, first-mover semiconductor fabs)
  Strategic trade theory (Krugman, Brander-Spencer): in industries with
  increasing returns and oligopoly, first-mover subsidies can be optimal
  "Infant industry" argument resurrected for semiconductors, EV batteries
```

## The Return of Inflation (2021-2023)

```
INFLATION SURGE AND POLICY RESPONSE
=====================================

CAUSES (multifactorial):
  1. Demand stimulus: COVID relief ($5T US); pent-up demand
  2. Supply disruption: factories shut; shipping bottlenecks; chip shortage
  3. Energy: Russia-Ukraine war (February 2022); natural gas spike;
     oil spike
  4. Labor market: "Great Resignation"; workers demanding wage increases
  5. Housing: low interest rates + remote work → housing price surge
  6. Some monetary (demand side): M2 grew rapidly; Fed was slow to act

US CPI:
  2020: 1.2%
  2021: 7.0% (December)
  2022: 9.1% (June peak; highest since 1981)
  2023: 3.4% (falling)

FED RESPONSE:
  March 2022: first rate hike (0.25%); initially hesitant
  2022 rate hikes: 4.25% total in 9 months (fastest pace since Volcker era)
  Federal Funds Rate: 0.25% (early 2022) to 5.25-5.50% (late 2023)

SOFT LANDING VS HARD LANDING DEBATE:
  Hard landing prediction: rate increases would cause recession
  (historical record: Fed rate increases → recession within 18 months;
  almost universal historical pattern)
  Soft landing result (as of 2024): inflation fell toward 3%;
  unemployment remained low (~3.7%); no recession
  Why? Labor market structural changes; pent-up demand buffer;
  supply chain resolution; fiscal support
  This was historically unusual; whether it continues is uncertain

COMPARISON TO 1970s STAGFLATION:
  Similarities: supply shock (COVID disruption, Ukraine/energy)
                policy slow to respond initially
  Differences: Fed acted within 2 years (Volcker: years of delay)
               Inflationary expectations better anchored (Fed credibility
               from Volcker era still partly intact)
               Wage-price spiral did not fully materialize
               (Unions much weaker than 1970s; 10% vs 30% union density)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**The Washington Consensus was not just "free markets everywhere."** Williamson's 10 points included public spending on education and health, property rights expansion, and competitive exchange rates — not just privatization. The "neoliberal" interpretation (pure market fundamentalism) was a more extreme version applied in practice.

**China's 2001 WTO accession was not a simple "China gets access" story.** China made enormous commitments: reduce tariffs from ~40% to ~9% average; open financial services; reduce agricultural subsidies; allow foreign investment in many sectors. The "China shock" was about the speed and scale of manufacturing export growth, not about China "cheating" on WTO rules (though enforcement was weak on some commitments).

**QE did not "print money" in the conventional sense.** QE created bank reserves (liability of the Fed), not currency in circulation. Reserves don't circulate in the economy. QE increased bank liquidity (which banks held at the Fed, earning interest on excess reserves). The inflation concern was about what banks MIGHT do with those reserves; largely they didn't lend them aggressively.

**The 2008 crisis was not caused by Fannie and Freddie.** The GSEs' exposure to subprime was substantial but not the origin. The origin was in private-label securitization (non-GSE MBS issued by investment banks) where the incentive problems were worst. The GSE exposure grew as they chased market share in the last years of the bubble.

**Deglobalization is not the same as zero trade.** Trade remains near historical highs. "Deglobalization" means: (1) slowing growth of trade/GDP ratio; (2) selective reshoring of strategic sectors; (3) regional rather than global supply chains in some industries; (4) political friction on trade agreements. Global trade in goods will not return to 1960 levels.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Washington Consensus author and date? | John Williamson, 1989; 10 policy recommendations for Latin America |
| Main Stiglitz critique of IMF? | Conditionality served creditor interests; austerity in recession is contractionary; sequencing wrong (capital liberalization before regulation) |
| China shock finding (Autor et al.)? | Chinese imports eliminated ~2.4M US manufacturing jobs 1999-2011; workers did NOT reabsorb as trade theory predicted |
| Why did MBS/CDO ratings fail? | Gaussian copula model assumed low default correlation; in housing bust, all mortgages correlated; diversification failed |
| What is a CDS? | Credit default swap: insurance-like contract on credit risk; AIG sold $400B+; not regulated as insurance; no reserve requirements |
| Why was Lehman's failure so catastrophic? | Counterparties froze; money market funds broke the buck; commercial paper market seized; interbank market froze simultaneously |
| QE mechanism? | Fed buys long-duration assets (Treasuries, MBS); injects reserves into banking system; lowers long-term yields; wealth effect |
| Triffin dilemma in SDR terms? | SDRs could theoretically replace dollar as reserve asset without requiring any country to run deficits; but were never made large enough |
| What reversed the Washington Consensus? | CHIPS Act ($52B, 2022) and Inflation Reduction Act ($369B, 2022) = explicit industrial policy; US acknowledged market failure in strategic industries |
| Why did 2022 inflation not become 1970s stagflation? | Fed acted faster; unions much weaker (10% vs 30%); inflationary expectations better anchored; wage-price spiral limited |
