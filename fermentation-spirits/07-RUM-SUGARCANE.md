## Sugarcane: The Raw Material

### Origin and Spread

```
SACCHARUM OFFICINARUM

Origin: New Guinea (~8000 BCE, earliest cultivation)
  -> South Asia (India) by 400 BCE ("reeds that give honey without bees")
  -> Arab expansion carries cane to Persia, then Mediterranean (700-1000 CE)
  -> Crusaders encounter sugar in Holy Land -> European demand
  -> Portuguese in Madeira and Sao Tome (1400s)
  -> Columbus carries cane to Hispaniola on second voyage (1493)
  -> Caribbean: ideal climate, slave labor -> sugar empire

C4 PHOTOSYNTHESIS:
  Sugarcane is a C4 plant (like corn, sorghum)
  More efficient carbon fixation than C3 plants at high temperatures
  -> Produces large biomass in tropical climates
  Sucrose accumulated in stalk pith: 10-15% by weight in mature cane
```

### Sugar Production Chain

```
CANE -> SUGAR -> MOLASSES -> RUM

Harvest (cane cut)
  |
  | MILLING: roller mills crush stalks -> extract juice (vesou)
  | ~95-97% juice extraction
  v
RAW CANE JUICE (vesou): sucrose + glucose + fructose + water + organic acids
  |
  | CLARIFICATION: lime added -> impurities precipitate -> filtered
  | EVAPORATION: multiple-effect evaporators concentrate juice
  v
SYRUP (~70-75 Brix)
  |
  | VACUUM CRYSTALLIZATION: controlled crystallization of sucrose
  v
MASSECUITE (sugar crystal + mother liquor mixture)
  |
  | CENTRIFUGE: separate crystals from liquor
  v
RAW SUGAR (sucrose crystals, 96-99% pure)
        +
MOLASSES (mother liquor = uncrystallizable residue)

MOLASSES GRADES:
  First molasses: after first centrifuge; ~50-60% sugars; lighter
  Second molasses: after second crystallization; ~35-45% sugars
  Blackstrap/final molasses: after third; ~25-30% sugars; high minerals, bitter
  Rum-grade molasses: typically second molasses or blend
  Blackstrap: too bitter for quality rum; animal feed / industrial ethanol
```

## Industrial Rum Fermentation

```
RUM FERMENTATION VARIABLES

SUBSTRATE:
  Molasses (industrial rum) or cane juice (agricole/cachaca)
  Diluted with water to ~15-20 Brix for fermentation

YEAST:
  Industrial: Saccharomyces cerevisiae pure culture
  Traditional/heavy: Schizosaccharomyces pombe (Jamaican tradition)
    -> Fission yeast; very different from S. cerevisiae
    -> Produces very high esters and unconventional congeners
  Wild/open: ambient yeasts + bacteria (clairin, some agricole)

DUNDER (Jamaican tradition):
  Still bottom residue from previous distillation runs
  Highly acidic (pH 3-4), mineral-rich, fermentation-active compounds
  Added back to fermenting wash: 5-25% of total volume
  Function: lowers pH (controls unwanted bacteria), contributes to ester development,
            adds "back flavor" compounds
  Essential for traditional heavy Jamaican rum production

MUCK PITS (Jamaican tradition):
  Large pits of decomposing organic matter (animal bones, fruit waste, stillage)
  Allowed to sit for years, developing complex microbial communities
  Small quantities added to ferment -> extraordinary ester levels
  Distilleries with muck pits: Hampden, Worthy Park, Long Pond
  Secret: the specific microbial flora of each pit is a distillery's genetic resource

ESTER FORMATION:
  Esters = organic acid + alcohol -> ester + water (Fischer esterification)
  Yeast-produced: ester synthase (Atf1, Atf2) + alcohols + organic acids
  -> Ethyl acetate (solvent, overpower), isoamyl acetate (banana), ethyl butyrate (pineapple)
  Temperature: lower fermentation temp -> more esters (like beer)
  Fermentation time: longer fermentation (5-20 days) -> more ester development
```

## Ester Classification in Rum

```
ESTER LEVELS (grams per hectoliter of pure alcohol = g/hlpa)

LIGHT RUM:              < 100 g/hlpa
  Bacardi Silver, Havana Club 3yr
  Column distilled, short fermentation
  Clean, neutral, cocktail-friendly

MEDIUM ESTER:           100-300 g/hlpa
  Barbados (Cockspur, Mount Gay), Trinidad (Angostura)
  Pot or multi-column; moderate fermentation
  Balanced, fruity, versatile

HIGH ESTER:             300-700 g/hlpa
  "Continental flavor" (older Jamaican classification term)
  Worthy Park, Hampden High Ester
  Rich, complex, fruity

HEAVY/TRADITIONAL:      700-1,600+ g/hlpa
  "Wedderburn" and "Plummer" (old Jamaican trade names for ester levels)
  Hampden TECA, LROK, Long Pond TPDT
  Intense, funky, barnyard, overripe tropical fruit
  Used for blending (adds character to neutral spirits)
  Single-bottle consumption: very different experience -- not for beginners

VERY HIGH:              1,600-4,000+ g/hlpa
  "German trading rums" (Batavia Arrack, historical)
  Extremely pungent; diluted extensively for blending
  WEDDERBURN was a high-ester style defined in Jamaica by pre-1900 trade categories
```

## Rhum Agricole

```
RHUM AGRICOLE vs INDUSTRIAL RUM

+-----------------------------------------------------------+
|              RHUM AGRICOLE        INDUSTRIAL RUM          |
|              (Fresh cane juice)   (Molasses)              |
|              ---------------      -------------------     |
| Base         Fresh cane vesou     Blackstrap molasses     |
| Sugar        Naturally present    Residual from refining  |
| Sugar type   Sucrose/glucose      Sucrose + minerals      |
| Ferment time 24-36 hrs (fast)     3-7 days typical        |
| Yeast        Often wild/ambient   Pure S. cerevisiae      |
| Still        Copper column or pot Column (mostly)         |
| ABV out      65-75% (agricole)    70-96%                  |
| Character    Grassy, vegetal,      Molasses sweetness,    |
|              fresh cane, earthy   caramel, dried fruit    |
|              mineral              more neutral            |
| Examples     Clement, Neisson,    Bacardi, Appleton,      |
|              Depaz, Saint James   Mount Gay (partly)      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------+

AOC MARTINIQUE (the benchmark agricole AOC):
  Geographic: Martinique island only
  Grapes: Specific sugarcane varieties (Canne Bleue most prized)
  Processing: Must use fresh cane juice, crushed within 24 hrs of cutting
  Fermentation: 24-30 hrs with selected yeasts (some producers use wild)
  Distillation: Single-column still, limited to 65-75% ABV
  Aging: Blanc (unaged), Elevé sous Bois (3 months+), VSOP (3yr+), XO (6yr+)
  Vintage dating permitted

CACHACA (Brazil):
  Made from fresh cane juice; by law: Brazil only
  "Cachaca" name protected as Brazilian GI
  "Brazilian rum" is NOT allowed on label (different regulation)
  "Agave rum" also not accurate (agave is unrelated)
  Must be 38-48% ABV
  Distilled in copper pot still or column
  Unaged (prata/silver) or aged in various Brazilian woods:
    Amburana (coumarin, vanilla, medicinal)
    Balsam (sweet, medicinal)
    Oak (international standard)
  Caipirinha: Cachaca + lime + sugar = Brazil's national cocktail
```

## Colonial Trade Triangle and Rum History

```
THE TRIANGLE TRADE (17th-18th century)

EUROPE ---------> AFRICA
  Ships: manufactured goods,      Ships: enslaved people
  firearms, textiles, metals       (Middle Passage)
       |                              |
       v                              v
  AMERICAS <---------- CARIBBEAN
       |                Ships: sugar, molasses, rum,
       |                tobacco, cotton
       |
  Ships to Europe: sugar, rum, tobacco (from colonies)

NEW ENGLAND RUM INDUSTRY:
  Caribbean molasses shipped to New England (especially Boston/Rhode Island)
  -> New England distilleries: 60+ in Massachusetts alone by 1750
  -> Rum exported to West Africa to purchase enslaved people
  -> Enslaved people to Caribbean to produce sugar/molasses
  -> Molasses to New England
  -> Circular dependency of the Atlantic slave economy

ROYAL NAVY RUM RATION:
  1655: Vice Admiral Penn captures Jamaica -> rum issues begin
  1687: First official naval rum ration (1 pint rum = replacing brandy)
  1740: Admiral Vernon orders rum diluted (2:1 water) -> "grog" (from "grogram" cloak)
  1756: Ration reduced to 1/2 pint rum daily (one gill twice daily)
  1824: Further reduced to 1/4 pint
  1970: BLACK TOT DAY (July 31) -- last rum ration issued Royal Navy
        Sailors wore black armbands; held "funerals" for rum tots

BACARDI AND THE CUBAN REVOLUTION:
  Facundo Bacardi Massó founded Bacardi in Santiago de Cuba 1862
  First rum to use charcoal filtration for light style (Lincoln County-like)
  First rum marketed as "mixable" with Coca-Cola ("Cuba Libre")
  1959: Fidel Castro nationalizes all industry -> Bacardi flees to Puerto Rico/Bahamas
  Cuban government keeps "Havana Club" brand name; Bacardi fights for it in US courts
  Current: Havana Club sold everywhere except USA; Bacardi sells a different "Havana Club" in US
```

## Aging Rum: Tropical Maturation

```
TROPICAL AGING vs SCOTCH AGING

Scotland:
  Temperature: 4-15 degrees C year-round
  Seasonal swing: ~10 degrees C
  Angel's share: ~2%/year
  10-year Scotch: ~82% remains
  Result: slow, patient maturation; subtle barrel interaction

Caribbean/Tropical:
  Temperature: 25-35 degrees C year-round
  Seasonal swing: ~5 degrees C (but diurnal swings may be larger)
  Angel's share: 8-10%/year (some islands report higher)
  "One year tropical = ~3-4 years Scotch" (approximate, contested)
  Result: rapid wood extraction; aggressive flavor development

IMPLICATION: a "15-year Caribbean rum" has seen far more barrel interaction
  than a "15-year Scotch" -- much smaller volume remaining

SOLERA SYSTEM (used by some rum producers, also sherry):
  Fractional blending across vintages
  Barrels arranged in rows; oldest on bottom (solera = floor level)
  When drawing off, pull fraction from oldest -> refill from next older -> etc.
  Result: perpetual blending; no single vintage; traces of original batch remain
  Diplomatico Reserva Exclusiva, Zacapa (partially solera), Flor de Cana

AGE STATEMENT DEBATE:
  Unlike Scotch (youngest must be stated), rum has NO international standard
  "15 Year" could mean anything in most jurisdictions
  European rum producers increasingly pushing for geographic indications and standards
  Rum critics (Serge Valentin, Luca Gargano/Velier) advocate for "single mark" transparency
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Rum is just sweet because it comes from sugarcane."**
Dry fermentation of molasses consumes all fermentable sugar. Light rum like Bacardi
is not sweet -- it's neutral and dry. Sweetness in commercial rum often comes from
post-distillation sugar addition (legal in most jurisdictions, rarely disclosed).
Many "premium" rums have 20-30g/L added sugar -- essentially liqueur-level sweetening.

**"Rhum agricole tastes like grass because it's inferior."**
The grassy/vegetal character is the terroir expression of fresh cane juice.
AOC Martinique agricole represents the pinnacle of rum craftsmanship -- it is not
inferior, just different in character from molasses-based rum.
Think: fresh fruit vs dried fruit -- neither is "better."

**"Jamaican rum is overproofed and crude."**
Jamaican overproof (Wray & Nephew 63%) is the local culture. Aged Jamaican pot still
rum (Hampden 8yr, Appleton Estate 12yr, Worthy Park 2015 vintage) is among the most
complex spirits in the world. The stereotype conflates raw overproof with the aged portfolio.

**"Bacardi originated the light rum style."**
Bacardi popularized light rum commercially. But the Cuban light style pre-existed Bacardi;
Bacardi's innovation was charcoal filtration and consistent production methods (and
eventually, massive marketing to American tourists and military during Prohibition).
