## Pre-Cocktail Era: Punch Culture

### The 17th-18th Century Punch Bowl

```
THE FIVE ELEMENTS OF PUNCH (sack = "PLAT"):
  P - Potent (spirit: rum, brandy, arrack)
  L - Lemon (acid: citrus juice)
  A - Ambrosial (sugar: loaf sugar or syrup)
  T - Tea (spice: nutmeg, allspice, or tea)
  (Water: fifth element)

HISTORICAL PUNCH FORMULA:
  "One of sour, two of sweet, three of strong, four of weak"
  1 part acid (lime/lemon) : 2 parts sugar : 3 parts spirit : 4 parts water

PUNCH CONTEXT:
  Colonial taverns: large communal bowl, served in cups
  Social drinking before individualized cocktails
  Ships: arrack punch (Batavia Arrack from Java -- precursor to modern cocktails)
  Officers' mess: punch bowl as status symbol
  Ladle served to guests from communal vessel
  First punch: India (likely 1630s British East India Company)
    -> From Hindi "panch" = five (elements theory)
    -> Or from Sanskrit "pañca" = five

PRE-PROHIBITION PUNCH BOOKS:
  William Terrington "Cooling Cups and Dainty Drinks" (1869)
  Jerry Thomas "Bartenders Guide" (1862) -- included punch section
```

## The First Definition of "Cocktail"

```
FIRST PRINT APPEARANCE (1806):

  "Balance and Columbian Repository"
  Hudson, New York, May 13, 1806

  "Cock tail, then is a stimulating liquor,
   composed of spirits of any kind,
   sugar, water, and bitters—
   it is vulgarly called a bittered sling."

ELEMENTS:
  Spirits (any)
  Sugar
  Water (dilution)
  Bitters (the defining ingredient that distinguishes cocktail from sling)

The "Old Fashioned" is the closest surviving heir to this original definition:
  Whiskey + sugar + water + bitters = cocktail in its purest form
  The name "Old Fashioned" itself came into use ~1880s when drinkers
  ordered a cocktail "the old-fashioned way" before it got too complicated.
```

## The Golden Age: 1860-1919

### Jerry Thomas and the Birth of the Profession

```
PROFESSOR JERRY THOMAS (1825-1885)

  First celebrity bartender; showman; innovator
  "The Bartender's Guide / How to Mix Drinks" (1862)
  -> First American cocktail book
  -> 236 recipes including punches, sours, cobblers, sangarees, flips, toddies

  SIGNATURE CREATION: THE BLUE BLAZER
    Scotch whisky + boiling water + sugar
    Poured back and forth between two large silver mugs while on fire
    Theatrical, dangerous, pure showmanship
    Performed only in cold weather ("to warm the inner man")

  THE MARTINEZ (prototype of the Martini):
    Old Tom gin + sweet vermouth + maraschino + orange bitters
    -> Evolved into the Martini as vermouth ratios changed over decades

  BASE CAMPS: Planters House (St. Louis), Metropolitan Hotel (NYC)
              Mark Hopkins Hotel (San Francisco)
```

### Ice: The Enabling Technology

```
THE ICE INDUSTRY (19th century)

FREDERIC TUDOR (1783-1864): "Ice King of the World"
  1806: First commercial ice shipment (Boston -> Martinique)
  -> Ice cut from New England ponds in winter, shipped globally
  -> By 1850s: ice trade to India, Caribbean, South America
  -> Created cold storage industry, changed food preservation globally

WHY ICE TRANSFORMED COCKTAILS:
  Cold dilution: crushed ice in a shaken drink -> precise dilution
  Temperature: cold suppresses harsh flavors, brightens aromatics
  Texture: cracked vs cubed vs crushed vs block -- different dilution rates
  Cobbler: first cocktail style requiring ice in the glass
  Julep: ice + spirit + mint = ice-dependent iconic form

LINDE REFRIGERATION (1876): Carl von Linde's ammonia compression refrigerator
  -> Industrial ice production anywhere, anytime
  -> By 1900s: ice no longer scarce or exotic

ICE REVIVAL (modern):
  Japanese ice cutting tradition (Suntory influence)
  Sawadori: large hand-cut block ice for highballs
  Craft cocktail bars: large format ice for clarity + slow dilution
  Kold Draft machine: standardized 1.25" cubes
```

### The American Classic Cocktails

```
THE CANONICAL AMERICAN CLASSICS (pre-Prohibition)

OLD FASHIONED (~1880s, Louisville)
  Rye or bourbon + sugar cube + Angostura bitters + orange peel
  The "cocktail" in pure form; spirit-forward
  Pendennis Club (Louisville): likely origin story

MANHATTAN (1870s, NYC)
  Rye or bourbon + sweet vermouth + Angostura bitters + cherry
  "Manhattan Club" origin story (probably apocryphal -- Lady Randolph Churchill)
  Perfect Manhattan: equal parts sweet and dry vermouth

MARTINI (evolved from Jerry Thomas's Martinez, 1880s-1920s)
  Gin + dry vermouth; ratio evolved toward drier over decades
  1890s: 1:1 gin to vermouth
  1920s: 3:1 or 4:1
  1950s-60s Churchill: "glance at the vermouth bottle" era
  Modern revival: wetter ratios (5:1 to 3:1) with quality vermouth

SAZERAC (New Orleans, 1850s-1880s)
  Rye whiskey (originally cognac pre-phylloxera) + Peychaud's bitters
  + absinthe (or Herbsaint) rinse + lemon peel (expressed, discarded)
  Peychaud's: Antoine Peychaud, New Orleans apothecary, 1830s
  Considered the "first American cocktail" by New Orleans tradition

DAIQUIRI (Cuba, attributed to American engineer Jennings Cox, 1898)
  Rum + lime juice + sugar = the foundational sour form
  Invented in Daiquiri iron mines, Cuba
  Ernest Hemingway frequented El Floridita (Havana) for frozen daiquiris
```

## Prohibition (1920-1933)

### The 18th Amendment

```
TIMELINE:
  1917: 65th Congress passes resolution
  1919: 18th Amendment ratified (36 states)
  January 17, 1920: Volstead Act takes effect
  -> Manufacture, sale, transport of "intoxicating liquors" (>.5% ABV) prohibited
  -> Not possession or consumption (loophole exploited by wealthy with home stocks)

  1933: 21st Amendment repeals 18th (only amendment to repeal another)
  -> December 5, 1933: Prohibition ends
  -> New regulatory frameworks established (TTB, state ABC systems)

EFFECTS ON DRINKING CULTURE:

  Speakeasies:
    Estimated 30,000 speakeasies in NYC alone (vs 15,000 legal bars pre-Prohibition)
    Elbow Room, El Fay, 21 Club (NYC), Green Mill (Chicago)
    Password culture, hidden bars, bribed police

  Quality degradation:
    Legal spirits confiscated; quality production ceased
    Bathtub gin: neutral industrial alcohol + juniper oil + flavoring
    Adulterated whiskey: industrial alcohol with added caramel and flavoring
    Real danger: methanol-adulterated products (government-mandated industrial alcohol
    was sometimes diverted -- government actually ordered industrial denaturants added
    to discourage drinking; some estimates: 10,000+ deaths from poisoned alcohol)

  Caribbean boom:
    Americans travel to Cuba, Bermuda, Bahamas for legal drinking
    Havana: golden age of cocktails; Cuban bartenders elevated craft
    Harry's Bar (Paris), Savoy American Bar (London): receive American barmen emigrants
    Harry Craddock at Savoy: "The Savoy Cocktail Book" (1930)

  Gangsterism:
    Al Capone Chicago, Lucky Luciano NYC
    Bootlegging = organized crime's financial foundation
    Tommy gun violence over territory
```

### Post-Prohibition Hangover

```
REPEAL AND THE DAMAGE:

  Palate damage: 13 years of bathtub gin and harsh spirits
  -> Taste shifted toward sweet, mixer-heavy drinks
  -> Ginger ale + rye; Tom Collins; Harvey Wallbanger
  -> Quality base spirits took years to rebuild (aged spirits need TIME)

  Standardization:
    Big producers consolidated (Seagram's, Brown-Forman, National Distillers)
    -> Industrial consistency over craft character
    -> Scotch became prestige default (American spirits seen as inferior post-Prohibition)

  Bar culture damaged:
    Master bartenders gone (emigrated or retired)
    Recipes lost or simplified
    Training culture disappeared
```

## Tiki: Escapism in a Highball Glass

```
TIKI CULTURE (1934-1970s)

DON THE BEACHCOMBER (Donn Beach, born Ernest Gantt):
  First tiki bar: "Don's Beachcomber Cafe," Hollywood, 1934
  Polynesian decor + rum-based punches + mystery recipes
  Drinks kept secret (mixed in batches; bartenders didn't know full recipes)
  ZOMBIE (1934): Created for Don's; rumored 2-per-customer limit
    3 rums + absinthe + grenadine + lime + grapefruit + falernum + cinnamon syrup
    Original recipe secret; Jeff Berry reconstructed from Don's notes (1990s)

TRADER VIC (Victor Bergeron):
  Trader Vic's restaurant chain (Oakland, 1934-present)
  MAI TAI (1944):
    "Maita'i" = Tahitian for "out of this world, good"
    Original: 17-year Jamaican rum + orange curacao + orgeat + lime
    Victor served it to Tahitian friends -- allegedly they said "Mai tai roa ae"
    Modern versions often debased with orange juice and multiple rums
  ZOMBIE adapted by Vic; competed with Don the Beachcomber

TIKI ELEMENTS:
  Rum (usually multiple): molasses-forward tropical association
  Citrus (lime dominant)
  Orgeat (almond-orange flower syrup)
  Falernum (clove, almond, lime, ginger syrup -- Barbados origin)
  Crushed ice + elaborate garnish (orchid, umbrella, swizzle stick, shark fin)
  Communal vessels (Scorpion Bowl for 2-4)

CULTURAL CONTEXT:
  Post-WW2 Pacific fascination; soldiers returned from Pacific theater
  "Polynesian" was a fantasy construction (mixed Pacific cultures inaccurately)
  Escapism: suburban conformity + tropical fantasy = tiki bar
  Decline: Vietnam War made "tropical" politically complicated; disco era followed

REVIVAL:
  Jeff "Beachbum" Berry: tiki archaeologist; recovered original recipes from 1990s
  Smuggler's Cove (SF), Three Dots and a Dash (Chicago): serious neo-tiki
  Garret Richard, Martin Cate: modern tiki elevated to serious cocktail context
```

## The Craft Cocktail Revival

### The Seeds: Dale DeGroff

```
DALE DeGROFF at RAINBOW ROOM (1987-1999)

  Warner LeRoy renovates Rainbow Room atop 30 Rock, 1987
  Hires Dale DeGroff to create authentic pre-Prohibition cocktail program
  DeGroff researches original recipes (Charles Baker, Jerry Thomas, David Embury)
  Reintroduces fresh citrus (replacing bottled sour mix)
  Reintroduces quality vermouth, bitters, liqueurs
  Trains first generation of modern American craft bartenders

  Why fresh citrus matters:
    Bottled sour mix: artificial citric acid + corn syrup + preservatives
    Fresh lime/lemon: volatile aromatics, true acid balance, no artificial aftertaste
    Daiquiri with fresh lime vs sour mix: completely different drink
    This one change was transformative for the whole category
```

### The New York Vanguard

```
FOUNDING ESTABLISHMENTS

MILK & HONEY (Sasha Petraske, 2000, NYC -- closed 2013, reborn as various)
  Speakeasy-style (no sign, phone reservation required)
  House rules: no name-dropping, no star-gazing, keep your voice down
  Rigorous technique: Kold Draft ice, fresh juice, Japanese whisky, obscure spirits
  Trained: Sam Ross (Paper Plane, Penicillin), Joseph Schwartz, others
  Legacy: every "serious" cocktail bar traces DNA here

PEGU CLUB (Audrey Saunders, 2005-2020, NYC)
  Open bar (no reservation) but equally rigorous quality
  Systematic training of staff (the "Pegu Club School")
  Research-driven: Audrey developed recipes through systematic testing

DEATH & CO (2007, NYC - still operating)
  Open until 4am; no speakeasy mystique but highest technique
  Systematic bar program; detailed cocktail book "Death & Co" (2014)
  Seasonal menus, creative originals alongside classics

PDT (Please Don't Tell, Brian Kreiger, 2007, NYC)
  Through a phone booth in a hot dog shop (Crif Dogs)
  Reservation only; 30-person capacity
  Jim Meehan: one of the premier systematizers of cocktail craft
```

### Molecular Mixology and Technique

```
DAVE ARNOLD and LIQUID INTELLIGENCE (2014)

  Head of culinary technology at French Culinary Institute
  Founder of Booker and Dax bar (NYC, closed)
  Book "Liquid Intelligence" (2014): the definitive technical cocktail text

  TECHNIQUES:
  Fat-washing:
    Infuse fat (brown butter, bacon fat, olive oil) into spirit at room temp
    Freeze: fat solidifies and is skimmed off
    -> Fat-soluble flavor compounds remain in spirit
    Brown butter old fashioned: bourbon + brown butter = nutty, rich

  Centrifuge clarification:
    Turbid liquid (cloudy citrus, pea foam) spun at high speed
    -> Solids separate -> crystal-clear liquid with flavor intact
    Clear cloudy lime juice, clarified green juice cocktails

  Rapid infusion (iSi whipper):
    Cream whipper pressurized with N2O
    Rapid pressure + depressure forces liquid into ingredients
    -> 2-minute infusion = hours of cold infusion
    Fat-washed spirits, herb infusions

  Rotary evaporation (Rotovap):
    Vacuum distillation at low temperature
    -> Distill aromatic compounds without heat degradation
    -> Transparent "gin and tonic distillate" retaining aromatics
    Used by Booker and Dax, Aviary (Chicago), Fat Duck
```

## Bitters: The Renaissance

```
PRE-CRAFT: Two bitter options
  ANGOSTURA (1824): Dr. Siegert, Venezuela/Trinidad
    Most ubiquitous; rhubarb-like bitterness; aromatic spice
  PEYCHAUD'S (1830s): New Orleans; gentian + anise
    Required for Sazerac; lighter, anise-forward

POST-2000 EXPLOSION:
  Fee Brothers (Rochester): expanded from 2-3 flavors to 20+
  Regans' Orange Bitters (1999): Gary Regan, orange peel forward
  Bittermens, Scrappy's, The Bitter Truth (Germany), Dr. Adam Elmegirab's
  -> By 2015: 100+ commercial bitters available
  -> Hundreds of house-made bitters at serious bars

FUNCTION OF BITTERS:
  Gentian root + alcohol extraction + flavorings
  Aromatic bitters: add complexity without dominant single flavor
  Digestive: originally medicinal (Angostura sold as anti-malarial)
  Balancing: bridge between sweet and spirit components
  "A cocktail without bitters is like food without salt" -- Dale DeGroff

DASHES AND MEASUREMENT:
  A "dash" = approximately 0.6-1.0 mL from a dasher-top bottle
  Bitters at 45-50%+ ABV: aromatic concentration makes tiny amounts impactful
```

## Cocktail Taxonomy

```
THE FUNDAMENTAL TEMPLATES

SOUR (most cocktails are sours):
  Spirit + Citrus (acid) + Sweetener
  Ratio: 2:0.75:0.75 or 2:1:0.75 depending on spirit and style
  Examples: Daiquiri, Margarita, Sidecar, Whiskey Sour, Gimlet, Cosmopolitan
  Variations:
    Add egg white -> Fizz
    Add sparkling water + egg white -> Collins
    Add egg white, dry-shake first -> Sour (specific style)

SPIRIT + MODIFIER + BITTERS (spirit-forward, stirred):
  Spirit + Aromatized wine or liqueur + Bitters
  Diluted by stirring with ice; spirit-forward
  Examples: Martini, Manhattan, Negroni, Rob Roy, Vieux Carre
  Negroni: gin + sweet vermouth + Campari (equal parts) = the modern template

HIGHBALL:
  Spirit + Carbonated mixer
  Dilution provided by ice + mixer
  Examples: Scotch + soda, G&T, Bourbon + ginger ale
  Japanese highball: Suntory Toki + sparkling water + large clear ice

OLD FASHIONED (pure cocktail form):
  Spirit + Sugar + Bitters
  Diluted by stirring; no citrus
  Template: any spirit + any sweetener + any bitters

FLIP:
  Spirit + Sugar + Whole Egg
  Shaken vigorously; rich, creamy
  Eggnog is a flip-based drink

JULEP:
  Spirit + Sugar + Mint + Crushed Ice
  Mint Julep (bourbon), Mojito (rum + mint + lime = Julian sour hybrid)
```

## Low-ABV and Non-Alcoholic Movements

```
APERITIVO CULTURE (Italian origin):
  Pre-dinner bitter aperitivo: Campari, Aperol, Select, Cynar
  Spritz: Prosecco + Aperol/Campari + soda + orange slice
  ABV: 8-12% (Aperol: 11%; Campari: 20-25%)
  Milan and Veneto tradition -> global export via Instagram
  "Aperol Spritz" went from regional Italian tradition to global phenomenon 2010s

N/A SPIRITS (non-alcoholic):
  Seedlip (Ben Branson, 2015): first modern premium N/A spirit
    Spiced 94, Garden 108, Grove 42
    Herbal distillates; not technically spirits (no ethanol)
  Lyre's: range of N/A spirit analogs (bourbon, rum, gin, absinthe)
  Athletic Brewing: N/A beer (first serious quality N/A beer brand in US)
  Aecorn, Monday: additional N/A spirits brands

WHY THE TREND:
  Health consciousness: Gen Z drinks less than previous generations
  Sober curious movement: Dry January, mindful drinking
  Designated drivers: safety culture improvement
  Pregnancy, medication, personal choice: broader social acceptance of N/A
  Problem: most N/A "cocktails" taste medicinal or tea-like; still a major quality gap

COMPETITIVE BARTENDING:
  USBG (United States Bartenders Guild): professional organization
  Tales of the Cocktail: annual symposium (New Orleans)
  World Class (Diageo): largest international bartending competition
  Bartenders Guide competitions (UK: UKBG)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"A Dry Martini has no vermouth."**
A dry Martini has LESS vermouth than a traditional Martini, not none.
Winston Churchill reportedly said he just glanced at the vermouth bottle.
But Martini without vermouth is just chilled gin. The vermouth's aromatics
(wine oxidation, herbal notes) are what elevate it. Contemporary craft bartenders
have swung back toward wetter ratios (3:1 to 4:1 gin:vermouth) with quality vermouth.

**"Shaking bruises the gin."**
No scientific basis. "Bruising" gin is a marketing phrase coined to distinguish
Bond's vodka martini style. The actual effect of shaking vs stirring: shaking
introduces aeration (tiny bubbles = cloudy texture), more dilution, and lower
temperature. Neither is "better" -- they produce different drinks.
Convention: spirit-forward cocktails stirred; cocktails with citrus or egg shaken.

**"Prohibition failed because Americans love to drink."**
Oversimplification. Consumption DID fall significantly during Prohibition
(cirrhosis deaths dropped ~50%). But it failed because: widespread organized crime,
unequal enforcement (wealthy had stored stocks), corruption, and the cultural
association of "freedoms" with the right to drink.

**"Jerry Thomas invented the cocktail."**
He systematized and published the first American cocktail book (1862). He did not
invent cocktails -- the word predates him by 56 years, and many of his recipes
were existing standards he documented. His contribution was professional legitimacy,
codification, and showmanship that elevated bartending from servitude to profession.
