# Utopian Thinkers: Plato, More, Campanella, Bacon

## The Big Picture

```
  THE UTOPIAN TRADITION: WHAT IT ACTUALLY IS
  ============================================

  "Utopia" (Thomas More, 1516): coined from Greek
  "ou-topos" = "no-place" + possible pun on "eu-topos" = "good-place"
  → A good place that is also no place
  → The purpose is NOT to be a blueprint

  THE FUNCTION OF UTOPIAN WRITING:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ CRITIQUE: By describing what a better world would be like,  │
  │ utopian writing implicitly criticizes the existing world.   │
  │ More's Utopia: private property is abolished. Implication:  │
  │ England's enclosures and aristocratic property rights are   │
  │ arbitrary, not natural.                                     │
  │                                                             │
  │ IMAGINATION: Opens possibility space. "Things could be      │
  │ otherwise" — in a world where most people experience the    │
  │ existing order as natural and inevitable.                   │
  │                                                             │
  │ THOUGHT EXPERIMENT: Tests institutional arrangements         │
  │ by imagining them completely. What kind of people would     │
  │ such a society produce? What would the failure modes be?    │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PLATO'S Republic (370 BCE):
  Philosopher-kings govern; guardians enforce; producers produce
  Ruled by reason, not election
  → Critique: democracy produces demagogues

  MORE'S Utopia (1516):
  Common property; planned hours; toleration
  → Critique: English enclosures; aristocratic privilege

  CAMPANELLA'S City of the Sun (1602):
  Theocratic technocracy; eugenics; no private property
  → Critique: Spanish colonial misrule; Counter-Reformation rigidity

  BACON'S New Atlantis (1627):
  Research institution (Solomon's House) produces knowledge
  that improves material life
  → Program: organized scientific research as state function
  → This one got built: Royal Society (1660)
```

## Plato (428–348 BCE) — The Republic

### The Political Vision

The *Republic* is a thought experiment: what kind of city would produce justice? Plato's answer: one governed by those who know what justice is — philosophers.

**The Tripartite City** (mirrors the tripartite soul):

```
  PLATO'S REPUBLIC: STRUCTURE
  =============================

  Class          Function        Soul Faculty
  ─────          ────────        ────────────
  Philosopher-   Govern;         Reason (logos)
  Kings          rule wisely

  Guardians      Enforce;        Spirit/Will (thumos)
  (Warriors)     defend; police

  Producers      Produce food,   Appetite (epithumia)
  (Craftsmen,    goods, trade
  Farmers)

  KEY FEATURES:
  - Guardians and philosopher-kings: no private property;
    no private families (communal wives/children)
    → Removes incentives for corruption and nepotism
  - Education: rigorous; music + gymnastics first;
    mathematics + philosophy for the potential rulers
  - Philosopher-kings: know the Form of the Good;
    can distinguish appearance from reality
  - Democracy = Plato's WORST regime (after tyranny)
    because it gives equal voice to the ignorant

  THE "NOBLE LIE":
  Citizens are to be told a founding myth —
  that they were born from the earth with gold, silver,
  or bronze in their souls determining their class.
  This makes the hierarchy feel natural.
  → Plato explicitly justifies state propaganda for
    social stability.
```

**The Philosopher-King Problem**: Plato's argument depends on the existence of people who genuinely know the Form of the Good and are motivated to govern rather than pursue their own interests. But:
1. How do you identify them without already knowing the Form of the Good?
2. Why would they want to govern (the "cave allegory" implies they must be forced to return)?
3. What prevents them from corrupting once in power?

Plato's own attempt to implement his philosophy — advising Dionysius II of Syracuse three times — failed each time. The philosopher-kings he found were not philosophers; the philosophers he found didn't want to be kings.

**What Made It Through**: The *Republic*'s lasting contribution is not its political prescription but its analytical framework: governance requires wisdom, not just will; there is a difference between apparent good and real good; democracy is vulnerable to demagogues who appeal to appetite rather than reason. These insights structure political philosophy to this day.

## Thomas More (1478–1535) — Utopia (1516)

### The Text

*Utopia* is written in two books:

**Book I**: A dialogue in which More meets the traveler Raphael Hythloday ("purveyor of nonsense" — More deliberately makes the character's reliability ambiguous). Hythloday criticizes English society: enclosures driving peasants from land; capital punishment for theft (which desperate hunger drove people to); the corruption and waste of court life.

**Book II**: Hythloday's description of the island of Utopia:

```
  UTOPIA: THE INSTITUTIONAL DESIGN
  ===================================

  PROPERTY: No private property; goods are common
  LABOR: Everyone works 6 hours/day (more productive
         than England because no idle aristocracy;
         no luxury trades serving idle rich)
  GOVERNANCE: Cities of 6,000 families; villages of 40
              families; senators elected annually;
              Prince elected for life (but removable)
  TOLERATION: All religions permitted except atheism
              (atheists don't trust gods to punish
               cheaters; untrustworthy in contracts)
  SLAVERY: Yes — for criminals and war prisoners
           (this is often glossed over)
  WAR: Preferable to use mercenaries; if own citizens
       fight, women fight alongside men
  GOLD AND SILVER: Used for chamber pots and chains for
                   slaves — to make precious metals
                   contemptible
  PLEASURE: Primary goal is rational pleasure; not
            Puritan denial but rational enjoyment

  THE IRONY QUESTION:
  Hythloday = "purveyor of nonsense" in Greek.
  More includes a letter saying he isn't sure whether
  all the customs are good.
  Is Utopia a blueprint or a provocation?
  Scholarly consensus: both; primarily a critique of
  England's actual problems through contrast.
```

**More's Execution**: More was executed in 1535 by Henry VIII for refusing to recognize the king's supremacy over the church in England. He died for a theological-political principle. The man who wrote about religious toleration died for his Catholic orthodoxy. This is not hypocrisy — Utopia allowed all religions except atheism; More was insisting on the specific claims of his Catholicism in the specific political crisis of the English Reformation.

## Tommaso Campanella (1568–1639) — The City of the Sun

### Context

Campanella was a Dominican friar who spent 27 years in prison (1599–1626) for involvement in a plot to overthrow Spanish rule in Calabria. He wrote *La città del Sole* (The City of the Sun) in prison in 1602 (published 1623).

```
  CITY OF THE SUN: KEY FEATURES
  ================================

  GOVERNANCE: Theocracy under "Hoh" (the priest-king)
              assisted by three princes:
              Power (war/peace/military)
              Wisdom (arts/sciences/education)
              Love (breeding, agriculture, medicine)

  PROPERTY: Common; no private property; no family
  EUGENICS: Mating controlled by the state for optimal offspring
            Magistrates determine who breeds with whom
            → One of the first explicit eugenic proposals

  EDUCATION: Walls of the city covered with images of all
             knowledge; children learn by walking through
             the city reading the walls
             → Visual learning environment; precursor of
               visual encyclopedism and eventually the idea
               of ambient learning environments

  RELIGION: Heliocentric religion (worship of the sun);
            natural religion compatible with reason;
            Christianity = highest form of natural religion
            → This got him in trouble with the Inquisition

  WORK: Manual labor is honorable; 4-hour workdays
        (more productive because everyone works;
        no idle rich)
```

**Campanella's Significance**: Historically minor as a direct influence, but his City of the Sun represents the application of Renaissance humanism and nascent scientific thinking to political design. The eugenic elements are disturbing and prescient of later horrors. The visual encyclopedism influenced Bacon and later educational theory. The theocratic technocracy is a predecessor of Saint-Simon's idea that government should be run by experts.

## Francis Bacon (1561–1626) — New Atlantis and the Inductive Method

### The Methodological Innovation

Bacon is in this directory not primarily as a utopian but as the architect of *organized empirical research as a program for human improvement*. His *Novum Organum* (1620) proposed a new method of inquiry; his *New Atlantis* (1627, unfinished, published posthumously) described the institution that would carry it out.

**The Two Contributions**:

```
  BACON'S PROGRAM
  ==================

  1. THE METHOD (Novum Organum, 1620):
  PROBLEM: Previous knowledge = either:
  - Authority (what Aristotle said)
  - Deductive reasoning from principles
    (produces elaborate systems that may not match nature)

  BACON'S ALTERNATIVE:
  - Systematic observation and experiment
  - Collect many instances
  - Inductive generalization from instances
  - Eliminate hypotheses by finding disconfirming instances

  "Four Idols" that distort thinking:
  Idol of the Tribe:    natural human tendencies to see patterns
  Idol of the Cave:     individual biases and experiences
  Idol of the Market:   imprecision in language
  Idol of the Theatre:  inherited philosophical systems

  → Rigorous method is not natural; must be cultivated
  → Clearing the idols = precondition of knowledge

  2. THE INSTITUTION (New Atlantis, 1627):
  "Solomon's House" (or "The College of Six Days' Works"):
  A research institution on the island of Bensalem.
  36 fellows, organized by function:
  - Merchants of Light (travel world; collect knowledge)
  - Depredators (extract experiments from books)
  - Mystery-Men (conduct experiments in trades)
  - Pioneers (try new experiments)
  - Compilers (reduce experiments to observations)
  - Dowry-men (look for practical applications)
  - Lamps (direct new experiments)
  - Inoculators (execute the experiments)
  - Interpreters of Nature (raise discoveries into axioms)

  THIS IS A RESEARCH ORGANIZATION with explicit division
  of labor between data collection, experiment, theory,
  and application.

  The Royal Society of London (founded 1660, 34 years
  after Bacon's death) is the direct institutional result.
  The modern research university is the successor.
```

**Bacon's Specific Visionary Claim**: Organized, systematic, collaborative research — not the lone genius but teams of people with different functions — can produce knowledge that improves the human condition materially. This is the modern research enterprise. Bacon described it before it existed; it was built (imperfectly) and it works.

**Bacon's Failures**: His *Idol* analysis, while insightful, does not itself provide a complete methodology for science. The specific inductive method he proposed (enumerate instances; eliminate hypotheses) was superseded by Popper's hypothetico-deductive falsificationism. The Royal Society did not follow his institutional blueprint precisely. But the core vision — organized research produces applicable knowledge — was correct.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Plato's Republic is a blueprint for totalitarianism."**
Karl Popper's *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945) makes this case. It's partly right — the noble lie, the class system, the censorship of art are genuinely authoritarian. But Plato was responding to the failure of Athenian democracy that executed Socrates; his critique of democracy has real substance even if his solution is unacceptable. The *Republic* is better read as a rigorous analysis of what governance requires than as a political program.

**"More's Utopia was serious as a political program."**
Most scholars think it was primarily an intellectual exercise and a critique of English society, not a literal proposal. More was a devout Catholic who served Henry VIII as Lord Chancellor; he could not actually have advocated the elimination of private property and the Catholic Church's authority simultaneously.

**"Bacon invented the scientific method."**
He articulated the inductive case for empiricism; the specific hypothetico-deductive method that actually drives science was developed through Galileo, Descartes, Newton, and later Popper. Bacon's contribution was the *program* (research as organized collective enterprise) and the *critique* of existing methods (Aristotelian deduction from received categories). He was right about the problem; his solution was incomplete.
