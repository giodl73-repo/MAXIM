# Early Futurists: Wells, Verne, Bellamy, Huxley

## The Big Picture

```
  FICTION AS PREDICTION: THE RECORD
  ====================================

  Speculative fiction has a documented but mixed record
  as a prediction tool. The successes are famous because
  we remember them; the failures are forgotten.

  JULES VERNE (1828–1905):
  Hit: Submarine (20,000 Leagues, 1870); airplane (Robur
       the Conqueror, 1886); Moon rocket (From Earth to Moon,
       1865 — wrong propulsion; right destination)
  Miss: Earth core is molten (Journey to Center, 1864)
  Method: Extrapolate known technology plausibly

  H.G. WELLS (1866–1946):
  Hit: Tank (The Land Ironclads, 1903 — 11 years before WWI tanks)
       Atomic bomb (The World Set Free, 1914 — named it "atomic bomb";
         described chain reaction; predicted aerial atomic warfare)
       Global state after catastrophic war (Shape of Things to Come)
  Miss: Most sociological predictions; many specific technology details
  Method: Follow scientific thinking to social consequences

  EDWARD BELLAMY (1850–1898):
  Hit: Credit cards ("credit card" named in Looking Backward, 1888)
       Mass media delivery to homes (described cable radio/TV)
       Department stores / catalog shopping
  Miss: Socialist transformation of USA by 2000 (completely wrong)
  Method: Project industrial organization to its logical completion

  ALDOUS HUXLEY (1894–1963):
  Hit: Reproductive technology / IVF / cloning (Brave New World, 1932)
       Psychopharmacology as social control
       Consumerism as social pacifier
       Genetic engineering + social stratification
  Miss: The specific timeline; specific mechanisms
  Method: Satirical projection of current trends to conclusions

  THE COMMON DENOMINATOR:
  They all extrapolated visible trends to imagined completion.
  The ones that "hit" identified real trend vectors.
  The ones that "missed" got the social response wrong.
  Technology was more predictable than social adaptation.
```

## Jules Verne (1828–1905)

### Method: Plausible Extrapolation

Verne was a French novelist who researched his novels extensively from engineering and scientific journals. His method: identify what is currently possible, then push it one or two steps further.

**The Submarine**:

```
  "TWENTY THOUSAND LEAGUES UNDER THE SEA" (1870)
  ================================================

  The Nautilus:
  - Electrically powered (batteries)
  - Air renewal system (chemicals to regenerate oxygen)
  - Steel hull; double hull; compressed-air locks
  - Pressure calculated for depth
  - Periscope-like viewing
  - Self-contained food supply

  WHAT WAS REAL IN 1870:
  - Submarines existed (CSS Hunley, Civil War, 1864)
  - Battery-electric propulsion existed
  - The engineering concepts were scientifically plausible

  WHAT WAS EXTRAPOLATED:
  - Range and depth far beyond anything built
  - Integration of systems into a habitable vessel
  - The Captain Nemo as operator (single brilliant operator)

  REAL SUBMARINE DEVELOPMENT:
  - USS Holland (1900): first practical submarine
  - Battery-electric propulsion: exactly Verne's design
  - The timeline from Verne (1870) to deployment: 30 years
```

**From the Earth to the Moon (1865)**: Verne's Moon rocket is launched from Florida (correct — Cape Canaveral is in Florida; Verne chose Tampa, also Florida); the capsule splashes down in the Pacific (correct); the astronauts are weightless in orbit (correct); the mission has an international crew; the capsule is a metal projectile (wrong — he used a gun, not a rocket; the g-forces of a gun would kill the occupants). The specific mechanism was wrong; the destination, launch site, and recovery method were right.

**What Verne Did Not Predict**: Social consequences of technology. His novels are largely technology demonstrations with adventure plots. He did not engage with questions of who would control these technologies, for what purposes, or what their social effects would be.

## H.G. Wells (1866–1946)

### Method: Follow Science to Social Consequences

Wells studied biology under T.H. Huxley (grandfather of Aldous; Darwin's primary champion). His scientific training shaped his fiction: he extrapolated from actual biology, physics, and chemistry, not from fantasy. But unlike Verne, he was primarily interested in the *social* consequences of technology.

**The Atomic Bomb (The World Set Free, 1914)**:

```
  WELLS'S ATOMIC BOMB (1914)
  ===========================

  "The World Set Free" (1914) — written before WWI began:

  WHAT WELLS DESCRIBED:
  - "Atomic bombs" (he coined the phrase in this novel)
  - Chain reaction: one reaction triggers others
  - Bombs that keep exploding for days ("perpetual bomb")
  - Aerial delivery by aircraft
  - Bombing of cities; millions dead; civilization collapse
  - Followed by global government to prevent future wars

  WHAT WELLS GOT RIGHT:
  - The name (atomic bomb — used by everyone thereafter)
  - Chain reaction principle
  - Aerial delivery
  - City destruction; mass casualties
  - International governance push after use (UN)

  WHAT HE GOT WRONG:
  - "Perpetual" bombs (dirty but not perpetual)
  - Timeline: "1959" for deployment (actual: 1945)
  - The specific chain reaction mechanism (he imagined a
    physical decay chain; actual is neutron chain reaction)

  THE INFLUENCE:
  Leo Szilard read "The World Set Free" in 1932.
  In 1933, he conceived the idea of the nuclear chain reaction
  and immediately thought of Wells's novel.
  He patented the concept and tried to keep it secret
  to prevent weaponization.
  He later helped initiate the Manhattan Project.

  A novelist's prediction → physicist's research program
  → actual weapon. The feedback loop is documented.
```

**The Tank (The Land Ironclads, 1903)**:

In 1903, Wells published "The Land Ironclads" — a story about armored vehicles with caterpillar tracks that cross trenches and defeat infantry. The British Army first used tanks at the Battle of the Somme on September 15, 1916 — 13 years after Wells described them.

Winston Churchill, who championed the tank's development, acknowledged Wells's influence. The specific mechanism Wells described was different (walking machines rather than tracked vehicles); the concept — armored ground vehicle that can cross trenches — was correct.

**The World State**: Wells believed that the solution to technological catastrophe (atomic war, biological weapons) was a world government — a Wellsian world state governed by scientific experts (a Saint-Simon heir). This was his lifelong obsession after about 1915. It did not happen; the UN is a pale shadow. His political vision was less accurate than his technological one.

## Edward Bellamy (1850–1898) — Looking Backward (1888)

### The Novel and Its Impact

*Looking Backward* is the third-best-selling American novel of the 19th century (after Uncle Tom's Cabin and Ben-Hur). It describes Julian West, who falls asleep in 1887 and wakes up in the year 2000 to find a socialist utopia. Over 160 "Bellamy Clubs" formed to advocate for his program.

```
  BELLAMY'S 2000: THE TECHNOLOGY
  ================================

  THE CREDIT CARD (1888 → 1950 actual):
  "Let me give you, then, a card of credit... good
   for the whole year." Government-issued credit;
   no individual pricing; everyone gets an equal share
   of annual production.
   → Concept of credit card predates the physical card
     by 62 years (Diners Club, 1950)

  HOME MUSIC DELIVERY:
  "If you wish to hear music the provision is quite
   as simple. At the pressing of a button... the finest
   music which the instrumentalists and vocalists
   of the age are able to produce."
  → Described cable radio / streaming audio
    before radio was invented (Marconi, 1895)

  DEPARTMENT STORES / UNIFIED RETAIL:
  All goods available in one large store;
  catalog ordering; delivery to homes.
  → The mail-order catalog (Sears, 1893) and
    eventually Amazon are the realization

  WHAT BELLAMY DIDN'T PREDICT:
  That these technologies would be deployed within a
  capitalist rather than socialist framework.
  He correctly identified what technology could do;
  he was completely wrong about who would own and
  operate it.
```

**The Socialist Vision and Its Failure**: By the year 2000, Bellamy predicted:
- All production nationalized in a single "industrial army"
- Universal equal income
- All citizens serve in the industrial army until age 45
- No money; only credit cards

None of this happened. The United States in 2000 was more economically unequal than in 1888 on most measures. The technology was right; the social transformation was not.

**The Lesson**: Bellamy's predictions illustrate that technology can be extrapolated more easily than social organization. The physical possibility of a technology does not determine how it will be deployed or who will control it.

## Aldous Huxley (1894–1963) — Brave New World (1932)

### The Anti-Utopia

Huxley was responding to the utopian tradition (especially Wells and Bellamy) by asking: what if the techno-optimistic utopia actually worked? What would be wrong with it?

```
  BRAVE NEW WORLD: THE ACCURATE PREDICTIONS
  ==========================================

  REPRODUCTIVE TECHNOLOGY (1932 → actual):
  "Bokanovsky's Process": human embryos industrially
    produced from a single egg; clones
  External gestation: Hatcheries; no natural birth
  Conditioning from gestation: chemical/hormonal
    manipulation of developing fetuses determines caste

  ACTUAL TIMELINE:
  1978: IVF (Louise Brown, first test-tube baby)
  1996: Dolly the sheep (first mammal cloned from adult cell)
  2022: Artificial uterus for sheep fetuses; human
        ectogenesis research ongoing
  Genetic modification of embryos: underway
  (He Jiankui, 2018: first gene-edited human babies)

  PSYCHOPHARMACOLOGY:
  "Soma": a drug that produces happiness without side effects
  "A gramme is better than a damn"
  Citizens take soma at the first sign of unhappiness

  ACTUAL: SSRIs (1980s), anxiolytics, psychedelics research
  Pharmaceutical happiness management: real and growing
  "Chemical happiness" as social policy: Brave New World
  was writing about this in 1932.

  CONSUMERISM AS SOCIAL CONTROL:
  Citizens conditioned to want new things constantly
  "Ending is better than mending"
  Happiness = consumption; stability = consumption

  ACTUAL: The attention economy, planned obsolescence,
  neuromarketing. Huxley's conditioning is advertising.

  HYPNOPAEDIA ("sleep-teaching"):
  Conditioning through sleep
  NOT accurate: sleep learning doesn't work for complex
               content (confirmed by research)
  BUT accurate in spirit: conditioning begins in early
               childhood and shapes preferences without
               conscious choice.
```

**The Orwell-Huxley Debate**:

```
  ORWELL (1984, 1949) vs. HUXLEY (BRAVE NEW WORLD, 1932)
  =========================================================

  ORWELL:                          HUXLEY:
  Authoritarianism through         Authoritarianism through
  PAIN and FEAR                    PLEASURE and DISTRACTION

  The boot stamping on a           Citizens love their
  human face, forever              servitude

  People know they're              People are too entertained
  oppressed; truth is              and comfortable to notice
  actively suppressed              they're controlled

  Big Brother                      Henry Ford ("Our Ford")
  Doublethink                      Conditioning

  NEIL POSTMAN (Amusing Ourselves to Death, 1985):
  "What Orwell feared were those who would ban books.
  What Huxley feared was that there would be no reason
  to ban a book, for there would be no one who wanted
  to read one."

  Both are partially correct descriptions of different
  authoritarian environments.
  Orwell describes surveillance states (USSR, North Korea).
  Huxley describes attention-capture consumer societies.
```

**Huxley's Perennial Philosophy (1945)**: Later Huxley moved toward mysticism and psychedelics (*The Doors of Perception*, 1954 — describing his mescaline experience; the book that gave The Doors their name). This is separate from his dystopian fiction. His later view: consciousness itself is the primary mystery, and psychedelics offer access to non-ordinary states of consciousness. The current psychedelic therapy research movement (psilocybin for depression, MDMA for PTSD) is in part a vindication of questions Huxley was asking.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Verne was more accurate than Wells."**
Verne's technology was more careful (he stayed closer to existing engineering). Wells's social predictions were less accurate than his technology predictions. They were doing different things: Verne built exciting adventures with plausible technology; Wells used fiction as social analysis. Different aims, different accuracy profiles.

**"Huxley's Brave New World is about the future."**
It's about the 1920s-30s, extrapolated. Henry Ford is literally worshipped as "Our Ford." Mass production, advertising, consumerism, psychological conditioning — these were new phenomena in 1932. The novel is a satire of the direction of contemporary culture, not a prediction of 2500 CE.

**"1984 is more accurate than Brave New World."**
They are accurate about different things. Mass surveillance (Orwell) is very real — the NSA surveillance programs Snowden revealed are more Orwell than Huxley. Consumerist distraction and pharmaceutical mood management are more Huxley than Orwell. Both are operating simultaneously in contemporary society.
