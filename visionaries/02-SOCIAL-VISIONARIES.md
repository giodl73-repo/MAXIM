# Social Visionaries: Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen, Marx

## The Big Picture

```
  THE SOCIALIST VISIONARIES: 1800-1883
  ======================================

  Context: Industrial Revolution + French Revolution
  The combination produced two questions:
  (1) Who should govern? (political question)
  (2) Who should own the means of production? (economic question)

  SAINT-SIMON (1760-1825): Expert governance replaces aristocracy
  → technocracy as the vision
  → "The government of things" replacing "the government of men"

  FOURIER (1772-1837): Cooperative communities (phalansteries)
  → human nature is fundamentally cooperative; capitalism represses it
  → "passionate attraction" as organizational principle

  OWEN (1771-1858): Environmental determinism
  → character is shaped by conditions; improve conditions = improve humans
  → New Harmony; cooperative movement; socialist terminology invented here

  MARX (1818-1883): Scientific socialism
  → historical materialism; class struggle; surplus value
  → capitalism contains its own contradictions → inevitable socialist revolution
  → the grand synthesis that consumed all prior socialist thought

  THE MARX / NON-MARX SPLIT:
  "Utopian socialism" (Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen): Marx's label;
   means: proposes alternatives without explaining why capitalism
   will be replaced or how the transition will happen

  "Scientific socialism" (Marx): Explains the mechanism
   (class struggle, surplus value extraction, alienation) and
   claims to make the outcome inevitable from material conditions.
   Marx thought he was doing for history what Darwin did for biology.
```

## Henri de Saint-Simon (1760–1825)

### What He Proposed

Saint-Simon was a French aristocrat who fought in the American Revolution (with Lafayette), survived the Terror by imprisoning himself before he could be guillotined, and spent the rest of his life developing a theory of industrial society.

**The Core Claim**:

```
  SAINT-SIMON'S VISION
  =====================

  THE EXISTING ORDER:
  Aristocracy + clergy govern
  on the basis of birth + supernatural authority
  They produce nothing
  They extract from those who do produce

  THE EMERGING ORDER:
  Scientists, engineers, industrialists, bankers
  understand how to make the economy function
  They govern by knowledge of what produces value
  → "The administration of things" replaces
    "the government of persons"
    (Engels later borrowed this phrase and credited
     Saint-Simon as its origin)

  THE VISION:
  A European parliament of scientists and industrialists
  plans the allocation of resources for maximum production
  Poverty is eliminated by scientific management
  The Catholic Church is replaced by a "New Christianity"
  of science and fraternal love

  WHAT THIS IS:
  Technocracy: government by experts who know how things work
  This idea runs through:
  - Bellamy's Looking Backward (1888): planned industrial state
  - The Soviet planning ideal (Gosplan)
  - Systems analysis and operations research (WWII)
  - The technocratic management ideal in large corporations
  - Every "meritocracy" argument in Silicon Valley
```

**Saint-Simon's Direct Influence**: Auguste Comte (founder of sociology and positivism) was Saint-Simon's secretary and intellectual successor. John Stuart Mill studied his work. Karl Marx engaged with it critically. The "scientific management" movement (Taylor, 1911) is a specifically limited version of the same idea. The entire tradition of using social science to improve governance traces through Saint-Simon.

**The Problem**: Saint-Simon's experts have interests of their own. "Government by experts" assumes either that experts are altruistic or that their interests align with the public good. Neither is automatically true. The 20th century's planned economies (which carried a version of this vision) are the empirical test cases; the results were not encouraging.

## Charles Fourier (1772–1837)

### What He Proposed

Fourier was a traveling salesman who spent his life developing an elaborate theory of social organization based on the idea that human nature is fundamentally composed of "passionate attractions" — natural desires that capitalism frustrates.

**The Phalanstery**:

```
  FOURIER'S PHALANSTERY
  =======================

  Unit: 1,620 people (representing all personality types)
        + building (phalanstery) + agricultural land

  ORGANIZATION BY PASSION:
  Fourier classified human passions into 12 types
  (5 sensory; 4 affective — friendship, love, ambition,
   paternal; 3 "distributive" — cabalist/intrigue,
   alternating/variety, composite/enthusiasm)

  Work groups ("series") organized by passionate attraction
  People join the work they find satisfying
  Variety principle: each person rotates through multiple
  occupations daily (prevents boredom)

  RESULT (Fourier's claim):
  When work aligns with passion:
  - 3x more productive than under capitalism
  - Competition between groups (not workers) within
    a cooperative framework
  - Children clean streets (they like dirt)
  - Adults teach children through example
  - Women have equal status; free love permitted

  THE MATH:
  144 phalansteries = one province
  2,985,984 phalansteries = cover the world
  → Global network of cooperative communities
```

**What Was Actually Built**:

Fourier attracted followers; he never saw his phalanstery built. After his death:
- Brook Farm (Massachusetts, 1841–46): Transcendentalist commune that converted to Fourierism; had ~60 residents; failed after a fire
- Red Bank Phalanstery (New Jersey, 1843–55): 12 years; failed
- La Réunion colony (Texas, 1855): failed within 3 years
- Others in France, Romania, Brazil

All the Fourierist experiments lasted a few years at most. The model did not survive contact with actual people.

**What Was Right**: Fourier identified that capitalism creates misalignment between what people find meaningful and what they get paid to do. The "job satisfaction" research tradition, intrinsic motivation theory (Deci and Ryan), and the entire "do what you love" cultural meme are all Fourierist in spirit. He was right about the problem; his proposed solution was impractical.

**What Was Wrong**: Fourier's elaborate categorization of passions (he had precise numbers for everything) had no empirical foundation. The assumption that 1,620 is the exactly right community size is pseudoscience. The assumption that passionate attraction + variety + cooperative competition would solve coordination problems ignores transaction costs and information problems that markets solve (imperfectly).

## Robert Owen (1771–1858)

(See also social-reformers/03-LABOR-MOVEMENT.md for the labor movement context)

### The Social Visionary Dimension

Owen's contribution as a social visionary is the *environmental determinism* argument: human character is the product of circumstances, not innate nature. If you change the circumstances, you change the character.

```
  OWEN'S CORE CLAIM
  ==================

  "The character of man is, without a single exception,
   always formed for him; that it may be, and is, chiefly
   created by his predecessors; that they give him, or
   may give him, his ideas and habits, which are the
   powers that govern and direct his conduct. Man,
   therefore, never did, nor is it possible he ever can,
   form his own character."
  — Owen, A New View of Society (1813)

  IMPLICATION:
  No one is born criminal, lazy, or inferior.
  Bad character = bad circumstances.
  Reform the circumstances → reform the character.

  This is the foundational claim of:
  - Progressive social policy (improve environments
    to improve people)
  - Environmentalism in psychology (Watson's behaviorism)
  - Rehabilitation over punishment in criminal justice
  - Anti-poverty programs (change conditions → change outcomes)
  - All of modern social work theory

  New Lanark (1800-1825): the empirical test.
  Workers under decent conditions = better productivity.
  (See social-reformers/03 for detail)
```

**Owen Invents the Term "Socialism"**: The word "socialism" was first used in *The Co-operative Magazine* in 1827, in the context of a debate about Owen's ideas. He was the primary British figure whose ideas were being called "socialist." The term was retrospectively applied to Fourier and Saint-Simon, but the word's origin is Owenite.

## Karl Marx (1818–1883)

### The Historical Vision

Marx's claim to the visionaries directory is not as a political reformer (see political-reformers/08-REVOLUTIONARY-LEADERS.md for Lenin, Mao, Castro) but as the *theorist who provided the grand historical framework* that shaped 20th-century political movements globally.

**Historical Materialism**:

```
  MARX'S THEORY OF HISTORY
  ==========================

  BASE → SUPERSTRUCTURE

  BASE: Material conditions of production
  - Who owns the means of production
  - How production is organized
  - The relations between owners and workers

  SUPERSTRUCTURE: Everything else
  - Law, politics, religion, philosophy, culture
  - These reflect and legitimize the base
  - "The ideas of the ruling class are in every epoch
     the ruling ideas"

  STAGES OF HISTORY (materialist version):
  Primitive communism → Slave society → Feudalism →
  Capitalism → Socialism → Communism

  The mechanism of transition: CLASS STRUGGLE
  Each stage contains contradictions that produce its
  successor. Capitalism's contradiction:
  - Forces of production (technology, organization)
    grow → require collective forms
  - Relations of production (private ownership)
    become a fetter on the growing forces
  - Contradiction = crisis → revolutionary transition

  KEY CONCEPTS:
  Surplus value: workers produce more than they're paid;
                 owner captures the difference
  Alienation: workers don't control their own labor;
              the product is alien to them
  Commodity fetishism: social relations between people
                        appear as relations between things
```

**What Marx Got Right**:

```
  MARX'S ACCURATE INSIGHTS (by modern reckoning)
  ================================================

  ✓ Capitalism produces inequality through the wage relation
  ✓ Economic structure shapes ideology and culture
  ✓ Capital tends to concentrate (Piketty's r > g)
  ✓ Business cycles and financial crises are inherent
    to capitalism (endogenous instability)
  ✓ Technological unemployment is possible (not just
    temporary — the "lump of labor" is partially real)
  ✓ Alienation from one's work is a real psychological
    phenomenon (now well-studied)
  ✓ Political ideas tend to reflect economic interests
    of those producing them
```

**What Marx Got Wrong**:

```
  MARX'S SIGNIFICANT ERRORS
  ===========================

  ✗ The labor theory of value: value comes from
    socially necessary labor time. Modern economics:
    value comes from scarcity + utility (subjective).
    Prices are not proportional to labor content.

  ✗ Immiseration: workers will get poorer as capitalism
    develops. In fact: real wages rose substantially
    in developed capitalist countries.

  ✗ Revolution in advanced capitalist countries:
    Revolution happened in agrarian Russia and China,
    not in industrial Germany, France, or Britain.
    The industrial working class organized reformist
    unions; didn't seek revolution.

  ✗ Communism = no alienation: the historical communist
    states produced extreme alienation and no classless
    society.

  ✗ Historical stages are not universal:
    The slave society → feudalism → capitalism sequence
    doesn't describe all societies.
```

**The Marx Legacy Problem**: Marx produced intellectual tools that were used to build states that he would not have recognized as his intended outcome. The question "is Marx responsible for Soviet communism?" is analogous to "is Freud responsible for psychiatry's abuses?" or "is Malthus responsible for Social Darwinism?" The intellectual lineage is real; direct responsibility for subsequent practitioners' choices is not.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Fourier's phalansteries failed because people don't cooperate."**
People cooperate all the time — in families, firms, teams, open-source projects. The failures of Fourierist communes reflected specific design problems: inadequate attention to governance of commons; the "freerider" problem in small communities; founder conflicts; the tension between Fourier's specific prescriptions and actual people's actual preferences. The cooperative model failed in Fourier's specific implementation; it works elsewhere (Mondragon, kibbutzim, housing co-ops).

**"Marx was wrong about everything."**
False. His analysis of capital concentration (Piketty's work validates this), his analysis of how economic structure shapes ideology, and his analysis of alienation are empirically supported. His specific historical predictions (revolution in advanced capitalism; proletarian immiseration; post-capitalist communism) were wrong. Distinguishing between his analytical contributions and his predictive failures is necessary for accurate assessment.

**"Owen's 'no bad people, only bad environments' means there's no individual responsibility."**
This is the common objection. Owen's claim is about the causal structure of character formation, not about whether it's useful to hold people responsible. A practical social policy can acknowledge that conditions shape character (and therefore invest in improving conditions) while maintaining legal accountability for individual actions.
