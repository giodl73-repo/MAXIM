# Oral History as Method: Interview Technique, Archive, Reliability

## The Big Picture

```
ORAL HISTORY vs. ORAL TRADITION
================================

ORAL TRADITION                    ORAL HISTORY
--------------                    ------------
Multi-generational transmission   Single-generation recorded memory
Community knowledge               Individual testimony
No original recording             Recorded at time of interview
No fixed "interview"              Structured interview protocol
Evolves over time                 Fixed at recording
Serves community function         Serves historian / researcher
Studied by folklorists,           Studied by historians,
anthropologists                   sociologists, journalists
Examples: epics, myths,           Examples: Holocaust testimony,
folk narrative                    labor history, civil rights
                                  veterans' accounts

BOTH:
  Oral as primary medium (at time of content creation)
  Valuable historical evidence
  Subject to memory limitations and self-presentation
  Require critical methodology
```

**The critical distinction**: oral history methodology was developed to record living memory before it is lost — a documentary act. Oral tradition is a living community practice that the researcher studies. Confusing the two produces methodological errors in both directions.

## The Columbia University Oral History Project

The oldest institutional oral history program in the world:

```
COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY ORAL HISTORY
==================================

Founded: 1948
Founder: Allan Nevins (historian)

Nevins's motivation:
  Post-WWII: major figures of early 20th century dying
  Written sources (letters, diaries) declining
    (telephone replaced letter-writing)
  Memory of critical events would be lost permanently

Original focus:
  Elite oral history
  Political, business, military leaders
  Long biographical interviews

Current collection: ~9,000 memoir volumes, 11 million+ pages
  (includes interviews, transcripts, supplementary materials)

Methodology innovations:
  Established interview as historical source
  Developed consent and archival deposit protocols
  Created standards for transcription and indexing
  Trained first generation of oral historians

Evolution: from elite to grassroots
  1960s: labor history, civil rights movement
  1970s: women's history, immigrant history
  1980s+: Holocaust survivors, veterans, community histories
```

## Interview Protocol: Core Methodology

```
ORAL HISTORY INTERVIEW DESIGN
==============================

PRE-INTERVIEW RESEARCH:
  Know your subject area before the interview
  Prepare questions but hold them loosely
  Research the interviewee's background
  Identify documents, photos to use as prompts

INTERVIEW TYPES:
  Life history interview:
    "Tell me about your life from the beginning"
    Open-ended, biographical, long (multiple sessions)
    Subject controls narrative shape
    Goal: understand the whole person in context

  Topical interview:
    Focused on specific events, period, role
    "Tell me about your work in the union, 1942-1955"
    More structured; shorter possible
    Goal: specific historical documentation

  Community documentation:
    Multiple interviews on same events from different perspectives
    "How did the community experience the factory closure?"
    Goal: collective memory of shared event

QUESTION TYPES:
  Open-ended (preferred): "What was that like?"
  Closed (avoid for depth): "Were you scared?"
  Clarifying: "Can you tell me more about that?"
  Probing: "You mentioned X -- what happened next?"
  Prompt: "I have a photograph here -- do you recognize...?"
```

### The "Grand Tour" Opening

Oral history interviewers typically begin with what researchers call the "grand tour" question:

```
GRAND TOUR TECHNIQUE
=====================

Opening question: "Tell me about yourself / your life /
                   your experience with [topic]."

Function:
  Gives subject control of narrative structure
  Reveals what the subject considers important
  Establishes trust and rapport
  Produces unfiltered first account
  (Interviewer can compare to prompted answers later)

The trap to avoid:
  Jumping to specific questions too early
  Interviewer's categories replace subject's categories
  Results in interview that confirms hypotheses
    rather than discovers new information
```

## Consent and Archival Deposit

Oral history has specific ethical obligations that distinguish it from journalism or ethnography:

```
CONSENT FRAMEWORK
==================

INFORMED CONSENT:
  Subject must understand:
    - Who will have access to the recording
    - How it will be used (academic, public, restricted)
    - Whether name will be used or pseudonym
    - Right to review and amend transcript
    - Whether they can withdraw consent

DEPOSIT OPTIONS:
  Open access: recording and transcript publicly available
  Restricted access: available to researchers only
  Sealed: not available until subject's death or X years
  Closed: only researcher can access
  Partial: some portions restricted

DEED OF GIFT:
  Legal document transferring rights to the repository
  Defines terms of access precisely
  Required for most institutional archives
  Subject and interviewer both sign

SPECIAL POPULATIONS:
  Vulnerable communities (prisoners, children, immigrants)
  require enhanced consent procedures
  Community consultation may be required
  Indigenous communities: community consent in addition
  to individual consent -- collective rights in narrative
```

## Reliability Questions: Memory, Hindsight, Self-Presentation

Oral history faces systematic reliability challenges that historians must account for:

```
MEMORY RELIABILITY CHALLENGES
================================

1. TELESCOPING:
   Recent events remembered as more distant (forward)
   or distant events remembered as more recent (backward)
   Example: "I started that job around '68 or '69"
   (records show 1972)
   Most common error in event dating

2. HINDSIGHT BIAS:
   People restructure memories to fit outcomes they know
   "I always knew that would fail"
   "I had a bad feeling about him from the start"
   Memory of pre-event state corrupted by knowledge
   of post-event outcome

3. SELF-PRESENTATION:
   Subjects construct narratives of their lives
   that serve identity and reputation purposes
   This is not lying -- it is narrative self-construction
   Alessandro Portelli: "oral history doesn't tell us
   what happened so much as what people think happened
   and why they think it matters"

4. FLASHBULB MEMORIES:
   Highly vivid, detailed memories of emotional events
   (Where were you when...?)
   Feel accurate; ARE often accurate for emotional core
   but peripheral details are less reliable
   Neisser & Harsch (1992): Challenger explosion memories
   -> 75% of subjects' detailed memories were wrong
   -> But confidence was high
   Emotional vividness does not predict factual accuracy

5. COLLECTIVE MEMORY CONTAMINATION:
   Later public accounts of events contaminate
   personal memories of those events
   Interviewees have absorbed media narratives
   about events they witnessed
   Memory of the event and memory of the reporting
   become fused
```

### Portelli's Framework

Alessandro Portelli is the most important theorist of oral history's epistemological status:

```
PORTELLI'S KEY DISTINCTIONS
=============================

Oral history as EVENT RECORD:
  What factually happened?
  -> Less reliable for specific details
  -> Reliable for structural/experiential features

Oral history as MEANING RECORD:
  What did this mean to this person?
  -> This is what oral history does UNIQUELY well
  -> No document can tell you this
  -> The "inaccuracy" is itself data

Famous case: Portelli (1991) on Luigi Trastulli
  Italian worker killed in anti-NATO demonstration, 1949
  Oral histories gave wrong date (said 1953)
  Wrong context (said factory dispute, not political)
  BUT: the error was systematic -- all narrators made
       same "mistake" in same direction
  Interpretation: the community was rewriting the memory
  to make Trastulli's death meaningful in the context
  they cared about (labor rights, not cold war politics)
  The "inaccuracy" reveals collective memory processing
```

## Reliability Enhancement Techniques

```
CORROBORATION STRATEGIES
=========================

TRIANGULATION:
  Multiple interviews on same events
  Cross-reference for agreement / disagreement
  Disagreement is as informative as agreement

DOCUMENT CROSS-REFERENCE:
  Compare testimony against documentary sources
  Photos, newspapers, official records
  Discrepancies: investigate, don't dismiss
  Sometimes testimony is correct; documents wrong

INTERNAL CONSISTENCY:
  Within a single interview, check for:
  - Consistent chronology
  - Consistent characterization
  - Contradiction of stated facts

COMMUNITY VALIDATION:
  Where possible, bring findings back to community
  "I understood from your interview that..."
  Subject can correct, clarify, or confirm

WHAT CANNOT BE CORROBORATED:
  Subjective experience, emotion, perception
  These do not require corroboration -- they ARE
  the subject's experience, not claims about
  external fact
```

## Oral History vs. Journalism

```
METHODOLOGICAL DIFFERENCES
============================

JOURNALISM                          ORAL HISTORY
----------                          ------------
Present-focused                     Historical focus
Anonymity common                    Named attribution standard
Minimal process documentation       Full methodology documented
Publication goal                    Archive goal (future researchers)
Verification = editorial standard   Verification = scholarly apparatus
No long-term preservation plan      Long-term deposit required
Subject has limited control         Subject has consent rights
Short interview                     Long interview (often multiple sessions)
Paraphrase acceptable               Verbatim transcript standard
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Issue | Oral history approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Dating events accurately | Cross-reference with documents; expect telescoping |
| Hindsight contamination | Ask for contemporaneous feelings before outcomes known |
| Self-presentation bias | Recognize it as data; don't try to eliminate |
| Collective memory contamination | Ask "Do you remember what you thought at the time?" |
| Conflicting testimonies | Both may be true; document and analyze the conflict |
| Accessing restricted communities | Community consent + individual consent; cultural mediator |
| Archiving sensitive material | Tiered access; sealed deposit; negotiated consent |

## Common Confusion Points

**Oral history inaccuracy is not a flaw to be fixed.** The inaccuracy is data about how memory works, how communities process events, and what meanings matter to people. Portelli's Luigi Trastulli case demonstrates that the "error" was the finding.

**Oral history is not oral tradition.** Oral tradition is multi-generational transmission studied as a community phenomenon. Oral history is recorded individual memory documented for future research. Conflating them produces methodological errors.

**High emotional recall does not predict factual accuracy.** Flashbulb memories feel vivid and accurate but are systematically unreliable for peripheral details. The emotional core may be accurate; the specific details often are not.

**Silence is evidence.** What a subject refuses to discuss, hedges around, or never mentions is as informative as what they say. Systematic omissions by multiple narrators may indicate community suppression of a topic.

**StoryCorps is not oral history methodology.** StoryCorps is a public radio project that records personal stories. It uses some oral history practices but lacks systematic research design, archival scholarly apparatus, and corroboration strategy. It is a valuable cultural archive, not a research methodology.
