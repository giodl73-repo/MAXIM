## Milman Parry: The Fieldwork and the Theory

### Background

Milman Parry (1902–1935) was an American classicist who noticed something about Homeric epithets that prior scholarship had treated as ornamental or arbitrary.

```
PARRY'S OBSERVATION
===================

Greek hero epithets in the Iliad are not chosen for
contextual meaning — they are chosen for METER.

"swift-footed Achilles" (podas okus Akhilleus)
   -> used when the line needs those syllables
   -> NOT when Achilles is actually running
   -> context-independent — metrically motivated

"Achilles of the great shout" (boen agathos Akhilleus)
   -> different metrical shape
   -> fills a different slot in the hexameter line
   -> same hero, different formula — different metrical need

Conclusion: the system is COMPOSITIONAL, not decorative.
Each noun-epithet formula fills exactly one metrical slot.
The system is complete and economical — no slot unfilled,
no slot filled by two formulas with the same referent.
```

### Parry's Dissertation (1928)

Published in French as two parts:
1. "L'Epithete traditionnelle dans Homere" — established the noun-epithet economy
2. "Les Formules et la metrique d'Homere" — extended to broader phrase patterns

The argument: the formula system could not have been invented by one poet. It was the product of generations of oral composition — a shared technology, not an individual style.

### The Yugoslav Fieldwork (1933-1935)

Parry's insight needed empirical proof: did living oral epic poets compose this way?

```
FIELDWORK DESIGN
================

Question: Do living oral bards recompose or memorize?

Method:
  1. Record same singer performing same song twice
     -> if memorizing: versions should be identical
     -> if recomposing: versions should be structurally
       similar but differ in length and surface wording

  2. Ask singers: do you sing word-for-word the same?
     -> Singers claim yes — but recordings show no

  3. Record multiple singers on same traditional epic
     -> Compare formula overlap across singers

Location: Bosnia-Herzegovina, rural Yugoslavia
Principal singers: Avdo Medjedovic, Mujo Kukuruzovic
Recording tech: aluminum disc recorder (1930s)
Archive: Harvard's Milman Parry Collection (now digital)
```

**Key finding from Avdo Medjedovic**: When asked to sing a song he had just heard for the first time, Medjedovic produced a version **three times longer** than the original while maintaining its structure. He was not memorizing — he was expanding using his formula toolkit.

### Parry's Death and Lord's Continuation

Parry died in 1935 at 33 (accidental gunshot). Albert Lord (1912-1991) was his graduate student on the Yugoslav expedition. Lord:
- Completed the analysis
- Wrote the definitive synthesis: *The Singer of Tales* (1960)
- Continued fieldwork in Yugoslavia into the 1950s
- Applied the framework explicitly to Homer and Beowulf

## The Formula: Precise Definition

**Parry's definition**: "a group of words which is regularly employed under the same metrical conditions to express a given essential idea."

This has three parts:

```
FORMULA COMPONENTS
==================

1. "GROUP OF WORDS"
   Multi-word phrase — not single words
   (single words are building blocks of formulas)

2. "REGULARLY EMPLOYED UNDER SAME METRICAL CONDITIONS"
   Metrical shape is invariant — formula fits one slot
   "swift-footed Achilles" = -- (dactyl + spondee)
   Only used when that metrical slot needs filling
   This is the FUNCTIONAL criterion

3. "TO EXPRESS A GIVEN ESSENTIAL IDEA"
   Semantic constant — same core meaning each time
   The idea triggers recall of the formula
   Context modifies nothing — the formula is pre-formed
```

### Formula Density Analysis

Lord measured what proportion of a Homeric line consists of formulaic material:

```
FORMULA DENSITY IN THE ILIAD (Lord's sample)
=============================================

Full formulas (exact repetitions of 4+ words):    ~30%
Half-line formulas (2-3 word phrases):             ~20%
Formula-type patterns (same structure, varied):    ~30%
Free composition:                                  ~20%

Total formulaic or formula-based:                  ~80%

Comparison: Beowulf (Old English)
Full formulas:                                     ~20%
Formula-types and compounds:                       ~50%
Total formulaic:                                   ~70%
```

**The interpretation problem**: high formula density is necessary but not sufficient evidence for oral composition. A literate poet could deliberately use traditional formulas (as Virgil does in the Aeneid). The question is whether the system as a whole — the economy, the completeness, the metrical precision — could have been designed by one person.

## Themes: The Structural Units Above the Line

A **theme** (Lord's term) is a recurring narrative unit — a "typical scene" that appears across multiple songs and across multiple traditions.

```
TYPICAL SCENE: ARMING OF THE HERO
==================================

Sequence (invariant order, variable expansion):

1. Decision to arm / reason stated
2. Greaves put on (lower leg protection)
3. Breastplate / corselet
4. Sword over shoulder
5. Shield
6. Helmet
7. Spear(s) taken up
8. Departure scene

Homer: Agamemnon arming (Iliad 11.16-46)   -- fully expanded
Homer: Achilles arming (Iliad 19.369-391)  -- fully expanded
Homer: Paris arming (Iliad 3.330-338)      -- abbreviated
Beowulf: hero preparation scenes           -- structurally parallel
```

Other major theme types in Greek epic:
- **Assembly/council**: heroes gather, debate, decide
- **Feast/hospitality**: arrival, washing, seating, wine, food, then speech
- **Battle narrative**: aristeia (single-hero rampage) structure
- **Lament**: discovery of death, formal crying, speech, burial
- **Sea voyage**: departure, sailing, arrival, disembarkation
- **Dream sequence**: approach, stance, address, message, departure

### Themes as Compression and Expansion Tools

The theme allows the singer to control length:

```
SAME THEME, VARIABLE LENGTH
============================

Short version (summary):
  "He put on his armor and went to battle."
  [1 line -- theme invoked but not expanded]

Medium version (standard):
  [Greaves -> breastplate -> sword -> shield -> helmet -> spear]
  [~10 lines -- normal expansion]

Full version (aristeia context):
  [Each item described in detail, with genealogy of item,
   comparison to light, divine craftsmanship, simile]
  [30-60 lines -- maximum expansion for important hero]
```

This is the mechanism by which a singer adjusts a performance to its occasion — a two-hour wedding feast gets more expansion than a brief entertainment.

## Composition-in-Performance vs. Memorization

```
TWO MODELS OF ORAL PERFORMANCE
================================

MEMORIZATION MODEL (rejected by Parry-Lord)
  Singer learns text fixed in childhood
  Recites same wording at each performance
  Error = deviation from canonical text
  Performance = faithful reproduction

  Problems:
  - Yugoslav recordings disprove identical versions
  - No mechanism for composing the first performance
  - Cannot explain 3-fold expansion by Medjedovic
  - Formula system would be unnecessarily complex

COMPOSITION-IN-PERFORMANCE MODEL (Parry-Lord)
  Singer knows: story outline + formula system + theme repertoire
  Rebuilds the song at each performance
  Each performance is a unique instantiation
  "Same song" = same story with same traditional resources

  Evidence:
  - Yugoslav double-recordings show structural identity,
    surface variation, length variation
  - Formula density matches metrical necessity, not meaning
  - Singers report "same" song that recordings show varies
  - Intermediate tradition bearers show intermediate patterns
```

**The paradox**: singers genuinely believe they sing word-for-word the same. They are wrong about their mechanism but right about their stability — the schema is stable, the surface varies.

## Implications for Homer

The Parry-Lord framework opened new questions about the Homeric texts:

| Question | Pre-Parry answer | Post-Parry position |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Why do epithets repeat "meaninglessly"? | Ornament, convention | Metrical function — fill the slot |
| Why is the same passage repeated verbatim? | Quotation or structural echo | Formula reuse — normal oral practice |
| Who is "Homer"? | Blind bard, individual genius | Name for the tradition or a late editor |
| How were texts fixed? | Homer dictated them | Dictation to a scribe; or literate editor compiled variant performances |
| Are the Iliad and Odyssey by the same poet? | Unitarian vs. analyst debate | Wrong question — oral tradition has no author |

### The Dictation Hypothesis

Lord's best explanation for how oral composition left fixed written texts:

```
DICTATION HYPOTHESIS
====================

Context: Greek alphabet adopted ~800-750 BCE
         (adapted from Phoenician abjad)

Process:
  Oral singer dictates slowly to scribe
  Without performance pressure, singer can elaborate
  Dictated text longer/more complex than live performance
  Scribe regularizes spelling, fixes text

Prediction:
  Dictated oral texts should be longer than performance
  Should show HIGHER formula density than performance
    (singer has time to fill every slot completely)

Evidence: Iliad (24 books, ~15,700 lines) is longer than
  any plausible live performance -- fits dictation model
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Concept | Precise meaning |
|---------|----------------|
| Formula | Metrically useful phrase in fixed syntactic slot; reused to express same essential idea |
| Theme | Recurring narrative unit (typical scene); variable length, invariant structure/order |
| Formula density | % of text consisting of exact or type-formula repetitions |
| Oral-derived text | Written text whose composition was shaped by oral tradition |
| Composition-in-performance | Recomposition using toolkit, not recitation of memorized text |
| Economy | Formula system property: each idea has one formula per metrical slot, no duplicates |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Oral" does not mean "improvised."** Composition-in-performance is disciplined and traditional — the singer uses inherited tools. Jazz improvisation is the closest modern analogy: within a structure, using learned vocabulary, no two takes identical.

**Formula density proves oral habit, not oral performance.** A highly literate poet can write formulaically. The key is whether the system — its economy, its metrical precision, its comprehensiveness — could have been engineered by one person. Parry argued it could not.

**Singers don't lie when they say they repeat word-for-word.** They are accurately describing the subjective experience. They have absorbed a formula-theme toolkit so thoroughly that deploying it feels like retrieval. The mechanism is invisible to them.

**Lord's "theme" differs from "motif" in folklore.** A theme is a structural narrative unit (typical scene with invariant sequence). A motif is a smaller recurring element (magic object, disguised deity). These are different levels of analysis.

**Medjedovic's 80,000-line performance** of the Wedding of Smailagic Meho is not evidence that all singers could do this. He was exceptional. The point is that the mechanism made it possible — no memorizer could triple the length of a song heard once.
