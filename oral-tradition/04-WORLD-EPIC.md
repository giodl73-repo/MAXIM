## Gilgamesh: Oldest Written Epic

```
GILGAMESH TRANSMISSION HISTORY
================================

~2700 BCE: Historical king of Uruk (Sumer)
~2100 BCE: Sumerian poems about Gilgamesh
           (five independent tales, not yet a cycle)
~1800 BCE: Old Babylonian version (OB)
           First integrated narrative
           Tablets I-XI (fragments)
~1200 BCE: Standard Babylonian version (SB)
           Compiled/edited by Sin-leqi-unninni
           12 tablets = "canonical" form
           Tablet XII: Sumerian addition, imperfectly integrated

ORAL ANTECEDENTS:
  The Sumerian poems show formula patterns
  suggesting earlier oral tradition
  The OB version shows "oral-derived" characteristics
  The SB version has literary editing (deliberate structure)

TABLETS FOUND:
  British Museum acquired from Austen Henry Layard (1840s)
  Nineveh library of Ashurbanipal (668-631 BCE)
  George Smith (1872): recognized and translated
    Tablet XI = the Flood narrative
    (predates Genesis Flood by centuries)
```

### Gilgamesh Flood and Biblical Comparison

| Element | Gilgamesh (Tablet XI) | Genesis 6-9 |
|---------|-----------------------|-------------|
| Divine decision | Council of gods | YHWH alone |
| Flood agent | Enlil / Ishtar | YHWH |
| Survivor | Utnapishtim | Noah |
| Boat | Cube (equal dimensions) | Rectangular (300 x 50 x 30 cubits) |
| Bird test | Dove, swallow, raven | Raven, then dove x3 |
| Landing site | Mount Nimush (Zagros) | Ararat |
| Immortality | Refused to Gilgamesh | Not applicable |

The structural parallels indicate a shared Near Eastern flood narrative tradition — both Gilgamesh and Genesis draw from the same oral substrate, not from each other.

## Mahabharata: The Immense Text

```
MAHABHARATA SCALE
==================

Scale: ~200,000 shloka half-lines
       = ~1.8 million words
       = 10 x (Iliad + Odyssey combined)
       = longest literary work in world history

Structure:
  18 parvas (books) + Harivamsa (appendix)
  Core narrative: Pandava-Kaurava war (Kurukshetra)
  Embedded: Bhagavad Gita (chapters 23-40 of Bhishma Parva)
            Nala and Damayanti story
            Savitri story
            Yaksha Prashna
            Viduraniti (political philosophy)

Historical layers visible:
  CORE (300-400 BCE): Bharata cycle -- war narrative
  EXPANSION I (300 BCE - 0 CE): philosophical additions
  EXPANSION II (0 - 400 CE): Brahmanical editing,
    additions of didactic material (Shanti Parva)
  HARIVAMSA (400 CE): Vishnu genealogy added

Oral substrate:
  Charioteer Sanjaya's narration to blind king Dhritarashtra
  = oral performance frame embedded in the text
  Janamejaya's snake sacrifice = another performance frame
  Multiple narrators and occasions embedded = oral tradition
  preserved trace of performance context
```

### Sanskrit Shloka Meter

The shloka (two 16-syllable lines = 32 syllables per verse) is the default epic meter, less strict than Greek hexameter, allowing more variation:

```
SHLOKA STRUCTURE
=================

Each line: 8 + 8 syllables (two pada of 8)
Pattern: xx-x-xx-x | xx-x-x-x-x
         (x = either short or long, - = long)

This looser constraint vs. Greek hexameter:
  Hexameter: ~6 specific metrical feet per line
  Shloka: 8-syllable units with partial constraints

Result: lower formula density than Greek epic
  (more room for surface variation)
  but still shows formulaic composition patterns
```

## Beowulf: Anglo-Saxon Oral-Derived Epic

```
BEOWULF OVERVIEW
================

Language: Old English
Manuscript: Cotton Vitellius A.xv (~975-1025 CE)
     (only surviving copy; damaged in 1731 fire)
Date of composition: debated, 700-1000 CE
Historical setting: 6th c. Scandinavia
Length: 3,182 lines

ALLITERATIVE VERSE STRUCTURE (not meter but initial sounds):
  Each line in two half-lines separated by a pause
  3 or 4 stressed syllables per line
  At least 2 stressed syllables begin with same consonant

  "Hwaeth! We Gardena     in geardagum"
  (Lo! We the Spear-Danes  in days of yore)
  H    alliterates with implied pause -- w w alliterate

FORMULA EXAMPLES:
  "beorn under beorge" (hero under the hill) -- alliterative formula
  "wrixle wordum" (exchange of words) -- formulaic kenning
  "geatolicne gup-cyning" (great war-king) -- noun-epithet pair
```

### Kennings: The Old English Formula

A **kenning** is a compound metaphorical phrase used as a noun substitute — the characteristic formula type of Germanic oral poetry:

| Kenning | Literal | Meaning |
|---------|---------|---------|
| hronrad | whale-road | sea |
| banhus | bone-house | body |
| goldwine | gold-friend | lord (gift-giver) |
| sweordbealo | sword-evil | violent death |
| woruldcandel | world-candle | sun |
| beadoleoma | battle-light | sword |
| gupcyning | war-king | king |

Kennings are oral formulas of a specific type — metrically appropriate compound expressions that fill alliterative slots. The system shows the same economy as Homeric noun-epithets.

## Sundiata: Living West African Epic

```
SUNDIATA MANDE TRADITION
=========================

Subject: Sundiata Keita, founder of the Mali Empire (~1235 CE)
         Defeat of Sumanguru Kante, king of Sosso
         Establishment of Mande (Mandinka) hegemony

Performance context:
  Griot (jeli/djeli): hereditary caste of praise-singers,
  historians, genealogists, musicians
  Performance occasions: weddings, funerals, royal ceremonies,
  market gatherings, political events

Living tradition:
  Still performed today in Mali, Senegal, Guinea, Gambia
  Each jeli has their own version
  No canonical text (until Djibril Tamsir Niane's 1960 written version)
  Niane's version is ONE jeli's performance, not "the" text

Oral characteristics:
  Variable length (2-8 hours depending on occasion)
  Embedded genealogies of both performer's and patron's lineage
  Historical events embedded in praise-formula structure
  Musical accompaniment (kora, balafon) integral to performance
```

## Mwindo: Nyanga Epic of the DRC

```
MWINDO CYCLE (Nyanga people, eastern DRC)
==========================================

Subject: Mwindo, culture hero with supernatural powers
         Battles with father, lightning deity Nkuba,
         descent to the underworld, return and transformation

Recorded version: Daniel Biebuyck, 1956
  Recorded from Shé-kárisi Candi Rureke
  Performance: 12 days, nights
  Length: ~30,000 words in transcription
  Audience: villagers; performance occasion = ceremonial

Structure:
  No fixed beginning or ending
  Episodic -- each episode is a complete oral composition
  Rureke performed a specific sequence for recording
  Other Mwindo performers have different sequences

Comparative features:
  Katabasis (descent to underworld) = universal epic theme
  Culture hero civilizing wilderness = universal theme
  Conflict with divine father = South Slavic parallel
  But: embedded commentary, audience interaction,
  performer commentary -- more visible oral frame
  than Homer (where frame has been edited out)
```

## Kalevala: Collected Oral Tradition

```
KALEVALA PRODUCTION
====================

Elias Lönnrot (1802-1884):
  Traveled 11 field trips collecting rune-singers
  Interviewed: Arhippa Perttunen (key informant, Karelia)
  Total collection: ~1.3 million verses gathered

1835 edition: "Old Kalevala" -- 32 cantos, 12,078 verses
1849 edition: "New Kalevala" -- 50 cantos, 22,795 verses
  (Lönnrot rearranged, bridged, and lightly edited)

Finnish runo-singing (kalevalaic meter):
  Trochaic tetrameter: four trochaic feet (stressed-unstressed)
  Alliteration and parallelism as constraints
  Example: "Vaka vanha Väinämöinen" (Steadfast old Väinämöinen)

The Kalevala is a COMPOSITE -- not one singer's performance
but Lönnrot's synthesis of multiple regional traditions.
It is to Finnish oral tradition what
the Alexandrian editors were to Homer --
a learned assembly of inherited material.
```

## Oral vs. Literary Epic: Diagnostic Criteria

| Criterion | Oral / Oral-derived | Literary epic |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| Formula density | >60% | <50% |
| Repeated passages | Extensive, exact | Rare, allusive |
| Ring composition | Structural pattern | Possible stylistic choice |
| Catalogue sections | Common, expandable | Rare or deliberate imitation |
| Length control | Variable by occasion | Fixed at composition |
| Narrator identity | Tradition, not individual | Named author |
| Textual consistency | Internal contradictions present | Author can re-read |
| Metaphor type | Kennings / epithets (formulaic) | Original similes and metaphors |

## Common Confusion Points

**Epic length is not comparable across traditions.** The Mahabharata's 1.8M words includes centuries of Brahmanical additions. The "core" Bharata war narrative is much smaller. Comparing Iliad length to Mahabharata length conflates different transmission histories.

**The Kalevala is Lönnrot's work as much as folk tradition.** He made editorial choices — ordering, bridging, compositing. Modern Finnish scholars distinguish his synthesis from the original runo-singer performances.

**Sundiata has no "original" text.** Niane's 1960 French version is one jeli's performance, compiled from Niane's field notes. It has become the de facto canonical text in Francophone scholarship. This is a textual-fixation event analogous to what happened to Homer.

**Gilgamesh's flood narrative is not proof of cultural diffusion to Genesis.** It is evidence of a shared Near Eastern oral tradition. Both texts draw from the same tradition pool; neither is necessarily copied from the other.

**The Mahabharata contains the Bhagavad Gita.** This is worth noting for context: the Gita (18 chapters of philosophical dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna) is embedded in the war narrative — roughly 700 verses out of 200,000 total. It is one episode in an epic, not a standalone text.
