# Transmission and Variation: Drift, Version, Censorship, Community Control

```

|  Prestige of "the old way"   Political recontextualize|

+-------------------------------------------------------+
        |                              |
        v                              v
   STABLE SCHEMA              SURFACE DRIFT

        |                              |
        +----------+   +--------------+
                   v   v
            VERSION (t=n)
            [recognizably same; details changed]
```

**The key insight**: transmission is not reproduction. Every retelling is a re-performance shaped by the bearer's memory, skills, cultural context, and the current audience. What survives is what is memorable, useful, or sanctioned.

## Version vs. Variant: The Distinction

```
TERMINOLOGY
============

VERSION:
  A significantly different form of a tale type
  that has achieved some stability as its own tradition
  Example: Perrault's "Little Red Riding Hood" (no rescue)
           vs. Grimm's (huntsman rescues)
           -> Two versions, both stable, both circulating

VARIANT:
  A single performance or text that differs from
  the standard version in minor details
  Example: one informant says the wolf came on Tuesday,
           another says Wednesday
           -> Same version, different variants

TRADITION:
  The living system of versions and variants
  maintained by a community of performers
  Example: "The Little Red Riding Hood tradition"
           = all versions, variants, performances,
             adaptations in all media

VULGATE:
  The dominant version that comes to be treated
  as canonical, even if not "original"
  Example: Grimm's Fairy Tales = vulgate of European folklore
           (Grimm's editorial choices became the standard)
```

## Frederic Bartlett: Serial Reproduction (1932)

Bartlett's *Remembering* (1932) is the foundational experimental study of how oral material changes in transmission:

```
BARTLETT'S METHOD
==================

Stimulus: Native American folk tale
  "The War of the Ghosts" (unfamiliar cultural context)

Serial reproduction procedure:
  Subject 1 reads story, waits, recalls from memory
  Subject 2 hears Subject 1's version, waits, recalls
  Subject 3 hears Subject 2's version...
  Iterate ~10 times

Results:
  1. SHORTENING: story shortened with each iteration
     Unfamiliar elements dropped first
  2. RATIONALIZATION: story made more culturally familiar
     Supernatural elements explained or removed
     Native American plot logic converted to English logic
  3. CONVENTIONALIZATION: story aligned with expected genre
     Became more like English short story structure
  4. DISTORTION IN DETAIL: names, places changed
     Sequence of events sometimes reordered
  5. SCHEMA DOMINANCE: the listener's cultural schema
     overwrote foreign schema at each step

Bartlett's conclusion:
  Memory is CONSTRUCTIVE, not reproductive.
  We remember a schema and reconstruct details.
  Oral transmission = serial reconstruction.
```

### What Bartlett's Results Mean for Oral Tradition

```
IMPLICATIONS
=============

1. Cultural normalization is not error -- it is memory.
   A tradition bearer in a different culture will
   tell a different story because their schema is different.

2. Structure is more stable than detail.
   Bartlett's stories retained the SEQUENCE of events
   while losing specific details.
   Propp's functions might survive better than
   character names or locations.

3. Transmission length matters.
   Ten iterations is the telephone game.
   But oral traditions have COMMUNITY CORRECTION --
   an audience that knows the story will intervene.
   Bartlett's experiment removed community correction.

4. The "War of the Ghosts" result is a WORST CASE:
   unfamiliar cultural material, no community correction,
   no mnemonic constraints (no meter, no formula).
   Fixed-text traditions (legal formula, sacred chant)
   are far more stable.
```

## The Version-Stability Spectrum

Not all oral tradition drifts equally. Jan Vansina's *Oral Tradition as History* (1985) classified oral forms by stability:

```
STABILITY SPECTRUM (Vansina)
==============================

MOST STABLE:
  Fixed-text forms
    Legal formula (word-for-word required for validity)
    Sacred recitation (error = ritual failure)
    Epic with strong mnemonic constraints (meter, formula)
  Why: community corrects deviations; error has consequences

INTERMEDIATE:
  Free-prose narrative
    Historical legend
    Epic in prose tradition
    Myth in non-ritual context
  Why: structure stable; surface drifts
  Pattern: "schema survives; detail drifts"

LEAST STABLE:
  Commentary and opinion
  Current event reporting
  Rumor
  Personal experience narrative
  Why: no mnemonic constraints; no community correction;
       context changes rapidly

IMPLICATION FOR HISTORIANS:
  Classify the tradition type before assessing reliability.
  A legal formula transmitted for 500 years may be
  more accurate than a personal recollection from last week.
```

## Conservative vs. Innovative Tradition Bearers

Individual variation in how tradition bearers treat their material:

```
BEARER TYPOLOGY
================

CONSERVATIVE BEARER
  Goal: faithful transmission of received tradition
  Strategy: stick closely to the version they learned
  Role perception: "guardian" of the tradition
  Innovation: minimized, marked as deviation if present
  Value in tradition: stability, authenticity
  Example: Homeric rhapsode (after fixation)

INNOVATIVE BEARER
  Goal: good performance for THIS audience
  Strategy: expand, abbreviate, incorporate new material
  Role perception: "artist" using traditional resources
  Innovation: unmarked -- presented as the tradition
  Value in tradition: vitality, adaptation
  Example: Avdo Medjedovic (Parry's guslar)

MIXED / COMMUNITY-STANDARD BEARER
  Most tradition bearers are somewhere between
  Conservative when material is sacred/legal
  Innovative when material is entertainment

ELITE BEARER (Vansina's "official tradition")
  Royal courts, religious institutions
  Tradition maintained with deliberate accuracy
  but: also deliberately shaped for political purposes
  Tension between accuracy and legitimacy
```

## Censorship in Oral Transmission

Oral traditions are subject to suppression and alteration by power structures:

```
CENSORSHIP MECHANISMS
======================

1. RELIGIOUS CENSORSHIP
   Christian missionaries collected and burned oral traditions
   OR rewrote them to align with Christian narrative
   Example: Grimm's tales were originally more sexually explicit;
            the Grimms' own editing cleaned them for Christian
            bourgeois audiences (1812 -> 1857 revision process)
   Example: Native American sacred narratives recorded only
            by male informants who excluded women's knowledge
            (Goody's gendered knowledge argument)

2. POLITICAL CENSORSHIP
   State control of historical narrative
   Colonial authorities dismissed Indigenous oral histories
   as unhistorical -- they preserved land rights, political claims
   Example: Aboriginal Australians' oral title to land
            denied legal standing until Eddie Mabo case (1992)
   Courts now accept oral tradition as evidence in land claims
   (Canada: Delgamuukw v. British Columbia 1997)

3. GENDERED CENSORSHIP
   Women's oral traditions systematically underrecorded
   Early folklorists (male) interviewed male informants
   Women's lullabies, birth songs, domestic narrative
   are underrepresented in canonical folklore collections
   Feminist folklore recovery: 1970s-present

4. SELF-CENSORSHIP
   Bearers omit material they judge inappropriate for
   the specific audience (children, outsiders, rival groups)
   Sacred material may be withheld from non-initiates
   This creates MULTIPLE LEVELS of a tradition:
     Public version (anyone can hear)
     Restricted version (initiates only)
     Most restricted (specialists only)
```

## Community Control Mechanisms

Oral tradition is not a free-for-all. Communities regulate it:

```
REGULATION MECHANISMS
======================

1. CORRECTION IN PERFORMANCE
   Audience intervention when version deviates
   "That's not how it goes" -- public correction
   Especially strong for:
     - Sacred or ritual material
     - Stories with political implications
     - Genealogies with property/status consequences

2. SPECIALIST MONOPOLY
   Some traditions are owned by specific roles:
     Griots: only griots may perform certain histories
     Brahmin priests: only Brahmins recite certain Vedic texts
     Clan elders: only elders know certain genealogies
   Prevents drift by limiting who can perform

3. INSTITUTIONAL PERFORMANCE OCCASIONS
   Fixed calendar occasions force regular rehearsal
   Festival recitation = community-wide calibration event
   Deviation is noticed because everyone knows the text

4. SACRED SANCTION
   Error in sacred recitation = ritual failure (or worse)
   Vedic recitation: 11 checks on correct pronunciation
   Any error requires starting from the beginning
   Mnemonic pressure + social consequence = stability

5. LEGAL FUNCTION
   Oral tradition with legal force is recalled accurately
   because inaccuracy has property consequences
   Common law "living memory" evidence
   Indigenous land rights oral testimony
```

## Oral Tradition in Legal Systems

```
LEGAL USES OF ORAL TRADITION
=============================

COMMON LAW EVIDENCE:
  "Living memory" principle: hearsay exceptions for
  statements made in the course of a business/community
  Custom demonstrated by oral testimony of community members
  "The hedge has always been the boundary" (village memory)

INDIGENOUS LAND RIGHTS:
  Canada: Delgamuukw v. British Columbia (1997)
    Supreme Court: oral histories of Gitxsan and Wet'suwet'en
    must be treated as evidence with equal weight to documents
    "Oral histories are not simply a literate culture's records
     in oral form. They serve different social functions."

Australia: Mabo v. Queensland (No 2) (1992)
    Overturned terra nullius doctrine
    Recognized Meriam people's oral traditions as evidence
    of connection to land for 100+ generations

New Zealand: Waitangi Tribunal (1975-present)
    Maori oral traditions accepted as historical evidence
    Waiata (songs) and oral histories used in treaty claims

IMPLICATION:
  Oral tradition shifts from "unreliable folklore" to
  legally admissible evidence when courts recognize
  that oral legal traditions are often MORE reliable
  than written records (which can be forged, lost,
  or produced by interested parties)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Bartlett's telephone game is the worst case.** His experiment removes all stabilizing factors: no mnemonic constraints, no community correction, unfamiliar material, one-time exposure. Real oral traditions that matter to the community drift far less.

**All oral tradition drifts at the surface level.** The schema — plot sequence, character functions, moral framework — is far more stable than names, dates, and details. Historians must distinguish schema-level claims from detail-level claims.

**Political censorship of oral tradition is ongoing.** It is not a historical phenomenon. Governments today suppress oral traditions that contradict state narratives or support land claims. The recording of oral tradition is itself a political act.

**Multiple versions are not a problem.** The existence of the Perrault and Grimm versions of Little Red Riding Hood is not a defect — it is how oral tradition works. Searching for the "original" version is usually a category error.

**"Telephone game" is often used to dismiss oral tradition.** This is intellectually lazy. The telephone game models the worst case (single-chain transmission, no community correction, unfamiliar material). It does not model religious recitation, court genealogy, or legal custom.
