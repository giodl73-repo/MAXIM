**The core insight of oral-formulaic theory**: oral epic poetry is not memorized and recited; it is recomposed at each performance using a systematic toolkit of formulas and themes. The singer is composer, not memorizer.

---

## Why This Field Exists

Literate cultures assume text is the primary form of communication — documents are produced, preserved, copied, and read. Oral cultures existed for 300,000 years of Homo sapiens before writing, and most human languages have never been written. The study of oral tradition reconstructs:

- How knowledge is stored without external inscription
- How long narratives (tens of thousands of lines) are composed in real time
- Why texts like the Iliad have the structural features they do
- Why similar tale types appear across unconnected cultures
- What happens to transmitted information over time

The discipline bridges linguistics, folklore, classical studies, anthropology, cognitive science, and archival studies.

## Core Theoretical Frameworks

### 1. Parry-Lord Oral-Formulaic Theory (Primary Framework)

```
MILMAN PARRY (1928-1935)
    |
    Fieldwork: Slavic guslars in Yugoslavia → sang epic without memorizing
    Dissertation: Homeric formulas are functional, not decorative
    Key: metrical necessity explains formula repetition
    |
    v
ALBERT LORD (1960 — "The Singer of Tales")
    |
    Systematized Parry's findings
    Formula: "a group of words regularly employed
              under the same metrical conditions
              to express a given essential idea"
    Theme: "groups of ideas regularly used in
            telling a tale in the formulaic style"
    |
    v
JOHN MILES FOLEY (1980-2012)
    Extended to Old English, South Slavic, Ancient Greek comparatively
    "Oral-derived text" distinction (not all old texts are primary oral)
    "Immanent art" — traditional referentiality (allusion without citation)
```

### 2. Walter Ong — Orality and Literacy (1982)

Nine characteristics of primary oral thought and expression:
1. **Additive** rather than subordinative (and...and...and)
2. **Aggregative** rather than analytic (not "soldier" but "brave soldier")
3. **Redundant** (repetition maintains thread without text reference)
4. **Conservative** (tradition bearer preserves what is sanctioned)
5. **Close to the human lifeworld** (abstract categories impractical without text)
6. **Agonistically toned** (verbal combat as a knowledge form)
7. **Empathetic and participatory** rather than distantly objective
8. **Homeostatic** (present-focus, past irrelevancies drop away)
9. **Situational** rather than abstract (thinking stays in context)

### 3. Jack Goody — The Domestication of the Savage Mind (1977)

Writing enables:
- Lists (non-narrative catalogues)
- Tables (bi-directional lookup)
- Formulas (algebraic, not just rhetorical)
- Retrospective re-reading (checking for contradiction)
- Syllogistic reasoning (abstract from context)

Critique: Goody over-generalizes; oral cultures have sophisticated logical structures (counter-argument from cognitive anthropology)

### 4. Vladimir Propp — Morphology of the Folktale (1928)

Russian fairy tales reduce to **31 invariant functions** in fixed sequence order. Not all functions present in every tale, but order never reverses. The abstraction is essentially a context-free grammar for narrative. (See 05-FOLKLORE.md for full treatment.)

## Primary Orality vs. Oral-Derived Texts

```
PRIMARY ORAL CULTURE
   No writing whatsoever
   Thought shaped by formulaic patterns
   Knowledge = what can be remembered
   Example: pre-literate Slavic guslars
        |
        v (writing introduced)
TRANSITIONAL / RESIDUAL ORALITY
   Writing exists but not widespread
   Oral composition habits persist in written texts
   "Oral-derived texts" — Homer, Beowulf, medieval chansons
   Formula density: 30-40% repeated phrases
        |
        v (literacy expands)
HIGH LITERACY
   Text is assumed primary medium
   Oral residue in folklore, gossip, rumor
   "Secondary orality" (Ong): radio, TV, internet
   New oral genres: podcast, talk radio, voicemail
        |
        v (networked digital)
SECONDARY ORALITY / DIGITAL ORALITY
   Text-based media with oral characteristics
   Participatory, formulaic (memes), agonistic (Twitter)
   Internet meme as oral formula analog
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Is Homer one poet or many? | Oral-formulaic theory makes the question wrong — each performance is a re-composition |
| Is an oral tradition accurate? | Stable structure, variable surface — facts drift, schema persists |
| Can oral cultures have complex thought? | Yes — Goody overreached; oral cultures have sophisticated logic in situational form |
| What is a formula? | Metrically fitting phrase reused across performances (not a cliché — functional unit) |
| What's the difference between myth and legend? | Myth: sacred, cosmological, set in other time/world; Legend: believed true, set in recent/here |
| What distinguishes oral history from oral tradition? | Oral history: recorded living memory (methodological); tradition: multi-generational transmission |

## Common Confusion Points

**Formula ≠ cliché.** In oral composition, a formula is a functional tool — a metrically specific, semantically essential unit that slots into a syntactic position. It is not lazy writing; it is a compositional resource.

**"Homer" as a name is not a problem for oral theory.** Whether a historical person named Homer existed is irrelevant to whether the epics were orally composed. The tradition is real; the individual biography is secondary.

**Oral tradition ≠ unreliable.** Structure and schema transmit with high fidelity; surface detail varies. Aboriginal Australians preserved accurate descriptions of coastal geography flooding events from 10,000+ years ago (Nunn & Reid 2016).

**Primary orality ≠ primitive.** Walter Ong was explicit: oral cultures are not cognitively inferior, they are differently organized. The characteristics of oral thought are adaptations to storing knowledge without external media.

**Oral-derived text ≠ oral text.** Beowulf is written. But its composition was shaped by oral tradition — its formulas, its themes, its digressive catalogues. "Oral-derived" is a precise technical term, not a synonym for "old."
