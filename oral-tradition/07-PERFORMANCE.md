## The Griot (Jeli): West African Hereditary Praise-Singer

### Role and Function

The **griot** (Wolof term; **jeli** in Mande languages) is among the most studied oral performer roles in the world:

```
JELI ROLE STRUCTURE
====================

HEREDITARY CASTE:
  Born into a jeli family
  Cannot become a jeli by choice (nor leave the role)
  Marries within the jeli caste
  Opposite caste (horon = free noble) cannot perform jeli functions
  This is not class subordination -- it is specialization
  The jeli holds power THROUGH knowledge

DOMAINS OF COMPETENCE:
  1. Genealogy: maintains lineages of noble families
     -> Can recite 40+ generations on demand
     -> Knowledge = property of specific patron families

  2. History: oral chronicle of kingdoms, battles, alliances
     -> "The jeli is the memory of the people" (common saying)
     -> Disputed historical claims resolved by jeli testimony

  3. Praise (jammu): public recitation of patron's lineage
     and achievements
     -> Social function: motivate, honor, shame, encourage

  4. Mediation: jeli can say what nobles cannot say to each other
     -> Diplomatic intermediary
     -> Can deliver insults or demands without consequence

  5. Music: kora (21-string harp-lute), balafon (xylophone),
     ngoni (lute), voice
     -> Music is inseparable from text
     -> Melody encodes genealogical information

POLITICAL POWER:
  A jeli who withholds praise or recites shameful genealogy
  can damage a patron's reputation publicly
  "Griots can make kings and unmake them"
  (Documented in Sundiata epic -- jeli's role in coronation)
```

### Jeli Knowledge System

```
JELI KNOWLEDGE ARCHITECTURE
==============================

PUBLIC LAYER: available to all
  Standard historical narratives
  Well-known genealogical lines
  Popular praise songs and formulas

RESTRICTED LAYER: patron-specific
  Detailed genealogy of patron family
  Embarrassing or powerful ancestral history
  Specific praise formulas for that lineage
  (paid access: patron pays for exclusive knowledge)

MOST RESTRICTED: inter-family
  Compromising information about rivals
  Sensitive historical events
  Strategic genealogical knowledge
  (may never be revealed; held as leverage)

KNOWLEDGE AS COMMODITY:
  Different patrons pay for different knowledge access
  The jeli who knows the most has the most power
  Information hoarding is structurally rational
```

## The Skald: Norse Court Poet

```
SKALDIC VERSE TRADITION
========================

Skalds: Norse/Icelandic court poets, 9th-13th centuries CE
Function: compose and perform praise poetry for kings and jarls
Context: formal court performance at feasts, battles, funerals

SKALDIC vs. EDDIC verse distinction:
  Eddic: simpler alliterative verse, mythological/heroic content,
         anonymous, collected in Poetic Edda (~1270)
  Skaldic: complex meter, kennings, named poets,
           specific historical occasion, court function

DROTTKVAETT (court meter):
  The dominant skaldic meter
  8 lines per stanza
  6 syllables per line
  Internal alliteration + rhyme within lines (not at line end)
  Each half-stanza = unit; stanzas link in complex chains

  Complexity example:
  Multiple simultaneous constraints:
    - Syllable count
    - Stress pattern
    - Initial alliteration (2-3 per line)
    - Internal rhyme (skothending or aohending)
    - Kenning conventions (no kenning repeats)
    - Syntactic entanglement (different clauses interlaced)

KENNING SYSTEM (skaldic):
  Kennings denser than in Beowulf
  Kennings stacked (kenning of kenning):
    sea = "whale road"
    warrior = "feeder of ravens" (ravens eat battle dead)
    sword = "wound-wand"
    Odin = "raven-god" -> "feeder of the raven-god's bird"
         = warrior (three-level kenning)
```

### Named Skalds

| Skald | Dates | Known for |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| Bragi Boddason | c.800 CE | Oldest named skald; Ragnarsdrápa |
| Egill Skallagrímsson | c.910-990 | Hövudlausn (Head-Ransom); Sonatorrek |
| Eyvindr Skáldaspillir | c.920-990 | "Skald-spoiler" — so good he eclipsed others |
| Snorri Sturluson | 1179-1241 | Prose Edda; codified skaldic system for posterity |

Snorri Sturluson's *Prose Edda* (1220) is the single most important source for both Norse mythology and skaldic poetics — he codified kenning conventions, listed acceptable kenning types, and provided the mythological context required to understand them.

## The Guslar: South Slavic Epic Singer

Parry's primary fieldwork subjects — the singers whose practice formed the empirical basis for oral-formulaic theory:

```
GUSLAR PROFILE
===============

Instrument: gusle (one-string bowed lute)
Context: Bosnia-Herzegovina, Serbia, Montenegro
Tradition: Christian (Serbian Orthodox) and Muslim (Bosnian)
           South Slavic epic tradition — overlapping subjects

PERFORMANCE OCCASION:
  Male-only gatherings (historically)
  Coffee house (kafana) performance
  Wedding feasts (several-hour performances)
  Funeral commemoration
  Market days
  Request performances (patron chooses subject)

REPERTOIRE:
  Cycle of Kosovo (1389 Battle of Kosovo vs. Ottoman)
  Wedding epics (Smailagic Meho cycle)
  Marko Kraljevic cycle (border warrior hero)
  Historical-mythological fusions

AVDO MEDJEDOVIC (1870-1953):
  Parry's most exceptional informant
  Illiterate but could compose epics of 12,000-80,000 lines
  "The Wedding of Smailagic Meho": 12,311 lines
    (performed for Parry over 2 days)
  The 80,000-line version of another epic:
    on hearing a song once, expanded it threefold
  Parry called him "the Homer of the 20th century"
  -- composition-in-performance at superhuman scale
```

## Performance Occasion and Composition Effect

The occasion determines what the singer produces:

```
OCCASION -> PERFORMANCE MAPPING
=================================

WEDDING FEAST:
  Long performance appropriate (several hours)
  Expansive themes (love, honor, union)
  Comic episodes welcome
  Audience is festive, patient, celebratory
  Singer maximizes: expands every theme fully

FUNERAL / COMMEMORATION:
  Shorter, more focused
  Themes: heroic death, lament, honor of dead
  Elegiac tone
  Audience: grief context, less patient for digression
  Singer minimizes: narrative skeleton

POLITICAL OCCASION (before king/patron):
  Praise of patron's lineage mandatory
  Historical narrative favoring patron's faction
  Singer self-censors content unfavorable to patron
  Risk: patron can punish offensive performance

MARKET / CASUAL:
  Audience freely arrives and departs
  Short episodes preferred (can start/stop)
  Entertaining over politically complex
  Singer adapts to staying audience

RITUAL OCCASION:
  Fixed-text form expected
  Deviation unacceptable
  Singer is reciter, not composer
```

## Audience Interaction and Composition Effect

The oral performer is not performing for a silent reading audience:

```
AUDIENCE AS CO-COMPOSER
========================

DIRECT INTERVENTION:
  Audience can (and does) request:
    - "Tell the part where..."
    - "Skip to the battle"
    - "Slower -- we missed that"
  Singer adapts in real time

APPLAUSE / SILENCE FEEDBACK:
  Sustained attention = expand this episode
  Restlessness = shorten or move on
  Vocalized approval = repeat that line/passage
  Singer reads the room continuously

CHALLENGE AND CONTEST:
  Another singer present may challenge a version
  "That's not how Avdo sings it"
  Competitive singing occasions (coffee house)
  Agonistic dynamics (Ong's characteristic #6)

COMMUNAL KNOWLEDGE CORRECTION:
  For historical/genealogical content:
  Members of the affected lineage may correct
  "My grandfather was there -- it was different"
  Creates accountability not present in written publication

IMPLICATION:
  Written text is FIXED; oral performance is RESPONSIVE.
  The audience shapes the text in real time.
  There is no "the text" -- only this performance, now.
```

## Embodied Knowledge in Performance

Oral performance is not just verbal — it is physical:

```
EMBODIED DIMENSIONS
====================

INSTRUMENTAL ACCOMPANIMENT:
  Gusle (guslar): drone string sets rhythmic framework
  Kora (jeli): 21-string harp, complex interlocking melodies
  Both: music is not background -- it IS the form
  Removing the music changes what is possible to say

VOICE QUALITY:
  Trained oral performers use specific vocal registers
  Tense/relaxed voice for specific characters or emotions
  Falsetto for supernatural characters
  Volume variation for emphasis and scene change

PHYSICAL GESTURE:
  Griots use specific gestures for genealogical recitation
  Hand movements encode relationship types
  Body orientation signals role shifts (different character voices)

SPATIAL ARRANGEMENT:
  Performer position vs. audience position is meaningful
  Movement in performance space has semantic content
  Some traditions: performer must face specific direction
    for sacred content

MEMORY IN THE BODY:
  Motor learning encodes verbal sequence
  Removing instrument can impair verbal recall
  (Cue removal: one mnemonic system triggers another)
  This is procedural memory (how to perform) interfacing
  with declarative memory (what the story says)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Griots are not simply "storytellers."** The jeli role is a hereditary political institution with legal, diplomatic, and economic functions. The English term "griot" (from Portuguese) flattens a complex social role into a romantic category.

**The gusle is not a guitar.** It is a one-string bowed lute, producing a drone-like tone. The musical function is rhythmic scaffolding, not melody. Parry's recordings preserve the actual sound.

**Skalds were not bards in the generic sense.** Skaldic verse is technically among the most demanding poetry ever composed — simultaneous constraints on syllable count, stress, alliteration, internal rhyme, and kenning use. Reading a drottkvaett stanza without annotation is genuinely difficult even for Old Norse scholars.

**"The Homer of the 20th century" is Parry's phrase for Medjedovic, not a comparison of quality.** The point is that Medjedovic is performing in an analogous way to how Homer (or the Homeric tradition) performed — composition-in-performance at exceptional scale.

**Oral performance occasions determine what the text says.** There is no version-independent text. What a singer performs at a wedding is not the same as what they perform at a funeral, even for "the same song." The text is always the text-for-this-occasion.
