## Plastic Degradation in the Environment

### What "Biodegradable" Actually Means

```
   PHOTODEGRADATION
   ─────────────────
   UV breaks C-C and C=O bonds
   Chains shorten → embrittlement → fragmentation
   NOT mineralization — still carbon polymer, just smaller
   PE in UV: 2–5 years to fragment (but fragments persist centuries)

   HYDROLYSIS
   ──────────
   Water cleaves ester/amide bonds
   PET, nylons, PU, PLA susceptible (hydrolyzable backbone)
   Rate: pH, temperature dependent
   PET bottle in ocean: ~450 years to full degradation (estimated)

   THERMAL OXIDATION
   ─────────────────
   O2 + heat → hydroperoxide → chain scission
   Compounds the photodegradation

   BIODEGRADATION (true)
   ─────────────────────
   Enzymes break bonds → monomers/oligomers → CO2 + H2O + biomass
   VERY slow for PE, PP (no enzyme recognition sites)
   Fast for PLA (industrial compost ≥ 58°C, specific microbiome)
   PHA: degrades in soil/water (months)
   Cellulose acetate: intermediate
```

### Persistence Estimates

| Polymer | Environment | Estimated degradation time |
|---------|-------------|---------------------------|
| PE film | Seawater | 200–400+ years (fragmentation, not mineralization) |
| HDPE pipe | Buried | 500+ years |
| PET bottle | Ocean | ~450 years |
| PVC pipe | Buried | 100–200 years |
| PP fishing net | Ocean | 600 years |
| Nylon fishing net | Ocean | 40–600 years |
| PLA (certified compostable) | Industrial compost | 3–6 months |
| PLA | Cold ocean | 100+ years (barely different from PE) |
| PHA | Soil/marine | Months to ~5 years |

**Key insight**: Under field conditions (cold, dark ocean), no commodity polymer
degrades on human timescales. UV-driven fragmentation just makes smaller pieces.

## Microplastics: Definition and Sources

### Size Classification

```
   MACROPLASTIC  > 5 mm
   MESOPLASTIC   1–5 mm
   MICROPLASTIC  1 µm – 5 mm   ← primary regulatory/research focus
   NANOPLASTIC   < 1 µm        ← emerging concern, analytical challenge
```

### Primary vs. Secondary Microplastics

```
   PRIMARY (manufactured as micro-scale):
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   Nurdles (resin pellets, 3–5 mm) — industrial feedstock spill
   Microbeads in cosmetics (now banned in many markets — PE, PP, PS)
   Industrial abrasives
   Road marking paint (glass beads, polymer particles)
   Synthetic textile fibers (~35% of primary MPs by source)

   SECONDARY (fragmentation from macro-plastic):
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────
   Tire wear: ~1.5 million t/yr (largest single source in many studies)
   Agricultural mulch film fragmentation
   Marine debris photo-UV fragmentation
   Littered single-use packaging
   Synthetic textiles washing (~0.5–1.5 million t/yr)
```

### Global Accumulation Estimates

Annual input to ocean: ~8–14 million metric tons (Jambeck et al., Lebreton et al.)
Cumulative ocean plastic (2023): ~150–200 million metric tons
Deep ocean and seabed: may hold 10× more than surface ocean
Microplastics in deep sea sediment: up to 1.9 million fibers/m²

```
   WHERE OCEAN PLASTIC CONCENTRATES:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Surface gyres: ~10–30% of total
   Seafloor sediment: ~70–90% of total
   Deep sea trenches (Mariana): fibers at 10,890m depth confirmed

   The "Great Pacific Garbage Patch":
   Not a solid island — a diffuse particle field
   Highest concentration: ~300,000 pieces/km²
   Particles mostly < 5mm, many microplastic size
   Area: ~1.6 million km² (France × 3)
```

## Environmental Fate and Ecological Effects

### Physical Effects

```
   MACROPLASTIC EFFECTS:
   ─────────────────────
   Marine megafauna entanglement (whales, turtles, seals)
   Seabird / sea turtle ingestion → gut blockage
   Ghost fishing (lost nets continue catching)
   Ship propeller fouling

   MICROPLASTIC INGESTION:
   ──────────────────────
   Filter feeders (mussels, oysters, copepods) ingest particles
   Transfer up food chain: zooplankton → fish → marine mammals
   Size-dependent: < 1 µm particles may cross gut/cell membranes
   Physical gut blockage, false satiety (reduced feeding), starvation
```

### Chemical Effects

```
   PLASTIC IS A CHEMICAL CARRIER:
   ───────────────────────────────
   1. Additive leaching: plasticizers (phthalates), flame retardants,
      antioxidants, stabilizers → released as plastic weathers
      DEHP from PVC: endocrine disruption documented in fish

   2. Hydrophobic organic pollutant concentration:
      PCBs, PAHs, DDT, dioxins from ocean water
      MPs concentrate these at 10³–10⁶ × ambient seawater
      → "Trojan horse" delivery to gut when ingested

   3. Additive mixtures in nanoplastics:
      When MP → NP (< 1 µm), surface area increases dramatically
      More leaching per gram, potential cellular uptake
```

### Human Exposure

```
   CONFIRMED HUMAN EXPOSURE ROUTES:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Seafood consumption (shellfish especially): ~11,000 MPs/year (WHO est.)
   Drinking water (both bottled and tap): MPs detected in all samples
   Salt: MPs found in sea salt globally
   Beer: MPs found in tap-water-brewed beer
   Air inhalation: indoor fibers, outdoor urban PM

   HUMAN TISSUE DETECTIONS:
   ─────────────────────────
   Blood (2022 study, van Dijk et al.): PET, PS, PE in 77% of donors
   Placentas (2020): MP detected in fetal side
   Lungs: MP fibers in 87% of surgical samples (multiple studies)
   Colon/intestine: MPs detected via endoscopy
   Heart tissue (2023): MPs detected in coronary artery disease patients

   HEALTH EFFECTS (emerging, not fully established):
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Inflammatory response to inert particles (as with other PM)
   Endocrine disruption potential (chemical payload)
   Mechanistic studies in vitro and animal models: DNA damage,
   oxidative stress, gut microbiome disruption
   Long-term human epidemiology: ongoing
```

## The Recycling Reality

### The Cascade Problem

```
   GLOBAL PLASTIC WASTE (2019, Geyer et al. updated):
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Total ever produced (1950–2019): ~9.2 billion tonnes
   Of this:
     Recycled:     ~9%  (~830 Mt)
     Incinerated:  ~12% (~1.1 Gt)
     In landfill:  ~49%
     Environment:  ~30%

   Of "recycled":
     Only ~7–8% truly mechanically recycled in closed-loop
     Rest downcycled (lower quality, eventually landfill)
     Chemical recycling (pyrolysis, gasification): <1% of plastic
```

### Why Only PET and HDPE Get Recycled

```
   RECYCLING ECONOMICS:
   ─────────────────────
   PET: high virgin price ($1.50–2/kg), well-established collection,
        single-stream (bottles), bottle-to-bottle possible
   HDPE: large-volume, homogeneous (bottles), clean MRF stream

   PS: low virgin price, lightweight (high volume low mass), brittle
       (breaks in collection) → collection uneconomic in most regions
   PVC: cannot mix with other streams (HCl release in pyrolysis)
   Multi-layer films: laminated PE+PP+tie layers → unrecyclable by design
   ABS: many sources (electronics often contain BFRs — hazardous)
```

### Chemical Recycling — Status

```
   PYROLYSIS (thermal depolymerization):
   ───────────────────────────────────────
   Heat plastic to 400–600°C without O2 → pyrolysis oil
   Oil quality: variable, contaminants, need upgrading
   Scale: limited, expensive vs. virgin
   Reality check: ~0.1% of global plastic capacity

   ENZYMATIC HYDROLYSIS (PET):
   ────────────────────────────
   PETase enzyme (Ideonella sakaiensis, engineered)
   Cleaves PET → TPA + EG (monomers)
   Commercial: Carbios (France) plant operational 2023
   Requires pre-processing, not all PET grades work
   True closed-loop chemical recycling — bottle-to-monomer-to-bottle

   GLYCOLYSIS/ALCOHOLYSIS (PET, PU):
   ────────────────────────────────────
   Industrial glycolysis of PET: EG + PET → BHET oligomers → repolymerize
   PU: glycolysis recovers polyols for reuse
   Commercial at scale for bottle-grade PET in some operations
```

```

                   Brazil, Vietnam, Bangladesh, Nigeria, Thailand
```

## Tire Wear Microplastics

Tire wear particles (TWP) are the largest source of microplastics in waterways in industrialized countries — larger than all other sources combined in urban runoff.

```
   TIRE WEAR PARTICLE PATHWAY
   ───────────────────────────
   GENERATION:
   SBR/NR rubber compound + carbon black + silica + steel belt
   ~1.5 kg of tire mass lost per tire over ~50,000 km service life
   → ~140,000 metric tons TWP/yr in US; ~500,000 metric tons/yr globally

   PARTICLE CHARACTERISTICS:
   Size range: 1 µm – 1 mm (most 100–200 µm); classified as MPs
   Composition: vulcanized SBR/NR rubber + carbon black (45–65 wt%)
               + ZnO residue + benzothiazole, amine antioxidants (leachable)
   Density: ~1.2 g/cm³ (sinks in freshwater)
   Persistence: highly resistant to degradation; no biodegradation pathway

   TRANSPORT PATHWAY:
   Road surface → stormwater runoff (first flush: high concentration
   after dry period) → storm drains → surface water → rivers
     ├── Sedimentation in urban waterways (most TWP — river bed reservoir)
     └── Dissolved + colloidal fraction → estuary → coastal ocean

   ECOTOXICOLOGY:
   ZnO from tire compounds: toxic to aquatic invertebrates at µg/L
   6-PPD-quinone (antiozonant reaction product): acutely lethal to
     coho salmon at ~0.1 µg/L — identified cause of Pacific NW
     urban runoff salmon die-offs

   STORMWATER MANAGEMENT:
   Bioretention cells (rain gardens): >90% TWP retention
   Vegetated swales: 50–70% retention
   Catch basin inserts: ~30% (coarse-only)
```

## Regulatory Frameworks

```
   KEY REGULATORY FRAMEWORKS
   ───────────────────────────

   EU SINGLE-USE PLASTICS DIRECTIVE (SUPD, EU 2019/904, effective July 2021):
   Bans: 10 SUP categories (cutlery, plates, straws, stirrers, balloon sticks,
         cotton buds, oxo-degradable plastics)
   EPR: food containers, cups, wrappers, tobacco filters, wet wipes, balloons
   Target: 90% collection rate for plastic bottles by 2029 (deposit return)

   EXTENDED PRODUCER RESPONSIBILITY (EPR):
   Core concept: producer bears end-of-life cost, not consumer/municipality
   EU: Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive — producers pay into PRO
       (Producer Responsibility Organization) by packaging weight placed on market
   US: state-level only; Maine, Oregon, Colorado, California enacted 2021–2023
   Design impact: EPR fees penalize difficult-to-recycle plastics (PVC, PVDC,
     multi-layer laminates) → economic pressure toward design-for-recycling

   BASEL CONVENTION PLASTIC WASTE AMENDMENTS (2019, effective 2021):
   Controls transboundary movement of hazardous/mixed plastic waste
   Effect: restricts exporting contaminated/mixed plastic waste to
           countries without adequate recycling infrastructure
   Significance: closed the export loophole that moved ~70% of global
                 plastic "recycling" to SE Asia; forces domestic solutions

   PFAS IN PACKAGING (EU + US EPA, 2021–2024):
   EU Food Contact Materials reform: banning PFAS in paper/board food contact
   US EPA: significant new use rules; food packaging phase-out timeline
   Impact: fluoropolymer grease barriers being replaced with bio-based
           alternatives (PLA, starch, chitosan) — performance and cost penalties
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Biodegradable plastic" in the ocean**: PLA "biodegrades" at 58°C+, high
humidity, industrial compost. In a cold ocean at 4°C: negligible degradation
for decades to centuries. "Compostable" and "marine biodegradable" are entirely
different certifications. Only PHA is certified marine biodegradable.

**Plastic fragmentation ≠ mineralization**: When PE fragments into
microplastics, the carbon is still polymer. It hasn't been mineralized to CO2
and water. Photodegradation just reduces particle size — it doesn't eliminate
the plastic from the environment.

**Recycling symbols convey type, not recyclability**: A #1 PET lid that is
black (carbon-black colored) is NOT sorted at optical MRF sensors — they can't
see it. A #1 clear PET bottle is recycled everywhere. Same polymer, completely
different fate at end-of-life.
