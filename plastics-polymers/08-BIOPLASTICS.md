## Polylactic Acid (PLA)

The dominant bio-based biodegradable polymer.

### Production Chain

```
   CORN / SUGARCANE / CASSAVA
         |
         | Fermentation (Lactobacillus)
         ↓
   LACTIC ACID (C3H6O3)
         |
         | Condensation oligomers → depolymerization
         ↓
   LACTIDE (cyclic dimer)
         |
         | Ring-opening polymerization (Sn(Oct)2 catalyst)
         ↓
   PLA  (poly-lactic acid) — Mw 50,000–400,000 g/mol
```

### PLA Properties vs. Conventional Polymers

| Property | PLA | PET | PS |
|----------|-----|-----|----|
| Tg (°C) | 55–60 | 80 | 100 |
| Tm (°C) | 145–175 (crystalline) | 260 | amorphous |
| Density (g/cm³) | 1.24 | 1.38 | 1.05 |
| Tensile (MPa) | 50–70 | 55–75 | 40–50 |
| Elongation (%) | 3–6% | 30–300% | 1–3% |
| Barrier to CO2 | Moderate | Good | Poor |
| Water vapor barrier | Moderate | Good | Moderate |
| Heat resistance | Poor — Tg 55°C | Good | Moderate |
| Food-contact approved | Yes | Yes | Yes |

**PLA's Achilles heel**: Tg = 55–60°C. A PLA cup holding hot coffee (>60°C)
will deform. A PLA food service tray left in a hot car (>60°C) will sag.
This severely limits food service applications beyond cold drinks.

### Stereocomplex PLA (scPLA)

PLLA (L-lactic acid) + PDLA (D-lactic acid) → form stereocomplex crystallites.
Tm of stereocomplex: ~230°C vs. 175°C for homopolymer.
Tg: ~65°C. Better heat resistance and barrier, but D-lactic acid is more
expensive and conversion is not straightforward.

### Biodegradation: The Important Caveat

```
   PLA BIODEGRADATION CONDITIONS REQUIRED:
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   Temperature: ≥ 58°C (industrial compost standard)
   Humidity: > 50%
   Microbial community: specific compost organisms
   Time: 3–6 months to 90% mineralization

   IN COLD OCEAN (4°C):
   ─────────────────────
   Hydrolysis rate: negligible
   PLA in seawater at 4–20°C: >100 years to significant degradation
   IDENTICAL degradation trajectory to PE at ocean conditions

   IN HOME COMPOST:
   ─────────────────
   5–20°C, lower microbial activity
   PLA: 2–5+ YEARS — not practically home-compostable
   Even "home-compostable" certified PLA may take years in practice

   INDUSTRIAL COMPOST:
   ────────────────────
   Works as designed — 3–6 months
   BUT: requires industrial composting infrastructure
   ~85% of PLA ends up in landfill, recycling streams, or environment
   where it provides no benefit over conventional plastic
```

### PLA in Current Use

- Cold drink cups, lids, cutlery (Europe, food service)
- 3D printing filament (most common FDM material by volume)
- Agricultural mulch film (most practical application — composted in field)
- Biodegradable packaging film (PLA/PBAT blends)
- Medical: sutures, screws, drug delivery (controlled hydrolysis intentional)

## Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA)

A family of bio-based, biodegradable polyesters produced by microbial
fermentation. The only polymer family certified for **marine biodegradation**.

### Microbial Production

```
   Carbon source (glucose, fatty acids, methane, CO2)
         |
         | Bacteria under nutrient stress conditions
         | (e.g., Ralstonia eutropha, Pseudomonas)
         ↓
   PHA granules accumulated in bacterial cytoplasm
         |
         | Cell lysis, solvent extraction / dry fractionation
         ↓
   PHA polymer powder / pellets
```

### The PHA Family

| Polymer | Monomer units | Tg (°C) | Tm (°C) | Properties |
|---------|---------------|---------|---------|------------|
| PHB | 3-hydroxybutyrate | 4 | 175 | Stiff, brittle, high xtal |
| PHBV | HB + HV copolymer | –1 to 4 | 110–160 | Tougher, more flexible |
| PHBHX | HB + HHx | –1 to 4 | 50–110 | Rubbery grades possible |
| P4HB | 4-hydroxybutyrate | –50 | 55 | Sutures, medical |
| PHO | 3-hydroxyoctanoate | –35 | 55 | Elastomeric |
| Medium-chain PHA | C6–C14 | –50 to –10 | Low | Elastomeric |

PHB (the simplest) is too brittle and too expensive for most packaging. PHBV
and PHBHX copolymers (Danimer Nodax, Tianan ENMAT) have better properties.

### PHA Cost and Scale

```
   PHA production cost (2023): ~$3–7/kg (vs. PP ~$1.1/kg)
   Target for broad adoption: <$2/kg — not yet achieved

   Capacity (2023): ~60,000 t/yr worldwide
   Major producers: Danimer Scientific (US), Tianan (China),
                    Kaneka (Japan), Newlight Technologies (US)

   Scale challenge: fermentation is land/water/energy intensive
   Sugar crops → PHAs: ~2–5 kg PHA per 10 kg sugar (yield depends on organism)
```

### Marine Biodegradation — The Real Differentiator

```
   PHA in marine conditions:
   ──────────────────────────
   PHB/PHBV: >95% mineralization in 1.5–5 years in ocean sediment
   P4HB: months in seawater at 25°C

   This makes PHA genuinely valuable for:
   - Fishing gear (lost nets and lines)
   - Agricultural mulch near water bodies
   - Marine survey equipment
   - Aquaculture applications
```

## Drop-in Bio-based Polymers (Not Biodegradable)

These use bio-based feedstocks but produce chemically identical polymers:

```
   BIO-PE (bio-HDPE):
   ───────────────────
   Sugarcane ethanol → ethylene → PE (Braskem, Brazil)
   Chemically: identical to fossil PE
   Carbon negative feedstock (sugarcane absorbs CO2)
   But: Not biodegradable (still PE)
   Price premium: ~15–30% over fossil PE
   Braskem sells ~200,000 t/yr to P&G, Hasbro, Toyota partners

   BIO-PET (partial):
   ──────────────────
   Bio-MEG (ethylene glycol from sugarcane) + fossil TPA → 30% bio-PET
   Used by Coca-Cola "PlantBottle" — partial bio content
   100% bio-PET: bio-PX (bio-based p-xylene) → bio-TPA required
   Virent, Anellotech working on bio-PX pathways (not yet commercial scale)

   BIO-PA (bio-based nylons):
   ──────────────────────────
   PA-11 (Rilsan, Arkema): 11-aminoundecanoic acid from castor oil
   PA-6,10: sebacic acid from castor oil + fossil hexamethylene diamine
   Used in: automotive fuel systems, oil & gas flexible pipes, electronics
   Price: $5–12/kg — justified for performance/sustainability dual claim
```

## Thermoplastic Starch (TPS) and Blends

Native starch (corn, wheat, potato) is hydrophilic, brittle, and water-sensitive.
Plasticization with glycerol or water → TPS — still poor compared to synthetic.

Real application: blended with PBAT (fossil-based but biodegradable) at 30–70%
starch content → Mater-Bi (Novamont), EcoFlex+starch (BASF).

```
   PBAT (polybutylene adipate-co-terephthalate):
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────
   Fossil-based, but meets EN 13432 industrial compost standard
   Flexible film, good elongation (~700%)
   Blended with PLA or starch for property balance
   PBAT/PLA (80/20 to 60/40): flexible compostable bags, mulch
   PBAT/TPS (70/30 to 30/70): various packaging applications
```

## The Reality Check: LCA and System-Level Thinking

```
   BIO-BASED ≠ LOWER EMISSIONS:
   ──────────────────────────────
   PLA full LCA:
   GWP: ~2.0–4.5 kg CO2eq/kg PLA (cradle to factory gate)
   Fossil PE: ~2.0–3.0 kg CO2eq/kg PE
   Overlap depends on land use, fertilizer, processing efficiency

   PHA LCA:
   GWP: ~3.5–8.0 kg CO2eq/kg PHA (fermentation energy-intensive)
   Clearly higher than fossil polymers on pure GHG metric

   Bio-PE (sugarcane):
   GWP: –0.5 to +0.5 kg CO2eq/kg (nearly carbon neutral)
   Best performing bio-based plastic on climate metric

   KEY INSIGHT: "Bio-based" does not automatically mean "lower carbon."
   Context (land use, energy source, transport) matters enormously.
```

### The End-of-Life Problem

```
   COMPOSTABLE PLASTIC REALITY CHAIN:
   ────────────────────────────────────
   Step 1: Consumer correctly identifies as compostable (often fails)
   Step 2: Correct bin used (industrial compost bin vs. recycling vs. landfill)
   Step 3: Local industrial composting infrastructure exists and accepts it
   Step 4: Not contaminated with conventional plastics (optical sorting needed)
   Step 5: Composted at ≥58°C, ≥2 weeks → actual biodegradation

   Failure at any step → compostable plastic in landfill or ocean
   → provides no benefit vs. conventional plastic

   California AB 1201 (2022): Banned use of "compostable" label unless
   accepted by 60% of California composters — most products cannot meet this.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Application | Best bioplastic | Notes |
|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Cold drink cup / lid | PLA | If industrial compost infrastructure exists |
| Hot food container | PHA or scPLA | PLA too low Tg for hot food |
| Agricultural mulch | PLA/PBAT blend, PHA | Composted in field |
| Marine-relevant item | PHA | Only marine-certified option |
| Drop-in PE replacement (not degradable) | Bio-PE | Carbon footprint claim |
| Drop-in PET (partial bio) | Bio-PET (30%) | PlantBottle-style |
| 3D printing (hobby/low-temp) | PLA | Easy processing, cheap |
| Medical suture / screw | P4HB, PDLA/PLLA, PCL | Controlled degradation |
| Flexible packaging film | PLA/PBAT or starch/PBAT | Compostable if infrastructure |

## Common Confusion Points

**PLA is not ocean-degradable**: This cannot be overstated. PLA marketed as
"green" or "natural" is just as persistent as PE in ocean conditions. Only PHA
carries marine biodegradability certification.

**Compostable ≠ recyclable**: Mixing PLA cups into PET recycling stream
contaminates the PET melt. PLA and PET cannot be separated by standard MRF
optical sorting (both clear, similar density). Compostables must be kept out
of recycling streams.

**Bio-based carbon content is not the right proxy for sustainability**: A
product can be 100% bio-based and have higher lifecycle GWP than its fossil
equivalent (if production is inefficient or land-use-intensive). LCA beats
feedstock origin as the metric.

**"Degradable" additives in conventional plastics (d2w, EPI)**: These
oxo-degradable additives are controversial. They cause PE to fragment faster
but the carbon remains as microplastics. EU banned them in single-use plastics
(2021). Not the same as compostable or biodegradable.
