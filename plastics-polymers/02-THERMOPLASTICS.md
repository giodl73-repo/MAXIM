## Polyethylene (PE)

### The Grade Landscape

```
    PRESSURE / CATALYST
         |
    HIGH P, FRP ──────────────────────► LDPE
    (autoclave, ~2000 atm)               Low-density PE
    Branches: short + long chain         d = 0.910–0.930 g/cm³
    χ_c = 40–55%                         Tg = –100°C  Tm = 110°C
                                         Flexible film, bread bags
         |
    LOW P, Ziegler-Natta ─────────────► HDPE
    (50–80°C, 10–30 atm)                 High-density PE
    Near-linear                          d = 0.940–0.970 g/cm³
    χ_c = 65–80%                         Tg = –120°C  Tm = 130–135°C
                                         Pipes, bottles, containers
         |
    LOW P, Comonomer (butene/hexene) ──► LLDPE
    Ziegler-Natta or metallocene         Linear LDPE
    Short branches only, no LCB         d = 0.915–0.940 g/cm³
    χ_c = 35–60%                         Stretch film, rotomolding
         |
    Very high MW ─────────────────────► UHMWPE
    (Mn > 1,000,000 g/mol)               d = 0.930–0.935 g/cm³
    Cannot injection mold — sinter       Prosthetic joints, ropes,
    or ram extrude                       ballistic panels
```

### PE Structure–Property Summary

| Grade | MFI (g/10min) | Tensile (MPa) | Key property | Main use |
|-------|---------------|---------------|--------------|----------|
| LDPE | 0.2–20 | 8–17 | Flexible, clarity | Film, coatings |
| LLDPE | 0.5–5 | 15–30 | Puncture resistance | Stretch wrap, liner |
| HDPE | 0.1–5 | 20–37 | Stiff, chemical resist | Pipe, bottle, crate |
| UHMWPE | N/A (no melt flow) | 35–45 | Wear, impact | Bearing, armor, joint |

## Polypropylene (PP)

### Tacticity Drives Everything

```
   ATACTIC (aPP)    — waxy, low Tg, poor properties → byproduct
   ISOTACTIC (iPP)  — commercial PP, χ_c = 60–70%, Tm = 160–170°C
   SYNDIOTACTIC     — specialty, slightly different Tm
```

Commercial PP is isotactic, made with Ziegler-Natta or metallocene catalysts.

### PP Grades

```
   HOMOPOLYMER     — Highest stiffness, Tg ~ –10°C
                      brittleness issue below 0°C
                      Fibers, rigid packaging, caps

   RANDOM          — Comonomer (ethylene ~5%) lowers Tm, more transparent
COPOLYMER           "clear PP" for food containers, medical
                      Tm ~ 140–155°C

   IMPACT          — Ethylene-propylene rubber (EPR) dispersed in iPP matrix
COPOLYMER           Core-shell rubber particles
                      Toughens below 0°C
                      Automotive bumpers, luggage, toolboxes
```

### PP vs. PE Comparison

| Property | PP | HDPE |
|----------|----|----|
| Density (g/cm³) | 0.90–0.91 | 0.94–0.97 |
| Tg (°C) | –10 | –100 to –120 |
| Tm (°C) | 160–170 | 130–135 |
| Max service T (°C) | ~110–130 | ~80–100 |
| Stiffness (GPa) | 1.5–2.0 | 0.6–1.2 |
| Chemical resistance | Excellent | Excellent |
| Fatigue (live hinge) | Excellent | Good |
| Cost | ~$1.1/kg | ~$1.1/kg |

PP's **living hinge** property: Oriented PP film can flex ~10⁷ cycles without
failure — used in flip-top caps everywhere.

## Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC)

### The Chlorine Complication

Repeat unit: –(CH2–CHCl)n–
35% chlorine by mass. Atactic (amorphous). Tg ~ 80°C — rigid at room temperature.
The chlorine makes it inherently flame-retardant and resistant to weathering.

**The plasticizer story**:

```
   RIGID PVC (uPVC)                  FLEXIBLE PVC
   ─────────────────                 ─────────────
   No plasticizer                    Plasticizer added (20–40 phr)
   Tg ~ 80°C                         Tg depressed to –30 to –10°C
   Stiff, dense, durable             Soft, leathery, flexible
   Pipes (DIN, ISO, ASTM)            Wire insulation, flooring
   Window frames                     Tubing, medical bags
   Siding, guttering                 Raincoats, inflatable toys
```

Plasticizers (discussed fully in 06-ADDITIVES.md) are typically phthalates
(DEHP, DINP, DINCH) or adipates — small molecules that insert between PVC
chains, increasing free volume and lowering Tg.

### PVC Stability Issues

PVC degrades above 100°C, releasing HCl gas (autocatalytic). Processing
requires **heat stabilizers** (Ca/Zn, organotin, or lead — lead now banned
in most markets).

```
   PVC at 200°C (processing temp):
   –(CH2–CHCl)n– ──► –(CH=CH)– + HCl   [dehydrochlorination]
                          |
                      Polyene sequence
                          |
                      Yellow → orange → brown → black discoloration
                          |
                      Autocatalytic (HCl catalyzes further HCl release)
```

Stabilizers work by:
1. Reacting with HCl (consuming the catalyst)
2. Substituting labile Cl atoms before they eliminate
3. UV absorption (outdoor stability)

### PVC Market

Largest-volume thermoplastic globally after PE and PP. ~44 million t/yr.
~68% used in construction (pipe, window, flooring, wire). Extraordinary
durability — PVC pipe installed in the 1950s still in service.

## Polystyrene (PS)

### The Transparency–Brittleness Tradeoff

Repeat unit: –(CH2–CHPh)n–
Atactic. 100% amorphous. Tg ~ 100°C. Crystal-clear, stiff, brittle.

```
   GENERAL PURPOSE PS (GPPS)
   ─────────────────────────
   Transparent, stiff (E ~ 3.5 GPa), brittle (εbreak < 3%)
   Petri dishes, CD cases, disposable cutlery, cosmetics packaging

   HIGH-IMPACT PS (HIPS)
   ─────────────────────
   Rubber-toughened: polybutadiene (PBD) particles dispersed in PS matrix
   Loses transparency, gains toughness
   Yogurt cups, refrigerator liners, printer cartridges

   EXPANDED PS (EPS) — "Styrofoam"
   ────────────────────────────────
   GPPS + blowing agent (pentane) + steam expansion
   ~98% air by volume, density 0.01–0.05 g/cm³
   Excellent thermal insulator (λ ~ 0.033 W/m·K)
   Coffee cups, fish boxes, building insulation boards

   EXTRUDED PS FOAM (XPS)
   ──────────────────────
   Continuous extrusion with CO2/HCFC blowing
   Denser than EPS, better moisture resistance
   Foundation insulation ("blue board", "pink board")
```

### PS vs. ABS

ABS = acrylonitrile-butadiene-styrene terpolymer. Made by rubber-toughening SAN
(styrene-acrylonitrile) with polybutadiene. The three components give:
- A (acrylonitrile): chemical resistance, heat resistance, hardness
- B (butadiene): toughness (Tg –80°C rubber phase)
- S (styrene): stiffness, processability, cost

```
   ABS properties vs. HIPS:

                    HIPS        ABS
   Tensile (MPa)   15–25       35–55
   Impact (J/m)    80–150      200–400
   Tg (°C)         ~100        100–110
   Chemical res     Poor        Moderate
   Cost (vs GPPS)  +15%        +50%

   ABS dominates: LEGO bricks, dashboards, power tool housings,
                  3D printing (FDM), luggage
```

## Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET)

### Multiple Lives of One Polymer

Same repeat unit: –[OC-C6H4-CO-O-CH2-CH2-O]n–
Tg = 80°C, Tm = 260°C. Semi-crystalline or amorphous depending on processing.

```
   AMORPHOUS PET (aPET)
   ─────────────────────
   Quench melt fast → Tg inhibits crystallization
   Crystal clear, rigid at room T (below Tg)
   Thermoform trays, "RPET" packaging

   BIAXIALLY ORIENTED PET (BOPET / Mylar)
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   Extrude → biaxially stretch (3–5×) at Tg+20°C
   χ_c ~ 25–40% (oriented crystallites)
   Excellent barrier, high tensile strength
   Food packaging film, capacitor film, photographic film

   INJECTION STRETCH BLOW MOLDED (ISBM) — THE BOTTLE
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Inject → preform → reheat → stretch+blow
   χ_c ~ 20–30%, wall alignment
   CO2 barrier: critical for carbonated drinks
   ~85 billion PET bottles/year globally

   PET FIBER ("Polyester")
   ───────────────────────
   Melt spin → draw 4–5× → crystallize
   Largest-volume synthetic fiber globally
   Clothing, carpet, tire cord, geotextile

   ENGINEERING PET (GF-PET)
   ─────────────────────────
   30% glass fiber reinforcement
   Connector bodies, under-hood automotive
```

### PET Bottle Physics

```
   Preform (test-tube shape)
         |
         | Reheat to Tg+40–60°C
         ↓
   Stretch rod elongates axially (×2.5–3)
         |
         | Blowing air (2–4 MPa) expands radially (×2–3.5)
         ↓
   Final bottle — biaxially oriented crystalline wall
         |
         | CO2 permeability: ~0.07 cm³·mm/(m²·day·atm)
         | Shelf life: 6–12 months for carbonation
```

| Application | Best choice | Why |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Grocery bag / film | LDPE / LLDPE | Flexible, tough film |

## Common Confusion Points

**LDPE vs. LLDPE**: LDPE uses high-pressure FRP; LLDPE uses low-pressure
Ziegler-Natta with comonomer incorporation. LLDPE has better puncture
resistance and tensile strength. Most stretch wrap is now LLDPE or blends.

**PP density < PE?** Yes — PP (0.91 g/cm³) is less dense than HDPE (0.95)
despite the methyl side groups, because the side groups disrupt packing.
Lightest commodity thermoplastic.

**PVC is the only commodity plastic that needs stabilizers to process**:
Without Ca/Zn or organotin stabilizers, PVC would degrade before reaching
melt temperature. This complexity is why PVC compounding is specialized.

**PET bottle vs. PET tray**: Both start the same. Bottle → ISBM → oriented,
partial crystallinity. Tray → quench fast → amorphous, clear. If you heat an
amorphous PET tray above 80°C (microwave), it goes rubbery. Crystallized PET
(CPET) trays are deliberately crystallized to white, enabling oven use to 220°C.
