# Agriculture — Overview

## The Big Picture

Agriculture is humanity's single largest transformation of the biosphere — covering ~50% of habitable land, using ~70% of freshwater withdrawals, responsible for ~25% of greenhouse gas emissions, and feeding 8 billion people. It is also the most consequential applied science: the Neolithic revolution enabled civilization; the Green Revolution prevented a Malthusian catastrophe in the 20th century. The next transformation must feed 10 billion by 2050 with a fraction of the environmental footprint.

## The Neolithic Revolution — 10,000 BCE Transition

Domestication of plants and animals (~10,000–5,000 BCE) independently in multiple regions:

```
DOMESTICATION CENTERS (Vavilov's centers + modern additions):

FERTILE CRESCENT (~10,000 BCE):     SW Asia; wheat (emmer, einkorn), barley,
                                     lentils, peas, goats, sheep, cattle, pigs

CHINA (~7,000–5,000 BCE):           Rice (O. sativa), millet, soybeans, pigs, chickens

MESOAMERICA (~7,000 BCE):           Maize (from teosinte), squash, beans,
                                     turkeys, chili peppers

SOUTH AMERICA (~5,000 BCE):         Potato, quinoa, tomato, cacao, llama, alpaca

AFRICA (~5,000 BCE):                Sorghum, cowpeas, pearl millet

NEW GUINEA (~7,000 BCE):            Taro, yam, sugarcane (early)
```

**Why agriculture?** — Not because it was obviously better than foraging (foragers had more leisure time, more diverse diet, better health in many metrics). Probable drivers: population pressure + climate change (Younger Dryas cold snap 12,800–11,700 BCE pushed people toward intensive plant harvesting) + sedentariness (from settled storage of wild grain → feedback toward cultivation).

**Consequences of the Neolithic transition:**
- Population growth (more calories per hectare, but less nutritious early)
- Social stratification (grain surpluses → storage → hierarchies → states)
- Epidemic diseases (close proximity to animals → zoonoses: smallpox, measles, flu)
- Reduced stature and health indicators (skeletal records)
- Soil depletion (Mesopotamia, early Mediterranean civilizations → collapse)

## Global Food Security Framework

```
FOUR PILLARS OF FOOD SECURITY (FAO):

1. AVAILABILITY:        Sufficient food produced/imported
2. ACCESS:              Economic + physical access to food
3. UTILIZATION:         Nutritional adequacy + food safety + diet quality
4. STABILITY:           Maintained over time (no shocks from climate, conflict, prices)

CURRENT STATUS (2024):
  ~733 million people food insecure (chronic hunger)
  ~2 billion people "hidden hungry" (micronutrient deficiency)
  ~2.8 billion cannot afford a healthy diet
  Despite: enough calories produced globally (distribution + waste failure)

FOOD WASTE: ~30-40% of food produced is wasted (production to consumer chain)
```

## Land Use — The Global Agricultural Footprint

```
GLOBAL LAND AREA: ~150 million km²
  Ice-free land:    ~130 million km²

  Forests:          ~40 million km² (31%)
  Shrubland:        ~30 million km² (23%)
  GRASSLAND/PASTURE: ~34 million km² (26%) ← livestock
  CROPLAND:          ~15 million km² (11%) ← crops
  Other:             ~11 million km² (8%)  (tundra, wetland, etc.)
  Urban/built:       ~1.5 million km² (1%)

50% of habitable land = agriculture (crops + pasture)
70% of freshwater withdrawals = agriculture (irrigation)
Agriculture = leading driver of deforestation, wetland loss, biodiversity loss
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Agriculture" is not uniform** — Industrial monoculture in Iowa and subsistence mixed farming in sub-Saharan Africa are both "agriculture" but share almost nothing technically or economically. Generalizations about agriculture's impacts or solutions must specify what kind of agriculture.

**Calorie availability ≠ food security** — Global calorie production exceeds global need, yet 700+ million are hungry. The problem is access (poverty), distribution, conflict, and waste — not aggregate production failure. This distinction matters enormously for policy.

**Organic farming ≠ sustainable farming** — "Organic" means no synthetic fertilizers or pesticides, but organic farms may still monocrop, cause erosion, use intensive tillage, and have lower yields (requiring more land for same output). Sustainability is multidimensional; organic is one dimension.
