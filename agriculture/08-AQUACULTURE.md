# Aquaculture — Farmed Seafood Production, Systems, Species, Sustainability

## The Big Picture

Aquaculture surpassed wild-catch fishing as the primary source of fish for human consumption around 2014. It now provides ~57% of seafood consumed globally and is the fastest-growing food production sector. But aquaculture systems span an enormous range — from low-intensity oyster beds that actively filter and clean water, to high-density salmon net pens that concentrate waste and require continuous antibiotic management, to recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) that are essentially liquid factories. Understanding which system produces what, at what cost, is the framework.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              AQUACULTURE PRODUCTION OVERVIEW                      |
|                                                                   |
|  GLOBAL PRODUCTION (2022, FAO):                                  |
|  Aquaculture total:   ~92 million tonnes (live weight)          |
|  Wild capture:        ~80 million tonnes                         |
|  → Aquaculture now > wild-catch for human consumption           |
|                                                                   |
|  BY ENVIRONMENT:                                                  |
|  Marine aquaculture: ~37 MT (seawater: salmon, shrimp, oysters) |
|  Freshwater:         ~52 MT (rivers, ponds: carp, tilapia)     |
|  Brackish:           ~3 MT (estuarine: shrimp, milkfish)       |
|                                                                   |
|  TOP SPECIES GROUPS (by volume):                                 |
|  Finfish (carp, tilapia, salmon): ~50 MT                        |
|  Molluscs (oysters, clams, mussels): ~20 MT                     |
|  Crustaceans (shrimp, prawns): ~12 MT                           |
|  Seaweed/algae: ~35 MT (not counted in all totals)              |
|                                                                   |
|  TOP PRODUCER: China (~58% of global aquaculture)               |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Production Systems — From Extensive to Intensive

```
EXTENSIVE SYSTEMS:
  Low stocking density; little or no supplemental feed
  Rely on natural productivity of ecosystem
  Examples: oyster/mussel longlines, clam beds
            traditional pond polyculture (China)
  Ecological footprint: often positive (filter feeders
    remove excess nutrients from water column)
  Yield: low per unit area; profitable when land/water free

SEMI-INTENSIVE SYSTEMS:
  Moderate stocking; supplemental feed added
  Examples: shrimp ponds in Asia; catfish ponds (US)
            seabream/seabass Mediterranean sea cages
  Management: feed, water quality, some disease treatment
  Yield: 5-20 tonnes/ha/yr

INTENSIVE SYSTEMS:
  High stocking; fully managed feed, water quality, health
  Examples: Atlantic salmon net pens; tilapia tanks
  Yield: 10-50 kg/m³ water volume
  Dependency: pelleted feed, aeration, waste management

RECIRCULATING AQUACULTURE SYSTEMS (RAS):
  Fully controlled closed systems on land
  See dedicated section below
  Yield: 50-150 kg/m³; land-independent
```

## Atlantic Salmon — Net Pen Systems

Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) is the most economically valuable farmed fish species:

```
NET PEN SYSTEM DESIGN:
  Circular or square net cages in fjords, bays, or open ocean
  Typical cage: 100-160 m circumference; 40-60 m depth
  Stocking density: 15-25 kg/m³ live weight at harvest
  Production cycle:
    Freshwater hatchery (egg → smolt: 12-18 months)
    → Net pen grow-out (18-24 months)
    → Harvest at 4-5 kg (commercial portion)
    → Cycle: ~3 years total

  FEED COMPOSITION (modern):
    Historically: fishmeal + fish oil (70%+)
    2020s: ~15-20% fishmeal, ~10-12% fish oil
    Replaced by: soy protein, wheat, poultry byproducts,
                 algae-derived omega-3s, single cell proteins
    FCR: 1.1-1.3 (very efficient; fish are poikilothermic —
         no energy wasted on body temperature maintenance)

  ENVIRONMENTAL ISSUES:
    Sea lice (Lepeophtheirus salmonis):
      Ectoparasite; spreads from farm fish to wild salmon
      Major operational challenge: chemical treatments (pesticides
      in water), biological control (cleaner fish: wrasse, lumpfish),
      laser systems (Stingray: laser targets moving lice)
      Delousing costs: up to $0.40/kg salmon
      Norwegian industry: spends $500M+/yr on sea lice

    Nutrient pollution:
      Uneaten feed + feces concentrate under cages
      Localized deoxygenation, H₂S production on seafloor
      Fallowing: rotating cage sites to allow recovery

    Escapes:
      Net failure → farmed fish escape to wild populations
      Atlantic salmon (sterile triploids developed to reduce impact)
      Interbreeding with wild fish → genetic introgression
      Norway: mandatory sterile fish planned

    Antibiotic use:
      Norway: 1987 peak → vaccines developed → near-zero now
        (vaccination model: disease prevented, not treated)
      Chile: high antibiotic use ongoing (quinolones, tetracyclines)
             regulatory failure in early industry development
      Canada, UK: intermediate between Norway and Chile
```

## Shrimp Aquaculture — Asia and Latin America

```
MAJOR SPECIES:
  Penaeus vannamei (Pacific white shrimp): ~80% of farmed shrimp
  Penaeus monodon (giant tiger shrimp): declining share
  Macrobrachium (freshwater prawns): tropical freshwater

  Top producers: China, Vietnam, India, Indonesia, Ecuador, Thailand

PRODUCTION SYSTEMS:
  Extensive: traditional coastal ponds; 500 kg/ha/yr
  Semi-intensive: managed ponds; 2-4 t/ha/yr
  Intensive: aerated ponds; 5-20 t/ha/yr
  Biofloc (zero water exchange): 30-60 t/ha/yr

BIOFLOC TECHNOLOGY:
  Maintain C:N ratio through C addition (molasses, tapioca)
  → Heterotrophic bacteria assimilate N as microbial biomass
  → Biofloc particles (~0.1-1 mm): nutrition + water quality control
  → Zero or minimal water exchange → less disease risk, less waste
  → Highest productivity; requires management expertise

ENVIRONMENTAL HISTORY:
  1980s-90s: massive mangrove clearing for shrimp ponds
  ~1-2 million ha mangrove lost to shrimp (Thailand, SE Asia)
  Mangroves: critical fish nursery habitat; carbon sink; coastal protection
  → Sustainability certifications now require no mangrove clearing

  DISEASE HISTORY:
    1990s-2000s: EMS (early mortality syndrome) / Acute Hepatopancreatic
    Necrosis Disease (AHPND): Vibrio parahaemolyticus → farm collapses
    WSSV (white spot syndrome virus): viral; wiped out farms in SE Asia
    → Led to better biosecurity, SPF (specific pathogen free) broodstock,
       biofloc systems, RAS for hatchery phases

CERTIFICATION:
  BAP (Best Aquaculture Practices): 4-star system
  ASC (Aquaculture Stewardship Council): most rigorous
  Global GAP: common in European supply chains
```

## Carp and Tilapia — Freshwater Dominance

```
CARPS (Chinese + Indian carp polyculture):
  ~35% of global aquaculture by volume
  Traditional Chinese polyculture: 5-6 carp species
    in one pond, occupying different feeding niches:
    Silver carp/bighead: filter-feed phytoplankton/zooplankton
    Grass carp: consume aquatic vegetation
    Common carp: benthic omnivore
    → LER-like concept: multiple ecological niches → higher yield
    → Minimal supplemental feed needed; pond ecosystem provides

  Production: 4-8 t/ha/yr (semi-intensive)
  Feed: grass, agricultural byproducts, manure; low-cost
  FCR: 1.5-2.5 (semi-intensive)
  Sustainability: generally high; low input systems

TILAPIA:
  "Aquatic chicken": robust, fast-growing, plant-eating
  Species: Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) dominant
  Origin: Africa; now produced globally (China, Indonesia, Egypt, Ghana)

  ADVANTAGES:
    Tolerates: crowding, low oxygen, high temperatures
    Feeds: primarily phytoplankton, algae, pellets
    Fast growth: market size (500g) in 6-8 months
    High protein efficiency; all-male production (hormone sex reversal
    or YY super-males) → no reproductive energy loss

  USE CASES:
    Cheap animal protein: developing world nutrition
    Global fish fillet market: mild white flesh
    Aquaponics integration: frequently used species
```

## Bivalves — The Most Sustainable Aquaculture

```
OYSTERS, MUSSELS, CLAMS, SCALLOPS:
  No feed required: filter natural phytoplankton from water
  Ecological services:
    Water filtration: 1 oyster filters ~50 L water/hour
    Eutrophication mitigation: remove N and P as shellfish harvest
    Reef habitat creation
    Coastal protection (oyster reefs)

  PRODUCTION METHODS:
    Longlines: suspended ropes at various depths in water column
    Raft culture: grow bags / nets hung from floating rafts
    Bottom culture: broadcast on sea floor (extensive)
    Cage culture: contained systems (for high-value, predator protection)

  CARRYING CAPACITY:
    Shellfish density limited by local phytoplankton production
    Excessive density → food competition → slow growth → disease
    Carrying capacity modeling: critical for bay/fjord management

  PEARL OYSTER AQUACULTURE:
    Japan, French Polynesia, Australia
    Nucleated with bead; 2-5 year grow-out
    High value; very low volume; specialized labor

SEAWEED AQUACULTURE:
  Kelp, Porphyra (nori), Eucheuma, Gracilaria
  ~35 million tonnes/yr (90% in Asia; mostly China/Indonesia)
  No feed, no freshwater: absorb dissolved nutrients
  Uses: food (nori), hydrocolloids (carrageenan, agar), fertilizer,
        animal feed, bioplastics, biofuels (emerging)
  IMTA role: see below
```

## Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS)

```
RAS CONCEPT:
  Land-based, closed-loop water treatment → fish growing

  WATER TREATMENT COMPONENTS:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  FISH TANK                                          │
  │  High stocking density (50-100 kg/m³)              │
  └─────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                    ↓ water with waste
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  MECHANICAL FILTRATION (drum filter)                │
  │  Removes suspended solids: uneaten feed, feces      │
  └─────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                    ↓ dissolved ammonia (NH₃/NH₄⁺)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BIOLOGICAL FILTER (biofilm on media)              │
  │  Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻               │
  │  (Nitrosomonas + Nitrospira bacteria)              │
  └─────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                    ↓ NO₃⁻ accumulates
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  DENITRIFICATION or water exchange                  │
  │  Anoxic reactor: NO₃⁻ → N₂ (denitrification)      │
  │  or: bleed NO₃⁻-rich water; replace with fresh     │
  └─────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                    ↓ degassed, oxygenated
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  UV/OZONE DISINFECTION                              │
  │  Kill pathogens before water returns to tank       │
  └─────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                    ↓ clean water returned
               BACK TO FISH TANK (95-99% water recycled)

ADVANTAGES:
  Location-independent (inland, urban)
  No ocean/disease connection
  Year-round production in any climate
  Minimal antibiotic use (controlled environment, no wild pathogens)
  Precise control: temperature, photoperiod, feed delivery
  Low water use: ~98% recycled vs flow-through systems

DISADVANTAGES:
  High capital cost: $3-10M for 1000 tonne/yr facility
  High energy cost: pumps, aeration, heating/cooling
    → Often 5-10 kWh/kg fish produced (vs net pen: ~0.5-1 kWh/kg)
  Biofilter management: disruption → ammonia spike → fish kill
  Economics: currently limited to high-value species (salmon, sea bass)
  Carbon footprint: energy-intensive; requires low-carbon electricity to compete
```

## Integrated Multi-Trophic Aquaculture (IMTA)

```
IMTA CONCEPT:
  Combine species at different trophic levels:
    Fed species (salmon, shrimp) → produce wastes (dissolved + particulate)
    Filter feeders (mussels, oysters) → consume dissolved organics + particles
    Extractive organisms (seaweed) → absorb dissolved inorganic nutrients

  MATERIAL FLOWS:
    SALMON
      ↓ dissolved waste (NH₄⁺, PO₄³⁻)     ↓ particulate waste (feces, feed)
  SEAWEED                               OYSTERS/MUSSELS
  (absorb N, P)                         (filter particles)

  BENEFITS:
    Nutrient reduction: bioremediation on-site
    Additional revenue streams: seaweed + shellfish sold
    Reduced environmental footprint per tonne fish
    "Ecological optimization" of the production system

  CHALLENGES:
    Spatial management (different species need different positioning)
    Regulatory approval (multiple species, multiple licenses)
    Market development (seaweed market limited in West)
    Seasonal mismatch (shellfish/seaweed growth seasonal)

  EXAMPLES:
    Bay of Fundy (Canada): salmon + mussels + kelp trials
    Norway: emerging seaweed + salmon combination
    China: traditional polyculture is proto-IMTA (fish + shellfish + seaweed)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Aquaculture question | Key consideration |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Most sustainable seafood choices | Bivalves (oysters, mussels, clams) > seaweed > certified tilapia > certified salmon (Norway > Chile) |
| Why is RAS capital-intensive but growing? | Site independence, no disease exposure, no escapes, precise control. Economic only for high-value species; energy cost must be green |
| Sea lice problem → Norway's solution vs Chile's | Norway: vaccines + mechanical/laser lice removal; very low antibiotics. Chile: historical antibiotic overuse; improving with regulation |
| Why farmed salmon FCR is lower than beef? | Fish are ectothermic — no energy wasted on body temperature. All feed energy goes to growth, not thermoregulation. |
| Shrimp pond vs tilapia pond sustainability | Depends enormously on management: biofloc shrimp = high production, low waste; poorly managed shrimp = high antibiotics, mangrove loss risk |
| IMTA: does it work economically? | Not yet at large scale. Bioremediation benefit clear; economic returns from bivalve/seaweed co-culture improving. Regulatory framework is often the bottleneck. |

## Common Confusion Points

**Aquaculture FCR ≠ wild fish FCR** — "Aquaculture feeds fish to fish" is the old critique. Modern salmon feed is ~15% fishmeal, mostly from trimmings. The forage fish (sardines, anchovies, herring) used for fishmeal are themselves highly efficient wild-captured protein. The "fish-in, fish-out" ratio is now <1.0 for most farmed salmon (more fish protein comes out than goes in as fishmeal).

**Tilapia and carp are "4th world" food, salmon is luxury** — This is a Western-centric view. Carp and tilapia provide the vast majority of animal protein for hundreds of millions of people in Asia, Africa, and Latin America at prices below chicken. The sustainability narrative focuses on salmon because that's what wealthy consumers eat; the real nutritional contribution of aquaculture is carps/tilapia.

**RAS is not automatically sustainable** — If powered by coal electricity, RAS has a large carbon footprint per kg of fish — potentially worse than net pen salmon. RAS sustainability depends almost entirely on energy source. Located near cheap renewable power (hydroelectric, wind) and RAS becomes compelling; otherwise the energy math is problematic.

**Seaweed aquaculture ≠ minor** — Seaweed is ~35 million tonnes/yr — comparable to all wild finfish capture. It's just nearly invisible in Western nutrition contexts (used as food in Japan/Korea, as hydrocolloids in Western food processing, and in enormous quantities for Asian domestic markets). The potential for seaweed as a climate tool (carbon sequestration, methane inhibitor for livestock) is actively researched.
