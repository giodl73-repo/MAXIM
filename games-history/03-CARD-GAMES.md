## Section 1: Chinese Origins — The Paper Money Theory

### The Accepted Origin

Playing cards almost certainly originated in China during the Tang dynasty (618-907 CE). The evidence:

- The **yezi ge** (leaf game, 葉子格) appears in Tang dynasty texts (~850 CE). A treatise on yezi ge is attributed to a woman named He Ji during the reign of Emperor Yizong (859-873 CE).
- The connection between paper money and cards is well-supported: Chinese paper money was folded, manipulated, and apparently used in games involving denomination matching.
- The Mamluk deck (14th century Egypt/Syria) is clearly derived from something that entered the Islamic world from East Asia via trade routes.

```
PAPER MONEY → CARD GAMES HYPOTHESIS
═════════════════════════════════════

Tang dynasty China:
  Paper money (jiaozi) in circulation ~7th-8th century CE.
  Money amounts to physical tokens with:
    - Distinct values (ranks)
    - Combinational rules (which beats which)
    - Trading between players (gambling)

  This is essentially the structure of cards.
  Whether paper money folded INTO game cards, or whether
  the conceptual structure migrated, is debated.

What is not debated:
  Cards predate their first European appearances by 400+ years
  in China. European cards clearly derive from this tradition
  via Islamic/Mamluk intermediation — the suit structures
  match too precisely for independent invention.
```

### Mamluk Deck (14th Century)

A nearly complete Mamluk card deck survives in the Topkapi Palace Museum, Istanbul. Dating to ~1370-1420 CE, it represents the oldest substantially complete deck outside China.

```
MAMLUK DECK STRUCTURE
══════════════════════

52 cards (same count as modern deck)
4 suits: CUPS (кубок), COINS (dirham), SWORDS (saif),
         POLO STICKS (jawkan)

Each suit: 10 pip cards + 3 court cards = 13 total
Court cards: MALIK (king), NA'IB MALIK (deputy king),
             THANI NA'IB (second deputy)
             — No queens (Islamic prohibition on depicting women)

DIRECT DESCENDANTS:
  Spanish/Portuguese suits TODAY:
    Cups → Copas (cups)
    Coins → Oros (gold coins)
    Swords → Espadas (swords)
    Polo sticks → Bastos (clubs/cudgels)
    (Polo sticks → cudgels because Europeans didn't know polo)

  Spanish court cards: Rey (King), Caballero (Knight),
                       Sota (Page/Jack)
  French court cards (added Queen later): Roi, Dame, Valet
```

## Section 2: European Transformation — Suits and Courts

### German Innovation: Stencil Printing

The critical mechanical change that democratized cards: German cardmakers (~1400-1430) developed stencil printing techniques that reduced the cost of card manufacturing by roughly an order of magnitude. Before this, cards were hand-painted luxury objects (surviving Italian hand-painted tarot decks are museum pieces). After, a deck cost what a day's bread cost.

```
EUROPEAN SUIT SYSTEMS
══════════════════════

LATIN SUITS (Spanish, Portuguese, Italian):
  Derived directly from Mamluk.
  Cups, Coins, Swords, Clubs/Batons.
  Still used in Spanish-speaking world and for tarot.

GERMAN SUITS:
  Hearts (Herzen), Bells (Schellen),
  Leaves (Laub), Acorns (Eicheln).
  Regional variants in Switzerland, Bavaria.

FRENCH SUITS (became dominant globally):
  Hearts (Coeur), Diamonds (Carreau),
  Clubs (Trefle), Spades (Pique).
  Key advantage: SIMPLER to stencil print.
  Single-color symbols vs. the elaborate Latin artwork.
  French suits spread via British trade and Empire.

WHY FRENCH SUITS WON:
  The suit symbols are:
  - Abstract (no complex artwork)
  - Two-color print-ready (red/black)
  - Distinguishable at a glance
  - Easy to produce with stencils
  This is essentially the same reason ASCII beat Unicode
  for early computing: simpler encoding wins in production.
```

## Section 3: Tarot — Game First, Divination Later

This is one of the most thoroughly misunderstood points in card history. Tarot was invented as a **game**. Divination came 300 years later.

### The Tarocchi Origin (15th Century Italy)

```
TAROT HISTORY TIMELINE
════════════════════════

1420s-1440s: Tarocchi (tarot) invented in northern Italy
  (Milan, Ferrara, Bologna). First evidence: payment records
  for hand-painted decks for the Visconti-Sforza family (~1450).

STRUCTURE: 78 cards = 56 suit cards + 22 trump cards
  Suit cards: 4 suits (Latin) × 14 cards each (including
              4 court cards: King, Queen, Knight, Page)
  Trump cards (trionfi/trionfo = "triumphs"):
    21 numbered trumps + The Fool (unnumbered)
    The trumps are a PERMANENT TRUMP SUIT — they beat
    all regular suit cards in trick-taking play.

THE GAME (Tarocchini/Tarot game):
  Standard trick-taking game WITH a permanent trump suit.
  Players bid or follow suit; trump cards win tricks.
  Italian tarocchini still played in Bologna today.
  French Tarot still played competitively in France.

1781: Antoine Court de Gébelin, "Le Monde Primitif" —
  falsely claims tarot is ancient Egyptian esoteric wisdom.
  This is ENTIRELY fabricated. He invented the theory.
  But it was compelling and spread widely.

~1790s: Etteilla (Jean-Baptiste Alliette) creates the first
  tarot deck designed explicitly for divination.
  This is where divination tarot begins. NOT before.

1910: Arthur Edward Waite + Pamela Coleman Smith: Rider-Waite
  tarot deck — the standard modern "divination" deck.
  Completely redesigned symbolism from original gaming cards.
```

### Tarot as Trick-Taking Game

The French Tarot game (still played, ranked competitive game in France):

```
FRENCH TAROT STRUCTURE
═══════════════════════

Players: 4 (most common), can be 3 or 5
Deck: 78 cards
Suits: Spades, Hearts, Diamonds, Clubs (French suits)
       14 cards each (10 pips + 4 courts + no Ace-high —
       actually Ace is LOW in French Tarot)
Trumps: 21 numbered trumps (1=petit, 21=monde/world)
        + The Fool (excuse, can play but doesn't take)

THE OUDLERS (special trump cards):
  Petit (trump 1), Monde (trump 21), Excuse (The Fool)
  Worth 4.5 points each in scoring.
  How many oudlers you have determines how many points
  you need to win the contract.

BIDDING:
  Prise (take): commit to winning with a handicap
  Garde (guard): higher commitment, better scoring
  Garde sans (guard without): no exchange with the kitty
  Garde contre (guard against): opponents' points count against
```

## Section 4: Trick-Taking Games — The Core Mechanic

Trick-taking is the fundamental mechanic of an enormous family of games. Understanding it unlocks Bridge, Whist, Hearts, Spades, Euchre, Pinochle, Skat, and Canasta variants.

```
TRICK-TAKING MECHANIC
══════════════════════

BASIC STRUCTURE:
  N players. One player leads a card.
  Others must "follow suit" (play same suit if possible).
  If you can't follow suit: play any card (including trump).
  Highest card of led suit wins the trick — UNLESS
  a trump was played, in which case highest trump wins.
  Winner of trick leads next.

COMPLETE INFORMATION PARADOX:
  In Bridge/Whist, the trick-taking phase is perfect
  information IF cards have been played openly.
  But it's bidding that makes it IMPERFECT INFORMATION —
  you don't know partner's cards during the auction.

INFORMATION FLOW:
  After all 13 tricks, both teams know what both
  hands contained. A good player DEDUCES partner's
  holding from bidding and play patterns.
  This is Bayesian inference: update distribution
  of unknown cards given observed plays.
<!-- @editor[bridge/P2]: The card-inference problem in Bridge maps directly to constraint propagation / belief propagation — as each card is played, the distribution over unknown cards is updated via conditioning; for the TCS learner, this is the same structure as BP on a factor graph (variables = unknown cards, factors = bid/play constraints); a one-sentence connection to this formal structure would be a valuable bridge -->

VARIANTS ON CORE:
  HEARTS: avoid tricks (negative scoring) for heart cards
          and Queen of Spades — the "shooting the moon"
          strategy (take ALL hearts + QoS = 26 points
          AGAINST opponents) is the game's elegant exception.
  SPADES: bid the exact number of tricks you'll take.
          Sandbag penalty for taking too many ("tricks"
          beyond bid accumulate as bags; 10 bags = penalty).
  EUCHRE: 24-card deck, bower system (J of trump suit =
          highest, J of same color = second highest).
```

## Section 5: Whist — The Direct Bridge Ancestor

Whist dominated English card culture from the 17th century through the early 20th century. Understanding Whist is understanding Bridge's structural DNA.

### Rules of Whist

```
WHIST STRUCTURE (17th-18th century)
═════════════════════════════════════

Players: 4 (two partnerships, N-S vs E-W)
Deck: 52 standard cards
Deal: 13 cards each
Trump: last dealt card flipped face-up = trump suit
       (removed from play, but suit established)
Scoring: 1 point per trick won beyond 6 ("books")
         First to 7 (or 9) points wins game

KEY RULES:
  No bidding. Trump is random (last card).
  Partners sit opposite each other.
  Must follow suit if possible.
  Partnership communication: ONLY through card play.
    Playing the highest card in a suit signals
    "lead this suit to me." Playing low signals
    "I don't have strength here."

EDMOND HOYLE (1672-1769):
  "A Short Treatise on the Game of Whist" (1742) —
  FIRST systematic rulebook for any card game.
  Hoyle documented the probabilities, strategies,
  and conventions of Whist scientifically.
  "According to Hoyle" entered English as a phrase
  meaning "according to the established rules."
  Hoyle later wrote similar books for Backgammon,
  Piquet, Chess, and Quadrille.
```

### The Hoyle Significance

Hoyle's treatise represents a pivotal moment: the application of systematic, probabilistic thinking to a recreational game. Before Hoyle, card game "strategy" was informal. Hoyle codified:
- Which cards to lead (strong vs passive leads)
- How to signal to partner through card order
- Probability calculations for remaining cards
- When to take vs duck (allow opponent to win) tricks

This is the direct ancestor of the massive body of card-game strategy literature that followed.

## Section 6: Contract Bridge — The Apex Trick-Taking Game

Bridge is the most complex widely-played card game in the world, combining:
- Trick-taking mechanics (13 rounds, perfect structure)
- An auction phase (bidding language, partnership communication)
- Probability and deduction (card inference, squeeze plays)

### The Evolution to Contract Bridge

```
BRIDGE EVOLUTION
═════════════════

WHIST (17th century):
  No bidding. Trump = last card.
  Pure play skill.

BIRITCH / RUSSIAN WHIST (~1880s):
  First version with declaring (calling trump or no-trump).
  Declarer plays from one hand, dummy (partner's hand)
  laid face-up on table. Declarer plays both hands.
  "Biritch" = disputed etymology (possibly Russian/Ottoman)

BRIDGE WHIST (~1895-1904):
  Declarer names trump suit before play.
  Dummy hand visible. No bidding on contract size.

AUCTION BRIDGE (1904):
  Players BID for the right to name trumps.
  Highest bidder names trumps AND commits to making
  that many tricks. Partnership bidding: can bid higher
  to "protect" partner's suit.
  Critical innovation: the auction phase as communication.

CONTRACT BRIDGE (Harold Vanderbilt, 1925):
  Played during a steamship crossing (SS Finland,
  October-November 1925, Vanderbilt and friends).
  Key innovations:
  1. VULNERABILITY: a team with a partial score is
     "vulnerable" — higher penalties for failing contracts,
     higher bonuses for making slam.
  2. SCORING REFORM: Only tricks bid AND made count toward
     game (in Auction Bridge, extra tricks beyond bid
     counted toward game too).
  These two changes made the bidding phase *strategically
  critical* — you must bid what you can make, precisely.
```

### The Culbertson-Lenz Match (1931-1932)

Bridge became a social phenomenon in 1930s America. Ely Culbertson vs Sidney Lenz:

```
THE BRIDGE MATCH OF THE CENTURY
═════════════════════════════════

Context: Culbertson had published "Contract Bridge Blue Book"
  (1930), establishing the Culbertson system. Critics said
  it was too complicated. Lenz championed a simpler approach.

The Match: December 1931 – January 1932
  150 rubber bridge sessions. Culbertson + wife Josephine
  vs. Lenz + rotating partner.
  Result: Culbertson wins 8,980 points.

Significance:
  - Front-page news in major US newspapers
  - Radio coverage
  - Bridge achieved mainstream cultural status
  - Culbertson's Blue Book sold millions of copies
  - Bridge became *the* upper-middle-class card game

The game peaked culturally in the 1950s-60s.
At peak, 40 million Americans played bridge regularly.
```

### Modern Bridge Structure

```
CONTRACT BRIDGE — BIDDING SYSTEM
══════════════════════════════════

AUCTION PHASE:
  Players bid in sequence: N, E, S, W, N, E, S, W...
  A bid = level (1-7) + denomination (clubs, diamonds,
          hearts, spades, or no-trump)
  Level = number of tricks BEYOND 6 you commit to make
          (bid 4 Hearts = commit to making 10 tricks)
  Suits rank: Clubs < Diamonds < Hearts < Spades < No-Trump

  Each bid must be higher than previous (higher level
  OR higher denomination at same level).

SPECIAL CALLS:
  Double: challenges opponent's contract (doubles penalty
          if they fail, doubles their bonus if they make it)
  Redouble: response to double (doubles again)
  Pass: no bid

GAME AND SLAM:
  Game contracts:
    3NT, 4 Hearts/Spades, 5 Clubs/Diamonds = game bonus
  Small Slam = 12 tricks (6-level): large bonus
  Grand Slam = 13 tricks (7-level): largest bonus

BIDDING SYSTEMS (communicate hand strength/shape):
  Standard American: 5-card major suits, 1NT = 15-17 HCP
  Precision: 1 Club shows 16+ HCP (strong, artificial)
  ACOL (UK standard): weaker NT, natural bidding
  2/1 (Two-over-one): forcing to game structures
  Puppet Stayman: advanced inquiry after NT opening

HAND EVALUATION:
  High Card Points (HCP): Ace=4, King=3, Queen=2, Jack=1
  Maximum possible: 40 HCP (all honors)
  Game expected when partnership has 25+ HCP
  Small slam: 32+ HCP
  Grand slam: 36-37+ HCP
  (Distribution adjustments apply: voids, singletons,
   long suits add distributional points)
```

### Duplicate Bridge

```
DUPLICATE BRIDGE
═════════════════

Problem with rubber bridge for skill comparison:
  Different deals have different inherent difficulty.
  Luck of the deal affects who wins.

Solution (duplicate): The SAME deals played at multiple
  tables. Your result compared to others playing
  identical cards.

  Table 1: N-S hold hand #17, bid 4 Hearts, make 5 = +450
  Table 2: N-S hold hand #17, bid 3 Hearts, make 5 = +200
  Table 1 N-S score better on the board.

  Scoring: Matchpoints (1 for beating each table,
           0.5 for tie) or IMPs (International Match Points,
           used in team events — logarithmic scale).

This makes bridge a true skill competition:
  Luck of the deal factored out entirely.
  World Bridge Championships use duplicate format.
  High-level bridge is as skill-dependent as chess.
```

## Section 7: Other Major Card Game Families

### Rummy Family

```
RUMMY MECHANIC
═══════════════

CORE: Draw a card from deck or discard pile.
      Meld (lay down) sets: 3-4 of same rank
      OR sequences: 3+ consecutive same suit.
      Discard one card. First player to meld all cards wins.

VARIANTS:
  Gin Rummy (1909): 10 cards, knock when unmatched card
    value ≤ 10. "Gin" = all cards melded, no deadwood.
  Canasta (Uruguay, 1939): partnership rummy with 2 decks,
    bonus for "canastas" (7+ card melds), wild cards.
    Dominant US game 1950-1960 before Bridge overtook it.
  Mahjong (China, ~1800s): Tile-based version of same
    mechanic — draw/discard to build complete hand of
    melds. 4 players, elaborate scoring.
  Oklahoma Gin, Hollywood Gin: Gin rummy variants.
```

### Solitaire / Patience

```
SOLITAIRE (Klondike)
═════════════════════

The most commonly played card game in history (because
it shipped with Windows 3.0 in 1990, teaching millions
to use a mouse with drag-and-drop).

Klondike: 7 tableau columns (1-7 cards face down,
  top card face up). Goal: build 4 foundation piles
  A→K by suit. Turn cards from stock (1 or 3 at a time).

PROBABILITY NOTE:
  Not all Klondike deals are solvable.
  Estimated solvability: ~80% of deals (with perfect play)
  but a player making reasonable choices solves ~43%.
  Perfect play has uncertain solvability — some deals are
  provably unsolvable, some are solvable but only with
  the right move sequence.

FREECELL: Variant where all cards are visible.
  Almost all deals solvable (4 freecells = buffer).
  Only 8 known unsolvable deals in the standard 32,000
  numbered deals. TRUE: 99.999% solvable.
  This makes FreeCell a puzzle, not a chance game.
<!-- @editor[bridge/P1]: FreeCell solvability is a planning/reachability problem in a deterministic state space — this is directly the kind of problem a TCS reader would recognize as graph reachability (is the goal state reachable from the start state?); FreeCell is known to be NP-complete in general (Helmert 2003), which is a clean TCS result that would resonate strongly with this learner; worth one sentence here -->

SPIDER SOLITAIRE: 2 decks, 10 columns.
  Much harder than Klondike. ~0.5% of deals unwinnable
  with perfect play (4-suit version).
```

### Banking Games (Casino Card Games)

```
BLACKJACK (21)
═══════════════

Origin: Vingt-et-Un (French, "21"), ~18th century.
American blackjack: added 10:1 bonus for Ace + black Jack
  (hence "blackjack"), later simplified to any natural 21.

MATHEMATICS:
  Basic strategy (optimal play, known since 1956):
    Using perfect basic strategy: house edge = ~0.5%
    Without basic strategy: house edge = 2-4%
    Basic strategy = lookup table of {hand, dealer upcard}
    → action (hit, stand, double, split, surrender)

CARD COUNTING:
  Edward Thorp "Beat the Dealer" (1962): first rigorous
  proof that counting cards changes house edge in player's favor.

  Hi-Lo count:
    Cards 2-6: +1  (low cards benefit dealer, count up)
    Cards 7-9:  0  (neutral)
    Cards 10-A: -1 (high cards benefit player, count down)

    Running Count / (decks remaining) = True Count
    True Count +2: player has edge
    True Count +4: raise bet significantly
    True Count > +4: substantial player edge

  House countermeasures:
    Multiple decks (6-8 deck shoes): reduces count efficacy
    Shuffle machines: automatic reshuffling eliminates counting
    Backoff/ban: casinos identify and ban counters
    Penetration games: less than half the shoe seen before
      reshuffling makes counting marginally useful.

BACCARAT:
  James Bond's game. Completely skill-free.
  Bet on Banker (1.06% house edge), Player (1.24% house edge),
  or Tie (14.4% house edge — never bet this).
  Card values: 10s/faces = 0, others = face value.
  Hand value = sum mod 10.
  Drawing rules: automatic third-card rule, no decisions.
  Mini-baccarat: same game, smaller table, faster pace.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Tarot cards are an ancient occult tradition"**: They are 15th-century Italian gaming cards. Antoine Court de Gébelin invented the "ancient Egyptian" mythology in 1781 — over 350 years after tarot was created as a game. The Rider-Waite "standard" divination deck was designed in 1910. Tarot is actively played as a trick-taking game in France and Italy today.

**"Blackjack basic strategy guarantees you win"**: Basic strategy minimizes the house edge to ~0.5%, but you still lose in the long run. The only positive-EV approaches require card counting + bet spreading — and casinos ban counters.

**"Card counting is cheating or illegal"**: Neither. You're using your brain to track public information. Nevada law (and most jurisdictions) allows casinos to refuse service to anyone — they can ban you, but you've committed no crime.

**"The standard deck has 52 cards because of the year"**: Folk etymology. The 52-card deck descends from the Mamluk 52-card deck through Spanish/Italian/French transmission. The 52-week correlation is coincidental.

**"Whist and Bridge are essentially the same"**: Structurally related but Bridge added the entire auction system, dummy hand as fully visible, vulnerability scoring, and game/slam bonuses. These transforms make Bridge several orders of magnitude more strategically complex than Whist.

**"Gin Rummy and regular Rummy are the same"**: Gin Rummy is a specific 2-player variant of Rummy with 10 cards, a "knock" mechanism, and its own distinct scoring. "Rummy" refers to the broad family; Gin Rummy is the most widely played member in the US.
