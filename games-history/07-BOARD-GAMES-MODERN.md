## Section 1: Monopoly — Corrupted Origins

### The Landlord's Game (1903)

The origin of Monopoly is one of game history's most significant cases of intellectual property erasure:

```
MONOPOLY ORIGIN STORY
══════════════════════

ELIZABETH "LIZZIE" MAGIE (1866-1948):
  Inventor, stenographer, political activist.
  Patented "The Landlord's Game" in 1903.

  PURPOSE: Explicitly designed to demonstrate
  Henry George's "Single Tax" economic theory:
    - Land values increase due to societal improvements,
      not landowner effort.
    - Therefore, land value should be taxed, not labor.
    - Georgism: one tax on land, abolish other taxes.

  TWO RULE SETS (deliberately included both):
    LANDLORD RULES (anti-monopolist):
      Taxes collected redistributed to all players.
      Cooperation rewarded. Distribution of wealth wins.
    MONOPOLIST RULES:
      Buy property, charge rent, bankrupt opponents.
      Winning = crushing everyone else.

  Magie's intent: show players that the monopolist rules
  create misery, while the landlord rules create prosperity.
  A teaching tool with TWO economic models embedded.

BOARD LAYOUT (original):
  Circular track. Properties to purchase.
  "Mother Earth" square (later: Free Parking/Go).
  Luxury Tax. Jail. Railroad squares.
  Atlantic City street names were NOT in Magie's original —
  those came from Darrow's Atlantic City version.
```

### The Charles Darrow Myth

```
WHAT PARKER BROTHERS TOLD THE PUBLIC:
  Charles Darrow (1889-1967), unemployed engineer from
  Philadelphia, "invented" Monopoly during the Depression.
  Sold it to Parker Brothers in 1935. Became first
  board game millionaire. A classic American success story.

WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED:
  1. Magie's game spread informally from ~1903 onward,
     passed hand-to-hand in progressive and academic circles.
  2. Charles Todd of Atlantic City had a version with
     Atlantic City street names by ~1929.
  3. Darrow LEARNED THE GAME from the Todds in ~1932.
  4. Darrow made a commercial version, patented it (1933-1935).
  5. Parker Brothers bought Darrow's version.
  6. Parker Brothers also purchased Magie's ORIGINAL PATENT
     for $500 with no royalties and no public credit.
  7. Parker Brothers then sued multiple people for infringing
     Darrow's patent, effectively suppressing the game's
     actual history.

THE SUPPRESSION WAS SUCCESSFUL FOR DECADES:
  Magie's role was known to game historians but
  largely unknown to the public until Ralph Anspach
  (the "Anti-Monopoly" creator) fought a decade of patent
  litigation and uncovered the history through discovery.
  Mary Pilon's book "The Monopolists" (2015) brought
  the full story to mainstream awareness.

LIZZIE MAGIE'S COMPENSATION:
  $500. No royalties. Monopoly has since sold to
  approximately 1 billion people across 300+ versions
  in 47 languages.
```

### Monopoly as Game Design

```
MONOPOLY AS STUDY IN BAD DESIGN
══════════════════════════════════

Ironically, the game that's played by the most people
is also widely cited by game designers as a study
in what NOT to do:

ELIMINATION MECHANICS:
  Players who go bankrupt are eliminated from the game.
  The eliminated player must then watch others play
  for hours. Eliminated players have no agency or fun.
  Modern design principle: keep players engaged throughout.

LUCK-DOMINANT IN DETERMINING WHO WINS:
  Dice rolls for movement. Dice rolls for chance/community chest.
  Property acquisition depends heavily on what you land on.
  Skill is minimal — bargaining is the only significant decision.
  Long game: with skilled negotiators, outcomes still largely
  determined by early dice luck (who lands on Boardwalk/Park Place
  vs. who doesn't).

RUNAWAY LEADER:
  The rich get richer mechanically. No comeback mechanism.
  Once a player has monopolies and houses, the game
  becomes a waiting game for the richer player to win.
  This is mechanically correct for the monopolist rules
  Magie designed — but it's a poor entertainment experience.

HOUSE RULE PROLIFERATION:
  The published rules are NOT what most families play.
  Common house rules: Free Parking jackpot, $200 for landing
  ON Go (not passing it), auctions when someone declines
  to buy (the ACTUAL RULE most families don't know).
  The official auction rule makes the game significantly
  shorter and more strategic.
  The Free Parking jackpot house rule extends the game
  dramatically (money that should leave the system stays in it).
```

## Section 2: Wargaming Lineage — Kriegsspiel to D&D

### Kriegsspiel (1824) — First Serious Wargame

```
KRIEGSSPIEL ("War Game" in German)
════════════════════════════════════

INVENTOR: Lt. Georg von Reisswitz (and father Johann)
  Berlin, 1812 (early version) → 1824 (published version)

PURPOSE: Military training tool. Prussian Army adopted it.
  General von Müffling: "This is not a game! This is
  military training! I shall recommend it to the whole army."
  Used to train officers in tactical and strategic decisions
  without actual combat.

MECHANICS:
  Scale map of battlefield.
  Blocks represent units (company/battalion level).
  Referee/umpire mediates the rules.
  Dice for combat outcomes.
  Movement rates based on troop type (infantry/cavalry/artillery).
  Fog of war: units only visible when in detection range.
  Two-player (or team-vs-team).

THE UMPIRE INNOVATION:
  Kriegsspiel introduced the "referee" or "umpire" role —
  a neutral party who knows the full state of the game but
  mediates between players who only know their side.
  This is the direct ancestor of:
  - Dungeon Master/Game Master in D&D
  - Narrator in narrative tabletop RPGs
  - Admin/moderator in online war sims

MILITARY ADOPTION:
  Prussia: mandatory for officer education ~1870s
  Germany: used to plan WWI (Schlieffen Plan analyzed via wargame)
  US military: adopted after observing German efficiency
  Modern: military war gaming still used (Naval War College)
```

### H.G. Wells and Civilian Wargaming

```
H.G. WELLS — "LITTLE WARS" (1913)
════════════════════════════════════

H.G. Wells (author of "The Time Machine," "War of the Worlds"):
  Published "Little Wars" — a rulebook for miniature wargaming
  with toy soldiers.
  Used model soldiers (typically 54mm) and spring-loaded cannons
  that could knock over opposing soldiers.
  Movement on floor or tabletop. No grid.
  Rules for infantry, cavalry, and artillery.

SIGNIFICANCE:
  First civilian wargame publication.
  Established the miniature wargaming hobby.
  Wells' famous quote: "I have not the slightest doubt that
  this game of mine is an infinitely better game than golf."
  Direct ancestor of Warhammer, modern miniatures gaming.
```

### Avalon Hill and Hex-and-Counter Wargames

```
HEX-AND-COUNTER WARGAMES
══════════════════════════

AVALON HILL (founded 1952, Baltimore):
  Charles Roberts publishes "Tactics" (1952) — first
  mass-market commercial wargame.
  "Gettysburg" (1958): first game about specific historical battle.
  Hex grid + cardboard counters = the standard format.

WHY HEX GRIDS:
  Square grids create diagonal movement asymmetry
  (diagonal = 1.414× distance, creating unrealistic movement).
  Hexagonal grids: 6 equal adjacent cells, uniform distance.
  Each cell has 6 neighbors at equal distance.
  Combat resolution: compare attack/defense factors + die roll.

AVALON HILL CATALOG:
  Gettysburg, Waterloo, Afrika Korps, Midway, Panzerblitz.
  Squad Leader (1977): tactical WWII, considered a masterpiece
  of historical wargame design. Thousands of scenarios.
  Advanced Squad Leader (ASL): expansion of Squad Leader,
  still actively supported and played by dedicated community.

SPI (SIMULATIONS PUBLICATIONS INC, 1969-1982):
  James Dunnigan, SPI. Prolific publisher.
  Strategy & Tactics magazine (included game with each issue).
  ENERGY: computer modeling + game design combined.
  SPI published hundreds of titles before going bankrupt.
  Acquired by TSR (D&D publisher) then Avalon Hill.

MODERN WARGAMES:
  GMT Games: dominant premium wargame publisher today.
    Commands & Colors, Twilight Struggle, Labyrinth,
    Washington's War, etc.
  Compass Games, Victory Point Games, Cole Wargames.
  Twilight Struggle (2005): #1 rated game on BGG for years.
    Cold War as card-driven wargame. 2-player, ~2 hours.
```

### Dungeons & Dragons (1974)

```
D&D ORIGIN
═══════════

CHAINMAIL (1971): Gary Gygax + Jeff Perren.
  Medieval miniature combat rules for Avalon Hill era gamers.
  First rules for man-to-man combat scale (not battalion).
  Added fantasy supplement: spells, monsters, heroes.
  Direct predecessor to D&D.

DAVE ARNESON (Minneapolis):
  Created "Braunstein" campaign with Chainmail rules.
  Key innovation: PERSISTENT CHARACTER.
  Rather than controlling an army, each player controlled
  a SINGLE CHARACTER who accumulated experience and survived
  between sessions. The dungeon as the scenario.
  Characters grew in capability over multiple sessions.
  This is the RPG innovation.

D&D (1974, TSR, Lake Geneva Wisconsin):
  Gygax + Arneson publish "Dungeons & Dragons" through TSR.
  Three booklets: Men & Magic, Monsters & Treasure,
    Underworld & Wilderness Adventures.
  Sold 1,000 copies first year; word spread via convention play.

  THE REVOLUTIONARY STRUCTURE:
    - No board (the Dungeon Master describes the environment)
    - No fixed victory condition
    - Characters persist and grow across sessions
    - Collective narrative creation
    - Dungeon Master as referee, narrator, and world creator
    - Combat resolution via dice + modifiers

  From Wargaming → From Kriegsspiel's umpire →
    Into: collaborative fiction with mechanical resolution.
    One of the genuinely novel game structures in history.
```

## Section 3: The Eurogame Revolution

### What Is a Eurogame?

```
EUROGAME vs AMERICAN-STYLE GAME
══════════════════════════════════

AMERICAN-STYLE ("Ameritrash"):     EUROGAME:
────────────────────────────       ─────────
Theme-heavy (war, horror, sci-fi)  Abstract or light theme
Player elimination common          No player elimination
Luck-heavy (dice combat)          Luck minimal or mitigated
Direct player conflict             Indirect conflict only
Long playing time                  Fixed, shorter playing time
Complex rules, exceptions          Elegant rules, few exceptions
Cool components/miniatures         Functional components
High drama and chaos               Consistent decision quality
Monopoly, Risk, Axis & Allies      Catan, Agricola, Puerto Rico

Neither is objectively better. Preference is aesthetic.
The "Ameritrash vs Euro" debate on BoardGameGeek
is one of the internet's longest-running game debates.
```

### Klaus Teuber and Settlers of Catan (1995)

```
SETTLERS OF CATAN — THE EUROGAME WATERSHED
════════════════════════════════════════════

CREATOR: Klaus Teuber (1952-2023, Germany).
  Dental technician who designed games as a hobby.
  Won Spiel des Jahres three times.
  Settlers (1995) was his career peak.

"DIE SIEDLER VON CATAN" (1995, Kosmos, Germany):
  Won Spiel des Jahres 1995 — the most prestigious
  German board game award (equivalent to an Oscar for games).
  Mayfair Games imported to US in 1996.
  English title: "The Settlers of Catan."

DESIGN INNOVATIONS:
  Modular board: hexagonal tiles create different map each game.
  Resource production: dice roll each turn determines which
    hexes produce (wheat, ore, brick, wool, lumber).
  Trading: players can trade freely with each other and with bank.
  No player elimination: you can always trade, always build.
  Settlement/City/Road building creates an economic engine.
  Longest Road / Largest Army: hidden victory point bonuses.

WHY IT MATTERED:
  1. Proved European games could sell globally.
  2. Introduced "designer game" concept to mainstream.
  3. Created the gateway drug for the hobby.
  4. Opened US market to European publishers.
  5. Spiel des Jahres became globally recognized.

RECEPTION STORY:
  Mayfair Games brought 3,000 copies to the US (1996).
  They were nervous — would Americans accept an unfamiliar game?
  Sold out immediately. Reprinted repeatedly.
  By 2015: 25+ million copies sold in 30+ languages.
  By 2023: 40+ million copies.
```

### The Mechanism Taxonomy

Modern board game designers think in terms of mechanisms — the structural building blocks that can be combined to create games. This taxonomy emerged from the BGG community and designer discourse:

```
GAME MECHANISM TAXONOMY
════════════════════════

WORKER PLACEMENT:
  Players have limited "worker" tokens.
  Each worker occupies one action space (blocking others).
  Actions: gather resources, build structures, convert goods.
  Examples: Agricola, Viticulture, Everdell, Terraforming Mars.
  Mechanism origin: largely invented by Uwe Rosenberg (Agricola).

DECK-BUILDING:
  Players start with weak identical decks.
  Purchase better cards from a shared market.
  Shuffle purchased cards into deck.
  Your deck improves each shuffle cycle.
  Examples: Dominion (2008, originator), Thunderstone, Ascension.
  Key innovation (Donald X. Vaccarino, Dominion):
    The deck IS the game state, not just the tool.

AREA CONTROL:
  Players place units on a map.
  Control regions for points or resources.
  Combat determines contested regions.
  Examples: Risk (classic), Small World, Blood Rage, Twilight Imperium.

COOPERATIVE GAMES:
  All players vs. the game system.
  Win or lose together.
  Communication about strategy is unrestricted (usually).
  Examples: Pandemic, Spirit Island, Arkham Horror, Gloomhaven.
  Innovation: "traitor" variant (one player is secretly working
    against the group) — Battlestar Galactica, Dead of Winter.

LEGACY GAMES:
  Permanent changes to the game based on outcomes.
  Write on the board, tear up cards, add stickers.
  Game evolves over multiple sessions (a "campaign").
  Each session is simultaneously repeatable (you own it)
    and irreversible (you changed it).
  Examples: Pandemic Legacy, Gloomhaven, Betrayal Legacy.
  First true legacy game: Risk Legacy (Rob Daviau, 2011).

ROLL-AND-WRITE:
  Roll dice (or draw cards). Write results on personal sheet.
  Minimal interaction; simultaneous play.
  Examples: Yahtzee (early), Wingspan (dice-drafting variant),
  Railroad Ink, Ganz Schön Clever.

ENGINE-BUILDING:
  Build a system of synergizing components.
  Components generate resources that buy more components.
  Goal: achieve most efficient engine, then score.
  Examples: Wingspan, Race for the Galaxy, Gizmos.
```

## Section 4: Social Deduction Games

### Mafia/Werewolf — The Original

```
MAFIA / WEREWOLF
═════════════════

ORIGIN: "Mafia" invented by Dmitry Davidoff (Soviet
  psychologist) at Moscow State University, 1986.
  Teaching social psychology experiment.
  Students assigned roles: Mafia members (know each other),
  Villagers (know nothing about who's Mafia).

CORE MECHANIC — TWO PHASES:
  NIGHT: Eyes closed. Mafia members silently select victim.
  DAY: Eyes open. Group discussion and vote. Eliminate one player.
  Repeat until: Mafia = half or more of remaining players (Mafia wins)
               OR: all Mafia eliminated (Villagers win).

THE GAME-THEORETIC STRUCTURE:
  Villagers: imperfect information (don't know Mafia roles).
  Mafia: imperfect information (don't know special role distribution)
         but PERFECT information about who is Mafia.
  Bayesian reasoning: villagers update beliefs about Mafia
    membership based on behavior, accusations, voting patterns.
  Mafia: must coordinate silently, create false narratives.

SPREAD:
  From Moscow → Novosibirsk → US (via Princeton, early 1990s)
  Published as "Werewolf" in Bavaria, Germany (~1994).
  Became "Werewolf" for thematic reasons (fantasy theme cleaner).
  Commercial versions: Ultimate Werewolf, One Night Ultimate Werewolf.

DIGITAL DESCENDANT: AMONG US (InnerSloth, 2018):
  Effectively Mafia in a video game with visual tasks.
  COVID-19 pandemic (2020): exploded to 500M+ players.
  Streaming channels on Twitch: millions of concurrent viewers.
  Secret Hitler (2016): Mafia mechanic with asymmetric
    role complexity and deduction.
```

### Codenames and Modern Party Games

```
NOTABLE MODERN GAME DESIGNS
═════════════════════════════

CODENAMES (Vlaada Chvátil, Czech Games Edition, 2015):
  Two teams. 25 word-cards on table.
  Each team has a "spymaster" who knows which words
  are their team's "agents."
  Spymaster gives one-word clue + number to help team
  identify multiple cards at once.
  Risk: incorrect guess may hit the assassin (instant loss)
    or opponent's agents (free turn for opponent).
  Mechanism: associative reasoning under competitive pressure.
  Won Spiel des Jahres 2016.

DIXIT (Jean-Louis Roubira, 2008):
  Art cards with surreal imagery.
  Active player gives a clue (word, phrase, sound, song).
  Others choose which card best matches clue.
  Scoring: you want some (not all) to guess your card correctly.
  If all guess correctly: you get no points (clue too obvious).
  If none guess correctly: you get no points (clue too obscure).
  Optimal play: ambiguity targeting.
  Won Spiel des Jahres 2010.

PANDEMIC (Matt Leacock, Z-Man Games, 2008):
  Players are disease-fighting specialists.
  Cooperative: all win or all lose.
  Diseases spread algorithmically from city to city.
  Players must cure 4 diseases before outbreaks cascade.
  "Epidemic" cards accelerate disease spread.
  Skill: resource management, action prioritization, role synergy.
  Legacy version (2015): won multiple awards, permanently altered
    game narrative across 12+ sessions.
  COVID-19 gave Pandemic massive pop-culture attention.

WINGSPAN (Elizabeth Hargrave, Stonemaier Games, 2019):
  Birds as engine-building currency.
  Each bird card has power + habitat it occupies.
  "Engines" created by birds in same habitat.
  Roll custom dice to gather food.
  Accessible ruleset + excellent production = breakout hit.
  2020 Kennerspiel des Jahres winner.
```

## Section 5: Kickstarter and the Board Game Industry Transformation

### The Crowdfunding Revolution

```
KICKSTARTER AND BOARD GAMES
════════════════════════════

PRE-KICKSTARTER (before 2009):
  Small publishers: print run of 2,000-5,000 games.
  Distribution: limited to hobby game stores.
  Marketing: word-of-mouth, convention demos.
  Risk: publishers pre-funded all production, ate unsold copies.

KICKSTARTER MODEL (post-2009):
  Design game. Create visual prototype. Launch campaign.
  Pledge levels: $50-100 for standard edition, etc.
  Only manufactured if goal reached → no unsold inventory risk.
  Stretch goals: additional content unlocked at higher funding.
  Backers: partial co-investors (get the game + extras).

KEY CAMPAIGNS:
  Zombicide (2012): $2.3M (one of first million-dollar campaigns)
  Exploding Kittens (2015): $8.8M in 30 days — most-backed game
    on Kickstarter at the time.
  Gloomhaven (2017): $12.9M (3rd campaign), became
    #1 rated game on BoardGameGeek, ~250,000 sold.
  Frosthaven (2020): $12.9M — matching Gloomhaven.
  Lancer (2023): $1.5M+ for a tabletop RPG.

MARKET TRANSFORMATION:
  Traditional publishers (Hasbro, Mattel): family games
    distributed through mass market (Target, Walmart).
  Hobby game publishers: direct-to-consumer or FLGS
    (Friendly Local Game Store).
  Crowdfunding created a third tier: premium "deluxe" games
    with better components, higher price points, and
    direct creator-to-consumer relationships.
  Dark side: fulfillment failures, vaporware projects,
    and "Kickstarter fatigue" have limited growth.
```

## Section 6: RPG Evolution — D&D to Present

```
RPG HISTORY POST-D&D
═════════════════════

TSR (D&D publisher):
  Advanced D&D (1977-1979): Gary Gygax restructures D&D.
  Multiple editions over years.
  Satanic Panic (1980s): D&D accused of promoting Satanism.
  Congressional hearings. FBI investigation.
  Generated massive free publicity — sales increased.
  "The Dark Dungeon" Jack Chick tract: unintentional marketing.

TSR bankruptcy (1997): purchased by Wizards of the Coast.
Wizards sells to Hasbro (1999).

D&D 3rd Edition (2000): Open Game License (OGL).
  Third-party publishers can create D&D-compatible material.
  Pathfinder (Paizo) emerged as major alternative.

D&D 4th Edition (2008): Controversial redesign.
  Heavy MMO influence. Combat-heavy. Some fans rejected it.
  Pathfinder siphoned market share significantly.

D&D 5th Edition (2014): Recovery.
  Streamlined rules. Broad appeal. Retained depth.
  Simultaneous with Critical Role cultural phenomenon.

CRITICAL ROLE (2015-present):
  Voice actors (Matthew Mercer DM, others playing)
  livestreaming D&D campaigns on Twitch.
  Became the most-watched Twitch channel in its genre.
  Animated Netflix series: "The Legend of Vox Machina" (2022).
  Proved tabletop RPGs work as spectator entertainment.
  Accelerated D&D mainstream crossover significantly.

OGL CRISIS (2023):
  Hasbro/WotC attempted to revoke OGL and create restrictive
  replacement (OGL 1.1). Community backlash was immediate and severe.
  Paizo, Kobold Press, others committed to moving to alternative
  open licensing (Creative Commons SRD).
  WotC retreated; released core rules under Creative Commons.
  Demonstrated community power in tabletop RPG economics.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Monopoly is the most-played board game because it's well-designed"**: Monopoly's ubiquity is a product of aggressive marketing by Parker Brothers and the suppression of alternatives. Its actual design is widely criticized by modern game designers for elimination mechanics, runaway leader problems, and luck dominance.

**"The Landlord's Game had the Atlantic City properties"**: No. Atlantic City street names were added by Charles Todd's version, which Darrow learned from Todd. Magie's original had generic street names.

**"Eurogames have no luck"**: Most Eurogames have dice or card draws, but the luck is mitigated. You can usually choose which resources to gather, when to act, etc. The design goal is: luck affects tactics, not strategy. Agriola has no dice; Puerto Rico has none; but Catan and Agricola's card system both include randomness.

**"RPGs require a board"**: D&D and most tabletop RPGs use no board. Space is described verbally by the Game Master. The "dungeon map" on graph paper is a player convenience tool, not a game component.

**"Warhammer is a board game"**: Warhammer (Games Workshop, 1983) is a miniature wargame — tabletop war gaming with painted miniatures. The game space is a physical table with terrain models. It's distinct from board games (fixed game board) and RPGs (narrative focus). Warhammer 40,000 (1987) is the most commercially successful miniature wargame in history.

**"Legacy games are just expansions"**: Legacy games permanently change between sessions — stickers applied, cards destroyed, story elements revealed. An expansion adds content; a legacy game changes irreversibly. You can't undo a Pandemic Legacy decision. Risk Legacy lets you name locations and permanently alter the board. Each copy of a legacy game becomes unique after play.
