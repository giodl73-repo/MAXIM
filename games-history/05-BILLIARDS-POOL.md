## Section 1: Origins — Ground to Table

### Ground Billiards (~1340s)

The earliest precursor to billiards was an outdoor lawn game in France (and possibly England), roughly contemporary with similar games. Players used curved mallets (from French *mail* or *maille*, related to the English word "mall" as in Pall Mall, London — named for the game played there). Balls were pushed through hoops or wickets.

The connection to both modern billiards and croquet is direct: the same game moved indoors to tables (becoming billiards) while also remaining outdoors in refined form (becoming croquet). Pall Mall in London was a famous venue for the outdoor game, giving its name to the street.

### Indoor Table (1470s)

The first documented indoor billiard table:
- Louis XI of France reportedly had a billiard table circa 1470
- Various royal inventories mention billiard tables in the 1470s-1490s
- Mary Queen of Scots played billiards (documented, ~1561)
- Shakespeare references billiards in Antony and Cleopatra (~1607)

```
EARLY TABLE DESIGN
═══════════════════

EARLY (pre-1700):                  MODERN (post-1800):
  Mace: club-shaped tool           Cue stick: thin taper,
  (flat face hits ball)            leather tip
  Used to push ball                Used to strike ball
  Useful in center of              through chalk-tipped
  table; awkward near              striking end
  cushions (walls)                 Allowed thin contact,
                                   spin application

TRANSITION (~1700):
  Players started using the handle end of the mace
  (the thin end) for shots near the cushion.
  "Handle end" → cue (from French queue = tail)
  Eventually the cue replaced the mace entirely.

TABLE EVOLUTION:
  First cushions: stuffed with hair (inconsistent bounce)
  ~1835: Vulcanized rubber cushions (Goodyear's process)
         Consistent, predictable bounce
  ~1836: Slate bed replaces wood (more level, stable)
  Modern: 3/4 inch slate, high-quality cushion rubber
```

## Section 2: Carom Billiards — The Pocketless Game

### French Billard

Carom billiards is played on a pocket-less table with only 3 balls: typically one white (cue ball), one yellow/spot white, and one red. The goal is to carom (bounce) the cue ball off both object balls in a single shot.

```
CAROM BILLIARDS SCORING
════════════════════════

STANDARD CAROM (Straight Rail):
  Cue ball must contact BOTH object balls in one shot.
  Each such shot = 1 point.
  Play to an agreed number (typically 100).

  Simple — but too simple. Players would cluster the
  balls in a corner and score indefinitely ("nurse" shots
  — keep the cluster together and stroke endlessly).

ARTISTIC BILLIARDS:
  Creative shots scored by difficulty (1-10 point scale).
  72 required shots in competition format.
  Extreme artistic shots: masse (extreme curve), piqué,
  draw with spin, etc.

THREE-CUSHION BILLIARDS (most prestigious carom form):
  Cue ball must contact AT LEAST 3 cushions (anywhere)
  AND contact both object balls in one shot.
  No more nursing: the 3-cushion requirement forces
  complex table-traversing shots.
  Skill ceiling: very high. Professional level requires
  averaging 1+ per inning (extremely difficult).

BALKLINE VARIATIONS:
  Table divided into zones by lines (1.5, 2.0 balk lines).
  Can only score N consecutive shots in each zone.
  Forces players to drive balls out of comfortable clusters.
```

### Three-Cushion Billiards Culture

Three-cushion billiards has a strong European and Latin American culture. The World Three-Cushion Championship (UMB) has been held since 1928. Top players include Torbjörn Blomdahl (Sweden) and Dick Jaspers (Netherlands). The United States had a strong billiard tradition (Willie Hoppe dominated internationally for decades) but declined relative to Europe and Asia in the late 20th century.

## Section 3: English Billiards — The Hybrid Form

English billiards uses the same basic 3-ball setup as carom but plays on a table WITH pockets (6 pockets, like a snooker table). This hybrid enables both carom scoring AND potting scoring.

```
ENGLISH BILLIARDS — DUAL SCORING
══════════════════════════════════

Table: 12 × 6 feet (full size), 6 pockets
Balls: 2 white cue balls + 1 red object ball

SCORING METHODS:
  In-off (losing hazard): pot your cue ball into a pocket
    after it has struck an object ball = 2 or 3 points
  Pot (winning hazard): pot an object ball into a pocket = 2 points
    (red = 3 if direct, 2 if via cannon off other ball)
  Cannon: cue ball strikes both object balls = 2 points
  Combinations possible in one shot = multiple scores

RUNNING UP:
  Skilled players could score hundreds of points without
  opponent getting a turn by combining cannons and hazards.
  World records: John Roberts Jr. scored hundreds of
  continuous points without opponent's intervention.

PROFESSIONAL ERA:
  Melbourne Inman, Tom Reece, Willie Smith were
  dominant English billiards professionals ~1900-1930s.
  The game slowly lost ground to snooker from ~1930s onward.
  Today snooker has completely replaced English billiards
  in professional play (though still played amateur).
```

## Section 4: American Pool — Pocket Billiards

### The American Evolution

American pool (pocket billiards) developed separately from the English tradition. The American game emphasized pocketing object balls more than the English hybrid carom/hazard play.

```
AMERICAN POOL EVOLUTION
════════════════════════

FOUR-BALL BILLIARDS (~1830s-1860s):
  4 balls (2 white cue balls, 1 red, 1 spot red).
  Both carom AND potting. Complex scoring.
  Multiple variations.

FIFTEEN-BALL POOL (~1870s):
  15 numbered balls, racked in triangle.
  Player pockets ball, scores = ball number.
  First player to 61 (sum of 1-15 = 120, need >60) wins.
  This is the origin of the "pool" table name:
    "Pool" = betting pool. Players bet into a pool,
    winner takes all. The game was a gambling game.

CONTINUOUS POOL / STRAIGHT POOL (14.1, ~1910):
  15 balls, rack 14 when 14 are pocketed (leave last ball).
  Call every shot (call pocket, call ball).
  Score 1 point per pocketed ball.
  Play to 125, 150, 200, etc.
  Considered the most demanding pool game.
  Still the standard for serious pool evaluation.

EIGHT-BALL (~1900, codified ~1920):
  15 object balls (1-7 solid, 9-15 stripe) + 8-ball.
  Players assigned solids or stripes on first pot.
  Must pocket 7 of your group, then 8-ball last.
  Most played pool game in the world globally.

NINE-BALL (~1920s):
  9 balls only. Must strike lowest numbered ball.
  Winner pots the 9-ball (by any legal means —
  combination shots, scratch-and-re-rack...).
  Fastest form. TV-friendly. Professional standard.
```

### Straight Pool (14.1) — The Skill Standard

```
STRAIGHT POOL RECORDS
══════════════════════

Willie Mosconi (1913-1993):
  "Mr. Pool" — 15 US national championships (1941-1957).
  World record HIGH RUN: 526 consecutive balls pocketed
    without missing (Springfield, Ohio, 1954).
  526 balls = 35+ racks of 15 pocketed perfectly.
  The record has never been broken in official competition.

Mosconi set EVERY major record in the game's history.
His records stood for decades. He was television's first
"Mr. Pool" — appeared regularly on ABC's Wide World of Sports.

The Hustler (1961 film):
  Paul Newman as "Fast Eddie" Felson vs. Minnesota Fats
  (Jackie Gleason). Dramatized the pool hustling culture.
  Shot at Ames Billiards, New York. Set the cultural image
  of the pool hall as a place of masculine competition,
  gambling, and skill.
  "The Color of Money" (1986) sequel: Newman returns as
  an aging player mentoring Tom Cruise.

Pool hall culture, 1920s-1960s:
  Urban pool halls (billiard rooms) were centers of
  working-class male social life.
  Hustling: pretend to be worse than you are, bet,
  then reveal true skill level. Documented across decades.
```

## Section 5: Snooker — The British Apex

### Origin Story

Snooker was invented in 1875 by British Army officers stationed in Jabalpur, India (Neville Chamberlain's mess — this Neville Chamberlain was a different person from the later Prime Minister).

The story: officers played "black pool" (15 red balls + 1 black). Chamberlain suggested adding colored balls. "Snooker" was British Army slang for an inexperienced first-year cadet or any person new to a situation. A player who missed an easy shot was called a "snooker." Chamberlain reportedly called an opponent a "snooker" when they missed, and the name stuck.

The game spread through the British military and expatriate community in India, then back to England, where it was codified and gradually replaced English billiards as the dominant cue sport.

```
SNOOKER TABLE AND BALLS
════════════════════════

TABLE: 12 × 6 feet (same as English billiards)
       but cushion geometry slightly different.
       Pockets: 4 corner pockets + 2 center side pockets.
       Smaller pocket openings than pool.
       (Pool pocket: ~4.5-5 inches; Snooker: ~3.5 inches)

BALL SET (22 balls total):
  15 × RED balls — value 1 point each
  YELLOW — 2 points
  GREEN  — 3 points
  BROWN  — 4 points
  BLUE   — 5 points
  PINK   — 6 points
  BLACK  — 7 points
  WHITE  — cue ball (no point value, just used to strike)

COLORED BALL PLACEMENT (D-line / baulk line area):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                              │
  │    Pink    Red reds reds reds               │
  │    Blue    reds reds reds reds              │
  │                   reds reds               │
  │                       reds               │
  │                                            │
  │──────────────────── baulk line ────────────│
  │ Yellow │ Brown │ Green                      │
  │   ○    │   ○   │   ○                        │
  │  (D-semicircle below baulk line)           │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Snooker Rules and Scoring

```
SNOOKER GAME STRUCTURE
═══════════════════════

FRAME (one game):
  1. Player must pot a RED first.
  2. If red is potted: player nominates a COLOR to pot next.
  3. If color potted: color returns to its spot (until all
     reds are gone). Another red must be potted next.
  4. Continue alternating red/color until all 15 reds gone.
  5. Final sequence: pot all 6 colors in ascending order
     (yellow, green, brown, blue, pink, black).
  6. Highest score at end of frame wins the frame.
  7. Matches: best-of-N frames (varies by competition).

SNOOKERS:
  A "snooker" occurs when the cue ball is placed such that
  the player cannot hit the required ball directly.
  A snookered player must escape without hitting the wrong ball
  (or accept a penalty — "foul" = minimum 4 points to opponent,
  or value of incorrectly hit ball if higher).
  Good safety play consists of deliberately snookering opponents.

FOUL AND A MISS:
  If a player cannot escape a snooker satisfactorily:
  Opponent may ask them to play the shot AGAIN.
  "Miss rule": prevents deliberate poor play to maintain
  a snooker for safety reasons.
```

### The 147 Maximum Break

The maximum possible score in a single visit to the table (a "break"):

```
147 MAXIMUM BREAK CALCULATION
══════════════════════════════

15 reds × 1 = 15 points
15 blacks × 7 = 105 points  (pot black after each red)
Yellow:         2
Green:          3
Brown:          4
Blue:           5
Pink:           6
Black:          7
─────────────────
TOTAL:         147 points

Requires: potting every single ball on the table in one visit.
  No misses. No fouls. No safety play.
  15 reds + 15 blacks, then the 6 colors in order.
  That's 36 consecutive pot-and-control shots.

FASTEST 147 IN HISTORY:
  Ronnie O'Sullivan at the 1997 UK Championships:
  Time: 5 minutes and 20 seconds.
  Commentator Mike Hallett: "That's just extraordinary."
  Ronnie used his left hand for some shots (he's right-handed).
  The BBC broadcast showed it live; audiences watched in disbelief.
  Previous record: over 8 minutes.

MOST 147s IN PROFESSIONAL SNOOKER:
  Ronnie O'Sullivan: 15+ official maximums (count varies
  by counting rules — some in exhibition matches not counted).
  Officially verified in ranking events: approximately 15.
  Next player: multiple players with 4-8 maximums.
```

### World Snooker Championship — The Crucible

```
WORLD SNOOKER CHAMPIONSHIP
════════════════════════════

HISTORY:
  First WC: 1927 (Joe Davis wins — dominated through 1946,
  retired unbeaten champion)
  Hiatus: 1952-1963 (match play era, not championship)
  Revival: 1964
  Crucible era: 1977 to present (Sheffield, England)

THE CRUCIBLE:
  The Crucible Theatre, Sheffield — capacity ~980.
  Small, intimate theatre creates electric atmosphere.
  No side tables, no barriers — players and spectators
  very close. The silence required is extreme.
  Every whisper audible. Players can hear audience breathing.
  Has generated its own legends and "Crucible curse"
  (winners who then never win again — more myth than fact).

FORMAT:
  First round: best of 19 frames
  Later rounds: progressively longer
  Final: best of 35 frames (played over two days)

CHAMPIONS (select):
  Joe Davis: first 15 champions (1927-1946, dominant era)
  Ray Reardon: 6 titles (1970s)
  Steve Davis: 6 titles (1980s)
  Stephen Hendry: 7 titles (1990s-2000s) — record
  Ronnie O'Sullivan: 7 titles (matched Hendry) — active
  Mark Selby: 4 titles

BROADCAST:
  BBC's snooker coverage: a British institution.
  Average audience for Crucible final: millions.
  Snooker became one of the BBC's most-watched sports events.
  Ted Lowe's commentary ("for those of you watching in
  black and white, the pink is behind the green") became
  legendary when snooker was first broadcast in color.
```

## Section 6: Physics of Billiards

The physics of billiard ball collisions is interesting enough to be taught in physics courses:

```
CUE BALL PHYSICS
═════════════════

CONTACT POINT → ENGLISH (SIDE SPIN):
  Hit center: no spin (top/back/side)
  Hit above center: topspin (cue ball follows object ball)
  Hit below center: draw/backspin (cue ball reverses)
  Hit left/right: sidespin ("english")

CUE BALL PATH AFTER CENTER-BALL HIT:
  When cue ball strikes object ball at center of object ball
  (no english), the cue ball travels at 90° to the
  object ball's path. This is the "90-degree rule."

  ─────────────→ cue ball
                 ○────────────→ object ball (full ball contact)
                 ↓
                 cue ball deflects 90° from object ball path
                 (with center-ball strike and no spin)

  This principle is used for position play:
  To get cue ball to a specific spot after potting,
  aim the object ball and use 90-degree rule to predict
  cue ball path. Spin modifies this.

THROW EFFECT:
  When the cue ball contacts an object ball, friction
  during contact throws the object ball slightly off
  the contact point direction. Affects cut shots.
  Compensated by "inside spin" or aiming adjustment.

MASSE SHOT:
  Extreme side and downward angle creates massive curvature.
  Cue held nearly vertical (60-80°).
  Ball curves dramatically around obstacles.
  Used to escape snookers, not for everyday play.
  Risk: table cloth damage.

PHYSICS OF SPIN DECAY:
  A spinning ball slows in spin as it rolls (rolling friction).
  The distance the ball travels before spin disappears
  determines how far from the contact point the cue ball
  ends up in its "natural" position.
  Professional players calculate these distances intuitively
  through thousands of practice hours.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Pool and billiards are the same"**: Pool is pocket billiards (American tradition). Billiards in its strict sense is carom (no pockets). "Billiards" is also used colloquially to mean any cue sport, but technically they're distinct games.

**"Snooker is played in the same pockets as pool"**: Snooker pockets are smaller (about 3.5 inches vs 4.5-5 inches for pool). Snooker balls are also smaller (52.5mm vs 57.15mm for pool). The tighter pocket on a larger table makes snooker demanding in a different way from pool.

**"The 147 break happens often in professional snooker"**: It's extremely rare. Over the entire professional era (~80+ years), there have been perhaps 200+ official 147s total — many by Ronnie O'Sullivan. A full season of professional snooker might see 5-10 maximums at most.

**"English put on the cue ball is the same as sidespin in pool"**: "English" and "side" are the same thing (side spin). The term "English" is an American term; British players say "side." Origin of "English" is disputed — possibly from "angle" corrupted, or from English-style play.

**"Carom billiards requires less skill than pool"**: Three-cushion billiards is arguably the most technically demanding cue sport. Professional three-cushion players routinely average 1+ point per inning; achieving a run of 10 is an extraordinary achievement. The position play required to control 3 balls across 3 cushions exceeds snooker in geometric complexity.
