# Watchmaking — 06 The Wristwatch Era
## From Pocket to Wrist: Industry, Complications, and the Luxury Economy of Mechanical Time

---

## The Big Picture

The wristwatch emerged from practicality (WWI), was industrialized by Swiss geography and labor organization, reached a technical apex in the mid-20th century complications arms race, survived the quartz crisis through positioning as luxury artifact rather than time-telling instrument, and now constitutes a ~$10B+ annual market in which accuracy is largely irrelevant to pricing.

```
WRISTWATCH ERA TIMELINE

1880s–1910s  Early wristwatches: ladies' jewelry items; men's watches worn
             in fabric "wristlets" (leather straps with pocket watch)

1914–1918    WWI: officers synchronize attacks; pocket watch impractical in
             trenches while handling weapons; mass adoption of wristwatches
             "Trench watch" features: luminous dials (radium paint; later
             tritium); wire lugs permanently attached to case; shrapnel guard
             over crystal

1930         Wristwatches outsell pocket watches in UK for first time

1926         Rolex Oyster: first waterproof wristwatch case (screw-back,
             screw crown); demonstrates that cases can be engineered
             for environmental protection

1931         Rolex Perpetual: first automatic wristwatch with rotor winding
             (Emile Borer's design); wrist motion winds the mainspring

1950s–1960s  Complications arms race: perpetual calendars, tourbillons,
             minute repeaters in wristwatch form; major complications in
             pocket watches downscaled with difficulty

1969         Seiko Quartz Astron: quartz wristwatch; ±5 sec/year
             Swiss mechanical industry begins multi-decade crisis

1970s–80s    Swiss employment: 90,000 → 28,000 watchmakers
             Many iconic brands sold, merged, or failed

1983         Swatch: repositions low-end watch as fashion accessory
             Swiss mechanical mid-range begins recovery as luxury market

1990s–2000s  High-end mechanical watch market recovers and grows
             Tourbillons, perpetual calendars, minute repeaters
             become luxury status markers, not accuracy tools

2010s–pres   Watch as investment vehicle; PP 5711 trades at 4× retail
             Independent makers; finishing quality as differentiator
```

## Pocket to Wrist: The WWI Transition

**Pre-war context:** Wristwatches were considered jewelry for women. Serious men used pocket watches — reliable, accurate, long-running, with visible seconds hands for timing. The wristwatch was seen as effeminate and impractical (the crown and setting mechanism are vulnerable when exposed on the wrist).

**WWI changes everything:** Synchronized military operations require officers to know the exact time without fumbling for a pocket watch while also handling a map, compass, and sidearm. Artillery barrages are timed to the second. Machine gun teams coordinate on precise windows. The wristwatch — strapped to the left wrist, readable with a glance — is immediately more practical.

```
TRENCH WATCH DESIGN FEATURES

┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                      TRENCH WATCH (WWI)                        │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Wire lugs: soldered or riveted loops for permanent strap       │
│  (pocket watches adapted by adding wire cages, pre-war)        │
│                                                                │
│  Luminous dial: Radium paint on hands and indices              │
│  (Ra-226, T½ = 1600 yr; intensely radioactive; later replaced  │
│   by tritium/H-3, then strontium aluminate phosphors today)    │
│                                                                │
│  Shrapnel guard: Metal cage over mineral crystal               │
│  (broken crystal in action = unusable watch)                   │
│                                                                │
│  Screw-back or snap-back cases: sealed against mud and water   │
│                                                                │
│  Large crown: gloves-on winding/setting                        │
│                                                                │
│  Bold Arabic numerals: legibility in poor light                │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

By 1930, wristwatches outsold pocket watches in Britain. By 1940, the pocket watch was essentially a niche product for older men and train conductors. By 1960, it was antique.

## The Swiss Watch Industry: Geography and Structure

Switzerland's dominance in watchmaking is not coincidental — it emerged from a specific combination of geography, Protestant work ethic, guild structure, and division of labor.

### The Vallée de Joux

The Vallée de Joux (Valley of Joux) in the Jura mountains of the canton of Vaud is the historical heartland of Swiss complications work. Long winters, poor agricultural land, a tradition of cottage industry (lace-making, then watchmaking), and a concentration of technically sophisticated families.

The ebauche system is the Swiss watch industry's platform architecture: ETA is the movement OS; brands are ISVs building differentiated products on top of it. The "manufacture vs ebauche-based" distinction maps exactly to build vs buy. The Swatch Group's threat to cut off ETA supply to competitors (which spawned Sellita as an alternative supplier) is the same dynamic as a platform owner leveraging vertical integration to squeeze third-party competitors — a pattern that recurs across platform economies.

### The Ébauche System

The ébauche (plural: ébauches) is a partially manufactured movement blank — the main plate, bridges, wheels, and mainspring barrel in rough-cut form, without jewels, springs, or finishing. Ébauche makers:

- **ETA (Eta SA):** By far the largest; owned by Swatch Group; makes movements for hundreds of brands; their ETA 2824-2 and Valjoux 7750 are ubiquitous
- **Sellita:** Independent competitor to ETA, emerged when Swatch Group threatened to stop supplying ETA movements to competing brands
- **Soprod:** Smaller independent supplier
- **Miyota (Citizen subsidiary):** Japanese; supplies movements to many lower-tier Swiss-branded watches

```
MOVEMENT SUPPLY CHAIN

ETA SA (movement manufacturer)
        │
        │ ébauche (rough movement blank)
        ▼
Watch brand's assembly / finishing facility
        │ adds: jewels, springs, regulation, timing
        │ optionally: decorative finishing (stripes, anglage, perlage)
        │ optionally: additional complications
        │ stamps with brand name on movement
        ▼
Complete movement → cased → dialed → sold

For ETA-based watches: ETA 2824 under 100 different brand names.
For "in-house" (manufacture) movements: brand designs and makes
their own movement from blank. Commands premium price.
Manufacture capability: Rolex, Patek, AP, Jaeger-LeCoultre, Omega, Lange...
```

**"Manufacture" vs "Ebauche-based":** A "manufacture" (mah-nu-FAHK-teur) is a brand that designs and manufactures its own movements in-house. In French, "manufacture" means factory — it's not the English word "manufacture." Patek Philippe, Rolex, and Jaeger-LeCoultre are manufactures. Many other brands use ETA or Sellita movements with their own dials and cases.

## Major Complications

A "complication" in watchmaking is any function beyond simple hours and minutes display. They demonstrate technical mastery and command premium prices.

### The Tourbillon (Abraham-Louis Breguet, 1801 Patent)

Breguet's insight: a pocket watch is typically carried in the same position (vertical, in the waistcoat pocket). Gravity consistently acts on the balance wheel in one direction, biasing the rate. If the escapement cage rotates continuously, gravity's effect is averaged out.

```
TOURBILLON MECHANISM

Standard lever escapement: fixed to movement plate
  Gravity acts consistently in one direction
  → creates systematic rate bias in vertical position

Tourbillon: escape wheel + lever + balance wheel
  mounted in a rotating cage
  cage rotates once per minute (typically)

Top view of tourbillon cage rotating:
  0:00  escape wheel bias ← from left
  0:15  escape wheel bias ↑ from bottom
  0:30  escape wheel bias → from right
  0:45  escape wheel bias ↓ from top

After one full rotation: gravity bias averages to zero

ENGINEERING REALITY CHECK:
  - Wristwatch position changes constantly → tourbillon provides minimal benefit
    vs well-regulated fixed lever escapement
  - Pocket watch held vertical, same position → tourbillon IS beneficial
  - Modern tourbillon manufacture: 70–100 parts in cage, each a few tenths of mm
    Total cage weight: <0.3g (to minimize inertia addition to balance)
  - A poorly finished tourbillon (cheap "flying" tourbillons, etc.) may be LESS
    accurate than a quality fixed lever escapement due to added moving mass

PRICE REALITY:
  Tourbillon movement: $15,000–$50,000+ wholesale (movement alone)
  Retail: $30,000–$300,000+
  High-end (AP, PP, Breguet): $100,000–$1,000,000+
```

The tourbillon is the canonical example of a mechanism that was technically meaningful when invented (1801, pocket watches) and became primarily a prestige marker by the time it was widely adopted in wristwatches.

**Modern variants:**
- Flying tourbillon: cage appears to float (no upper bridge spanning the cage)
- Multi-axis tourbillon: cage rotates on 2 or 3 axes; each additional axis requires additional gear stages; adds weight without additional accuracy benefit
- Peripheral tourbillon: cage driven from the rim rather than center; allows unobstructed view of movement

### Perpetual Calendar

A perpetual calendar mechanism automatically advances the date, day, month, and (often) moon phase, accounting for months of different lengths and for leap years.

```
PERPETUAL CALENDAR — MECHANICAL IMPLEMENTATION

The problem:
  January:  31 days
  February: 28 days (29 in leap years)
  March–August: 28/30/31 alternating
  September–December: 30/31 alternating
  Century years not divisible by 400 are NOT leap years
    (2100 will not be a leap year — most perpetual calendars require
     one correction that year)

Mechanical solution — cam/lever cascade:

  ANNUAL CAM: one revolution per year
    Profile encodes the number of days in each month
    Lever follows cam profile → tells date wheel when to skip
    February notch deepest → jumps from 28 (or 29) to 1
    Other short months: shallower notch → jumps from 30 to 1

  LEAP YEAR CAM: one revolution per 4 years
    Deeper February notch in non-leap years (28-day skip)
    Shallower February notch in leap years (29-day skip)

  MOON PHASE DISC:
    Driven at (29.5 day)^-1 rate
    Synodic month: 29 days, 12 hours, 44 minutes, 3 seconds
    Standard mechanical moon phase: 29.5 days
    Error: ~1 day per 2.7 years (correct manually every 3 years or so)
    High-accuracy moon phase: 135 teeth → 29d 12h 45m 19s
    Error: ~1 day per 122.6 years

Parts count: ~1000 parts for a full grande complication perpetual calendar
Patek Philippe 5970: 556 parts just for the perpetual calendar mechanism
Most robust implementation: PP, AP, JLC (Geophysic True Second, etc.)
```

**The 2100 problem:** Most perpetual calendars treat every fourth year as a leap year. The Gregorian calendar rule is: divisible by 4 = leap year, EXCEPT divisible by 100 = not a leap year, EXCEPT divisible by 400 = leap year. So 1900 was not a leap year; 2000 was; 2100 will not be. A perpetual calendar that doesn't account for this will show Feb 29, 2100 (incorrect) and be one day off from March 2100 onward. Correction requires manually advancing the date by one day. Some high-end brands (IWC with the 5000-year calendar mechanism) implement the 100-year/400-year exception.

### Minute Repeater

A minute repeater strikes the time on demand (typically by pushing or pulling a slide on the case) using gongs (not bells — a common misstatement).

```
MINUTE REPEATER — MECHANISM

Output: audible chimes indicating hours, quarter-hours, and minutes

Three gong types (typically):
  HOURS: single gong, low pitch
         strikes once per complete hour
         e.g., 3:00 PM → three low strikes

  QUARTER HOURS: two-gong chord (ding-dong)
                 strikes once per quarter hour
                 e.g., 3:47 → three quarter strikes (ding-dong ding-dong ding-dong)

  MINUTES: single gong, high pitch
           strikes once per minute past the last quarter
           e.g., 3:47 → two high strikes (7 - 15×2 = 7... wait no:
           3:47 = 3 hours + 3 quarters (3×15=45 min) + 2 minutes
           → three low + three ding-dong + two high

MECHANISM:
  Slide winds a small auxiliary spring (separate from mainspring)
  Snail cams on the time train read the current hour, quarter, and minute
  Locking mechanism releases hammers sequentially:
    1. Hour snail sets hour rack → hour hammer strikes low gong N times
    2. Quarter snail sets quarter rack → two hammers strike ding-dong chord N times
    3. Minute snail sets minute rack → minute hammer strikes high gong N times
  All-or-nothing: if insufficient energy in slide spring, mechanism
  doesn't fire at all (prevents partial striking which would be confusing)

ACOUSTIC ENGINEERING:
  Gongs are coiled springs (like a watch spring, but tuned for acoustics)
  Mounted to the movement plate via a "foot" that amplifies vibration
  into the case and ultimately the outside air
  Case material matters: gold resonates well; steel less so
  Caseback must be open or thin-metal for sound transmission
  Solid caseback (for durability) kills the acoustic output

Patek Philippe 5078G: 500+ parts in the repeating mechanism alone
Price: $300,000–$500,000+
```

### Chronograph

A chronograph (not "chronometer" — different word) is a stopwatch function integrated into the watch. "Chrono" = time; "graph" = to write; originally, these mechanisms drew a mark on a paper tape. Now: a separate seconds hand (or sub-seconds register) that starts, stops, and resets independently.

```
CHRONOGRAPH TYPES

STANDARD CHRONOGRAPH:
  Pusher at 2 o'clock: start/stop
  Pusher at 4 o'clock: reset (only when stopped)
  Mechanism: column wheel OR cam-actuated coupling

FLYBACK CHRONOGRAPH:
  Single pusher: start/stop/reset in one action
  Push while running: resets AND restarts immediately
  Military and aviation use: timing multiple sequential events
  without the start/stop/reset sequence

SPLIT-SECONDS (Rattrapante):
  Two overlapping seconds hands
  One starts with the pusher; the other can be stopped independently
  to mark a split time, then "rattrapante" (catches up) to rejoin the first
  Used for timing competitors, partial laps

COLUMN WHEEL vs CAM:
  Column wheel: rotating wheel with columns/teeth that actuate levers;
    precise engagement, smooth action, expensive to manufacture;
    used by AP, Patek, JLC, Rolex (modern)
  Cam system: plate cams cheaper to produce; functionally equivalent;
    early Valjoux ebauches used cams; ETA 7750 uses cam system
```

## The Luxury Economics of Mechanical Watches

Post-quartz crisis, the mechanical watch industry repositioned entirely. Mechanical watches are not purchased as accurate timekeepers — every phone is more accurate by 3–4 orders of magnitude. The purchase motivations:

```
PRICE vs ACCURACY — MECHANICAL WATCHES

$200–$500   | Entry: Seiko, Orient; ETA ébauche or in-house Seiko NH35
            | Accuracy: ±10–30 sec/day
            | Why buy: reliable, repairable, aesthetic, mechanical curiosity

$500–$2,000 | Mid-range: Tissot, Longines, Hamilton, Mido, Tissot
            | Often COSC-certified; ETA or Sellita ébauche
            | Accuracy: ±4–8 sec/day

$2,000–$10,000 | Upper-mid: TAG Heuer, IWC entry, Omega, Tudor
               | Often COSC; sometimes in-house movement
               | Accuracy: ±3–6 sec/day; Omega: ±0–5 (cased)

$10,000–$50,000 | Luxury: Rolex, AP, Jaeger-LeCoultre, JLC, IWC
                | In-house movements; hand-finished; sometimes complications
                | Accuracy: ±2–4 sec/day (Rolex: ±2); PP Seal: ±2 (max slow)

$50,000–$1M+ | Ultra-luxury / grand complication
              | PP, AP Royal Oak Tourbillon, AP Ultra-Complication
              | Perpetual calendar, minute repeater, tourbillon
              | Accuracy: comparable to simpler movements (±2–5 sec/day)
              | (The complications don't improve rate accuracy)

The accuracy ceiling is reached at ~$500–$2,000. Everything above that
buys finishing quality, craftsmanship, complications, brand heritage, rarity.
```

**The co-axial escapement (George Daniels, 1974; Omega, 1999):**
George Daniels, the foremost English watchmaker of the 20th century, invented a modified lever escapement that requires significantly less lubrication at the pallet-escape wheel interface. The co-axial escapement uses a third pallet to separate the impulse and locking functions, reducing sliding friction. Omega licensed it in 1999. Claim: service intervals extended from 5 years to 10+ years. This is a genuine engineering improvement, not marketing — it addresses the primary mechanical wear failure mode.

## Common Confusion Points

**"In-house movements are always better than ETA-based movements."**
Not necessarily. An ETA 2892-A2 that is well-regulated and serviced is more accurate than a sloppy in-house movement. "In-house" means the brand controls the entire manufacturing process — quality can vary across brands. Some brands use in-house movements for marketing prestige while their movements are less refined than their ETA-based predecessors. Seiko's NH35 (in-house, affordable) is exceptional value; some boutique "manufacture" movements are expensive and mediocre.

**"A tourbillon wristwatch is more accurate than a non-tourbillon wristwatch."**
Modern test data consistently shows tourbillon wristwatches are no more accurate — often slightly less accurate — than high-quality fixed lever escapement movements. The tourbillon benefit exists only in vertical pocket watches in a single fixed position. A wristwatch on a moving wrist already changes position constantly, which averages out the gravity effect that the tourbillon was designed to address.

**"Chronograph and chronometer are the same thing."**
No. Chronograph: stopwatch function in a watch. Chronometer: a watch that has passed a precision timing test (typically COSC). A watch can be both (Rolex Cosmograph Daytona, Omega Speedmaster Professional), or either one, or neither.

This is a canonical Innovator's Dilemma case (Christensen uses it explicitly): the Swiss invented the disrupting technology, evaluated it against their high-margin incumbent products, and chose not to cannibalize themselves. The same structural pattern as Kodak inventing digital photography, Nokia having a touchscreen prototype in 2004, or Blockbuster passing on Netflix. The failure was organizational, not technological.

**"The Swiss watch industry was killed by Japan in the 1970s."**
More precisely: by Japanese quartz technology, led by Seiko, but the crisis was precipitated by Swiss domestic invention. CEH (Centre Electronique Horloger), a Swiss consortium, developed quartz watch technology in the 1960s and demonstrated it at Neuchâtel Observatory in 1967 (before Seiko's Astron). The Swiss industry chose not to aggressively commercialize it — a strategic error. By the time they understood the threat (early 1970s), Seiko and Citizen had established mass production. The Swiss were undermined by their own technology development that they failed to exploit.
