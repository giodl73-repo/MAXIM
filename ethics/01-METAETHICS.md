## Moral Realism

### Naturalism

```
MORAL NATURALISM
=================

THESIS: Moral facts are natural facts.
"Good" refers to some natural property.
Moral knowledge is empirical, not sui generis.

VERSIONS:
  1. HEDONISTIC NATURALISM: "Good" = pleasure, "bad" = pain.
     "Causing suffering is wrong" = "causing suffering produces pain"
     = a natural fact.

  2. FUNCTIONALIST NATURALISM: "Good" = what promotes human flourishing.
     Peter Railton: moral facts = what would benefit a fully
     rational, fully informed agent.

  3. CORNELL REALISM (Boyd, Sturgeon, Brink):
     Moral properties are natural properties, but not analytically
     reducible.
     "Flourishing" is a natural kind; the concept is epistemic,
     not definitional.

G.E. MOORE'S OPEN QUESTION ARGUMENT (1903):
  For any proposed natural property F:
  It is always an open (non-trivial) question to ask:
  "Yes, X is F -- but is X good?"

  Example: "Causing pleasure is good."
  Even if we define good = pleasure-producing,
  the question "but is causing pleasure *good*?" still seems open.
  This suggests "good" =/= any natural property.
  "Good" names a non-natural property.

  The argument:
  IF "good" = "pleasure-producing" (analytically)
  THEN "Is pleasure-producing things good?" = "Are pleasure-producing
  things pleasure-producing?" -- trivially true, not open.
  But it IS a meaningful open question.
  THEREFORE: "good" =/= "pleasure-producing" analytically.
  THEREFORE: naturalism fails as an analytical reduction.

  NATURALIST RESPONSE (Cornell realism):
  The identification is a posteriori, not a priori.
  "Water = H2O" is not analytically true either.
  The open question argument only defeats analytical naturalism.
  Synthetic/empirical naturalism survives.
```

### Non-Naturalism

```
MORAL NON-NATURALISM (G.E. Moore, Plato's Forms)
==================================================

THESIS: Moral facts are genuine facts, but not natural facts.
They are sui generis -- a unique kind of fact.

MOORE: "Good" is a simple, indefinable, non-natural property.
Just as "yellow" is simple and indefinable (you can't define
yellow by listing its physical correlates without circularity),
"good" is simple and indefinable.

We know good by moral intuition -- a direct, non-inferential
apprehension of moral facts.

MACKIE'S QUEERNESS OBJECTION (1977, "Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong"):
  If moral non-naturalism is true:
  1. Moral facts are metaphysically queer (unlike anything else in nature)
  2. Our knowledge of them would require epistemically queer faculties
     (moral intuition as a special faculty)
  3. We have no good reason to believe in such facts or faculties

  Better explanation: moral judgments do not report facts.
  We explain moral discourse without positing queer entities.

CONSTRUCTIVISM (Korsgaard, Street, Rawls):
  Moral facts are constructed by practical reason or agreement,
  not discovered in nature.
  Korsgaard: Moral requirements are constituted by our commitments
  as rational agents. Reflective endorsement = creating normative facts.
  Not platonic non-naturalism; not naturalism.
  A third way.
```

## Moral Anti-Realism

### Expressivism

```
EXPRESSIVISM
=============

THESIS: Moral statements do not express propositions.
They express ATTITUDES (emotions, prescriptions, commitments).

A.J. AYER'S EMOTIVISM (1936, "Language, Truth and Logic"):
  "Murder is wrong" = expressing disapproval of murder.
  Equivalent to saying "Murder!" in a tone of horror.
  Not a truth-apt statement.
  Moral disagreements are expressions of conflicting attitudes,
  not disputes about facts.

OBJECTION TO AYER (Frege-Geach Problem):
  "If murder is wrong, then getting one's little brother to commit
  murder is wrong."
  Here "murder is wrong" occurs in a non-assertoric context (if...).
  You can't express disapproval in a conditional.
  The sentence makes logical sense.
  Ayer's theory can't explain this.

SIMON BLACKBURN: QUASI-REALISM (1984, 1998)
  Expressivism can simulate realist-seeming discourse.
  Moral claims: expressions of attitudes/commitments.
  "Murder is wrong" = "Boo to murder!" (attitudinally)
  But we can construct logical operators for attitudes:
  "If boo-murder, then boo-getting-your-brother-to-murder"
  = conditional on having anti-murder attitude, extend it.
  This reconstructs the logic of moral discourse within expressivism.

  Quasi-realism: earn the right to speak as if moral realism is true,
  from an expressivist starting point.

GIBBARD: NORM-EXPRESSIVISM (1990, "Wise Choices, Apt Feelings"):
  Moral judgments express acceptance of norms.
  "Murder is wrong" = "I accept a norm prohibiting murder."
  Norms are not facts; they are practical stances.
  Disagreement = difference in accepted norms.
  Logic of moral discourse = logic of norm-acceptance.
```

### Error Theory

```
MACKIE'S ERROR THEORY (1977)
==============================

THESIS: Moral statements are truth-apt (they ARE propositions).
But: all positive moral claims are FALSE.

Why? Because moral claims carry a presupposition:
that there are OBJECTIVE moral facts.
But: there are no objective moral facts.
Therefore: "Murder is wrong" (presupposing an objective moral fact
           that murder is wrong) is false.

MACKIE'S TWO ARGUMENTS:

1. ARGUMENT FROM RELATIVITY:
   Moral views vary widely across cultures and history.
   The best explanation: no objective facts, just different attitudes.
   If objective facts existed, we'd expect more convergence.
   (RESPONSE: scientific views also vary historically; this doesn't
   show no objective scientific facts. Disagreement doesn't prove
   no fact of the matter.)

2. ARGUMENT FROM QUEERNESS:
   See above. Objective moral facts would be metaphysically and
   epistemically bizarre.
   No good reason to believe in them.

PRACTICAL CONSEQUENCE:
  Mackie: we should still act morally.
  Morality is a useful human institution even if it lacks
  metaphysical backing.
  We can INVENT moral standards even if none are discovered.

RESPONSE (moral error theory is true but practically irrelevant?):
  Some argue: knowing error theory is true should change how
  we talk (more hedged) but not fundamentally change behavior.
  Others: it should radicalize our ethics (if nothing is objectively
  forbidden, why not do anything convenient?).
  Mackie: no, because human social life requires moral practices.
```

### Relativism

```
MORAL RELATIVISM
=================

CULTURAL RELATIVISM (descriptive):
  Moral beliefs vary by culture.
  (This is an empirical fact, not a normative thesis.)

MORAL RELATIVISM (normative):
  Moral truths are relative to cultures or frameworks.
  There is no framework-independent moral truth.

METAETHICAL RELATIVISM:
  "Murder is wrong" is true relative to Framework F1.
  "Murder is permitted" is true relative to Framework F2.
  Both are true, relatively.

PROBLEMS:
  1. Self-refutation: "All moral truths are relative" -- is this
     itself a relative or absolute claim?

  2. No cross-cultural criticism: if each culture is right by its own
     standards, we can't criticize historical slavery, genocide, etc.
     Most people find this unacceptable.

  3. Moral progress: how does cultural evolution constitute progress
     if there is no framework-independent standard?
     Abolishing slavery was progress, not just change.

MILD RELATIVISM (Harman):
  Some moral claims ("Murder is wrong in my community") are
  relative to a practice or agreement.
  Others ("The Holocaust was evil") are assessments from a
  standpoint most of us share.
  Relative and absolute moral claims can coexist.
```

## Moral Epistemology

### Intuitionism

```
MORAL INTUITIONISM (Ross 1930, Parfit)
========================================

W.D. ROSS: "The Right and the Good" (1930)
  We have direct moral knowledge of prima facie duties:
  fidelity, reparation, gratitude, non-maleficence, beneficence,
  self-improvement, justice.

  These are self-evident to careful moral reflection.
  Not known by argument from more basic premises.
  Known by intuition -- direct rational apprehension.

  PRIMA FACIE: these duties have weight but can be overridden.
  ("Prima facie" = at first appearance, other things equal.)
  The actual duty in a situation = whichever prima facie duty is
  strongest in context.

PROBLEMS:
  1. How do we resolve conflicts between prima facie duties?
     Ross: we weigh them by practical wisdom (phronesis).
     But this seems to make ethics intuition-dependent at every step.

  2. Are intuitions culturally biased?
     Different people have different intuitions.
     Which intuitions are the reliable ones?
     Moral intuitionism has no answer.

EVOLUTIONARY DEBUNKING (Sharon Street 2006):
  Our moral intuitions were shaped by natural selection.
  Natural selection tracks survival value, not moral truth.
  If moral intuitions evolved for survival, they're not tracking
  mind-independent moral facts.
  This is a debunking argument against moral intuitionism.
```

### Reflective Equilibrium (Review)

```
REFLECTIVE EQUILIBRIUM AND ITS LIMITS
=========================================

Strengths: avoids dogmatism; iterative; responsive to new cases.
Weaknesses:
  1. No guarantee of truth: coherent evil systems are possible.
  2. Parochialism: equilibrium may reflect cultural starting points.
  3. Path dependence: different starting intuitions lead to different
     stable equilibria.

WIDE REFLECTIVE EQUILIBRIUM (Rawls):
  Include background theories (moral psychology, societal theory,
  ideals of the person) in the equilibrium.
  More constrained; less parochially personal.

  But: still doesn't guarantee access to mind-independent moral truth.
  Rawls himself was a constructivist: moral facts are constructed,
  not discovered. Wide RE constructs them.
```

## Metaethics and Normative Ethics

```
DOES METAETHICS CONSTRAIN NORMATIVE ETHICS?
============================================

WEAK CONNECTION:
  If moral realism is true: normative ethics = discovering real facts.
  If error theory is true: normative ethics = inventing useful standards.
  If expressivism is true: normative ethics = working out coherent norms.

  But: different metaethical positions can support similar normative
  conclusions. A consequentialist can be a realist, expressivist,
  or constructivist.

STRONGER CONNECTION:
  If error theory: there's no external authority behind moral rules.
  This might lead to rule utilitarianism (we should adopt rules
  that produce best outcomes, not rules we're "obligated" to follow).
  Or: to nihilism.

  If strong moral realism: moral facts are out there to be discovered.
  This might lead to natural law theory (align with nature's purposes).
  Or: to a kind of moral physics where we investigate moral facts.

IN PRACTICE:
  Most working ethicists hold their metaethical views independently
  of their normative views, with loose connections.
  Rawls: freestanding political conception (doesn't require
  metaethical commitment to realism).
  Singer: utilitarian + preference realist (but could be expressivist).
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Position | Moral claims truth-apt? | Moral facts exist? | How we know |
|---|---|---|---|
| Moral naturalism | Yes | Yes (= natural facts) | Empirical investigation |
| Moral non-naturalism | Yes | Yes (non-natural) | Moral intuition |
| Cornell realism | Yes | Yes (natural, a posteriori) | Empirical + conceptual |
| Constructivism | Yes (constructed) | Yes (constructed) | Rational reflection |
| Emotivism (Ayer) | No | No | N/A (express attitudes) |
| Quasi-realism (Blackburn) | Yes (earned) | Functionally yes | Quasi-real justification |
| Norm-expressivism (Gibbard) | Yes (norms) | No mind-independent | Coherent norm acceptance |
| Error theory (Mackie) | Yes | No — all false | They're just false |
| Cultural relativism | Yes, relatively | Relative to cultures | Cultural investigation |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Metaethics doesn't affect practical ethics."**
The connection is loose but not absent. If error theory is true and you know it, should you follow moral rules? Mackie says yes (social utility). Others say the debunking undermines motivation. At minimum, metaethics affects how confidently you hold moral views and how you respond to moral disagreement (persistent disagreement is expected if error theory is true; surprising if realism is true).

**"The open question argument refutes all naturalism."**
Moore's argument refutes *analytical* naturalism — the view that "good" is analytically equivalent to a natural property. Cornell realism holds that moral properties are natural properties discovered a posteriori, like "water = H2O." The open question argument doesn't touch this version because it doesn't claim analytical equivalence.

**"Expressivism means moral claims are just preferences."**
Sophisticated expressivism (Blackburn's quasi-realism) reconstructs the full logic of moral discourse including objectivity-talk, within an expressivist framework. The expressivist doesn't say "I prefer that you don't steal" instead of "stealing is wrong." They say the latter has the same surface form as before but expresses a norm-commitment rather than representing a fact. The logical structure is preserved.

**"If error theory is true, we should be moral nihilists."**
Error theory is a metaethical position about the metaphysical status of moral facts, not a first-order normative recommendation. Mackie explicitly said: error theory being true doesn't mean we should abandon moral practices. We should continue to operate within morality because human social life requires it. First-order conclusions don't follow mechanically from metaethical positions.

**"Moral relativism means I should respect all cultures' practices."**
This confuses descriptive relativism (moral beliefs vary) with normative relativism (each culture's morality is right for it). Even if normative relativism were true, "respect all practices" is a normative claim — which would also be relative! The enjoinder to respect all cultures is a universal normative claim inconsistent with thoroughgoing normative relativism.
