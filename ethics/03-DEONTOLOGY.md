## Kant's Ethics: The Groundwork

```
KANT: "GROUNDWORK FOR THE METAPHYSICS OF MORALS" (1785)
=========================================================

THE PROJECT:
  Kant wants to identify the supreme principle of morality
  that holds unconditionally -- not dependent on contingent
  facts about human nature, culture, or consequences.

  The principle must be:
  - A PRIORI: known by reason alone, not by experience
  - NECESSARY: true in all possible worlds
  - BINDING ON ALL RATIONAL BEINGS: not just humans

GOOD WILL:
  "Nothing can possibly be conceived in the world, or even out of it,
  which can be called good without qualification, except a good will."

  Intelligence, courage, wealth, happiness -- all can be used for ill.
  Even good consequences don't make an action morally good.
  What makes an action morally worthy: acting from DUTY.

  Acting FROM duty vs. IN ACCORDANCE WITH duty:
  Shopkeeper gives correct change because reputation demands it:
    in accordance with duty, but motivated by self-interest.
    Not morally worthy.
  Shopkeeper gives correct change because it is the right thing:
    from duty. Morally worthy.

HYPOTHETICAL vs. CATEGORICAL IMPERATIVES:
  HYPOTHETICAL: "If you want X, then do Y."
    Conditional on a desire or goal.
    Not binding if you don't have the desire.
    "If you want to be healthy, exercise."

  CATEGORICAL: "Do Y." (No conditions.)
    Binding on all rational agents regardless of desires.
    Moral commands are categorical.
    "Keep your promises." (Period. Not: "if you want to be trusted.")
```

## The Three Formulations of the Categorical Imperative

### Formulation 1: Universal Law

```
FORMULATION 1: UNIVERSAL LAW (FUL)
=====================================

"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the
same time will that it should become a universal law."

PROCEDURE:
  1. Identify your maxim (the principle you're acting on).
     "I will make a false promise when I need money."
  2. Universalize it: "Everyone will make false promises when they need money."
  3. Ask: Is this universalizable?

  TYPE 1: Contradiction in conception.
  Could you even conceive of this as a universal law?
  If everyone makes false promises: the practice of promising collapses.
  No one would believe promises. Promising would be impossible.
  The maxim defeats itself in universalization.
  VERDICT: Impermissible.

  TYPE 2: Contradiction in will.
  The law is conceivable but you couldn't rationally will it.
  "Never help others in need."
  You could conceive of a world with this law.
  But you couldn't rationally will it, because you may need help.
  As a rational agent, you can't will a world where help is unavailable.
  VERDICT: Impermissible.

PROBLEMS:
  1. Maxim formulation is too flexible.
     You can always re-describe your maxim to pass the test.
     "I will break promises when my friend's life is at stake"
     -- might universalize fine.
  2. Wrong actions can pass.
     "Always wear shoes when indoors" universalizes fine.
     This doesn't make it a moral duty.
     The test is necessary but not sufficient.
```

### Formulation 2: Humanity Formula

```
FORMULATION 2: HUMANITY FORMULA (FH)
======================================

"Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or
in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."

KEY CONCEPTS:
  RATIONAL NATURE / HUMANITY:
  Kant means: the capacity for rational self-governance.
  The ability to set ends, deliberate, and act on principle.
  This capacity has unconditional worth -- it is the source of all value.

  MEANS vs. ENDS:
  Treating as a means: using someone as an instrument for your purposes
  without regard for their own ends and choices.
  Treating as an end: respecting their rational nature; not overriding
  their capacity for self-determination.

  "Never as a means ONLY" -- you CAN use someone as means instrumentally
  if you also respect their rational nature.
  Hiring someone to do a job = using them as a means.
  But if you also pay fairly, respect their choices, inform them honestly:
  you're also treating them as an end.

EXAMPLES:
  Lying: violates Humanity Formula.
  You override the deceived person's rational agency.
  They can't make an informed choice; you manipulate their reasoning.

  Coercion: violates Humanity Formula.
  You replace their will with yours by force.

  Sexual exploitation: violates Humanity Formula.
  Using another purely as an instrument for gratification.

POWERFUL APPLICATION -- THE LYING TO MURDERER:
  Kant: even if a murderer asks where your friend is hiding,
  you may not lie.
  Reason: lying violates the categorical prohibition on treating
  rational nature as a mere means (you manipulate the murderer's
  rational processes -- even though he's a murderer).

  MOST PEOPLE FIND THIS UNACCEPTABLE.
  Kant's absolute prohibition on lying is his most criticized claim.
  Korsgaard's response: the murderer has forfeited his claim to
  truthful treatment (but this modifies Kant significantly).
  Ross: the duty of non-maleficence outweighs the duty of honesty here.
```

### Formulation 3: Kingdom of Ends

```
FORMULATION 3: KINGDOM OF ENDS (KOE)
=======================================

"Act according to maxims of a universally legislating member
of a merely possible kingdom of ends."

A KINGDOM OF ENDS:
  An ideal community of rational agents, each treating others as ends.
  Each member is simultaneously:
  - Subject to the moral law (obeys it)
  - Legislator of the moral law (as a rational being, gives the law to all)

  This is the idea of moral autonomy:
  I am not subject to a law imposed from outside.
  I am subject to a law I give to myself as a rational being.
  And this self-imposed law is the same law every rational being gives.

  AUTONOMY vs. HETERONOMY:
  Autonomy: acting on a law you give yourself as rational.
  Heteronomy: acting on a law from inclination, tradition, authority.
  Only autonomous action has full moral worth.

  SOCIAL ANALOGY:
  This is similar to Rawls' original position:
  What rules would we agree to if we were legislating for all rational agents,
  including ourselves?
  Kant's test is rational, not (as Rawls') behind a veil of ignorance.
```

## Perfect vs. Imperfect Duties

```
PERFECT AND IMPERFECT DUTIES
==============================

PERFECT DUTIES: Allow no exceptions. Must be fulfilled always.
  - Don't lie
  - Don't murder
  - Keep promises
  - Don't suicide (Kant's controversial view)
  Grounded in: contradiction in conception (universalization fails).

IMPERFECT DUTIES: Allow discretion in how/when fulfilled.
  - Develop your talents
  - Help others in need
  You have a duty to help others, but you choose when, how, and how much.
  Grounded in: contradiction in will (you couldn't will a world where
  you never helped anyone).

  IMPLICATION: Imperfect duties cannot be demanded in specific instances.
  You can't demand that I give you money right now.
  But if I never give to anyone: I'm violating an imperfect duty.
```

## W.D. Ross's Prima Facie Duties

```
W.D. ROSS: "THE RIGHT AND THE GOOD" (1930)
============================================

ROSS'S PLURALISM:
  Kant's single principle (CI) is too blunt.
  Real moral situations involve conflicts between multiple duties.
  Ross: there are multiple fundamental, irreducible prima facie duties.

PRIMA FACIE DUTIES (selected):
  +-------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | FIDELITY          | Keep promises; be truthful              |
  | REPARATION        | Make amends for past wrongs             |
  | GRATITUDE         | Reciprocate benefits received           |
  | NON-MALEFICENCE   | Don't harm others                      |
  | BENEFICENCE       | Help others where possible              |
  | SELF-IMPROVEMENT  | Improve your own virtue and intelligence |
  | JUSTICE           | Distribute goods according to merit     |
  +-------------------+------------------------------------------+

  These are not absolute -- they can be outweighed.
  The actual duty in a situation is the one whose prima facie
  weight exceeds the others.

  "Prima facie" duty = a duty that is binding unless overridden
  by a stronger duty in this context.

CONFLICTS:
  "If I can only save one person (a stranger or my child),
  who should I save?"
  Special obligations to family (gratitude, relationship) > impartial
  beneficence to strangers.
  Context matters; no formula decides.

MORAL PARTICULARISM (descendant of Ross):
  Every moral situation is unique.
  There are no exceptionless moral rules.
  Moral perception: seeing what features of a situation matter.
  (Dancy 2004: "moral particularism")
```

## Rights-Based Deontology: Nozick

```
ROBERT NOZICK: "ANARCHY, STATE, AND UTOPIA" (1974)
====================================================

CORE CLAIM:
  Rights are SIDE CONSTRAINTS on action, not goals to maximize.
  They set boundaries on how you may treat persons.
  These boundaries may not be violated even for very good consequences.

  Consequentialist: rights violations can be justified by sufficient
  good consequences.
  Nozick: "Individuals have rights, and there are things no person
  or group may do to them (without violating their rights)."

SIDE CONSTRAINTS vs. MAXIMIZING RIGHTS:
  Maximizing view: minimize total rights violations.
  Might justify violating one person's rights to prevent
  many other rights violations.
  Nozick: NO. Each person's rights set a constraint. Period.
  You may not violate one to prevent others' violations.

  WHY? The SEPARATENESS OF PERSONS.
  There is no social entity that bears the benefits and costs.
  Persons are separate. You cannot make a trade-off for a person
  that you can make for yourself over time.
  (This parallels Rawls' separateness objection to utilitarianism.)

ENTITLEMENT THEORY:
  Justice in holdings is determined by:
  1. JUST ACQUISITION: how the original holding was acquired
     (Lockean proviso: you may appropriate what is unowned if
     you leave "enough and as good" for others)
  2. JUST TRANSFER: voluntary exchange, gift, contract
  3. RECTIFICATION: correcting past injustices

  If your holdings came through just acquisition and transfer:
  they are just, regardless of the distribution that results.
  No end-state principle (like Rawls' difference principle) is valid.
  Taxation for redistribution = forced labor (taking the fruits of work).
```

## Threshold Deontology

```
THRESHOLD DEONTOLOGY
======================

PROBLEM FOR ABSOLUTE DEONTOLOGY:
  Kant says never lie, even to save your friend from a murderer.
  Nozick says never violate rights even to prevent catastrophe.
  What if a nuclear bomb will kill millions unless you kill one
  innocent person?
  Absolute deontology: still prohibited.
  Most people: this seems fanatical.

THRESHOLD DEONTOLOGY:
  Rights and duties create constraints that hold up to some threshold.
  When consequences are catastrophic enough, the constraints can yield.
  "Deontological side constraints apply unless the consequences
  of adhering to them are catastrophically bad."

  This is not consequentialism: it doesn't always maximize.
  It has deontological constraints that apply under normal conditions.
  But: at some threshold, consequentialist reasoning overrides.

WHERE IS THE THRESHOLD?
  This is the hard question and threshold deontology has no
  principled answer.
  What counts as "catastrophic"? 10 deaths? 1 million? 1 billion?
  Most threshold deontologists are vague.
  Critics: if the threshold is anywhere, then it can be found anywhere
  by clever argument -- which erodes the constraints entirely.
```

## Deontology and AI

```
DEONTOLOGICAL CONSTRAINTS ON AI
==================================

HUMANITY FORMULA APPLIED TO AI SYSTEMS:
  Users have rational nature.
  AI systems that manipulate, deceive, or exploit users
  treat them as means, not as ends.
  This grounds:
  - Transparency obligations (no deceptive design)
  - Honest communication (no manipulation)
  - Informed consent (users must understand what AI is doing)
  - Respect for user autonomy (don't override user judgment)

  MICROSOFT AI PRINCIPLES (relevant):
  These principles map onto Kantian deontological constraints:
  - Transparency = no deception
  - Fairness = treating users as ends in themselves
  - Reliability = keeping commitments (fidelity duty)

TREATING AI AS ENDS:
  The question of AI as ends is live (see 09-AI-CONSCIOUSNESS).
  If LLMs have something like rational nature (contested):
  they cannot be treated purely as instruments.
  This is a deontological argument for AI welfare research.

DUTY TO TRUTHFULNESS IN AI:
  Kant: lying is absolutely prohibited because it undermines
  the practice of communication that rational beings depend on.
  AI-generated misinformation: a Kantian analysis would classify
  this as a severe wrong, not just a consequentialist harm.
  The categorical prohibition applies regardless of consequences.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Kant says act from duty, so you should do the right thing even if you don't want to."**
More precisely: acting from duty means acting because the principle is rationally required, not because you happen to feel like it. Kant doesn't recommend suppressing all inclinations — he recommends that when duty and inclination conflict, duty wins. When they align, acting on duty and inclination simultaneously is fine. The moral worth comes from the dutiful motivation, not from the effort.

**"The categorical imperative is just the Golden Rule."**
They're related but distinct. The Golden Rule ("do to others what you want done to you") is a hypothetical: what you want matters. It's also susceptible to masochistic counter-examples (a masochist who wants pain applied universally). The categorical imperative tests the rational universalizability of principles, independently of what the agent wants.

**"Kant says you should never lie."**
Kant said this; it is one of his most criticized positions. He explicitly said you should not lie even to a murderer asking where your friend is. Most contemporary Kantians find this too extreme. Ross's prima facie duty framework allows the duty of truthfulness to be outweighed by stronger duties (preventing grievous harm). Korsgaard argues the murderer has forfeited his right to truthful treatment. Kant's absolute ban is a specific application of his principle that most people, including most Kantians, do not accept.

**"Rights-based deontology (Nozick) is the same as libertarianism."**
Nozick's entitlement theory is the philosophical foundation for libertarian political philosophy. But deontology as a moral theory is much broader: Kant was not a libertarian, Ross was not a libertarian. Rights-based deontology is one strand within a broader deontological tradition. You can be a deontologist (Kant, Ross) without being a political libertarian (Nozick).

**"The Humanity Formula means you can't use anyone as a means."**
The formula says "never as a means ONLY." It's permissible to use someone as a means in the instrumental sense (paying a plumber to fix pipes). What's prohibited is treating them *only* as a means — overriding their rational agency, deceiving them, coercing them — without also respecting their status as a rational agent with their own ends.
