## Bentham's Utilitarianism

```
JEREMY BENTHAM: "AN INTRODUCTION TO THE PRINCIPLES OF MORALS
AND LEGISLATION" (1780/1789)
================================================================

THE FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE:
  Nature has placed mankind under the governance of two sovereign
  masters: pain and pleasure.
  The right action maximizes pleasure and minimizes pain
  across ALL affected parties.
  "The greatest happiness of the greatest number."

THE FELICIFIC CALCULUS:
  Pleasures and pains can be measured and compared.
  Seven dimensions:
  +------------------+-------------------------------------------+
  | Intensity        | How strong is the feeling?                |
  | Duration         | How long does it last?                    |
  | Certainty        | How probable is it to occur?              |
  | Propinquity      | How soon will it occur? (time discount)   |
  | Fecundity        | Will it lead to more pleasures?           |
  | Purity           | Will it NOT be followed by pain?          |
  | Extent           | How many people are affected?             |
  +------------------+-------------------------------------------+

  Sum up across all affected parties.
  Choose the action with the highest aggregate.

BENTHAM'S EGALITARIANISM:
  "Each to count for one, and none for more than one."
  The pleasure of a beggar counts the same as the pleasure of a prince.
  Radical in its time.

CRITICISMS:
  1. Utility commensurable? Can you really compare pleasures quantitatively?
  2. Distribution blindness: N people each with 1 unit of pleasure
     = equal to 1 person with N units.
     This can justify redistribution (or concentration).
  3. Boundary problem: whose pleasures count? (animals? AI?)
```

## Mill's Refinements

```
JOHN STUART MILL: "UTILITARIANISM" (1863)
==========================================

QUALITY OF PLEASURES:
  Bentham: pushpin = poetry (if they produce equal pleasure).
  Mill: some pleasures are intrinsically higher.
  "Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."
  "Better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied."

  OBJECTION: How do you compare them?
  Mill's answer: those who have experienced both prefer the higher.
  "Competent judges" prefer intellectual/aesthetic pleasures.
  This introduces a paternalistic element.

HARM PRINCIPLE (On Liberty, 1859):
  The only legitimate reason to exercise power over another is
  to prevent harm to others.
  Self-regarding actions: state and society have no jurisdiction.
  Other-regarding actions: legitimate to restrict.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Drug use, sexual behavior, heresy, heterodox opinions:
  all self-regarding -- state must stay out.
  This is the founding principle of liberal political philosophy.

  TENSIONS:
  - Harm is difficult to define
  - Many actions have diffuse effects on others
  - Mill also endorsed colonial paternalism (revealing a blind spot)

RULE UTILITARIANISM (seeds in Mill):
  Instead of calculating consequences case-by-case,
  adopt RULES whose general observance produces best consequences.
  "Act on the rule that, if generally followed, maximizes utility."
  This makes utilitarianism less demanding and closer to common sense.
```

## Act vs. Rule Utilitarianism

```
ACT vs. RULE UTILITARIANISM
==============================

ACT UTILITARIANISM:
  For each action: calculate expected utility of this specific act.
  Do whichever act maximizes utility.
  Problem: can justify lying, promise-breaking, rights-violation
  in any case where consequences are favorable enough.

  "Should I testify falsely to save 5 innocent lives?"
  Act U: Yes, if that maximizes utility.
  But: this seems to violate basic obligations.

RULE UTILITARIANISM:
  For each action: consider the rule "Everyone should do X in
  circumstances like these."
  Ask: if everyone followed this rule, what would the utility be?
  Act on whichever rule produces best utility if generally followed.

  "Should lying ever be permitted?"
  Rule U: What rule about lying, if generally followed, maximizes utility?
  Answer: "Don't lie (except in specific extreme cases)"
  -- because general lying destroys social trust, which is catastrophically bad.
  Rule U often aligns with common moral intuitions.

HARE'S TWO-LEVEL THEORY:
  R.M. Hare: two levels of moral thinking.
  INTUITIVE LEVEL: Use common-sense rules (don't lie, keep promises).
    Fast, practical, appropriate for normal situations.
  CRITICAL LEVEL: Fully utilitarian calculation.
    Reserved for conflicts between rules, edge cases, policy design.

  This is analogous to compiled vs. interpreted code:
  run on compiled intuitions normally; interpret/recalculate for edge cases.
```

## Peter Singer and Preference Utilitarianism

```
PETER SINGER: PREFERENCE UTILITARIANISM AND APPLIED ETHICS
============================================================

PREFERENCE UTILITARIANISM:
  Replace pleasure/pain with preference-satisfaction.
  The right action maximizes the satisfaction of preferences.
  Advantage: more neutral about what counts as good.
  Disadvantage: whose preferences? All preferences equal?
  (Sadist's preference to cause pain counts too?)
  Singer: weighted by strength, excluding malicious preferences.

THE EXPANDING CIRCLE:
  Singer (following Bentham): the ability to suffer is the morally
  relevant criterion, not species membership, race, or intelligence.

  ANIMAL LIBERATION (1975):
  Pigs and cows can suffer.
  Their suffering counts as much as equivalent human suffering.
  Factory farming causes massive suffering.
  THEREFORE: most people are complicit in massive moral wrong.

  NOT: animals have the same rights as humans.
  Animals don't have interests in political participation.
  But: equivalent suffering should count equally.

THE DROWNING CHILD ARGUMENT:
  "If I could prevent something bad from happening,
  without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance,
  I am obligated to do it."

  A child drowning in a shallow pond:
  You would ruin your shoes to save it.
  No one would think twice.

  SAME MORAL LOGIC applies to children dying of preventable diseases:
  The cost of saving them (donating money) is less than the
  cost of the ruined shoes in your life.
  THEREFORE: you are obligated to donate until you reach the
  threshold of comparable sacrifice.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  If median US income: you are obligated to give significantly.
  Singer doesn't give an exact figure -- context-dependent.
  Effective altruism: focus on cost-effective interventions.
```

## Effective Altruism and Longtermism

```
EFFECTIVE ALTRUISM (EA)
========================

Core idea (Singer, MacAskill, Karnofsky):
  Use evidence and reason to determine how to maximize good.
  Focus on cause areas where you can do the most good.

CAUSE AREA SELECTION:
  Three criteria (EA framework):
  1. SCALE: How many people affected and how severely?
  2. NEGLECTEDNESS: How few resources currently directed here?
  3. TRACTABILITY: How much can additional resources achieve?

GiveWell analysis (2023):
  Against Malaria Foundation: ~$4,500 per life saved.
  GiveDirectly: direct cash transfers to extreme poor.
  Helen Keller International: vitamin A supplements, $3,500/life.
  Compare: typical US charitable giving, much higher cost per life.

CRITICISMS OF EA:
  1. Demandingness: EA's logic pushes toward total self-sacrifice.
  2. Systemic change: individual donations don't address root causes.
  3. Paternalism: who decides what's "effective"?
  4. Distorted priorities: near-term suffering vs. existential risk?

LONGTERMISM (MacAskill "What We Owe the Future", 2022; Ord "The Precipice"):
  Future people count morally as much as present people.
  There are vastly more future people (if civilization continues).
  10^23+ future humans if civilization lasts millions of years.
  THEREFORE: existential risk reduction is the most important cause.
  A 0.01% reduction in extinction probability = billions of saved lives.

  CRITICISMS:
  1. Speculative: we don't know future people exist.
  2. Fungibility with present harm: can justify ignoring present suffering.
  3. Strong Longtermism (Greaves/MacAskill): existential risk DOMINATES
     all other considerations. Critics: too extreme.
  4. Population ethics: total utilitarianism leads to the Repugnant Conclusion.
```

## The Major Objections

```
OBJECTIONS TO CONSEQUENTIALISM
================================

1. THE DEMANDINGNESS OBJECTION:
   Consequentialism requires you to maximize utility at all times.
   You should donate every dollar you don't strictly need.
   You should give your bone marrow to strangers.
   You have no time for personal projects, relationships, hobbies.
   This seems to eliminate the possibility of an individual life.

   RESPONSES:
   Rule U: General adoption of "give everything" is too demanding
   to be a realistic rule; a more moderate rule produces better outcomes.
   Satisficing consequentialism: "good enough" is sufficient.

2. THE INTEGRITY OBJECTION (Williams):
   Jim is captured by soldiers who are about to kill 20 villagers.
   The commander offers: Jim can kill one villager himself,
   and the rest will be freed.
   Consequentialism: Jim should kill the one (19 net lives saved).
   Williams: This violates Jim's integrity as a moral agent.
   He is being conscripted into the moral mathematics.
   His own commitments and character matter, not just outcomes.

3. THE JUSTICE OBJECTION:
   Suppose executing an innocent man prevents rioting that would
   kill 10 people. Consequentialism: execute him.
   This violates his rights and the demands of justice.
   "Separateness of persons" (Rawls): each person has a life to live;
   you can't aggregate across persons as if they were one person.

4. THE REPUGNANT CONCLUSION (Parfit):
   Total utilitarianism: maximize the sum of all welfare.
   This implies: a world with billions of people living lives
   barely worth living can be better than a world with fewer
   people living excellent lives.
   (Call the barely-worth-living world Z, the good world A.
   Z > A by total welfare if |Z| is big enough.)
   Most people find this conclusion repugnant.
   But it follows from total utilitarianism.
   This motivates average utilitarianism and other variants.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Utilitarianism says 'the ends justify the means.'"**
This is a common characterization, but it obscures the structure. Consequentialism says: assess acts by their consequences. Acts are not divided into "means" (bad) and "ends" (good) — every act is evaluated for its outcomes. The concern is that consequentialism can justify acts that have bad secondary effects (rights violations, reduced trust) when the immediate consequences favor them. This is the justice objection, not the simple "ends justify means" slogan.

**"Rule utilitarianism is just deontology in disguise."**
Rule U selects rules by their utility. Deontology grounds rules in rational principles, dignity, rights — not consequences. Rule U rule-selection is entirely consequentialist. The objection (which has force) is that rule U *behaves like* deontology in output but the *justification* is entirely consequentialist. Whether this is a feature or a flaw depends on your starting metaethical commitments.

**"Singer's drowning child argument is obviously right."**
The argument is logically valid. But many philosophers contest the key premise: "no sacrifice of comparable moral importance." Singer sets the threshold low (marginal utility decreases quickly with income for affluent people). Critics argue: the distinction between duty to rescue a drowning child in front of you (special obligations from proximity) and general obligations to distant strangers is morally significant. Distance and proximity are not morally irrelevant, even if they're not absolutely decisive.

**"Longtermism is just existential risk concern."**
Longtermism is the philosophical thesis that future people matter morally equally to present people, and that their vastly greater numbers make existential risk reduction overwhelmingly important. This has implications for many cause areas, not just AI safety: nuclear risk, biosecurity, climate, governance. Strong longtermism (it dominates all present concerns) is more controversial than weak longtermism (future people matter morally).

**"The Repugnant Conclusion can be avoided by switching to average utilitarianism."**
Average utilitarianism (maximize average welfare) avoids the Repugnant Conclusion but generates its own: adding a very happy person to a very happy world doesn't improve it (they're above average); adding a person just above average happiness improves it even if they are miserable by absolute standards. Parfit showed no simple modification avoids all problems. Population ethics is genuinely hard.
