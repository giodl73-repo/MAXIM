## Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics

### Eudaimonia and the Function Argument

```
THE FUNCTION ARGUMENT (NE Book I, 7)
======================================

Aristotle: Every activity has a function (ergon).
What is the function of a human being?
(This is the telos -- the purpose, end, or goal.)

ARGUMENT:
  Every craft, inquiry, action, and pursuit aims at some good.
  The highest good is what we aim at for its own sake, not as
  means to something else.

  What is this highest good?
  ALL AGREE: eudaimonia (happiness/flourishing/well-being).
  But: what IS eudaimonia?

  FUNCTION ARGUMENT:
  A good knife = one that performs the knife function excellently.
  A good flute player = one who plays the flute excellently.
  What is the human function?
  Not: mere life (plants share this).
  Not: perception (animals share this).
  The distinctively human function: rational activity.
  Eudaimonia = excellent performance of rational activity.
  = Virtuous activity in accordance with reason.

THE POINT:
  Eudaimonia is an ACTIVITY, not a state.
  It is what you DO when living well -- not a feeling of happiness.
  "The happy person is not the one who feels happy but the one
  who ACTS excellently."
```

### Doctrine of the Mean

```
DOCTRINE OF THE MEAN (NE Book II)
====================================

VIRTUES ARE MEANS BETWEEN EXTREMES:
  Virtue is a stable disposition to feel and act appropriately --
  not too much, not too little -- in the relevant circumstances.

  +-------------------+------------------+-------------------+
  | DEFICIENCY        | VIRTUE (MEAN)    | EXCESS            |
  +-------------------+------------------+-------------------+
  | Cowardice         | Courage          | Rashness          |
  | Miserliness       | Generosity       | Prodigality       |
  | Lack of ambition  | Proper ambition  | Vainglory         |
  | Flattery          | Truthfulness     | Boastfulness      |
  | Grumpiness        | Good temper      | Irascibility      |
  | Boorishness       | Wit              | Buffoonery         |
  | Shyness           | Modesty          | Shamelessness     |
  | Self-deprecation  | Truthfulness     | Boastfulness      |
  | Insensibility     | Temperance       | Self-indulgence   |
  | Spiritlessness    | Proper ambition  | Over-ambition     |
  +-------------------+------------------+-------------------+

RELATIVE MEAN:
  The mean is RELATIVE TO THE PERSON AND SITUATION.
  Not arithmetic mean.
  "The right amount of food for an athlete is not the right amount
  for a beginner."
  What courage requires of a soldier in battle is different from
  what it requires of a person defending their property.

HABITUATION:
  Virtues are acquired through practice.
  "We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts,
  brave by doing brave acts."
  Character is formed by repeated action.
  The virtuous person ENJOYS doing what is virtuous (not fighting it).
```

### Phronesis (Practical Wisdom)

```
PRACTICAL WISDOM (PHRONESIS)
==============================

Phronesis is the MASTER virtue that knows how to apply other virtues.
It is an intellectual virtue -- excellence of practical reasoning.

WHAT PHRONESIS DOES:
  1. Perceives the morally relevant features of a situation.
     Which features matter here? (Not all do equally.)
  2. Deliberates about how virtues apply in this specific context.
     Courage in a battle ≠ courage at a workplace confrontation.
  3. Determines the mean for this person in this situation.
  4. Acts accordingly.

PHRONESIS vs. RULE-FOLLOWING:
  Moral rules are useful heuristics.
  But rules can't specify their own application -- context matters.
  Phronesis is the meta-ability that knows how to use rules and when
  to deviate from them.

  ANALOGY (MIT TCS):
  Rules are to phronesis as formal specifications are to engineering judgment.
  A formal spec tells you WHAT to achieve.
  Engineering judgment tells you HOW to achieve it and when the spec
  needs revision.
  No formal specification fully determines its own application.
  The "judgment" that applies it is irreducible.
  Aristotle's point: same for ethics.

THE VIRTUOUS PERSON AS STANDARD:
  "What would a person of practical wisdom do?" (phronimos)
  This is Aristotle's decision procedure.
  The virtuous person is the practical standard, not an abstract rule.
  Circularity worry: virtuous = what virtuous people do.
  Response: the virtuous person is developed through a specific
  process (habituation, experience, education) that is not arbitrary.
  We recognize them reliably even without a complete definition.
```

## The Virtues in Detail

```
ARISTOTLE'S VIRTUE LIST (SELECTED)
=====================================

INTELLECTUAL VIRTUES:
  Sophia (theoretical wisdom): contemplating eternal truths.
                               The best human activity.
  Phronesis (practical wisdom): deliberating well about what to do.
  Episteme (scientific knowledge): demonstrative knowledge of necessary facts.
  Nous (intuitive understanding): grasping first principles.
  Techne (craft/art): skill in making things.

MORAL (ETHICAL) VIRTUES:
  Courage: between cowardice and rashness in matters of danger.
  Temperance: between insensibility and self-indulgence in pleasure.
  Generosity: between miserliness and prodigality with wealth.
  Justice: the master virtue of social interaction.
           Treating others as they deserve.
           Distributive justice: fair allocation.
           Corrective justice: rectifying transactions.
  Magnanimity: great-souledness; appropriate self-valuation.
               (undervaluing yourself is a deficiency.)
  Truthfulness: accurate self-representation; neither boasting nor
                self-deprecation.
  Wit: appropriate humor; neither humorless nor buffoonish.
  Friendliness: appropriate warmth; neither obsequious nor quarrelsome.
```

## Stoic Virtue Ethics

```
STOICISM AND VIRTUE
====================

CORE THESIS:
  Virtue is the only good.
  Everything else (health, wealth, reputation, pleasure) is
  "indifferent" (adiaphoron).

  PREFERRED INDIFFERENTS: health, wealth -- rational to pursue them.
  DISPREFERRED INDIFFERENTS: sickness, poverty -- rational to avoid them.
  But: their presence/absence doesn't affect whether you live well.
  Only virtue makes a life go well.

  "A good person flourishes even on the rack."
  The virtuous person who is tortured still has what matters.
  The wicked person who has everything is still wretched.

KEY STOIC VIRTUES:
  Wisdom (sophia/phronesis): the guiding virtue.
  Justice (dikaiosyne): virtue in dealing with others.
  Courage (andreia): virtue in enduring hardships.
  Temperance (sophrosyne): virtue in desires and pleasures.

EPICTETUS (THE ENCHIRIDION):
  "Some things are in our power, while others are not."
  In our power: opinion, motivation, desire, aversion.
  Not in our power: body, property, reputation, office.
  Focus on what is in your power.
  Accept what is not with equanimity.

  LEADERSHIP RELEVANCE:
  This is directly applicable to VP-level work:
  You cannot control stock price, board decisions, competitor moves.
  You CAN control your own reasoning, values, effort, integrity.
  Stoic equanimity: maintain character regardless of external outcomes.

MARCUS AURELIUS (Meditations):
  Written as a private journal.
  Daily practice of Stoic principles by the most powerful person on earth.
  "Confine yourself to the present."
  "Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one."
```

## Neo-Aristotelian Virtue Ethics

```
ALASDAIR MACINTYRE: "AFTER VIRTUE" (1981)
==========================================

MACINTYRE'S DIAGNOSIS:
  Modern moral philosophy is incoherent.
  We use moral vocabulary (rights, utility, duty) whose background
  context (teleological framework) has been abandoned.
  It's like using the terminology of physics without the framework:
  the terms have lost their meaning.

  The Enlightenment project of grounding morality in reason alone
  (Kant) or utility (Mill) fails because it abandoned the teleological
  framework that gave moral concepts their content.

THE ARISTOTELIAN ALTERNATIVE:
  Virtues make sense only within a social context with:
  1. PRACTICES: coherent activities with internal goods.
     Chess: the internal good is excellently played chess.
     Medicine: the internal good is health of patients.
     Internal goods can only be achieved by participating in the practice.
     External goods (money, fame) can be gotten other ways.
  2. NARRATIVE UNITY OF A LIFE: virtues make sense over a whole life.
     Who I am is the story of my life.
     Virtues are what you need to live a good story.
  3. MORAL TRADITION: the framework that sustains practices and narrative.

  Without this framework, moral terms are fragments of a lost whole.

COMMUNITIES:
  MacIntyre: virtues are community-specific.
  There is no "human virtue" in the abstract.
  Virtues for a warrior culture (Homeric) differ from
  virtues for an Athenian democracy, which differ from
  virtues for medieval Christian society.
  This has been criticized as relativism.
  MacIntyre responds: there's rational progress between traditions.
```

### Philippa Foot: Natural Goodness

```
PHILIPPA FOOT: "NATURAL GOODNESS" (2001)
==========================================

Foot: "Good" in ethics is the same concept as "good" in biology.
A good wolf: one that successfully does what wolves do (hunts, runs, cares for young).
A good human: one that successfully does what humans do.

What do humans do? They are rational, social, linguistic animals.
The virtues are what a human needs to flourish as a human.
This reconnects ethics to biological/naturalistic facts.

NOT moral naturalism in the open-question sense:
The evaluations are genuinely normative, not just descriptive.
But: they are grounded in what a member of a natural kind needs.

IMPLICATION:
The virtues are not arbitrary cultural constructions.
They reflect what a rational social animal genuinely needs.
This gives virtue ethics a more objective basis than mere cultural tradition.
```

## Virtue Ethics in Organizations

```
VIRTUE ETHICS AND LEADERSHIP (Relevant for VP context)
========================================================

VIRTUES IN ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT:
  A VP who makes good decisions is one who:
  - Has courage to tell difficult truths to power
  - Has practical wisdom to navigate complex political situations
  - Has integrity (unified character across contexts)
  - Has justice in treating team members fairly
  - Has temperance in exercising authority without overreach
  - Has truthfulness in representing information to leadership

  Competence alone is not virtue:
  MacIntyre's distinction: internal vs. external goods.
  A VP pursuing only external goods (salary, title, bonus)
  vs. pursuing internal goods (excellent software engineering,
  team flourishing, organizational health) = different character.

VIRTUE EPISTEMOLOGY:
  Intellectual virtues relevant to leadership:
  - Open-mindedness: considering views that challenge current direction
  - Intellectual humility: acknowledging the limits of your knowledge
  - Thoroughness: not cutting corners in analysis
  - Intellectual courage: publishing unpopular findings

  Sosa/Zagzebski: knowledge = true belief from virtuous intellectual process.
  A good decision process has epistemic virtue:
  Not just: did we get the right answer?
  But: did we use appropriate methods, consider alternatives,
  acknowledge uncertainty?
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Virtue ethics can't give action guidance — it just says 'be good.'"**
The objection has some force but overstates it. Virtue ethics gives action guidance via: (1) WWVPD — what would a virtuous person do? (2) the mean principle — avoid excess and deficiency; (3) phronesis — the trained capacity to perceive relevant features. The guidance is more flexible than rule-following, but that's a feature (context-sensitivity) not only a bug.

**"Aristotle's eudaimonia is just 'feeling happy.'"**
Eudaimonia is often translated "happiness" but it's not a feeling. It's the activity of living and doing well — flourishing. Aristotle explicitly contrasts it with pleasure (hedone). A virtuous person who is tortured can still have eudaimonia in the sense that their character is excellent, even if they're not experiencing pleasure. The life of a just person who suffers is still a better life than the life of an unjust person who prospers.

**"The doctrine of the mean means always be moderate and avoid extremes."**
The mean is relative to the person and situation, not arithmetic. Aristotle explicitly says: the mean for a wrestler's portion of food is not the same as the mean for a beginning trainee. Courage may require what looks like extreme action in some circumstances. The mean is the appropriate response, not the "middle-ground" in some statistical sense.

**"Stoicism is about suppressing emotions."**
Stoics distinguish between passions (pathê: irrational emotions based on false value judgments) and eupatheiai (good emotional states that a virtuous person has). You should not suppress all emotion. You should not have emotions based on false beliefs (like distress over things not in your control). A virtuous Stoic has joy, caution, and rational desire — but not anxiety, craving, or fear, which are based on wrong valuations.

**"MacIntyre is a relativist because virtues differ across communities."**
MacIntyre acknowledges that specific virtues have community-relative content, but argues this doesn't make virtue ethics relativistic. There is rational progress between traditions: traditions can be evaluated by internal coherence and their ability to resolve problems their predecessors couldn't. This is analogous to scientific revolutions (Kuhn) where progress is real even through paradigm shifts.
