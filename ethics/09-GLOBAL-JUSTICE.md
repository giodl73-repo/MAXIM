## Rawls: The Law of Peoples

```
RAWLS: THE LAW OF PEOPLES (1999)
==================================

Rawls extended his domestic theory of justice to international relations.
His conclusion: STATISM, not cosmopolitanism.
This is one of his most criticized works.

THE TWO LEVELS:
  1. DOMESTIC: Apply the two principles of justice.
     The difference principle governs domestic distribution.
     All citizens of a society are entitled to basic liberties +
     fair opportunity + benefit of the difference principle.

  2. INTERNATIONAL: A different theory governs.
     The "Society of Peoples" (not Society of Individuals).
     Basic unit: peoples (liberal and decent), not individual persons.

THE LAW OF PEOPLES (eight principles):
  1. Peoples are free and independent; their freedom respected.
  2. Peoples observe treaties and undertakings.
  3. Peoples are equal and parties to agreements.
  4. Peoples observe a duty of non-intervention.
  5. Peoples have the right of self-defense but not to instigate war.
  6. Peoples honor human rights.
  7. Peoples observe certain specified restrictions in conduct of war.
  8. Peoples have a duty to assist other peoples living under
     unfavorable conditions that prevent them from having
     a just or decent political and social regime.

WHY NO GLOBAL DIFFERENCE PRINCIPLE?
  Rawls' argument: the difference principle presupposes a shared
  cooperative scheme among persons who interact as a polity.
  The domestic society is such a scheme.
  International relations: no such shared scheme exists.
  Therefore: no global difference principle.

  DUTY TO ASSIST (Principle 8):
  Not a redistributive duty based on comparative wealth.
  A duty to help "burdened societies" achieve a just or decent regime.
  Not: richer countries must share until equality.
  Just: help poor countries achieve institutional competence.

  Once a society has functioning just institutions, the duty ends --
  regardless of remaining inequalities between peoples.

CHARLES BEITZ (1979): REBUTTAL
  Beitz: Rawls is wrong about the absence of a global cooperative scheme.
  International trade, finance, technology transfer, resource extraction:
  there IS a global cooperative scheme.
  By Rawls' own logic: a global difference principle should apply.
  Rich countries have benefited from this global scheme;
  they owe redistribution to the global poor.
  This is Rawls-derived cosmopolitanism.
```

## Cosmopolitanism

```
COSMOPOLITANISM
================

CORE THESIS:
  All human beings have equal moral worth.
  National boundaries have no fundamental moral significance.
  Our primary obligations are to humanity, not to co-nationals.

THOMAS POGGE: NEGATIVE DUTY NOT TO HARM
  Pogge (2002, World Poverty and Human Rights):
  The question is NOT whether wealthy nations have a positive duty
  to help the global poor.
  The question is whether wealthy nations and international institutions
  are actively HARMING the global poor.

  HIS ARGUMENT:
  1. Global economic institutions (WTO, IMF, World Bank) are designed
     by and for wealthy nations.
  2. Trade rules, intellectual property, agricultural subsidies:
     systematically disadvantage poor countries.
  3. "Borrowing privilege": lenders lend to any government that controls
     a country, even if illegitimate. Dictators can mortgage the country.
  4. "Resource privilege": any government that controls a country can
     sell its resources, even if illegitimate.
  These institutional arrangements harm the poor.
  We have a negative duty not to harm: we must reform these institutions.

  THIS IS MORE RADICAL THAN SINGER:
  Singer: positive duty to assist drowning children (requires sacrifice).
  Pogge: negative duty not to perpetuate institutional harm.
  Most Kantians accept negative duties as more binding than positive duties.
  Pogge exploits this to demand structural change.

PETER SINGER: POSITIVE DUTY
  (See also 02-CONSEQUENTIALISM for full treatment)
  Drowning child argument: if you can prevent something terrible at low cost,
  you must do it.
  Applied globally: affluent people are obligated to give substantially.
  No fundamental moral difference between near and distant drowning children.
  The only differences are contingent (distance, proximity, citizenship).

  WHAT MAKES CITIZENSHIP SPECIAL? (Singer's challenge)
  Rawlsians say: special duties to compatriots.
  Singer: why? We didn't choose our citizenship.
  Citizenship is morally arbitrary in the same way that race is arbitrary.
  You cannot build moral duties on morally arbitrary facts.

THOMAS NAGEL: STATIST RESPONSE
  Nagel (2005, "The Problem of Global Justice"):
  Justice and the special demands of equality arise in a particular
  kind of relationship: fellow members of a state.
  The state exercises coercive authority and claims to act in the name
  of all its citizens.
  This relationship -- coercive authority + claimed political membership --
  generates the obligations of egalitarian justice.
  This relationship does not exist globally.
  Therefore: no egalitarian obligations to non-citizens.
```

## Global Poverty and Development Ethics

```
GLOBAL POVERTY
===============

SCALE:
  World Bank definition of extreme poverty: $2.15/day (2022 PPP).
  ~700 million people in extreme poverty (2022).
  Down from ~2 billion in 1990.
  Concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa.

  Moderate poverty ($3.65/day): ~2 billion people.
  From the perspective of a U.S. VP-level income,
  effectively the entire lower quartile of the global distribution
  lives on an unimaginable fraction of the income.

EFFECTIVE ALTRUISM FRAMEWORK:
  See 02-CONSEQUENTIALISM for EA full treatment.
  Key figures:
  Against Malaria Foundation: ~$4,500 per life saved (GiveWell 2023).
  GiveDirectly: direct cash transfer to extreme poor in Kenya/Uganda.
  Seasonal malaria chemoprevention: ~$3,500/life.
  These are the empirical estimates that drive EA donations.

DEVELOPMENT ECONOMICS AND ETHICS:
  RANDOMIZED CONTROLLED TRIALS (RCTs):
  Abhijit Banerjee, Esther Duflo, Michael Kremer (Nobel 2019).
  Brought RCT methodology to development economics.
  J-PAL (MIT Poverty Action Lab): 1,000+ RCTs.

  KEY FINDINGS (counterintuitive):
  - Direct cash transfers are highly effective; poor people spend
    them wisely (consumption, health, business investment).
    Undermines paternalistic "they'll waste it" assumption.
  - Deworming: extremely cost-effective in high-worm-burden areas.
  - Conditional cash transfers (CCT): effective in health and education.
  - Microcredit: much more modest effects than originally claimed.
    2000s hype was not supported by RCT evidence.

  INSTITUTIONAL VS. INDIVIDUAL APPROACHES:
  Daron Acemoglu/James Robinson (Why Nations Fail, 2012):
  Poverty is caused by extractive political and economic institutions.
  Individual transfers don't fix the root cause.
  The colonialism legacy: extractive institutions set up for resource
  extraction persist and perpetuate poverty.

  POGGE'S CRITIQUE:
  Individual transfers leave the unjust global institutional framework intact.
  Addressing structural causes (WTO rules, borrowing privilege) would do more.

DUTIES OF ASSISTANCE: WHO OWES WHAT?
  SINGER: All affluent individuals must give substantially.
  POGGE: Affluent nations must reform unjust global institutions.
  RAWLS: Duty to assist burdened societies to achieve just institutions.
         Not an ongoing redistributive duty.

  These are not necessarily in conflict.
  Singer's and Pogge's arguments are compatible:
  Give individually + push for structural reform.
```

## Climate Justice

```
CLIMATE JUSTICE
================

THE PROBLEM:
  Climate change imposes severe burdens.
  The burdens are not distributed proportionally to the causes.
  Poor countries contribute less to cumulative emissions but bear
  more of the costs (sea level rise, drought, extreme weather).
  Future generations will bear the worst costs.

RESPONSIBILITY PRINCIPLES:

  COMMON BUT DIFFERENTIATED RESPONSIBILITIES (CBDR):
  UNFCCC (1992) Principle 7.
  All states have responsibility for climate change (common).
  But responsibilities differ based on historical emissions and
  economic capacity (differentiated).
  Rich countries that industrialized first have emitted more
  historically and have more capacity to act.
  CBDR became the foundation of the Paris Agreement framework.

  POLLUTER PAYS PRINCIPLE:
  Those who cause harm are responsible for costs.
  Applied to climate: high-historical-emission states owe
  the most in mitigation and compensation.
  Problem: many historical emitters no longer exist (e.g., UK in 1870s
  industry); current citizens bear costs but may not have been the agents.

  CAPACITY TO PAY:
  Independent of historical emissions: those with capacity to act
  have greater obligations.
  Rawlsian: help the worst-off is the primary duty.
  Utilitarian: maximize global reduction per dollar spent.

INTERGENERATIONAL JUSTICE:
  Future generations will bear most of the costs.
  They are not party to current decisions.
  They cannot consent to current emissions.
  They have no representation in current political processes.

  DISCOUNT RATE CONTROVERSY:
  In climate economics (Stern Review vs. Nordhaus):
  Low discount rate (Stern ~1.4%): future welfare counts nearly as much
  as present. THEREFORE: aggressive near-term mitigation.
  High discount rate (Nordhaus ~5-6%): future is heavily discounted.
  THEREFORE: gradual mitigation; adaptation may be preferred.

  The discount rate is not empirical -- it is a normative choice
  about how much we value future generations' welfare.
  This is one of the most consequential applied ethics choices.
  A VP at Microsoft whose company sets internal carbon prices is
  implicitly making this normative choice.

LOSS AND DAMAGE:
  Even with mitigation and adaptation, some harms are unavoidable.
  Low-lying islands (Tuvalu, Maldives): will be uninhabitable.
  "Loss and damage" = compensation for unavoidable harms.
  COP27 (2022): created a Loss and Damage fund.
  Details (who pays, how much, eligibility) remain contested.

INDIVIDUAL VS. INSTITUTIONAL ACTION:
  Individual carbon footprint concept was popularized by BP.
  (BP commissioned the personal carbon footprint calculator.)
  Framing: responsibility lies with consumers, not producers.
  Critics: 100 companies responsible for 71% of global emissions.
  Structural change (carbon taxes, regulations) more effective than
  voluntary individual behavior change.
  But: individual action has normative significance independent of scale.
```

## Migration Ethics

```
MIGRATION ETHICS
=================

JOSEPH CARENS: THE ETHICS OF IMMIGRATION
  "Open Borders" argument (1987, 2013):
  The right to move freely is a fundamental right.
  We accept free movement within countries as a basic liberty.
  National borders are morally arbitrary: we no more chose our
  birth country than our race or sex.
  Restricting movement based on birth location is like
  restricting movement based on birth class in feudalism.

  FORMAL ARGUMENT:
  Rawlsian original position: if you didn't know which country
  you'd be born in, you'd choose free movement.
  Most people are born into a country they didn't choose.
  The global inequality of birth locations is a form of
  structural injustice -- morally equivalent to domestic caste systems.
  THEREFORE: open borders are required by justice.

OBJECTIONS TO OPEN BORDERS:
  1. SOVEREIGNTY: States have the right to control their territory.
     Carens: sovereignty is not absolute; it cannot justify any
     restriction, including highly harmful ones.

  2. CULTURAL PRESERVATION: Immigration may change national culture.
     Carens: weaker cultures can adjust. Cultural change is normal.
     The same argument was used to justify racial exclusion laws.
     (Chinese Exclusion Act 1882: framed as cultural preservation.)

  3. ECONOMIC HARMS TO EXISTING WORKERS:
     Immigration may depress wages for low-skill workers.
     Evidence is mixed: aggregate effects are positive;
     distributional effects may harm specific groups.
     Carens: still doesn't justify exclusion, but may justify
     other policies to compensate affected workers.

  4. NATIONAL SELF-DETERMINATION:
     Michael Walzer: communities have the right to determine
     their own membership.
     Response (Carens): Walzer also argued for robust rights
     for those already inside -- once you're there, you have claims.

  5. WELFARE STATE BURDEN:
     Open borders + welfare state: unsustainable?
     Carens: this is a real concern, but restrictive immigration
     policies are a blunt instrument for managing welfare costs.

DIFFERENTIATED OBLIGATIONS:
  Even if full open borders isn't required:
  Michael Blake: states have stronger obligations to let in
  people fleeing persecution (refugees) than economic migrants.
  But: the refugee/economic migrant distinction is contested.
  (Climate migration: fleeing uninhabitable conditions is economic
  AND forced migration.)

  BRAIN DRAIN PROBLEM:
  Wealthy countries recruit doctors, engineers from poor countries.
  This drains human capital from countries that need it most.
  Canada, U.S., UK actively recruit healthcare workers from countries
  (Philippines, Nigeria) that trained them at their own expense.
  Thomas Pogge: brain drain is another way wealthy nations harm
  poorer nations through institutional arrangements.

REFUGEES:
  1951 Refugee Convention: non-refoulement principle.
  Cannot return a person to a country where they face persecution.
  Applies to: race, religion, nationality, political opinion, social group.
  Does NOT expressly cover: climate refugees, economic migrants.

  Climate refugees: not covered by the 1951 Convention.
  2022 UNHCR guidance: some climate displacement may qualify if
  states fail to protect from foreseeable climate harm.
  Legal framework is far behind the reality.
```

## Global Governance

```
GLOBAL GOVERNANCE
==================

THE BASIC PROBLEM:
  Global problems (climate, pandemics, nuclear) require global coordination.
  But: no world government exists.
  Only sovereign states + intergovernmental organizations (IGOs).
  IGOs lack enforcement power and depend on state cooperation.

KEY INSTITUTIONS:
  +---------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | UN Security Council | Primary peace/security decision body.   |
  |                     | P5 veto (US, UK, France, Russia, China). |
  |                     | Structural problem: veto protects P5    |
  |                     | interests; can't act on P5 violations.  |
  +---------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | WTO                 | Trade rules + dispute resolution.       |
  |                     | Binding arbitration -- unusual for IGOs. |
  |                     | Doha Round (development-friendly trade) |
  |                     | collapsed 2016.                         |
  +---------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | IMF                 | Financial stability, loans to countries.|
  |                     | Conditionality: austerity requirements. |
  |                     | Criticized as imposing neoliberal model.|
  +---------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | World Bank          | Development loans/grants.               |
  |                     | Governance reform conditions.           |
  |                     | Effectiveness contested.                |
  +---------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | ICC                 | International Criminal Court.           |
  |                     | Prosecutes genocide, war crimes,        |
  |                     | crimes against humanity.                |
  |                     | Limited jurisdiction (states must join).|
  |                     | US, Russia, China not members.         |
  +---------------------+------------------------------------------+

DEMOCRACY DEFICIT IN GLOBAL GOVERNANCE:
  Decisions affecting billions are made by:
  - Technocrats (IMF, World Bank, WTO)
  - Diplomats appointed by national governments
  - Bodies dominated by powerful states (UNSC)
  None are directly accountable to the populations affected.

  COSMOPOLITAN DEMOCRACY (Held, 1995):
  Extend democratic accountability globally.
  Directly elected global parliament?
  Enhanced NGO participation?
  Transnational deliberative forums?

  SOVEREIGNTY OBJECTION:
  Global democratic institutions would override national sovereignty.
  States resist ceding authority to unaccountable global bodies.
  Subsidiarity principle: decisions should be made at the lowest
  feasible level.

HUMANITARIAN INTERVENTION:
  When may states intervene militarily to stop atrocities?

  RESPONSIBILITY TO PROTECT (R2P, 2005):
  UN World Summit document.
  Three pillars:
  1. States have responsibility to protect their populations from
     genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, crimes against humanity.
  2. International community has responsibility to assist states.
  3. International community has responsibility to intervene when
     states fail (with UNSC authorization).

  CASES:
  Kosovo (1999): NATO intervention without UNSC authorization.
  Libya (2011): UNSC authorized NATO intervention under R2P.
     Regime change resulted; used as precedent for Syria (Russia vetoed).
  Syria: UNSC paralyzed by Russian veto. R2P not applied.

  SELECTIVE APPLICATION PROBLEM:
  R2P has been applied inconsistently.
  Libya: yes. Syria: no. Yemen: no. Myanmar: no.
  Selection based on strategic interests of powerful states.
  This selective application undermines the principle's universality.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Position | Key claim | Theorist | Key problem |
|---|---|---|---|
| Statism (domestic justice) | Principles of justice apply within a political community | Rawls (A Theory of Justice) | Leaves global poor outside the theory |
| Law of Peoples | Peoples (not individuals) are the unit; duty to assist burdened societies | Rawls (1999) | No global difference principle; widely criticized |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Rawls supports a global difference principle."**
Rawls explicitly rejected a global difference principle in The Law of Peoples (1999). His two principles of justice apply domestically. His international theory is the duty to assist "burdened societies" to achieve just institutions — which ends once those institutions are achieved, regardless of remaining inequalities. Beitz and others argued this is inconsistent with Rawls' own starting premises, but Rawls maintained the distinction.

**"Cosmopolitanism requires open borders."**
Cosmopolitanism holds that individuals have equal moral worth regardless of nationality. This generates obligations to the global poor, but does not automatically entail open borders as a matter of political policy. Many cosmopolitans (Singer, Pogge) focus on redistribution and institutional reform rather than migration policy. Carens' open borders argument is a distinct claim about migration rights, derived from cosmopolitan premises but requiring additional premises.

**"Carbon offsets solve individual climate responsibility."**
Carbon offsets are contested on multiple levels. Additionality problem: the offset must represent emissions reductions that wouldn't have happened otherwise (hard to verify). Permanence problem: forests that are "carbon offsets" today may burn down. Leakage: protecting one forest may displace logging to another. Many offset projects have failed these criteria. The deeper normative question is whether the very concept of "offsetting" lets wealthy individuals purchase exemption from the moral demand to reduce their own emissions.

**"Humanitarian intervention requires UN Security Council authorization."**
The UN Charter prohibits use of force except in self-defense or with UNSC authorization. R2P as adopted in 2005 requires UNSC authorization. However, Kosovo (1999) proceeded without UNSC authorization (Russia and China would have vetoed). The normative debate continues: Is there a moral permission to intervene to stop genocide without legal authorization? Michael Walzer: yes, in extreme cases. Strict positivists: no — unauthorized intervention is illegal regardless of motive.

**"Development aid doesn't work."**
Development aid is not a single thing. Randomized evaluations (J-PAL) show high effectiveness for specific interventions (malaria nets, deworming, direct cash transfers). These are very different from large-scale infrastructure loans or balance-of-payments support. The claim that "aid doesn't work" typically conflates these very different types of assistance. The evidence-based EA approach is specifically about identifying aid that works, as distinguished from aid that doesn't.

**"The global poor are poor because of their culture or governance."**
Acemoglu and Robinson argue that institutional quality explains cross-country development differences. But their historical analysis shows those institutions were typically established or distorted by colonial powers for extraction. The "culture" explanation (Max Weber's Protestant ethic variant) has been empirically undermined — rapid development in East Asian Confucian societies, the persistence of colonial institution effects. Poverty has structural and historical causes, not purely internal ones. This is not to say governance is irrelevant; it is to say the causation runs through historical forces that included external exploitation.
