## Bioethics: Beauchamp-Childress Principlism

```
BEAUCHAMP AND CHILDRESS: PRINCIPLES OF BIOMEDICAL ETHICS
(1st ed. 1979, now 9th ed. 2019)
==========================================================

THE FOUR PRINCIPLES:
  +--------------------+-----------------------------------------------+
  | AUTONOMY           | Respect persons' right to make their own     |
  |                    | informed decisions.                           |
  |                    | Grounded in: Kantian rational agency.         |
  |                    | Operationalized as: informed consent.         |
  +--------------------+-----------------------------------------------+
  | BENEFICENCE        | Act in the patient's best interest.          |
  |                    | Positive duty to benefit, not just avoid harm.|
  |                    | Doctor's traditional Hippocratic duty.        |
  +--------------------+-----------------------------------------------+
  | NON-MALEFICENCE    | "First, do no harm" (primum non nocere).     |
  |                    | Distinct from beneficence: avoiding harm.    |
  |                    | Why separate? Strong asymmetry: causing harm  |
  |                    | is worse than failing to provide benefit.    |
  +--------------------+-----------------------------------------------+
  | JUSTICE            | Fair distribution of benefits, risks, costs. |
  |                    | Who gets access to treatments?               |
  |                    | How are burdens of research distributed?     |
  +--------------------+-----------------------------------------------+

EACH PRINCIPLE IS PRIMA FACIE (Ross):
  Not absolute -- can be overridden by other principles.
  Conflict cases require balancing.
  No lexical ordering (unlike Rawls).

SPECIFICATION vs. BALANCING:
  Specification: making a principle more concrete.
  "Respect autonomy" → "obtain informed consent before invasive procedures"
  Balancing: choosing between conflicting specified principles.

WHY FOUR PRINCIPLES?
  Beauchamp (utilitarian) + Childress (Kantian) disagreed on foundations.
  The four principles represent convergence from different theories.
  They worked as a practical common ground -- "principlism."
  Critique: without a theoretical foundation, how do you balance?
  Response: this IS the method -- specified principles as a common language.
```

## Informed Consent

```
INFORMED CONSENT: HISTORY AND DOCTRINE
========================================

WHAT IT REQUIRES:
  1. DISCLOSURE: Patient must receive relevant information.
     Diagnosis, prognosis, treatment options, risks, alternatives.
  2. COMPREHENSION: Patient must understand the information.
     Not just technically informed -- actually understands.
  3. VOLUNTARINESS: Decision must be free of coercion and undue influence.
  4. COMPETENCE: Patient must have decision-making capacity.
     Adults are presumed competent.
     Exception: unconscious, severely mentally ill, certain dementia.

LEGAL STANDARD FOR DISCLOSURE:
  Two standards:
  1. Professional standard: what a reasonable doctor would disclose.
     Problem: medical paternalism -- doctors decide what patients "need."
  2. Patient standard: what a reasonable patient would want to know.
     U.S. trend has moved toward patient standard.
     Canterbury v. Spence (1972): "Prudent patient" standard.

WAIVER OF CONSENT:
  Emergency exception: unconscious patient, immediate risk of death.
  Therapeutic privilege (very restricted): information would harm patient.
  Incompetence: substitute decision maker.
  Research contexts: strict requirements (Belmont Report).

HISTORICAL FAILURES (see 07-RESEARCH-ETHICS for full treatment):
  Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932-1972): No consent, active deception.
  Nazi medical experiments: Extreme coercion, no consent.
  These failures drove the modern informed consent framework.
```

## End-of-Life Ethics

```
END-OF-LIFE ETHICS
===================

KEY DISTINCTIONS:
  +------------------------------+------------------------------------+
  | Allowing to die              | Withdrawing/withholding treatment  |
  |                              | at patient's request.              |
  |                              | LEGAL and ethically mainstream.    |
  +------------------------------+------------------------------------+
  | Physician-assisted suicide   | Doctor prescribes lethal medication|
  | (PAS)                        | patient self-administers.          |
  |                              | LEGAL in 10 U.S. states (Oregon,   |
  |                              | Washington, etc.) + many countries.|
  +------------------------------+------------------------------------+
  | Voluntary active euthanasia  | Doctor administers lethal dose.    |
  | (VAE)                        | Legal in Netherlands, Belgium,     |
  |                              | Canada (MAID). Illegal in most U.S.|
  +------------------------------+------------------------------------+
  | Involuntary euthanasia       | Killing without patient's consent. |
  |                              | ILLEGAL everywhere.                |
  +------------------------------+------------------------------------+
  | Non-voluntary euthanasia     | Patient not competent to consent   |
  |                              | (e.g., persistent vegetative state)|
  |                              | Contested and highly regulated.    |
  +------------------------------+------------------------------------+

DOCTRINE OF DOUBLE EFFECT (DDE):
  Thomas Aquinas: an action that causes harm may be permissible if:
  1. The action itself is not intrinsically wrong.
  2. The agent intends the good effect, not the bad.
  3. The bad effect is not the means to the good.
  4. There is proportionate reason for allowing the bad effect.

  Application in palliative care:
  Administering high-dose opioids to relieve pain
  may secondarily hasten death (respiratory depression).
  Under DDE: permissible, because intent is pain relief, not death.

  DDE criticism: intention is overdetermined; it creates a loophole.
  "I intended only pain relief" when everyone knows death was also intended.
  Tooley: the distinction between intending and foreseeing effects
  does not bear the moral weight DDE assigns it.

OREGON DEATH WITH DIGNITY ACT (1997):
  First U.S. state law permitting physician-assisted death.
  Requirements:
  - Terminally ill (prognosis ≤ 6 months)
  - Two oral + one written request
  - Two physicians must confirm terminal diagnosis
  - Mental health evaluation if requested
  - 15-day waiting period
  - Patient must self-administer

  Data after 25+ years: ~200-300 Oregonians per year use the law.
  Primary reasons cited: loss of autonomy, decreased ability to engage
  in enjoyable activities, loss of dignity.
  NOT primarily pain relief.

PERSISTENT VEGETATIVE STATE CASES:
  KAREN ANN QUINLAN (1975):
  21-year-old, PVS after drug overdose.
  Parents sought to remove ventilator.
  New Jersey Supreme Court: parents may authorize removal.
  Established right to refuse life-sustaining treatment.
  Precedent for surrogate decision-making.
  (Quinlan lived 9 more years after ventilator removed -- breathing sustained.)

  TERRY SCHIAVO (1990-2005):
  Cardiac arrest at 27, left in PVS.
  Husband (legal guardian) vs. parents: dispute over withdrawal of feeding tube.
  Florida courts, U.S. Congress, President Bush, Supreme Court all involved.
  Tube withdrawn 2005 after years of litigation.
  Case drove widespread adoption of advance directives.

ADVANCE DIRECTIVES:
  Living will: written instructions for medical treatment if incapacitated.
  Healthcare proxy / durable power of attorney for healthcare:
  designates a person to make medical decisions.
  DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) order: specific directive for CPR.
  POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment):
  medical order for patients near end of life.

  Policy tension: directives are often not followed.
  Reasons: not available (not in chart), staff unaware, ambiguity,
  surrogate decision-making by non-designated parties.
```

## Reproductive Ethics

```
REPRODUCTIVE ETHICS
====================

ABORTION:

JUDITH JARVIS THOMSON: "A DEFENSE OF ABORTION" (1971)
------------------------------------------------------
The most influential philosophical argument for abortion rights.
NOTABLE: Does NOT contest fetal personhood.

The Violinist Argument:
  Imagine you wake up connected to a famous unconscious violinist.
  The Society of Music Lovers connected you while you slept.
  You are the only one with the right blood type.
  If you disconnect yourself, the violinist dies.
  You must remain connected for nine months.
  Are you morally obligated to remain connected?

  MOST PEOPLE SAY: No, even if the violinist has a right to life.
  Thomson's conclusion: A right to life does not include the right to use
  another person's body. Even if the fetus has a full right to life,
  abortion may be permissible because no one has the right to use
  another's body without consent.

  OBJECTION 1: Pregnancy is different from the violinist case.
  You didn't wake up connected by strangers -- sex led to pregnancy.
  Thomson's response: unless you think every sexual act carries
  implicit consent to sustain any resulting pregnancy. This seems
  especially weak in cases of rape or contraceptive failure.

  OBJECTION 2: The argument only works for rape cases.
  If you voluntarily had sex, you "took the risk."
  Thomson: even if true, the argument still works for rape cases,
  which many anti-abortion arguments fail to handle.

DON MARQUIS: "WHY ABORTION IS IMMORAL" (1989)
----------------------------------------------
Does NOT argue that the fetus is a person.
Argues: what makes killing wrong is depriving someone of a future.
"Future-like-ours" (FLO) argument:
  Killing an adult is wrong because it deprives them of all
  their future experiences, projects, and enjoyments.
  A fetus has a future-like-ours.
  Therefore: abortion is wrong for the same reason killing adults is wrong.

  ADVANTAGE: doesn't rely on contested notion of personhood.
  OBJECTION 1: Contraception also deprives potential persons of a future.
  Marquis responds: before conception, there is no subject to be deprived.
  The future-like-ours must be the future OF a specific individual.
  OBJECTION 2: What about acorn → oak? Acorn has a future-like-ours?
  OBJECTION 3: The argument is undermined in early abortion where
  the fetus has no psychological continuity with the future self it "will be."

REPRODUCTIVE TECHNOLOGIES:
  IVF: Creates embryos outside the body; some destroyed.
  Moral status of the embryo is central.
  Range from full personhood (Catholic view -- strict limits)
  to bundle of cells with no moral status (some secular bioethicists).
  Most views: embryos have intermediate moral status, warranting respect
  but not full rights.

  Surrogacy: Gestational (unrelated genetically to surrogate)
  vs. traditional (surrogate contributes egg).
  Commercial vs. altruistic.
  Objections: commodification of women's bodies; exploitation
  of poor women; risks to surrogate; best interests of child.
  Defenses: reproductive autonomy; genuine need; many surrogates
  report positive experiences.

  Prenatal selection (PGT): Preimplantation genetic testing.
  Selects embryos without genetic diseases.
  Disability rights objection: implies that disability = worse life
  (expressive harm to disabled people).
  Response: selecting against disease ≠ devaluing disabled persons.
  Slippery slope concern: selection for non-disease traits (sex, appearance).
```

## Enhancement Ethics

```
ENHANCEMENT ETHICS
===================

TREATMENT vs. ENHANCEMENT DISTINCTION:
  TREATMENT: restoring or maintaining normal species-typical functioning.
  ENHANCEMENT: improving beyond normal species-typical functioning.

  Controversy: the boundary is contested.
  Short stature due to GH deficiency: treatment.
  Short stature due to idiopathic short stature: enhancement?
  Depression treatment vs. mood enhancement?
  ADHD treatment vs. cognitive enhancement?

  Norman Daniels: treatment/enhancement distinction matters because
  healthcare resources should restore normal functioning (species-typical),
  not provide enhancements. Enhancements are not a matter of justice.
  Critics: the distinction is arbitrary given genetic luck in "normal" baseline.

COGNITIVE ENHANCEMENT:
  Methylphenidate (Ritalin), dextroamphetamine: prescribed for ADHD.
  Used off-label by students, professionals for focus/cognitive performance.
  Modafinil: approved for narcolepsy/shift work, used off-label for alertness.

  OBJECTIONS:
  1. Safety: long-term effects unknown; side effects (CV, addiction).
  2. Fairness: rich students enhance, poor ones don't.
     Like doping in sports -- undermines fair competition.
  3. Authenticity: achievements under enhancement not "really" yours.
  4. Coercion: if widely used, others feel compelled to use.

  RESPONSES:
  1. Safety: same as coffee, exercise -- dose matters.
  2. Fairness: tutors, private schools, good nutrition are also enhancers.
     Why draw the line at pharmacological enhancement?
  3. Authenticity: false self-conception. We already use the brain we have;
     its development was also a product of circumstance.
  4. Coercion: same applies to many competitive advantages.

MORAL ENHANCEMENT:
  Ingmar Persson and Julian Savulescu: we face existential risks
  from technology (bioweapons, nuclear, climate change).
  Human moral psychology evolved for small-group settings.
  It is not equipped for global civilizational risks.
  THEREFORE: we should explore biological moral enhancement.
  E.g., oxytocin to increase empathy and cooperation.

  OBJECTIONS:
  1. Who decides what moral enhancement is? Risk of authoritarian moral
     engineering.
  2. Moral worth requires rational agency -- chemically induced virtue
     is not genuine virtue (Kantian critique).
  3. Oxytocin increases in-group cooperation but increases out-group hostility
     (empirical complication).

GENETIC ENHANCEMENT:
  SOMATIC vs. GERMLINE:
  Somatic cell therapy: modifies individual patient's cells.
    Effect confined to patient. Not inherited.
    Generally accepted for treatment.
  Germline modification: modifies embryo.
    Effect inherited by future generations.
    Permanent, heritable change.

  HE JIANKUI (2018):
  Chinese researcher claimed to have edited human embryos (CCR5 gene)
  to confer HIV resistance.
  Resulted in live births.
  Condemned internationally:
  - No medical necessity (parents HIV-negative mothers)
  - No genuine informed consent
  - Scientific irresponsibility (off-target effects unknown)
  - Crossed line from therapy to enhancement without societal consensus

  He convicted of "illegal medical practice" in China (2019).
  Signals the consensus bright line: germline editing for enhancement
  without societal consensus is impermissible, even for disease prevention.

TRANSHUMANISM:
  Bostrom, More: enhance human capacities through technology.
  Cognitive, physical, emotional enhancement; life extension.
  See constraints of "human nature" as contingent, not normative.

  BIOCONSERVATISM (Sandel, Habermas, Fukuyama):
  Enhancement undermines the "giftedness of life."
  We are responsible for outcomes of what we choose.
  Enhanced children's autonomy is compromised (Habermas: they can't
  retrospectively consent to the genetic choices made for them).
  Fukuyama: "human nature" is what gives us moral status; enhancing
  it risks undermining the egalitarian moral framework.
```

## Professional Ethics: Law

```
LEGAL ETHICS
=============

DUTIES OF AN ATTORNEY:
  Governed by: ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct (U.S.).
  Core duties: competence, diligence, communication, confidentiality,
               loyalty (conflicts of interest), candor to tribunal.

CONFIDENTIALITY:
  Duty of confidentiality is near-absolute.
  Rationale: clients must be able to speak freely to attorneys.
  Without confidentiality, clients can't get effective representation.

  EXCEPTIONS:
  Attorney-client privilege (evidentiary): protects communications
  from compelled disclosure.
  Duty of confidentiality (ethical): broader -- protects all information.

  CRIME-FRAUD EXCEPTION:
  Privilege does not apply to communications facilitating crime/fraud.
  "I need your help to commit this fraud" ≠ protected.
  "I committed this fraud last year; what are my options?" = protected.

  FUTURE HARM EXCEPTION:
  ABA Rule 1.6(b): may reveal to prevent death/substantial bodily harm.
  Narrow: "reasonably certain death or substantial bodily harm."
  Does NOT include financial harm.

CONFLICTS OF INTEREST:
  Current client conflicts: lawyer may not represent adverse interests.
  Former client conflicts: may not work against former client on same/
  substantially related matters (duty of loyalty persists).
  Concurrent representation: two clients in same matter (usually prohibited).
  Waivable by consent with full disclosure.

DUTY TO TRIBUNAL vs. DUTY TO CLIENT:
  Candor to the tribunal (Rule 3.3):
  Lawyer must not make false statements to court.
  Must disclose adverse controlling precedent.
  Must not suborn perjury.

  TENSION: Zealous advocacy for client vs. duty of candor.
  Resolution: You can argue the law should be different.
  You cannot lie about the facts.
  If client insists on lying, lawyer must withdraw.

CONFIDENTIALITY OF GUILTY KNOWLEDGE:
  Client tells you: "I did it."
  You must still represent them zealously.
  You may not put client on stand knowing they will lie (perjury).
  Ethical dilemma: many lawyers advise client not to testify.
  The system relies on prosecution proving guilt beyond reasonable doubt.
```

## Professional Ethics: Medicine

```
MEDICAL ETHICS
===============

BENEFICENCE vs. AUTONOMY TENSION:
  Historical medicine was paternalistic: doctor knows best.
  Modern medicine has shifted to patient autonomy.
  "Doctor knows best" → "Patient decides, doctor informs."

  PATERNALISM: overriding patient's preferences for their own benefit.
  Hard paternalism: overriding competent patient's informed preference.
  Soft paternalism: overriding preferences due to incompetence, impaired
  decision-making, or defective information.

  Soft paternalism is generally accepted (the intervention is FOR the patient).
  Hard paternalism: highly contested. Generally rejected in modern bioethics.
  Exception: patient safety/imminent harm to others (psychiatric holds).

CONFIDENTIALITY IN MEDICINE:
  Stronger duty than law in some respects (HIPAA in U.S.).
  TARASOFF CASE (California 1976):
  Psychiatrist's patient threatened named victim.
  Psychiatrist did not warn victim.
  Victim was killed.
  California Supreme Court: psychiatrist had duty to warn.
  "The protective privilege ends where the public peril begins."
  Established duty to warn in psychiatry (varies by state).

RATIONING AND DISTRIBUTIVE JUSTICE:
  ICU beds, organ transplants, ventilators (COVID) are scarce.
  ALLOCATION CRITERIA:
  First-come-first-served: treats all equally but advantages the well-connected.
  Medical need: prioritizes those who need it most.
  Social utility: prioritizes those who will contribute most.
     (Historically used and generally rejected for being inegalitarian.)
  Life-years saved: QALY analysis -- utilitarian.
  Lottery: equal probability for eligible patients.
  Fair innings: priority to younger patients who have had less.

  Modern transplant allocation (UNOS): multi-criteria.
  Medical urgency, expected benefit, time waiting, geographic factors.
  Continuous refinement to reduce disparities (racial, geographic).

THE RULE OF RESCUE:
  Strong psychological tendency to help identified individuals
  (the trapped miner) vs. statistical lives (pollution policy).
  Utilitarian: the statistical lives matter equally.
  Bioethics debate: is the rule of rescue a bias or a legitimate moral factor?
  Jonathan Baron: it is a bias. We should resist it.
  Others: there is something morally significant about identified individuals.
```

## Professional Ethics: Engineering

```
ENGINEERING ETHICS
===================

FORD PINTO CASE (1970s):
  Ford's Pinto had a gas tank that ruptured in rear-end collisions.
  Internal Ford memo (the "Pinto Memo") calculated:
  - Cost of litigation from deaths/injuries: $49.5 million
  - Cost of redesigning the gas tank: $137 million
  Cost-benefit analysis recommended against redesign.

  1977 Mother Jones investigation revealed the memo.
  Ford faced criminal prosecution for reckless homicide (acquitted).
  Massive civil liability and reputational damage.

  ENGINEERING ETHICS LESSONS:
  1. Cost-benefit analysis is not sufficient for safety decisions.
     The memo failed to include all costs (reputational, regulatory).
  2. Engineers have an independent duty to public safety.
     NSPE Code of Ethics: "Engineers shall hold paramount the safety,
     health, and welfare of the public."
  3. The engineer who does the analysis must not let cost override safety.
  4. Organizational pressure is not a defense.

CHALLENGER DISASTER (1986):
  Seven astronauts killed when Challenger broke apart 73 seconds after launch.
  Cause: O-ring failure on solid rocket booster in cold temperature.
  Roger Boisjoly (Morton Thiokol engineer): recommended against launch.
  Management overruled him. Launch proceeded.

  ETHICS FAILURE:
  1. Organizational pressure reversed the engineering recommendation.
  2. The decision meeting inverted the burden of proof: engineers had to
     PROVE it was unsafe, not NASA prove it was safe.
  3. Boisjoly became a whistleblower. He was marginalized by Thiokol.
  4. Richard Feynman's investigation revealed the O-ring temperature concerns
     had been known for years.

  WHISTLEBLOWING:
  Engineers' duty to public safety may require going outside the organization.
  WHEN IS WHISTLEBLOWING REQUIRED?
  DeGeorge's criteria (widely cited):
  1. Significant harm to public.
  2. Reported internally first.
  3. Internal remedies exhausted.
  4. Documented evidence of harm.
  5. Reasonable belief that going public will prevent harm.

  Tension: employee loyalty vs. public safety.
  Engineering ethics code: public safety takes precedence.

THERAC-25 (1985-1987):
  Radiation therapy machine. Software bugs caused lethal overdoses.
  6 patients seriously injured or killed.
  Cause: software race condition when operator corrected input quickly.
  Removed hardware safety interlocks that the older Therac-20 had.
  Relied entirely on software safety.

  LESSONS:
  1. Removing hardware backups for software-only safety is dangerous.
  2. Testing must be comprehensive; intermittent bugs can be lethal.
  3. Error messages must be clear. Therac-25 displayed "MALFUNCTION 54"
     with no indication of the lethal overdose. Operators ignored it.
  4. Manufacturer response was inadequate: blamed operators, minimized reports.

SOFTWARE ENGINEERING ETHICS (VP-relevant):
  Professional codes (ACM, IEEE):
  - Public interest over employer/client interest.
  - High quality; professional standards.
  - Honesty and full disclosure.
  - Access to computing as broadly as possible.

  AI SYSTEMS:
  The engineering ethics framework applies directly.
  Engineers who build AI systems that cause harm bear professional responsibility.
  "Following orders" (business requirements, management decisions) is not
  a full defense when you deploy systems you know can cause serious harm.
```

## Professional Ethics: Journalism

```
JOURNALISM ETHICS
==================

SPJ CODE OF ETHICS (Society of Professional Journalists):
  Four core principles:
  1. SEEK TRUTH AND REPORT IT
  2. MINIMIZE HARM
  3. ACT INDEPENDENTLY
  4. BE ACCOUNTABLE AND TRANSPARENT

TRUTH-TELLING:
  Distinguish between: verified facts, opinion, speculation.
  Attribution requirements: on-record vs. on background vs. off-record.
  Correction culture: errors must be corrected promptly and prominently.

  TRUTH vs. BALANCE:
  "Both sides" journalism: can be misleading when one side has no evidence.
  Anthropogenic climate change: 97% scientific consensus.
  Reporting "both sides" implies equal credibility.
  Tension: journalistic norm of balance vs. epistemic responsibility.
  Modern shift: "evidence-based" rather than "balance" in areas of
  scientific consensus.

HARM MINIMIZATION:
  What private information is newsworthy?
  Balancing: public interest vs. privacy.
  Outing HIV status, sexual orientation, mental health: only if newsworthy
  AND the public has a legitimate right to know.
  The public's desire to know ≠ the public's right to know.

  Sources: protecting anonymous sources vs. letting false information
  stand to protect sources.
  Prior restraint: rare -- government may not usually enjoin publication.
  National security exception: Classified information.
  Pentagon Papers case (1971): Supreme Court ruled against prior restraint.

INDEPENDENCE:
  Conflicts of interest: accepting gifts, entertainment from sources.
  "Revolving door": journalists becoming PR people for their sources.
  Embedded journalism: access in exchange for favorable coverage.
  Advertiser influence: news/advertising "wall" increasingly porous.

ACCURACY IN THE AGE OF SOCIAL MEDIA:
  Speed/accuracy trade-off: 24/7 news cycle pressures.
  Verification standards have declined.
  Viral misinformation often outruns corrections.
  Platform responsibility: Twitter/X, Facebook as vectors of misinformation.
  Epistemic consequences: "epistemic commons" erosion, polarization.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"The four principles of bioethics give a decision procedure."**
They do not. Beauchamp and Childress explicitly say the four principles are a framework for identifying morally relevant considerations, not an algorithm. When principles conflict (autonomy vs. beneficence in treating a patient who refuses life-saving care), the framework identifies the conflict but doesn't resolve it. Resolution requires judgment, specification of principles, and weighing context.

**"The doctrine of double effect allows doctors to do anything, as long as they claim good intent."**
DDE requires four conditions, not just good intent. Critically: (3) the bad effect must not be the means to the good effect. If the intended mechanism of action goes through the harm (e.g., suppressing the patient's immune system "for" the patient's benefit, where the harm is the means), DDE does not apply. The doctrine is subject to legitimate criticism but it's not as permissive as "intend the good."

**"Thomson's violinist argument proves abortion is permissible in all cases."**
Thomson explicitly does not argue that. Her argument shows that even granting fetal personhood, abortion may be permissible — because no one has a right to use another's body without consent. The argument is strongest for pregnancies due to rape. She acknowledges it may not cover all cases of consensual sex, though she argues that consensual sex does not typically imply consent to sustain any resulting pregnancy.

**"The treatment/enhancement distinction is scientifically clear."**
It is not. The distinction is normative, not purely descriptive. "Normal species-typical functioning" is contested at the edges. The DSM revises what counts as a disorder over time (homosexuality was a disorder until 1973). The distinction is useful but inherits the contestability of the concept of "normal."

**"Engineers just have to follow the specifications."**
Engineering codes of ethics explicitly establish that engineers hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public — above employer or client interests. The Challenger case illustrates the failure mode: engineer Boisjoly recommended against launch; management overruled him. He was subsequently recognized as a moral exemplar, not a disloyal employee. Following organizational directives does not discharge an engineer's professional responsibility for public safety.

**"Whistleblowing is always required when you know of wrongdoing."**
DeGeorge's criteria require that internal remedies be exhausted first. Whistleblowing has significant costs to the whistleblower and the organization; those costs must be proportionate to the harm being prevented. The duty to blow the whistle is triggered when: (1) the harm is serious, (2) internal channels have failed, (3) there is documented evidence, (4) the disclosure will actually prevent harm. Casual or unverified allegations do not trigger the duty.
