# Education — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Education science spans how learning works (cognitive and developmental science), how teaching is designed (pedagogy and curriculum), how systems are organized (institutional structure and policy), and how power operates within and through education (sociology and equity). The field is uniquely practical: every finding eventually confronts the classroom, the teacher, and the learner.

## Major Frameworks

| Framework | Core Claim | Level of Analysis | Policy Implication |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|--------------------|
| **Behaviorism** | Learning = behavior change through reinforcement | Individual | Structured reinforcement, direct instruction |
| **Cognitive constructivism** (Piaget) | Learners build knowledge through active experience | Developmental/individual | Age-appropriate tasks; discovery learning |
| **Social constructivism** (Vygotsky) | Learning is social; ZPD is the productive zone | Social/cultural | Scaffolding, collaborative learning |
| **Information processing** | Working memory limits constrain learning | Cognitive architecture | Reduce extraneous load; use schemas |
| **Cognitive load theory** | Manage intrinsic/extraneous/germane load | Instructional design | Worked examples, segmenting, signaling |
| **Spacing/retrieval** | Spaced practice + retrieval practice = durable learning | Cognitive science | Redesign schedules and assessment |
| **Sociocultural** | Context, identity, and power shape learning | Macro/social | Culturally responsive teaching; equity |

## Education System Architecture (US)

```
  US EDUCATION SYSTEM LAYERS
  ===========================

  K-12 (public):
    Governed by states and local school districts.
    Federal role: Title I (funding for high-poverty),
    IDEA (special education), ESEA/Every Student Succeeds Act.
    ~13,000 school districts; enormous variation.

  K-12 (private):
    ~25% of students in private (charter + parochial + independent)
    Charter schools: publicly funded, independently operated.
    Voucher programs: public funds for private tuition.

  HIGHER EDUCATION:
    Community colleges: 2-year, open access.
    State universities: 4-year, regional.
    Research universities (R1): doctoral/research focused.
    Private liberal arts: residential, discussion-based.
    Flagship state universities: state's academic peak.
    For-profit: very mixed quality; some predatory.

  CREDENTIALING:
    High school diploma.
    Associate's degree (2-year).
    Bachelor's degree (4-year).
    Graduate degrees: MA, MS, PhD, JD, MD, MBA.
    Vocational certifications.
    Micro-credentials / badges (emerging).
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Learning Theory vs. Educational Philosophy**
Learning theory describes empirically how learning occurs (cognitive processes, behavioral reinforcement). Educational philosophy asks normative questions: what is education for? (Dewey: democracy; Freire: liberation; classical: truth). These are different levels of analysis. Good education needs both.

**Cognitive Science vs. Folk Theories of Learning**
Most people believe they learn best through their "learning style" (visual, auditory, kinesthetic). This is a widely-held belief with essentially no empirical support. Cognitive science has identified what actually works (spacing, retrieval, interleaving) -- and these are often counterintuitive and effortful.

**Constructivism Is Not Discovery Learning**
Constructivism (Piaget, Vygotsky) says learners build knowledge through active engagement. This is a theory of how learning works, not a prescription for "minimal guidance" teaching. Well-designed explicit instruction is constructivist in the relevant sense -- it guides learners to build accurate knowledge structures. Discovery learning with minimal guidance often fails, especially for novices.

**Assessment Drives Learning (Goodhart's Law)**
What gets measured gets managed. High-stakes standardized testing creates incentives to teach to the test, narrow the curriculum, and exclude difficult-to-test competencies (creativity, collaboration, civic participation). Assessment design is not neutral -- it shapes what education produces.
