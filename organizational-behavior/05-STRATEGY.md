# Strategy — Porter Five Forces, RBV, OKRs, Platform Strategy

## The Big Picture

Strategy answers two questions: where to compete (which markets, which customers) and how to win there (what competitive advantage). The academic field has two dominant traditions that are in productive tension: Porter's industry-positioning view (industry structure determines profitability) and the resource-based view (firm-specific capabilities determine advantage). Neither is complete alone. Platform economics added a third lens for digital businesses.

```
STRATEGY THEORY LANDSCAPE
===========================

WHAT STRATEGY TRIES TO EXPLAIN:
 Why some firms earn consistently higher profits than others
 Two answers (in tension):

  POSITIONING VIEW (Porter, 1980s):
   Industry attractiveness × competitive position
   "Be in a good industry AND occupy a defensible position"
   Environment determines profitability; strategic choice = which market

  RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (Barney, 1991):
   Firm-specific, inimitable resources/capabilities → sustained advantage
   "Build something competitors can't copy"
   Internal resources determine profitability; market is secondary

  DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES (Teece, 1997):
   In fast-moving markets, the ability to CHANGE capabilities matters more
   than any specific capability (which can become obsolete)
   "Build the capacity to rebuild"

LEVELS OF STRATEGY:
 Corporate strategy: which businesses to be in; portfolio management
 Business unit strategy: how to compete in each business
 Functional strategy: how functions support the competitive strategy

EXECUTION ≠ STRATEGY:
 Having a strategy doesn't execute it; many strategies fail at execution
 McKinsey 7-S: strategy must be consistent with structure, systems,
  staff, skills, style, shared values
 "Strategy without execution is hallucination" — attributed to Drucker
 "Culture eats strategy for breakfast" — attributed to Drucker
  More precisely: culture + incentives + structure together implement strategy
```

## Porter's Five Forces

```
PORTER'S FIVE FORCES (Michael Porter, 1979/1980)
==================================================

Framework for analyzing industry attractiveness.
All five forces reduce profitability when they increase intensity.

FORCE 1: THREAT OF NEW ENTRY
 When low barriers → competitors enter → prices fall → profits erode
 Barriers to entry:
  Economies of scale: incumbent cost advantage (manufacturing, distribution)
  Switching costs: customers locked in (platform stickiness; data lock-in)
  Capital requirements: high CapEx creates floor for entry
  Network effects: value increases with scale (software; platforms)
  Access to distribution: incumbent controls shelf space / channels
  Incumbency advantages (location, IP, brands, government relationships)
  Government policy: licensing, regulation can limit entry
  Expected retaliation: if incumbents will fight → deters entry
 HIGH-BARRIER INDUSTRIES: aerospace defense, pharma (patents + FDA),
  utilities (regulatory), commercial aircraft manufacturing

FORCE 2: BARGAINING POWER OF BUYERS
 When buyers have power → they extract value (lower prices, more features)
 High buyer power when:
  Buyers are concentrated (few buyers, many sellers)
  Product is undifferentiated (commodity → price shopping)
  Low switching cost for buyer
  Buyer has backward integration option (can make product themselves)
  Product is a significant cost component for buyer (scrutinize closely)
 Examples: Walmart vs food suppliers (Walmart has enormous buyer power);
           Apple vs component suppliers (Apple concentration of purchase)

FORCE 3: BARGAINING POWER OF SUPPLIERS
 When suppliers have power → extract value via higher input prices
 High supplier power when:
  Few suppliers; many buyers (opposite of buyer power situation)
  Switching costs for buyer are high (proprietary components)
  Supplier has forward integration option (can sell direct)
  Supplier's product is important / unique input
 Examples: ASML (sole supplier of EUV lithography machines) → enormous supplier
  power; Nvidia GPUs in AI boom → extraordinary supplier power

FORCE 4: THREAT OF SUBSTITUTES
 Different products/technologies that fulfill the same function
 High substitute threat → price ceiling on industry profits
 Substitutes to watch: anything that does the same job, not just direct competitors
  Email → postal mail; video call → air travel; streaming → movie theaters
  "Jobs to be done" thinking captures substitution threats better than
  traditional competitive analysis

FORCE 5: RIVALRY AMONG EXISTING COMPETITORS
 Intense rivalry → price competition → profit erosion
 High rivalry when:
  Many competitors of similar size and capability
  Slow growth (market share is zero-sum)
  High fixed costs → must maintain volume → price wars
  Low differentiation → commodity competition
  High exit barriers (stranded assets keep weak competitors in market)
 Examples: Airlines (high rivalry; low differentiation in economy);
           accounting software (moderate)

FIVE FORCES USAGE:
 Analyze industry BEFORE entry decision
 Assess industry attractiveness across all five
 Consider: which forces are structural vs cyclical?
 Then: is there a defensible position within this industry?
```

## Porter's Generic Strategies and Value Chain

```
GENERIC COMPETITIVE STRATEGIES (Porter)
=========================================

COST LEADERSHIP:
 Serve a broad market at the lowest cost
 Source of cost advantage: economies of scale; process efficiency;
  input cost advantages; experience curve; technology; location
 Requirements: continuous operational improvement; efficient processes;
  high market share usually required; tight cost controls
 Risk: cost position can be matched by others (scale/technology accessible);
       technology disruption can reset the cost curve
 Examples: Walmart (retail); Southwest (airlines); Amazon (logistics)

DIFFERENTIATION:
 Serve a broad market with something buyers value that competitors can't match
 Source: product features; service quality; brand; technology; expertise;
         speed; customization; relationships
 Requirements: sustained innovation; customer understanding; premium pricing ability
 Risk: differentiation becomes irrelevant (needs change);
       cost difference exceeds premium buyers will pay;
       imitation narrows differentiation (must continuously reinvest)
 Examples: Apple (design + ecosystem); BMW (performance + brand);
           McKinsey (reputation + network)

FOCUS (COST OR DIFFERENTIATION):
 Serve a narrow target segment extremely well
 Cost focus: lowest cost within a niche
 Differentiation focus: unique value for specific customer segment
 Risk: niche disappears; broad competitor enters and outperforms on both cost and differentiation;
       niche becomes too small to sustain scale

STUCK IN THE MIDDLE:
 Porter's warning: organizations that try to be both low-cost AND differentiated
 → achieve neither; underperform pure strategies
 BUT: some modern companies (IKEA; Toyota) do both through operational excellence
 Modern view: "operational effectiveness" is not strategy; a company can be
  operationally excellent (low cost) AND have a differentiated position

VALUE CHAIN ANALYSIS:
 Porter's tool: disaggregate firm activities to identify where value is created
 Primary activities: inbound logistics → operations → outbound logistics →
  marketing/sales → service
 Support activities: firm infrastructure; HR; technology development; procurement
 Key question: which activities are sources of cost advantage or differentiation?
  Then: invest in those; outsource/standardize the rest
 Strategic linkages: activities that reinforce each other; hard to imitate
  because the whole system must be copied (IKEA: design + supply chain + format)
```

## Resource-Based View (RBV)

```
RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (Jay Barney, 1991)
========================================

"Why do firms that operate in the same industry have systematically different returns?"
Answer: firm-specific resources and capabilities that aren't easily imitated

VRIN FRAMEWORK:
 VALUABLE: the resource enables value creation (customers pay for what it enables)
 RARE: few or no competitors have it
 INIMITABLE: competitors cannot easily copy it
 NON-SUBSTITUTABLE: no alternative resource can perform the same function

SOURCES OF INIMITABILITY (WHY HARD TO COPY):
 Path dependency: history matters; resources built over time can't be shortcut
  Amazon's logistics infrastructure: 25 years of building; not replicable quickly
 Causal ambiguity: competitors don't know which resources create advantage
  (or even: you don't know — tacit knowledge in organizational routines)
 Social complexity: depends on human interactions (culture; trust; leadership)
  that can't be engineered by copying the org chart
 Patents/legal protection: contractual; legally excludable (not structural)

DISTINCTION: RESOURCES vs CAPABILITIES:
 Resources: assets (physical, financial, intellectual, human)
  Tangible: buildings, cash, equipment, data
  Intangible: brand, patents, trade secrets, algorithms
 Capabilities: what the organization can DO with its resources
  Processes; routines; coordination mechanisms
  Capabilities are harder to imitate than resources
  (You can hire the same people; you can't immediately replicate the system they work in)

SUSTAINED COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE:
 Competitive advantage: achieve outcomes competitors can't
 Sustainable: maintained despite competitors' attempts to neutralize
 RBV: sustained advantage requires all four VRIN conditions
  In practice: most advantages erode; some last longer than others
  "Sustainable" is often interpreted as "lasting 10+ years" not "permanent"

CORE COMPETENCE (Prahalad & Hamel, 1990):
 Related to RBV but corporate strategy lens
 Core competence: collective learning; coordination of diverse skills;
                  integration of technology streams
 Three tests: provides access to wide variety of markets;
              makes significant contribution to perceived customer benefits;
              difficult for competitors to imitate
 Honda engines; Sony miniaturization; 3M surface/bonding technology
 Risk: "core competence" framing → overextension; every resource becomes "core"
```

## RBV Applied: Engineering Org Investment Decisions

VRIN is not just an academic typology — it is an analytical tool for evaluating the most consequential decisions an engineering org makes: build vs. buy, platform investment, and capability development prioritization.

```
VRIN APPLIED TO ENGINEERING INVESTMENT DECISIONS
=================================================

BUILD vs BUY DECISION — VRIN LENS:
  The question: should we build this capability internally or buy/license it?
  VRIN answer: build only if the capability can be Valuable + Rare + Inimitable
               + Non-substitutable for YOUR competitive position

  BUILD (potential VRIN resource):
  - Core algorithms that differentiate your product from competitors
  - Data pipelines that accumulate proprietary training or behavioral data
  - Developer experience tooling that encodes institutional knowledge of your stack
  - Integrations where tight coupling to internal systems creates moat

  BUY / CONSUME AS SERVICE (not a VRIN candidate):
  - Infrastructure that competitors can access on the same terms (commodity cloud)
  - Tooling where category leaders have 10x+ investment (monitoring, observability)
  - Compliance and security primitives (certifications, audit tooling)
  - Any capability where your build will take 2+ years and you need it in 6 months

PLATFORM INVESTMENT — VRIN LENS:
  Q: Should we invest heavily in an internal developer platform?
  Path dependency: platform capabilities compound over time (data, tooling,
    institutional knowledge); the advantage is built over 5-10 years, not quarters
  Causal ambiguity: competitors can see your platform outputs; they cannot
    easily identify which internal processes, team structures, and historical
    decisions create the capability → classic causal ambiguity
  Social complexity: an internal developer platform's value is partly in the
    culture of contribution, the shared mental models, and the trust between
    platform and stream teams — these cannot be bought or rapidly replicated

CAPABILITY vs RESOURCE DISTINCTION APPLIED:
  What you build: tangible resource (code, infrastructure, data)
  How you build it: capability (processes, team structure, institutional knowledge)
  Capabilities are harder to imitate: a competitor can hire your engineers
    but cannot immediately replicate the system those engineers work in
  The most durable engineering advantage is usually a capability
    (e.g., rapid experimentation, high-trust incident response, fast onboarding)
    rather than any specific technical resource

DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES APPLIED:
  Sensing: organizational investment in scanning for platform shifts
    (AI/ML 2022-2024; quantum computing timeframe; next shift?)
    → Technology radar processes; dedicated research function; external partnerships
  Seizing: the org's ability to redirect investment when the signal is clear
    → This is the reorg problem: can you move 20% of your engineering capacity
      to a new platform in 18 months? Most orgs cannot.
  Transforming: reconfiguring the capability base, not just redirecting resources
    → Upskilling existing engineers vs. hiring new vs. acquiring teams
    → The portfolio management of capability transitions
```

## Dynamic Capabilities

```
DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES (TEECE, PISANO, SHUEN, 1997)
====================================================

"Competitive advantage in dynamic markets requires abilities to
 integrate, build, and reconfigure competencies."

THREE CAPABILITY TYPES:
 SENSING: identify and shape opportunities and threats
  Market intelligence; customer observation; research; signals detection
  "What's changing in the environment that matters?"
 SEIZING: mobilize resources to address opportunities/threats
  Investment decisions; business model design; resource commitment
  "How do we capture the opportunity once identified?"
 TRANSFORMING/RECONFIGURING: renewing and reconfiguring organizational assets
  Portfolio reshaping; reorganization; building new capabilities
  "How do we build new strengths while managing current ones?"

WHY THIS MATTERS:
 Static capabilities: valuable at one point in time; can become liabilities
  Kodak's film expertise → liability not asset when digital changed the market
  Nokia's phone hardware → liability when software became the game
 Dynamic capabilities: the meta-capability to adapt; not industry-specific
 Fast-changing environments: sensing + seizing + transforming > any static resource
 Technology companies especially: competitive advantage is ephemeral;
  the ability to build new advantages continuously = the real moat

EVIDENCE:
 Difficult to empirically test (how do you measure the meta-capability?)
 More theoretical than empirical; directionally well-accepted
 Used extensively in tech strategy (Andreessen Horowitz frameworks;
  McKinsey technology practice)

LIMITATIONS:
 "Dynamic capabilities" as a concept = hard to operationalize
  What exactly is the organizational capability that enables change?
 Risk of tautology: "successful firms have good dynamic capabilities"
  → identified by their success; not measured independently
 The framework is most useful as a lens for analysis, not prediction
```

## OKRs and Balanced Scorecard

```
OKRS (OBJECTIVES AND KEY RESULTS)
====================================

ORIGIN:
 Andy Grove (Intel, 1970s): MBO (Management by Objectives) adapted
 John Doerr (Kleiner Perkins): brought to Google (1999) in Google's first year
 "Measure What Matters" (Doerr, 2018): popularized widely

STRUCTURE:
 Objective: qualitative, aspirational, motivating direction
  "What do we want to achieve?" — not a number
  Example: "Make Azure the most developer-loved cloud platform"
 Key Results: 3-5 quantified measures that define success
  "How will we know we achieved it?" — specific numbers
  Example: "NPS score ≥70 on developer survey" + "GitHub integrations: 500"
  + "Top 3 in developer satisfaction benchmark"

HIERARCHY:
 Company OKRs → Team OKRs → Individual OKRs
 Alignment (not cascade): team OKRs should support company OKRs
  "Cascade" model (top-down assignment) vs "alignment" model (bottom-up alignment
  to company OKRs) — alignment produces more commitment and autonomy

GRADING:
 Score 0.0-1.0 per key result
 Average to get objective score
 Google guideline: 0.6-0.7 = success (aspirational OKRs)
  If always 1.0 → targets too easy; sandbag indicator
  If consistently 0.0-0.3 → disconnect between ambition and reality; demoralization
 Committed vs Aspirational:
  Committed OKRs: must be achieved; operational; close to 1.0 expected
  Aspirational OKRs: stretch; 0.6-0.7 expected; failure not punished

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
 Too many: more than 5 objectives → each gets diluted attention
 Sandbagging: deliberately setting easy KRs to ensure 1.0 score
  Incentivized when OKRs tied to compensation (should NOT be)
 Activity metrics masquerading as outcomes:
  "Completed 10 features" (output) vs "Customer retention +5%" (outcome)
 Gaming: optimizing the KR metric at the expense of the actual objective
  Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"
 No cadence: OKRs set in January; reviewed in December; not a management system
 Confusion with task tracking: OKRs are directional, not task lists

RELATIONSHIP TO GOAL-SETTING THEORY:
 OKRs operationalize Locke & Latham's findings:
  Specific + difficult + with feedback → performance
 Aspirational grading (0.6-0.7) acknowledges: too difficult goals discourage
  if they feel impossible; Locke/Latham nuance on goal difficulty range
 Quarterly cadence = continuous goal review + feedback loop

BALANCED SCORECARD (KAPLAN & NORTON, 1992):
 Four perspectives: Financial / Customer / Internal Process / Learning & Growth
 Strategy map: causal connections between objectives across perspectives
 Lagging indicators (financial results) + leading indicators (customer satisfaction)
 Purpose: translate strategy into operational terms; align org to strategy
 Critique: complexity; implementation rarely matches theory; not predictive
 Use: better as strategy communication tool than performance management tool
  (many companies use BSC to communicate strategy; don't actually balance the card)
```

## Platform Strategy and Network Effects

```
PLATFORM STRATEGY
==================

Geoffrey Parker, Marshall Van Alstyne, Sangeet Choudary
"Platform Revolution" (2016)

PLATFORM vs PIPELINE:
 Pipeline: linear value chain; firm creates value → delivers to customer
  Example: traditional manufacturer; book publisher; software ISV
 Platform: facilitates exchanges between two or more user groups
  Example: Uber (drivers + riders); App Store (developers + users);
           Azure (developers + enterprises)
 Distinction: platform doesn't own the inventory or create the content

NETWORK EFFECTS:
 DIRECT (same-side): value increases for user A when more users of same type
  Example: telephone network; social media (more friends = more value)
 INDIRECT (cross-side): value for one group increases when another group grows
  Example: App Store developers benefit from more iPhone users;
           iPhone users benefit from more App Store developers
 MULTI-SIDED PLATFORMS: multiple interdependent groups
  Credit cards (merchants + cardholders); video games (developers + players)
  Advertising platforms (advertisers + users)

WINNER-TAKE-ALL vs WINNER-TAKE-MOST:
 Winner-take-all markets:
  Strong network effects + low multi-homing + high switching costs
  Example: social networks (Facebook dominated; MySpace died);
           Uber vs Lyft (partial; not complete winner-take-all)
 Winner-take-most:
  Network effects + some multi-homing + moderate switching costs
  Example: cloud (AWS dominates but Azure + GCP viable); mobile OS (iOS + Android)
 FACTORS PREVENTING WINNER-TAKE-ALL:
  Multi-homing is cheap (users use multiple platforms)
  Niche demands not served by leading platform
  Regulatory intervention
  Complementary to other industries (platform success requires external partners)

PLATFORM ENVELOPMENT (Eisenmann et al.):
 Stronger platform attacks adjacent weaker platform's users
 Amazon: started in books → media → apparel → groceries → cloud
 Apple: iPod → iTunes → iPhone → App Store → Apple Music → Apple Arcade
 Microsoft: OS → Office → Azure → M365 → Teams → Copilot
 Defense: differentiation; bundling; exclusive access; regulatory protection

PLATFORM GOVERNANCE — DEVELOPER ECOSYSTEMS:
 Platform governance = the rules, APIs, and contracts that shape how
   external developers build on and with the platform

 API CONTRACT MANAGEMENT:
  Stability tiers: stable / preview / experimental / deprecated
  Versioning strategy: additive vs breaking change policies
  Deprecation cadence: how much notice; migration path; support window
  The governance tradeoff:
   → Strict stability: enterprise trust + ecosystem investment + slow evolution
   → Loose stability: fast innovation + fragmentation + developer attrition
   → Best practice: separate stable APIs for enterprise; fast lanes for preview

 ECOSYSTEM HEALTH MANAGEMENT:
  Developer funnel: onboarding friction, time-to-first-success, certification paths
  Partner tiers: ISV programs, marketplace certification, revenue share models
  Developer experience investment: documentation quality, SDK maintenance, samples
  Community governance: feedback mechanisms, public roadmaps, RFC processes
  Enforcement: policy violations (security, ToS), appeals, reinstatement
  → Reference: Stripe's developer experience as category-defining example
     Amazon's marketplace governance as cautionary tale on extractive dynamics

 PLATFORM ENVELOPMENT GOVERNANCE RISK:
  When the platform expands into adjacent apps where ecosystem partners compete
  → "Embrace, extend, extinguish" perception destroys ecosystem trust
  → Mitigation: clear policy on which layers the platform will and won't compete;
    structural separation between platform team and competing product teams
  → The "kill zone": the adjacency radius within which startups avoid building
    because platform expansion risk is too high → self-defeating for ecosystem

 BREAKING CHANGE GOVERNANCE:
  RFC process: announce → comment period → decision → migration period
  Automated detection: semver; backward-compatibility test suites
  Migration tooling: codemods, adapters, compatibility shims
  Version lifecycle: supported / maintenance-only / end-of-life
  Communication: changelog quality, migration guides, support channels

ANTI-NETWORK EFFECTS:
 Congestion: too many users → quality declines → value falls
  Highways; crowded restaurants; overfilled social media feeds
  Platform management: manage quality as scale grows (moderation; curation)
 Negative externalities: bad actors on platforms harm good actors
  Twitter/X bot problem; marketplace fraud; security platform vulnerabilities
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Porter's Five Forces is about industry attractiveness, not competitive position within an industry**:
The framework answers: is this industry attractive for any competitor? It doesn't tell you which competitor will win. After the five forces, you still need to choose and build a competitive position. Many strategic analyses stop at the five forces and forget to answer "and what's our strategy within this industry?"

**RBV doesn't say "find your core competence" and stick to it forever**:
The point of VRIN is identifying and protecting inimitable resources. It doesn't say the same resources stay relevant forever. Dynamic capabilities is the corrective: in changing markets, you need to sense changes AND transform your resource base, not just protect what you have.

**OKRs ≠ MBO (Management by Objectives)**:
OKRs evolved from Grove's Intel MBO, but they differ in important ways. OKRs are typically: quarterly (not annual), explicitly aspirational (vs committed MBO targets), separated from compensation (vs tied-to-pay MBO), and more visible (public within the org). OKRs done badly look like MBO done badly.

**Network effects ≠ economies of scale**:
Economies of scale: cost per unit decreases as volume increases (supply side).
Network effects: value per user increases as user count increases (demand side).
Both create competitive moats; they operate via different mechanisms and have different strategic implications.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Strategy Question | Answer/Tool |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Is this industry worth entering? | Porter's Five Forces |
| Why is Supplier X so powerful? | Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Force 3) |
| How to choose which strategy to pursue | Generic strategies (cost/differentiation/focus) |
| Why is Company X hard to imitate? | VRIN analysis (which condition is the constraint?) |
| How to build advantage in a fast-changing market | Dynamic capabilities (sensing/seizing/transforming) |
| Translating strategy into quarterly goals | OKRs |
| Why OKRs fail | Too many; sandbagging; activity metrics; no cadence |
| When platform > pipeline strategy | Two-sided market with network effects |
| Microsoft Azure as strategy | Platform; cross-side network effects (developers + enterprises) |
| "Winner takes all" in cloud? | No; winner-take-most; multi-homing (AWS + Azure + GCP) |
