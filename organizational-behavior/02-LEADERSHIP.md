# Leadership — Transformational, LMX, Dark Triad, Predictors

## The Big Picture

Leadership research has gone through cycles: from great-man trait theories, to behavioral theories (what leaders do), to situational/contingency theories (it depends), to modern approaches focusing on leader-follower relationships and organizational impact. The accumulated evidence: a handful of things actually matter and are replicable; most popular leadership frameworks are intuitive but weakly supported.

```
LEADERSHIP THEORY EVOLUTION
=============================

GENERATION 1: TRAIT THEORIES (~1900-1940s)
 "Great man" theory: leaders born, not made
 Search for universal leadership traits
 Result: hundreds of traits proposed; no consistent set
 Modern revival: Big Five personality as predictor (better evidence)

GENERATION 2: BEHAVIORAL THEORIES (~1940s-1960s)
 Ohio State studies: "initiating structure" vs "consideration"
 Michigan studies: task-oriented vs relationship-oriented
 Managerial Grid (Blake & Mouton): 9×9 grid of concern for
  people vs production → "team management" (9,9) as ideal
 Critique: no robust interaction effects; context ignored

GENERATION 3: SITUATIONAL/CONTINGENCY (~1960s-1980s)
 No universal best style; effectiveness depends on context
 Fiedler's Contingency: match leader style to situation
  favorableness (LPC scale; controversial methodology)
 Situational Leadership (Hersey/Blanchard): match style to
  follower "readiness" or development level
 Path-Goal Theory (House): leader helps followers achieve goals;
  different behaviors for different contexts
 Critique: weak empirical support; too many moderators; not predictive

GENERATION 4: RELATIONSHIP / TRANSFORMATIONAL (~1980s-2000s)
 Leader-Member Exchange (LMX): leader relationship quality varies
  by follower; in-group vs out-group
 Transformational vs Transactional leadership: still the dominant framework
 Servant leadership; authentic leadership; ethical leadership

CURRENT FRONTIER:
 Leader characteristics + context + follower → multi-level models
 Shared/distributed leadership: leadership as property of system, not person
 Remote/hybrid leadership: proximity bias and documentation demands
 Machine learning: behavioral data prediction of leadership effectiveness
```

## Trait Theories — Big Five and Intelligence

```
WHAT PREDICTS LEADERSHIP EMERGENCE AND EFFECTIVENESS?
======================================================

META-ANALYTIC FINDINGS (Judge et al., 2002; others):

BIG FIVE AND LEADERSHIP EMERGENCE:
 Extraversion: r = .31 (strongest predictor of emergence)
  Makes sense: extraverts speak up; initiate; command attention
 Conscientiousness: r = .28 (second; reliability, planning, persistence)
 Openness to experience: r = .24 (ideas; intellectual curiosity)
 Neuroticism (neg.): r = -.24 (low emotional stability predicts less)
 Agreeableness: r = .08 (weak; "nice" ≠ leader)
 All Big Five: combined multiple R ≈ .53 (leadership emergence)

INTELLIGENCE (GMA — General Mental Ability):
 Leadership effectiveness: r ≈ .27 (meta-analysis: Judge et al., 2004)
 Moderated by: follower intelligence (leaders more effective when
  moderately more intelligent than followers; too large a gap → disconnect)
 GMA predicts: strategic decision quality; handling complexity;
               learning in new domains
 Problem: many leadership selection processes don't formally test GMA
  despite it being one of the best predictors

KEY DISTINCTION: EMERGENCE vs EFFECTIVENESS
 Emergence: who gets perceived as a leader, who gets selected
 Effectiveness: who actually achieves group goals and member outcomes
 They differ: extraverts emerge; conscientiousness predicts effectiveness more
 The person selected is not always the most effective person

DARK TRIAD IN LEADERS:
 Three overlapping "dark" personality traits:
  Narcissism: grandiosity, entitlement, exploitation, need for admiration
  Machiavellianism: strategic manipulation; cynical view of human nature;
                     willingness to use others
  Subclinical Psychopathy: callousness; low empathy; impulsivity;
                             fearlessness; surface charm
 INCIDENCE: higher in executive/senior leader populations than general population
  (selection effect: organizations select partially for dominant, confident,
   ambitious individuals → dark triad scores elevated)
 SHORT-TERM BENEFITS (real):
  Narcissism: charismatic; decisive appearance; impressive first impressions
   → emergent leadership; crisis situations; visibility in competitive fields
  Psychopathy: calm under pressure; willing to make hard calls; not inhibited
   by social concern
  Machiavellianism: strategic; effective coalition-building at political level
 LONG-TERM COSTS:
  Narcissistic leaders: organizational focus shifts to leader's image;
   talent leaves (subordinates' success = threat to narcissist's status);
   ethical violations increase when narcissist feels entitled
  Psychopathic leaders: organizational harm; employees suffer; trust erodes
  Machiavellian leaders: relationships transactional; defection when useful
 THE RESEARCH VERDICT: dark triad "works" for short-term visible performance
  metrics and for individual career advancement; it is organizational poison
  for long-term institutional health, talent retention, and trust
```

## Transactional vs Transformational Leadership

```
FULL RANGE LEADERSHIP MODEL (Bass, 1985)
==========================================

Bernard Bass, building on James MacGregor Burns (1978)

TRANSACTIONAL LEADERSHIP:
 Exchange-based: leader provides rewards/punishments for performance
 COMPONENTS:
  Contingent Reward: clear expectations + rewards for meeting them
   (functional; the baseline of management)
  Management by Exception — Active (MBE-A): actively monitor for deviations;
   intervene before problems compound
  Management by Exception — Passive (MBE-P): wait for problems to occur;
   then correct (less effective than MBE-A)
  Laissez-faire: abdication of leadership; no direction or feedback
   (negative: not neutral)
 Effect: produces expected performance; followers do what's required;
         not transformative; tends to maintain status quo

TRANSFORMATIONAL LEADERSHIP (the 4 I's):
 Idealized Influence (II): leader as role model; admired and emulated;
  sets example of high ethical standards and competence
 Inspirational Motivation (IM): articulates compelling vision; creates
  meaning; elevates followers' aspirations beyond self-interest
 Intellectual Stimulation (IS): challenges assumptions; encourages
  creative thinking; tolerates mistakes as learning opportunities
 Individualized Consideration (IC): treats each follower as individual;
  coaches; mentors; attends to developmental needs

 Effect: followers perform beyond expectations ("augmentation effect");
          higher commitment; creativity; lower turnover intention
 Meta-analytic evidence: r ≈ .44 with performance (corrected); strong

AUGMENTATION EFFECT:
 Transformational leadership augments transactional leadership
 NOT a replacement: a leader who only "transforms" without clear
  performance expectations and follow-through doesn't perform well
 The combination: clear contingent reward (transactional) +
  vision + inspiration (transformational) = highest performance

CRITIQUE:
 Construct overlap: the 4 I's correlate highly (discriminant validity?)
 Cause/effect: leaders of high-performing teams rated more transformational
  → performance → ratings? or ratings → performance?
 Charisma risk: dark triad leaders can score high on "idealized influence"
  Narcissistic charisma = transformational? Research shows overlap
 Attribution: followers attribute transformational behaviors more to
  high-performing leaders → halo effect inflation in measurement
```

## Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)

```
LMX — DIFFERENTIATED RELATIONSHIPS
=====================================

George Graen and colleagues; developed 1970s-1990s

CORE IDEA:
 Leaders don't treat all followers equally
 → develop different quality exchange relationships with each follower
 The relationship quality varies from:
  HIGH-QUALITY LMX (in-group): trust, mutual respect, reciprocity,
   liking, latitude; goes beyond the employment contract
  LOW-QUALITY LMX (out-group): formal role-based exchange; formal
   authority; compliance to job requirements only

DEVELOPMENT PROCESS:
 Role-taking: leader offers new responsibilities; follower accepts/declines
 Role-making: mutual testing; leader observes follower response;
  follower observes leader fairness and support
 Role-routinization: stable relationship pattern established
 (typically 2-4 weeks in a new assignment to crystallize)

CONSEQUENCES OF DIFFERENTIATION:
 High-LMX followers: more challenging assignments; better information;
  more support; higher performance evaluations (some bias);
  more likely to be promoted; higher job satisfaction and commitment
 Low-LMX followers: routine assignments; formal communication only;
  less access to resources; may perceive unfairness
 Team level: high LMX differentiation (some high, some low in same team)
  → reduced team cohesion; perceived unfairness; interpersonal conflict

EQUITY CONCERN:
 Who gets into the "in-group"? Research shows:
  - Similarity to leader (demographic, attitudinal) → higher LMX
  - Early performance signal → self-fulfilling prophecy
  - Social skills → higher LMX
  - Extraversion → higher LMX
  Implication: systematic bias can advantage certain employees;
  diversity implications if in-group demographics ≠ workforce demographics

PRACTICAL MANAGEMENT IMPLICATION:
 Leaders benefit from intentionally developing high-LMX relationships
  with all direct reports, not just the "natural" in-group
 This requires: deliberate attention; scheduled 1:1s; developmental
  assignments offered broadly; feedback provided consistently
 "Managed LMX": awareness of differentiation risk → mitigation
```

## Situational Leadership

```
SITUATIONAL LEADERSHIP (HERSEY & BLANCHARD)
============================================

Paul Hersey and Ken Blanchard (1969; later Blanchard updated model)

THE MODEL:
 Four leadership styles matched to follower "development level":
  (combination of competence + commitment/motivation)

 D1: Low comp / High commit (enthusiastic beginner)
  → S1: Directing (high task / low relationship)
  Clear instructions; close supervision; "do it this way"

 D2: Low-moderate comp / Low commit (disillusioned learner)
  → S2: Coaching (high task / high relationship)
  Direction + explanation + encouragement + feedback

 D3: Moderate-high comp / Variable commit (capable but cautious performer)
  → S3: Supporting (low task / high relationship)
  Less direction; more encouragement; collaborative problem-solving

 D4: High comp / High commit (self-reliant achiever)
  → S4: Delegating (low task / low relationship)
  Empower; provide resources; stay out of the way

WIDE ADOPTION:
 One of the most widely used leadership frameworks in corporate training
 Intuitive; easy to teach; appears actionable
 Versions: SL I (Hersey), SL II (Blanchard separately)

EVIDENCE CRITIQUE:
 Meta-analytic evidence: weak; does not consistently replicate
 Problems:
  No validated measure of "development level"
  The four-quadrant matching rule not empirically demonstrated
  Leader style self-assessment (LEAD instrument): questionable validity
  Alternative explanation: better leaders just adapt generally; the
   specific D1-S1, D2-S2 matching isn't uniquely predictive
 Professional verdict: widely used, intuitively useful as a communication
  framework, not a reliable predictive theory

THE USEFUL INSIGHT BEHIND IT:
 Different followers at different stages genuinely need different things
 → a new hire needs more direction than a 10-year veteran
 → the principle is correct; the specific quadrant-matching rule is not
```

## Servant and Authentic Leadership

```
SERVANT LEADERSHIP
===================

Robert Greenleaf (1970, "The Servant as Leader")

CORE IDEA:
 Leader's first orientation: to serve followers and community
 Power: used for others' benefit, not leader's advancement
 Behaviors: listening; empathy; healing; awareness; persuasion;
            conceptualization; foresight; stewardship; commitment to
            growth of people; building community

EVIDENCE:
 Growing research base; positive correlations with:
  Employee wellbeing; organizational citizenship behavior;
  trust in leader; customer satisfaction
 Meta-analysis (Liden et al., 2014): positive effects on follower
  satisfaction, commitment, and OCB
 Critique: construct overlaps with transformational (individualized
  consideration) and ethical leadership; unique contribution unclear

AUTHENTIC LEADERSHIP (Luthans, Avolio, others)
===============================================
 Four dimensions:
  Self-awareness: understand your own values, emotions, strengths
  Relational transparency: share information; accurate self-presentation
  Balanced processing: objectively analyze relevant data before deciding
  Internalized moral perspective: ethical decision-making from
   internalized values, not external pressures
 Evidence: moderate; meta-analyses show positive relationship with
  follower performance and wellbeing
 Critique: self-report of authenticity is unreliable
  ("I'm authentic" = demand characteristic); construct overlaps
  with integrity and transformational leadership
 Dark triad overlap problem: narcissists believe they ARE authentic
  → self-report doesn't differentiate
```

## Psychometric Predictors and Assessment

```
PREDICTING LEADERSHIP EFFECTIVENESS
=====================================

WHAT ACTUALLY PREDICTS (meta-analytic evidence hierarchy):

Predictor                       Validity   Notes
————————————                    ————————   —————
GMA (cognitive ability)         r ≈ .27    Complex environments > simple
Conscientiousness                r ≈ .28    Stronger for effectiveness than emergence
Structured interview             r ≈ .51    Highest validity of common assessments
Situational judgment test (SJT)  r ≈ .26   Context-specific; culture-sensitive
360-degree feedback               r ≈ .28   Multi-rater reduces bias vs single-rater
Work sample / assessment center   r ≈ .37   Expensive; high face validity
Unstructured interview            r ≈ .20   "Culture fit" interviews; lower validity
Personality test (Big Five)       r ≈ .22   Conscientiousness + Extraversion together

GMA + Conscientiousness combined: r ≈ .50+ (compound validity)

LEADERSHIP ASSESSMENT CENTERS:
 In-baskets; group exercises; role plays; presentations
 Assessors: trained raters observing behaviors
 Cost: $2,000-$10,000 per candidate (full day)
 Validity: among the highest of any selection tool
 Used for: high-stakes leadership selection (military; senior executive)

360-DEGREE FEEDBACK:
 Multiple raters: boss, peers, direct reports, self
 More accurate than single-source (reduces bias)
 Self-other agreement: leaders' self-ratings vs others' ratings
  Self-raters who overestimate themselves (relative to others):
   → correlated with worse outcomes in some studies (overestimators underperform)
  Self-raters who underestimate ("underestimators"): tend to be higher performers
 Development use: 360 as development tool is well-supported
 Selection use: 360 as selection tool is less appropriate
  (candidates know; gaming; relationships affect ratings)

INTERVIEW DESIGN:
 Structured: same questions for all; behavioral or situational
  Behavioral: "Tell me about a time when..." → past behavior
  Situational: "What would you do if..." → judgment
 Unstructured: conversation-based; perceived as more valid by interviewers
  REALITY: unstructured has LOWER validity (r≈.20 vs .51 structured)
  → interviewer confidence ≠ predictive accuracy

REMOTE AND HYBRID LEADERSHIP:
 Proximity bias: remote workers perceived as less engaged, less committed
  → disadvantaged in performance reviews and promotions
 Documentation becomes leadership: visible work products (writing,
  comments, updates) substitute for observable presence
 Async communication: requires clearer, more explicit communication
  → leadership skill of written clarity becomes critical
 Trust: built more slowly without FtF; higher variance in trust levels
  across distributed teams vs co-located
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Leadership emergence ≠ leadership effectiveness**:
Extraverts emerge as leaders in leaderless groups. Conscientiousness predicts effectiveness more than emergence. The person who becomes the leader is not necessarily the person who leads most effectively.

**Charisma ≠ transformational leadership**:
Idealized influence (one of the 4 I's) involves being a role model and inspiring confidence. Charisma is a perception phenomenon that can come from dark triad characteristics as well as genuine transformational behavior. High charisma + low conscientiousness + high narcissism = dangerous leader profile that tests as "transformational."

**Situational Leadership is widely used, not well-supported**:
A high majority of Fortune 500 companies have trained managers in Situational Leadership. The empirical support for the specific D-S matching is weak. The meta-principle (adapt to the person and situation) is correct; the specific model prescription is not proven.

**360 feedback for development ≠ 360 for selection**:
When employees know their 360 results affect compensation or promotion, they adjust their relationships strategically. The value of 360 as a developmental tool disappears when stakes are high. Keep developmental feedback separate from evaluative decisions.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Leadership Question | Answer |
|--------------------|--------|
| Best single Big Five predictor of leadership emergence | Extraversion |
| Best predictor of leadership effectiveness | Conscientiousness + GMA combined |
| What the "4 I's" describe | Transformational leadership (II, IM, IS, IC) |
| Why dark triad leaders survive | Short-term visible metrics; charisma; selection bias |
| Highest validity leadership selection tool | Structured behavioral interview (r≈.51) |
| LMX differentiation risk | In-group bias; demographic similarity effects |
| Why "do your best" goals underperform | Goal-setting theory: vague goals → less effort mobilized |

| Situational leadership evidence | Widely used; weak empirical support for specific matching |
