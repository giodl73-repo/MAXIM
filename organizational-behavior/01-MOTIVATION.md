# Motivation — SDT, Herzberg, Expectancy Theory, Goal-Setting, Pay

## The Big Picture

Motivation theory in OB asks: what drives people to exert effort, persist, and perform? Three generations of theory: content theories (what motivates), process theories (how motivation works mechanically), and modern integrated frameworks (when and why different motivators work). The core tension: intrinsic motivation (doing something for its own sake) vs extrinsic motivation (doing something for external reward) — and whether one crowds out the other.


## Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

```
SDT — THE CURRENT BEST FRAMEWORK
==================================

CORE PREMISE:
 Humans have basic psychological needs whose satisfaction → wellbeing + growth
 Need deprivation → diminished functioning and wellbeing
 The framework explains motivation quality, not just quantity

THREE BASIC PSYCHOLOGICAL NEEDS:
 AUTONOMY: acting with volition; experiencing oneself as the origin
           of one's actions (NOT independence — can be autonomous while
           following someone else's direction if you endorse it)
           → the distinction between "I chose to do this" vs "I must do this"
 COMPETENCE: feeling effective; experiencing growth and mastery
              → need for challenge appropriate to skill level
 RELATEDNESS: feeling connected; caring for and being cared for by others
               → belonging; mattering to others

MOTIVATION CONTINUUM (SDT's key contribution):
 Not just intrinsic vs extrinsic — a spectrum of internalization:

 Amotivation → External → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic
      |              |           |             |            |           |
  No intention  "Have to"   "Should"    "Want to"   "Part of me"  "Enjoy it"
  (apathy)      Reward/     Guilt/      Personal    Coherent with  Own interest
                punishment  shame       value       values/self    for its own sake

 ← Controlled regulation ——→ ←—— Autonomous regulation ——→

 IMPLICATIONS:
  Identified, integrated, and intrinsic = autonomous → better outcomes:
  greater persistence, wellbeing, creativity, performance on complex tasks
  External and introjected = controlled → lower quality motivation:
  performance decreases when reward removed; reduced creativity; stress

COGNITIVE EVALUATION THEORY (sub-theory of SDT):
 Extrinsic rewards can crowd out intrinsic motivation:
  Contingent rewards (paid for doing task X) → undermine intrinsic interest
   especially for tasks that were already intrinsically interesting
  Mechanism: external reward → "I'm doing this for the money" → sense of
   control → loss of autonomy → reduced intrinsic motivation
  Classic study: Deci (1971) with Soma puzzle; paid group → intrinsic
   motivation decreased after pay stopped
  WHEN extrinsic rewards don't crowd out: if they provide informational
   (competence) feedback AND don't feel controlling → can enhance motivation
  Performance-contingent "doing well" pay: less damaging than task-contingent
   "doing the task" pay; informational rather than controlling
  This is why flat salaries + intrinsic recognition can outperform piece-rate
   for complex, creative, or knowledge work

OB APPLICATION:
 Management actions that support autonomy:
  Offering choice; providing rationale for requests; acknowledging feelings
  Avoiding surveillance, deadlines, evaluative pressure where possible
  NOT: hands-off neglect; supporting autonomy while maintaining structure
 Competence support: optimal challenge (not too easy, not overwhelming);
  positive feedback; skill development opportunities
 Relatedness support: team cohesion; manager care for individuals;
  psychological safety (see 03-TEAMS-GROUPS)

UNIVERSALITY EVIDENCE:
 SDT tested in 77+ countries; basic needs appear universal
 Cultural variation: form of need satisfaction differs (individualist vs
  collectivist cultures satisfy relatedness differently)
 BUT: the basic need structure replicates cross-culturally
```

## Herzberg Two-Factor Theory

```
HERZBERG TWO-FACTOR (HYGIENE-MOTIVATOR) THEORY
================================================

Frederick Herzberg (1959, "The Motivation to Work")

METHODOLOGY:
 Critical incident technique: ask employees to describe events
  associated with "good feelings" and "bad feelings" about work
 Finding: different factors described each:
  Good feelings: achievement, recognition, work itself, responsibility, growth
  Bad feelings: company policy, supervision, working conditions, salary,
                relationship with supervisor, status, security

TWO FACTORS:
 HYGIENE FACTORS (maintenance factors):
  Salary, supervision quality, company policy, working conditions,
  job security, relationship with supervisor and peers
  Effect: when present → no dissatisfaction; when absent → dissatisfaction
  But: providing more does NOT produce satisfaction or motivation
  "Removing the pain doesn't create pleasure"

 MOTIVATORS:
  Achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility,
  advancement, personal growth
  Effect: when present → satisfaction and motivation; when absent →
  no particular dissatisfaction (neutral)
  "Adding genuine motivators produces real motivation"

JOB ENRICHMENT:
 The practical implication: make work more intrinsically motivating
 Vertical loading (not horizontal):
  Give employees more responsibility and control, not just more tasks
  (adding more tasks at same level = horizontal loading = not enriching)
 Tactics: remove controls; increase accountability; complete work units;
           give direct feedback; introduce new tasks; grant additional authority

METHODOLOGICAL CRITIQUE:
 Attribution bias: people attribute good events to self (achievement);
  bad events to external factors (company policy, bad boss)
  → methodological artifact of critical incident technique?
  (Same bias that makes us take credit for success, blame context for failure)
 Test-retest: other methods don't reliably reproduce two-factor structure
 Cross-cultural: hygiene/motivator distinction doesn't replicate well
  in all cultures

VERDICT:
 The two-factor distinction is a useful heuristic: salary doesn't motivate
 beyond a threshold; interesting work does. But as a precise theory with
 two cleanly separable factors, it doesn't hold up to scrutiny.
 Still widely cited; still useful as a framing device.
```

## Expectancy Theory

```
VROOM'S EXPECTANCY THEORY (1964)
===================================

Victor Vroom, "Work and Motivation"

THE VIE MODEL:
 Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence

 EXPECTANCY (E): "Can I do this?"
  Probability that effort → performance
  Influenced by: skill, experience, confidence, job clarity,
                 resource availability
  Range: 0 to 1

 INSTRUMENTALITY (I): "Will performance → reward?"
  Probability that performance → outcome (reward)
  Influenced by: trust in management, transparency of pay system,
                 consistency of rewards, clear performance metrics
  Range: 0 to 1

 VALENCE (V): "Do I value this reward?"
  Subjective value of the outcome to the individual
  Range: -1 (strongly disvalue) to +1 (strongly desire)
  Key: different employees value different outcomes

 MULTIPLICATION: any term at zero → motivation = zero
  Even high expectancy and high instrumentality: if the reward is irrelevant
  to the employee → motivation = 0
  This captures: "work harder for a promotion I don't want" = doesn't work

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:
 Manager interventions for each component:
  E (effort → performance): ensure clear job design; training; feedback;
   remove obstacles; ensure adequate resources
  I (performance → outcome): make reward systems transparent; be consistent;
   follow through on commitments; reduce uncertainty
  V (valence): understand what individuals actually value;
   don't assume everyone wants the same outcomes
   (money vs autonomy vs recognition vs development vary by individual)

CRITIQUE:
 Mechanistic: assumes conscious calculation before each action
  (most motivated behavior is habitual, not calculated)
 Measurement: how do you measure expectancy and valence objectively?
  (self-report; subject to biases)
 Multiplication vs addition: actual combination rule unclear
 Limited empirical support in field (lab support stronger)

VERDICT:
 Excellent for analysis and communication. Helps managers ask the right
 questions about motivation barriers. Not a precise predictive model.
```

## Goal-Setting Theory

```
GOAL-SETTING THEORY (LOCKE & LATHAM)
=======================================

Most well-supported motivation theory in OB. Replication rate: high.

Edwin Locke & Gary Latham; developed 1960s-1990s.

CORE FINDING:
 Specific + difficult goals → higher performance than:
  - "Do your best" (vague)
  - No goal
  - Specific + easy goals (more effort than easy; less than hard)
 Effect size: d ≈ 0.68 (Schmidt & Hunter meta-analysis — strong effect)

MECHANISMS (why goals work):
 Direction: attention focused on goal-relevant activities
 Intensity: goals mobilize effort proportional to perceived difficulty
 Persistence: maintain effort over time until goal met
 Cognitive strategies: hard goals prompt strategy development

MODERATORS:
 Goal acceptance: goals must be accepted/committed to (cannot just assign)
  Goal acceptance → participation OR clear explanation of rationale
 Feedback: goals + feedback combined > goals alone
  The goal tells you where to go; feedback tells you where you are
 Ability: if goal is beyond current ability, no benefit (need training first)
 Task complexity: less effective on novel/complex tasks requiring
  strategy discovery (goal may prematurely narrow focus)

GOAL CONFLICT:
 Multiple goals compete for effort allocation
 Prioritization required; goal conflict → reduced performance on each
 OKR design: max 3-5 objectives; key results specific and few
  (Doerr's guidance; mirrors Locke & Latham's operationalization)

DISTAL VS PROXIMAL GOALS:
 Distal (outcome goal): "lose 20 lbs"; "increase revenue 30%"
 Proximal (process/behavior goal): "exercise 45 min daily"; "contact 5 new customers/week"
 Research: proximal behavioral goals → better for complex behavior;
  distal outcome goals → better when performance already high (stretch)
  Combined (proximal + distal): often best

SMART GOALS:
 Specific / Measurable / Achievable / Relevant / Time-bound
 OB comment: "achievable" is WRONG if goal-setting theory is right
  Difficult (not just achievable) goals → higher performance
  SMART as typically taught misapplies the research:
  Should be: specific, difficult (stretch), with feedback, time-bounded

OKRs (OBJECTIVES AND KEY RESULTS):
 Andy Grove (Intel) → John Doerr → Google → widespread adoption
 Objective: qualitative, aspirational direction ("define the metric for success")
 Key Results: 3-5 quantified outcomes that operationalize the objective
 Cadence: quarterly typically; annual for larger objectives
 Grades: 0-1.0 scale; 0.6-0.7 as success indicator (if always 1.0 → too easy)
 "Committed OKRs" vs "Aspirational OKRs": committed = must hit;
  aspirational = stretch, 0.7 = success
 Common failure modes:
  Too many: more than 5 objectives dilutes focus → all fail
  Sandbagging: deliberately setting easy KRs to guarantee 1.0
  Gaming: measuring a proxy instead of the real outcome
  Neglect: quarterly review not happening → OKRs become wallpaper
  Output vs outcome confusion: KRs measuring activities, not results
```

## Equity Theory and Pay

```
EQUITY THEORY (ADAMS)
======================

John Stacey Adams (1963, 1965)

CORE MECHANISM:
 Perceived fairness of: (My outcomes / My inputs) vs (Referent's outcomes / Referent's inputs)
 Inputs: effort, skill, experience, education, loyalty, time
 Outcomes: pay, recognition, status, autonomy, challenging work
 Referent: comparison target (co-worker, industry average, "myself last year")

INEQUITY STATES:
 UNDERPAYMENT INEQUITY (my ratio < referent's):
  Response: anger, resentment
  Behavior: reduce inputs (lower effort), demand more outcomes,
             change referent ("well, compared to...")
             or exit (quit)
  Effect on performance: decreases
  Effect on theft: Adams' famous finding — piece-rate workers who were
   told they were overpaid reduced their quantity but improved quality;
   underpaid workers reduced both
 OVERPAYMENT INEQUITY (my ratio > referent's):
  Response: guilt (but less intense; humans adapt quickly)
  Behavior: increase inputs (briefly), cognitive reappraisal
   ("I'm worth more"; change perception of referent's inputs)
  Effect: weaker behavioral change than underpayment

KEY INSIGHTS:
 The ratio matters more than absolute level: $80K when peers earn $60K
  feels better than $100K when peers earn $120K
 Perceived inputs and outcomes (subjective) → cognitive reappraisal is easy
 Reference group matters: which "referent" is activated changes the calculus
 Applies to non-pay outcomes: recognition, interesting assignments, title

PAY TRANSPARENCY:
 Information about others' pay activates equity comparisons
 Effect: underpaid employees intensely demotivated on discovering disparity
 Mixed evidence: transparency can improve or worsen outcomes depending on
  whether pay is actually equitable and explainable
  Companies with explained pay bands: transparency → trust
  Companies with arbitrary pay: transparency → resentment + exits

TOURNAMENT THEORY BRIDGE:
 Large pay gaps between levels → intense competition for promotion
 Works when: output measurable; competition is healthy; team interdependence low
 Backfires when: cooperation required; promotion = sabotage motive;
                 employees are risk-averse (don't compete for winner-take-all)
```

## From Incentive Design to Motivation Theory

The economic models of incentive design and the behavioral OB models of motivation are in productive tension. Understanding both — and where they diverge — is the core engineering-management design problem.

**The principal-agent model assumes extrinsic incentives are the primary lever; SDT says this is wrong for the work that matters most.** Principal-agent theory (the foundation of most management accounting frameworks) treats agents as utility maximizers who will shirk when monitoring is imperfect and will respond to financial incentives proportional to measured output. This model is accurate for algorithmic tasks (narrow, rule-governed, measurable output) — piece-rate in agriculture, fixed-scope deliverables, call center volume. For heuristic and creative tasks (software design, architecture decisions, research, strategy), the model is wrong in a specific direction: contingent pay activates a controlled motivation frame that substitutes for intrinsic engagement and narrows the search behavior needed for complex problem-solving. This is Deci's cognitive evaluation theory — not management philosophy, but a mechanism with empirical support.

**The economic intuition that "you get what you measure" formalizes as Goodhart's Law, and SDT explains the mechanism.** When an engineer is evaluated on story points completed per sprint, story points become a target rather than a measure. The SDT mechanism: the metric becomes a controlling external pressure that shifts the engineer from identified motivation ("I care about this product") to introjected or external motivation ("I need to hit the number"). Once the shift happens, optimization is against the metric, not against the underlying goal. This is why OKR grading should be separated from compensation — the moment it is tied to pay, it triggers the crowding-out dynamic.

**Efficiency wages (pay above market) work through multiple SDT-compatible channels simultaneously.** The economic argument: above-market pay reduces shirking (job worth keeping) and attracts higher-ability workers. The SDT complement: above-market pay, when perceived as equitable and non-controlling, sets a high reference anchor that employees feel they must justify — which activates identified motivation ("I'm proud of where I work; I want to contribute to something worth this"). The two mechanisms are not competing explanations; they operate through different channels. What degrades both is the perception of inequity or arbitrariness: if the above-market pay is perceived as unequally distributed (equity theory) or as a controlling tool rather than fair compensation (SDT), neither mechanism operates.

**Tournament theory (large pay gaps between levels) predicts behavioral effects that are well-documented in engineering orgs.** The logic: large level-to-level pay differences create intense competition for promotion, which increases effort. The failure mode: tournament incentives destroy cooperation precisely when cooperation is the core production technology. In engineering organizations where code review, architecture alignment, incident response, and knowledge transfer require active peer cooperation, tournament dynamics create the wrong incentive. The observed pathology — engineers who hoard information, resist code review that would educate competitors, and avoid mentoring junior engineers — is not cultural failure; it is the predicted equilibrium of a tournament incentive structure.

## Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Rewards in Practice

```
WHEN PERFORMANCE PAY WORKS (AND WHEN IT BACKFIRES)
====================================================

ALGORITHMIC TASKS (narrow rules; clear procedure; single correct approach):
 Performance pay → higher output
 Piece rate: well-studied in agriculture, manufacturing, sales
 Effect: positive; consistent with expectancy theory + equity theory
 Risk: measurement → gaming (employees optimize metric, not underlying goal)

HEURISTIC / CREATIVE TASKS (novel; require judgment; no clear procedure):
 Performance pay → no improvement; often decreases performance
 Evidence: Glucksberg (1962) candle problem; Amabile creativity studies;
           multiple replications
 Mechanism: performance pressure → attentional narrowing → less
  creative exploration; "local search" not "global search"
 OB implication: software development, research, strategy, design, consulting
  → contingent pay on narrow outputs is counterproductive
  → pay for broader, longer-term outcomes or pay fairly and focus on
     intrinsic motivation

DAN PINK'S "DRIVE" (2009):
 Popularized the intrinsic/extrinsic distinction for business audiences
 Key message: three drivers for complex work — autonomy, mastery, purpose
  (mapped to SDT: autonomy ≈ autonomy; mastery ≈ competence; purpose ≈ meaning)
 Criticism: Pink oversimplified the research (not all performance pay is bad;
  the algorithmic/heuristic distinction is real but not always clean)
 Verdict: directionally correct; oversold in popular use

EFFICIENCY WAGES:
 Pay above market → reduces shirking; attracts higher-ability workers; reduces turnover
 Research: large-employer wage premium is documented; plausible efficiency wage
 Not a motivation theory per se — more of a labor economics argument
 OB integration: the "above market" pay sets a reference anchor;
  employees feel they're getting a fair deal (equity) AND can't easily leave
  (job is worth keeping) — two motivation mechanisms simultaneously

RECOGNITION AND PRAISE:
 Non-monetary recognition: strong motivator when it conveys competence
  informational feedback vs social desirability pressure
 Public recognition: can backfire if it embarrasses; depends on culture
  and individual (introversion vs extroversion)
 SDT: recognition that supports competence + autonomy → motivation
  recognition that feels controlling or conditional → undermines motivation
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Maslow's hierarchy has no empirical support for the sequence**:
The idea that lower-order needs must be satisfied before higher-order needs activate is not well-supported. People can have strong growth and belonging needs even when basic security needs aren't fully met. The list of needs is useful; the hierarchy is not.

**Intrinsic motivation being crowded out by extrinsic rewards is conditional**:
Not all extrinsic rewards undermine intrinsic motivation. The key is whether the reward feels controlling (contingent on doing the task) vs informational (feedback on doing it well). Performance-contingent "you did that very well" feedback can enhance intrinsic motivation.

**"Autonomy" in SDT ≠ independence**:
Autonomy means volitional — experiencing yourself as the source of your action. You can be highly autonomous while following instructions if you endorse those instructions. A nurse following a protocol she understands and endorses can be more autonomous than an employee who makes arbitrary independent choices she doesn't believe in.

**OKRs ≠ goal-setting theory perfectly applied**:
OKRs operationalize goal-setting principles (specific, difficult goals with feedback), but organizational politics often corrupt them. Sandbagging, gaming, and metric fixation are predictable failure modes. The theory is right; the implementation routinely fails.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Theory/Tool |
|-----------|-------------|
| Diagnosing why someone isn't performing | Expectancy theory (E × I × V analysis) |
| Designing performance goals | Goal-setting (specific, difficult + feedback) |
| Why generous salary doesn't motivate | Herzberg (hygiene factor — removes dissatisfaction only) |
| Why employee feels unfairly treated despite good pay | Equity theory (comparison group activated) |
| Designing creative team's work environment | SDT (support autonomy, competence, relatedness) |
| When performance pay backfires | Heuristic/creative tasks; cognitive evaluation theory |
| Best supported motivation theory overall | Self-Determination Theory (SDT) |
| OKRs: failure diagnosis | Too many objectives; sandbagging; gaming metrics |
