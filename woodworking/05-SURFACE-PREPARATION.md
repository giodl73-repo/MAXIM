# Surface Preparation: Flattening, Planing, Scraping, Sanding

## The Big Picture

Surface preparation is the bridge between raw stock and finishing. The goal is a surface that is flat, smooth, and scratch-free — in that order. Getting the order wrong (sanding before flattening, or finishing before removing machine marks) produces work that looks poor under any finish. Every finish amplifies surface defects.

```
SURFACE PREPARATION SEQUENCE
==============================

ROUGH LUMBER → FINAL SURFACE (correct order):

1. FLATTEN (establish one reference plane)
   Winding sticks → identify twist
   Hand plane or jointer → remove twist and high spots

2. MILL TO THICKNESS
   Thickness planer or hand plane traverse → uniform thickness
   Parallel faces; right angles to edges

3. REMOVE MILL MARKS
   Traverse plane passes → removes planer tracks and any remaining twist
   Diagonal plane passes → blends the traverse passes

4. FINAL SURFACE
   With-grain passes → final smoothing, parallel to grain
   Card scraper OR
   Fine-grit sandpaper (80 → 120 → 180 → 220 → 320)

5. PRE-FINISHING CHECK
   Rake light across surface to find remaining scratches
   Test under finish (wipe with naphtha or water)
   Grain raise check (water-based finishes: raise grain first; sand)

↓
APPLY FINISH
```

## Winding Sticks — Detecting Twist

The most important skill in surface preparation: knowing whether a board is flat or twisted.

```
WINDING STICKS
===============

Two identical parallel sticks placed across the board:

FLAT BOARD:
  ┌─────────┐
  │   ════  │
  │         │
  │         │
  │   ════  │
  └─────────┘
  Top stick = near stick; bottom stick = far stick.
  Sticks appear parallel → board is flat.

TWISTED BOARD:
  ┌────────────────┐
  │   ════         │
  │                │
  │         ════   │
  │   ════         │
  └────────────────┘
  Top = near stick; bottom = far stick; middle slash shows offset.
  Sticks are out of parallel → board is twisted (in winding).

SETUP:
  Sticks: any two straight, parallel pieces of wood (same width, same height)
  Place at near end and far end of board
  Sight across at eye level — low, parallel to the near stick
  High corner: indicates where to plane

CORRECTING TWIST:
  Plane diagonal across the high corners first
  Recheck with winding sticks after each diagonal pass
  Continue until sticks are parallel
  Then: standard traverse passes to remove high spots; check with straightedge
```

## Hand Planing Sequence

```
COMPLETE PLANING SEQUENCE
===========================

PHASE 1: TRAVERSE PLANING (perpendicular to grain)
  ─────────────────→  board grain direction
  ↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑     plane direction: 90° to grain
  Use: No.5 jack with cambered iron
  Purpose: Removes high spots quickly; very aggressive
  Risk: tear-out at grain transitions (accepts this at this stage)
  Check: straightedge across width (should rock on nothing)

PHASE 2: DIAGONAL PLANING (~45° to grain)
  ↑ ↑ ↑ ↑ ↑             two passes: one diagonal, one reverse diagonal
   ↑ ↑ ↑ ↑ ↑            blends the ridges left by traverse planing
  Use: No.5 jack or No.4 smoother
  Check: still with straightedge; should now be smooth across width

PHASE 3: WITH-GRAIN PLANING (parallel to grain)
  ↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑     parallel to grain direction
  Use: No.4 smoother OR No.7 jointer (for long boards requiring flatness)
  Light cut: 0.001–0.003" shaving thickness
  Check: surface should be smooth; shavings should be continuous gossamer
  This is the final step before finish or scraper

PLANING DIRECTION MATTERS:
  Grain "runs uphill" toward the cut:
  ← ← ← ← ←   grain fibers here slope like this
  Plane from right to left: fibers are severed cleanly (with grain)
  Plane from left to right: fibers are lifted and torn (against grain)
  Check grain direction at the edge; always plane "with" the slope

TEAR-OUT:
  Cause: planing against grain lifts fibers rather than severing them
  Solutions:
    1. Reverse direction (plane the other way)
    2. Narrow the mouth of the smoother
    3. Set chip breaker very close to edge (0.5mm)
    4. Use a bevel-up plane with higher blade angle (50–60°)
    5. Use a card scraper (no grain direction sensitivity)
```

## The Card Scraper

One of woodworking's most underrated tools. A thin steel plate with a tiny burr on its edge — that burr does the cutting.

```
CARD SCRAPER GEOMETRY
======================

CROSS-SECTION OF A SCRAPER:
  ─────────────────────────
        SCRAPER BODY
        (0.032" thick carbon steel)
  ─────────────────────────
                 ↑
               BURR (wire edge)
               formed by burnishing

  The burr is ~0.001–0.003" — a tiny hook
  This hook peels fine shavings from the wood surface
  Different from sanding: CUTTING, not abrading
  Result: no scratches (cuts fibers clean); can work against grain

HOW TO CREATE THE BURR:
1. File the edge perfectly flat and square (flat file)
2. Stone the edge flat and the face flat (both sides, both edges)
   → Removes file marks; produces clean reference edges
3. Burnish the edge at 90° with hardened burnisher
   → Consolidates the edge, removes micro-irregularities
4. Burnish with burnisher tilted ~5–10° to one side
   → Rolls the edge into a slight hook = the cutting burr

HOLDING AND USING:
  Grip with thumbs on back, fingers wrapped on front
  Flex slightly with thumbs (convex = cuts in center; concave = cuts edges)
  Push at ~70° to the surface; hook catches and takes a shaving
  Heat in thumbs = friction from cutting = edge wearing (resharpen)

WHEN THE SCRAPER IS USEFUL:
  ✓ Difficult grain (curly, burl, interlocked)
  ✓ After hand planing: final surface without grain direction constraint
  ✓ Removing glue squeeze-out (without sandpaper scratches)
  ✓ Small repairs; localized surface leveling
  ✓ Final prep before oil finish (thins finish well)
  ✗ Not for material removal (too slow for bulk stock)
```

## Sandpaper — When and How

Sanding is the most misused process in woodworking:

```
SANDPAPER GRIT PROGRESSION
============================

PURPOSE OF GRIT SEQUENCE: each grit removes the scratches from the previous

Typical starting point by condition:
  Machine planer tracks: start at 60–80 grit
  Hand-planed, some mill marks: start at 100–120 grit
  Card-scraped, clean surface: start at 150–180 grit
  Already fine, just raising grain: start at 220 grit

STANDARD PROGRESSION:
  80 → 120 → 150 → 180 → 220 → (320)

  Each step: sand until all scratches from previous grit are gone
  (not "until it feels smooth" — visually inspect at raking light)

  Skipping grits doubles the work:
  If you skip 120 and go 80→150, the 150 must remove 80-grit scratches,
  which takes longer than going 80→120→150

  When to stop:
    Oil/wax penetrating finishes: 180–220 adequate
    Film-building finishes (lacquer, poly): 180–220 adequate
    Water-based finishes (raise grain first, sand at 220 after first coat)
    Beyond 320: generally no benefit under most finishes

ORBITAL SANDER NOTES:
  Always move in the direction of grain for the final passes
  Circular orbits leave spiral scratches (visible under finish)
  Last few passes: use an in-line sander or hand-sand with grain
  Final sanding by hand (with grain): removes orbital scratch pattern

AVOID:
  Sanding across grain at any grit (scratches won't hide under finish)
  Sanding painted parts with too fine a grit (finish won't bond)
  Sanding beyond 220 before water-based finish (so smooth finish won't adhere)
```

## Grain Raising and Pre-Conditioning

```
GRAIN RAISING
==============

WHY GRAIN RAISES:
  Wood fibers are like cut straws — many are severed at the surface
  First application of water-based finish wets these fibers → they swell
  → Fuzzy surface under finish → requires sanding after first coat
  Sanding after first coat = scraping and recoating = delays project

SOLUTION: Raise the grain BEFORE finishing
  1. Wipe bare wood with water (or very dilute finish)
  2. Let dry completely (overnight preferred)
  3. Sand with 220 grit to knock down the raised fibers
  4. Apply finish: grain has already raised; won't raise again much
  5. No sanding required between coats (or minimal)

PRE-CONDITIONING (for staining):
  Softwoods and ring-porous hardwoods (pine, alder, birch, cherry early):
  Absorb stain unevenly → blotchy appearance
  Solution: pre-conditioner (dilute shellac or commercial pre-conditioner)
  Seals the more porous areas → uniform stain penetration
  Alternative: gel stain (thicker; less penetration; more uniform)
  Or: skip stain; use a colored finishing oil or tinted topcoat

SPECIFIC TO CHERRY:
  Cherry darkens dramatically with UV light exposure
  First year: pale pink → warm honey-amber-red
  Managing: allow to darken before applying UV-blocking finish
  Or: use oil finish (allows UV through; fastest natural patina development)
```

## Detecting Remaining Scratches

```
SCRATCH DETECTION METHODS
===========================

1. RAKING LIGHT (most effective):
   Position a single light source at a very low angle (almost horizontal)
   Scratch lines catch and reflect the light; flat surface doesn't
   Inspect from multiple directions: scratches in one direction
   may be invisible from other angles
   Use: a desk lamp on one side of the workbench; darken the shop

2. NAPHTHA WIPE (test under finish):
   Wipe with mineral spirits or naphtha (or VM&P naphtha)
   This simulates what a finish will look like (temporarily wets surface)
   Any scratches, mill marks, or tearout visible now: will be visible under
   finish (amplified, not hidden)
   Let dry completely (naphtha: 5 min; mineral spirits: 20 min)

3. WETTING WITH WATER (for water-based finishes):
   Same principle as naphtha but uses water
   Also reveals grain direction (will it raise? yes if you see fuzzy surface)

RULE: The last step before finishing should be this naphtha/water check.
      Resand anything that shows up in the check.
      Finishing is not a substitute for surface prep.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What do winding sticks show? | Twist (wind) in a board — sticks appear non-parallel when twisted |
| What is the correct planing sequence? | Traverse → diagonal → with-grain |
| When should you use a card scraper? | Difficult or reversing grain where planing causes tear-out |
| How is the burr on a scraper formed? | Filing flat → stoning → burnishing at 90° → rolling with burnisher at 5–10° tilt |
| What grit do you start with for machine-planed surfaces? | 80 grit (machine marks are coarse) |
| Can you skip grits? | No — each grit removes scratches from previous; skipping doubles work |
| What does grain raising prevent? | Fuzzy surface under first coat of water-based finish |
| What shows remaining scratches before finishing? | Raking light; naphtha wipe (simulates finish appearance) |
| When should you stop sanding? | 180–220 for most finishes; no benefit beyond 320 under typical topcoats |
| What causes blotching in pine/cherry staining? | Uneven porosity; fix with pre-conditioner or gel stain |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Flat" and "smooth" are not the same.** You can have a very smooth surface (fine grit sanding) that is not flat (has gentle dips and ridges). Flatness comes from sequential traversal with long tools (jointer, No.7 jointer plane); smoothness comes from fine cuts or sanding. Get flat first, smooth second.

**Sanding does not fix surface defects — it amplifies them.** Sanding rounds edges, fills and disguises scratches temporarily (until finish is applied), and creates its own scratch pattern. If a surface has mill marks, tear-out, or a high spot, sanding progressively reveals rather than eliminates these issues. Plane flat first, then sand to remove the planer's marks.

**Traverse planing direction matters but is often wrong at first.** Traverse is 90° to grain and seems like it would tear out badly — in fact it doesn't, because the fibers are being severed laterally rather than lifted. The high speed of the jack plane's cambered iron across the grain is exactly the right geometry for rough leveling. With-grain finish passes follow to smooth the ridges left by traverse.

**Card scrapers are being rediscovered and remain unmatched for difficult grain.** A sharp cabinet scraper produces a surface that is arguably better than a 400-grit sanded surface: no grit contamination, no rounded edges, no scratch direction issue. The surface looks and feels different — a more alive quality. The limitation is speed of coverage. For large flat panels, sanding wins on time. For small figured panels, nothing beats the scraper.

**Pre-raise the grain or live with sanding between coats.** With water-based finishes (which many use for durability or VOC reasons), the grain raise on the first coat is not optional — it will happen. The choice is to deal with it proactively (wet, dry, sand before first coat) or reactively (sand lightly between first and second coats, which is also valid and often done).
