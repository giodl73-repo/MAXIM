# Turning and Carving: Lathe Work, Bowl Turning, Relief Carving

## The Big Picture

Turning and carving are the sculptural disciplines of woodworking — the intersection of craft and three-dimensional form. Turning creates radially symmetric objects by rotating stock against a stationary tool; carving removes material from a static blank to create three-dimensional relief or sculpture. Both require understanding grain direction in three dimensions, not just two.

```
TURNING vs. CARVING — COMPARISON
===================================

TURNING                             CARVING
-------                             -------
Stock rotates; tool stationary      Stock stationary; tool moves
Always radially symmetric           Any form possible
Limited by: tool, speed, grain      Limited by: tool, grain, skill
Good for: spindles, bowls, vessels  Good for: decorative panels, sculpture,
          hollow forms, pen blanks         lettering, furniture detail
Tool contact: continuous           Tool contact: selective
Time per piece: fast                Time per piece: hours to weeks
Grain: must suit rotation           Grain: must suit carving direction
```

## Lathe Fundamentals

```
LATHE MODES
============

SPINDLE TURNING (between centers):
  Stock axis = lathe axis = grain direction (long grain)
  Drive end: spur drive (multi-tooth center bites into end grain)
  Support end: live center (bearing; rotates with stock)

  ←─────── grain direction ──────────→
  [SPUR]     stock     [LIVE CENTER]
  headstock                tailstock

  Grain running parallel to rotation axis:
  + Supported fiber cut; cleanest surfaces possible
  + Tool presents to long grain at most contacts
  - Catches can be violent; control critical

FACEPLATE TURNING (bowl/platter):
  Stock grain PERPENDICULAR to rotation axis
  Stock mounted on faceplate (screws) OR in chuck (4-jaw)

  rotation
    ↻
  [grain direction = horizontal = perpendicular to rotation]
     BOWL

  Every rotation: tool meets end grain, then long grain, alternating
  This is why bowl turning requires specific gouge geometry
  (bowl gouge swept-back flute: manages the grain transition)

CHUCK TYPES:
  4-jaw scroll chuck: most common; grips tenon or recess
  Faceplate: flat disc with screw holes; most secure for large blanks
  Spur drive: spindle turning only
  Cole jaws: hold finished bowl bottom for re-chucking; finish bottom
```

## Lathe Speed Selection

```
SPEED SELECTION BY DIAMETER
=============================

Formula: Maximum safe RPM ≈ 9,000 ÷ diameter (inches)

DIAMETER    MAX RPM    TYPICAL RANGE     NOTES
---------   -------    ---------------   -----
Pen (1")    9,000      2,000–4,000       Go fast for fine finishing
Small bowl
  4"        2,250      600–2,000         Check balance before speed up
  6"        1,500      400–1,200
  8"        1,125      300–900
  10"       900        200–700
  12"       750        150–600
Large bowl
  16"       563        100–400
  20"       450        100–300           Start very slow; balance check

PROCESS:
  Always start the lathe at low speed, then increase
  Large or out-of-balance blanks: start at 100–200 RPM
  Vibration at speed: STOP; rebalance or reduce RPM
  Knock-out test: tap blank before starting; hollow sound = crack; don't run

RULE: The minimum speed that cuts cleanly is the right speed.
  High speed is not better if it causes vibration or tears grain.
  Each wood and each blank shape has a sweet spot.
```

## Spindle Turning — Technique

```
SPINDLE TURNING TOOLS AND CUTS
================================

ROUGHING GOUGE:
  Large, heavy; U-shaped flute
  Only for roughing square stock to round
  NEVER use on faceplate work (catches violently)
  Motion: rolled slightly sideways; presented at 45°; traverse the length

SPINDLE GOUGE:
  Shallow flute; finger-nail profile grind
  Primary spindle shaping tool
  Beads, coves, curves; most spindle work
  Technique: ride the bevel (bevel must contact wood); cutting edge at center

SKEW CHISEL:
  Angled cutting edge; most difficult tool; most rewarding
  Planing cut (long-grain surface): presents long point down; bevel rubbing
  V-cut: incises line (long point); defines bead transitions
  Common mistake: presenting too much edge → catch → "spiral of death" catches
  Rule: use the lower 1/3 of the cutting edge only

PARTING TOOL:
  Narrow, scraper-profile; cuts depth stops and parting cuts
  Use to establish diameter at key points before shaping between them
  Use calipers while parting to hit target diameter

BEAD TECHNIQUE:
  Bead = convex rounded form between two V-cuts
  1. Cut V-cuts with long point of skew (or spindle gouge)
  2. Roll gouge from center of bead down each side into the V
  3. Clean up with skew planing cut
  Key: maintain bevel contact throughout rolling motion
```

## Bowl Turning Technique

```
BOWL GOUGE GEOMETRY
====================

STANDARD GRIND:
  Flute: deeper than spindle gouge (more rigid; more material removal)
  End: ground to 60–70° (less steep than spindle; handles harder use)
  Wings: not ground back (simple grind)
  Limitation: catches on some cuts; OK for learning

SWEPT-BACK ("FINGERNAIL") GRIND:
  Flute: deep U-channel
  Wings: ground back (long point at center; wings trail behind)
  Profile: looks like a pointed fingernail

  Why it matters: the swept-back wing shape presents metal leading the
  cut into the transition zones (end grain to long grain boundary)
  instead of catching. This is the grind required for confident bowl turning.

BOWL TURNING OUTSIDE (exterior):
  Tool rest outside bowl blank
  Start cut near rim; work toward base
  Direction: downhill from rim to base (going with grain slope)
  Cut with the flute pointing at roughly 10 o'clock position
  Bevel rubbing throughout

BOWL TURNING INSIDE (interior):
  Tool rest across bowl opening
  Start near rim; work toward center
  Direction: from rim toward center (downhill on interior)
  Watch: presentation of tool must prevent the bevel digging in
         as bowl gets deep; handle drops; tool angled up more
```

## Turning Green Wood

```
GREEN vs. DRY TURNING
======================

GREEN WOOD (fresh-cut, high moisture):
  Advantages: cuts easily; minimal tool pressure; chips well
              can rough-turn and set aside to dry
  Disadvantages: will warp and crack if turned to final thickness while green
  Technique: rough turn to ~10% over-thickness (1" bowl = 1.1" wall thickness)
             remove from lathe; coat end grain with wax (slows drying)
             store in paper bag or shavings (not plastic — traps moisture)
             dry for 6–12 months (1 hour per inch of wall thickness, approx)
             re-chuck dried blank; finish-turn to final dimensions
  Result: some warping from drying → oval bowl → must correct in finish turning

DRY TURNING:
  Advantages: predictable; final dimensions stable; no warping
  Disadvantages: harder on tools; more dust; can crack from heat in deep cuts
  Technique: slower feed rate; keep tool sharp; light cuts in deep areas

GREEN WOOD TURNING FOR VESSELS:
  Natural-edge bowls: live edge retained on rim
  Inclusions (bark, sapwood boundary) = design feature
  Must turn while some green (dry natural-edge tends to crack at rim)
```

## Chip Carving vs. Relief Carving

```
CARVING TYPES
==============

CHIP CARVING:
  Geometry: remove triangular "chips" from surface with knife/chisel
  Pattern: geometric; compass-drawn rosettes; borders
  Tools: chip carving knife (short bent blade) + skew knife
  Technique: two or three cuts per chip; meet at a point at the bottom
  Best wood: basswood, butternut (fine grain; low resistance)
  Result: geometric pattern pressed into flat panel surface

RELIEF CARVING:
  Geometry: figure raised from background; background lowered
  Types:
    Low relief (bas-relief): figure raised 1/4–1/2" from background
    High relief: figure raised to 1/2+ its actual depth
    Deep relief: nearly fully three-dimensional; deep undercutting
  Process: transfer pattern → set-in (define outline) → grounding
           (lower background) → modeling (shape the figure) → detailing
  Tools: #1 (flat chisel), #3–#7 gouges, #11 veiners (deep U), V-tools
  Best wood: basswood, butternut, cherry, walnut

LETTERING / INCISED CARVING:
  V-tool cuts: V-shaped groove; used for lettering, veining
  Incised letter: cut directly into surface; no raised figure
  Serif letters easier than sans-serif (serifs hide join lines)

GOUGE NUMBERING (Swiss/Pfeil/Stubai system):
  Number = sweep (curvature of blade)
    #1: Flat chisel (0° curve)
    #3: Very shallow curve
    #5: Moderate curve (most common general-purpose)
    #7: Steep curve
    #9: Very steep; nearly U-shaped
    #11: Veiner (full U; very deep; thin)
    #12–#13: V-tools (60° and 45°)
  Width: in millimeters (e.g., 5mm #7 = 5mm wide, sweep 7)
```

## Starter Gouge Sets

```
PRACTICAL CARVING TOOL SETS
=============================

MINIMUM START SET (relief carving):
  #3 × 12mm (shallow gouge; most-used general carving)
  #5 × 10mm (medium gouge; modeling curves)
  #7 × 10mm (deep gouge; undercutting; bowl-shaped cuts)
  #11 × 3mm (veiner; fine detail lines)
  #12 × 6mm (V-tool; outlines, texture lines, lettering)
  #1 × 12mm (flat chisel; cleaning backgrounds)

EXPANDED SET adds:
  #3 × 6mm (fine detail at small scale)
  #5 × 20mm (fast ground removal)
  #9 × 6mm (tight concave curves)
  Bent gouges (spoon bent): for deep inside curves

SHARPENING CARVING TOOLS:
  Slip stones: curved stones that match the inside flute curvature
  Outside bevel: flat stone or leather strop on flat surface
  Inside bevel (if present): slip stone curved to match flute
  Strop: critical for carving tools — carving constantly works the edge
         strop every 15–20 minutes during a session (far more than bench work)

POWER CARVING:
  Angle grinder carving discs (Arbortech, King Arthur's tools):
    Replace grinding disc with multi-tooth carving disc
    Works like a circular plane; fast stock removal
    Best for: bowl rough-out from chainsaw blank; large sculpture
    Safety: full face shield, chaps if using chainsaw blank disc
  Die grinder + carbide burs: detail work on hard wood; guitar building
  Foredom flexible shaft machine: standard professional power carving
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Speed and quality are not directly related in turning.** The intuition "faster = cleaner cut" is only partly true. At excessive speed for the diameter, vibration increases and torn grain at high-speed contact points becomes worse. The correct speed is the minimum that produces clean cuts — often lower than new turners expect.

**Bowl gouge ≠ roughing gouge.** Using a roughing gouge for bowl/faceplate work is a serious safety error. The roughing gouge is designed for spindle work with grain parallel to the rotation axis. On faceplate work, the grain is perpendicular, and the roughing gouge's geometry creates catches that can eject the tool or the workpiece violently. Use a bowl gouge for all faceplate work.

**Relief carving tools must be razor-sharp to be controllable.** Dull carving gouges require excessive force, which causes them to skid and "run" with the grain rather than cutting where directed. Sharp tools cut where placed, stop when stopped. The frustrating tendency of carved work to "run away" from the design is almost always a sharpness problem.

**Chip carving and relief carving require different tool angles.** Chip carving uses a very low angle (blade nearly flat) to slice cleanly across grain. Relief carving uses a higher angle to control how deeply the tool penetrates. Working at the wrong angle produces either torn grain or tools that won't remove material efficiently.

**Green wood turning produces oval bowls.** This is expected and exploitable. The bowl is round when turned; it dries oval (moisture releases unevenly); the finish-turning round corrects the oval. The slight irregularity in the dried rough-turned blank is worked away in the finish-turning pass. This is the correct workflow, not a defect.
