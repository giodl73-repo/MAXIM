# Finishing: Oils, Shellac, Lacquer, Varnish, Water-Based

## The Big Picture

Wood finish serves two functions: protection (from moisture, scratches, staining) and appearance (color, sheen, depth). The chemistry of the finish determines how it cures, how it builds, how to repair it, and whether it's compatible with other finishes. Getting the chemistry wrong — applying incompatible products — can prevent curing or cause permanent adhesion failure.

```
FINISH CHEMISTRY MAP
=====================

PENETRATING FINISHES (absorbed into wood; no film on surface)
+----------------------------------------------------------+
| Boiled linseed oil (BLO)  | Oxidative curing; slow; amber|
| Tung oil                  | Oxidative curing; more water |
|                           | resistant than BLO           |
| Danish oil                | BLO or tung + varnish (combo)|
| Waterlox                  | Tung oil + phenolic varnish  |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
→ Feel: "in" the wood, not "on" the wood
→ Protection: low-moderate
→ Repair: easy (reapply)

FILM-BUILDING FINISHES (form a film on wood surface)
+-------+----------------+-----------------------------+
| Shell-| Evaporative     | Shellac (alcohol-dissolved) |
| ac    | (re-dissolves   | NC lacquer (solvent-diss.)  |
|       | in own solvent) | CAB-acrylic lacquer         |
+-------+                 +-----------------------------+
|       | Reactive (cross-| Oil-based varnish           |
|       | linked; does NOT| Polyurethane (varnish type) |
|       | re-dissolve)    | Catalyzed lacquer           |
|       |                 | Epoxy                       |
+-------+-----------------+-----------------------------+
→ Feel: on the surface; visible as film
→ Protection: high (if thick enough)
→ Repair: harder (must blend into existing film)
```

## Penetrating Oils

```
OIL FINISH COMPARISON
======================

BOILED LINSEED OIL (BLO):
  Chemistry: linseed oil + metallic driers (cobalt, manganese)
             cures by oxidative polymerization
  Drying: 24–72 hours per coat (true linseed: months; BLO: days)
  Coats needed: 3–5 for any protection
  Result: low sheen; yellow-amber; "Danish" look
  Protection: poor; penetrates but provides little barrier
  Problems: spontaneous combustion risk (rags soaked in oil must be
            spread flat to dry or submerged in water in sealed container)
  Best for: tool handles, carving, shop items (not furniture)

RAW LINSEED OIL: Never use. Cures in weeks to months. BLO only.

TUNG OIL (pure):
  Chemistry: Tung nut oil; cures by oxidation + polymerization
             more water-resistant than BLO when cured
  Drying: 24–48 hours per coat
  Result: very low sheen (matte); does not darken as much as BLO
  Coats: 3–5
  Important: most products labeled "tung oil" are NOT pure tung oil
             (they're wiping varnish — see below)

DANISH OIL / WATERLOX:
  Really: blended products (oil + varnish + mineral spirits)
  Penetrating AND film-building simultaneously (small film)
  More protection than straight oil
  Brands: Waterlox Original, Tried and True, Rubio Monocoat

WIPING VARNISH (often mislabeled "tung oil" or "danish oil"):
  Varnish diluted with mineral spirits
  Apply thin coats; penetrates + builds thin film
  More protection than pure oil; less than full varnish
  Can be built with many coats
  "Wipe-on poly" (Minwax) = polyurethane varnish diluted for wiping
```

## Shellac — The Universal Sealer

Shellac deserves special attention because it is compatible with nearly everything and solves specific problems no other finish can.

```
SHELLAC PROPERTIES
==================

CHEMISTRY:
  Natural secretion of lac bug (Laccifer lacca) in India and Southeast Asia
  Dissolved in denatured alcohol (ethanol-based shellac)
  Cures by EVAPORATION (solvent flashes off; film remains)
  Rewettable: alcohol re-dissolves it (evaporative finish)

COMPATIBILITY:
  ✓ Goes under anything (universal sealer/primer)
  ✓ Goes over almost anything (sticking to most surfaces)
  ✓ Exception: cannot go over wax (wax is below shellac; shellac won't stick to wax)
  ✓ Can go under lacquer, varnish, oil, water-based finishes
  Use: when in doubt about compatibility, seal with shellac first

DEWAXED vs. WAXED SHELLAC:
  Standard shellac (Zinsser Bullseye, amber): contains natural wax
    Cannot be used under water-based or polyurethane (wax prevents adhesion)
  Dewaxed shellac (Zinsser SealCoat): wax removed
    Can go under anything; safe universal sealer
  NOTE: SealCoat is 100% dewaxed; use this as your universal sealer

CUT RATIOS:
  "Pound cut" = lbs of shellac flakes per gallon of alcohol
  1-pound cut = very thin (sealer, washcoat)
  2-pound cut = standard furniture finish
  3-pound cut = thick; heavy builds; less workable
  Pre-mixed (SealCoat): ~2-lb cut equivalent

USES:
  Seal knots (prevents bleed-through of pine pitch into finish)
  Washcoat under stain (reduces blotching)
  Seal oil finishes before topcoating
  Vintage/reproduction finishes (furniture, musical instruments)
  Food-safe (when fully cured; used on food containers historically)
```

## Nitrocellulose Lacquer vs. CAB-Acrylic

```
LACQUER TYPES
==============

NITROCELLULOSE (NC) LACQUER:
  Chemistry: Nitrocellulose + alkyd resin + solvents
  Evaporative: redissolves in its own solvents (retarder thinner)
  Drying: 15–30 minutes to touch dry; 24 hours full cure
  Application: spray only (too fast to brush; can brush for small work)
  Appearance: warm, amber tone; yellows with age (classic "antique" tone)
  Clarity: moderate; warm cast
  Durability: moderate (not great water resistance)
  VOC: HIGH (flammable; requires spray booth; explosion risk)
  Repair: Easy — solvent weld new coats into old coats
  Used in: guitar finishing, vintage furniture, musical instruments

CAB-ACRYLIC LACQUER:
  Chemistry: Cellulose acetate butyrate + acrylic resin
  Evaporative; same application as NC lacquer
  Appearance: CLEAR — non-yellowing; critical for light-colored woods
              (maple, ash, lighter hardwoods look better under CAB)
  Durability: similar to NC
  Clarity: excellent; no amber cast
  Use when: work will be exposed to light (NC yellows); pale woods; photography
  Compatible: same solvents as NC; can be intermixed

LACQUER vs. WATERBORNE LACQUER:
  Waterborne: low VOC; raises grain; harder to spray (needs HVLP)
              less self-leveling than solvent lacquer; often acceptable finish
              for production shops where VOC compliance is required
```

## Oil-Based Varnish and Polyurethane

```
VARNISH CHEMISTRY
==================

OIL-BASED VARNISH (generic):
  Chemistry: drying oil + resin + mineral spirits (carrier)
  Cures by OXIDATIVE POLYMERIZATION (crosslinking)
  Once cured: CANNOT be redissolved by solvent
  Drying: 8–24 hours between coats; slow
  Result: hard, durable film; good water resistance
  Application: brush; "flowing" brush technique; good self-leveling
  Amber tone: moderate; darkens slightly with age (less than NC)

POLYURETHANE:
  Varnish with polyurethane resins replacing or supplementing alkyd
  Harder, more impact-resistant, better chemical resistance than standard varnish
  Oil-based poly: amber; thick; 3–4 coats typical; 24 hours between
  Water-based poly: clear; low VOC; raises grain; faster (2–4 hours between coats)
  "Wipe-on poly" = oil-based poly thinned 50% with mineral spirits
    Apply thin coats with rag; builds slowly but excellent control; less brush marks

VARNISH TYPES:
+------------------+----------------+---------------+-----------+
| Type             | Sheen Control  | Water Resist  | Flexibility|
+------------------+----------------+---------------+-----------+
| Alkyd varnish    | Any (adjust)   | Good          | Moderate  |
| Spar varnish     | Semi-gloss only| Excellent     | HIGH (UV) |
| Wiping varnish   | Any            | Good          | Moderate  |
| Oil-based poly   | Any            | Excellent     | Moderate  |
| Water-based poly | Any            | Very good     | Lower     |
+------------------+----------------+---------------+-----------+

SPAR VARNISH: Marine grade; very flexible (UV stabilizers; boat movement);
              never use on furniture (too soft; always feels tacky in heat)
```

## Water-Based Finishes

```
WATER-BASED FINISH PROPERTIES
================================

CHEMISTRY:
  Waterborne alkyd, polyurethane, or acrylic emulsion
  Polymer particles suspended in water
  Cures: water evaporates; polymer particles coalesce into film
  Additional: some crosslink on cure (better durability)

ADVANTAGES:
  Low VOC (less odor; compliant with strict VOC rules)
  Non-yellowing (stays crystal clear)
  Fast dry (1–2 hours between coats; stack coats same day)
  Easy cleanup (water + soap)
  Better for pale woods (ash, maple, birch, holly)

DISADVANTAGES:
  Raises grain (pre-raise or sand between coats)
  Less self-leveling (brush marks more visible; HVLP spray preferred)
  Less forgiving in cold / humid conditions
  Shorter pot life after opening
  Less depth/warmth than oil-based (looks more "plastic")

WATER-BASED TOPCOAT RECOMMENDATIONS:
  General furniture: Target Coatings EM6000 or EM2000
  Production shops: Sherwin-Williams Kemvar (catalyzed)
  DIY available: Varathane Crystal Clear (water-based poly; widely available)
  Floors: Bona Traffic HD (very hard; semi-commercial)

COMPATIBILITY:
  Can go over: shellac (dewaxed), water-based stain, waterborne primer
  Cannot go over: wax, oil-based stain that has not fully cured,
                  silicone (anything)
  Rule: test adhesion if uncertain (apply small amount; try to lift after cure)
```

## Finishing Schedule — Decision Tree

```
FINISHING DECISION TREE
=========================

START: What is the piece for?

Is it furniture?
  ├── Fine furniture for display (not heavy use)?
  │     → Oil finish (Danish/Waterlox) or shellac
  │     → Excellent appearance; easy to repair
  │
  ├── Tabletop / dining / heavy use?
  │     → Oil-based polyurethane (3 coats minimum)
  │     → Or catalyzed finish (professional shops)
  │
  ├── Kitchen cabinets?
  │     → Pre-catalyzed lacquer or water-based poly
  │     → Hardness + chemical resistance needed
  │
  └── Painted furniture?
        → Shellac prime; latex or oil-based paint topcoat

Is wood pale/light-colored?
  → Water-based or CAB-acrylic lacquer (no yellowing)
  → Avoid NC lacquer and oil-based poly (yellowing)

Is this outdoor furniture?
  → Teak oil / outdoor-rated oil (not interior finishes)
  → Spar urethane for high protection
  → Plan for annual recoating (all exterior finishes degrade)

Is this a floor?
  → Water-based floor finish (Bona, etc.): low VOC, hard, fast
  → Or oil-based polyurethane: extremely hard; slower; strong smell

Is food contact a concern?
  → Walnut oil or pure tung oil (both cure to inert state)
  → Shellac (historically food-safe; still generally considered safe)
  → Mineral oil (never cures; must be reapplied; safe; butcher blocks)
  → Avoid: linseed oil-based finishes (metallic driers)
```

## Sheen Levels

```
SHEEN COMPARISON
=================

Sheen level     Approximate gloss angle       Appearance
-------------   ----------------------        ----------
Flat / Matte    <10 units (60°)               No reflections; close-up reveals texture
Satin           25–35 units                   Slight sheen; soft appearance; popular
Semi-gloss      35–55 units                   Noticeable sheen; practical and attractive
Gloss           70+ units                     Mirror-like; shows every surface defect
High gloss      90+ units                     Piano finish; requires flattening + polishing

ACHIEVING SHEEN:
  Most finishes sold at multiple sheen levels (flatting agents added to dull)
  Flatting paste (fumed silica): reduces sheen; can mix into any finish
  Rubbing out: sand dried finish with 400–2000 grit + steel wool + paste wax
               → Control final sheen exactly by hand rubbing
  High-gloss piano finish: multiple coats; flatten with 400 grit; rub with
                           1000, 1500, 2000 grit wet/dry; polish with automotive
                           polishing compound; wax

BURNISHING SHELLAC:
  After cure: rub with fine steel wool (0000)
  Then: paste wax (Renaissance, Minwax)
  Result: beautiful low luster; easy to repair; traditional appearance
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What finish is the universal sealer / compatible with everything? | Dewaxed shellac (Zinsser SealCoat) |
| Can waxed shellac go under polyurethane? | No — wax prevents adhesion; use dewaxed only |
| What causes NC lacquer to yellow? | Nitrocellulose oxidation with time and light; use CAB-acrylic for non-yellowing |
| Which finish is best for pale/light woods? | CAB-acrylic lacquer or water-based (non-yellowing) |
| What is wipe-on poly? | Oil-based polyurethane thinned ~50% with mineral spirits; applied by rag |
| Does oil-based varnish re-dissolve in solvent once cured? | No — oxidative crosslinking; cannot be redissolved (unlike shellac/lacquer) |
| What finish for butcher block / food contact? | Mineral oil (never cures, must re-apply) or pure tung/walnut oil |
| What is the main disadvantage of water-based finish? | Raises grain; less self-leveling; looks less warm/deep than oil-based |
| Can you apply lacquer over oil without sealing? | Not reliably — oil must be fully cured AND sealed (with dewaxed shellac) first |
| How do you achieve a satin finish from gloss? | Sand with 400+ grit wet/dry or rub with 0000 steel wool + paste wax |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Tung oil" products are almost always not tung oil.** Minwax Tung Oil Finish, Formby's Tung Oil Finish, and most similarly named products contain little to no actual tung oil. They are wiping varnishes — a useful product, but not pure tung oil. If you want pure tung oil, buy from a specialty supplier and verify it contains only tung oil.

**Danish oil is not a specific product.** It's a category — a blend of oil and varnish in varying proportions. Different brands have wildly different formulations. The behavior of "danish oil" varies substantially between brands. Waterlox and similar products are more predictable.

**Finish does not add color to wood — it reveals what's there.** An oil finish on walnut makes the walnut look more like walnut; it enriches the existing color. It does not change the color per se, but the wetted appearance may be dramatically different from the dry appearance. Water or naphtha wiped on dry wood is the best preview of what any finish will look like.

**Compatibility failures can be invisible until months later.** Applying oil-based stain + water-based polyurethane when the oil hasn't fully cured: adhesion failure shows up weeks or months after the project is complete, when the finish starts to flake. The rule: if unsure, test. Apply finish to a scrap of the same wood treated the same way; check for adhesion after a week.

**Shellac has a shelf life.** Pre-mixed shellac (in cans) has a shelf life of about 3 years from manufacture, 1 year after opening. Old shellac may not cure properly (stays sticky). Check the manufacture date on the can. Dry shellac flakes mixed fresh have no shelf life issue. If canned shellac doesn't seem to be curing, it's likely old product.
