# Wood Selection: Species, Grain, Figure, Defects, Moisture Content

## The Big Picture

Wood selection is the most consequential decision in a woodworking project. The wrong species or moisture content can doom a piece regardless of how well it's built. Get this right first.

```
WOOD SELECTION DECISION TREE
==============================

START: What is this piece for?
    |
    +---> HIGH WEAR SURFACE (tabletop, flooring, cutting board)?
    |         → Hard, dense species: maple, walnut, oak, cherry
    |         → Check Janka hardness: >1,000 lbf for tabletops
    |
    +---> BENT OR CURVED PARTS (chair backs, Windsor spindles)?
    |         → High shock resistance: white oak, ash, hickory
    |         → Steam bending: ash, oak, walnut work well
    |
    +---> VISIBLE JOINERY (exposed dovetails)?
    |         → Crisp edge retention: maple, cherry, walnut
    |         → Avoid: soft pines (crush under mallet)
    |
    +---> PAINTED SURFACE?
    |         → Poplar, soft maple, MDF (painting hides grain)
    |         → No need to pay for figured wood
    |
    +---> OUTDOOR USE?
    |         → Teak, white oak, cedar, black locust
    |         → Avoid: cherry, walnut, most hardwoods (rot prone)
    |
    +---> TURNING?
            → Dense, stable when green: apple, box, hornbeam
            → Good dry: maple, cherry, walnut
            → Large bowls: stabilized green wood works well
```

## Sawn Orientation

The orientation of the log cut relative to the annual rings determines movement, figure, and stability:

```
THREE SAWN ORIENTATIONS
=========================

LOG CROSS-SECTION:
         |tangential
    _____|_____
   /     |     \
  |  radial     |
  |      +      |  ← pith
  |             |
   \___________/

FLATSAWN (plain-sawn):
  Rings roughly parallel to board face
  Most common; most yield from log; least waste
         ~~~~~~~  ← face; "cathedral" grain pattern
  Movement: TANGENTIAL direction (large)
  Typical: 7–12% movement across width with seasonal humidity change
         ═══════  ← edge

QUARTERSAWN:
  Rings roughly perpendicular to board face
  Annual rings visible as lines on face
  Requires more log passes; more waste; more expensive
  Movement: RADIAL direction (smaller)
  Typical: 3–5% movement
  In oak: shows medullary ray figure (striking silver/ray fleck pattern)
  Best for: tabletops, any wide panel requiring stability

RIFTSAWN:
  Rings at ~30–60° to face
  Between flat and quarter; even, straight-line grain with no fleck
  Most waste; most expensive per board foot
  Used for: legs (four matching sides with straight grain all around)
            flooring (consistent wear surface)
```

## Figure Types

Figure = optical effect beyond basic grain pattern. Desirable for decorative applications; adds significant cost.

```
FIGURE DESCRIPTIONS
====================

BIRD'S EYE:
  Small, circular swirls; looks like bird's eye dots
  Cause: localized grain distortions around deformed buds
  Species: primarily hard maple; occasionally birch, ash
  Common use: veneers, furniture panels, turnings
  Grading: light / medium / heavy bird's eye

CURLY (tiger, fiddleback):
  Alternating light/dark bands across grain; appears to shimmer
  Cause: undulating grain fibers (wavy grain)
  Species: maple, cherry, walnut, birch, elm
  Use: instrument faces (violins → "fiddleback"), furniture panels
  Important: curly figure creates reversing grain → tear-out risk when planing
  Solution: plane with very fine cut; use a card scraper to finish

QUILTED:
  Similar to curly but with a 3D "puffed" or cushion effect
  Species: maple, cherry (less common)
  High value; difficult to predict from outside log

BURL:
  Gnarled, distorted growth from dormant bud cluster
  Cause: stress, injury, fungi, or genetic abnormality
  Species: walnut, maplewood, redwood, elm
  Pattern: wild, irregular, unpredictable — highly decorative
  Challenge: often contains voids, bark inclusions; must be stabilized
  Uses: veneers, bowls, knife handles, decorative panels

SPALTED:
  Dark zone lines from fungal colonization in partially decayed wood
  Dramatic graphic lines (zone lines = fungal territory markers)
  Species: maple, birch, beech — any vulnerable species
  Risk: may be structurally weak; stabilizing resin helps
  Best for: bowls, decorative pieces where structural demands are low

MEDULLARY RAYS (ray fleck):
  Horizontal lines / flashes in quartersawn oak / sycamore / beech
  Not actually "figure" in the traditional sense — it's structural anatomy
  Some of the most prized aesthetic in furniture-making
  Oak with prominent ray fleck: "English brown oak", "rifled oak"
```

## Defects — What to Avoid or Work Around

```
COMMON DEFECTS
===============

KNOTS:
  Dead knot (black knot): loose; will fall out; creates void
  Live knot (encased knot): tight; wood flows around it; often usable
  Effect on tool use: knots cause grain reversals; hard spots
  Rule: avoid knots in structural parts; use/accept in decorative

CHECKS (surface cracks):
  Radial cracks from drying stress (end checks = at log ends)
  Surface checks: from too-rapid drying or re-drying after wetting
  Use: if shallow, surface-prepare away; if deep, cut around them

WARP TYPES:
  +----------------+------------------+------------------+
  | BOW            | CUP              | TWIST            |
  | End-to-end     | Across width     | Propeller shape  |
  | curve along    | (tangential      | (corners at      |
  | face           | shrinkage in     | diff. heights)   |
  |                | flat-sawn)       |                  |
  +----------------+------------------+------------------+
  | CROOK          | KINK             |                  |
  | Side-to-side   | Abrupt crook;    |                  |
  | bow (edge)     | sharp bend       |                  |
  +----------------+------------------+------------------+

REACTION WOOD:
  Compression wood (softwoods): formed on lower side of leaning trunk
    Dense, hard, unpredictable movement; avoid
  Tension wood (hardwoods): formed on upper side of leaning trunk
    Woolly surface, difficult to finish; causes severe warp on drying

SAPWOOD vs. HEARTWOOD:
  Heartwood: darker, denser, more decay-resistant (natural preservatives)
  Sapwood: lighter, less durable, susceptible to stain and rot
  Most furniture applications: heartwood preferred
  Exception: maple (sapwood IS the prized "white maple"; heartwood darker)
```

## Moisture Content and EMC

```
MOISTURE CONTENT (MC) SYSTEM
==============================

DEFINITION: MC = (wet weight - dry weight) / dry weight × 100%

KEY BENCHMARKS:
  MC %    State                         Notes
  -----   --------------------------    --------------------------------
  100%+   Green (freshly cut)           Unstable; shrinks dramatically
  28–30%  Fiber Saturation Point (FSP)  Below here: dimensions change
  20–25%  Air-dried (partially)         Still needs further drying
  15–18%  Partially dried               Still moves too much indoors
  12–15%  "Dry lumber" (rough use)      OK for exterior, painting
  6–9%    Kiln-dried (furniture grade)  Target for fine indoor furniture
  6–8%    Fully dried / kiln-dried      Target for cabinets / furniture
  <6%     Over-dried                    May re-absorb humidity; swell

EQUILIBRIUM MC (EMC) AT COMMON CONDITIONS:
  30% RH (winter heating)  → ~7% EMC
  50% RH (spring/fall)     → ~9% EMC
  65% RH (summer)          → ~12% EMC
  85% RH (damp basement)   → ~16% EMC

RULE: Buy lumber that is at or near the EMC of its final environment.
  Furniture for heated interior: want wood at ~6–8% MC
  Exterior furniture: typically air-dried at 12–15%; accepts movement

MEASURING MC:
  Pin moisture meter: electrodes into wood; measures resistance
    Accurate to ±1–2%; cheap ($20–$100)
  Pinless meter: electromagnetic scanning; less accurate; no holes
  Oven-dry method: most accurate; destructive; not practical for stock
```

## Buying Lumber

```
LUMBER GRADING AND PURCHASING
================================

HARDWOOD GRADING (NHLA system):
  FAS (Firsts and Seconds): ≥83.3% clear face; best grade; most expensive
  Select and Better (Sel & Btr): one face FAS, other #1 Common
  #1 Common: ≥66.7% clear; serviceable; most economical
  #2 Common: ≥50% clear; suitable for shorter pieces, painting

SOFTWOOD GRADING (different system):
  Select (A, B, C): appearance grades; mostly clear
  #1, #2, #3 Common: increasing knot/defect count
  Construction grade: structural; not appearance

BOARD FEET (BF): US lumber unit
  1 BF = 1 foot × 1 foot × 1 inch = 144 cubic inches
  Formula: (thickness" × width" × length") / 144
  Or: (thickness" × width" × length in feet) / 12

SOURCES:
  Big-box stores (Home Depot, Lowe's): softwood only; construction grade
  Hardwood lumber dealers: kiln-dried, graded hardwoods
  Sawyers / urban lumber: often interesting local species; may be green
  Online lumber (Bell Forest, Woodfinders): wide selection; higher cost + shipping

TIP: Buy 20–30% extra (waste from defect cutoff, jointing, planing)
     Check MC before purchase if building immediately
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Kiln-drying doesn't "lock in" moisture content permanently.** Kiln-dried lumber will re-absorb or release moisture based on ambient humidity. If you buy 8% MC lumber and store it in a 70% RH garage, it will climb toward 13% MC. Allow newly acquired lumber to acclimate in the shop for 2–4 weeks before working it.

**Figure and stability often trade off.** The same cellular abnormalities that create beautiful curly or bird's eye figure also create interlocked or reversing grain that tears out under a hand plane. Figured wood often requires scrapers, specialized cutting angles, or sanding instead of planing.

**Heartwood/sapwood distinction matters for walnut and cherry.** Walnut sapwood is creamy-white; heartwood is chocolate-brown. Cherry sapwood is pale; heartwood is reddish-brown that darkens with light exposure. For a consistent piece, you want all heartwood (or a deliberate design choice to use both).

**Janka hardness doesn't predict workability.** Mahogany has a lower Janka rating than oak but is far easier to work because it has straight, predictable grain. Teak has moderate hardness but dulls tools rapidly due to high silica content. Grain character and silica content matter as much as hardness.

**Board feet math trips up beginners.** Lumber dealers sell by the board foot. A 1" × 6" × 8' board is 4 BF. A 2" thick board at the same face dimensions is 8 BF. Remember that rough lumber (sold by nominal dimensions) is thicker and wider than the stated dimensions — a "4/4" (four-quarter, nominally 1") board is actually ~1-1/8" thick as rough-sawn.
