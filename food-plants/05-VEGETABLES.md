# Vegetables — Brassica, Solanaceae, Allium, and the Domestication of Diversity

## The Big Picture

The category "vegetable" is culinary, not botanical — it includes leaves, stems, roots, flowers, seeds, and fruits from dozens of unrelated plant families. But within this diversity, a few genera demonstrate extraordinary breadth: *Brassica oleracea* produced seven distinct crops from a single wild ancestor through selective breeding. The story of vegetables is a master class in how artificial selection can sculpt plant form.

```
WHAT WE CALL "VEGETABLE" — BOTANICAL DIVERSITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Part selected    Example                     Family
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Leaf             Spinach, lettuce, chard     Amaranthaceae, Asteraceae
Stem             Asparagus, kohlrabi         Liliaceae, Brassicaceae
Root             Carrot, parsnip, beet       Apiaceae, Amaranthaceae
Tuber            Potato, yam                 Solanaceae, Dioscoreaceae
Flower bud       Broccoli, cauliflower       Brassicaceae
Fruit (culinary) Tomato, pepper, cucumber    Solanaceae, Cucurbitaceae
Seed             Peas, beans                 Fabaceae
Bulb             Onion, garlic               Amaryllidaceae

There is no unified botanical definition of "vegetable."
It is purely a culinary/cultural category.
```

## Brassica oleracea: One Ancestor, Seven Crops

This is the most remarkable case of artificial selection in plants. *Brassica oleracea* is a wild coastal plant from the Atlantic European coastline — a modest, mustard-family plant. Every following crop was created by selecting different plant parts for development.

```
BRASSICA OLERACEA WILD ANCESTOR
Mediterranean/Atlantic coast plant
Unremarkable mustard-family herb
Biennial: year 1 = vegetative; year 2 = flowers/seeds

                         B. oleracea (wild)
                               │
          ┌────────────────────┼────────────────────┐
          │                    │                    │
   SELECT terminal      SELECT axillary      SELECT stem
   leaf bud             buds                (internode)
          │                    │                    │
     CABBAGE             BRUSSELS               KOHLRABI
   (tight head of        SPROUTS               (swollen stem,
    modified             (small heads           not root)
    leaves)              on stem)
          │
   Further modification:
     Leaf surface:    → KALE (open leaves, no head)
     Leaf curliness:  → COLLARD GREENS (flat kale)
     Flower clusters: → BROCCOLI (immature flower heads)
     Flower clusters
     (abnormal):      → CAULIFLOWER (dense white, albino)
     Cauliflower
     (variant):       → ROMANESCO (fractal geometry —
                                   Fibonacci spiral florets)
```

All are the same species (*Brassica oleracea*) and are interfertile — they can cross-pollinate. This is why open-pollinated brassicas can produce bizarre hybrids in the garden.

### Nutritional Properties

Brassicas are rich in glucosinolates — sulfur-containing compounds that give them their characteristic pungency. When cells are disrupted:

```
GLUCOSINOLATE HYDROLYSIS
Intact cell: glucosinolate (stored in vacuole) + myrosinase (stored in cell)
Cell damaged (cutting, chewing):
  glucosinolate + myrosinase + water → isothiocyanates + glucose + sulfate

Products include:
  Sulforaphane (broccoli): induces phase 2 detoxification enzymes,
    associated with cancer-risk reduction in epidemiological studies
  Allyl isothiocyanate: mustard oil pungency
  Erucin: similar structure to sulforaphane

KEY: myrosinase is denatured by heat → cooked broccoli has less
     sulforaphane unless gut bacteria compensate (they partially do)
     Raw broccoli > cooked broccoli for sulforaphane activity
```

## Solanaceae: The Nightshade Family

The Solanaceae are a dramatic example of parallel domestication across the Americas. Four unrelated crops from one family, each domesticated independently in different regions.

### The Alkaloid Issue

All Solanaceae contain some level of alkaloid compounds as herbivore deterrents.

```
SOLANACEAE ALKALOIDS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Solanine (potato): concentrated in green skin, sprouts, unripe parts
  Toxic threshold: ~2-5 mg/kg body weight
  Green potato: up to 200 mg/100g (dangerous)
  Normal potato flesh: <10 mg/100g (safe)
  Mechanism: cholinesterase inhibitor (same as nerve agents, but far milder)

Tomatine (tomato): present in unripe tomatoes + leaves
  Ripe tomato: very low levels, effectively non-toxic
  Green tomato: higher but still low; culinary use is traditional

Capsaicin (pepper): technically not a "solanine" alkaloid but alkaloid-related
  Does not harm most mammals at food doses
  See spices/06 for full treatment

HISTORICAL FEAR: When tomatoes/potatoes arrived in Europe (~1500s),
  they were correctly identified as nightshade relatives
  (Deadly nightshade = Atropa belladonna) → refused for 100+ years
  The fear was not irrational — the plant family includes true toxins
```

### Tomato: From Wild Cherry to Modern Cultivar

```
TOMATO DOMESTICATION AND DEVELOPMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Wild ancestor: S. pimpinellifolium — 1cm red cherries, Peru/Ecuador
~3,500 BP: Mexican/Central American selection → larger fruit
1519: Hernán Cortés sees tomatoes in Aztec markets → sent to Spain
~1520-1550: Italy introduces "pomo d'oro" (golden apple) — likely yellow var.
~1550-1600: Grown in Italy, Spain; used medicinally; eaten by poor
~1700-1800: Gradual uptake in Southern Italy (Neapolitan poor eat it)
1820: Robert Johnson eats tomato on Salem NJ courthouse steps
       to prove it's not poisonous (story disputed)
1860s: Transcontinental US = tomato mainstream
1893: US Supreme Court rules tomato is vegetable for tariff purposes
       (Nix v. Hedden: "tomato" = vegetable in commerce, fruit in botany)
1994: Flavr Savr tomato — first GM food approved for sale
       (antisense polygalacturonase → slower softening)
       Commercial failure (didn't taste better + GMO stigma)
```

### The Thiosulfinate Chemistry

```
ALLIUM PUNGENCY MECHANISM
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INTACT CELL:
  Precursor: S-allylcysteine sulfoxide (ACSO) stored in cytoplasm
  Enzyme: alliinase stored in vacuole — PHYSICALLY SEPARATED

CELL DAMAGED (cutting, crushing):
  ACSO + alliinase → alliin → allicin + other thiosulfinates

ALLICIN (diallyl thiosulfinate):
  The principal fresh garlic compound
  Highly unstable: breaks down within hours to:
    Diallyl disulfide (main cooked garlic compound)
    Diallyl sulfide, ajoene (long-term fermentation products)
  Not present in cooked garlic (heat destroys alliinase)

ONION TEARING:
  Propanethial S-oxide gas released by LF-synthase enzyme
  Diffuses to eye mucous membranes → reacts with water → sulfenic acid
  Sulfenic acid → propanethial S-oxide → irritates
  Prevent: chill onion (slower enzyme), cut under water, wear goggles

PRACTICAL:
  Raw garlic: allicin (sharp, antibacterial)
  Cooked garlic: diallyl disulfide + other compounds (sweet, mellow)
  Aged black garlic: allicin converted to S-allylcysteine (antioxidant)
  Garlic powder: no allicin (heating during drying destroys alliinase)
```

## Cucurbitaceae: Gourd Family

Cucumbers, squash, pumpkins, zucchini, melons, watermelons — all cucurbits. Some of the earliest domesticated plants.

```
KEY CUCURBITS AND ORIGINS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cucumis sativus (cucumber): India, ~3,000 BP
Cucumis melo (melon, cantaloupe, honeydew): Africa/India, ~4,000 BP
Citrullus lanatus (watermelon): Sub-Saharan Africa, ~5,000 BP
  Wild ancestor: bitter; cultivated for water in desert
Cucurbita pepo (pumpkin, zucchini, acorn squash): Mexico, ~10,000 BP
  One of the "Three Sisters" with maize and beans
Cucurbita maxima (butternut squash, Hubbard): S. America, ~4,000 BP
Cucurbita moschata (butternut): Mexico/Central America
Lagenaria siceraria (bottle gourd): ANOMALY
  African origin, but found in Asia and Americas pre-Columbian
  Floated across oceans? Carried by early humans? Mystery solved?
  DNA evidence (2014): bottle gourd reached Americas ~10,000 BP —
  from Africa via Atlantic (not Asia via Pacific); first plant known
  to cross Atlantic independently before humans
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the wild ancestor of kale, broccoli, cabbage, and cauliflower? | *Brassica oleracea* — wild coastal plant |
| What makes broccoli good for cancer prevention? | Sulforaphane (from glucosinolate hydrolysis) — enzyme myrosinase needed |
| Does cooked broccoli have as much sulforaphane as raw? | No — heat deactivates myrosinase; gut bacteria partially compensate |
| Why did Europeans fear tomatoes for 100 years? | Correctly identified as nightshade family; Solanaceae contains real toxins (belladonna) |
| What makes garlic pungent? | Allicin (from alliinase enzyme + ACSO precursor on cell damage) |
| Why does cooking garlic make it milder? | Heat destroys alliinase → allicin doesn't form; existing compounds mellow |
| What makes onions cause tears? | Propanethial S-oxide gas → eye mucous membrane → sulfenic acid |

## Common Confusion Points

**Kohlrabi is a stem, not a root.** The swollen globe of kohlrabi is the hypocotyl (stem just above the root). Confusingly grows above ground. Celery is also a stem (petiole, technically).

**Broccoli and cauliflower are the same species.** Brassica oleracea var. italica (broccoli) and var. botrytis (cauliflower). A cauliflower is a broccoli mutant in which the flower-forming process goes awry, producing dense undifferentiated tissue (the "curd").

**Solanine in potato is real but not a daily risk.** A 60kg person would need to eat ~500g of green potato to approach toxic doses. Normal storage conditions (cool, dark) prevent greening. Don't eat obviously green potatoes; don't worry about normal ones.

**"Garlic is good for X" depends heavily on preparation.** Raw garlic (allicin) ≠ cooked garlic (diallyl sulfides) ≠ aged black garlic (S-allylcysteine) ≠ garlic powder (no allicin). The health claims vary by compound; most research uses specific preparations.
