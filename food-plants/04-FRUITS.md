# Fruits — Apple, Citrus, Banana, and the Geometry of Seed Dispersal

## The Big Picture

Botanically, a fruit is any seed-bearing structure derived from the ovary of a flowering plant. This includes tomatoes, cucumbers, squash, and peppers — culinary "vegetables" that are botanically fruits. The evolutionary purpose is seed dispersal: fruits attract animals that consume the flesh and disperse seeds.

```
MAJOR FRUIT CROPS — LANDSCAPE BY ORIGIN, PROPAGATION, AND TYPE

FAMILY         KEY FRUITS       ORIGIN          PROPAGATION      FRUIT TYPE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rosaceae       Apple (Malus)    Kazakhstan       Grafting/clonal  Pome (false fruit)
               Pear (Pyrus)     China/Caucasus   Grafting         Pome
               Peach (Prunus)   China            Budding          Drupe
               Cherry (Prunus)  Europe/W Asia    Grafting         Drupe
               Strawberry       Hybrid (N+S Am.) Runners          Aggregate
               (×Fragaria)

Rutaceae       Orange           Hybrid           Grafting         Hesperidium (berry)
               (Citrus sinensis) (pomelo×mandarin) + seed          (leathery rind)
               Lemon            Hybrid           Grafting         Hesperidium
               (C. limon)       (citron×sour orange)
               Grapefruit       Hybrid           Grafting         Hesperidium
               (C. paradisi)    (pomelo×sweet orange, ~1750 AD)
               Kumquat          China            Grafting         Hesperidium

Musaceae       Banana           SE Asia          Vegetative       Berry (true!)
               (Musa)           (New Guinea)     (corms, offsets)  (no stone; seedless)
               → Cavendish: triploid (sterile, no seeds, clonal worldwide monoculture)

Vitaceae       Grape (Vitis)    Caucasus/Near E  Cuttings         Berry
               vinifera         (~8,000 BP)      + grafting

Bromeliaceae   Pineapple        S America        Crowns/ratoons   Multiple fruit
               (Ananas comosus) (Paraguay/Brazil)  suckers         (syncarp)

Caricaceae     Papaya           Mesoamerica      Seed             Berry
               (Carica papaya)                    (fast-growing)

KEY PROPAGATION PATTERNS:
  Grafting/budding: prevents genetic variation in seeds; maintains cultivar;
    standard for tree fruits (apple scions on rootstock = clonal flavor consistency)
  Vegetative (runners, corms, offsets): inherently clonal; fast; banana model
  Seed: genetic recombination; used for annual crops, papaya; loses cultivar fidelity

FRUIT TYPES — BOTANICAL CLASSIFICATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SIMPLE FRUITS (from single flower, single ovary)
  Fleshy:
    Berry (true): entire ovary wall fleshy (tomato, grape, banana, kiwi, citrus)
    Drupe: inner layer (endocarp) stony pit (cherry, peach, plum, olive, almond)
    Pome: receptacle tissue forms most of fruit (apple, pear, quince)
  Dry:
    Achene: dry, single-seeded (sunflower "seed" = achene)
    Nut: hard, indehiscent (acorn, chestnut)
    Legume: dehiscent, 2-valved (bean pod)
    Samara: winged (maple, ash)

AGGREGATE FRUITS (multiple ovaries, one flower)
  Strawberry, raspberry, blackberry

MULTIPLE FRUITS (multiple flowers fused)
  Pineapple, mulberry, fig (fig = syconium — inverted flower head)

ACCESSORY FRUITS (receptacle tissue contributes)
  Apple, pear: "hypanthium" (receptacle) forms most of what we eat
  Strawberry: receptacle = red part; true fruits = achenes (seeds on surface)
```

## Apple: From Kazakhstan to the World

### Origin and Wild Ancestor

Malus sieversii, growing wild in the mountains of Kazakhstan (Tian Shan range), is the primary ancestor of all cultivated apple varieties. This was established genetically by 1999 (Barrie Juniper, Oxford) — a clean correction of the earlier hypothesis that apples domesticated from multiple wild species.

```
APPLE DOMESTICATION PATH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Malus sieversii: Tian Shan mountains, Kazakhstan
  Wild forest tree, fruit variable (4cm-10cm, sweet to astringent)
  Grows along ancient Silk Road trade routes
       │
  Human selection + seed dispersal along trade routes (horses, traders)
       │
  Hybridization with European wild species (Malus sylvestris) in transit
       │
  Roman-era cultivation: documented in Roman agricultural writing
       │
  Medieval European varieties: primarily CIDER apples (astringent)
       │
  18th-19th century: eating apple selection (sweet, soft flesh)
       │
  Modern period: ~7,500 named cultivars; most commercial production
  from ~20 cultivars
```

### Grafting: The Technology That Preserves Identity

An apple grown from seed is a genetic lottery — offspring of highly heterozygous parents produce wildly variable fruit. The apple's extreme heterozygosity means sexual reproduction cannot reliably reproduce traits.

```
APPLE GRAFTING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Scion (desired variety)            Rootstock (vigor, disease resistance,
  Cutting from cultivar              size control)
       │                                   │
       └───────────┬───────────────────────┘
                   ▼
            UNION (callus forms)
            Cambium layers align and fuse
            Result: genetically identical top
                    with rootstock characteristics

Every 'Honeycrisp' apple in the world is genetically identical —
all clones propagated by grafting. This is vegetative reproduction
of a specific genotype.

Johnny Appleseed (John Chapman, 1774-1845):
  Planted apple seedlings across Ohio/Indiana/Illinois
  SEEDS → genetic lottery → mostly astringent cider apples
  Did NOT propagate named eating varieties by grafting
  His apples were primarily for fermented hard cider, not eating
  The "wholesome American fruit" mythology is a 20th-century confection
```

### The Monoculture Parallel: Honeycrisp and the Market

The modern apple industry is a monoculture problem waiting to happen. Commercial orchards grow a handful of cultivars. Honeycrisp (developed by University of Minnesota, released 1991) went from specialty to ~15% of US market in 30 years.

## Citrus: All Hybrids, No Originals

Citrus is one of the most surprising botanical stories: essentially no commercially cultivated citrus is a "real" species. Almost everything is a hybrid.

```
CITRUS PRIMARY SPECIES (the actual species)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Pomelo (Citrus maxima): grapefruit-sized ancestor, SE Asia, large, mild
  Mandarin (Citrus reticulata): small, loose-skinned, China
  Citron (Citrus medica): thick-skinned, very little flesh, Middle East
  Papeda (Citrus micrantha + others): wild Asian species

COMMON "CITRUS SPECIES" ARE ALL HYBRIDS:
  Sweet orange = pomelo × mandarin
  Lemon = citron × (sour orange? = pomelo × mandarin × citron)
  Grapefruit = pomelo × sweet orange (18th century, Barbados)
  Clementine = sweet orange × mandarin
  Lime (Persian) = citron × papeda hybrid
  Blood orange = sweet orange mutant

There is no natural "orange" as a pure species.
Every citrus fruit humans eat is an ancient or modern hybrid.
```

**Citrus domestication:** Began in SE Asia and southern China ~4,000 BP. Spread via Arab traders to Mediterranean, Persian culture → lemon and sour orange to Mediterranean by ~900 CE, sweet orange later (~1500 CE from Portuguese trade routes).

### Vitamin C and the Scurvy Connection

```
SCURVY AND CITRUS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Scurvy: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) deficiency
  Symptoms: collagen breakdown → bleeding gums, wound failure, death
  Timeline: deficiency symptoms in ~4-6 weeks without vitamin C
  Naval impact: estimated to have killed MORE sailors than all sea
               battles combined from 1500-1800

1747: James Lind (Royal Navy surgeon): controlled trial
  12 scurvy patients, 6 treatment groups
  Citrus (oranges + lemons) group: cured within 6 days
  Others: no improvement
  First recorded controlled clinical trial

1795: Royal Navy mandates lime juice (British "limeys")
  → Scurvy eliminated from Royal Navy

But mechanism unknown until 1932 (Albert Szent-Györgyi isolates vitamin C)
The empirical cure preceded the mechanistic understanding by 185 years.
```

## Banana: Cavendish and the Coming Collapse

### Domestication and Genetics

```
BANANA DOMESTICATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Wild ancestors: Musa acuminata (A genome), Musa balbisiana (B genome)
  Wild bananas: full of seeds, hard to eat
  SE Asia/New Guinea: ~8,000 BP domestication begins

Multiple domestication events → natural hybridization + selection:
  AA diploid: soft, edible, some seeds
  AAB triploid: better yield, fewer seeds (Plantain/Silk types)
  AAA triploid: most commercial types including Cavendish

TRIPLOID KEY: 3 chromosome sets → gametes can't form properly
  → sterile (no seeds!) → but must be propagated vegetatively
  → entire Cavendish crop = clones
  → genetic vulnerability = zero variation
```

### The Gros Michel Disaster and the Coming TR4 Crisis

```
THE BANANA MONOCULTURE CRISIS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PRE-1950: "Gros Michel" was THE commercial banana
  Creamier, more flavorful, longer shelf life than Cavendish
  Standard banana flavor = Gros Michel (banana-flavored candy still mimics it)

1950s-1965: Fusarium oxysporum f.sp. cubense Race 1 ("Panama disease")
  Soil-borne water mold; persists for decades in soil
  No chemical treatment effective
  Destroyed virtually all commercial Gros Michel plantations worldwide

SOLUTION: Replace with Cavendish (Musa AAA Cavendish subgroup)
  Resistant to Race 1
  Became global standard 1960s-70s
  The banana everyone now eats

CURRENT THREAT: TR4 (Tropical Race 4) strain
  Emerged ~1990 in Taiwan, spread to SE Asia, Australia, Middle East,
  Africa, Latin America (Colombia confirmed 2019 — CATASTROPHIC)
  TR4 kills Cavendish exactly as Race 1 killed Gros Michel
  TR4 resistant banana varieties: under development but not commercially ready
  2023: TR4 spreading through major banana-producing regions
```

**The monoculture lesson:** Genetic uniformity = zero resistance. Both crises (Gros Michel then Cavendish) were predictable from the clonal propagation model. The Irish potato famine had the same architecture. See `09-MODERN-BREEDING.md` for breeding solutions.

## Strawberry: An Accidental Hybrid

The modern garden strawberry (*Fragaria* × *ananassa*) was an 18th-century accident.

```
STRAWBERRY HYBRID ORIGIN
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fragaria virginiana (NA): small, flavorful, scarlet
  Brought to Europe by settlers
  Grown in Versailles garden by ~1600

Fragaria chiloensis (S. America, Chile): large, pale, bland
  Brought to France by Amédée-François Frézier, 1714
  Planted in Brittany for French navy (large size → promising)
  Had been planted near F. virginiana in botanical garden

Accidental cross-pollination (~1740s, Brest, France):
  → Fragaria × ananassa (the garden strawberry)
  Large fruit from chiloensis + flavor from virginiana
  Named "ananas" (pineapple-like) for its strong aroma

Antoine Nicolas Duchesne (1766, age 18!): described the hybrid
  and its parents correctly in his Monographie de la fraise
  One of the first correctly understood plant hybridizations

Modern strawberry: still hexaploid hybrid, massive selective breeding
  CA + FL produce 90%+ of US supply
```

## Fruit Ripening Biology

```
CLIMACTERIC vs NON-CLIMACTERIC FRUITS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CLIMACTERIC fruits: continue ripening after harvest
  Apple, banana, pear, tomato, avocado, mango, peach, plum
  Ethylene autocatalytic: ethylene → more ethylene → accelerates ripening
  Ethylene trigger: converts starch → sugar, softens pectin,
                    changes color (chlorophyll breakdown), develops aroma
  Practical: can pick unripe, ripen in transit/storage

NON-CLIMACTERIC fruits: do not ripen after harvest
  Strawberry, citrus, grapes, cherries, raspberries, pineapple
  Must ripen on plant
  Once picked: only decay, no quality improvement

ETHYLENE APPLICATIONS:
  Commercial ripening rooms (bananas): ethylene gas applied
  "Keeping apples away from bananas": banana ethylene ripens apples faster
  Tomato: commercial tomatoes picked green, ethylene-treated → red
    (this is why commercial tomatoes lack flavor — never vine-ripened)
  Avocado: picked firm, softened with ethylene in distribution
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Botanically, strawberry is not a true berry.** A true berry (botanical) = entire ovary wall is fleshy (tomato, grape, banana). Strawberry = accessory fruit (receptacle tissue). Conversely, tomatoes, kiwis, and bananas are true berries botanically. The culinary/botanical categories don't align.

**The banana you eat is seedless because of sterility, not breeding for seedlessness.** Triploid chromosome set means meiosis fails → no functional gametes → no seeds. The "seedlessness" is a side effect of polyploidy.

**All apples taste different because of grafting, not seed.** A Honeycrisp from Washington and one from New Zealand are genetically identical clones. Taste differences come from terroir (soil, climate, elevation), not genetics.

**Citrus "species" names are mostly historical accidents.** What we call "lemon" or "orange" were named before genetic analysis. The "species" distinctions are conveniences, not evolutionary realities — they're all interfertile hybrids.
