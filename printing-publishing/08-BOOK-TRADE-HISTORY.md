# Book Trade History: Guilds, Copyright Origins (Statute of Anne 1710), Paperback Revolution

## The Big Picture

The book trade is a content distribution platform that has been disrupted three times: by print (1450s), by cheap binding (1930s), and by digital (2000s). Each disruption follows the same pattern — cost reduction + new distribution channel + new audience segment + regulatory response.

## The Stationers' Company and Pre-Publication Censorship

```
STATIONERS' COMPANY (London, chartered 1557)

FORMATION:
  1403: Scribes and illuminators form guild
  1557: Queen Mary I grants royal charter
  Motivation: control Protestant literature
  (Mary was Catholic, suppressing Reformation texts)

MONOPOLY STRUCTURE:
  All printing in England must be done by members
  All books must be registered in Stationers' Register
  Unregistered printing: illegal, books seized
  Company polices its own members + unauthorized printers

FUNCTION:
  +---------------------------+
  |  STATIONERS' COMPANY      |
  |                           |
  |  Member registers title   |
  |  -> exclusive right to    |
  |     print that title      |
  |                           |
  |  Not author rights:       |
  |  PUBLISHER/PRINTER rights |
  |  Author has no standing   |
  |                           |
  |  Political control:       |
  |  Crown + Church approve   |
  |  books before printing    |
  |  Company enforces this    |
  +---------------------------+

KEY POINT: Pre-1710, copyright was a publisher right, not an author right.
The Stationers' system protected publishers from each other (piracy)
while simultaneously enforcing state censorship.
The author had no rights; the stationer had all rights.

LAPSE OF LICENSING ACT:
  1662: Licensing Act renewed (required pre-publication approval)
  1679, 1685, 1693: Lapsed and renewed
  1695: Parliament allows Licensing Act to LAPSE permanently
  Reason: political (Parliament didn't like crown censorship power)
  Not reason: philosophical commitment to press freedom
  Practical result: printing no longer requires government license
  New problem: what legal framework protects publishers' investments?
```

## The Statute of Anne (1710) — First Copyright Law

This is the foundational document of intellectual property law. Understanding it is essential for understanding modern copyright, software licensing, and IP debates.

```
STATUTE OF ANNE (April 10, 1710)
Full title: "An Act for the Encouragement of Learning, by Vesting
the Copies of Printed Books in the Authors or Purchasers of such
Copies, during the Times therein mentioned"

PROBLEM IT SOLVED:
  Without licensing act: no legal basis for exclusive printing rights
  Publishers faced immediate piracy of any successful title
  Risk: publishers won't invest in printing if they can't recoup
  Goal: provide limited monopoly to enable investment in publishing

KEY PROVISIONS:

1. DURATION: 14 years from first publication
   + author still living: additional 14 years (total 28 max)
   Note: term was SHORT by design — balance between publisher
         protection and public domain entry

2. WHO HOLDS COPYRIGHT: AUTHORS (not publishers)
   Revolutionary: first time author had any legal standing
   Practical reality: authors immediately assigned to publishers
   But: legal principle established that authorship = right-holder

3. DEPOSIT: 9 copies to named libraries (Cambridge, Oxford, etc.)
   In exchange for monopoly, public gets access through libraries
   Foundational principle: copyright is a PUBLIC BARGAIN
   Author gets time-limited monopoly; public gets:
   (a) eventual free access (public domain)
   (b) immediate library access

4. SUBJECT MATTER: Books only
   Not music, drama, art — these came in later statutes

IDEA-EXPRESSION DICHOTOMY (implicit in Statute of Anne):
  Copyright protects the EXPRESSION (specific words)
  Not the IDEAS, facts, or knowledge contained in those words
  You can't copyright: "Newton's laws of motion"
  You CAN copyright: Newton's specific prose describing them
  This distinction is fundamental to: all IP law, open source
  licenses, software copyright, API copyrightability debates

COMPARISON TO SOFTWARE:
  Software copyright: expression (source/object code)
  Not protected: algorithms, data structures, APIs
    (Google v Oracle partially challenges this)
  Same principle as Statute of Anne, same problem:
    "You can copy the idea of moving a cursor,
     but not the exact GUI code that does it"
```

## Copyright Term Expansion

```
COPYRIGHT TERM ESCALATION: 1710 to PRESENT

1710  Statute of Anne:  14 years + 14 renewal = 28 max
      Rational: recover investment + decent public domain timing

1790  US Copyright Act: 14 + 14 = 28 (copied Statute of Anne)

1831  US extension:     28 + 14 = 42 years

1909  US extension:     28 + 28 = 56 years

1976  US extension:     Author's life + 50 years
                        OR 75 years from publication (works for hire)
      Rational given: justification drifting from "investment recovery"
                       toward "author's natural rights"

1998  CTEA "Mickey Mouse Act":
      Author's life + 70 years
      Work for hire: 95 years from publication OR 120 from creation

EU Harmonization (1993-1996):
      Life + 70 years (harmonized across EU)

OBSERVATION:
  Original 1710 term: 14-28 years
  Current US term: life + 70 years = ~140-170 years
  Increase ratio: ~10x

  The 1976 and 1998 extensions were driven by:
  1. Entertainment industry lobbying (Disney, most visibly)
  2. International harmonization arguments
  3. "Creator's rights" framing (sounds better than "Disney's profits")

  Economic argument for long terms:
    Incentive: longer monopoly = more investment in creation
  Economic argument against:
    Investment recovery: most economic value in first 5-10 years
    The extra 100+ years of copyright creates minimal new creation incentive
    while removing vast amounts of work from public domain

  PUBLIC DOMAIN ENTRY:
  Under 1710 original: works from 1710 enter public domain by 1738
  Under current US law: works from 1927 entered public domain Jan 1, 2023
                        (95-year rule for pre-1978 works)
```

## Publishing Houses — The Intermediary Industry

```
PUBLISHING HOUSE EVOLUTION

EARLY COMMERCIAL PUBLISHERS (18th c.):
  Jacob Tonson (London): publisher of Milton, Dryden, Shakespeare rights
  Robert Dodsley: Dr. Johnson, Edmund Burke connection
  Function: finance printing, acquire manuscripts, distribute, market

19th CENTURY CONSOLIDATION:
  John Murray (1768, London): still operating today
  Longman (1724): still operating (Pearson subsidiary)
  Harper & Brothers (1817, New York): mass market orientation
  Charles Scribner's Sons (1846): prestige literary
  Random House (1927): founded by Bennett Cerf

THREE-DECKER NOVEL SYSTEM (Victorian England):
  Circulating libraries (Mudie's, W.H. Smith): bulk buyers
  Standard format: 3-volume novels, price: 31 shillings 6 pence
  Economics: too expensive for most buyers; people borrowed
  Library buys 1,000 copies of a 3-decker novel
  Publisher only needs to sell to libraries for a profit
  Authors: paid advances based on library orders

  This created: cautious, conservative publishing
  Long novels (fill 3 volumes), safe content (library approval)
  George Mudie essentially had editorial veto power
  System collapsed 1894 when libraries demanded cheaper one-volume editions

CONGLOMERATE ERA (1960s-2000s):
  Random House: acquired by Newhouse (1980) -> Bertelsmann (1998)
  Simon & Schuster: acquired by Gulf+Western -> Paramount -> Viacom
  HarperCollins: Rupert Murdoch / News Corp (1987)
  Penguin Random House: merger 2013 (attempted Simon & Schuster
    acquisition 2022 blocked by DOJ antitrust)

CURRENT "BIG 5" (actually 4 as of attempts):
  Penguin Random House (Bertelsmann)
  HarperCollins (News Corp)
  Simon & Schuster (KKR, after DOJ blocked PRH merger)
  Hachette Book Group (Lagardere/Vivendi)
  Macmillan Publishers (Holtzbrinck)

  ~80% of US retail book sales through Big 5
  Concentration level comparable to music labels, movie studios
```

## The Paperback Revolution

```
PAPERBACK REVOLUTION (1935)

BEFORE PAPERBACK:
  Standard hardcover book: $2-4 (1930s US dollars)
  Average US worker weekly wage: $15-25
  Books: ~10-20% of weekly wage per title
  Not impulse purchase; not working-class medium

  "Pulp" magazines existed (cheap, lurid)
  "Dime novels" existed (Beadle & Adams, 1860s)
  But serious literature: hardcover only

PENGUIN BOOKS (1935):
  Allen Lane: bored at train station, couldn't find good book
  (Agatha Christie's account of the origin)
  Founded Penguin, July 30, 1935
  First 10 titles: published simultaneously
  Price: 6 pence (price of 10 cigarettes)
  Cover design: three horizontal bands of color + penguin
               (consistent branding, easily recognized)
  Distribution: NOT just bookshops
               Woolworths (chain store) as primary distributor
               Sold beside sundries, not in specialist shops

  First 10 Penguin titles (sold out in weeks):
  Ariel by André Maurois
  A Farewell to Arms by Ernest Hemingway
  Poet's Pub by Eric Linklater
  [+ 7 others]

  First year: 3 million Penguin paperbacks sold
  Previously: 3 million books/year was total UK book market

AMERICAN PAPERBACK:
  Pocket Books (1939): first US mass-market paperback
  Robert de Graff: 25 cents, sold through newsstands and drugstores
  Bantam, Dell, NAL/Signet follow in 1940s
  Paperback Originals: 1950s — original titles in paperback
  (Not just reprints of hardcovers)

ECONOMICS:
  Hardcover: $2-4 | Royalty: 10-15% | Print run: 5,000-10,000
  Paperback: $0.25 | Royalty: ~8% | Print run: 100,000-500,000
  Revenue model: lower margin, much higher volume
  Distribution: mass market (newsstands, drugstores) vs bookshops

IMPACT:
  Created: mass reading culture
  Enabled: soldiers reading paperbacks in WWII (Armed Services Editions)
  Price reduction: ~85-90% (comparable to Gutenberg's 80-90% reduction)
  Audience expansion: working class, students
  Analogous to: streaming vs DVD purchase
    Same content, different pricing model, vastly larger audience
```

## Textbook Market

```
TEXTBOOK MARKET: A CAPTIVE BUYER PROBLEM

STRUCTURE:
  Professor assigns textbook
  Student must buy (no choice)
  Professor doesn't pay (no price sensitivity)
  Publisher can charge monopoly prices on each edition

  Classic captive buyer market:
  Instructor = specifier (selects product, doesn't pay)
  Student = buyer (must pay, no choice)
  Publisher = monopolist per course, per edition

EDITION CYCLING:
  New edition every 3-4 years
  Typically: ~20-30% content change
  Effect: destroys used book market
  Used books: previous edition, worth $0-5
  New edition: $150-300

  Used book market (bookstore resale):
  Student sells at $50, bookstore resells at $100
  Publisher gets $0 from used transactions
  Solution: NEW EDITION eliminates used market

RECENT DISRUPTION:
  Chegg (2007): textbook rental ($20-30/semester vs $200 purchase)
  PDF piracy: massive, especially in developing countries
  OER (Open Educational Resources): openly licensed textbooks
    OpenStax: free digital textbooks, used by 2M+ students
  Publishers respond: bundled digital access codes
    Code required for homework platform
    Code non-transferable: used book + new code = purchase
    This maintains captive buyer even in digital era

  Legal action:
  Book rental gray market: Kirtsaeng v Wiley (2013)
  Supreme Court: first-sale doctrine applies to imported books
  Result: international price discrimination partially undermined
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Copyright was created to protect authors."** The Statute of Anne gives rights to authors rhetorically but to publishers practically. Authors immediately assigned rights to publishers as a condition of publication. The legal form said "author"; the commercial reality was "publisher captures all economic value." Modern work-for-hire doctrine and standard publishing contracts continue this tradition.

**"Public domain works are free to use without restriction."** Public domain means no copyright protection. Other restrictions can still apply: moral rights (some countries), trademark (character names), contracts (licensed versions), database rights (EU), privacy rights (private individuals depicted). "Public domain" is specifically about copyright expiry, not a general freedom from all legal constraints.

**"Publishers are necessary for distribution."** They were, when distribution meant physical copies through physical retail. Amazon's publishing infrastructure (Kindle Direct Publishing) demonstrates that the production and distribution functions can be disaggregated. What publishers currently provide is: editorial quality filter, marketing, advance payment (risk transfer), and physical retail relationships. These remain valuable but are not logically necessary.

**"Penguin invented the paperback."** Paperback books existed before Penguin — German "Tauchnitz Edition" paperbacks from the 1840s served English-language readers in Europe. What Penguin invented was the mass-market paperback at impulse-buy pricing through non-specialist distribution. The innovation was business model, not format.

**"The conglomerate era improved publishing."** Mixed. Consolidation created resources for major marketing campaigns and international distribution. It also created pressure for bestseller-driven acquisitions, shortened timescales, and reduced investment in literary midlist books. The "Big 5" reject approximately 99% of manuscripts submitted — most from agents who rejected the other 95% of submissions first.
