# Print Trade and Spread: Incunabula, Frankfurt Book Fair, Censorship, Reformation as Media Event

## The Big Picture

The printing press created an information industry in one generation. The 45 years from 1455 to 1500 (the incunabula era) saw print technology spread to 270+ cities and produce ~30,000 book titles. Then the Reformation showed what happens when a disruptive information technology meets an existing power structure: the power structure loses, but regulation follows.

## The Incunabula Era (1455-1500)

"Incunabula" (Latin: cradle) — books printed before 1501. The term was coined retrospectively by bibliographers.

```
INCUNABULA ERA STATISTICS

Duration: 1455-1500 (45 years)
Cities with presses: ~270 (in 20+ modern countries)
Estimated books produced: ~28,000-30,000 titles
Estimated total copies: 8-15 million
Printers working: ~1,000+

LEADING PRINTING CITIES (1480s-1490s):
  Venice:    ~417 editions/year   (most) — luxury trade, Mediterranean routes
  Paris:     ~200 editions/year   — university + clergy market
  Rome:      ~180 editions/year   — church + papal administration
  Cologne:   ~130 editions/year   — German merchant city
  Strasbourg:~100 editions/year
  Basel:      ~90 editions/year   — humanist scholars, Erasmus connection

WHAT WAS PRINTED:
  Religious texts:    45%  (bibles, liturgy, theology)
  Classics (Latin):   30%  (Cicero, Virgil, Aristotle)
  Law texts:           8%  (expanding legal profession)
  Science/medicine:    5%
  Literature:          5%
  Other:               7%

NOTE: The popular conception that the press immediately produced
vernacular popular literature is wrong. Early printing served
the existing learned and religious elite. Vernacular popular
print came later (16th century).

INCUNABULA CHARACTERISTICS:
  - No title pages (appear late 15th c.)
  - Colophon at END: printer, place, date
  - Blank spaces for illuminators to add hand-painted initials
  - Gothic blackletter (German) vs Roman type (Italian)
  - No page numbers (signatures for binding only)
  - Catchwords (last word of gathering repeated first word of next)
```

## Nicholas Jenson and the Roman Typeface

```
JENSON'S TYPE REVOLUTION (Venice, 1469-1480)

Nicholas Jenson: French royal mint-master
  Sent to Mainz ~1458 to learn printing for France
  Instead: moved to Venice, set up press 1469

JENSON'S CONTRIBUTION: Roman type
  Previous: Gothic blackletter (imitated German manuscripts)
  Jenson: designed type based on Roman inscriptions and
          Italian humanist manuscript hands (carolingian)
  Result: clean, readable, proportional letterforms
          that we still use today (basis of serif fonts)

  +----------------------+  +----------------------+
  |  GOTHIC BLACKLETTER  |  |  ROMAN (JENSON 1469) |
  |  Gutenberg/German    |  |  Venetian/Italian    |
  |                      |  |                      |
  | Tall, narrow, dark   |  | Proportional, light  |
  | Angular strokes      |  | Round letterforms    |
  | Dense texture        |  | Clear, open          |
  | Hard to read fast    |  | Easy to read fast    |
  | Looks like MS        |  | New aesthetic        |
  +----------------------+  +----------------------+

IMPACT:
  Roman type became standard for Latin and all European
  vernacular languages outside Germany
  German printers used blackletter until 20th century
  (Fraktur finally abandoned 1941 by Nazi decree —
   ironically, for readability in occupied territories)

Aldus Manutius (Venice, 1490s-1515):
  Further refined roman type
  Invented ITALIC type (1501) for compact portable books
  Created "pocket books" — small octavo format
  Cut prices dramatically
  His Aldine Press: precursor to modern trade publisher
```

## The Frankfurt Book Fair

Still operating today. Started as a consequence of geography, not planning.

```
FRANKFURT BOOK FAIR: ORIGINS AND FUNCTION

WHY FRANKFURT:
  - Crossroads of major European trade routes
  - Two annual fairs (spring: Easter, autumn: harvest)
  - Pre-existing merchant fair infrastructure
  - Neutral city (Imperial Free City status)

FUNCTION IN BOOK TRADE (from ~1478):
  Not primarily retail (consumers buy locally)
  WHOLESALE: printers and publishers trade with each other

  Publisher A (Venice) brings 500 copies of Cicero
  Publisher B (Paris) brings 300 copies of Aristotle
  They EXCHANGE: each gets the other's inventory
  to sell in their local market

  This was BARTER at first, then ledger credit
  Basis: Frankfurt develops credit/settlement system
  that prefigures modern financial clearing

CATALOGS:
  Frankfurt Messkatalog (1564): first book trade catalog
  Listed all books available at the fair
  Became the de facto index of what was published
  Analog of: database of record for the book trade
  Continued until 1749 (then various successors)

TRADE SECRETS AND PIRACY:
  Printers with exclusive rights to popular texts
  Competition: others pirated popular titles quickly
  No international copyright: piracy was legal across borders
  Solution: trade reputation, relationship networks
  Frankfurt fair = trust and reputation enforcement mechanism

MODERN FRANKFURT BOOK FAIR:
  Still the largest book rights-trading fair in the world
  ~7,000 exhibitors from 100+ countries
  Now primarily: rights trading (film, translation, digital)
  Same function, same location, 550+ years continuous operation
```

## Censorship and the Index Librorum Prohibitorum

```
REGULATION AS RESPONSE TO DISRUPTION

TIMELINE OF PRINT CENSORSHIP:

1501: Pope Alexander VI — first general press censorship decree
      Printers must submit texts for Church approval before printing

1517: Luther's 95 Theses — the trigger event
      Printed and distributed rapidly across Germany
      (See Reformation section below)

1521: Diet of Worms — Luther excommunicated
      Printing of Luther's works banned
      EFFECT: ban ignored, printing accelerated

1524: Peasants' War in Germany
      Printed pamphlets as organizing tool
      State panic about print-enabled revolution

1543: Copernicus "De Revolutionibus" published
      Not immediately banned (took 73 years)

1557: Stationers' Company chartered in England
      Royal licensing monopoly on printing
      Controlled who could print in England
      Printers must register with Company
      Company polices unauthorized printing
      FUNCTION: private-sector censorship by monopoly franchise

1559: Index Librorum Prohibitorum (Catholic Church)
      List of banned books
      Catholics forbidden to read, own, or distribute
      Maintained and updated until 1966 (yes, 1966)
      Peak: ~4,000 titles including major scientific works

1660s: Lapse of English Licensing Act
        Multiple lapse and renewal cycles
        Final lapse 1695: ends pre-publication censorship in England
        Background condition for the Statute of Anne (1710)

ENFORCEMENT EFFECTIVENESS:
  Generally poor. Banned books circulated widely.
  "First published in Amsterdam" = code for suppressed in home country
  Index paradox: listing a book drew attention to it
  Scholars memorized banned books or kept hidden copies
  Cross-border printing: work banned in France, printed in Geneva/Holland

  Analogy: SOPA/PIPA (2012) — internet censorship attempts
  Same dynamic: technical distribution defeats legal restriction
  when the distribution technology is decentralized
```

## The Protestant Reformation as Media Event

This is the first documented case of a political/religious movement using a new information technology to defeat a centralized information monopoly. The parallel to the internet's role in the Arab Spring, or social media and political disruption, is structural.

```
REFORMATION AS MEDIA EVENT (1517-1560)

BEFORE PRINTING: Information monopoly
  Church: controlled production and interpretation of scripture
  Latin liturgy + Latin bible = only clergy can read
  Sermon = the only delivery mechanism for religious content
  Heresy attempts: Wycliffe (1370s), Hus (1415) — suppressed
    Hus was burned; his followers were crushed
    Pre-printing, a heretical movement can be physically eliminated

AFTER PRINTING: Distributed information
  Luther nails (or sends) his 95 Theses, Oct 31, 1517
  Key: Theses written in Latin (scholar-to-scholar argument)
       NOT initially intended as popular mobilization

  WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED:
  Unknown printer(s) immediately translated to German and printed
  Within 2 weeks: distributed across Germany
  Within 2 months: all major German cities had copies
  Within 6 months: printed in France, England, Spain

  "The printing presses have spread Luther's theses everywhere"
  — Papal nuncio, 1518

PRINT PRODUCTION COMPARISON:
  Pre-1517:   ~40 pamphlets/year in German vernacular
  1517-1520:  ~400 pamphlets/year (10x increase)
  1520-1525:  ~600+ pamphlets/year

  Luther's output (1517-1523):
  ~400 separate publications
  Estimated 300,000 copies of his works in print by 1523
  Germany's first mass media celebrity

  "I would have thought nothing of this affair had it
   been proceeded against quietly." — Luther, 1518
   (acknowledging that publicity transformed a local dispute
    into a movement)

STRATEGIC MEDIA USE:
  Luther + associates understood the medium:
  1. German vernacular text (not Latin): mass audience
  2. Woodcut illustrations: literate and illiterate readers
  3. Broad sheet format: cheap, distributable
  4. Pamphlet form: quick production, wide distribution
  5. Theses format: numbered list, easy to memorize/discuss

  Erasmus: "Printing has given wings to the truth"

CHURCH'S MEDIA RESPONSE:
  Catholic counter-reformation printed too, but:
  - Slower institutional decision-making
  - Hierarchical approval processes
  - Less appealing to vernacular emotion
  - Had to defend existing position vs attacking it

  First-mover advantage in information warfare:
  Luther's frame set; Catholics arguing against it
```

## Early Book Trade Economics

```
BOOK PRICING AND ECONOMICS (Late 15th c.)

PRODUCTION COSTS (approximate):
  Type: amortized over many editions
  Paper: 50-60% of total book cost
  Labor: 30-40% of total cost
  Press setup: fixed cost per title

PRICING:
  Incunabula prices (1470s-1480s):
  Simple pamphlet (4-8 pages): 1-2 pfennig
  Standard book (~200 pages): 1-3 florins
  Illuminated luxury volume: 10-50+ florins

  COMPARISON:
  Skilled craftsman daily wage: ~0.3-0.5 florins
  Scribal copy of same 200-page book: 15-25 florins
  Printing reduction: 80-90% cost reduction
  Access implication: artisan could now afford books
                      (with weeks of saving)

SUBSCRIPTION MODEL (proto):
  Large print runs financed by advance orders
  Church and institutional buyers pre-ordered
  Aldus Manutius: subscription list for expensive editions
  Same economics as modern Kickstarter for books

PIRACY ECONOMICS:
  A successful book is immediately pirated by competitors
  First printer: bears full production cost, earns margin
  Pirate printer: only sets type and prints, lower cost
  Result: race to print, price compression, first-mover wins
  Solution: speed + reputation + exclusive relationships
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Luther intended to start a mass movement with his 95 Theses."** The Theses were a scholarly Latin disputation — he sent them to the Archbishop of Mainz and nailed them to the Wittenberg church door (perhaps). Anonymous printers translated and distributed them. Luther was surprised by the spread. The medium acted on the message before the author could.

**"The Index Librorum was only about religion."** The Index banned books across categories: heresy, moral corruption, political subversion, and eventually science (Copernicus, Galileo). It functioned as comprehensive knowledge censorship. Galileo's *Dialogo* was added in 1633 and remained on the Index until 1835.

**"The Frankfurt Book Fair started in the 16th century."** The Frankfurt trade fairs date to the 12th century. The book trade's use of the fair dates to ~1478. The first book trade catalog (Messkatalog) was published in 1564. The modern Frankfurt Book Fair as an annual dedicated event descends from this continuous tradition.

**"Printing immediately created mass literacy."** No. The literacy rate changed slowly. Printing first served the already-literate. The feedback loop was: printing makes books affordable -> reading becomes more useful -> literacy increases slowly over generations -> demand for print increases. Mass literacy is an 18th-19th century development in Europe, 400 years after Gutenberg.

**"Protestant reformers supported press freedom."** Many did not. Luther himself supported censorship of views he considered heretical (Anabaptists, Zwinglians). The pattern: every movement that uses a new medium to gain power then attempts to control that medium once it has power. Print freedom was a byproduct of state and church failure to enforce censorship effectively, not of principled libertarianism among reformers.
