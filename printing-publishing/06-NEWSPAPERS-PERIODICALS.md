# Newspapers and Periodicals: First Newspapers, Coffeehouse Culture, Penny Press, Yellow Journalism, Wire Services

## The Big Picture

The newspaper is a periodical delivery system for time-sensitive information — a SaaS model applied to news. The economics are completely different from books: readers care about freshness, not permanence. This changes everything about the technology, economics, and social function.

## Pre-Newspaper: Handwritten Newsletters

```
MANUSCRIPT NEWS NETWORKS (14th-16th c.)

AVVISI (Italian plural of "notice"):
  Handwritten newsletters, Venice as center
  Produced by professional information gatherers
  Content: shipping, trade, military, political news
  Subscribers: merchants, diplomats, courts
  Frequency: weekly (when ships arrived)
  Price: equivalent to a day's wages per copy
  Distribution: courier networks

FUGGER NEWSLETTERS:
  Augsburg banking house (Fuggers: 16th c. European banking giants)
  Maintained private newsletter network across Europe
  1568-1605: ~400 surviving letters (published 1926)
  Content: intelligence for business and financial decisions
  Function: private information advantage = competitive edge
  Exact analog of: Bloomberg Terminal (1982)
    Private information network for financial advantage
    High-quality, expensive, used by institutions not public

HANDWRITTEN ADVANTAGE:
  No printing meant no censorship bottleneck
  Could be produced anywhere quickly
  Harder to intercept than printed text
  Governments could not seize "a press" — distributed production

TRANSITION TO PRINT:
  Printers recognized periodic news as a business
  But: news required faster production cycles than books
  Weekly schedule forced process optimization
  First regular printed newspapers: emerge 1605
```

## First Newspapers (1605-1650)

```
FIRST PRINTED NEWSPAPERS

"Relation aller Fürnemmen und gedenckwürdigen Historien"
(Account of All Distinguished and Commemorable Stories)
  Publisher: Johann Carolus
  Location: Strasbourg
  Date: 1605 (exact first issue)
  Format: Weekly, 4-page quarto
  Language: German
  Oldest surviving printed newspaper

Contemporaries (~1605-1620):
  Wolfenbütteler Aviso (Germany): 1609
  Nieuwe Tijdinghen (Belgium, Antwerp): 1605
  Courante uyt Italien, Duytslandt & c. (Amsterdam): 1618
  Oxford Gazette (England): 1665 (later: London Gazette)
  La Gazette (France, Richelieu's state paper): 1631

EARLY NEWSPAPER CHARACTERISTICS:
  No headlines (all text, dense)
  No bylines (anonymous writers)
  Translated/reprinted foreign dispatches
  No original reporting in modern sense
  Printed on both sides of folded sheet
  Weekly schedule (daily papers: early 18th c.)
  News mixed with commercial ads

STATE CONTROL:
  Many early newspapers were state organs or licensed
  La Gazette (1631): founded with Cardinal Richelieu's backing
  Richelieu wrote articles himself
  Licensing: governments quickly sought control
  English papers: required licensing until 1695
```

## Coffeehouse Culture and the Public Sphere

The coffeehouse and the newspaper co-evolved in 17th-18th century Europe. This is the first documented case of a physical network that aggregates distributed information consumers around a shared medium.

```
COFFEEHOUSE AS INFORMATION NODE

LONDON COFFEEHOUSES (1652-1750):
  First London coffeehouse: 1652 (Pasqua Rosée, Cornhill)
  Peak: ~2,000+ coffeehouses by 1700 in London alone
  Entry fee: one penny (hence "penny universities")

FUNCTION:
  +------------------------------------------+
  |           COFFEEHOUSE                    |
  |                                          |
  |  * Multiple newspapers on tables         |
  |  * Patrons read, discuss, debate         |
  |  * Single subscription fee shared       |
  |    by many readers (10-50x leverage)    |
  |  * Information exchange (shipping,      |
  |    trade, politics)                     |
  |  * Social mixing across classes         |
  |                                          |
  |  LLOYD'S COFFEE HOUSE (1688):            |
  |  -> became Lloyd's of London            |
  |     (insurance market)                  |
  |  -> shipping intelligence sharing       |
  |     -> mutual risk information          |
  |     -> insurance pricing based on       |
  |        shared information               |
  |                                          |
  |  JONATHAN'S COFFEE HOUSE (1698):         |
  |  -> became London Stock Exchange        |
  |     (share trading)                     |
  |  -> same pattern: price information     |
  |     sharing -> market making            |
  +------------------------------------------+

JÜRGEN HABERMAS: "PUBLIC SPHERE" CONCEPT
  The coffeehouse created a space where
  private individuals debated public affairs
  Not state, not private: "public sphere"
  Newspapers provided common information base
  Coffeehouses provided discussion infrastructure
  This is the predecessor to democracy in the modern sense

  Habermas's thesis: the mass media destroyed the public sphere
  (passive consumption replaced active discussion)
  Debatable, but the observation about coffeehouses is accurate.
```

## The Penny Press Revolution (1833)

The most important structural change in newspaper economics: shifting from reader subscription to advertiser subsidy.

```
BEFORE PENNY PRESS (1820s):
  Six-penny papers: cost 6 cents per issue
  Average daily wage (US): 75-100 cents
  Price = ~6-8% of daily wage
  Subscription: annual, sold to businesses, professionals
  Audience: upper/middle class men

THE PENNY PRESS INNOVATION:
  September 3, 1833: Benjamin Day launches "The Sun" (New York)
  Cover price: 1 penny
  Distribution: newsboys sell on street corners (vs mail)
  Content: crime, courts, human interest, local news
  Business model: ADVERTISING pays most of cost
  Reader pays 1 cent to read; advertisers pay to reach readers

  ECONOMICS:
  Reader: 1 cent/issue (impulse purchase, no commitment)
  Advertiser: pays for access to mass audience
  Newspaper: earns on BOTH sides (reader + advertiser)
  Key insight: readers are the product, not the customer
               Advertisers are the customer

  This is exactly the Facebook/Google business model, 180 years earlier.

PENNY PRESS PAPERS:
  The Sun (New York): 1833 — Benjamin Day
  New York Herald: 1835 — James Gordon Bennett
  New York Tribune: 1841 — Horace Greeley
  New York Times: 1851 — Henry J. Raymond

CIRCULATION EXPLOSION:
  1830 largest US paper: ~4,000 daily readers
  1835 New York Sun: ~15,000 daily (largest in world)
  1850s largest US papers: 50,000+
  1880s largest: 100,000+
  1890s yellow journalism peak: 500,000-700,000

WHY IT REQUIRED STEAM PRESS:
  Penny papers only profitable at scale
  Scale requires fast printing
  The Koenig steam press (1814) and successors:
  -> 8,000 sheets/hour vs 250 by hand
  -> industrial output needed to serve mass audiences
```

## The Telegraph and Wire Services — Network Effects

The telegraph (1837 UK, 1844 US) created a new constraint and a new advantage for newspaper production.

```
TELEGRAPH TRANSFORMS NEWS

BEFORE TELEGRAPH (pre-1844):
  News travels at horse/ship speed
  European news in US: 2-4 weeks old
  Distant news = old news
  Regional papers: local news focus
  Little incentive to share news rapidly

AFTER TELEGRAPH:
  Transmission speed: nearly instantaneous
  Wire services emerge: news as a utility

ASSOCIATED PRESS (1846):
  Founded by 6 New York newspapers (including Herald, Tribune, Sun)
  To share telegraph costs for war news (Mexican-American War)
  Pooled resource: one wire subscription, shared among members
  Each member gets all news; contributes news to pool

  AP as infrastructure company:
  Same model as: TCP/IP routing, DNS, NTP
  Shared protocol benefits all users; no one owns it

COMPETITIVE WIRE SERVICES:
  United Press (1882, later UPI): 1958 merger
  Reuters (London, 1851): Paul Julius Reuter, founded on
    telegraph intelligence during Crimean War
    Still operating as major global wire service

TELEGRAPH ECONOMICS:
  Charged per word
  Forced newspaper style revolution:
  - "Inverted pyramid" structure: most important facts first
    (if wire cut mid-transmission, editor has usable story)
  - Concise writing: no purple prose in expensive telegrams
  - Standard dateline format: CITY, Date — (text)
  - "Who, What, When, Where, Why" lead paragraph
  These conventions persist in journalism today

WIRE SERVICE NETWORK EFFECT:
  Larger wire network = more value
  More subscribers = lower cost per subscriber
  AP covered the US; Reuters covered Europe; agreements for global
  Same economics as: Visa/Mastercard network effects
  Winner-take-most dynamic in wire service market
```

## Yellow Journalism and the Circulation Wars

```
YELLOW JOURNALISM (1890s)

PRINCIPALS:
  Joseph Pulitzer: New York World (bought 1883)
  William Randolph Hearst: New York Journal (bought 1895)
  Direct competition: same city, same market

  "YELLOW KID" CARTOON:
  Character in Pulitzer's World (1896)
  Hearst poached the cartoonist
  Pulitzer hired a second cartoonist to draw same character
  Two competing "Yellow Kid" cartoons -> "yellow press"
  Derogatory term for sensationalism

YELLOW JOURNALISM TECHNIQUES:
  - Screaming headlines in large type
  - Dramatic illustrations (often fabricated)
  - Emotional appeals over factual reporting
  - Personal crusades (moral outrage campaigns)
  - Made-up or embellished stories
  - Crime, scandal, celebrity focus

SPANISH-AMERICAN WAR (1898):
  Hearst's Journal: aggressive pro-war coverage
  USS Maine explosion (Feb 1898): Hearst blamed Spain
    HEADLINE: "Remember the Maine! To Hell with Spain!"
    Evidence for Spanish involvement: minimal
    Result: public pressure contributed to war declaration
  Hearst quote (possibly apocryphal):
    Telegram to illustrator in Cuba: "You furnish the pictures,
    I'll furnish the war."

BUSINESS LOGIC:
  Sensationalism -> circulation -> advertising revenue
  Circulation war between Pulitzer and Hearst:
  NY World: 200,000 (1895) -> 600,000 (1897)
  NY Journal:  launched 1895 -> 1,000,000 (1898, war coverage)

  Exactly: engagement metrics -> ad revenue
  Same incentive structure that produces clickbait today
  Platform (newspaper) rewards sensationalism
  Quality journalism is a negative externality

AFTERMATH:
  Pulitzer endowed Columbia School of Journalism (1912)
  Established Pulitzer Prizes (1917): prestige for quality journalism
  Attempt to create counter-incentive for quality
```

## Advertising Evolution

```
NEWSPAPER ADVERTISING EVOLUTION

PHASE 1: CLASSIFIEDS (1700s-1850s)
  Small text ads: shipping notices, slave auctions, job listings
  No display, pure information
  Affordable for small business

PHASE 2: DISPLAY ADS (1850s-1900s)
  Large type, illustrations, brand promotion
  Department stores: Macy's, Wanamaker's first major display advertisers
  J. Walter Thompson: first advertising agency (1864)
  Agencies: intermediary between newspaper and advertiser
  Same role as: ad exchanges in digital advertising

PHASE 3: CLASSIFIED DOMINANCE (1900s-1990s)
  "Help Wanted", Real Estate, Automotive: major revenue
  Classified: ~40% of newspaper ad revenue peak
  Craigslist (1995-2004): digital classifieds free
  -> $5-10 billion/year revenue destroyed from newspaper industry
  -> Craig Newmark estimated loss: ~$30 billion newspaper revenue
  -> This single disruption was more damaging than digital news

PHASE 4: DIGITAL ADVERTISING COLLAPSE (2000s-2010s)
  Google (2000): search advertising eats brand spending
  Facebook (2004): social advertising eats local spending
  Display CPM rates: $25-40/1000 (print) -> $1-10/1000 (digital display, programmatic)
                     Video pre-roll: $15-30+/1000; premium digital inventory $8-20+/1000
                     The print-digital CPM gap has narrowed in high-value segments; the
                     gap persists in volume/remnant inventory where programmatic drives rates down
  Newspaper digital revenue: never replaced print losses
  US newspaper ad revenue: $60B (2000) -> $9B (2020)
  Newsroom employment: 71,000 (2008) -> 31,000 (2020)
  Root cause: targeting efficiency destroyed the premium
               for broad-reach print advertising
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Newspapers have always been objective."** Newspapers were openly partisan from their earliest days. The 18th-19th century press was explicitly affiliated with political parties and viewpoints. "Objectivity" as a professional norm emerged in the late 19th/early 20th century — partly influenced by wire services (AP had to write for papers of all political persuasions, so neutrality was commercially necessary).

**"Craigslist destroyed newspapers."** Craigslist destroyed the classified revenue base (~40% of ad revenue). This was devastating. But display advertising was simultaneously being taken by Google and Facebook. The internet attack came from multiple directions; Craigslist was the most damaging single blow to one specific revenue stream.

**"The penny press democratized news."** Partially. It created a new audience (working-class urban readers) but also created the advertiser-funded model where readers are the product. The penny press didn't eliminate elite information advantages — it created a mass-market layer below the existing quality press while Bloomberg-equivalents continued to serve elites.

**"Yellow journalism was fringe."** At peak, Hearst and Pulitzer together dominated New York newspaper circulation, which was the largest market in the US. Yellow journalism was mainstream, not fringe. The quality press (New York Times, founded 1851) was a minority of the market by circulation.

**"Wire services are neutral."** Wire services are written to be usable by all subscribers regardless of politics, which creates an appearance of neutrality. But the selection of what to transmit (and what not to), story framing, and sourcing all embed perspectives. The AP "objectivity" norm constrains form, not essence.
