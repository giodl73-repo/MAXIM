# Conservation Zoology and Defaunation

## The Big Picture

Conservation zoology applies the full toolkit of zoological science — systematics, ecology, behavioral ecology, physiology, genetics — to the problem of biodiversity loss. The field has undergone a conceptual shift since the 1990s: from species-centered rescue operations to ecosystem-level thinking, from static preservation to dynamic process restoration. The central empirical reality is defaunation: the systematic loss of animal biomass, abundance, and diversity from ecosystems at all scales, with cascading functional consequences that extend far beyond the species lost.

## The Sixth Mass Extinction

```
BACKGROUND EXTINCTION vs MASS EXTINCTION
==========================================

BACKGROUND (normal) EXTINCTION RATE:
  ~0.1-1 E/MSY (extinctions per million species-years)
  Based on: fossil record; stratigraphic ranges

CURRENT RATE (estimated):
  100-1000x background rate
  Vertebrates: ~1.8 E/MSY estimated; fossil baseline ~0.1 E/MSY
  -> Order of magnitude higher than background

HISTORICAL MASS EXTINCTIONS (Big Five):
  Ordovician-Silurian (444 Mya): ~86% species lost; glaciation + sea level
  Late Devonian (375 Mya): ~75% species; multiple pulses
  Permian-Triassic (252 Mya): ~96% species; LARGEST; Siberian Traps volcanism
  Triassic-Jurassic (201 Mya): ~80% species; CAMP volcanism
  K-Pg (66 Mya): ~76% species; Chicxulub impact; wiped dinosaurs + ammonites
                  MAMMALS and BIRDS survived and radiated

CURRENT (Sixth) MASS EXTINCTION:
  NOT an event; an ongoing process driven by human land use
  DIFFERENT from prior extinctions:
    Prior: abiotic drivers (volcanism, asteroid, glaciation)
    Current: biotic driver (Homo sapiens); can be modified
    Prior: global, often rapid (10k-1M yr)
    Current: uneven; affecting certain taxa + regions first
    Rate of current change unprecedented in biotic history

KEY DATA:
  IUCN Red List (2024): >44,000 species threatened (~28% of assessed species)
  Vertebrates: 32% threatened
  Amphibians: ~41% threatened (highest of any vertebrate class)
  Plants: ~40% threatened
  Insects: estimated ~10% threatened (but poorly assessed)

  Living Planet Index (WWF 2024):
    Average vertebrate population size: -73% since 1970
    Freshwater vertebrates: -85% since 1970
    Tropical regions: highest declines
```

## Defaunation: The Core Problem

```
DEFAUNATION (Dirzo et al. 2014, Science)
==========================================

DEFINITION: Human-caused loss of animals from ecosystems
  Includes both EXTINCTION and POPULATION DECLINE
  Often: local loss before global extinction
  Functional defaunation: species still exists; too rare to function

BODY SIZE BIAS — the "megafauna" problem:
  Large animals disproportionately affected
  Reasons:
    Low reproductive rates (K-strategists; slow population recovery)
    Large home ranges (more exposed to habitat fragmentation)
    High meat yields (preferential hunting targets)
    High trophic level (bioaccumulation of toxins)
    Long generation time (slow evolutionary response)

SIZE-BIASED LOSS EVIDENCE:
  Mammals: mean body mass of threatened species >> non-threatened
  Late Pleistocene megafauna: ~40 genera of large mammals extinct
    in N. America at end-Pleistocene; coincided with human arrival
  "Sixth wave" concentrated in largest body size classes

FUNCTIONAL EXTINCTION:
  Species may survive but population density too low to fill ecological role
  Seed dispersal: large frugivores (elephants, tapirs) disperse large seeds
    -> without them, large-seeded trees cannot regenerate
    -> "the living dead": trees present but not regenerating
  Pollination: if pollinator rare enough, plant fails to set seed
  Apex predator: if density too low, cannot regulate mesopredators

DEFAUNATION METRICS:
  Biomass: total animal mass per unit area (down in most ecosystems)
  Abundance: individuals per unit area
  Occupancy: fraction of historical range occupied
  Interaction diversity: number of mutualistic/predation links realized
```

## Trophic Downgrading

```
TROPHIC DOWNGRADING (Estes et al. 2011, Science)
==================================================

DEFINITION: Systematic loss of apex predators -> ecosystem restructuring

MECHANISM:
  Apex predator loss:
    -> mesopredator release (secondary predators increase)
    -> herbivore outbreak
    -> vegetation overgrazing
    -> loss of habitat structure
    -> loss of species dependent on that structure

DOCUMENTED EXAMPLES:

TERRESTRIAL:
  Wolf extirpation from most of North America (1850-1960):
    -> Coyote expansion (mesopredator release)
    -> Deer overabundance (ungulate release)
    -> Streamside vegetation loss (overgrazing)
    -> Songbird decline (nest predation by coyotes)
  Wolf reintroduction (Yellowstone, 1995): partial reversal

  Lion/leopard decline in African savanna:
    -> Baboon population explosion (mesopredator release)
    -> Crop raiding (human-wildlife conflict)
    -> Infant mortality increase in nearby human villages
       (baboons raid stored food; disease transmission)

MARINE:
  Shark decline (~90% in North Atlantic; overfishing):
    -> Cownose ray explosion (shark prey)
    -> Scallop + clam collapse (ray prey)
    -> Scallop fishery failure (North Carolina 2004)

  Sea otter decline (fur trade + killer whale predation shift):
    -> Urchin explosion
    -> Kelp forest collapse ("urchin barrens")
    -> Total restructuring of kelp ecosystem
    -> Marine biodiversity loss cascading through all kelp-dependent species

FRESHWATER:
  Large piscivore removal:
    -> Planktivore fish increase
    -> Zooplankton decrease (eaten by planktivores)
    -> Phytoplankton increase (algal bloom)
    -> Eutrophication + anoxia
    -> Biomanipulation strategy: reintroduce piscivores to clear water

QUANTITATIVE:
  Ripple et al. (2014): 31 apex predator species in decline
  Consequences span: nutrient cycling, vegetation structure,
  fire regimes, carbon storage, disease regulation
```

## Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

```
HABITAT LOSS: THE PRIMARY DRIVER
====================================

EXTENT:
  ~50% of original vegetated habitat modified or destroyed by humans
  Tropical forests: ~50% original area remaining (with heavy degradation)
  Temperate grasslands: ~25% intact (most converted to agriculture)
  Wetlands: ~35% remaining; drained for agriculture
  Mangroves: ~36% lost since 1980
  Coral reefs: ~50% severely degraded

FRAGMENTATION EFFECTS (beyond simple area loss):
  Edge effects: altered microclimate; invasive species; predators
    penetrate from edge into interior
  Minimum patch size: many species require core interior habitat
  Isolation: colonization rate drops; genetic drift in small pops
  Matrix permeability: agriculture vs roads vs urban = different barriers

SPECIES-AREA RELATIONSHIP (see ornithology/08):
  S = cA^z
  z = 0.25-0.35 for islands; ~0.15-0.20 for habitat patches
  Consequence: 90% habitat loss -> ~50% species loss (for z=0.3)
  BUT: rate of extinction often LAGS habitat loss (extinction debt)

EXTINCTION DEBT:
  After fragmentation: some species persist temporarily
  Population too small -> stochastic extinction over decades-centuries
  Consequence: current biodiversity overestimates equilibrium diversity
  "Committed to extinction" even if habitat destruction stops now
  Estimated: hundreds of future extinctions already committed globally

ROADKILL AND LINEAR INFRASTRUCTURE:
  Roads: >1 million km paved; ~6.6 million km globally
  Direct mortality: ~1 billion vertebrate road-kills/year (US estimate)
  Barrier effect: even roads not crossed by traffic act as barriers
    (behavioral avoidance; genetic differentiation across roads)
  Railways, powerlines, fences: similar fragmentation effects
```

## Overexploitation

```
HUNTING AND BUSHMEAT
=======================

COMMERCIAL HUNTING HISTORY:
  Passenger pigeon (Ectopistes migratorius): 3-5 billion birds
    Flocks darkened the sky for hours; 1880-1900 commercial hunting
    Last wild individual: killed 1900; last captive (Martha): died 1914
    Extinction rate: <30 years from billions to zero
    -> Demonstrates: population size is not protection against rapid extinction

  North American bison: ~30-60 million; reduced to ~1000 by 1890
    Commercial hide hunting + government policy to eliminate Plains Indian food source
    Recovery to ~500,000 in managed herds (but mostly beefalo hybrids; narrow genetics)

  Great whales: Blue whale ~99% reduction; Fin ~75%; Sperm ~67%
    International Whaling Commission moratorium (1986)
    Recovery: variable; Blue whale still Endangered; some Right whale populations
    declining toward extinction (~450 North Atlantic Right whales remain)

BUSHMEAT CRISIS (Africa, Southeast Asia):
  Wildlife meat = significant protein source for millions of people
  Not sustainable at current commercial scale
  Particularly devastating for slow-reproducing species (great apes, elephants)
  Great ape decline linked strongly to bushmeat in West/Central Africa

LIVE WILDLIFE TRADE:
  Legal + illegal combined: ~$23 billion/year estimated
  Largest commodities: live animals (reptiles, birds, primates), skins, ivory, timber
  CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species, 1973):
    Appendix I: no commercial trade permitted (most Endangered species)
    Appendix II: trade controlled; permits required
    Appendix III: national request for cooperation
  Enforcement: weak in many range countries; demand-side reduction also required

SELECTIVE PRESSURE ON PHENOTYPE:
  Trophy hunting selects AGAINST large horns/tusks (the desired target)
  Elephant: declining average tusk size in populations hunted heavily
  Bighorn sheep: horn length declining in trophy-hunted populations
  -> Harvest-induced evolution: selection on heritable traits
     favoring small size/horns -> rapid phenotypic change
```

## Invasive Species

```
INVASIVE SPECIES AND ISLAND EXTINCTIONS
=========================================

SCALE:
  Invasive species: #2 driver of extinction globally (after habitat loss)
  #1 driver on islands (surpasses habitat loss as primary extinction cause on islands)
  ~42% of threatened species affected by invasive species

ISLAND BIODIVERSITY AND VULNERABILITY:
  Islands: ~17% of bird species; ~5% land area; ~90% of bird extinctions
  Island species vulnerabilities:
    No exposure to mainland predators -> no predator-avoidance behavior
    Often flightless or semi-flightless (energy saving when no predators)
    Small populations -> low genetic diversity
    Restricted range -> single introduction can affect entire population

INVASIVE PREDATORS:
  Rats (Rattus rattus, R. norvegicus, R. exulans):
    Spread by ships globally during European expansion
    Nest on ground or low vegetation -> seabird eggs + chicks
    Major cause of decline: petrels, shearwaters, albatrosses, rails
    Responsible for ~40% of bird and reptile extinctions since 1500

  Cats (Felis catus):
    Domestic + feral; present on >80% of island groups worldwide
    Estimated 1.3-4 billion birds killed/year in US alone (all cats)
    Islands: eradicated entire seabird colonies; small mammal extinctions

  Mongoose (Herpestes javanicus):
    Introduced Hawai'i + Caribbean + Pacific for rat control
    Did not control rats effectively (rats nocturnal; mongoose diurnal)
    Did devastate ground-nesting birds + native reptiles
    -> Classic example of poorly-planned biological control

  Brown tree snake (Boiga irregularis) -- Guam:
    Accidentally introduced post-WWII (probably via military cargo, ~1950)
    By 1980s: 9 of 12 native forest bird species extinct on Guam
    All native lizards extinct
    Most native bats extinct
    Population: up to 5,000 snakes/km2 at peak density
    Containment: intensive trapping + acetaminophen bait drops by air
    Prevention: strict biosecurity to prevent spread to other Pacific islands

INVASIVE HERBIVORES:
  Feral goats: extirpate vegetation on many oceanic islands
  Rabbits: overgrazing -> soil erosion -> elimination of burrowing birds
  European red deer (New Zealand): introduced; no natural predators
    -> Overgrazing of forest understory
    -> Restoration: aerial poison drops; reintroduction of predators (stoats now targeted)

INVASIVE PLANTS AND HABITAT TRANSFORMATION:
  Cheatgrass (Bromus tectorum): N. American intermountain West
    Annual grass; invades sagebrush steppe after disturbance
    Creates continuous fine fuel load -> more frequent fire
    Fire eliminates sagebrush (not fire-adapted) -> favors cheatgrass
    -> Self-reinforcing feedback loop: alters fire regime -> transforms ecosystem
    Greater sage-grouse: requires sagebrush; declining with cheatgrass spread

ISLAND RESTORATION — ERADICATION CAMPAIGNS:
  Rat eradication feasible on islands if small enough (now up to >10,000 ha)
  Method: aerial broadcast of brodifacoum bait (anticoagulant) in winter
  Success rate: >90% if well-planned + isolated island
  Recovery: seabird colonies return within 2-10 years
  Largest successful: South Georgia (5,675 km2): Rattus norvegicus eradicated 2015
    South Georgia pipit, South Georgia pintail recovering
    Seabird nesting colonies returning to historic sites
```

## Genetics in Conservation

```
CONSERVATION GENETICS
=======================

GENETIC DIVERSITY AND FITNESS:
  Small populations -> inbreeding -> inbreeding depression
  Mechanism: rare deleterious recessive alleles become homozygous
  Fitness consequences: reduced survival, fecundity, disease resistance
  Minimum Viable Population (MVP): rule of thumb ~500-5000 individuals
    for maintaining genetic diversity long-term (depends on Ne/N ratio)

  EFFECTIVE POPULATION SIZE (Ne):
    Ne < N in most populations (due to unequal sex ratios, variance in
    reproductive success, population size fluctuations)
    Ne/N typically 0.1-0.2
    -> Population of N=1000 may have Ne=100-200

  50/500 RULE (Franklin 1980):
    Ne > 50 to avoid short-term inbreeding depression
    Ne > 500 for long-term evolutionary potential (mutation-drift balance)
    Modern revision (Frankham): 100/1000 more appropriate

GENETIC RESCUE:
  Introduce individuals from another population -> reduce inbreeding
  Purebred Florida panther (Puma concolor coryi): severe inbreeding
    -> Kinked tail; heart defects; poor sperm quality; immune deficiency
    Texas cougars (closest relative) introduced (1995): 8 females
    -> Heterozygosity increased; kinks disappeared; population recovered
    Classic genetic rescue success

DELETERIOUS ALLELE PURGING:
  Long-term small population: purging of strongly deleterious alleles
  (homozygosity exposes them to selection -> eliminated)
  Benefit: reduced genetic load after purging
  Risk: purging also eliminates favorable alleles; drift + stochastic loss
  Net: purging does not reliably substitute for maintaining large Ne

GENOMICS IN CONSERVATION:
  RADseq, low-coverage whole genome: cheap genotyping of thousands of loci
  Applications:
    Population structure: identify distinct management units
    Adaptive variation: identify locally adapted genotypes for translocation
    Forensic identification: source of illegally traded animals
    Relatedness: pedigree reconstruction in captive populations
    Disease resistance loci: identify resistant individuals for breeding

ANCIENT DNA + CLONING:
  Woolly mammoth genome (>70% complete): de-extinction discussed
  Key barriers: surrogate species (Asian elephant), epigenetics,
    microbiome, development, ecological suitability
  "De-extinction" projects (Colossal Biosciences): mammoth + thylacine + dodo
  Debate: resources better spent on preventing current extinctions?
  More achievable: assisted evolution of domesticated species back to wild-type traits
```

## Protected Areas

```
PROTECTED AREA SYSTEMS
========================

IUCN CATEGORIES:
  Ia: Strict nature reserve (minimal human presence)
  Ib: Wilderness area (large, mostly undisturbed)
  II: National Park (ecosystem protection + recreation; tourism allowed)
  III: Natural monument
  IV: Habitat/species management area
  V: Protected landscape (human-natural mosaic)
  VI: Protected area with sustainable use

GLOBAL COVERAGE:
  Current: ~16.6% of terrestrial land; ~8% of ocean
  30x30 target: 30% of land and ocean protected by 2030 (Kunming-Montreal GBF 2022)
  Quality problem: many protected areas are "paper parks"
    (legally protected; minimal enforcement; ongoing degradation)

PROTECTED AREA DESIGN — SLOSS DEBATE:
  SLOSS: Single Large Or Several Small
  MacArthur-Wilson prediction: single large > several small (fewer extinctions)
  Counter: several small can protect different communities
  Modern synthesis: BOTH; large core areas + connectivity corridors

GAP ANALYSIS:
  Map threatened species ranges + current protected area network
  Identify "gaps": where species are underrepresented
  Priority areas (global biodiversity hotspots, Myers 2000):
    25 (now 36) regions with >1500 endemic plant species AND >70% habitat lost
    Cover ~2.4% of Earth's land surface; contain ~43% of vertebrate endemics
    Priority for protection investment

EFFECTIVENESS:
  Protected areas reduce deforestation significantly (vs unprotected land)
  But effectiveness varies with: governance quality, local community support,
    funding, enforcement, ecological connectivity
  "Fortress conservation" vs community-based conservation debate:
    Exclusion of indigenous communities from ancestral lands
    -> conflict; loss of traditional ecological knowledge
    -> Many conservation failures linked to exclusion model
  Co-management with indigenous communities: increasingly evidence-based standard
```

## Ex Situ Conservation and Breeding Programs

```
CAPTIVE BREEDING AND EX SITU CONSERVATION
===========================================

ROLE:
  Last resort when wild population below threshold for self-sustaining
  Bridge: maintain genetic diversity while in situ threats addressed
  Insurance: against catastrophic wild population loss
  Education: public engagement; behavioral research

MAJOR PROGRAMS:

California condor (Gymnogyps californianus):
  Wild population: 9 birds (1987); all captured for captive breeding
  Lead poisoning: from ingesting carcasses shot with lead ammunition
  Program: San Diego + LA Zoo; artificial incubation; puppet-reared to avoid imprinting
  1991: first reintroductions; current wild population: ~340+ birds (2024)
  Ongoing management: lead ammunition bans in condor range; veterinary monitoring
  -> Long-term threat remains: lead poisoning requires ongoing intervention

Arabian oryx (Oryx leucoryx):
  Wild extinction: 1972 (last wild individual shot)
  Captive population maintained: San Diego Zoo + others
  Reintroduction: 1982 (Oman); now ~1,000 wild individuals across range
  IUCN: delisted from Extinct in Wild; now Vulnerable
  -> First mammal to recover from wild extinction to self-sustaining population

Black-footed ferret (Mustela nigripes):
  Rediscovered 1981 after presumed extinct (18 individuals found)
  Captive breeding established
  Reintroduction across W. North America; prairie dog prey base required
  Current: ~300+ wild; disease (sylvatic plague) remains major threat
  Novel intervention: sylvatic plague vaccine distributed via peanut butter bait

GENETICS IN CAPTIVE BREEDING:
  Pedigree management: minimize inbreeding; maximize mean kinship
  Software: PMx, Sparks (Species Survival Plans)
  Mean kinship (MK): lower MK individual = more genetically valuable
    -> preferentially paired to maximize gene diversity retention
  Target: retain 90% of wild genetic diversity for 100 years
  Reality: gene diversity declines ~0.5-1%/generation in most programs

REINTRODUCTION BIOLOGY:
  Hard release: animals released with minimal post-release support
  Soft release: acclimatization pen; supplemental food; monitoring
  Failure modes: predator naive; food inexperience; social disruption
  Success predictors: adequate wild habitat; threat removed; sufficient numbers;
    genetic diversity; behavioral competence; long-term monitoring

GENOME RESOURCE BANKS:
  Frozen Zoo (San Diego): genetic material from >10,000 individuals; >1,000 taxa
  Cryopreserved: sperm, eggs, embryos, somatic cells, blood samples
  Long-term purpose: insurance against extinction; future reproductive technology
```

## Rewilding

```
REWILDING
==========

DEFINITION AND TYPES:
  PLEISTOCENE REWILDING (Donlan et al. 2006):
    Introduce proxies for Pleistocene megafauna (extinct since ~11,000 Mya)
    Rationale: restore ecological processes lost with megafauna
    Proposal: African megafauna (lions, cheetahs, elephants) to N. America
    Controversy: introduced exotics; ecological naivete about trophic context

  TROPHIC REWILDING (most implemented):
    Restore apex predators to reactivate trophic cascades
    Examples: wolf reintroduction (Yellowstone), lynx (Scotland proposal),
    Eurasian beaver (UK), sea eagle (UK)
    Target: restore self-regulating ecosystems with minimal intervention

  PASSIVE REWILDING:
    Remove human pressures; allow natural recovery
    Shrinking agricultural land in Europe: natural recolonization
    Netherlands (Oostvaardersplassen): large fenced reserve with Konik horses,
    Heck cattle, red deer -> landscape-level natural processes without predators

REWILDING EUROPE + NORTH AMERICA:
  Key projects:
    Pleistocene Park (Siberia, Zimov): large herbivores to restore mammoth steppe
      -> mammoth steppe: maintained permafrost; important for carbon storage
      -> Yakutian horses, bison, musk oxen introduced
    Iberian lynx: reintroduction (Spain + Portugal): 2002 (94 animals) ->
      2023 (~1,200 animals); fastest large cat recovery on record
    Eurasian beaver (UK): extirpated ~16th century; reintroduced River Tay (1995)
      Beavers create wetlands, slow floods, improve water quality, increase fish

BARRIERS TO REWILDING:
  Human-wildlife conflict (depredation; crop damage)
  Insufficient continuous habitat (landscape fragmentation)
  Regulatory and liability frameworks
  Public acceptability (fear of predators)
  Disease risk to livestock
  Political economy of land use
```

## Climate Change and Zoological Conservation

```
CLIMATE CHANGE IMPACTS ON ANIMALS
=====================================

RANGE SHIFTS:
  Species tracking suitable climate: move poleward or upslope
  Average: ~17 km/decade poleward; ~11 m/decade upslope
  Constraint: dispersal ability vs rate of climate change
    Mobile species (birds, large mammals): can track shift
    Slow-dispersing species (small mammals, reptiles, invertebrates): lag behind
  New species interactions: competitors + predators encounter naive species

PHENOLOGICAL MISMATCH:
  Consumer and food resource decoupled in timing
  Classic: great tit (Parus major) and caterpillar peak
    Caterpillar peak: 2 weeks earlier than 1970s
    Great tit laying date: advanced only 1 week
    -> Food peak missed -> chick survival decline
  Similar: caribou calving vs sedge plant emergence (timing diverging)
  Insect pollinator emergence vs flower timing (multiple documented mismatches)

CORAL BLEACHING:
  Zooxanthellae (symbiotic dinoflagellates) expelled when water too warm
  Bleached coral: white; still alive but nutritionally stressed
  If heat stress persists >4 weeks: coral dies
  Mass bleaching: 1998, 2002, 2016, 2017, 2020, 2022, 2024 (Great Barrier Reef)
  GBR: ~50% coral lost since 1995; 2016 event killed ~30% in one year
  1.5°C global warming: ~70-90% coral loss predicted
  2.0°C: >99% predicted loss
  -> Single most sensitive major ecosystem to climate change

SEA-LEVEL AND COASTAL SPECIES:
  Sea turtle nesting beaches: submerged or eroded
  Temperature-dependent sex determination: warming -> female-biased sex ratios
    GBR green sea turtles: northern beaches >99% female (extreme warming)
    Population risk: insufficient males for fertilization
  Coastal bird nesting: storm surge flooding of ground nests

ARCTIC MEGAFAUNA:
  Polar bear: sea ice platform for hunting ringed seals
    Declining ice extent + duration -> shorter hunting season -> reduced body condition
    Fasting duration extended; female reproductive success declining in some populations
  Walrus: use sea ice as haul-out; forced onto shore -> stampedes + injury
  Narwhal + beluga: highly specialized ice-associated; less flexible than some cetaceans

CONSERVATION RESPONSES:
  Assisted migration: move species to suitable future habitat
    (controversial: novel ecology; disease risk; public resistance)
  Climate-smart corridors: habitat corridors aligned with predicted shift directions
  Refugia identification: areas projected to maintain suitable climate (priority protection)
  Assisted evolution: selectively breed heat-tolerant coral genotypes (coral gardening)
```

## Conservation Success Stories

```
RECOVERY CASES AND WHAT MADE THEM WORK
=========================================

NORTHERN ELEPHANT SEAL (Mirounga angustirostris):
  Population bottleneck: ~20-50 individuals (1890s; hunted nearly to extinction)
  Recovery: hunting protection (Mexico; then US); population grew
  Current: ~250,000 individuals
  Genetic consequence: extreme genetic uniformity (population bottleneck preserved)
  -> functional recovery without full genetic diversity restoration

HUMPBACK WHALE (Megaptera novaeangliae):
  Population: ~90% reduction by 1966 from commercial whaling
  IWC moratorium (1986) + commercial whaling cessation
  Recovery: S. Atlantic population: 25,000+ (near pre-whaling levels)
  N. Atlantic: recovering but more slowly
  Recovery rate: ~10% per year (matches life history constraints)
  -> Shows: remove the threat, population can recover without active intervention

BALD EAGLE (Haliaeetus leucocephalus):
  Population crash: DDT -> eggshell thinning -> reproductive failure
  DDT banned (US, 1972): Rachel Carson's Silent Spring (1962) instrumental
  Legal protection: Endangered Species Act (1973)
  Recovery: 500 pairs (1963) -> 10,000+ pairs (2006 delisted)
  -> Chemical regulation + legal protection sufficient for recovery

AMERICAN ALLIGATOR (Alligator mississippiensis):
  Hunted commercially for hides; Endangered by 1960s
  Protected: Endangered Species Act (1973)
  Recovery: Endangered -> Least Concern by 1987 (14 years)
  Now: ~5 million; sustainable harvest programs active
  -> Fastest major US recovery; legal protection alone was sufficient

PRZEWALSKI'S HORSE (Equus ferus przewalskii):
  Extinct in wild by 1960s; captive population maintained in zoos
  Reintroduction: Mongolia (1992) + China
  Current: ~2,000 wild individuals across multiple populations
  Genetic challenge: all descended from 12 wild-caught founders + 1 domestic horse

GRAY WOLF (Canis lupus):
  Extirpated from most of lower 48 US by 1970
  ESA protection; reintroduction programs (Yellowstone: 1995; Southwest: 1998)
  Recovery: ~6,000 wolves in lower 48 (2023)
  Trophic effects (Yellowstone): documented (see zoology/08)
  Political conflict: ongoing; wolf management remains contentious

PATTERNS ACROSS SUCCESS STORIES:
  1. Threat removal (hunting ban / chemical regulation)
  2. Legal protection (national + international)
  3. Habitat protection (where needed)
  4. Active population management (where required)
  5. Long time horizon (decades; recovery not instant)
  6. Monitoring + adaptive management
  7. Political + social will (often hardest)
```

## Valuation and Ecosystem Services

```
ECONOMIC VALUATION OF BIODIVERSITY
=====================================

ECOSYSTEM SERVICES FRAMEWORK (MA 2005):
  Provisioning: food, water, timber, fiber, medicinal compounds
  Regulating: climate regulation, flood control, pollination, water purification
  Cultural: recreation, spiritual value, scientific knowledge, aesthetics
  Supporting: nutrient cycling, primary production, soil formation

BIODIVERSITY UNDERPINS ALL FOUR:
  Remove top predators: destabilize trophic structure -> service degradation
  Remove pollinators: crop yield collapse -> ~$577 billion/year globally
  Remove forest: loss of carbon storage, watershed regulation, local climate
  Reduce soil invertebrates: soil structure + nutrient cycling collapse

INSTRUMENTAL vs INTRINSIC VALUE:
  Instrumental (economic): species valuable because of services they provide
    Valuation: contingent valuation, hedonic pricing, travel cost methods
    Critique: underestimates existence value; misses option value; utilitarian framework
  Intrinsic (deontological): species have value independent of human utility
    Deep ecology (Naess): nature has inherent value; humans not privileged
    Critique: operationally difficult; doesn't translate to policy easily
  Practical policy usually combines both

ECONOMIC VALUATION EXAMPLES:
  Global ecosystem services: ~$125 trillion/year (Costanza et al. 2014)
    (vs global GDP ~$80 trillion)
  Coral reef: ~$375 billion/year in goods and services globally
  Tropical forest: ~$2,000-6,000/ha/year in ecosystem services
    (often exceeds deforestation income if priced correctly)

  Direct payment to landowners for maintaining ecosystems
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Problem | Mechanism | Key intervention | Time horizon |
|---------|-----------|------------------|--------------|
| Habitat loss | Area reduction -> extinction debt | Protect large core areas + corridors | Decades-centuries |
| Trophic downgrading | Apex predator loss -> cascade failure | Restore apex predators (rewilding) | 5-50 years |
| Island invasive predators | Rat/cat/snake prey on naive species | Eradication (brodifacoum aerial drop) | 1-10 years post-eradication |
| Hunting/overexploitation | Direct mortality > reproduction | Legal protection + enforcement + demand reduction | Decades |
| Inbreeding in small pop | Rare recessive alleles -> homozygous | Genetic rescue (translocation); captive breeding with pedigree management | Generations |
| Captive population management | Loss of genetic diversity per generation | Minimize mean kinship pairings; retain Ne > 500 | 100-year plans |
| Climate phenology mismatch | Consumer-resource timing decoupled | Assisted migration; refugia protection | Decades |
| Functional extinction | Too rare to fill ecological role | Increase population to functional density; not just survival | Decades |

## Common Confusion Points

**Extinction vs functional extinction**: A species can still exist on the IUCN Red List and be non-threatened in absolute numbers, but if population density is too low to perform its ecological function (large frugivore dispersing large seeds, apex predator suppressing mesopredators), it is "functionally extinct." This distinction matters for management: a population of 200 individuals spread across a vast landscape may be ecologically irrelevant even if not at extinction risk.

**Biodiversity loss rate estimates vary widely**: The "1000x background" extinction rate estimate involves significant methodological assumptions (which species to count, how to estimate the baseline, how to handle species not yet described). Estimates range from 100x to 10,000x background depending on method. The uncertainty doesn't undermine the conclusion — all credible estimates agree the rate is dramatically elevated — but precise numbers should be treated as order-of-magnitude estimates.

**Protected area percentage is not equivalent to conservation effectiveness**: 16% of land "protected" includes millions of km2 of "paper parks" with no enforcement, inadequate funding, legal access by extractive industries, and ongoing degradation. The quality, connectivity, and ecological representativeness of protected areas matters as much as total percentage. "30x30" is a coverage target, not a guarantee of effectiveness.

**Rewilding is not a rejection of management**: Rewilding aims to restore self-regulating ecological processes, reducing the need for continuous human intervention. But achieving that endpoint typically requires significant active management: predator reintroductions, invasive species control, corridor creation, conflict mitigation. "Hands-off" rewilding is the target state, not the implementation method.

**Genetic rescue doesn't always work**: Introducing individuals from a different population can increase heterozygosity and reduce inbreeding depression. But it can also introduce maladapted alleles (outbreeding depression) if the donor population is ecologically very different. Risk of outbreeding depression increases with genetic distance and ecological divergence. The Florida panther case worked because the donor population (Texas cougar) was the closest relative and divergence was limited.

**"Sustainable use" is empirically contested**: The argument that wildlife has higher value when exploited sustainably (hunting, wildlife tourism) than when protected for existence alone is often true in practice — it aligns conservation with local economic incentive. But "sustainable" is difficult to define and enforce; many "sustainable use" programs have not prevented population decline. The debate between protectionist and sustainable-use approaches runs through all major conservation policy disputes (trophy hunting, bushmeat regulation, shark finning).
