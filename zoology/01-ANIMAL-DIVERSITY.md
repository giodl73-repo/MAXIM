# Animal Diversity and Phylogeny

## The Big Picture

Animal phylogeny has been transformed by molecular data. The deep topology — Bilateria splitting into Protostomia and Deuterostomia; Ecdysozoa and Lophotrochozoa within Protostomia — was largely unknown before 1997 (Aguinaldo et al.). Most "classic" animal relationships based on morphology were wrong. The molecular tree is now well-resolved at the phylum level; within-phylum relationships and species-level systematics remain active research.

```
ANIMAL PHYLOGENY (molecular consensus)
=========================================

ANIMALIA
|
+-- Porifera (sponges) -- basal; debate: sister to all others or within animals
|
+-- Placozoa (Trichoplax: 3 spp; simplest animals)
|
+-- Ctenophora (comb jellies -- controversial: may be BASAL, not Cnidaria sister)
|
+-- Cnidaria (jellyfish, corals, anemones, hydrozoans)
|
+-- BILATERIA (~99% of animal species)
    |
    +-- PROTOSTOMIA
    |   |
    |   +-- LOPHOTROCHOZOA
    |   |   Mollusca, Annelida, Platyhelminthes, Brachiopoda, Nemertea,
    |   |   Phoronida, Bryozoa, Rotifera, Acanthocephala
    |   |
    |   +-- ECDYSOZOA (molt to grow)
    |       Arthropoda, Nematoda, Tardigrada, Kinorhyncha,
    |       Loricifera, Priapulida, Onychophora
    |
    +-- DEUTEROSTOMIA
        Echinodermata, Hemichordata, Chordata
        (+ Xenoturbella and some minor phyla)

CONTROVERSIAL NODES:
  Ctenophora: some analyses place as outgroup to all other animals
              ("Ctenophora-first" hypothesis) -- implications for nervous
              system evolution (evolved twice?)
  Acoela: position debated; possibly early-diverging bilaterian
  Within Ecdysozoa: arthropod internal topology (complete metamorphosis origin)
```

## Porifera: Sponges

```
PORIFERA (~10,000 SPP)
========================

BODY PLAN:
  No true tissues (no ectoderm/endoderm layers)
  Choanocytes (flagellated collar cells): create water current + filter bacteria
  Pinacocytes: outer covering
  Amoebocytes: distribute nutrients; produce spicules
  Spicules: support structure (silica OR calcium carbonate)
  Spongin: protein fibers; bath sponge is spongin skeleton without cells

CLASSES:
  Calcarea: calcium carbonate spicules
  Demospongiae: spongin + silica; most species (~80%)
  Hexactinellida (glass sponges): silica; deep sea; fused spicule skeleton
  Homoscleromorpha: recently separated; have basement membrane (tissue-like)

ECOLOGY:
  Filter feeders: can process 100x their volume/day
  Reef builders (some Sclerospongia): bioerosion + carbonate deposition
  Symbiosis: harbor ~50% body mass as microbes (bacteria, archaea, cyanobacteria)
  Chemical ecology: produce diverse secondary metabolites
    -> anti-fouling, anti-predator, antimicrobial
    -> source of pharmaceutical leads (cyclosporin-related, discodermolide)
```

## Cnidaria: The Radially Symmetric Predators

```
CNIDARIA (~10,000 SPP)
========================

DIAGNOSTIC:
  Cnidocytes (stinging cells with nematocysts): unique to Cnidaria
  Two tissue layers (ectoderm + endoderm = diploblastic)
  Radial symmetry
  Gastrovascular cavity (single opening; mouth = anus)

TWO BODY FORMS (often alternate in life cycle):
  POLYP: sessile; mouth up; tubular body; tentacles around mouth
  MEDUSA: planktonic; mouth down; bell-shaped; tentacles hanging

CLASSES:
  Hydrozoa:
    Hydra (freshwater; no medusa alternation); Obelia (colonial)
    Physalia (Portuguese man-o'-war): colonial; specialized zooids
  Scyphozoa (true jellyfish):
    Aurelia, Cyanea; dominant medusa stage
    Chrysaora fuscescens: Pacific sea nettle (bloom-former)
  Cubozoa (box jellyfish):
    Chironex fleckeri: most venomous marine animal; ocelli eyes
    Complex eyes (24 eyes in 4 types); sophisticated behavior for Cnidarian
  Anthozoa (no medusa; polyp only):
    Sea anemones, corals, sea pens
    Reef-building corals (Scleractinia): symbiosis with zooxanthellae (Symbiodinium)

CNIDOCYTE MECHANISM:
  Nematocyst: coiled thread under high osmotic pressure
  Trigger: mechanoreceptor (cnidocil)
  Discharge: ~3 microseconds; ~40,000g force
  Thread penetrates prey cuticle; venom injected
  Once discharged: cannot re-coil; replaced by new cnidocyte
```

## Lophotrochozoa

### Mollusca

```
MOLLUSCA (~100,000 SPP)
========================

BODY PLAN (ancestral):
  Head (sensory), Visceral mass (organs), Foot (locomotion)
  Mantle: secretes shell (in shelled taxa)
  Mantle cavity: houses gills (ctenidia) + opens to exterior
  Radula: toothed feeding ribbon (absent in Bivalvia)

MAJOR CLASSES:
  Polyplacophora (chitons, ~1,000 spp):
    8-plate shell; marine; graze algae; radula with magnetite teeth
  Gastropoda (~65,000 spp):
    Snails, slugs, nudibranchs
    Torsion: ~180 degree twist of visceral mass during development
    Opisthobranchiate: secondary untorsion in some; nudibranch (shell lost)
  Bivalvia (~20,000 spp):
    Clams, oysters, mussels, scallops; no head; no radula
    Filter feeding; two valves (hence bivalve)
    Scallop: clap valves -> jet propulsion; many compound eyes on mantle edge
  Cephalopoda (~800 spp):
    Octopus, squid, cuttlefish, nautilus
    Shell internal (squid) or absent (octopus) or external (Nautilus)
    Highly derived: closed circulatory system; complex nervous system
    Chromatophores + iridophores: rapid color change (cephalopod skin = display + camo)
    Giant squid (Architeuthis): largest invertebrate (~13m)
    Colossal squid (Mesonychoteuthis): largest eyes in animal kingdom (30cm)

CEPHALOPOD INTELLIGENCE:
  Octopus: distributed CNS (2/3 neurons in arms); short-term + long-term memory
  Problem solving: open jars; navigate mazes; play
  Not social -> intelligence evolved without sociality (unusual)
  Color blind but produce complex skin color patterns
    (cryptochrome in skin may detect light directly -- hypothesis)
```

### Annelida

```
ANNELIDA (~22,000 SPP)
========================

DIAGNOSTIC:
  Metamerism: true body segmentation (each segment is repeat unit)
  Coelom: true (within mesoderm)
  Closed circulatory system (most)
  Chaetae (setae): chitinous bristles

CLASSES:
  Polychaeta (~13,000 spp): marine; parapodia (paddle-like appendages); diverse
    Tube worms (Sabella, Spirobranchus): sessile; filter feed; elaborate radioles
    Scale worms (Polynoidae): dorsal scales; predatory
    Ragworms (Nereis): errant; burrowing; predatory
    Palolo worm (Eunice viridis): reproductive swarming; epitoky
  Oligochaeta (~3,500 spp): earthworms, freshwater worms
    Lumbricus terrestris: bioturbation; Darwin studied earthworms extensively
    No parapodia; few chaetae; hermaphroditic
  Hirudinea (leeches, ~700 spp):
    No chaetae; suckers; ectoparasites + predators
    Hirudo medicinalis: medical leech; hirudin anticoagulant
    Medicinal use: reattachment surgery (venous congestion)
```

### Platyhelminthes (Flatworms)

```
PLATYHELMINTHES (~20,000 SPP)
==============================

BODY PLAN:
  Acoelomate (no body cavity)
  Bilaterally symmetric
  No circulatory system: diffusion limits body to flat/thin shape
  Gastrovascular cavity with single opening (in most)
  No anus

CLASSES:
  Turbellaria (free-living flatworms, ~4,000 spp):
    Planaria: regeneration; classic experimental organism
    Marine polyclads: colorful; diverse
  Trematoda (flukes, ~11,000 spp):
    Parasites; complex life cycles (multiple hosts)
    Schistosoma: blood flukes; schistosomiasis (~200M infected)
    Fasciola hepatica: liver fluke; livestock parasite
  Cestoda (tapeworms, ~5,000 spp):
    Intestinal parasites; no digestive system (absorb through tegument)
    Proglottids: reproductive segments; shed with eggs
    Taenia solium: pork tapeworm; cysticercosis (brain: seizures)
    Largest: Ligula intestinalis: up to 3m; some tapeworms to 20m
```

## Ecdysozoa

### Nematoda

```
NEMATODA (~25,000 DESCRIBED; ~1M ESTIMATED)
=============================================

BODY PLAN:
  Pseudocoelomate (body cavity not lined with mesoderm)
  Round in cross-section (hence "roundworms")
  Cuticle (collagen-based; molted 4x in life cycle)
  No circulatory system (diffusion in small diameter)
  Hydrostatic skeleton (pressurized body fluid)
  Fixed cell number (eutelic): C. elegans has exactly 959 somatic cells

DIVERSITY:
  Free-living: soil + marine sediments; 10M/L in rich soil
  Parasitic plants: Meloidogyne (root-knot nematode); major crop pest
  Parasitic animals: Ascaris, hookworm, Wuchereria, Onchocerca
  C. elegans: premier model organism (see below)

Caenorhabditis elegans MODEL ORGANISM:
  302 neurons (entire connectome mapped, 1986 White et al.)
  959 cells (adult hermaphrodite); entire cell lineage known
  First metazoan genome sequenced (1998)
  Transparent body: live imaging of all cells + divisions
  3-day generation; easy genetics; RNAi functional genomics
  Nobel prizes: apoptosis (Brenner, Horvitz, Sulston 2002)
                RNA interference (Fire, Mello 2006)
```

### Arthropoda

```
ARTHROPODA (~1,000,000+ SPP) -- 80% OF ALL ANIMALS
=====================================================

DIAGNOSTIC:
  Exoskeleton (cuticle; chitin + protein)
  Jointed appendages (arthr = joint, poda = feet)
  Segmented body (tagmosis)
  Coelom reduced; hemocoel (open circulatory system)
  Ventral nerve cord

MAJOR SUBPHYLA:
  Chelicerata: Arachnida (spiders, scorpions, mites, ticks);
               Merostomata (horseshoe crabs); ~80,000 spp
  Myriapoda: Chilopoda (centipedes), Diplopoda (millipedes); ~16,000 spp
  Crustacea (paraphyletic with Hexapoda):
    Malacostraca (crabs, lobsters, shrimp, isopods, amphipods); ~40,000 spp
    Copepoda: most numerous multicellular animals on Earth (marine zooplankton)
    Branchiopoda (fairy shrimp, Daphnia), Barnacles (Cirripedia)
  Hexapoda: ~1,000,000 spp (see entomology/ directory)

Arthropoda dominance:
  ~80% of all described animal species
  Ecological keystone functions: pollination, decomposition, predation, prey
```

## Deuterostomia

### Echinodermata

```
ECHINODERMATA (~7,000 SPP)
============================

UNIQUE FEATURES:
  Pentaradial symmetry (5-fold) in adults; bilateral as larvae
  Water vascular system: hydraulic tube feet for locomotion + feeding
  Dermal endoskeleton (calcium carbonate ossicles)
  Mutable collagenous tissues (change stiffness by neural command)
    -> rapid stiffening for defense; loosening for autotomy

CLASSES:
  Asteroidea (sea stars): central disc + arms; can regenerate whole body from arm
    Predation on bivalves: evert stomach; digest prey outside body
  Ophiuroidea (brittle stars): arms sharply delimited from disc; rapid locomotion
  Echinoidea (sea urchins, sand dollars): globular/flattened; spiny
    Strongylocentrotus purpuratus: model organism for development
  Holothuroidea (sea cucumbers): elongated; deposit feeders; eviscerate predators
  Crinoidea (sea lilies): most ancient form; sessile (mostly); filter feed

ECHINODERM-CHORDATE RELATIONSHIP:
  Deuterostome development shared with Chordata
  Pharyngeal slits: shared with Hemichordata -> indicates common ancestor
  Echinoderm larvae: bilateral + coelomate -> link to bilaterian ancestor
```

### Chordata

```
CHORDATA (~65,000 SPP)
========================

PHYLUM DIAGNOSTIC (4 shared characters at some stage):
  1. Notochord: flexible rod; replaced by vertebral column in vertebrates
  2. Dorsal hollow nerve cord
  3. Pharyngeal slits (gill slits)
  4. Post-anal tail

SUBPHYLA:
  Urochordata (tunicates): adult loses all chordate characters
    Larva: all 4 characters present
    Adult: sessile; filter feeding; only tunic + pharyngeal slits remain
    ~3,000 spp; most abundant in some marine communities

  Cephalochordata (lancelets, Amphioxus):
    All 4 characters retained in adult
    ~30 spp; shallow marine sediments
    Segmented; filter feeding; no brain
    Model for ancestral chordate body plan

  Vertebrata:
    Notochord replaced by vertebral column (with exceptions)
    Skull + cranium; neural crest cells
    ~58,000 spp; see zoology/00-OVERVIEW.md for vertebrate diversity
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Primitive" vs "basal"**: Sponges are not "primitive" animals — they are fully adapted to their ecological niches and have been for ~600 Mya. "Basal" means they diverged early from the animal stem. They have sophisticated cell biology (including some gene families thought absent in non-bilaterian animals).

**Ctenophora-first controversy**: The traditional view placed ctenophores as sister to cnidarians (together called "Radiata"). Molecular analyses (2013+) suggest ctenophores may be the MOST basally-diverging animals (Dunn et al., Ryan et al.). If true: nervous systems either evolved twice (ctenophore + all other neural animals) or were lost in sponges. Both interpretations have profound evolutionary implications and the debate is ongoing.

**Arthropoda paraphyly of "Crustacea"**: Crustaceans as traditionally defined are paraphyletic with respect to Hexapoda (insects). Insects are a derived crustacean lineage (specifically, closest to Remipedia). The correct clade is Pancrustacea (insects + crustaceans). "Crustacean" is still used informally but is not monophyletic.

**C. elegans is not representative of Nematoda**: C. elegans is a soil bacterivore. Most nematodes are not like it. Parasitic nematodes (Strongylida, Trichinella, filarial worms) have completely different biology. C. elegans is a model for cell biology and development; extrapolating to nematode biology generally requires caution.

**Echinoderm radial symmetry is secondary**: Echinoderm adults have pentaradial symmetry. Their larvae are bilaterally symmetric. The radial symmetry evolved secondarily within Bilateria from a bilateral ancestor — confirmed by larval symmetry and phylogenetic position within Deuterostomia.
