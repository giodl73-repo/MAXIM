# Body Plans: Symmetry, Segmentation, Coelom

## The Big Picture

Animal body plans are defined by a small number of architectural variables: symmetry type, tissue layers (grade), body cavity type, and segmentation pattern. These are not arbitrary — they represent solutions to the mechanical and physiological problems of being a multicellular heterotroph. The deep convergences (multiple independent origins of eyes, limbs, segmentation) and the constraints (all large aquatic bilaterians need a coelom) reveal the physics of animal design.

```
BODY PLAN AXES
===============

SYMMETRY AXIS:
  Asymmetric -> Spherical -> Radial -> Bilateral
  (Porifera)   (early dev)  (Cnidaria)  (most animals)

TISSUE GRADE:
  No true tissues -> Diploblastic (2 layers) -> Triploblastic (3 layers)
  (Porifera)         (Cnidaria, Ctenophora)    (Bilateria)

BODY CAVITY:
  Acoelomate -> Pseudocoelomate -> Eucoelomate
  (no cavity)   (cavity not lined)  (true coelom)
  Flatworms     Nematodes           Annelids, Arthropods,
                Rotifers            Molluscs, Vertebrates

SEGMENTATION:
  Unsegmented -> Segmented (repeat units)
  Most animals   Annelida, Arthropoda, Chordata (convergent?)
```

## Symmetry

### Radial Symmetry

```
RADIAL SYMMETRY
================

DEFINITION: body organized around central axis;
            multiple planes of symmetry through that axis

  Top view:
        *
      * | *
     *--+--*
      * | *
        *
  Any cut through center -> mirror halves

ORGANISMS: Cnidaria, Ctenophora, adult Echinodermata
CORRELATES:
  Sessile or slow-moving lifestyle
  Environmental stimulus can come from any direction equally likely
  Food capture (tentacles) works in any direction
  No defined "front" or "back" needed
  Radial animals: not directional movers; respond to stimuli in plane

EXCEPTIONS:
  Echinoderms: pentaradial (5-fold) NOT full radial
  Bilateral larvae of echinoderms -> radial adults
    = secondary evolution of radial symmetry within Bilateria
  Sea anemones: 6-fold or 8-fold (hexamerous, octomerous)
```

### Bilateral Symmetry

```
BILATERAL SYMMETRY
===================

DEFINITION: single plane of symmetry (sagittal); left-right mirror
            (dorsal-ventral axis different from left-right axis)

  Side view:
  +---[head]---[thorax]---[abdomen]---[tail]---+
  (one plane cuts into mirror halves)

CORRELATES:
  Directed movement (head leads; anterior orientation)
  Cephalization: sensory organs + nervous tissue concentrated anteriorly
    (head = data-processing + sensory concentration)
  Defined dorsal-ventral polarity (gravity-related)
  Efficient locomotion: bilateral is optimal for forward movement

PHYLOGENETIC SIGNAL:
  All Bilateria share bilateral symmetry at some life stage
  (~99% of all animal species)
  Blastopore positioning, Hox gene domains, left-right asymmetry mechanisms
  all conserved across Bilateria

LEFT-RIGHT ASYMMETRY within bilateral plan:
  Most bilaterians have internal asymmetry
  Human heart: left; liver: right; stomach: left
  Nodal cilia: rotate unidirectionally -> leftward flow -> asymmetric gene expression
  Nodal/Lefty/Pitx2 signaling: conserved L-R axis determination in vertebrates
  Mutations: situs inversus totalis (complete mirror image -- benign)
             situs ambiguus (heterotaxy -- often heart defects)
```

## Germ Layers: Tissue Architecture

```
GERM LAYER EVOLUTION
=====================

DIPLOBLASTIC (2 germ layers):
  Ectoderm: outer layer
  Endoderm: inner layer (gastrovascular cavity)
  MESOGLEA: between ecto and endoderm; not a true mesoderm layer
    In Cnidaria: gelatinous, largely acellular
    Limited: no true organs; no muscle (but myoepithelial cells)
  Animals: Cnidaria, Ctenophora

TRIPLOBLASTIC (3 germ layers):
  Ectoderm -> skin, nervous system, sense organs
  Mesoderm -> muscles, skeleton, circulatory system, gonads
  Endoderm -> gut epithelium, digestive glands, respiratory epithelium
  Animals: all Bilateria

MESODERM IS THE KEY INNOVATION:
  True muscles: from mesoderm (not myoepithelial)
  Gonad tissue: mesodermal origin
  Body cavity (coelom): mesodermal-lined space
  Circulatory system: mesoderm-derived
  Coelom allows:
    Fluid circulation without circulatory system (small animals)
    Hydrostatic skeleton for movement
    Organ development in protected space
    Organ independence (organs can move within coelom)
```

## The Coelom

```
COELOM TYPES
=============

DEFINITION: body cavity lined with mesoderm (peritoneum)
  -> Tissues + organs can be suspended in fluid
  -> Organs can develop and move independently
  -> Fluid provides internal "environment" and hydrostatic skeleton

ACOELOMATE:
  No body cavity; organs embedded in solid tissue
  Animals: Platyhelminthes, Acoela
  Limitation: flat body (diffusion limited); limited organ development
  +---[body wall]---[solid parenchyma]---[gut]---+

PSEUDOCOELOMATE:
  Body cavity present but NOT lined with mesoderm
  Cavity: former blastocoel (embryonic cavity) persists
  Animals: Nematoda, Rotifera, Gastrotricha
  +---[body wall]---[pseudocoelom = blastocoel]---[gut]---+
  Advantage over acoelomate: fluid-filled cavity + hydrostatic skeleton
  Limitation: no mesodermal lining = no peritoneum = no organ suspension

EUCOELOMATE (TRUE COELOM):
  Body cavity lined with mesoderm (coelomic epithelium/peritoneum)
  Animals: Annelida, Mollusca, Arthropoda, Echinodermata, Chordata

  Two origins:
  Schizocoely (Protostomes): cavity forms by splitting in solid mesoderm
    Annelida, Arthropoda, Mollusca
  Enterocoely (Deuterostomes): outpocketings from gut wall
    Echinodermata, Chordata
```

### Why the Coelom Matters

```
FUNCTIONAL SIGNIFICANCE OF THE COELOM
========================================

1. INTERNAL HYDROSTATIC SKELETON:
   Fluid in coelom under pressure
   Antagonistic muscles work against it
   Earthworm locomotion: circular muscles (elongate) vs
                         longitudinal muscles (shorten)
   Without coelom: can't generate directed movement this way

2. ORGAN DEVELOPMENT:
   Kidneys (nephridia): develop in coelomic epithelium
   Gonads: develop in coelomic epithelium
   Heart: pericardia derived from coelomic space
   Protected space: organs can enlarge without compressing others

3. CIRCULATORY FUNCTION (secondary):
   In annelids: coelomic fluid transports O2 + nutrients
   Later replaced by closed circulatory system in vertebrates

4. SHOCK ABSORPTION:
   Fluid-filled space absorbs mechanical shock to organs
   More important in larger animals

REDUCTION IN ARTHROPODS:
  Arthropods have true coelom in embryo, but it is largely
  reduced during development to a hemocoel (open blood space)
  Functional blood cavity (hemocoel) replaces most of true coelom
  Exception: gonads + nephridia are true coelomic derivatives
```

## Segmentation

### Three Independent Origins

```
SEGMENTATION IN ANIMALS
=========================

DEFINITION: Serial repetition of body units (segments/metameres)
            Each segment is a repeat of basic unit (usually modified)

THREE GROUPS WITH SEGMENTATION:
  Annelida:     first evolved? deep; segment = metamere
  Arthropoda:   derived from annelid-like ancestor? (Cambrian Hallucigenia)
                Onychophora (velvet worms) = transitional?
  Chordata:     segmented: vertebrae, somites, pharyngeal arches
                INDEPENDENTLY of annelid/arthropod origin?

MOLECULAR EVIDENCE:
  Segmentation controlled by different molecular pathways in each group:
  Arthropod segmentation: pair-rule genes (eve, ftz) + segment polarity (en, wg)
    -> anterior-posterior defined by gap + pair-rule cascade
  Vertebrate segmentation: Notch/Delta oscillator (segmentation clock)
    -> somites laid down sequentially from anterior to posterior
  Annelid: different developmental genetics
  CONCLUSION: segmentation is CONVERGENT (homoplasy) at deep level
    Despite using some of same gene families (Hox genes)
```

### Tagmosis

```
TAGMOSIS: SEGMENT SPECIALIZATION
===================================

DEFINITION: evolution of different functions in different segments
            -> fusion of segments into functional groups (tagmata)

ARTHROPOD TAGMOSIS (examples):
  Crustacea (crab):
    Cephalothorax (head + thorax fused): feeding + locomotion
    Abdomen: reproductive + vestigial
  Insecta:
    Head (feeding + sensory)
    Thorax (locomotion -- legs + wings)
    Abdomen (viscera + reproduction)
  Chelicerata (spider):
    Prosoma (cephalothorax): legs + mouthparts
    Opisthosoma (abdomen): silk glands + viscera

  +------------------------------------------+
  |  ANCESTRAL:  uniform segments            |
  |  [S1][S2][S3][S4][S5][S6]...             |
  |                                          |
  |  TAGMOSIS:   specialized regions         |
  |  [HEAD: S1-6][THORAX: S7-13][ABD: S14+]  |
  +------------------------------------------+

VERTEBRATE TAGMOSIS (analogous):
  Cervical vertebrae: neck (rotation + flexibility)
  Thoracic: rib attachment + breathing
  Lumbar: power + flexibility (load bearing)
  Sacral: hip attachment
  Caudal: tail (variable; often reduced in humans)
```

## Body Wall and Support Mechanisms

```
BODY SUPPORT MECHANISMS
=========================

HYDROSTATIC SKELETON:
  Internal fluid under pressure
  Muscles deform shape; fluid transmits force
  Organisms: annelids, nematodes, cnidarians, sea cucumbers
  Advantage: no rigid framework; can change shape
  Limitation: confined to smaller sizes OR needs large coelomic fluid volume

EXOSKELETON:
  External rigid covering (cuticle, shell, carapace)
  Growth requires molting (ecdysis) or addition of material
  Arthropods: chitin-protein; molted
  Molluscs (shell): calcium carbonate; added at edge; not molted
  Advantage: protection; muscle attachment; waterproofing
  Limitation: weight; growth restriction

ENDOSKELETON:
  Internal mineralized structure
  Echinoderms: calcite ossicles in mesoderm (not internal in same sense as vertebrates)
  Vertebrates: bone (calcium phosphate hydroxyapatite + collagen) or cartilage
  Advantage: internal support allows large body sizes; continues to grow
  Growth: remodeling; not requiring molt
  Limitation: heavy (but density close to muscle)

COMPARISON TABLE:
+--------------------+----------+--------+--------+
| Attribute          | Hydrost  | Exo    | Endo   |
+--------------------+----------+--------+--------+
| Max body size      | Small    | Medium | Large  |
| Protection         | Poor     | High   | Low    |
| Growth mode        | Continuous| Molt  | Remodel|
| Metabolic cost     | Low      | Medium | Higher |
| Flexibility        | High     | Low    | Medium |
+--------------------+----------+--------+--------+
```

## Hox Genes and Body Plan Specification

```
HOX GENES AND BODY PLAN
=========================

HOX GENES: transcription factors; specify regional identity along A-P axis
  First discovered in Drosophila (homeotic mutations -- legs where antennae)
  Homeobox: 180 bp conserved DNA-binding domain

CONSERVATION:
  Drosophila Hox cluster -> vertebrate Hox cluster: homologous positions
  Vertebrates: 4 Hox clusters (Hoxa, Hoxb, Hoxc, Hoxd; duplication events)
  Cnidarians: simplified Hox; bilaterian-like Hox present
  Spatial colinearity: 5' -> 3' position in cluster = posterior -> anterior expression

  Drosophila: lab pb Dfd Scr Antp Ubx abd-A Abd-B
  Vertebrate:  1   2  3   4   5-8  9  10-11  12-13 (paralog groups)

HOMEOTIC TRANSFORMATION:
  Express thorax Hox gene in leg segment -> leg with thorax identity
  Vertebrate equivalent: Hox-A2 knockout -> jaw elements duplicate atlas
  -> Hox genes specify REGIONAL IDENTITY; not structures directly

DEEP HOMOLOGY:
  Pax6 specifies eye development in flies AND vertebrates
    -> "master control gene" for eye; independent evolution?
    -> OR both evolved from a common Pax6-expressing ancestor with simple eye
  Tinman/Nkx2-5 specifies heart/dorsal vessel in flies and vertebrates
  Evidence: same genes used for analogous structures -> deep developmental homology
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Coelom vs pseudocoelom**: Both are fluid-filled body cavities. The distinction is whether mesoderm lines the cavity (eucoelom: yes) or not (pseudocoelom: remnant blastocoel). Functionally similar; evolutionarily and embryologically different. The coelom allows true organ suspension and independent development.

**Segmentation is convergent**: Despite using some of the same gene families (Wnt, Notch, Hox), segmentation in Annelida, Arthropoda, and Chordata evolved independently. The molecular mechanisms are different enough (pair-rule cascade vs segmentation clock) to indicate independent evolution, not deep homology.

**Arthropod coelom is "lost"**: Arthropods have a true coelom in their embryos, but it is largely replaced during development by the hemocoel. The hemocoel is not a true coelom — it's a modified open circulatory space. The actual coelomic derivatives (gonads, nephridia) are small remnants.

**Radial echinoderm symmetry is secondary**: Adult echinoderms have pentaradial symmetry; their larvae are bilateral. Phylogenetically, echinoderms are within Bilateria (Deuterostomia). Their radial symmetry is a secondary evolutionary acquisition from a bilateral ancestor, NOT a primitive state.

**Deep homology vs analogy**: Pax6 specifies eyes in both flies and vertebrates. Does this mean fly eyes and vertebrate eyes are homologous? Not necessarily — the eyes themselves may have evolved independently, both co-opting the same ancient regulatory gene. This is "deep homology" — conserved gene network, independently deployed morphology.
