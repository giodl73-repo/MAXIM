# 11 — The Art Market

## The Big Picture

```
THE ART MARKET: STRUCTURAL OVERVIEW
=====================================

  WHO MAKES ART:  Artists
        |
        v
  WHO ENABLES IT: Dealers (primary market) — first sale
                  Auction houses (secondary market) — resale
                  Galleries (display, relationship-building)
                  Fairs (TEFAF, Art Basel, Frieze) — discovery
                  Critics (discourse that creates value)
                  Curators (institutional validation)
        |
        v
  WHO BUYS:       Collectors (private)
                  Museums (public and private, non-profit)
                  Corporations (prestige holdings)
                  Investors (financial return)
        |
        v
  WHERE IT ENDS:  Museum (public, permanent)
                  Private collection (may or may not be seen)
                  Freeport storage (tax-advantaged never-seen)
                  Auction (back to market)
                  Donation (tax write-off)

  MARKET SIZE (~2023):
  Global art market: ~$65 billion per year
  US: ~44% share
  China: ~18%
  UK: ~17%
  Top 10 artists: account for ~40% of auction turnover
  (extreme concentration at the top)
```

## Patronage Arc: From Church to Global Market

### Medieval: Guild + Church System

```
MEDIEVAL PATRONAGE SYSTEM
============================

  PATRON: Church (bishops, abbots, cathedral chapters)
           Guilds (Wool Guild commissioned Ghiberti's
           bronze doors; Cloth Guild funded church chapels)
           Private noble families (family chapels in churches)

  MECHANISM:
  - Artist = craftsman in guild
  - Contract specifies: subject, size, materials (especially
    gold, ultramarine lapis lazuli -- the expensive ones
    specified and paid for separately)
  - Iconographic program set by patron/clergy
  - Artist had limited input on subject or composition
  - No signature, often; the guild guaranteed the work

  WORKSHOP SYSTEM:
  Master → Journeymen → Apprentices
  The master takes the commission; apprentices do
  grounds, drapery backgrounds; master does faces,
  hands, the critical sections
  Training: 7-10 years before independent practice
  This is exactly the senior-engineer mentorship model --
  the journeyman years build skill before autonomy
```

### Renaissance and Baroque: Medici, Papal, Royal Courts

The Medici banking dynasty (Florence, c. 1430–1492) established the model of
secular private patronage organized around cultural prestige:

- **Cosimo de' Medici**: piety + political legitimacy through art (San Lorenzo,
  San Marco library); de facto ruler through economic power, not title
- **Lorenzo il Magnifico**: the artist as intellectual peer; Botticelli's mythological
  subjects for private spaces (Neoplatonist programs, not religious)
- **The papal court**: Julius II, Leo X — art as institutional power; the Vatican's
  image under construction by the greatest artists of the generation

The Baroque artist as courtier-diplomat (Rubens, Velázquez) is the peak of this model.

### The Royal Academy System (18th–19th c.)

```
ROYAL ACADEMY SYSTEM (England, France, Spain)
===============================================

  STRUCTURE:
  - State-chartered academy of art
  - England: Royal Academy of Arts (1768, Joshua Reynolds)
  - France: Académie Royale de Peinture et de Sculpture (1648)

  FUNCTION:
  - Defines what counts as art (the genre hierarchy)
  - Controls training (the Schools)
  - Controls exhibition access (the Salon / Summer Exhibition)
  - Controls public commissions and awards
  - Controls criticism (the Academy sets the standards)

  HOW TO SUCCEED:
  1. Train in the Academy schools
  2. Study in Rome (prix de Rome fellowship)
  3. Submit to the annual exhibition
  4. Get accepted, then praised, then decorated
  5. Become an academician -- sit on juries
  6. Award yourself commissions

  THE CLOSED LOOP:
  The jury that accepts or rejects is composed of
  academicians who have succeeded by the system's rules.
  They tend to perpetuate the rules that elevated them.
  Genre hierarchy: history painting > portrait > genre >
  landscape > still life. Non-academicians had no
  path to major commissions.

  BREAKDOWN:
  - Salon des Refusés (1863): Napoleon III orders
    exhibition of rejected works -- the system begins
    to crack
  - Dealer-critic system emerges: alternative path
    to market and reputation outside the Academy
```

### The Dealer-Critic System: Durand-Ruel and the Impressionists

Paul Durand-Ruel (1831–1922) is the pivotal figure in the transition from the
Academy system to the modern art market:

```
DURAND-RUEL'S STRATEGY
========================

  SITUATION (1870s):
  - The Impressionists are rejected by the Salon
  - Without Salon success, no buyers
  - Durand-Ruel begins buying their work speculatively

  THE MODEL:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. EXCLUSIVE RELATIONSHIPS:                              │
  │    Buy entire studio contents from artists;              │
  │    guarantee income in exchange for exclusivity          │
  │    Artist's financial security = productive output       │
  │                                                          │
  │ 2. PRICE MAINTENANCE:                                    │
  │    Do not sell below a floor price even under pressure   │
  │    If you sell cheap in a down market, you establish     │
  │    the low price as the correct price                    │
  │                                                          │
  │ 3. CRITICAL APPARATUS:                                   │
  │    Commission favorable reviews; fund exhibitions;       │
  │    build the narrative that makes the work significant   │
  │                                                          │
  │ 4. INTERNATIONAL EXPANSION:                              │
  │    New York exhibitions (1886): the Impressionists       │
  │    broke in America before they were fully accepted      │
  │    in France. American robber-baron collectors were      │
  │    the first large buyers.                               │
  │                                                          │
  │ 5. LONG PATIENCE:                                        │
  │    Durand-Ruel held Monet and Renoir inventory for       │
  │    20+ years before the market moved                     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  RESULT: The dealer-critic system replaces the Academy
  as the primary mechanism for establishing artistic
  value and market price.

  MODERN GALLERIES (Gagosian, Hauser & Wirth, Pace,
  White Cube): the same model, vastly scaled.
  Gagosian represents ~100 artists; has ~20 locations
  globally; functions as artist career management +
  market maker simultaneously.
```

## The Museum as Public Institution

Museums emerged as the alternative to private collection: the state or public holds
the cultural heritage.

```
MUSEUM DEVELOPMENT
====================

  1793: The Louvre opens as a public museum (revolutionary
        conversion of the royal collection)
  1824: The National Gallery, London
  1870: The Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York
  1929: MoMA, New York (the model for modern art institutions)

  THE MUSEUM'S ROLE:
  - Canonical validation: being in a museum = being art
  - Conservation: preservation for future generations
  - Public access: democratic against private hoarding
  - Research: scholarship and attribution

  THE MUSEUM'S PROBLEMS:
  - Collecting = removing from original context
  - Colonial collections: taken without consent
  - Canon-making: the museum's choices ARE the canon
  - "White cube" (Brian O'Doherty): the gallery's
    white walls and even lighting make anything within them
    look like art -- the institutional frame does more work
    than we admit

  MUSEUM AS ENDORSEMENT:
  A work's value on the secondary market is substantially
  affected by museum exhibition and acquisition history.
  A Warhol that has been in MoMA is worth more than the
  identical (almost) work that has not.
  Museums are market participants whether they intend to
  be or not.
```

## Auction Houses: How the Market Works

Christie's (est. 1766, James Christie, London) and Sotheby's (est. 1744, Samuel Baker,
London) are the two dominant global auction houses:

```
AUCTION MECHANICS
==================

  PRIMARY vs SECONDARY MARKET:
  PRIMARY: First sale by the artist (via gallery/dealer)
  SECONDARY: Resale of previously sold works (auction)

  At auction, the seller pays the SELLER'S COMMISSION
  (10-15% of hammer price).
  The buyer pays the BUYER'S PREMIUM (25-28% of hammer
  price for expensive lots -- rates are tiered).
  The house makes ~30-40% of the hammer price total.

  ESTIMATE:
  Published low-high range: "this work might sell for
  $800,000–$1,200,000." The estimates are calculated
  to attract bidders and sellers simultaneously.
  A work selling below estimate = failure.
  A work selling above estimate = success, newsworthy.

  RESERVE:
  The confidential minimum price the seller will accept.
  If bidding does not reach reserve, the work is
  "bought in" (withdrawn) at hammer and the result
  is not published. A high bought-in rate at a sale
  indicates market weakness.

  GUARANTEES:
  For major lots, auction houses or "irrevocable bidders"
  guarantee a minimum sale price. If the guarantee-giver
  is not the eventual buyer, they earn a fee. If they
  ARE the eventual buyer, they paid the guaranteed price.
  This removes risk for the consignor but reduces
  upside for the house.

  EVENING vs DAY SALES:
  Evening (Impressionist & Modern; Contemporary): the
  marquee lots, the high prices, the press.
  Day sales: follow the next day; less important works,
  lower prices, institutional and trade buyers.

  THE THIRD CATEGORY (online, post-2020):
  Online bidding expanded dramatically post-COVID.
  Christie's and Sotheby's now run online-only sales
  continuously.
```

### Price Formation

What makes a price?

```
ART PRICE DETERMINANTS
========================

  ARTIST FACTORS:
  - Historical importance (in the canon / taught in art schools)
  - Museum representation (how many major museums have acquired)
  - Critical literature (how much scholarship)
  - Estate control (estates maintain prices / manage supply)
  - Living vs dead: the market for living artists is illiquid
    compared to established historical figures

  WORK FACTORS:
  - Period: "early/late" vs "peak period" — peaks command premiums
  - Subject: nudes, flowers, more accessible subjects command
    more than abstract/difficult works by the same artist
  - Provenance (see below): great collections add premium
  - Size: generally, larger = more expensive (not always)
  - Condition: any restoration reduces value significantly
  - Exhibition history: documented shows add to history

  MARKET FACTORS:
  - Contemporary market conditions (are buyers spending?)
  - Comparable sales: what did similar works sell for?
  - Supply: how many comparable works come to market?
  - Active bidder competition: two passionate bidders
    for one work produce the highest prices
```

## Provenance: The Ownership Chain

*Provenance* = the documented history of ownership of a work of art.

```
PROVENANCE DOCUMENTATION
==========================

  COMPLETE PROVENANCE (ideal):
  Work was in the artist's studio until 1890.
  1890: Sold to Galerie Bernheim-Jeune, Paris.
  1895: Sold to Mr. and Mrs. Henri Bernstein, Paris.
  1938: [gap -- see below]
  1946: Acquired by the Heinemann Gallery, Munich.
  1952: Purchased by the Museum of Framed Things.

  THE CRITICAL GAP: 1933-1945
  Nazi Germany looted art systematically from Jewish
  collectors, museum collections, and occupied territories.
  Estimated 600,000 works looted; significant fraction
  never recovered.

  The Washington Principles (1998):
  44 nations agreed to make "best efforts" to identify
  Nazi-looted works and return them to pre-war owners
  or their heirs. Non-binding. Implementation varies.

  KEY INSTITUTIONS:
  - The Art Loss Register (London): database of stolen
    and looted works (500,000+ entries)
  - German Lost Art Foundation: database of German
    wartime losses
  - Claims Resolution Tribunal
```

### The Monuments Men and Post-War Restitution

The Monuments, Fine Arts, and Archives program (1943–1946): Allied unit tasked with
protecting cultural property during and after WWII. 345 men and women.

Egon Schieles: In 1998, Sotheby's New York attempted to sell two Schieles loaned to
MoMA for an exhibition; both were seized on behalf of claimants who alleged the works
were looted from their families. Sotheby's had to return them. This case changed
the legal landscape for art loans.

## Forgery: The Technical and Legal Questions

### Van Meegeren: The Vermeer Scandal

Han van Meegeren (1889–1947) forged Vermeers so convincingly that Hermann Göring
acquired one (Christ with the Woman Taken in Adultery) for his personal collection:

```
VAN MEEGEREN'S METHOD
=======================

  The challenge: how to forge a 300-year-old painting
  that will pass contemporary scientific tests?

  VAN MEEGEREN'S SOLUTIONS:
  1. Used canvas from the period (stripped 17th-c. paintings
     of their image, retained the canvas)
  2. Ground lapis lazuli and lead white himself (right period
     pigments -- no synthetic pigments introduced after 1700)
  3. Added phenol formaldehyde (Bakelite) to the paint --
     hardens like old, brittle oil paint when baked
  4. Baked the finished painting at 105°C for 2 hours
  5. Rolled the canvas over a cylinder to create fine
     age cracks (craquelure)
  6. Rubbed India ink into the cracks and wiped off
     (simulates the dark material that fills old cracks)

  The forged "Vermeers" passed examination by Abraham
  Bredius, the leading Vermeer scholar.

  EXPOSURE:
  Van Meegeren was caught not by art historical analysis
  but because after WWII he was charged with selling Dutch
  cultural heritage to the enemy (Göring). To prove he
  had not sold a real Vermeer, he confessed to the forgery.
  Then had to paint another one in front of witnesses
  to prove he was the forger, not a collaborator.
```

### Scientific Detection Methods

```
FORGERY DETECTION TOOLS
=========================

  DENDROCHRONOLOGY:
  For panel paintings on wood:
  Tree rings in the panel can be dated precisely.
  A panel cannot have been painted before the tree was cut.
  Many "early" paintings are revealed to have wood
  from the wrong period.

  PIGMENT ANALYSIS:
  X-ray fluorescence (XRF), infrared spectroscopy (FTIR)
  Can identify specific compounds in the paint.
  Titanium white (TiO2) was introduced commercially 1921.
  Any work claimed to be pre-1921 that contains titanium
  white is either later or has been retouched.
  Prussian blue (synthetic) invented 1704.
  Zinc white commercial from 1834.

  LEAD ISOTOPE ANALYSIS:
  Lead white from different ore sources has different
  isotope ratios. A work whose lead white comes from
  a 19th-century Belgian mine cannot be a Renaissance painting.

  X-RADIOGRAPHY AND INFRARED REFLECTOGRAPHY:
  Reveal underdrawing, pentimenti (changes of mind),
  compositional alterations.
  Authentic old masters show specific working methods;
  forgers rarely replicate these exactly beneath the surface.

  AUTORADIOGRAPHY:
  Very old paintings have specific patterns of radioactive
  isotope decay in the lead white and other materials.
  This pattern cannot be forged (the isotopes have decayed;
  you cannot add the right amount of the right isotopes).
  One of the most definitive tests for very old paintings.
```

## Repatriation Debates

### The Structural Arguments

```
REPATRIATION: THE COMPETING CLAIMS
=====================================

  FOR RETURN:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. OWNERSHIP: the objects were taken without consent  │
  │    (colonial looting, wartime theft, illicit trade)   │
  │    Ownership law: you cannot acquire good title from  │
  │    a thief; the original owner's title persists.      │
  │                                                       │
  │ 2. CULTURAL PROPERTY: some objects belong to a        │
  │    community's living culture, not to "universal      │
  │    human heritage"                                    │
  │                                                       │
  │ 3. CONTEXT: objects made for specific sites gain      │
  │    their full meaning only in those sites             │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  AGAINST RETURN (the "encyclopedic museum" argument):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. UNIVERSAL HERITAGE: the British Museum (James      │
  │    Cuno's argument) holds human heritage in common    │
  │    for all humanity                                   │
  │                                                       │
  │ 2. PRESERVATION: objects in Western museums were      │
  │    preserved; conditions in origin countries may be   │
  │    less secure                                        │
  │                                                       │
  │ 3. LEGAL OBSTACLES: the British Museum Act (1963)     │
  │    prohibits the trustees from disposing of objects   │
  │    in the collection. A literal Act of Parliament     │
  │    would be required to return the Elgin Marbles.     │
  │                                                       │
  │ 4. SLIPPERY SLOPE: if the Elgin Marbles return,       │
  │    what remains in any encyclopedic museum?           │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Key Cases

**Elgin Marbles (Parthenon Sculptures)**:
- Removed by Thomas Bruce, 7th Earl of Elgin, 1801–1812
- Sold to the British government 1816; in British Museum since
- Greece has been requesting return since 1983 (Melina Mercouri, Culture Minister)
- The British Museum position: they were legally acquired; they are safer in London;
  they represent universal heritage
- The Greek position: they were removed under dubious permission from Ottoman
  occupiers; they belong with the other Parthenon sculptures in Athens

**Benin Bronzes**:
- Looted by British Punitive Expedition 1897
- ~3,000 objects dispersed across ~160 institutions
- Germany (Humboldt Forum, Berlin) began returning collections 2022
- UK: Horniman Museum returned 72 objects 2022; Aberdeen University returned 1 object;
  British Museum has not returned any
- Nigeria established the Edo Museum of West African Art (EMOWAA) to receive returns

**Nefertiti Bust**:
- Egyptian queen's limestone bust, discovered in Amarna (Egypt) 1912 by German
  expedition
- Now in Neues Museum, Berlin
- Egypt has repeatedly requested return
- Germany argues: acquired legally by German excavation team (disputed); the bust
  is too fragile to move

## Art as Asset Class

### The Mei Moses Indices

AMR (the "Mei Moses" indices, originally Jianping Mei and Michael Moses):
tracks repeat-sale auction prices to estimate art as investment class:

```
ART INVESTMENT CHARACTERISTICS
================================

  RETURNS: Long-run annual returns roughly comparable to
  equities (~5-6% real per year) but with enormous variance.

  VERSUS STOCKS:
  Advantages:
  - Low correlation to stock market (diversification value)
  - Tangible asset (physical possession)
  - Aesthetic enjoyment ("psychic return")
  - Status signaling

  Disadvantages:
  - Extremely illiquid (selling takes months, years)
  - High transaction costs: 25-30% buyer's premium
  - Storage costs, insurance, climate control
  - Fakes risk
  - Attribution risk (reattributed works lose value immediately)
  - No yield (stocks pay dividends; art pays nothing)
  - Market is opaque (private sales not reported)

  THE CONCENTRATION PROBLEM:
  The "investment grade" art market is extreme Pareto:
  A tiny fraction of artists account for almost all value.
  Investing in "art" is really investing in Picasso,
  Basquiat, Hirst, Koons, and 50 others.
  The broader market is illiquid and mostly declining.
```

### Freeports

```
ART FREEPORTS (GENEVA, SINGAPORE, LUXEMBOURG)
===============================================

  A FREEPORT is a bonded warehouse outside the customs
  area of a country. Goods stored there are not imported --
  they have not crossed the customs boundary.

  WHY ART ENDS UP THERE:
  - No import duties until the work actually enters
    the country (which may never happen)
  - No VAT/sales tax on transactions within the freeport
  - Transactions between parties in the freeport: no tax
  - Works can be bought and sold multiple times without
    leaving the freeport

  THE SCALE:
  Geneva Freeport (the largest):
  Estimated 1.2 million artworks
  Estimated value: $100 billion+
  Works by Monet, Picasso, da Vinci stored in
  climate-controlled vaults, never seen.

  THE CRITIQUE:
  Art that no one sees serves no cultural purpose.
  It is a tax-advantaged commodity.
  If art's value is cultural, freeport storage
  represents the complete separation of financial
  value from cultural value.

  THE REGULATORY RESPONSE:
  Post-2016: increased transparency requirements,
  AML (anti-money laundering) regulations applied
  to the art market (EU 5th Anti-Money Laundering
  Directive, 2020).
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Key mechanism | Key risk |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Buying at auction | Buyer's premium ~28% on top of hammer | Bought something attributed to X; X is reattributed |
| Consigning to auction | Seller's commission + reserve setting | Work bought-in = failed publicly, damages price |
| Buying from gallery (primary) | One price, no haggling; relationship matters | Gallery goes out of business; no aftermarket support |
| Collecting as investment | Long-term hold, illiquid | Authentication problem discovered after purchase |
| Acquiring from estate / heirs | Provenance research critical | Nazi-era gap in ownership history |
| Museum acquisition | Donor restrictions, provenance check | Repatriation claim after acquisition |

## Common Confusion Points

**Hammer price vs selling price**: The price you hear quoted ("sold for $45 million")
is typically the hammer price. The actual cost to the buyer was $45M + ~28% buyer's
premium = ~$57.6 million. The seller received $45M minus the seller's commission.
When news says "achieved $45 million," that's the hammer. The actual market-clearing
price is higher.

**Provenance vs authentication**: Provenance is the ownership history. Authentication
is the determination of who made it. A well-provenanced work can still be a forgery.
An authenticated work can have problematic provenance (looted or stolen). They are
separate questions requiring separate evidence.

**The art market is opaque**: Unlike the stock market, there is no central reporting
of all transactions. Private gallery sales are never reported. Only auction results
are public. The figures that circulate for "the art market" are necessarily incomplete.

**Repatriation vs loan**: Some institutions have offered long-term loans to origin
countries as a compromise (return in use without legal transfer of title). Greece
has refused this for the Elgin Marbles because accepting a loan would imply the
British Museum's legal title is valid. The legal vs moral ownership question is
genuinely unresolved by current international law.

**The market does not validate artistic quality**: Jean-Michel Basquiat's prices
are a function of: rarity, the current market for post-war and contemporary art,
collector competition, and the art market's appetite for Black American artists
post-2010. These factors correlate imperfectly with the work's historical importance
(which is real and would survive the market's collapse). High prices don't make
art good; low prices don't make it bad.

**NFTs and copyright**: Buying an NFT does not give you copyright in the image.
Copyright belongs to the creator unless explicitly transferred. Most NFT sales
are sales of a certificate, not of the underlying intellectual property.
