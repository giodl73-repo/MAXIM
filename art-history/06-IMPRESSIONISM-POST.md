# 06 — Impressionism and Post-Impressionism

## The Big Picture

```
IMPRESSIONISM AND POST-IMPRESSIONISM
=======================================

  PRECONDITIONS (material and social):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1841: Portable tin paint tubes (John Goffe Rand, American)   │
  │        --> leave the studio, paint outdoors                   │
  │  1840s-1870s: Railway expansion across France                 │
  │        --> reach Normandy coast, Barbizon forest, Argenteuil  │
  │  1853-1870: Haussmann rebuilds Paris -- new boulevards,       │
  │        cafés, department stores, leisure culture emerge       │
  │  1860s-1880s: Photography matures -- challenges painting      │
  │  1856: Japonisme begins (trade with Japan reopens)            │
  │        -- Japanese prints: flat color, radical cropping,      │
  │           high horizon, decorative line                       │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  IMPRESSIONISM (c. 1865–1886):
    Monet, Renoir, Pissarro, Sisley, Degas, Morisot, Cassatt
    8 independent exhibitions (1874–1886)

  POST-IMPRESSIONISM (c. 1886–1905) -- not a movement, a label:
    Seurat (Neo-Impressionism/Pointillism)
    Cézanne (proto-Cubism / structure)
    Van Gogh (proto-Expressionism / color-emotion)
    Gauguin (Synthetism / primitivism)

  THE HINGE: Salon des Refusés 1863
    Napoleon III orders exhibition of rejected works.
    Manet's Déjeuner sur l'herbe causes scandal:
    not the nudity (academic nudes abounded) but the
    modern male dress + nude woman = the wrong mixture
    of real and ideal.
```

## Technical Innovations of Impressionism

### Broken Brushwork and Painting Light

The central technical claim: Impressionists paint light falling on objects, not the
objects themselves. The difference is fundamental:

```
ACADEMIC PAINTING vs IMPRESSIONIST APPROACH
============================================

  ACADEMIC:                         IMPRESSIONIST:
  ┌───────────────────────────────┐  ┌───────────────────────────────┐
  │ Object has a LOCAL COLOR       │  │ Light modifies every surface   │
  │ (grass is green, sky is blue) │  │ Local color + light quality   │
  │                               │  │ + reflections from other      │
  │ Blend smooth transitions      │  │ surfaces + atmospheric haze   │
  │                               │  │                               │
  │ Finish the surface until no   │  │ BROKEN BRUSHWORK:             │
  │ brushstroke is visible        │  │ Separate strokes of different │
  │ (the "licked" academic finish)│  │ colors placed side-by-side    │
  │                               │  │                               │
  │ Model in the studio under     │  │ At viewing distance, the eye  │
  │ controlled light              │  │ mixes the strokes optically   │
  │                               │  │                               │
  │ Photograph-like surface       │  │ The surface is loose, sketch- │
  │ = technical mastery           │  │ like -- critics said "unfinished"│
  └───────────────────────────────┘  └───────────────────────────────┘

  The Impressionists were not technically careless.
  They were technically deliberate in a different direction:
  capturing the momentary appearance of light, not the
  permanent "truth" of objects.
```

### The Palette Revolution

```
19TH-CENTURY PIGMENT TECHNOLOGY
==================================

  New synthetic pigments available to Impressionists:
  - Cerulean blue (1805, cobalt stannate): clear, sky blue
  - Cadmium yellow (1817): bright, warm yellow
  - Viridian (1859, chromium oxide): transparent, cool green
  - Cobalt violet (1859): first lightfast violet
  - Zinc white (vs lead white): slower drying but non-toxic

  COLOR THEORY INPUT:
  - Chevreul's Simultaneous Contrast (1839):
    Adjacent complementary colors intensify each other.
    Blue shadow next to orange highlight = more intense
    than either color alone.
  - Orange shadow in blue snow: Monet's observation that
    shadows are not simply darker versions of the surface
    color -- they contain the complementary of the light
    source color.
```

## Key Impressionist Figures

### Monet and the Series Paintings

Claude Monet (1840–1926) creates the series concept: multiple paintings of the
same subject under different light and atmospheric conditions:

```
MONET'S SERIES PAINTINGS
===========================

  HAYSTACKS series (1890–1891): ~30 paintings
  - Same haystacks, different: morning/afternoon/evening/winter
  - Each canvas a specific duration of light
  - Sold as a series -- viewers understood the concept

  ROUEN CATHEDRAL series (1892–1894): ~30 paintings
  - The stone facade as light's recording surface
  - Different times of day, different weather
  - The cathedral's form almost irrelevant -- it is a
    fixed reference point against which light changes
  - Some surfaces are THICK -- pigment built up to simulate
    the rough stone texture

  WATER LILIES (Nymphéas, 1896–1926): ~250 paintings
  - Giverny water garden designed and built by Monet
  - The surface of water: reflections, the lily pads, depth
  - The horizon disappears -- no clear sky/water division
  - The late large panels (Orangerie installation, 8 panels,
    100m of painted canvas): the viewer inside the painting

  SERIES CONCEPT:
  Light as the subject; the motif as pretext.
  This is a radical shift in what painting is FOR.
```

### Degas: Photography and Movement

Edgar Degas (1834–1917) is the Impressionist most influenced by photography:

```
DEGAS'S PHOTOGRAPHIC BORROWINGS
==================================

  CROPPING:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ In academic painting: important figures centered,      │
  │ complete within the frame.                             │
  │                                                        │
  │ In Degas: figures cut at the edge of the canvas by     │
  │ the frame, as in a snapshot. The framing is arbitrary.│
  │ This implies the scene continues beyond the canvas.    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HIGH VIEWPOINT / UNUSUAL ANGLES:
  - Ballet dancers seen from above (box seats)
  - Horses at the track from oblique angles
  - Bathers from vertiginous overhead positions

  MUYBRIDGE'S EADWEARD INFLUENCE:
  Muybridge's sequential horse-motion photographs (1878)
  proved galloping horses are not in the rocking-horse pose.
  Degas's horse paintings after 1878 show anatomically
  correct gallop. Before: incorrect.

  PASTELS: Degas's primary medium in later career.
  Not a "preparatory" medium -- he worked pastel to an
  extreme of layering and reworking unusual for the medium.
  The dancers in pastel: the color is ON the surface,
  granular, not sunk into oil.
```

### Pissarro and Renoir

- **Camille Pissarro (1830–1903)**: the elder, the teacher — Cézanne and Gauguin both
  spent time working with him. His consistent engagement with political anarchism shaped
  his painting: peasant and working subjects, not fashionable bourgeois leisure.
  Systematically repainted the same Paris intersections from his hotel window in
  different weather — a Monet-like series less well known.

- **Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841–1919)**: the social surface of bourgeois leisure —
  boating parties, cafés, dancing. *Bal du moulin de la Galette* (1876): the dappled
  light through trees on a Sunday-afternoon dance. Renoir's concern is pleasure itself;
  he produces the least critical, most comfortable vision of the Impressionist circle.

## Post-Impressionism

Post-Impressionism is not a style — it is a category created retrospectively by
Roger Fry in 1910 for artists who FOLLOWED Impressionism and departed from it in
four different directions.

### Seurat: Neo-Impressionism and Pointillism

Georges Seurat (1859–1891) takes Impressionism's empirical color observations and
makes them systematic:

```
SEURAT'S POINTILLIST SYSTEM
==============================

  BASIS: Helmholtz and Rood's color theory (optical mixing
  vs pigment mixing)

  PIGMENT MIXING (subtractive):
  Red + Blue = Purple (but muddy)
  Primary pigments mixed together SUBTRACT color

  OPTICAL MIXING (additive):
  Red dot + Blue dot side-by-side at distance:
  Eye combines them into vivid purple
  Light mixes additively -- the result is brighter

  SEURAT'S METHOD (Divisionism / Pointillism):
  - Small dots (points) of pure color applied systematically
  - Color from perceived object + color from light source
    + complementary color of shadow all calculated
  - Dots of specific size related to viewing distance
  - Compositions architecturally organized (not casual)

  A Sunday on La Grande Jatte (1884-1886):
  - 2.08 x 3.08m, ~3.5 million dots
  - 2 years to complete
  - The composition: geometrically rigid, static --
    figures are almost hieroglyphic (Egyptian stiffness)
  - NOT impressionist casualness: a designed system
  - The pointillist dot: early halftone printing (1880s)
    works on exactly this principle -- ink dots at
    varying density/size creating tone in newspaper
    reproduction. Seurat's art is contemporary with
    the technology.

  LEGACY: Pointillism = precursor to screen pixels.
  Additive color mixing on an RGB screen = the same
  principle as optical mixing of separate color dots.
```

### Cézanne: Structure and Multiple Viewpoints

Paul Cézanne (1839–1906) is the most consequential of the Post-Impressionists
for the development of 20th-century painting. The Cubists studied him directly.

```
CÉZANNE'S FORMAL INNOVATIONS
================================

  THE PASSAGE (passage):
  Where two planes meet, Cézanne sometimes allows the
  boundary to dissolve -- the surface "passes" from one
  plane to another without a hard edge.
  Effect: space is continuous, not divided into objects.

  MULTIPLE VIEWPOINTS IN A SINGLE IMAGE:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ A Cézanne still life: the table is seen slightly      │
  │ from above. The far edge of the table tilts up to   │
  │ show the tabletop. The fruit may be seen from a       │
  │ different angle than the plate. The objects are       │
  │ shown from their most informative angles combined.    │
  │                                                       │
  │ This is NOT incompetence. It is the recognition that  │
  │ we know more about an object than a single-viewpoint  │
  │ snapshot can show.                                    │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE CONSTRUCTIVE BRUSHSTROKE:
  Short, parallel strokes organized into planes.
  The surface of a mountain and the surface of an apple
  are treated with the same planar logic.
  The brushstroke is no longer mimetic (imitating texture)
  -- it is structural (building form).

  MONT SAINTE-VICTOIRE SERIES (~60 paintings + watercolors):
  - The same mountain from his estate in Provence
  - Cézanne asks: what is the essential structure of the
    mountain, independent of the accident of light?
  - The mountain is reduced to planes and masses
  - NOT a photograph of the mountain -- a meditation on it

  THE PATH TO CUBISM:
  Cézanne → Braque and Picasso (1907 onward)
  "Everything in nature is modeled on the sphere, cone,
  and cylinder" (Cézanne to Emile Bernard, 1904)
  Braque and Picasso take this literally.
```

### Van Gogh: Color-Emotion Equation

Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890) — 2,100 artworks in a 10-year career, two of which
he spent institutionalized, one in which he died:

```
VAN GOGH'S COLOR THEORY
==========================

  From his letters (among the most articulate in art history):

  "Instead of trying to reproduce exactly what I have before
  my eyes, I use color more arbitrarily, in order to express
  myself forcibly." (to Theo, 1888)

  COLOR AS PSYCHOLOGICAL NOTATION:
  - Night Café (1888): green gas lamps, red walls
    "I have tried to express the terrible passions of
    humanity by means of red and green." The color
    is NOT observed -- it is calculated for emotional effect.

  - Bedroom at Arles (1888): flat color areas, Japanese-
    influenced perspective -- "I wanted to express absolute
    restfulness" -- blue and lilac walls, orange and yellow

  - Starry Night (1889): painted from his room at the
    asylum; the stars have halos and spiral movements
    that no observer would see -- they are expression
    of emotional intensity, not observation

  BRUSHSTROKE AS ENERGY:
  Van Gogh's brushstroke is directional and energized --
  it follows the form it describes (wheat field: horizontal
  strokes; cypress tree: spiral strokes; sky: long sweeping
  curved strokes).

  SPEED AND INTENSITY:
  His productivity was extreme: some periods, a painting
  per day. The brushwork is fast -- the impasto is thick.

  THE MENTAL ILLNESS QUESTION:
  Diagnosed variously (epilepsy, bipolar disorder,
  Meniere's disease, lead poisoning from paint). The
  famous ear cutting (December 1888): probably during
  a breakdown. The art quality does not degrade in
  the asylum period -- Starry Night is from the
  Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum (1889).
```

### Gauguin: Synthetism and Primitivism

Paul Gauguin (1848–1903) leaves Paris for Pont-Aven (Brittany), then Tahiti, then
the Marquesas:

```
GAUGUIN'S FORMAL SYSTEM (SYNTHETISM)
=======================================

  Against Impressionist optical dissolution:
  Gauguin wants SYNTHESIS -- bold color areas bounded
  by thick dark outlines, like stained glass or cloisonné.

  PONT-AVEN SCHOOL (1886-1891):
  - Bold, unmodeled color areas
  - Dark cloisonné outlines (cloisons = dividers in
    metalwork where enamel is separated by metal strips)
  - Flat pattern over depth
  - The "primitive" as formal strategy:
    children's art, folk art, non-Western art
    all share flatness and boldness that Gauguin deploys
    against European academic naturalism

  TAHITI (1891-1893, 1895-1901):
  - Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?
    (1897, 374 x 139cm): a synthesis painting, figures
    arranged across a frieze like a monument, no Western
    perspective, color deep and saturated
  - Gauguin's project: find pre-industrial, pre-Christian
    authenticity in the Pacific that European civilization
    has lost

  THE CRITIQUE:
  Gauguin exploited Polynesian women sexually and economically.
  He depicted them through a French male colonist's fantasy
  of noble savagery and erotic availability. His formal
  innovation and his ethical position are not separable
  from each other -- but critics have often tried.
```

**Signal processing bridge — Seurat's system is the display stack:** Seurat's Divisionism is not merely *analogous* to additive RGB display — it is the same underlying physical principle. The human visual system performs spatial low-pass filtering: at sufficient viewing distance, high-frequency spatial variation (individual dots of different color) is averaged by the retina's cone-sampling resolution into a single perceived color. Your monitor exploits exactly this. An RGB LED display at arm's length: each pixel is three sub-pixels (red, green, blue); the eye blends them into a single perceived color through the same spatial integration mechanism Seurat was calculating in 1884. The differences are implementation details — Seurat's dots vary in size and position, LCD sub-pixels are fixed-pitch; Seurat calculated color relationships by observation and Chevreul's/Rood's color theory, your GPU calculates them in linear color space with gamma encoding. The principle — discrete emitters at sub-resolution spacing, optically mixed by the viewer — is identical. Seurat was working at the physical limit of human spatial acuity, sizing his dots to be just barely below the eye's angular resolution at the intended viewing distance. That is spatial frequency engineering performed without Fourier analysis.

## The Salon des Refusés 1863: Institutional Rupture

The Académie des Beaux-Arts (Paris) controlled the Salon (the primary exhibition and
sale event) which controlled careers. The jury of 1863 rejected approximately
4,000 works — so many that Napoleon III ordered an exhibition of the rejected works:

```
THE SALON SYSTEM
==================

  ACADEMIC HIERARCHY:
  History painting (highest prestige)
  Portrait
  Genre
  Landscape
  Still life (lowest)

  HOW IT WORKED:
  - The Salon exhibited thousands of works annually
  - Acceptance = career advancement
  - Rejection = career damage
  - The jury was composed of Academic painters who
    had a stake in maintaining the hierarchy

  SALON DES REFUSÉS (1863):
  - Manet's Déjeuner sur l'herbe creates scandal
  - Not because nudity (academic nudes everywhere)
  - But because: contemporary male dress + naked woman
    = the wrong register; not myth, not allegory,
    just a real naked woman in a real park with clothed men
  - This was the social transgression

  IMPACT:
  - Established that there could be an alternative
    venue to the Académie
  - The Impressionists later form their own exhibitions
    (1874: First Impressionist Exhibition, Boulevard des
    Capucines)
  - The dealer-critic system gradually replaces the Salon
    as the mechanism for art careers
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Impressionism was not immediately popular**: The first Impressionist exhibitions
were largely met with ridicule. The name "Impressionism" was coined by a hostile
critic (Louis Leroy, 1874) mocking Monet's Impression, Sunrise. The Impressionists
adopted it defiantly. The popularity came decades later; dealers like Durand-Ruel
carried the movement financially during the lean years.

**Degas called himself a Realist, not an Impressionist**: He resisted the label,
never worked en plein air, worked primarily from memory and drawings in the studio.
His inclusion in the Impressionist exhibitions was social (he organized the first one)
not stylistic.

**Cézanne's distortions are intentional**: Tables that tilt, objects shown from
multiple viewpoints — these are not errors. Cézanne was a rigorous observer but
subordinated strict single-viewpoint accuracy to pictorial logic. This is the
opposite of incompetence: it requires knowing what correct perspective is and
consciously departing from it.

**Pointillism ≠ pixels**: The analogy to digital pixels is illuminating but
inexact. Seurat's dots are variable in size, and their color is determined by
colorimetric calculation + observation; pixels are fixed in size and determined
by numerical values. The optical mixing principle is the same; the implementation
is different.

**Post-Impressionism is not a movement**: Seurat, Cézanne, Van Gogh, and Gauguin
did not form a group, did not share a manifesto, and had significantly different
aesthetic programs. The label was invented 20 years after the fact.

**Gauguin's "primitivism" is contested**: His use of non-Western formal strategies
is genuine and influential (it directly feeds Fauvism and Expressionism). His
treatment of Polynesian subjects as fantasy objects of Western desire is a documented
harm. These are not mutually exclusive — the formal innovation was enabled in part
by the colonial relationship that allowed him to operate there.
