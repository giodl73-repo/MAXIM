## Caravaggio (1571–1610): Tenebrism and the Counter-Reformation

### Tenebrism: The Technical System

```
TENEBRISM (from Latin tenebra = darkness)
==========================================

  STANDARD CHIAROSCURO (Leonardo, High Renaissance):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Gradual tonal transition from light to shadow         │
  │ Ambient light fills the shadows (they have color)     │
  │ Shadow = less light, not absence of light             │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CARAVAGGIO'S TENEBRISM:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Single strong light source (often from upper left)    │
  │ Dramatic direct illumination of selected surfaces     │
  │ Surrounding areas plunge into near-total darkness     │
  │ Shadows are BLACK, not shadowed colored ground        │
  │ Effect: forms emerge from void, not from space        │
  │ Theatrical, dramatic -- a single spotlight on stage   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PHYSICAL MODEL:
  Caravaggio reportedly worked in a dark room with a
  single high light source, painting directly from posed
  models -- no preliminary drawing, direct alla prima
  (wet into wet, single session per section)
```

### Direct Observation and Low-Life Subjects

Caravaggio's subjects violate Renaissance decorum:

- **The Calling of St. Matthew (1600, San Luigi dei Francesi, Rome)**: Christ (hard to
  identify without the halo) points at Matthew, a tax collector, at a tavern table
  with associates who look like contemporary Roman lowlifes. The light is natural.
  The setting is a Roman *osteria*, not a sacred space. The miracle happens in the
  ordinary world.

- **Death of the Virgin (1601–1606, rejected)**: The Virgin shown as a dead peasant
  woman, possibly a drowned prostitute used as model (according to contemporaries);
  bloated, feet visible, surrounding mourners genuinely grieving. Rejected by the
  patrons as indecorous. Now the Louvre's greatest Baroque painting.

- **Counter-Reformation context**: The Church of Trent demanded accessible, emotionally
  direct images that would move the viewer to piety. Caravaggio's work is the extreme
  version of this demand. His street people ARE the saints; the miraculous interrupts
  the ordinary. His controversial figures are too ordinary; the Church preferred
  somewhat elevated ordinary.

### Artemisia Gentileschi (1593–c.1653)

Not a footnote to Caravaggio — an independent master:

- **Judith Slaying Holofernes (1614–1620, Uffizi)**: compared to Caravaggio's version
  (1598–1599), Gentileschi's is more technically engaged — two women doing difficult
  physical work, Judith's grip and posture mechanically correct for beheading.
- The rape trial against her teacher Tassi (1612) and its aftermath have been
  interpreted in her Judith paintings — this is the interpretive trap Nochlin warns
  about: reducing the work to biography.

## Rembrandt van Rijn (1606–1669)

### The Group Portrait as Psychological Space

```
DUTCH BAROQUE CONTEXT
=======================

  Protestant Netherlands: no church commissions
  Commercial republic: wealthy merchant class, not aristocracy
  Art market: paintings sold at markets, not commissioned
  Genre hierarchy established:
    History painting (highest -- but limited market)
    Portrait (lucrative -- primary market)
    Landscape, still life, genre scenes (large bourgeois market)

  Group portrait = primary lucrative commission:
    Civic guard companies (schutterij)
    Guild meetings (surgeons, cloth merchants)
    Each figure paid separately -- must be visible and dignified
```

Rembrandt's Night Watch (1642, full title: Militia Company of District II under
the Command of Captain Frans Banninck Cocq):

- Not actually a night scene — it was a day scene darkened by varnish
- Revolutionary: figures in motion, moving outward from the canvas, into the viewer's
  space — the group portrait as action, not static documentation
- The light falls on two figures (Cocq in black, Visscher in yellow) while others
  are in shadow — unprecedented for a group portrait where each patron paid for
  visible representation
- The golden girl in the lower center is a visual device (she catches the light),
  not an identified individual — a compositional element in a commissioned portrait

### The Self-Portrait Series as Career-Long Project

Rembrandt made ~50 self-portraits across his career (1628–1669):

```
REMBRANDT SELF-PORTRAIT ARC
==============================

  Early (1628–1635):
    Experiments with facial expressions, grimacing
    Testing light effects on the face
    Modest scale, rapid execution

  Middle (1636–1650):
    Success period: elaborate costumes, historical dress
    Self-fashioning as bourgeois intellectual
    The elegant self-portrait as aspiration

  Late (1652–1669):
    After bankruptcy (1656), death of wife and son
    The face is the subject: time, loss, age
    Simplified palette, broad brushwork
    Self-Portrait at the Easel (1660, Louvre): the artist
    at work, studying us studying him -- circular gaze
    Self-Portrait as Zeuxis (c. 1668): possibly laughing
    at the old woman he is painting (mythological allusion)
    or laughing at himself -- ambiguous to the end

  The series as document: the same individual face
  across 40 years is unprecedented in Western art.
  Not documentation -- investigation.
```

### Rembrandt's Etching Mastery

Etching was Rembrandt's primary print medium:

```
ETCHING PROCESS
=================

  1. Metal plate (copper) coated with acid-resistant
     ground (wax or resin)
  2. Draw through the ground with a needle (not cutting
     the metal -- just exposing it)
  3. Immerse in acid: exposed lines are bitten into metal
  4. Remove ground, ink plate, wipe surface, print
  5. For darker lines: re-ground and re-bite (multiple
     acid baths = deeper lines = more ink = darker)

  DRYPOINT (Rembrandt's addition to etching):
  Draw directly into the metal with a sharp tool.
  The tool throws up a burr (small metal ridge) alongside
  the line. The burr holds extra ink -- produces a
  characteristic VELVETY dark tone.
  The burr wears down with printing -- early prints
  have the burr quality, later prints lose it.
  Rembrandt printed and sold his own prints -- he knew
  the rarity of early burr states and priced accordingly.
```

## Vermeer (1632–1675): Optics and Interiority

### The Camera Obscura Hypothesis

Philip Steadman (*Vermeer's Camera*, 2001) and David Hockney's *Secret Knowledge*
(2001) argue that Vermeer used a camera obscura as a compositional tool:

```
CAMERA OBSCURA
================

  A darkened room (or box) with a small hole or lens
  in one wall projects an inverted image of the outside
  scene onto the opposite wall.

  Evidence in Vermeer's work:
  - Circles of confusion: out-of-focus areas at edges
    (only visible with optics -- eye would sharpen them)
  - Halation: light "bleeding" around bright highlights
    (characteristic of early lenses)
  - Specific tonal ratios that match projected images
    more than unaided vision
  - Distorted perspective in some works matching
    wide-angle projection

  The debate:
  PRO (Hockney/Steadman): optical evidence is real;
    Vermeer's space is too accurate for unaided observation;
    camera obscura use known in Delft in this period
    (Constantijn Huygens describes one 1622)

  CONTRA: using a camera obscura does not produce a
    painting automatically; Vermeer still made all the
    pictorial decisions; his genius is not diminished
    by a compositional tool

  RESOLUTION: probably used for compositional setup,
    not as a literal tracing device
```

### Domestic Interior as Philosophical Space

Vermeer's ~35 paintings are almost entirely set in the same Delft room:

- **Girl with a Pearl Earring (c. 1665)**: not a portrait (no identified sitter) —
  a *tronie* (character study); the turning, glancing, half-open-mouth pose is
  a formal device about attention and gaze
- **Woman Reading a Letter (c. 1663)**: light from the left window, woman reading,
  implied narrative (from whom? what does it say?) — meaning is withheld
- **The Art of Painting (c. 1666–1668)**: Vermeer's largest, kept by his wife until
  death — possibly his most deliberate statement about painting. The painter (seen
  from behind) is not Vermeer; the woman (Clio, muse of history) wears a laurel
  crown and holds a trumpet. Painting claims for itself the status of history.

The tile floors in Vermeer's interiors have been used to reconstruct the perspective
geometry of his studio. His working method can be partially reconstructed.

## Rubens (1577–1640): Workshop System and Diplomacy

Peter Paul Rubens is an artist-diplomat operating at a European scale:

- **Workshop system**: Rubens designed compositions, made oil sketches, supervising
  assistants who produced the final works; Rubens returned for final touches and
  corrections. This was standard practice but Rubens scaled it to an industrial
  level — hundreds of paintings produced.
- **Color as structure**: Rubens learned from Titian in Venice — color (not line)
  defines form; warm flesh tones against cool shadow, the whole composition keyed
  to color relationships rather than drawn contour
- **Kermis (1636–1638)**: peasant celebration, writhing mass of bodies, joyful
  chaos — but organized through color rhythms and diagonal thrusts
- **The Consequences of War (1638–1639)**: Mars (war) drags himself from Venus
  (peace) while Alekto (fury) leads him; Europe mourns; figures symbolize
  architecture, the arts, fertility destroyed — allegorical machine functioning
  perfectly, commissioned by Ferdinand II of the Holy Roman Empire

Rubens negotiated peace between Spain and England as Philip IV's special envoy
(1629–1630) and was knighted by Charles I while in London painting the Banqueting
House ceiling. The painter as sovereign peer.

## Velázquez (1599–1660): Las Meninas as Meta-Painting

Las Meninas (1656, Prado, 318 × 276 cm):

```
LAS MENINAS SPATIAL ANALYSIS
================================

  WHAT YOU SEE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Large painter (Velázquez) facing out from behind a large     │
  │ canvas. He is painting something. What?                      │
  │                                                              │
  │ Center: Infanta Margarita (5 years old) flanked by           │
  │ two meninas (ladies-in-waiting), two dwarfs, a dog           │
  │                                                              │
  │ Background: a mirror reflecting two blurry figures           │
  │ -- identified as King Philip IV and Queen Mariana            │
  │                                                              │
  │ Doorway: a man looking in (Don José Nieto, royal             │
  │ chamberlain -- or is he leaving?)                            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE INTERPRETIVE PROBLEM:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Velázquez is painting something. Options:                    │
  │ A: The king and queen (whose reflection we see in mirror)    │
  │ B: Las Meninas (the scene we are watching)                   │
  │ C: Both -- the large canvas is us, the king and queen,       │
  │    standing where we stand                                   │
  │                                                              │
  │ We (the viewer) occupy the position of the king and queen.   │
  │ The mirror confirms our position.                            │
  │ We are painted into the painting while looking at it.        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FOUCAULT'S READING (Les Mots et les Choses, 1966):
  This is a painting about representation itself: the king
  (sovereign, gaze) is present only as reflection; the painter
  is both subject and agent of representation; the viewer
  completes the circuit. The Classical Age's representational
  order is shown in its full complexity -- and instability.
```

## Bernini (1598–1680): Marble That Moves

Gian Lorenzo Bernini is Baroque sculpture's defining figure:

- **Apollo and Daphne (1622–1625, Borghese Gallery)**: the moment of transformation —
  Daphne's fingers become laurel branches, bark climbs her body, her feet root. The
  material (marble) is made to seem like it is changing state. The technical
  achievement: thin marble projections (fingers, branches) that should have broken
  under their own weight; Bernini calculates load paths through the composition.

- **The Ecstasy of St. Teresa (1647–1651, Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome)**:
  Teresa of Ávila's mystical experience (described in her autobiography as physical)
  depicted as erotic/spiritual ambiguity — the angel's lance about to pierce her;
  her expression beyond consciousness. The Cornaro Chapel frames it as theater: marble
  spectators in boxes on the side walls watch it. The viewer is implicated as
  audience to a private mystical event.

- **Baldacchino, St. Peter's (1623–1634)**: Bronze, 29m tall, twisted columns over
  the tomb of St. Peter. Scaled to the basilica's vastness while maintaining detail.
  The bronze stripped from the Pantheon's portico — Pasquino: "Quod non fecerunt
  barbari, fecerunt Barberini" (What the barbarians didn't do, the Barberini did —
  Barberini being the pope's family).

## Rococo (c. 1720–1780)

### Rococo as Aristocratic Language

After Louis XIV's death (1715), the French court relaxed. The Rococo replaced:
- Baroque grandeur (large scale, heroic subjects) with intimacy (small scale, pleasure)
- Religious solemnity with secular frivolity
- Deep moral seriousness with refined pleasure and ornament

### Watteau and the Fête Galante

Antoine Watteau (1684–1721) invented the *fête galante* — elegant parties in parkland:

- **Pilgrimage to Cythera (1717)**: couples about to embark for (or return from?)
  the island of love; mood is melancholy despite the supposed pleasure — departure
  or return? The ambiguity IS the subject
- Watteau's characters are borrowed from Italian commedia dell'arte (Pierrot,
  Harlequin) — theatrical identity rather than stable selfhood
- Watteau himself died of tuberculosis at 37 — whether his melancholy atmosphere
  is biographical or formal is the interpretive question

### Rococo vs Baroque: The Formal Contrast

```
BAROQUE                          ROCOCO
=============================================================

  Large scale                     Small scale
  Heroic / religious subject      Amorous / pastoral subject
  Deep shadow, strong contrast    Pale pastels, diffuse light
  Diagonal, dynamic composition   S-curves, asymmetry
  Architectural setting           Garden, outdoor setting
  Weighty, monumental             Light, playful
  Emotional intensity             Refined surface pleasure
  Counter-Reformation mission     Aristocratic entertainment
  Dark ground (Caravaggio)        Pale, creamy ground (Boucher)
  Stone, bronze                   Gilded plaster, porcelain
```

**Market economics bridge — from bespoke commission to commodity market:** The Dutch art market of the 17th century is the first instance of a modern commodity art market, and the structural shift maps directly to any industry transition from bespoke to commodity production. The before/after:

```
COMMISSION MODEL (pre-Dutch)          OPEN MARKET MODEL (Dutch Republic)
═══════════════════════════════════   ════════════════════════════════════
Buyer defines product before          Seller produces speculatively,
production begins                     buyer selects from inventory

Single buyer, unique work             Many buyers, standardized genres
                                      (still life, landscape, interior —
                                      each a product category)

Price set by negotiation              Price set by market at fairs
between artist and patron             and through dealers (middlemen)

Artist's reputation secondary         Reputation drives repeat purchase;
to patron's status                    Rembrandt's name is a brand

Production to known demand            Overproduction, gluts, crashes
(no inventory risk)                   (Dutch market collapsed 1672)

Failure = rejected commission         Failure = unsold inventory
```

The Dutch Republic's Calvinist bourgeoisie wanted affordable art for domestic interiors — a mass market with defined price points. Artists responded by specializing: Jan Steen did genre scenes, Hobbema did landscapes, Pieter Claesz did still lifes. Specialization = product differentiation in a commodity market. This is the same structural logic as any industry moving from enterprise bespoke to SaaS: standardized product categories, volume pricing, dealer networks as distribution layer, reputation/brand as the primary differentiator when quality is hard to assess pre-purchase.

## Patronage Shift: Church vs Secular

The Baroque period witnesses a decisive shift in patronage:

```
PATRONAGE TRANSFORMATION
==========================

  ITALY (Catholic):
  Church still dominant -- Counter-Reformation building
  program (Gesù, Sant'Andrea, etc.) + papal commissions
  Bernini = papal architect and sculptor
  Private noble patronage (Borghese, Barberini)

  NETHERLANDS (Protestant):
  Church commissions effectively zero after iconoclasm
  Open art market: fairs, dealers, lotteries
  Genre hierarchy inverts: landscape, still life,
  genre scenes command large market
  Wealthy merchant class collects: Rembrandt's portraits,
  Vermeer's interiors, Hals's citizens

  FRANCE (absolute monarchy):
  Louis XIV: art as state propaganda
  Le Brun: court painter controlling all royal imagery
  Versailles (1661-1710): total artwork -- architecture,
  garden, painting, sculpture, furniture, tapestry unified
  Manufacture des Gobelins: royal factory producing
  tapestries, furniture to control visual culture

  ENGLAND (constitutional monarchy):
  Charles I: extraordinary collector (Van Dyck as court
  painter, Rubens ceiling at Banqueting House)
  Civil War interrupts then resumes under Charles II
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Caravaggio's models**: The claim that Caravaggio used a prostitute as the Virgin
in Death of the Virgin comes from the 17th-century biographer Bellori. This may be
gossip rather than fact. However, the painting was rejected — the patrons said the
figure was undignified. The reason given may or may not have been the model.

**Night Watch is not dark**: The painting's full title refers to a day scene. The
"night" reading comes from centuries of discolored varnish. Cleaning in the 20th
century revealed a lighter work than previously known.

**Vermeer's production**: ~35 paintings are attributed to Vermeer with confidence.
He may have made more (some lost) or this may be his total output (extremely low for
a professional painter). He died in debt; the estate was entangled in debt.
His work was largely forgotten until the 1860s-1880s when Thoré-Bürger rediscovered
and attributed many works.

**Rubens did not paint every inch of every work**: The workshop system is documented.
The question for each Rubens painting is: what is the autograph section? Oil sketches
are entirely by Rubens; some large works are largely by assistants with Rubens
retouching. Connoisseurship (handling, quality assessment) is the tool for distinguishing.

**Las Meninas interpretation**: Foucault's reading is influential but not universally
accepted. Art historians have questioned whether 17th-century painters had
self-consciousness about "representation" in the post-structuralist sense Foucault
implies. The painting does what it does regardless of which theory best describes it.

**Rococo is not just decoration**: Boucher and Fragonard are sometimes dismissed as
mere decorators. But Rococo is a coherent visual ideology: pleasure as virtue,
the rejection of Protestant and Counter-Reformation earnestness, the surface as
itself a value. Its politics (aristocratic, pre-Revolutionary) became untenable
after 1789, which is why David's Neoclassicism displaced it so thoroughly.
