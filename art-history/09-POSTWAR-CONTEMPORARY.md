## Pop Art (1956–1970)

### The Context: Consumer Culture and Mass Media

```
POP ART'S MATERIAL CONDITIONS
================================

  POST-WWII PROSPERITY:
  - Television penetration in US: 9% (1950) → 90% (1960)
  - Supermarkets, branded goods, planned obsolescence
  - Celebrity culture: Hollywood, pop music, sports
  - The advertising image as the primary visual experience
    of daily life -- more than "fine art" by orders of
    magnitude

  POP'S CLAIM:
  If most people's visual experience is advertising,
  packaging, television, and magazine images, then art
  that ignores this is irrelevant.
  Use the visual vocabulary of mass culture.
  Do NOT subordinate it -- take it on its own terms.

  THE IRONY QUESTION:
  Is Pop Art:
  A. Celebrating consumer culture (affirmative)
  B. Criticizing it through distance and repetition
     (critical)
  C. Ambiguous by design (both / neither)

  Warhol's position: consistently refused to clarify.
  The ambiguity is the point.
```

### Warhol: Seriality, Silk-screen, and Celebrity

Andy Warhol (1928–1987):

```
WARHOL'S FORMAL STRATEGIES
=============================

  SILK-SCREEN PRINTING:
  - Photographic image transferred to silk screen
  - Ink squeegeed through screen onto canvas
  - Repeatable, mass-reproducible -- like commercial printing
  - MISREGISTRATION deliberately preserved or introduced:
    the ink doesn't always align perfectly.
    In commercial printing: a defect.
    In Warhol: evidence that the process is a process.

  SERIALITY:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 32 Campbell's Soup Cans (1962): 32 individual         │
  │ canvases, one per variety. Installed in a row.        │
  │                                                       │
  │ What does repeating the image do?                     │
  │ - Drains the iconic image of uniqueness              │
  │ - Reveals the repetition that is already present     │
  │   in the supermarket (32 identical cans on shelf)    │
  │ - Raises: is each painting unique? Is any canvas?    │
  │ - The uniqueness claim of "fine art" confronted by   │
  │   commercial multiples                               │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CELEBRITY AND DEATH:
  Marilyn Monroe (1962): Warhol begins the Marilyns
  immediately after Monroe's death (August 5, 1962).
  The photograph used is a publicity still from
  Niagara (1953) -- already a media image.
  Warhol reproduces a reproduction.
  The color: garish, artificial, over-saturated.
  The repeated face: the media production of celebrity.

  MAOAND ELECTRIC CHAIR:
  Warhol repeats Mao's official portrait from the
  Cultural Revolution (a mass-produced image already).
  He silk-screens electric chair photographs.
  The repetition of violent or totalitarian imagery:
  does it critique power or aestheticize it?
```

### Lichtenstein: Benday Dots and Appropriation

Roy Lichtenstein (1923–1997):

- **Source material**: takes single frames from romance comics and war comics and
  enlarges them to painting scale (1.5–2m)
- **Benday dots**: hand-paints the mechanical dot-pattern of cheap comics printing
  (Ben-Day dots, after the printing method) at large scale — the medium of mass
  reproduction made visible as fine art
- **Steel Blue (1965)**: a fighter pilot in a cockpit; "I pressed the fire control...
  and ahead of me rockets blazed through the sky..." — the romance of war technology
  depicted in the visual language of cheap war comics

The appropriation question: Lichtenstein rarely credited the original comic artists
(many of whom were identified decades later). He improved the composition and
execution compared to the originals. The ethical problem (taking without credit)
and the formal achievement are distinct.

### Hamilton: The British Origin

Richard Hamilton's collage *Just what is it that makes today's homes so different,
so appealing?* (1956, for the "This is Tomorrow" exhibition, London):

- Bodybuilder, pin-up, television, tape recorder, tin of ham labelled "Pop",
  comic-strip ceiling, photograph of the street below
- The room is assembled from magazine images — a consumer fantasy
- The word "POP" is in the image, on a lollipop
- This predates American Pop by 5 years

## Minimalism (1960–1975)

### The Formal Claim

```
MINIMALISM'S ARGUMENT
=======================

  AGAINST ABSTRACT EXPRESSIONISM:
  - AbEx is "too personal" -- Pollock's gesture records
    the artist's psychological state; the viewer comes
    second
  - The painting still maintains an ILLUSION (spatial
    recession, depth in Rothko's layers)
  - Abstract Expressionism is still "painting" in the
    old sense: a framed surface with an image on it

  FOR:
  - The object should be what it is, nothing more
  - No illusion, no expression, no reference
  - The viewer's body in real space with a real object
  - Experience over image

  DONALD JUDD: "Specific Objects" (1965):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ "Three-dimensionality is real space. That gets rid   │
  │ of the problem of illusionism and of literal space,  │
  │ space in and around marks and colors -- which is     │
  │ riddance of one of the salient and most objectionable│
  │ relics of European art."                             │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Judd's stacks: galvanized iron boxes mounted at
  regular intervals on the wall. Industrially fabricated
  (not hand-made). Identical units in a series.
  You walk past them -- the experience is temporal.
```

### Key Minimalist Artists

**Donald Judd (1928–1994)**:
- Steel and Plexiglas boxes, stack reliefs, progressions
- Fabricated industrially (Bernstein Brothers, NY; later his own fabrication shop)
- The hand of the artist is absent; the art is in the conception and the specification

**Carl Andre (1935–)**:
- Floor pieces: flat, horizontal, the opposite of pedestal sculpture
- *Equivalent VIII* (144 identical fire bricks arranged in a rectangle on the floor,
  1966) — the "Tate bricks" controversy; the question of what constitutes sculpture
- The viewer walks around or past; the work is the encounter

**Richard Serra (1938–2024)**:
- Steel plates, torqued ellipses, massive weight and mass
- *Tilted Arc* (1981, Federal Plaza, NYC): a 36.5m curved steel plate across a plaza
  Removed 1989 after public protest — the only commissioned public sculpture in the US
  to be removed by public vote. Serra: removing it is destroying it.

**Sol LeWitt (1928–2007)**:
- Instructions-based art: "Wall Drawing #46: Vertical lines, not straight, crossing
  and touching... using four colors, uniformly dispersed with maximum density."
- The instruction is the art; the execution can be done by anyone
- The same work can be made and unmade repeatedly: it is a score, not an object
- LeWitt is the bridge between Minimalism and Conceptual Art

## Conceptual Art (1966–1975)

### Dematerialization

Lucy Lippard's term (*Six Years: The Dematerialization of the Art Object*, 1973):
a systematic movement away from the physical art object toward ideas, propositions,
and language.

```
CONCEPTUAL ART'S PROGRAM
==========================

  JOSEPH KOSUTH: Art as Idea as Idea

  One and Three Chairs (1965):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Three things side by side on a gallery wall:             │
  │                                                          │
  │ 1. An actual folding chair                               │
  │ 2. A full-scale photograph of that chair                 │
  │ 3. An enlargement of the dictionary definition of "chair"│
  │                                                          │
  │ Which one is the "real" chair?                           │
  │ The physical object? The visual representation?          │
  │ The linguistic definition?                               │
  │                                                          │
  │ Kosuth's argument (from Wittgenstein):                   │
  │ Art = analytic proposition (true by definition)          │
  │ NOT synthetic proposition (true by reference to facts)  │
  │ "Art is the definition of art." (tautology)             │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ART & LANGUAGE (group, UK):
  - Art criticism IS art
  - The discourse around the work IS the work
  - They published a journal -- the journal was the artwork

  LAWRENCE WEINER:
  - "A 36" x 36" removal to the lathing or support
    wall of plaster or wallboard from a wall."
  - Statement = the work. Does not need to be executed.
  - "The piece need not be built." The proposition is complete.
```

## Feminist Art (1970s)

### Judy Chicago and The Dinner Party (1974–1979)

*The Dinner Party* (1974–1979, Brooklyn Museum permanent collection):

- A triangular table, 39 settings
- Each setting: a unique ceramic plate (many explicitly vaginal in form) +
  embroidered runner + gold chalice and cutlery
- 39 women at the table: Primordial Goddess, Hatshepsut, Sappho, Hildegard of Bingen,
  Artemisia Gentileschi, Mary Wollstonecraft, Virginia Woolf...
- Heritage Floor beneath: 999 women's names on porcelain tiles

The critical debate: the ceramic vulvar plates — feminist reclamation of female
biology as power, or biological essentialism? The debate continues.

### Guerrilla Girls: Data as Activism

Founded 1985, anonymous, wear gorilla masks:

- Used statistics as weapons against the art world's gender and racial exclusion
- **1989 poster, MoMA retrospective**: "Do women have to be naked to get into the
  Met. Museum? Less than 5% of the artists in the Modern Art sections are women,
  but 85% of the nudes are female."
- The data was more damaging than any aesthetic argument
- Updated the statistics regularly — the numbers changed slowly

### Body Art

- **Carolee Schneemann** (*Interior Scroll*, 1975): pulled a scroll from her vagina
  and read it aloud — the female body as source of knowledge and text, not just image
- **Ana Mendieta** (1948–1985): *Silueta* series — imprints of her body in earth,
  sand, and rock in Mexico and Iowa; the body as landscape and landscape as body;
  disappeared and "returned" to earth; Mendieta died falling from a high-rise (her
  husband Carl Andre acquitted of her murder — the case is unresolved)

## Installation, Performance, and Land Art

### Smithson and Land Art

Robert Smithson (1938–1973):

- **Spiral Jetty (1970, Great Salt Lake, Utah)**: 6,650 tons of black basalt and
  earth in a 460m spiral jetty extending into the lake
- Made when the lake level was low; submerged for decades; reemerged when the lake
  level fell further
- Uses the site's geology (salt crystals on the rocks, brine shrimp turning the water
  red) as part of the work — not imposed form but form in dialogue with the site
- Smithson's concept: entropy; the work exists in geological time, not art-market time

### Christo and Jeanne-Claude: Wrapping

- **Running Fence (1976, Sonoma/Marin counties, CA)**: white nylon fabric, 40km,
  existing for 14 days
- **Wrapped Reichstag (1995, Berlin)**: the German parliament building wrapped in
  silver fabric for 2 weeks; 5 million visitors
- The work: the act of realizing it (decades of permits, financing, logistics) as
  much as the visible result; the temporary displacement of the familiar; beauty through
  concealment

### Nauman: What Art Can Ask

Bruce Nauman (b. 1941) — the widest-ranging of the conceptualists:

- **Neon texts**: "The True Artist Helps the World by Revealing Mystic Truths"
  (1967) — self-satirizing? sincere? the neon is a commercial medium for the statement
- **HUMAN/NEED/DESIRE (1983)**: stacked neon words, one word per tube, cycling —
  HUMAN, then NEED, then DESIRE; the needs we cannot satisfy
- **Clown Torture (1987)**: video monitors everywhere, clowns in distress — extended
  joke taken to the point of cruelty; video as psychological oppression medium

## Postmodernism in Art

### Appropriation and the Death of Originality

```
POSTMODERN ART'S CLAIMS
==========================

  THEORETICAL BASE:
  - Roland Barthes, "The Death of the Author" (1967):
    The meaning of a text is not in the author's intention
    but in the reader's reading. The author is irrelevant.
  - Jean Baudrillard: the simulacrum -- copies precede
    originals; the map precedes the territory;
    reality IS representation, not the reverse.
  - Derrida: no text has a fixed, determinate meaning.

  IN ART:
  - Sherrie Levine, After Walker Evans (1981):
    Rephotographed Evans's Depression-era FSA photographs
    (already iconic -- already in museums) and exhibited
    them as her work. The aura of the original?
    The concept of authorship? Of originality? Of art?

  - Richard Prince: rephotographed Marlboro cowboy ads.
    "Are you a picture of a picture, or a painting?"
    Court cases (the Cariou v Prince case established that
    "transformation" allows appropriation).

  - Cindy Sherman, Untitled Film Stills (1977-1980):
    69 photographs of herself as different characters
    from imaginary B-movies. She is photographer, model,
    character -- all simultaneously. There is no "real"
    Sherman underneath the performance.
    The question: which of these women is the authentic self?
    Answer: none; selfhood is performance, not essence.
```

### Basquiat: Race, Commodity, Neo-Expressionism

Jean-Michel Basquiat (1960–1988):

- Began as SAMO, a street-art tag (graffiti as art world intervention)
- Neo-Expressionist painting: text, symbols, anatomical diagrams, crowns, racial
  slurs written and crossed out — the Black male body navigating white art world
- Commodified extremely fast: Warhol collaboration, Andy Warhol Factory, art
  market speculation. Died of heroin overdose at 27.
- The work is simultaneously: evidence of genius, document of Black American
  experience, commodity exploited by the white art market

## Digital Art and NFTs

### Net.art (1994–2001)

```
NET.ART: THE FIRST WAVE
=========================

  jodi.org (Joan Heemskerk and Dirk Paesmans):
  - Websites that appeared to be error messages,
    broken HTML, corrupt files
  - Revealed the code underlying the interface
  - The bug as aesthetic form

  Olia Lialina: My Boyfriend Came Back from the War (1996)
  - Hyperlink narrative: a story told through clicking
  - The HTML interface IS the medium, not a container for art
  - The browser window as material

  CHARACTERISTIC:
  Net.art used the internet as both medium and subject.
  It was not art about the internet; it was art IN the internet.
  Its context was the browser; it could not be hung on a wall.
  This created immediate institutional problems:
  How do you collect it? Exhibit it? Preserve it?
```

### NFTs: What They Actually Are

```
NFT EPISODE (2017-2022)
==========================

  WHAT AN NFT IS:
  Non-Fungible Token: a record on a blockchain that
  certifies the "ownership" of a specific digital asset.

  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ WHAT NFTS SOLVED (genuine problem):                   │
  │                                                       │
  │ Digital files are infinitely copyable. There is no   │
  │ "original." Photography had this problem for physical │
  │ prints; the edition system (numbered, signed) was the │
  │ solution. NFTs are the digital equivalent of the     │
  │ edition number -- a certificate of limited issuance. │
  │                                                       │
  │ Digital provenance: who made it first, who bought it │
  │ when -- this IS genuinely useful for artists and     │
  │ collectors of digital work.                          │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHAT NFTS DID NOT SOLVE:
  - The file itself is usually NOT on the blockchain
    (too large) -- only the token is. The file is
    typically on IPFS or a centralized server.
    If the server disappears, the NFT points to nothing.
  - Owning the NFT does not grant copyright.
    Anyone can copy the image. The "original" is the
    token record, not the image.
  - The art quality: much NFT art was low effort,
    speculative junk (CryptoPunks, Bored Ape Yacht Club)
    chosen for investment potential, not aesthetic value.

  BEEPLE'S "EVERYDAYS: THE FIRST 5000 DAYS" (2021):
  - Christie's auction: $69.3 million
  - A collage of 5,000 daily digital images
  - Established NFTs as "real" art market
  - The buyer (Vignesh Sundareswaran / MetaKovan):
    had financial interests in the NFT market
  - The record set the market; the market collapsed 2022

  WHAT SURVIVES:
  The provenance concept is real and useful.
  The speculative bubble collapsed.
  Artists who want digital-native collectors will continue
  using blockchain-based certificates.
  The form is not the hype.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Movement/period | Central question | What it rejects | What it produces |
|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Pop Art | What is art if everything is already an image? | Abstract Expressionism's individualism | Seriality, silk-screen, appropriation |
| Minimalism | What is the irreducible experience? | Illusion and expression | Object + viewer in real space |
| Conceptual | Is the idea the art? | The object itself | Language, instructions, propositions |
| Feminist art | Whose body is represented and how? | The male gaze; canon exclusion | Recovery, deconstruction, body art |
| Land Art | What if the work is the land itself? | The gallery system | Earthworks, temporary, site-specific |
| Postmodernism | Can there be originality after images are all copies? | Modernist authenticity | Appropriation, pastiche, critique |

## Common Confusion Points

**Pop is not uncritical of consumer culture**: Warhol's refusal to explain his
intentions has been misread as celebration of capitalism. But repetition de-auratic-izes
the image (Benjamin); the serialization of Marilyn's face the same week she died has
a cold, death-adjacent quality. The ambiguity is productive, not evasive.

**Minimalism and decoration**: Judd's boxes in Marfa look decorative in photos. In
person they are confrontational — the scale, weight, material presence, and the
serialized repetition produce an experience photos cannot convey. The decisive
argument for Minimalism requires physical encounter, not reproduction.

**Conceptual art has objects**: "Dematerialization" is not absolute. Many Conceptual
artworks do have physical forms (Kosuth's chair installation). What changes is the
relationship between the object and the idea — the idea now has priority over the
object's craft or beauty.

**NFT ownership**: Buying an NFT of a digital artwork does not give you copyright
to that artwork. You own a token. The distinction matters enormously for anyone
thinking about NFT art as an investment or collection.

**Postmodern appropriation and copyright**: Richard Prince's appropriation practice
has been repeatedly litigated. The Cariou v Prince decision (2013) established a
relatively wide "transformative use" standard. This is US law, not universal.
What counts as transformation versus infringement is genuinely unsettled.

**Basquiat's posthumous market**: Basquiat's prices have exploded posthumously to
among the highest for any American artist. The market dynamics — a young Black artist
who died at 27, the contemporary art market's hunger for diversity narratives, the
NFT moment creating new buyers — are entangled with the genuine quality of the work.
Separating the two requires looking at the work, not the price.
