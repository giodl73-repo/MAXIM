# 07 — Early Modernism

## The Big Picture

```
EARLY MODERNISM MAP (c. 1905–1930)
=====================================

  1905: Fauvism (Paris) -- Matisse, Derain, Vlaminck
        Color completely unchained from description
                    |
  1905: Die Brücke (Dresden) -- Expressionism
        Kirchner, Heckel, Nolde
                    |
  1908-1913: CUBISM (Paris) -- Picasso / Braque
        Analytic Cubism (1908-1912):
          Multiple simultaneous viewpoints, monochrome
        Synthetic Cubism (1912-1914):
          Collage, papier collé, newspaper fragments
                    |
  1909: Futurism (Italy) -- Marinetti manifesto
        Boccioni, Severini, Balla
        Speed, machine, dynamism, violence
                    |
  1910: Der Blaue Reiter (Munich) -- Kandinsky / Marc
        Inner necessity, toward pure abstraction
                    |
  1913: Armory Show (New York) -- European modernism
        hits the American public
                    |
  1916: DADA (Zurich / Berlin / New York / Paris)
        Tzara, Arp, Ball, Hausmann, Duchamp
        Anti-art, anti-war, institutional critique
                    |
  1924: Surrealism (Paris) -- Breton's First Manifesto
        Ernst, Dalí, Magritte, Kahlo, Miro

  STRUCTURAL CONDITION:
  WWI (1914-1918) destroys the confidence in European
  civilization that had underwritten academic art's
  claim to represent universal human progress.
  Dada is the direct response; Surrealism the elaboration.
```

## Fauvism (1905–1908)

### Matisse: Color as Autonomous Form

Henri Matisse (1869–1954) is the central figure; Fauvism is the first, not the last,
of his innovations:

```
THE FAUVE FORMAL PROGRAM
==========================

  AT THE 1905 SALON D'AUTOMNE:
  A critic, Louis Vauxcelles, refers to the work as that
  of "fauves" (wild beasts) -- Matisse et al. accept the name.

  WHAT FAUVISM DOES TO COLOR:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Before Fauvism: even Impressionism and Post-Impressionism  │
  │ related color to observation. Seurat: scientific.         │
  │ Van Gogh: emotionally heightened but still FROM the scene.│
  │                                                           │
  │ Fauvism: color is AUTONOMOUS.                             │
  │                                                           │
  │ The skin can be green.                                    │
  │ The shadow can be red.                                    │
  │ The road can be pink.                                     │
  │ Color is chosen for its PICTORIAL function --             │
  │ its relation to other colors, its expressive energy --    │
  │ NOT for its descriptive accuracy.                         │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WOMAN WITH A HAT (1905):
  Matisse's wife with a hat and jacket.
  The face: green, orange, purple -- shadows are not the
  absence of light but chromatic complements and contrasts.
  A collector bought it; others at the Salon were appalled.

  FORMAL PRIORITIES:
  - Flat color areas (like Gauguin's cloisonné)
  - Line and color operating INDEPENDENTLY
    (line doesn't bound color; color doesn't follow line)
  - Decoration as a serious aesthetic value
    (Matisse: "an art of balance, purity... like a good
    armchair in which to rest")
```

### Matisse's Longer Career

Fauvism was a two-year moment. Matisse's career spans 65 years:

- **Dance (1909–1910, Hermitage)**: five figures, circle, primary red-orange + blue +
  green, almost no space — pure form and movement
- **The Red Studio (1911)**: the studio as red field — everything absorbed into red;
  the artworks depicted are identifiable but flattened into the surface
- **Cut-outs (1940s–1954)**: after surgery, unable to paint standing; colored paper
  cut and arranged — Jazz (1947), The Snail (1953). The cut-out technique eliminates
  the distinction between drawing and color: "cutting directly into color"

## Expressionism (1905–1920s)

### Die Brücke (The Bridge, Dresden, 1905–1913)

Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, Erich Heckel, Karl Schmidt-Rottluff — inspired by:
- Munch's psychological intensity (*The Scream*, 1893)
- African and Oceanic objects in the Dresden ethnographic museum
- Gauguin's formal primitivism
- German late Gothic woodcut tradition (Dürer's era)

```
EXPRESSIONIST FORMAL VOCABULARY
==================================

  AGAINST:
  - Impressionist optical observation
  - Academic finish and beauty
  - Social propriety in subject matter

  FOR:
  - Psychological truth over visual accuracy
  - Distortion as emotional amplification
  - Raw, unfinished surfaces
  - "Ugly" subjects: prostitutes, nightclubs, the city's
    underside, anxiety, alienation

  KIRCHNER'S BERLIN STREET SCENES (1913-1915):
  - Prostitutes and crowds in Potsdamer Platz
  - Elongated, angular figures (El Greco influence)
  - Acid colors: green, purple, orange
  - The city as psychological threat

  WOODCUT AS PREFERRED PRINT MEDIUM:
  Die Brücke artists revived the woodcut -- the cut marks
  show, the grain shows, the directness of hand to material
  is visible. Antithesis of academic engraving's precision.
  Connects to German Gothic woodcut tradition (= historical
  nationalism as aesthetic strategy).
```

### Der Blaue Reiter (The Blue Rider, Munich, 1911–1914)

Wassily Kandinsky and Franz Marc — spiritually oriented, theoretical:

- **Kandinsky**: on the path to pure abstraction (see module 08-ABSTRACTION.md);
  the goal is spiritual expression through pure visual elements without reference
  to the physical world
- **Marc**: animals as subjects — horses as pure being, uncorrupted by human
  consciousness; Blue Horse I (1911); the Tower of Blue Horses (1913, lost)
- **Der Blaue Reiter Almanac (1912)**: the key document — Kandinsky and Marc compile
  essays and images juxtaposing folk art, Bavarian glass painting, African masks,
  medieval woodcuts, and modern work — the canon-leveling act: all is equally valid
  evidence of spiritual necessity in visual form

## Cubism (1908–1921): The Central Revolution

### Preconditions

1. **Cézanne's retrospective (Salon d'Automne, 1907)**: 56 paintings, a year after
   his death. Braque and Picasso both attend repeatedly.
2. **African and Iberian sculpture**: Picasso sees masks in the Trocadéro museum (1907)
   that he later describes as a revelation about non-European formal possibilities.
3. **Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907)**: usually cited as the proto-Cubist painting
   — five women (proto-Cubist formal vocabulary), the right two with faces influenced
   by African masks. The painting was not exhibited until 1916 — it was a studio
   working-out, not a public statement.

### Analytic Cubism (1908–1912)

```
ANALYTIC CUBISM: WHAT IT IS
==============================

  The core claim: a 3D object seen from a single viewpoint
  at a single instant loses most of its information.
  We know more about an object than one photograph of it
  can contain.

  FORMAL SOLUTION:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Show multiple simultaneous viewpoints in one image:      │
  │   - The face shown from front AND profile simultaneously │
  │   - The guitar shown from above AND from the side        │
  │   - The surface of a table tilted to show the top        │
  │   - The drawer open to show its contents                 │
  │   - Multiple light sources from different angles         │
  │                                                          │
  │ MONOCHROME (near-black, grey, ochre):                    │
  │   Color is eliminated to prevent the eye from            │
  │   "reading" color as surface or object.                  │
  │   Only form and structure remain.                        │
  │                                                          │
  │ SHALLOW SPACE:                                           │
  │   The multiple planes stack in a shallow zone,           │
  │   not in deep illusionistic space.                       │
  │   The picture surface is acknowledged, not hidden.       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PICASSO: Ma Jolie (1911-1912): A guitar-playing figure.
  Almost completely unreadable without knowing the subject.
  The stenciled text ("Ma Jolie") is a fragment of reality
  anchoring the analytic dissolution.

  BRAQUE: Man with a Guitar (1911): The same approach.
  Braque and Picasso worked in such close collaboration
  (1908-1914) that their Analytic Cubist works are
  sometimes difficult to distinguish -- they kept their
  paintings unsigned until exhibition.

  THE SPACETIME CONNECTION:
  Contemporaries (and later critics) connected Analytic
  Cubism to Einstein's 4D spacetime (special relativity 1905).
  The idea: if space and time are unified, an object's position
  in time (its appearance from different angles at different
  moments) can be shown simultaneously.
  BUT: Picasso was not reading Einstein. The connection is
  structural, not causal -- parallel responses to a Zeitgeist,
  not direct influence.
```

### Synthetic Cubism (1912–1921)

```
SYNTHETIC CUBISM: THE COLLAGE TURN
=====================================

  INVENTION: Braque and Picasso simultaneously, 1912
  Braque: papier collé (glued paper)
  Picasso: Still Life with Chair Caning (1912) -- oil on
    canvas with oilcloth fragment (chair caning pattern) +
    rope frame

  THE REVERSAL:
  Analytic Cubism: started with the world, broke it down
  Synthetic Cubism: starts with materials, builds up

  MATERIALS INTRODUCED:
  - Newspaper fragments (Le Journal -- the title visible)
  - Wallpaper samples
  - Sheet music fragments
  - Playing cards
  - Cigarette packages

  WHAT COLLAGE DOES:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. REAL vs DEPICTED: the newspaper is real; the drawing  │
  │    around it is depicted. What is the boundary?          │
  │                                                          │
  │ 2. SIGN vs OBJECT: the newspaper's text signifies news,  │
  │    date, text. It is also a visual texture. Both at once.│
  │                                                          │
  │ 3. TROMPE L'OEIL REVERSED: Classical trompe l'oeil      │
  │    painted things to look real. Collage inserts real     │
  │    things into painting. The hierarchy inverts.          │
  │                                                          │
  │ 4. COLOR RETURNS: collage materials bring color and       │
  │    texture that Analytic Cubism eliminated               │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Futurism (1909–1944)

### The Manifesto Machine

Futurism begins as text: Filippo Tommaso Marinetti's First Futurist Manifesto
(Le Figaro, Paris, February 20, 1909):

"We declare that the splendor of the world has been enriched by a new beauty: the
beauty of speed. A racing car with its bonnet adorned with great tubes like serpents
with explosive breath... is more beautiful than the Victory of Samothrace."

```
FUTURIST FORMAL PROGRAM
=========================

  AGAINST:
  - Tradition, museums, antiquity ("We will destroy the museums")
  - Stasis, harmony, the past
  - Feminism, pacifism (explicitly)

  FOR:
  - Speed, technology, machinery
  - Simultaneity of sensation
  - Violence and war as hygienic
  - The factory, the automobile, the aeroplane

  FORMAL STRATEGIES:
  - Simultaneous motion: multiple positions of moving
    object in same image (Balla's Dynamism of a Dog
    on a Leash -- the dog's legs and tail multiplied
    into a blur -- cartoon motion lines made in paint)
  - Force lines (lines of force): directional marks
    showing energy fields and movement through space
  - Interpenetrating planes from Cubism + color + motion

  BOCCIONI'S States of Mind (1911): three paintings
  depicting the railway station as emotional states:
  The Farewells / Those Who Go / Those Who Stay
  The train's force lines move through and between
  the human figures -- body and machine interpenetrate.

  FUTURISM'S POLITICS:
  Marinetti supported Italian Fascism.
  The aesthetic program (machine worship, violence as
  beauty, destruction of tradition) was ideologically
  continuous with Fascism's actual program.
  This is not coincidental -- it is the logic of the
  aesthetic position taken to its conclusions.
  Benjamin: the aestheticization of politics (Fascism)
  vs. the politicization of aesthetics (Communism).
```

## Dada (1916–1924)

### The Anti-Art Response to WWI

Hugo Ball opens the Cabaret Voltaire in Zurich, February 5, 1916:

```
DADA'S CONTEXT AND CLAIM
==========================

  WWI:
  - 8.5 million soldiers dead
  - Poison gas, trench warfare, industrial killing
  - The European civilization that produced Beethoven,
    Kant, Raphael, and Newton also produced this
  - The cultural confidence that sustained academic art
    and bourgeois taste was a lie

  DADA'S POSITION:
  If civilization produced this, then:
  - Art that upholds civilization is complicit
  - The aesthetic ideals (beauty, harmony, truth) are
    the ideals of the civilization that started the war
  - Therefore: destroy the ideals themselves

  "DADA" = chosen for its meaninglessness:
  Dada means rocking horse in French, yes-yes in Russian,
  hobbyhorse in German. It was chosen from a dictionary
  at random. The name performs the program.

  TECHNIQUES:
  - Chance operations: Arp's collages made by dropping
    torn paper and arranging it where it falls
  - Noise poetry: Schwitters' Merzbau (assembled junk)
  - Sound poetry: Ball's phonetic poems (pure sound,
    no semantic content) -- precursor to performance art
  - Photomontage: cutting and reassembling photographs
    (Hausmann, Hoch, Heartfield)
```

### Duchamp's Readymades: The Institutional Critique

Marcel Duchamp (1887–1968) is Dada's most consequential figure:

```
THE READYMADE AS PROPOSITION
================================

  Fountain (1917):
  - R. Mutt submits a urinal (manufactured by J.L. Mott
    Ironworks) to the Society of Independent Artists
    exhibition (which had promised to accept all works
    submitted with the $6 entry fee)
  - The selection committee rejects it
  - Duchamp (who submitted it) resigns from the committee
  - Alfred Stieglitz photographs it (the photograph
    becomes the primary record -- the original is lost)

  THE ARGUMENT:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ A work of art is defined by:                             │
  │ A. The artist's designation (I call it art)              │
  │ B. The institutional context (exhibition = art context)  │
  │ C. The discourse around it (art critics discuss it)      │
  │                                                          │
  │ NOT by:                                                  │
  │ - Craft (the artist did not make it)                    │
  │ - Beauty (a urinal is not conventionally beautiful)     │
  │ - Uniqueness (it is mass-produced)                      │
  │ - Intention to make art (Mutt is anonymous)             │
  │                                                          │
  │ If an artist can designate any object as art,           │
  │ then what is art? The question is the point.            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  OTHER READYMADES:
  - Bicycle Wheel (1913): wheel mounted on a stool --
    first readymade, before he had a theory of it
  - In Advance of the Broken Arm (1915): snow shovel
  - L.H.O.O.Q. (1919): Mona Lisa postcard with moustache
    drawn on; title sounds like "she has a hot ass" in French.
    The most famous artwork in the world defaced
    as institutional critique.

  LEGACY:
  Duchamp's readymades are the founding documents of
  Conceptual Art (1960s), installation art, and every
  subsequent debate about "what counts as art."
  His claim: art is about ideas, not objects.
  The idea is transferable; the object is incidental.
```

### Dada Photomontage as Political Weapon

Berlin Dada (1918–1920) — Raoul Hausmann, Hannah Höch, John Heartfield:

- **Hannah Höch, Cut with the Dada Kitchen Knife Dada through the Last Weimar Beer-Belly
  Cultural Epoch of Germany (1919–1920)**: 90 x 144cm photomontage; military leaders,
  politicians, machine parts, athletes, Einstein, the kaiser — the world as cut-up
  chaos of mass media images. The title is itself a manifesto.

- **Heartfield (Helmut Herzfeld)**: changed his name as anti-German act during WWI;
  used photomontage for direct political attack against Hitler and the Nazis (AIZ
  magazine covers, 1930–1938); *Adolf the Superman: Swallows Gold and Spouts Tin* (1932).

Photomontage uses mass media against itself: the material is newspaper photographs,
already political objects, cut and reassembled to reveal what the original photographs
concealed. The strategy is: the medium carries meaning in its material.

## The Connections: Cubism and Spacetime

The question whether Cubism was influenced by Einstein's special relativity (1905):

```
CUBISM / RELATIVITY PARALLEL
================================

  EINSTEIN (1905):
  - No absolute simultaneity
  - Space and time unified (spacetime)
  - An observer's measurement depends on their frame
    of reference
  - What counts as "the position of an object" is
    relative to the observer's velocity

  ANALYTIC CUBISM (1908-1912):
  - No single correct viewpoint
  - Multiple temporal moments shown simultaneously
  - What counts as "the appearance of an object" is
    relative to the observer's position

  STRUCTURAL PARALLEL: YES
  DIRECT INFLUENCE: PROBABLY NOT
    - Picasso had no documented exposure to Einstein
    - The technical content of special relativity
      was not widely understood outside physics
    - The parallel is real but likely convergent:
      both responding to the same historical moment's
      challenge to fixed, universal viewpoints

  Henderson, Linda Dalrymple: "The Fourth Dimension
  and Non-Euclidean Geometry in Modern Art" (1983)
  -- the actual connection was through popular
  writings about the "fourth dimension" (not
  Einstein's technical spacetime but a popular
  concept circulating in literary/artistic circles)
```

|----------|--------------|---------|---------|
| Fauvism | Color is autonomous, not descriptive | Matisse, Woman with a Hat (1905) | Still tied to representation |
| Die Brücke | Psychic interiority over external reality | Kirchner, Berlin street scenes | "Primitivism" borrows without attribution |

## Common Confusion Points

**Fauvism is not Expressionism**: Both use non-naturalistic color. Fauvism is
primarily formal (color relationships, decoration, pleasure). Expressionism is
primarily emotional and psychological (inner states, anxiety, alienation). Matisse
said he wanted his art to be "like a comfortable armchair"; Kirchner was depicting
neurosis.

**Picasso "invented" Cubism**: Braque and Picasso developed Analytic Cubism in
close, documented collaboration. For two years they deliberately made it hard to
distinguish who made which painting. Attribution between 1908-1912 is sometimes
genuinely uncertain. "Picasso invented Cubism" is simplification.

**African art and Cubism**: Picasso's encounter with African masks is documented
and his formal debt is real. The ethical problem is that he used the formal
properties while stripping the objects of their cultural context and meaning —
the same "primitivism" critique that applies to Gauguin. The formal innovation
does not cancel the extractive relationship.

**Duchamp's Fountain was not displayed**: It was rejected from the exhibition and
photographed by Stieglitz. The photograph is the primary record. Subsequent
"originals" (from 1964 onward) are Duchamp-authorized replicas. The original
was likely thrown away. The most famous anti-art object of the 20th century
survives only as a photograph.

**Dada is not nihilism**: Dada's destruction is purposive. Ball, Tzara, Heartfield,
Höch all had political positions — pacifism, socialism, anti-nationalism. The
destruction of cultural hierarchy was in service of human values that the
destroyed culture had betrayed. Dada is not "nothing matters" — it is "these
particular claims to matter are fraudulent."
