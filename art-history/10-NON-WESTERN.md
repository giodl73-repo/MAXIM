## Chinese Literati Painting (Wenrenhua)

### The Ideal and Its Origins

*Wenrenhua* (文人画) = literati painting; literally "cultured men's painting."
The tradition asserts that painting by scholar-bureaucrats (the *wenren*) is inherently
superior to painting by professional craftsmen — because the amateur's purpose is
self-expression and cultivation, not commercial production.

```
WENREN IDEAL vs PROFESSIONAL PAINTING
========================================

  PROFESSIONAL PAINTER:           LITERATI PAINTER:
  ┌───────────────────────────┐   ┌───────────────────────────────┐
  │ Hired for skill            │   │ Paints as personal cultivation │
  │ Client sets the subject    │   │ Chooses subject for meaning   │
  │ Technical virtuosity valued│   │ Technical ease APPEARS effortless│
  │ Illusionistic rendering    │   │ Brushwork = moral character   │
  │ Color, decoration           │   │ Ink, not color (primarily)    │
  │ Work as product            │   │ Work as self-portrait of mind │
  └───────────────────────────┘   └───────────────────────────────┘

  THE CLAIM: You can read a person's moral character from their brushwork.
  A controlled, virtuous brush = a controlled, virtuous person.
  A hasty, careless brush = a careless person.

  This connects painting to calligraphy:
  Both use brush and ink.
  Both are judged on the same criteria: qi (spirit resonance),
  yun (resonance), bone structure, muscle.
  The Chinese character and the painted landscape rock
  are made by the same hand with the same instrument
  and are judged by the same aesthetic criteria.
```

### The Four Masters of Yuan (c. 1280–1368)

Under Mongol rule (Yuan dynasty), many Han Chinese scholar-officials withdrew from
government service and developed the literati aesthetic to its fullest:

- **Huang Gongwang (1269–1354)**: *Dwelling in the Fuchun Mountains* (1350) — long
  handscroll depicting the mountains along the Fuchun River; completed over 3-4 years;
  the brushwork records time passing in its variations
- **Wu Zhen (1280–1354)**: bamboo as the literati subject par excellence; bamboo bends
  but does not break — moral resilience; its hollow stem = the empty mind receptive
  to truth
- **Ni Zan (1301–1374)**: the emptiest painter in the tradition — minimal rocks,
  a pavilion or two, empty water, distant mountains; deliberately sparse; he said he
  was not depicting mountains and rocks but depicting "the untrammeled spirit in my
  breast"
- **Wang Meng (c. 1308–1385)**: dense, complex compositions, obsessive brushwork

```
THE LITERATI LANDSCAPE: WHAT IT DEPICTS
==========================================

  The landscape is not an empirical record of a specific place
  (though it may be inspired by one).

  It is a MENTAL LANDSCAPE: the painter's inner state.

  NI ZAN's compositions:
  - Foreground: a few bare rocks and trees
  - Middle ground: empty water (often no actual lake --
    just unpainted white space)
  - Far distance: faint mountains

  The emptiness is NOT failure to complete the painting.
  It IS the painting's subject: emptiness = spiritual freedom,
  the mind unencumbered by desire and attachment.

  HANDSCROLL vs HANGING SCROLL:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ HANDSCROLL: horizontal, unrolled progressively        │
  │ - Time-based: the landscape reveals itself section    │
  │   by section as you unroll it                        │
  │ - Not displayed -- handled, stored, shared            │
  │ - Inscriptions and colophons by subsequent owners     │
  │   accumulate: the scroll is a community object        │
  │                                                       │
  │ HANGING SCROLL: vertical, displayed on a wall         │
  │ - Seasonal: changed with the season or occasion       │
  │ - Not permanently displayed (would fade/bore)         │
  │ - The tokonoma (alcove) is designed for it            │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Japanese Visual Tradition

### Yamato-e to Ink Painting: Sesshu

*Yamato-e* (Japanese painting, c. 9th–15th c.): flat color, strong outline, no
cast shadows, bird's-eye view — the native Japanese decorative tradition, often
depicting court life, Tales of Genji, seasonal subjects.

**Sesshu Toyo (1420–1506)**: the peak of Japanese ink painting (*suiboku-ga*):

- Trained in Zen Buddhism; studied Chinese Song Dynasty painting in China (1467–1469)
- *Winter Landscape* (Haboku-sansui): a few brushstrokes suggesting mountains, tree,
  and cliff; minimal but total — the mountain is more present in suggestion than
  in complete depiction
- *Long Landscape Scroll* (1486): 16-meter handscroll of the four seasons — the most
  complete statement of the Japanese ink landscape tradition

### Ukiyo-e: The Floating World

*Ukiyo-e* (浮世絵) = "pictures of the floating world" — woodblock prints depicting
the pleasure districts of Edo (Tokyo) in the 17th–19th centuries:

```
UKIYO-E: MEDIUM AND MEANING
==============================

  "FLOATING WORLD" (ukiyo):
  Buddhist concept of the transient world of pleasure --
  theater, music, courtesans, wrestling, sumo.
  Originally melancholy (impermanence = suffering).
  By Edo period: affirmative celebration of the transient.

  WOODBLOCK PRINTING TECHNIQUE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Artist draws composition on paper                     │
  │ 2. Block carver cuts blocks (one per color + keyblock)  │
  │ 3. Printer registers each block precisely                │
  │ 4. Each impression adds one color layer                  │
  │ 5. A full-color print: 5-20 blocks                       │
  │                                                          │
  │ COLLABORATION: artist, carver, printer, publisher        │
  │ are separate people. The artist designed; specialists   │
  │ executed. Like software: the algorithm vs the build.    │
  │                                                          │
  │ PRODUCTION: editions of hundreds or thousands           │
  │ Price: affordable for urban middle class                │
  │ Subject: contemporary life, not mythology              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY ARTISTS:
  - Katsushika Hokusai (1760-1849): Thirty-Six Views of
    Mount Fuji (1831-1833) -- The Great Wave off Kanagawa;
    Fuji as constant against the turbulent world
  - Utagawa Hiroshige (1797-1858): One Hundred Famous
    Views of Edo; The Sudden Shower over Shin-Ohashi Bridge --
    rain depicted as diagonal lines; the city in weather
  - Kitagawa Utamaro (1753-1806): bijin-ga (beautiful women);
    close-up, intimate, the neck and nape as erotic
  - Toshusai Sharaku (c. 1795): kabuki actor portraits;
    psychologically intense, exaggerated; active for only
    10 months -- identity unknown
```

### Japonisme: The Western Debt

From the 1860s onward, Japanese prints flooded Paris:

- **Van Gogh**: copied Hiroshige prints in oil (Plum Park, Sudden Shower at Ohashi),
  collected hundreds of prints; the flat color, radical cropping, and high horizon
  in his work comes directly from ukiyo-e
- **Manet**: The Japanese woman in La Japonaise (1876) — awkward Orientalism
- **Degas**: the high viewpoint, asymmetric compositions, figures cropped by the
  frame — the photographic framing Degas is credited with inventing was already
  present in ukiyo-e 50 years earlier
- **Toulouse-Lautrec**: the poster aesthetic (flat color, strong silhouette, diagonal
  composition) is ukiyo-e applied to commercial lithography
- **Whistler**: "Japonesque" aestheticism; the peacock room; the arrangement as
  aesthetic object (not decorative accessory)

## Islamic Art and Architecture

### The Prohibition Debate

The common claim: "Islam forbids figural representation." The actual situation:

```
FIGURAL ART IN ISLAMIC TRADITION
====================================

  THE HADITH CITATIONS (against images):
  "Those who make images will be among those who receive
  the most severe punishment on the Day of Resurrection."
  (Attributed to the Prophet -- hadith literature)

  THE ACTUAL PRACTICE:
  - Persian and Mughal miniature painting: elaborate
    figural scenes, portraits of rulers, mythological
    narratives -- THROUGHOUT Islamic history
  - Umayyad palaces (Qusayr Amra, Jordan, 8th c.):
    frescoes of nude women, hunting scenes, zodiac dome
  - The prohibition applies most strictly to SACRED
    contexts (mosques) and possibly to worship objects

  THE DISTINCTION:
  Sacred space (mosque): non-figural almost always
  Secular space (palace, manuscript, textile): figural
    representation common in many traditions

  CONCLUSION:
  The "Islam forbids images" rule is an oversimplification.
  The prohibition is context-specific, contested, and
  historically variable across different regions and periods.
  Iranian, Ottoman, Mughal, and North African traditions
  diverged significantly in their practices.
```

### Geometric Art: The Mathematical Dimension

Islamic geometric ornament (especially in architectural contexts) is among the most
mathematically sophisticated visual art ever produced:

```
ISLAMIC GEOMETRIC PATTERN: MATHEMATICAL STRUCTURE
====================================================

  UNDERLYING MATHEMATICS:
  - Tessellations: tiling the plane with repeating units
  - Symmetry groups: there are 17 distinct wallpaper
    symmetry groups; Islamic art explores many of them
  - Girih patterns (Persian): star polygons and irregular
    pentagons that tile without gaps
  - Quasicrystal structure: Peter Lu and Paul Steinhardt
    (2007) showed that Islamic girih patterns from c. 1200
    use the same mathematical principles as quasicrystals
    (aperiodic tiling discovered in Western mathematics
    only in 1973 by Penrose)

  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ ALHAMBRA PALACE (Granada, 13th-14th c.):             │
  │ Mathematicians have identified 13 of the 17 possible  │
  │ plane symmetry groups in the Alhambra's tilework.     │
  │ M.C. Escher visited in 1936; his repeating figure     │
  │ tessellations directly derive from this study.        │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE ARABESQUE:
  - Continuous, interlacing vine or geometric scroll
  - Fills the visual field completely -- no empty ground
  - Horror vacui (fear of empty space) as aesthetic choice
  - Each unit generates the next -- the pattern is in
    principle infinite; the frame stops it arbitrarily

  MUQARNAS:
  - Three-dimensional stalactite vaulting (pendentives)
  - Used in squinches and niches to transition between
    geometric forms (square plan to round dome)
  - Mathematical: muqarnas units follow geometric rules
    that allow arbitrary elaboration in any scale
  - The surface is dissolved into crystalline projection;
    the structural function is hidden; what is visible
    is pure geometric transformation
```

### Calligraphy as the Highest Art

In Islamic culture, calligraphy (*khatt*) is the highest visual art form because
the Quran is the word of God — writing that word is a sacred act:

- **Kufic**: the earliest formal script; angular, geometric; early Quran manuscripts
- **Naskh**: the cursive script that replaced Kufic for manuscript use
- **Thuluth**: monumental script for inscriptions; large, sweeping
- **Nastaliq**: Persian cursive; "the bride of scripts"; used in Persian poetry

Calligraphy in architecture: the band of Quranic text running around the interior
of the Dome of the Rock (691 CE) is the earliest major Islamic architectural
inscription; the text and the building are not separable.

## West African Art

### Benin Kingdom: Bronze and Brass

The Benin Kingdom (modern Nigeria, c. 1300–1897) produced one of the great
sculptural traditions:

```
BENIN BRONZES: TECHNICAL AND CONTEXTUAL FACTS
================================================

  TECHNIQUE: Lost-wax (cire perdue) casting
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Model in wax                                       │
  │ 2. Encase in clay                                     │
  │ 3. Heat: wax melts out (cire perdue = lost wax)       │
  │ 4. Pour molten metal into the void                    │
  │ 5. Break clay: unique bronze                          │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MATERIAL: "Bronzes" are actually brass (copper + zinc).
  The copper and zinc came via trans-Saharan trade routes
  (later via Portuguese trade from the 15th c. onward).

  FUNCTION: Royal altar objects, not aesthetic display.
  - Memorial heads of past Obas (kings) on ancestral altars
  - Plaques (c. 1500-1600): depictions of warriors, nobles,
    Portuguese traders -- historical records, not decoration

  THE 1897 EXPEDITION:
  British "Punitive Expedition" (1897) looted approximately
  3,000 bronzes, ivory carvings, and other objects from the
  royal palace. These are now in museums worldwide.
  The repatriation debate: Nigeria/Edo State government
  has been requesting return since 1960.
  As of the early 2020s: some German museums (Humboldt Forum,
  Stuttgart, Hamburg) returned collections; British Museum
  has not. The Benin Bronzes are the central case in the
  global repatriation debate.

  THE NATURALISM:
  The memorial heads of Obas show realistic portraiture --
  specific individuals, not type forms. This confronted
  19th-century European assumptions that sub-Saharan Africa
  had not developed "representational art." The assumption
  was racist and demonstrably false.
```

### Yoruba Sculpture and Masks

- **Ife bronzes (11th–15th c.)**: extraordinary naturalistic heads from the Yoruba
  city of Ife; the technical and aesthetic level so unexpected that early European
  observers attributed them to the lost civilization of Atlantis rather than
  acknowledge African origin
- **Gelede masks**: performed in annual festivals honoring the power of women
  (especially elder women and female ancestors); the masks are elaborate, often
  humorous, but the ritual function is to honor and propitiate the "mothers"
- **Egungun masquerade**: the ancestor literally present; the costume covers the
  performer completely; the ancestor (not the performer) dances in the world

**Key concept**: these objects are *functional*, not aesthetic in the Western sense.
They are activated by performance, by ritual, by the presence of community.
The Western museum decontextualizes them — what you see in the glass case is the
husk of an object whose life was in ceremony.

## Pre-Columbian Art

### Aztec (Mexica) Codices and Sculpture

The Aztec world view is encoded in visual objects:

```
AZTEC VISUAL CULTURE
======================

  CALENDRICAL SYSTEM:
  Two calendars running simultaneously:
  - 365-day solar calendar (xiuhpohualli): 18 months of 20
    days + 5 unlucky days; agricultural and civil
  - 260-day ritual calendar (tonalpohualli): 20 day signs x
    13 numbers; for divination and naming children

  The two calendars align every 52 years (a Calendar Round).
  The Sun Stone (the "Aztec Calendar" -- actually more
  than a calendar): 3.6m diameter, 25 tons, tonalpohualli
  encoded with the four previous suns (destroyed worlds)
  and Tonatiuh (the sun god) at center.

  AZTEC SCULPTURE:
  - Coatlicue (Mother Earth goddess): c. 2.5m, found 1790.
    Heads of two serpents forming her face, skulls and
    hands around her skirt. Simultaneously beautiful
    and terrifying in the same aesthetic object.
  - The Aztec aesthetic: terror and beauty are not opposed.
    The universe requires blood sacrifice to continue.
    The sculpture makes this theology visible.

  CODICES:
  Screenfold books made from bark paper or deer skin.
  Record: genealogies, tribute lists, ritual calendars,
  historical narratives, astronomical observations.
  Most destroyed by Spanish missionaries (1524-1560).
  What survives (15 Aztec/Mixtec codices) is a fraction.
  The Codex Borgia: 76 pages of ritual content -- complex
  diagrams of deities, astronomical periods, divination.
```

### Mayan Stelae and Architecture

The Maya developed one of the only fully logographic writing systems in the Americas:

- **Stelae**: upright stone monuments recording specific historical events --
  accession, capture of enemies, ritual events -- with Long Count calendar dates.
  These are not generic religious monuments; they are specific historical records.
- **The Long Count calendar**: the Maya tracked time from a mythological creation
  date (August 11, 3114 BCE in GMT correlation). A date like 9.15.0.0.0 4 Ahaw
  13 Yax encodes a specific day within millions of years of cyclical time.
- **Palenque, Temple of the Inscriptions**: King Pakal's tomb (683 CE); the
  sarcophagus lid depicts Pakal falling into the underworld on the World Tree —
  a complex cosmological image; was misread by Erich von Däniken as an astronaut

### Nazca Lines

The Nazca Lines (Ica, Peru, c. 200 BCE–700 CE): geometric lines, trapezoids,
and animal figures (condor, spider, hummingbird, monkey — up to 370m across)
drawn by removing dark surface stones to reveal the lighter ground beneath.

- **Scale**: only visible clearly from the air — but Nazca culture had no aircraft.
  They were planned and executed using simple geometry (string and stakes) and
  verified by surveying from elevated points.
- **Function**: probable ritual use (ritual walkways, astronomical alignments,
  water cult); not signals to extraterrestrials (the von Däniken claim)
- **Permanence**: the hyper-arid Nazca Desert (virtually no rain, no wind to
  erode) preserves the lines; they are fragile — tourist and agricultural
  encroachment threatens them

```
SYNCHRONIC ANALYSIS ACROSS TRADITIONS
========================================

  SAME CENTURY, DIFFERENT TRADITIONS:

  14th CENTURY (c. 1300-1400):
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ ITALY: Giotto breaks Byzantine convention; Trecento        │
  │        proto-Renaissance begins                            │
  │                                                            │
  │ CHINA: Yuan Dynasty Four Masters (Ni Zan, Wu Zhen,        │
  │        Wang Meng, Huang Gongwang) produce the literati    │
  │        tradition's definitive works                        │
  │                                                            │
  │ WEST AFRICA: Ife bronzes reach their peak naturalism      │
  │              (redated to 11th-15th c. range)              │
  │                                                            │
  │ MESOAMERICA: Late Classic Maya (Palenque, Uxmal)          │
  │                                                            │
  │ ISLAMIC: Alhambra completion; geometric art pinnacle       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  What is "the 14th century in art"?
  - The Eurocentric answer: Giotto, the beginning of the
    Renaissance, the end of the Gothic
  - The actual answer: a century with simultaneous
    high achievements across multiple traditions,
    none of which knows about the others
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Islamic art does not prohibit all figuration**: The prohibition is contextual
and historical, not universal. Persian manuscripts, Mughal miniatures, and Umayyad
palace frescoes all contain human figures. The mosque is the most restricted space;
secular contexts vary widely.

**Benin Bronzes are brass**: The common name "Benin Bronzes" is technically incorrect
— the metal is brass (copper-zinc alloy), not bronze (copper-tin). However, the name
is established usage and is not changed.

**Ukiyo-e artists did not have a single unified style**: Hokusai, Hiroshige, Utamaro,
Kuniyoshi, and Sharaku have significantly different visual languages. "Ukiyo-e" is
a genre classification (subject matter and technique) not a style.

**Chinese landscape is not plein air**: The literati painting tradition does not
generally depict specific places from direct observation (though landscape journeys
inspired some works). The landscape is assembled from learned conventions — a vocabulary
of rock types, tree types, water types — that expresses an inner state.

**The "Aztec Calendar" is not a calendar**: The Sun Stone (Piedra del Sol) is not
a functional calendar. It is a cosmological diagram encoding Aztec theology about
cyclical creation and destruction. The actual Aztec calendars were codices and
could be read and manipulated; the Sun Stone was a monumental religious object.

**Nazca Lines are not mysterious**: They required no advanced technology, no alien
assistance, and no aerial observation to construct. They required: string, stakes,
knowledge of basic geometry, and the labor of a medium-sized organized community.
The mystery is the deliberate mystification by popular culture, not an inherent
feature of the lines.
