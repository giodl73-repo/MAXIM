# Art History — Overview: Art as Evidence of Mind and Society

## The Big Picture

Art history is not aesthetics. It is the study of visual objects as evidence — of belief
systems, power structures, technical capabilities, trade networks, and cognitive states.
The discipline asks: what did this object mean to the people who made and used it, and
what does it mean to us now?

```
WHAT ART HISTORY STUDIES
========================

  OBJECT ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  (painting, sculpture, building, print, photograph, digital artifact)     │
                                                                            │
  FOUR ANALYTICAL FRAMES (apply in layers, not exclusive):                 │
                                                                            │
  1. FORMAL ANALYSIS          2. ICONOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS                     │
     ┌──────────────────┐        ┌──────────────────────────────┐          │
     │ Visual elements: │        │ What do depicted subjects    │          │
     │ line, color,     │        │ mean symbolically?           │          │
     │ composition,     │        │ Conventions, attributes,     │          │
     │ texture, space   │        │ allegory, narrative program  │          │
     └──────────────────┘        └──────────────────────────────┘          │
                                                                            │
  3. CONTEXTUAL ANALYSIS       4. MATERIALIST / SOCIAL ANALYSIS            │
     ┌──────────────────────┐      ┌──────────────────────────────┐        │
     │ Who commissioned?    │      │ Production conditions,       │        │
     │ For what site?       │      │ labor, trade networks,       │        │
     │ Original audience?   │      │ class, gender, race,         │        │
     │ Historical moment?   │      │ colonial extraction          │        │
     └──────────────────────┘      └──────────────────────────────┘        │
                                                                            │
  OUTPUT: Interpretation — always provisional, always arguable             │
  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Methodological Schools

The discipline has cycled through competing frameworks. No single method suffices;
sophisticated analysis deploys several simultaneously.

### Formalism — Wölfflin (1864–1945)

Heinrich Wölfflin's *Principles of Art History* (1915) proposed analyzing style through
five polar pairs, independent of content:

```
WÖLFFLIN'S FIVE POLARITIES
===========================

  Renaissance                    Baroque
  ───────────────────────────────────────
  Linear                    vs   Painterly
  (edges sharp, drawn)           (edges dissolve, light-defined)

  Plane                     vs   Recession
  (composition parallel          (composition in depth,
   to picture surface)            diagonal thrust)

  Closed form               vs   Open form
  (self-contained,               (elements escape frame,
   clear boundaries)              suggest continuation)

  Multiplicity              vs   Unity
  (parts legible as parts)       (parts fuse into whole)

  Absolute clarity          vs   Relative clarity
  (everything shown clearly)     (some things deliberately unclear)
```

Wölfflin's legacy: rigorous formal vocabulary. His problem: style evolves for reasons
formalism cannot explain — patronage, function, technology, ideology.

### Iconography / Iconology — Panofsky (1892–1968)

Erwin Panofsky's three-level analysis framework (from *Studies in Iconology*, 1939):

```
PANOFSKY'S THREE LEVELS
========================

  Level 1: PRE-ICONOGRAPHIC (natural subject matter)
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Identify what you literally see: a man in a robe raising      │
  │ his right hand over a prone figure                            │
  │ Tool: practical experience with the world                     │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              │
                              ▼
  Level 2: ICONOGRAPHIC (conventional subject matter)
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Identify the story: The Baptism of Christ                     │
  │ Attributes: dove = Holy Spirit, water = baptism ritual        │
  │ Tool: knowledge of literary sources, conventions, attributes  │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              │
                              ▼
  Level 3: ICONOLOGICAL (intrinsic meaning / cultural symptom)
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ What does it reveal about the mentality of the era?           │
  │ Why this moment? Why this patron? What anxieties, desires?    │
  │ Tool: synthetic intuition + historical knowledge              │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Iconology's limit: risks projecting modern meaning onto historical objects.

### Social History of Art — Hauser, Clark, Berger

Arnold Hauser (*Social History of Art*, 1951), T.J. Clark, John Berger (*Ways of Seeing*):
art as product of social relations, class struggle, ideology.

- Clark's thesis on Manet: the broken finish of *Olympia* is not technical failure but
  the visual register of social ambiguity in Haussmann's Paris — the uncertainty of class.
- Berger: "Men look at women. Women watch themselves being looked at." Representation
  as power structure, not innocent aesthetics.
- Problem: risks reducing art to symptom, losing the specificity of visual form.

### Feminist Art History — Nochlin, Pollock, Chicago

Linda Nochlin's 1971 essay "Why Have There Been No Great Women Artists?" reframed the
question: the problem is institutional exclusion, not innate ability.

Key interventions:
- Recovery projects: naming and documenting women artists erased by canon formation
- Griselda Pollock: the social spaces available to women (domestic interior, garden)
  shaped Impressionist women's work differently from men's
- Analysis of the male gaze as structural, not individual

### Post-Colonial / Global Critiques

- The Western canon was constructed in specific institutional contexts (Royal Academy,
  Winckelmann's Greece, New York's post-WWII ascendancy)
- "Primitivism" in 20th century art (Picasso's engagement with African objects) =
  aesthetic borrowing without cultural understanding or attribution
- Repatriation debates: Benin Bronzes, Elgin Marbles — where do objects belong?
- Global art history: synchronic analysis across cultures, not subordinate to Western arc

## The Analyzing-a-Work Workflow

```
SYSTEMATIC ANALYSIS PROTOCOL
==============================

  Step 1: SLOW LOOKING (5–10 minutes, no reading labels)
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ What is your eye drawn to first? Why?                  │
  │ What is the scale? How does it relate to your body?    │
  │ What medium and technique? What does the surface do?   │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                        │
                        ▼
  Step 2: FORMAL ANALYSIS
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ COMPOSITION: how are elements arranged? Triangles?     │
  │   Diagonals? Symmetry? What's centered/marginal?       │
  │ COLOR: hue, saturation, value. Warm vs cool. Local     │
  │   color vs optical color vs symbolic color             │
  │ LINE: contour, implied, directional flow               │
  │ SPACE: flat/illusionistic, foreground/middle/back      │
  │ LIGHT: source, quality, what it reveals/conceals       │
  │ TEXTURE: real vs simulated                             │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                        │
                        ▼
  Step 3: ICONOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ What narrative/subject?                                │
  │ What symbols, attributes, conventional signs?          │
  │ What is included? What conspicuously absent?           │
  │ What textual sources inform it?                        │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                        │
                        ▼
  Step 4: CONTEXTUAL ANALYSIS
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ PATRON: who commissioned, for what purpose, for        │
  │   what site? Private devotion? Public propaganda?      │
  │ ARTIST: training, workshop, career moment              │
  │ HISTORICAL MOMENT: what is happening politically,      │
  │   religiously, economically?                           │
  │ ORIGINAL SITE: altarpiece seen kneeling, portrait      │
  │   hung in state room — site changes meaning            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                        │
                        ▼
  Step 5: INTERPRETATION
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Synthesize all levels. What argument can you make?     │
  │ What does this object do that no other medium could?   │
  │ Why does it matter — then? now?                        │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Periodization: The Western Canon Arc

The standard Western periodization is a construct, but a useful scaffold:

```
WESTERN ART HISTORY TIMELINE
==============================

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  30,000 BCE   Prehistoric: Chauvet, Lascaux, Venus figurines
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  3000 BCE     Ancient Egypt: canonic grid, ka, registers, frontality
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  800 BCE      Ancient Greece: Geometric → Archaic → Classical → Hellenistic
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  500 BCE–     Rome: verism, triumphal relief narrative, mosaic
  400 CE
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  400–1400     Medieval: Byzantine (gold ground, inverse perspective),
               Romanesque (pilgrimage), Gothic (pointed arch, stained glass)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1400–1600    Renaissance: linear perspective (1413), Florentine
               quattrocento, High Renaissance, Northern Renaissance
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1600–1750    Baroque: tenebrism, drama, Counter-Reformation;
               Rococo: aristocratic ornament, frivolity
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1750–1850    Neoclassicism + Romanticism: Enlightenment vs sublime,
               political painting, photography arrives 1839
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1850–1905    Impressionism → Post-Impressionism: plein air, broken
               brushwork, optical color, proto-modernism
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1905–1945    Early Modernism: Fauvism, Expressionism, Cubism,
               Futurism, Dada, Surrealism
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1945–1970    Post-WWII: Abstract Expressionism (NY), Pop, Minimalism
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1970–2000    Conceptual, Performance, Land Art, Postmodernism,
               Feminist art, institutional critique
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  2000–        Digital, Net.art, NFTs, global contemporary
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Technology as Enabler of Style

A through-line the formalists miss: material and technical conditions shape what is
aesthetically possible.

```
TECHNOLOGY → STYLE CAUSATION CHAIN
=====================================

  TECHNOLOGY                  ENABLED STYLE
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Ground lapis lazuli         → Ultramarine blue (costly = holy/royal)
  (Afghanistan trade routes)    Marian blue, celestial prestige

  Glassmaking advances        → Gothic stained glass as primary medium
  (12th-13th c.)               (Bible in light, not stone)

  Oil binder (Van Eyck        → Glazing layers, luminous depth,
  codifies c.1420s)             slow revision — portrait realism possible

  Portable tin paint tubes    → Impressionism: leave the studio,
  (invented 1841)               paint outdoors, catch the moment

  Photography (Daguerre       → Crisis for academic painting;
  1839, Eastman 1888)           photography takes documentary role,
                                painting forced into expressionism

  Color photography (1907     → Modernism accelerates: if photography
  autochrome; mass by 1930s)    captures reality, what is painting FOR?

  Railway networks            → Impressionists travel to Normandy coast,
  (1840s–1870s France)          Barbizon forest, new suburban subjects

  Cheap newsprint/halftone    → Dada photomontage: cut-and-paste
  printing (1880s–1910s)        critique using mass media's own tools

  Internet                    → Net.art (1990s): Olia Lialina, jodi.org
                                Art about networks using networks

  Blockchain / NFTs (2017–)   → Digital provenance experiment
                                (problem solved ≠ art necessarily made)
```

## The Methodological Schools — Comparative Table

| School | Key Figures | Primary Question | Strength | Weakness |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|----------|----------|
| Formalism | Wölfflin, Fry, Greenberg | How does it look? | Rigorous visual analysis | Ignores context, ideology |
| Iconography | Panofsky, Ripa | What does it depict? | Recovers symbolic programs | Over-reads intent |
| Social History | Hauser, Clark, Berger | Who made it, for whom, under what conditions? | Grounds art in history | Reduces art to symptom |
| Psychoanalytic | Freud, Lacan, Krauss | What unconscious structures? | Opens latent content | Unfalsifiable |
| Feminist | Nochlin, Pollock, Chadwick | How does gender structure representation? | Exposes invisible ideologies | Risk of essentialism |
| Post-colonial | Said, hooks, Mosquera | Whose history? Whose canon? | Expands global scope | Can become presentist |
| Semiotics | Barthes, Metz | What systems of signs? | Precision about meaning-making | Can become bloodless |
| Reception theory | Baxandall, Jauss | What did audiences bring to it? | Recovers period eye | Evidence is thin |

## Global Art History vs Western Canon

The Western periodization — Greek → Roman → Medieval → Renaissance → Modern —
is geographically narrow and teleological. It implies progress toward Enlightenment ideals.

What it excludes or subordinates:
- Chinese painting traditions of equal sophistication and different priorities
- Islamic geometric art (non-figurative not from prohibition alone, but from mathematical delight)
- Sub-Saharan African sculpture, which Picasso "discovered" — but which had continuous
  sophisticated traditions he stripped of context
- Pre-Columbian monumental architecture and image-making
- South Asian sculptural and architectural traditions (Angkor Wat, Khajuraho)

The structural problem: art history was institutionalized in 19th-century German
universities with Greek antiquity as the baseline. That institutional origin still shapes
which objects are studied, where, and how.

## Analyzing a Work — Quick Reference Card

```
FORMAL ELEMENTS VOCABULARY
============================

  LINE        Contour / implied / gestural / geometric / diagonal
  SHAPE       Geometric vs organic; positive vs negative space
  FORM        3D: mass, volume, weight, void
  SPACE       Pictorial space: flat / shallow / deep / illusionistic
  COLOR       Hue / value / saturation; warm/cool; local vs optical vs symbolic
  TEXTURE     Real (sculpture, collage) vs simulated (painted fur)
  VALUE       Light/dark; tonal range; chiaroscuro
  COMPOSITION Symmetry / asymmetry; rule of thirds; triangles; diagonals
  SCALE       Hierarchical (importance) vs naturalistic vs environmental

  COMMON COMPOSITIONAL DEVICES:
  - Pyramidal composition (stability) — Leonardo's Madonna of Rocks
  - Diagonal thrust (movement) — Baroque altarpieces
  - S-curve / contrapposto (life) — Classical and Renaissance figure
  - Rule of thirds (dynamic balance) — landscape painting
  - All-over composition (no hierarchy) — Pollock, Islamic ornament
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet — Which Analytical Method?

| Question | Best Method |
|----------|-------------|
| How does the visual structure work? What holds it together? | Formal analysis (Wölfflin) |
| What does the iconography mean — who is that figure? | Iconographic analysis (Panofsky Level 2) |
| What does this reveal about the culture that made it? | Iconology (Panofsky Level 3) |
| How did production conditions, class, and money shape this? | Social history of art (Clark) |
| Why are women absent from canonical narrative? | Feminist art history (Nochlin/Pollock) |
| Why was this made here and not elsewhere? Trade routes, materials? | Material culture / economic history |
| What did contemporary audiences understand that we miss? | Reception theory (Baxandall's "period eye") |
| How does this object function in a system of signs? | Semiotics (Barthes) |
| Who does this canonical narrative erase or subordinate? | Post-colonial critique |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Art history" vs "aesthetics"**: Aesthetics is a philosophical discipline (what is
beauty? what is the beautiful object?) — Kant, Hume, Dewey. Art history is empirical
and historical. They overlap but are not the same.

**Attribution vs Authentication**: Attribution is scholarly judgment about who made a
work. Authentication is legal/commercial certification. They use overlapping but distinct
evidence and have very different stakes.

**Influence vs Convergence**: Two artists making similar forms simultaneously may be
influencing each other, or may be responding independently to the same conditions. Assume
neither without evidence.

**"Primitive" as a category**: Avoid without irony. The term encodes the Western
assumption that non-Western art is earlier on a single developmental arc. It is not.
Different traditions have different goals.

**Style vs Movement**: A movement is social and institutional (Impressionism = a
network of artists, a dealer, the Salon des Refusés). A style is formal. Artists can
work in a style without belonging to the movement, and movement members don't always
share a homogeneous style.

**Iconography vs Iconology**: Iconography = identification of subjects (Panofsky Level 2).
Iconology = interpretation of cultural meaning (Level 3). The word "iconography" is
frequently misused to mean iconology.

**Period Eye (Baxandall)**: Fifteenth-century Florentine merchants, trained to compute
volumes and percentages by eye, brought that skill to gauging painted drapery. Visual
competencies are historically specific. You cannot see a painting the way its original
audience did — you can only approximate.
