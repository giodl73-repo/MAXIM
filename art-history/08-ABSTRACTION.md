## Kandinsky (1866–1944): Toward Pure Abstraction

### The Theory in Concerning the Spiritual in Art

Wassily Kandinsky's *Über das Geistige in der Kunst* (1911) is the founding text
of non-representational painting. Its argument:

```
KANDINSKY'S ARGUMENT STRUCTURE
================================

  PREMISE 1: Materialism threatens civilization
  (Written in 1910 -- before WWI confirmed it)
  Materialism = reduction of everything to physical
  and economic explanations; spiritual dimension lost.

  PREMISE 2: Art can access the spiritual directly
  Without the mediation of religious narrative or
  representation of the physical world.

  MECHANISM: Pure visual elements trigger inner resonance
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ COLOR has inherent psychological effects:             │
  │                                                       │
  │ Yellow: sharp, insistent, "earthly" excitement       │
  │         (highest pitch of a trumpet)                 │
  │ Blue:   deep, infinite, heavenly, withdrawal         │
  │         (cello, then organ, then double bass)         │
  │ Red:    glowing, purposeful, confident               │
  │         (forte tuba)                                  │
  │ Green:  peaceful, motionless, bourgeois              │
  │         (middle violin)                               │
  │ White:  silence that is not dead, possibility        │
  │ Black:  dead silence, finality, nothingness           │
  │                                                       │
  │ COLOR + FORM combinations have distinct resonances   │
  │ Yellow works best in angular forms (triangle)        │
  │ Blue works best in circular forms                    │
  │ Red in squares                                        │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SYNESTHESIA CONNECTION:
  Kandinsky had likely synesthesia -- he heard color
  and saw music. His color-sound correspondences are
  not arbitrary metaphors but possibly direct perceptual
  experience.
  Scriabin (composer) was a friend; both explored
  music-color correspondence at the same moment.
```

### The First Abstract Watercolor (1910)

The "First Abstract Watercolor" (Pompidou collection) — attribution and date are
disputed (some argue it was dated retrospectively to 1910 but made later). Regardless
of the exact date, it marks the deliberate abandonment of recognizable objects:

- No identifiable objects remain
- Color areas and lines interact in spatial ambiguity
- The image is kinetic — forms seem to be in motion or dissolution

Kandinsky did not abandon all representation immediately. His Impressions,
Improvisations, and Compositions series show a gradient from observed reality
to pure abstraction. The Compositions (his most deliberate works) were preceded
by extensive studies. "Pure" abstraction was a program, not a single moment.

## Mondrian (1872–1944) and Neo-Plasticism

### The Reduction to Grid and Primary Colors

Piet Mondrian's development is the most documented self-willed reduction in art history:

```
MONDRIAN'S FORMAL EVOLUTION
==============================

  1907-1910: Post-Impressionist painting -- Dutch landscape
             Duinenlandschap (Dunes): still recognizable

  1911-1913: Cubist influence -- trees, architecture
             Still Life with Gingerpot I & II:
             The first version recognizable; the second
             almost abstract -- the same subject at two
             points in the self-reduction process

  1913-1917: Plus-and-Minus series:
             A seascape or a building reduced to short
             horizontal and vertical strokes arranged
             in oval compositions -- cruciform atoms

  1917:      De Stijl (The Style) journal founded with
             Theo van Doesburg.
             Neo-Plasticism: the new plastic art

  1920-1942: The canonical Mondrian:
             Black orthogonal lines (horizontal + vertical)
             dividing the canvas into asymmetric rectangles.
             Primary colors (red, blue, yellow) + non-colors
             (white, grey, black).
             Nothing curved. Nothing diagonal. Nothing natural.

  1940-1944: New York / Broadway Boogie-Woogie (1942-43):
             The lines become yellow and the grid loosens --
             the street grid of Manhattan + jazz syncopation.
             The reduction was not the final word.
```

### The Philosophical Claim

Neo-Plasticism was not just style — it was ontology:

```
MONDRIAN'S NEO-PLASTIC ARGUMENT
==================================

  PARTICULARS vs UNIVERSALS:
  - Natural forms (curves, irregular lines) = the particular
  - Right angles and primary colors = the universal
  - Art should transcend the particular toward the universal

  THE THEOSOPHICAL BACKGROUND:
  Mondrian was a committed theosophist (Blavatsky, Steiner).
  Theosophy: spiritual reality underlies the physical.
  Art should access the spiritual substrate.

  THE GRID:
  Horizontal = earth, female, rest
  Vertical = sky, male, dynamic
  The right angle = the stable resolution of opposites
  (male/female, space/time, particular/universal)

  THE PRIMARY COLORS:
  Red, yellow, blue = irreducible -- cannot be made from
  other colors (in pigment mixing). They are the
  "atoms" of color.
  White, black, grey = "non-colors" -- presence/absence
  of color, structural only.

  THE CLAIM:
  A painting composed ONLY of right angles and primary
  colors is not a depiction of anything. It IS the
  universal plastic order made visible.
```

## Malevich and Suprematism (1913–1935)

### Black Square (1915)

Kasimir Malevich's Black Square (exhibited December 1915, "The Last Futurist
Exhibition of Paintings 0.10, Petrograd"):

```
BLACK SQUARE: THE ZERO DEGREE
================================

  WHAT IT IS:
  - A black square (roughly -- the edges are not precise)
    painted on a white canvas
  - Hung in the "beautiful corner" of the gallery --
    the corner where Orthodox Russian homes traditionally
    hung their icons
  - This placement is deliberate: Suprematism replaces
    the icon (the sacred image of the divine) with the
    suprematist form (the sacred image of pure sensation)

  MALEVICH'S CLAIM:
  "I have transformed myself in the zero of form and
  dragged myself out of the rubbish pool of academic art.
  I have destroyed the ring of the horizon and gotten
  out of the circle of objects..."

  The Black Square = zero degree of representation.
  Beyond it: only white-on-white (Malevich 1918).
  After that: what?

  SUPREMATISM:
  "Supremacy of pure feeling" over representation.
  Pure geometric forms floating in white space.
  White = infinite space (not a surface or void --
  infinite pure possibility).
  The forms do not depict -- they ARE.
```

### Suprematist vs Constructivist

```
SUPREMATISM vs CONSTRUCTIVISM
================================

  SUPREMATISM (Malevich):             CONSTRUCTIVISM (Rodchenko,
                                       Tatlin, El Lissitzky):

  Pure spiritual sensation            Social utility / revolution
  Art for art's sake (ultimate)       Art in service of society
  Transcendent                        Material / immanent
  Withdrew from politics              Embraced Soviet program
  Against representation              Typography, propaganda,
                                       industrial design

  CONSTRUCTIVIST PRODUCTS:
  - Tatlin's Tower (proposed monument to Third International)
  - El Lissitzky's Proun compositions (geometric)
  - Soviet propaganda posters (Rodchenko)
  - Bauhaus influenced by Constructivist ideas
    via El Lissitzky and visiting Russian artists
```

## The Bauhaus (1919–1933)

### The Institution and Its Program

Walter Gropius founds the Bauhaus (Weimar, 1919) with a foundational manifesto:

```
BAUHAUS PROGRAM
=================

  FOUNDING PRINCIPLE:
  "The ultimate aim of all creative activity is building!
  The architect, sculptor, painter -- we must all return
  to craft!"

  ANTI-SEPARATION OF ART AND CRAFT:
  Academic art schools separated "fine art" (painting,
  sculpture) from "applied art" (furniture, textiles).
  This separation is ideologically loaded and practically
  damaging. The Bauhaus reunites them.

  WORKSHOP STRUCTURE:
  Each workshop had TWO masters:
  1. Meister der Form (Master of Form) -- a famous artist
  2. Meister des Handwerks (Master of Craft) -- a craftsman

  Why two masters? Artists knew design, craftsmen knew
  material. The goal: people who knew both.

  WORKSHOPS:
  Carpentry, metal, typography/graphic design, textiles,
  ceramics, stage design, mural painting, architecture
  (added later)

  PRELIMINARY COURSE (Vorkurs):
  Johannes Itten (1919-1923), then Moholy-Nagy (1923-1928),
  then Albers (1928-1933)
  Mandatory for all incoming students: study of materials,
  visual elements (line, color, texture, form) without
  preconceptions. Unlearning before relearning.

  ITTEN's PRELIMINARY COURSE:
  - Material studies: students examine different materials,
    make tactile/visual comparisons
  - Opposites (contrast exercises): rough/smooth, soft/hard,
    light/dark
  - Rhythm and form exercises
  - Breathing and body awareness (influenced by Mazdaznan
    cult -- Gropius eventually asked Itten to leave)

  ALBERS'S PRELIMINARY COURSE:
  - More systematic, less mystical
  - Paper folding exercises (paper as structural material)
  - Color studies (basis of his later Interaction of Color,
    a foundational work in design education)
```

### Key Bauhaus Faculty

| Name | Workshop/Role | Contribution |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| Walter Gropius | Director / Architecture | The institution itself; functionalist design theory |
| Paul Klee | Painting teacher, form theory | Pedagogical notebook; color and movement theory |
| Wassily Kandinsky | Painting teacher | Developed color-form grammar into a teachable system |
| László Moholy-Nagy | Preliminary course | Light, photography, kinetics; typography revolution |
| Marcel Breuer | Furniture | Tubular steel furniture (Wassily chair) |
| Herbert Bayer | Typography | Universal typeface (no caps); modern graphic design |
| Mies van der Rohe | Director (1930-33) | "Less is more"; Barcelona Chair; IIT campus |

### Bauhaus Closure and Diaspora

The Bauhaus moved: Weimar (1919) → Dessau (1925) → Berlin (1932). Closed 1933 under
Nazi pressure. The diaspora was global:

- Albers → Black Mountain College, NC; then Yale
- Moholy-Nagy → New Bauhaus, Chicago (→ IIT Institute of Design)
- Gropius → Harvard GSD
- Mies van der Rohe → IIT, Chicago
- Breuer → Harvard then NY practice

The Bauhaus's influence on 20th-century design education and modernist design was
proportionally greater than its 14-year existence suggests because the faculty
who scattered took the pedagogical program with them.

## Abstract Expressionism (New York, 1945–1965)

### The Context: Cold War and the New York School

```
ABSTRACT EXPRESSIONISM: CONTEXT
==================================

  BEFORE WWII:
  - Paris is the world center of modern art
  - Many European avant-gardists flee to New York:
    Ernst, Mondrian, Léger, Chagall, Duchamp, Dalí
  - American artists (Pollock, de Kooning, Rothko,
    Krasner, Newman) absorb European modernism directly

  WWII AND AFTER:
  - Europe devastated; Paris displaced as center
  - New York inherits the modernist project
  - The philosophical atmosphere: existentialism
    (Sartre, Camus, Heidegger) -- action, authenticity,
    the individual creating meaning in an absurd world

  COLD WAR POLITICS:
  - The CIA's Congress for Cultural Freedom funds
    international exhibitions of Abstract Expressionism
  - The argument: AbEx = individual freedom, creative
    autonomy = American democratic values
  - Socialist Realism (USSR) = state-directed art =
    totalitarianism
  - This was actual cultural policy, documented
    (declassified 1974)
  - Greenberg's aesthetic theory served (however
    unknowingly) as the theoretical justification
```

### Action Painting: Pollock

Jackson Pollock (1912–1956):

```
POLLOCK'S DRIP TECHNIQUE
==========================

  THE METHOD:
  - Unstretched canvas on the floor (not a wall)
  - Commercial enamel paint (Duco brand) -- house paint,
    not oil paint: more fluid, different viscosity
  - Applied by dripping from sticks, hardened brushes,
    basting syringes, pouring directly from the can
  - Pollock moves around ALL four sides and across the
    canvas (like a dance around a center)
  - Layers: multiple pours, different colors, the lower
    layers dry before the upper are applied

  WHAT IT IS NOT:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ NOT random splashing: the trajectory of poured paint │
  │ follows the laws of fluid dynamics. Pollock controls │
  │ the viscosity, height of pour, speed of movement.   │
  │ The results are predictable within a range.          │
  │                                                       │
  │ NOT unconscious: Pollock worked until it was right   │
  │ and stopped. He edited by cutting sections, painting │
  │ out areas. The "accidents" are accepted when they    │
  │ work and rejected when they don't.                   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE CLAIM (Harold Rosenberg, 1952):
  "Action painting": the canvas is an arena in which to
  ACT. The process IS the content. The painting records
  a performance, a physical event in time.

  Lavender Mist (Number 1, 1950): 7.2 sq meters of
  interlaced lines with no center, no hierarchy, no
  edge-to-center movement -- all-over composition.
```

### Color Field: Rothko

Mark Rothko (1903–1970):

```
ROTHKO'S FORMAL SYSTEM
========================

  THE FORM:
  Two or three horizontal color rectangles with soft,
  breathing edges, stacked on a ground.
  The edges dissolve and vibrate.

  THE EFFECT INTENDED:
  "The people who weep before my pictures are having
  the same religious experience I had when I painted them."

  TECHNICAL MEANS:
  Multiple thin layers of oil and egg tempera, diluted.
  The paint soaks INTO the canvas rather than sitting
  ON it (technique influenced by Color Field painters:
  Helen Frankenthaler's soak-stain method).
  The color has depth -- you can look INTO it, not just
  at a surface.

  SCALE:
  Large (2m+ tall). Not to impress: "I paint large pictures
  because I want to create a state of intimacy. A large
  picture is an immediate transaction: it takes you into it."
  The viewer is enveloped, not observing.

  THE LATE ROTHKO (Seagram Murals, 1958-1959):
  Commissioned for the Four Seasons restaurant (Mies van
  der Rohe Seagram Building, NY). Rothko visited the
  restaurant in progress and withdrew the commission:
  "Anyone who will eat that kind of food for those
  kind of prices will never look at a painting of mine."
  The murals went to the Tate Modern.

  Rothko Chapel (Houston, 1971): 14 large dark paintings
  in an octagonal chapel. Non-denominational meditation
  space. The most explicit statement of his intention:
  the paintings ARE sacred objects.
```

### De Kooning: The Figure Returns

Willem de Kooning (1904–1997) refused to give up the figure when it was theoretically
improper:

- **Woman I (1950–1952)**: worked on and destroyed repeatedly over two years; the
  female figure fragmented and reassembled with aggressive brushwork; neither
  portrait nor pure abstraction — the tension is the subject
- De Kooning against Greenberg's demand for "pure" abstraction: "Flesh was the reason
  oil paint was invented"
- The late work (1980s): loose, lyrical, ambiguous between figure and pure form —
  possibly influenced by early Alzheimer's, which raises the question of what
  late style means for an artist losing cognitive capacity

## Greenberg's Critical Theory: Formalist Doctrine

Clement Greenberg (1909–1994) is the most influential American art critic:

```
GREENBERG'S ARGUMENT (condensed)
===================================

  From "Avant-Garde and Kitsch" (1939) and
  "Modernist Painting" (1960):

  Each art medium has specific qualities that belong
  to IT ALONE and cannot be replicated by other media.

  PAINTING'S SPECIFIC QUALITIES:
  - Flatness (2D surface)
  - The delimitation of that surface (the edge)

  MODERNISM'S TASK:
  To acknowledge and work WITHIN the constraints of
  the medium, not pretend they don't exist.
  Illusionistic space = denying that the canvas is flat.
  Modernist painting should ADMIT its flatness.

  THE CRITICAL SEQUENCE (according to Greenberg):
  Manet: first acknowledged the flatness
  Impressionism: broke up the illusion
  Cézanne: used structure to affirm the picture plane
  Cubism: shattered illusionistic space
  Abstract Expressionism: the logical conclusion
  Color Field (Noland, Louis): pure color on flat surface

  THE PROBLEMS:
  - Greenberg excludes sculpture, photography, mixed media
  - His "logic of history" is retrospective rationalization
  - His political entanglement (Cold War funding) corrupts
    the "pure aesthetic" claim
  - He was wrong about what came after: Minimalism and
    Conceptualism broke his theory
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Kandinsky's watercolor dating**: The "First Abstract Watercolor" (1910 vs 1913)
attribution is disputed. Even if it was made in 1913 and dated backward, Kandinsky's
theoretical program was already clearly stated in the published *Concerning the
Spiritual in Art* (1911). The exact first date matters less than the program.

**Mondrian's grid is not the only grid**: Mondrian's red-blue-yellow-black grid became
so famous that it spawned endless copies and parodies (clothing, tiles, furniture).
This reproducibility is its own critical problem: does the universalist program
become mere style? Mondrian himself was horrified by imitators.

**Bauhaus "less is more" and "form follows function"**: "Less is more" (Mies)
is about architecture and design — simplicity as the highest elegance.
"Form follows function" (Louis Sullivan, earlier, not Bauhaus) is a different
claim — that function should determine form, not ornamentation. These are related
but not identical principles.

**Pollock's technique is controllable**: The myth of pure accident is wrong.
Fractal analysis of Pollock's drip paintings shows fractal dimensions consistent
with deliberate, skilled manipulation of the poured fluid. Richard Taylor's analysis
(2002) suggests Pollock's specific fractal dimension falls in the range humans find
most visually satisfying. Whether this is conscious or skilled intuition is unclear.

**Abstract Expressionism and the CIA**: The funding of AbEx tours through the
Congress for Cultural Freedom (a CIA front) was documented by Eva Cockroft in
"Abstract Expressionism: Weapon of the Cold War" (1974). The artists were generally
not aware of the source of funding and had no agreement with US government policy
(many were leftists). But the institutional use of their work as anti-Soviet
propaganda is a fact that complicates the "pure aesthetic" framing.

**Rothko's suicide (1970)**: Rothko died of a self-inflicted drug overdose in his
studio. The late work — the dark, almost monochromatic panels — is sometimes read
as presaging his death. This biographical reading risks reducing the formal choices
to symptom. The dark panels also solve formal problems Rothko had been working on;
they are not simply documents of depression.
