# 05 — Neoclassicism and Romanticism

## The Big Picture

```
NEOCLASSICISM VS ROMANTICISM: COMPETING RESPONSES TO MODERNITY
================================================================

  SHARED CONTEXT (1750–1850):
  - Enlightenment: reason, empiricism, progress, universal rights
  - American Revolution (1776), French Revolution (1789)
  - Napoleonic Empire and Wars (1799–1815)
  - Industrialization: factory labor, urbanization, environmental
    transformation begins in England (1760s onward)
  - Pompeii excavations (1748 onward): antiquity returns as evidence
  - Photography: Daguerre (1839), Fox Talbot -- painting's identity crisis

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ NEOCLASSICISM                    ROMANTICISM                    │
  │                                                                 │
  │ Reason as guide                  Emotion as guide               │
  │ Universal human nature           Individual / national spirit   │
  │ Greece and Rome as models        Medieval, exotic, natural      │
  │ Clarity of line and form         Color, atmosphere, indistinct  │
  │ Civic virtue                     Sublime terror                 │
  │ Public subjects                  Private experience             │
  │ Order                            Chaos                          │
  │ Greece as universal ideal        The particular place, time     │
  │ (Winckelmann)                    (Herder, Hegel)                │
  │                                                                 │
  │ David, Canova, Ingres            Turner, Friedrich, Delacroix,  │
  │                                  Goya, Cole, Géricault          │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY: these are not sequential. They overlap, interpenetrate,
  and argue with each other throughout 1750–1850.
```

## The Intellectual Context

### Winckelmann and the Greek Ideal

Johann Joachim Winckelmann's *History of Ancient Art* (1764) is the founding text
of art history as a discipline AND of Neoclassicism as an aesthetic:

- **"Noble simplicity and quiet grandeur"**: the ideal of Greek Classical sculpture
  (especially the Laocoon and the Apollo Belvedere) as the highest artistic achievement
- **The white marble fallacy**: Winckelmann believed Greek sculptures were white marble
  in their ideal state. We now know they were brightly painted (polychromy). His
  aesthetic is based on a material misunderstanding. The entire Neoclassical aesthetic
  of pure white marble is built on ignorance of the archaeological evidence.
- **The invention of the canon**: Winckelmann establishes the normative sequence —
  Greek → Roman → decline → Renaissance rebirth — that still structures art history.

### Edmund Burke: The Sublime

Burke's *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and
Beautiful* (1757):

```
BURKE'S CATEGORIES
====================

  BEAUTIFUL:                          SUBLIME:
  ┌──────────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────────────┐
  │ Smooth, small,       │            │ Vast, rugged, powerful   │
  │ delicate, bright     │            │ Obscure, dark, infinite  │
  │ colors               │            │ Danger at safe distance  │
  │                      │            │                          │
  │ Pleasure             │            │ Astonishment, terror,    │
  │ Love                 │            │ awe -- the mind overwhelmed│
  │ Social               │            │ Individual facing the    │
  │                      │            │ incomprehensible         │
  └──────────────────────┘            └──────────────────────────┘

  Kant's elaboration (Critique of Judgment, 1790):
  The sublime is our recognition that nature's vastness exceeds
  our senses, but our REASON can comprehend what our senses
  cannot contain. The sublime is ultimately flattering to human
  rationality -- we are greater than nature because we can
  conceive of infinity.

  Turner and Friedrich paint the Burkean/Kantian Sublime:
  the experience of being overwhelmed by natural forces.
```

## Neoclassicism

### David: Political Painting as Republican Instrument

Jacques-Louis David (1748–1825) is the most explicitly political painter in Western art:

**Oath of the Horatii (1784)**:

```
OATH OF THE HORATII: FORMAL ANALYSIS AS POLITICAL READING
============================================================

  SUBJECT: Roman legend -- three Horatii brothers swear
  on their father's swords to fight the Curatii (from Alba).
  They fight for Rome; one survives. Rome wins.

  FORMAL STRUCTURE:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Three archways behind (architectural clarity, stability)  │
  │ Left: three brothers with outstretched right arms         │
  │   - geometrically rigid, triangulated, erect              │
  │   - Roman military costume, swords                        │
  │ Center: the father, swords in hand, the axis              │
  │ Right: the women, collapsed, curved, weeping              │
  │   - one is a Curatii woman (the Horatiis' sister)         │
  │     mourning -- she will die when her brother kills       │
  │     her betrothed, condemned by Horatius                  │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  POLITICAL READING (painted 5 years before the Revolution):
  - The message is explicitly Republican: civic duty over
    personal/family loyalty; self-sacrifice for the state
  - The women's grief is NOT the point; the oath is
  - Male public virtue (linear, erect, swords) vs.
    female private grief (curved, collapsed, horizontal)
  - Painted in Rome for Louis XVI but read as a call to
    Republican virtue by the public -- it was already a
    political incitement before the Revolution
  - David becomes the Revolution's visual director:
    organizes the Festival of the Supreme Being (1794),
    designs the revolutionary symbols
```

**Death of Marat (1793)**:
- Marat assassinated in his medicinal bath by Charlotte Corday
- David's painting: pieta-like composition — Marat's arm hanging like Christ's
  in a lamentation, the letter from Corday, the pen still in hand
- No assassin, no blood pool, no drama — only the martyr's last moment
- The painting lies not about facts but about meaning: Marat is secular saint

### Ingres: The Classical Line Sustained

Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres (1780–1867) is Neoclassicism's last major figure:

- **La Grande Odalisque (1814)**: elongated spine (anatomically impossible — extra
  vertebrae added for aesthetic curve), smooth modeled flesh, explicitly "incorrect"
  but defended by Ingres as ideal necessity over accuracy
- **Ingres vs Delacroix**: the central 19th-century debate — the line (Ingres) vs.
  color (Delacroix); purity of classical ideal vs. sensory immediacy of paint;
  Poussin's tradition vs. Rubens's tradition replayed

## The Sublime: Visual Expressions

### Turner (1775–1851): The Dissolution of Form

Joseph Mallord William Turner pushes Romantic landscape to its extreme:

- **Hannibal Crossing the Alps (1812)**: the army tiny, the storm vast —
  the sublime as fact; human historical ambition dwarfed by natural force
- **Rain, Steam and Speed (1844)**: a locomotive emerging from rain and mist
  on the Great Western Railway — the sublime is now industrial; nature and
  technology merge in atmospheric obscurity
- **The Fighting Temeraire (1839)**: the old warship (Trafalgar veteran) being
  tugged to the breakers by a steam tug; nostalgia, industrial modernity,
  the dying of an age in a sunset of impossible color

Turner's late paintings:
```
TURNER'S FORMAL EVOLUTION
===========================

  Early Turner (1790s-1810s):
  - Topographically specific landscapes
  - Clear spatial recession
  - Dramatic Claudian light

  Middle Turner (1820s-1840s):
  - Incident: the historical event in the landscape
  - Form begins to dissolve into light and atmosphere
  - Dutch and Venetian color influences (Venice visits)

  Late Turner (1840s-1851):
  - Forms almost entirely dissolved into colored atmosphere
  - The light IS the subject
  - Critics called it "snow and cream"
  - Abstract in effect (Rothko later claimed Turner as precursor)
  - The specific becomes the atmospheric universal
```

### Caspar David Friedrich (1774–1840): Rückenfigur

Friedrich's formal innovation: the *Rückenfigur* (back figure) — a person seen
from behind, facing vast landscape:

```
RÜCKENFIGUR DEVICE
====================

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                         │
  │   [Human figure seen from behind, facing landscape]     │
  │                                                         │
  │   We cannot see the figure's face.                      │
  │   We CANNOT identify their emotion.                     │
  │   We are put in their position:                         │
  │   we look WITH them at the landscape.                   │
  │                                                         │
  │   The landscape becomes a projection screen for         │
  │   our interiority, not a depiction of an emotion.       │
  │                                                         │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog (1818):
  - Man in frock coat on a rocky peak, looking over
    fog-filled valleys
  - The Romantic hero: elevated, isolated, surveying
  - Is he triumphant? melancholy? seeking? lost?
  - Friedrich refuses to tell us

  Monk by the Sea (1809-1810):
  - A tiny monk facing an almost entirely empty canvas
  - Sky takes up 2/3 of the surface
  - One of the emptiest, most radical paintings in history
  - Kleist: looking at it is like looking at your own
    eyelids -- there is nothing to hold on to
```

Friedrich's pietism: the landscape is not secular — it is the visible sign of
divine presence (Protestantism's theology of the natural world as God's text).
The bare oak trees, Gothic ruins, and rising suns are religious symbols, not
decoration.

## Romanticism's Political Edge

### Delacroix: Liberty and Sensualism

Eugène Delacroix (1798–1863) is Romanticism's most explicitly political French painter:

**Liberty Leading the People (1830)**:
- July Revolution of 1830 (Three Glorious Days) depicted within months
- Liberty is allegorical (bare-breasted, Phrygian cap) but the people behind her
  are specific social types: working class, bourgeois, child
- Dead bodies in the foreground — the cost, not the glory
- The tricolor flag: Liberty carries it into the painting from outside it
- Delacroix is visible in the crowd (bourgeois in top hat) — the painter as citizen

**The formal argument**: Delacroix uses color, diagonal thrust, loose brushwork
against Ingres's line. This is the Rubens tradition in French painting — warm
bodies, color as primary structural element, energy over calculation.

### Géricault: The Raft of the Medusa (1819)

Theodore Géricault's painting of the 1816 shipwreck of the French frigate Méduse:

- 147 survivors set adrift on a raft; after 13 days, 15 alive; evidence of cannibalism
- The government suppressed the scandal (the captain was incompetent, politically
  appointed); Géricault's painting is a political attack
- Pyramidal composition: bodies at the bottom (dead), figures rising toward the apex
  (the man waving to the rescue ship on the horizon)
- Scale: 491 × 716 cm — history painting scale for what was officially a scandal
- The rescue ship on the horizon: is it coming or going? The ambiguity is political

### Goya: Saturn and the Disasters of War

Francisco Goya (1746–1828) is the artist who most directly confronts horror:

**Saturn Devouring His Son (c. 1819–1823)** — the "Black Paintings":
- Painted directly on the walls of his house near Madrid
- Never intended for exhibition — private psychic statement
- Saturn: gigantic figure, wild eyes, devouring the body of a child
- Not mythology as allegory — mythology as psychic truth (power consumes)
- The scale and rawness anticipate 20th-century Expressionism

**Disasters of War (1810–1820, published 1863)**:

```
GOYA'S DISASTERS: WHAT THEY DO
================================

  Context: Peninsular War (1808-1814), French occupation
  of Spain, guerrilla warfare, atrocities on both sides

  Structure: 82 etchings, published posthumously
  (too dangerous to publish during his lifetime)

  What is different from earlier war art:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Traditional war art:    │ Goya's Disasters:         │
  │ Heroism, cavalry,       │ Mutilated bodies, torture │
  │ generals at the moment  │ Civilians dead, hanged    │
  │ of victory              │ Both sides atrocity       │
  │ The general is named    │ No generals named         │
  │ War as spectacle        │ War as slaughterhouse     │
  │ Death is noble          │ Death is a corpse         │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Plate 39: "Great deeds! Against the dead!"
    -- French soldiers mutilating corpses
  Plate 44: "I saw this"
    -- most direct caption, first-person witness

  Goya's captions are ironic ("What courage!"),
  horrified ("Why?"), despairing ("There is no remedy")

  The modern war documentary begins here.
```

**Technology disruption bridge — photography as the new algorithm:** Photography's arrival in 1839 follows the standard incumbent-disruption pattern: a new technology captures the commodity function of the incumbent, which forces the incumbent to redefine its value proposition around what the disruptor cannot do. The sequence:

```
DISRUPTION PATTERN — PHOTOGRAPHY VS PAINTING

  BEFORE 1839:
  Painting = only medium for visual documentation and portraiture
  Portrait painters = highly paid professionals; landscape painting
  = documentation of specific places for patrons who owned them

  1839: DAGUERREOTYPE ARRIVES
  Disruption vector: cheaper, faster, more accurate than painting
  for the commodity functions (portraiture, documentation)

  INCUMBENT RESPONSE (painting):
  Cannot compete on the commodity dimension → redefine value proposition

  Moves painting could NOT do before but did after 1839:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Impressionism: capture transient light photography     │
  │   cannot stop (before fast shutters)                   │
  │ Symbolism / Expressionism: subjective inner state,     │
  │   emotion, distortion — camera is neutral by default   │
  │ Abstraction: pure formal investigation; camera always  │
  │   depicts something                                    │
  │ Scale and material presence: a 3m canvas has           │
  │   physical presence no print matches                   │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  The same pattern: mainframes → minicomputers → PCs
  Each displacement forces up-market into what the disruptor
  cannot yet do. On-prem → cloud forced on-prem vendors to
  emphasize control, compliance, latency — the dimensions
  cloud initially couldn't match.
```

This framing makes the post-1839 art history legible as a rational competitive response, not a random sequence of avant-garde experiments. Each movement is a strategic pivot away from photography's expanding capability envelope.

## Photography Arrives: 1839

Louis Daguerre publicly demonstrates the daguerreotype in 1839 (Paris);
William Fox Talbot demonstrates the calotype (paper negative) the same year:

```
PHOTOGRAPHY'S IMPACT ON PAINTING
====================================

  IMMEDIATE:
  - Portrait painters lose their primary market within decades
  - The daguerreotype miniature portrait replaces painted miniature
  - Landscape painting challenged: photography can document more
    accurately and cheaply

  MEDIUM-TERM:
  - Photography takes over the documentary function of painting
  - Painting must redefine itself: what can it do that photography
    CANNOT do?
  - Answer 1 (Impressionism): it can capture light, time, sensation
    that the slow photographic exposure misses
  - Answer 2 (Expressionism): it can express psychological states
    that photography cannot access
  - Answer 3 (Abstraction): it can explore pure form and color
    without reference to the visible world at all

  PHOTOGRAPHY AND PAINTING INFLUENCE EACH OTHER:
  - Degas uses photographic cropping (figures cut at edge)
    and photographic foreshortening
  - Muybridge's stop-motion photography (1878) proves the
    galloping horse's four legs leave the ground simultaneously
    -- all galloping horses in painting before 1878 are
    anatomically incorrect (rocking horse position)
  - Cézanne's multiple viewpoints might be understood as
    collaging multiple photographic exposures from different
    positions into one image
```

## The Hudson River School: American Sublime

The American parallel to European Romanticism:

- Thomas Cole (1801–1848): *The Oxbow* (1836) — the Connecticut River from Mt. Holyoke;
  the stormy wilderness at left, the pastoral cultivated valley at right, Cole as tiny
  figure in the middle with his easel; where America is and where it is going
- The Course of Empire (1833–1836): five paintings — Savage State → Pastoral →
  Consummation → Destruction → Desolation; a moral warning about the fate of empires
  addressed to the young American republic
- Frederic Edwin Church: geological grandeur at global scale — *Heart of the Andes*
  (1859), 1.7m × 3m, displayed in New York with velvet drapes as a viewing experience

## Common Confusion Points

**Neoclassicism is not classicism**: Classical art was made in ancient Greece and Rome.
Neoclassicism is a modern revival movement (18th–19th c.) inspired by antiquity. David
is not ancient; he is modern using ancient conventions for contemporary political ends.

**Winckelmann's white marble is wrong**: The 18th-century aesthetic of pure white
marble as the ideal of beauty is based on the fact that ancient polychromy (paint)
had worn off by the time Europeans excavated and studied the sculptures. Color
analysis (since the 1980s) has shown that the Parthenon, the Apollo Belvedere, and
most other canonical "white" sculptures were brightly painted. The Neoclassical
aesthetic of purity = whiteness is an accident of preservation, not ancient intent.

**The Romantic vs. Enlightenment dichotomy is too clean**: Goya is a product of
both the Spanish Enlightenment (he worked for the Bourbon reformers) and Romantic
emotional extremity. Turner's late work is rational in its construction of atmospheric
effect even if the effect is of sublime irrationality. The categories are useful
heuristics, not air-tight classifications.

**Photography did not kill painting**: The conventional narrative says photography
caused Impressionism by taking over painting's documentary function. The reality is
more complex: Impressionism began before photography dominated, and the crisis of
photography was gradual. Some academic painters adopted photography as a tool
(Ingres photographed models); others ignored it. The causal story is overstated.

**Ingres's distortions are intentional**: When critics said La Grande Odalisque's
spine was impossibly long, Ingres reportedly responded that it was perfect. He was
not unaware of anatomy — he was subordinating anatomy to ideal form. The same logic
as Egyptian hierarchical scale: the rule overrides naturalism.
