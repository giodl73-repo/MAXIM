## Prehistoric Art

### Chauvet vs Lascaux: The Technical Comparison

The surprise of Chauvet (discovered 1994) was that the oldest known cave art is
technically sophisticated in ways that contradict linear-progress models:

```
CHAUVET (c. 32,000 BCE)         LASCAUX (c. 17,000 BCE)
==========================================================================

DATE        ~32,000 BP                  ~17,000 BP
LOCATION    Ardèche, France             Dordogne, France

TECHNIQUE   Charcoal scraping of        Polychrome: manganese black +
            wall surface to create      yellow/red ochre; some engraving;
            lighter ground, then        blown pigment through tubes for
            drawing in charcoal         stippled texture

ANIMALS     Rhinoceros, cave bear,      Horses, aurochs, deer, bison —
            cave lion (predators        prey animals dominate; some
            dominate, unusual)          cattle, feline

SPATIAL     Panel of Lions: overlap-   Some overlap, but less systematic;
SOPHISTI-   ping figures suggest        more side-by-side arrangement
CATION      depth; foreshortening       across the picture plane
            of body parts evident

PERSPECTIVE Twisted perspective         Twisted perspective (most common
(HEAD)      (body frontal, head         in Paleolithic): head turned to
            profile) common             profile for clarity regardless of
                                        body position

SCALE       Variable; bear skull        Variable; "Great Hall of the Bulls"
            placed on central rock      — figures up to 5m long
            as deliberate installation
```

**What these are not**: direct windows onto "primitive" thinking. The execution requires
planning, material preparation (grinding pigment, mixing binder), knowledge of animal
anatomy, and the cognitive capacity to project a 3D animal onto a 2D surface in the dark
by firelight. Scaffolding evidence at Lascaux confirms organized labor.

**What they might be**: hunting magic, shamanic vision records, territorial markers,
myth narratives — all hypotheses. The fact that the same caves show evidence of use
across thousands of years suggests returning ritual practice, not casual marking.
The deep interior locations, inaccessible without light sources, reinforce deliberate
ceremonial access rather than casual decoration.

### Venus Figurines

Found from France to Siberia (c. 28,000–20,000 BCE), these small female figures share:

- Exaggerated breasts, hips, abdomen
- Featureless or schematic face (identity is not individual)
- Often no feet (not meant to stand — held? hung? worn?)
- Made of ivory, bone, stone, fired clay (among earliest fired clay objects known)

The Venus of Willendorf (c. 25,000 BCE, 11cm, oolitic limestone, red ochre traces) is
canonical. The Willendorf figure shows hair in intricate rows — time invested in
non-functional detail. Interpretation debates: fertility goddess? pregnant self-portrait?
erotic objects? universal female ideal? — no consensus. The "Venus" label (goddess of
beauty) imposed by 19th-century male archaeologists is itself interpretively loaded.

Distribution across 8,000km of Europe with similar formal characteristics suggests
either connected cultural practice or convergent response to shared cognitive framework.

### Stonehenge as Visual/Spatial Design

Stonehenge (Wiltshire, UK; constructed ~3100–1600 BCE in phases) organizes cosmological
experience through spatial composition:

```
STONEHENGE SPATIAL LOGIC
==========================

  AXIS: aligned to midsummer sunrise / midwinter sunset
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │        [Heel Stone]                                 │
  │             |  <- midsummer sunrise axis            │
  │        [Avenue]                                     │
  │             |                                       │
  │      +──────+──────+  <- Sarsen circle (30 uprights)│
  │      |  +────────+ |  <- Bluestone circle           │
  │      |  | +────+ | |  <- Trilithon horseshoe        │
  │      |  | | [A]| | |  <- Altar stone                │
  │      |  | +────+ | |                                │
  │      |  +────────+ |                                │
  │      +─────────────+                                │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Sarsen stones: from Marlborough Downs, ~30km north
  Bluestones: from Preseli Hills, Wales, ~240km
  Transport = massive logistical investment = high social value
  Ditch + bank (henge) predates the stones by ~500 years
```

The megalithic tradition (standing stones, dolmens, passage graves — Newgrange, Ireland,
c. 3200 BCE) represents Neolithic societies organizing the landscape as a visual-spatial
system marking astronomical events, the dead, and territorial claims — design at
landscape scale preceding any urban environment.

## Ancient Egypt

### The Egyptian Convention System

Egyptian art is not failed naturalism. It is a rigorously codified symbolic system where
every convention serves a functional purpose: ensuring the correct representation of
being for ritual efficacy.

```
FRONTALITY / TWISTED PERSPECTIVE
===================================

  THE RULE: Each body part shown from its most
  characteristic/legible angle

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                                │
  │   EYE: front view     --> full eye visible                    │
  │   SHOULDERS: front    --> full chest width, both arms seen    │
  │   TORSO: slight 3/4   --> reads as chest, shows waist         │
  │   HIPS: side view     --> clear profile                       │
  │   LEGS: side view     --> walking pose, two feet visible      │
  │                                                                │
  │   Result: LEGIBILITY MAXIMIZED over optical realism           │
  │   The figure is an information diagram, not a snapshot        │
  │                                                                │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HIERARCHICAL SCALE: Status = size
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                                │
  │   [PHARAOH -- large]                                          │
  │       flanked by [consort -- medium] and [servants -- small]  │
  │                                                                │
  │   NOT spatial recession -- social hierarchy made visible      │
  │                                                                │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Ka and Why Images Matter

The ka is one component of the Egyptian soul — the life force that persists after death
and requires sustenance (food offerings, ritual attention). The image is not a
representation for contemplation; it is a functional dwelling for the ka, a substitute
for the living body.

This explains the Egyptian obsession with:
- **Completeness of representation**: missing a body part in the image means the ka lacks it
- **Identification texts**: the image must be named to function (hieroglyphs are part of
  the image's function, not decorative addition)
- **Hidden images**: tomb paintings are effective without being seen by living eyes — the
  ritual purpose is independent of living audience. This breaks every modern assumption
  about art requiring a viewer.

The destruction of images was a weapon: erasing a pharaoh's cartouche from monuments
(as Akhenaten was erased after his death) could destroy their existence in the afterlife.
Images have ontological weight.

### The Canonic Grid System

Egyptian artists used a proportional grid to ensure that figures at any scale maintained
standard proportions:

```
EGYPTIAN CANON OF PROPORTIONS
================================

  Old + Middle Kingdom: 18-square grid
  New Kingdom onward (and later Saite revival): 21-square grid

  18-square grid key measurements:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Hairline      = square 18      │
  │  Top shoulder  = square 15-16   │
  │  Armpit        = square 14      │
  │  Elbow         = square 11      │
  │  Waist         = square 10      │
  │  Lower knee    = square 6       │
  │  Ankle         = square 1       │
  │  Base (soles)  = square 0       │
  └─────────────────────────────────┘

  Grid applied to wall surface first; multiple craftsmen
  work different sections and produce proportionally
  consistent figures across an entire tomb wall.

  This is a proto-modular design system — the grid as
  shared protocol enabling distributed production.
```

### Registers

Egyptian narrative art uses horizontal bands (registers) to organize multiple scenes:

- Upper register = higher status or earlier in time
- Lower register = subordinate narrative or lower-status figures
- Registers read right-to-left or left-to-right depending on convention
- A battle scene: pharaoh at colossal scale on the main field; enemy fleeing in
  disorder on registers below — spatial and hierarchical coding simultaneously

The tomb of Ti at Saqqara (c. 2400 BCE): agricultural scenes in registers showing
the complete cycle of food production — not decorative but functional, ensuring
the deceased's eternal supply through image magic.

## Ancient Greece

### The Greek Sculptural Arc

```
GREEK SCULPTURAL DEVELOPMENT
================================

  GEOMETRIC (c. 900–700 BCE)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Pottery: geometric bands — meander, lozenge,        │
  │   chevron                                           │
  │ Human figures: schematic, silhouette, abstracted    │
  │ Funerary kraters (mixing bowls) as grave markers    │
  │ The Dipylon Amphora: registers of mourners, horses  │
  │ Figures reduced to triangles and rectangles         │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                          |
  ARCHAIC (c. 700–480 BCE)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ KOUROS (pl. kouroi): standing male youth            │
  │   - Both feet planted, left foot slightly forward   │
  │   - Arms at sides, fists clenched                   │
  │   - "Archaic smile" -- formulaic, not expressive    │
  │   - Hair in elaborate formal patterns               │
  │   - Nude -- the Greek male ideal, not modesty       │
  │   - Increasingly anatomically observed over time:   │
  │     early kouroi smooth and schematic; late kouroi  │
  │     show specific muscle groups                     │
  │                                                     │
  │ KORE (pl. korai): standing draped female            │
  │   - Always clothed (unlike male nudity)             │
  │   - Elaborate drapery patterns -- technical display │
  │   - Archaic smile, formal hair                      │
  │                                                     │
  │ FUNCTION: votive offerings (at temples) and grave   │
  │   markers -- NOT portraits of specific individuals  │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                          |
  CLASSICAL (c. 480–323 BCE)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ CONTRAPPOSTO: the formal breakthrough               │
  │                                                     │
  │   Weight shifts to one leg (stance leg)             │
  │   Hip on weight-bearing side rises                  │
  │   Shoulder on weight-bearing side drops             │
  │   Spine follows S-curve to compensate               │
  │   Figure is ALIVE -- caught in a moment of          │
  │   potential motion, not static bilateral symmetry   │
  │                                                     │
  │ POLYKLEITOS (c. 450 BCE):                           │
  │   - Doryphoros (Spear-Bearer) = canon made flesh    │
  │   - Proportional system: head = 1/7 body height     │
  │   - Chiasmus: left arm raised / right arm lowered,  │
  │     left leg relaxed / right leg supporting         │
  │   - Opposing tensions create dynamic harmony        │
  │   - Original bronze lost; we know it from marble    │
  │     Roman copies (copying tradition = evidence loss)│
  │                                                     │
  │ PARTHENON (447-438 BCE, Phidias supervising):       │
  │   - Entasis (column has slight convex bulge)        │
  │   - Columns lean slightly inward                    │
  │   - Stylobate platform curves upward at center      │
  │   - No perfectly straight line on the building      │
  │   - Optical corrections for visual perfection       │
  │   - Chryselephantine cult statue of Athena (gold    │
  │     on ivory armature, ~12m tall) now lost          │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                          |
  HELLENISTIC (c. 323–31 BCE)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ After Alexander: Greek culture spreads; also absorbs│
  │ Pergamon, Egypt, Syria, India                       │
  │                                                     │
  │ PATHOS: emotional expression becomes primary        │
  │   - Laocoon Group: agony, writhing, diagonal        │
  │   - Nike of Samothrace: movement in marble          │
  │   - Dying Gaul (Pergamon): defeated enemy with      │
  │     dignity -- pathetic (in classical sense) not    │
  │     mocking                                         │
  │                                                     │
  │ VIRTUOSITY as aesthetic value:                      │
  │   - Difficult poses, extreme contrapposto           │
  │   - Transparent cloth over flesh rendered in stone  │
  │   - Veins, tendons -- anatomical demonstration      │
  │                                                     │
  │ EVERYDAY SUBJECTS: old fisherman, drunken woman,   │
  │   sleeping satyr -- genre subjects alongside heroes │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Polykleitos's Canon: The Mathematical Dimension

Polykleitos wrote a treatise (the *Canon*, text lost, practice survives in the
Doryphoros) proposing that ideal beauty is a matter of commensurability — each part
of the body standing in a precise numerical ratio to every other part and to the whole.

This is the same impulse as Pythagorean musical harmony: simple integer ratios produce
harmonious sound; analogous ratios produce beautiful form. Beauty is mathematical,
not arbitrary or subjective. The Parthenon's architects applied the same principle:
every dimension in a numerical relationship to the column diameter (the module).

## Ancient Rome

### Portrait Realism (Verism)

Republican Roman portraiture adopts extreme naturalism — warts, wrinkles, jowls, the
specific face of an aging senator:

```
GREEK IDEAL vs ROMAN VERISM
==============================

  Greek Classical ideal:          Roman Republican verism:
  ┌──────────────────────┐        ┌──────────────────────────────┐
  │ Idealized youth      │        │ Specific aged individual      │
  │ No distinguishing    │        │ All identifying features      │
  │   marks              │        │   retained: baldness, scars,  │
  │ Beauty = ideal form  │        │   wrinkles, jowls             │
  │ Could not function   │        │ Virtue lies in experience, not│
  │   as a portrait      │        │   beauty; gravitas over kalos │
  │                      │        │ Portraits for ancestor cult:  │
  │                      │        │   wax masks of the dead worn  │
  │                      │        │   at funerals by living men   │
  └──────────────────────┘        └──────────────────────────────┘

  Imperial period synthesis:
  Idealized body (heroic pose, athletic) + somewhat realistic head
  Augustus of Prima Porta (c. 20 BCE):
    - General's pose (adlocutio -- addressing troops)
    - Bare feet = apotheosis/divine status
    - Cupid on dolphin at feet = descent from Venus claimed
    - Breastplate relief = Parthian return of Roman standards, 20 BCE
    - Found at Livia's villa -- posthumous propaganda
```

### Triumphal Reliefs: Continuous Narrative

Column of Trajan (113 CE, Rome) is the key example of *continuous narrative*:

- 200-meter spiral frieze wound around the column
- Single ongoing story: the two Dacian campaigns of 101-102 and 105-106 CE
- No separate framed scenes — continuous frieze reads like an unrolling scroll
- Trajan appears repeatedly across the narrative (omnipresent commander figure)
- Same figure in multiple moments of time = *simultaneous narrative*

This logic appears in: medieval manuscript illumination, predella panels, comics
strips, film storyboards. The Column of Trajan is the ancient precedent.

The original column was topped by a bronze eagle; later replaced by a bronze Trajan;
currently surmounted by a bronze St. Peter (since 1588) — the history of the object
is itself a history of power.

### Roman Mosaic

Tessera (pl. tesserae) = small cubes of stone, glass, or fired clay:

```
MOSAIC TECHNIQUE SPECTRUM
============================

  OPUS TESSELLATUM:
    Larger tessera (1-2cm), geometric and decorative patterns
    Floor mosaics, faster production, durable underfoot

  OPUS VERMICULATUM ("worm-work"):
    Tiny tessera (2-4mm), curved rows following form
    Tracks the form like brushstrokes
    Used for faces, figures, pictorial content
    Battle of Issus (Alexander vs Darius), Pompeii, c. 100 BCE:
    ~1.5 million tesserae, 2.72 x 5.13m

  OPUS SECTILE:
    Shaped marble cut to fit figure -- not cubes but custom pieces
    More expensive, less common

  GLASS TESSERA:
    Can achieve translucency; gold leaf sealed in glass
    --> Byzantine gold-ground mosaics of Ravenna inherit
        this Roman glass-tesserae tradition
```

## Technical Concepts

### Stone Carving: Subtractive Sculpture

Stone carving is irreversible. Every cut removes material. Techniques in sequence:

- **Point work**: rough shaping with pointed chisel, knocking off large volumes
- **Claw chisel**: parallel scratches creating rough texture, modeling broad form
- **Flat chisel**: refining surface planes
- **Drill**: creating deep undercutting (Hellenistic hair curls, Baroque shadow)
- **Abrasives**: final smoothing — sandstone, pumice, emery

The ancient carver's method: work from multiple faces simultaneously using measured
transfer points. Not "liberating" a figure from inside the block — projecting the
form through the mass from multiple orthographic views.

### Fresco

```
FRESCO TECHNIQUES
===================

  BUON FRESCO (true fresco):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Arriccio: rough underplaster applied               │
  │ 2. Sinopia: red underdrawing on arriccio              │
  │ 3. Intonaco: final smooth plaster layer -- small      │
  │    patch (giornata = "day's work") applied wet        │
  │ 4. Paint with water-based pigments WHILE WET          │
  │ 5. Pigment bonds chemically as calcium hydroxide      │
  │    carbonate (CaCO3) forms -- image IS the wall       │
  │ 6. Extremely durable when protected from moisture     │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SECCO (dry fresco):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. Paint onto dry plaster with egg or glue binder     │
  │ 2. Not chemically bonded -- less durable              │
  │ 3. Used for corrections and details added to buon     │
  │ 4. Azurite and ultramarine blue added a secco:        │
  │    most blue robes in medieval works have flaked      │
  │    because blue pigments cannot survive the alkaline  │
  │    wet plaster (they turn green)                      │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Encaustic

Wax-based painting medium, used in ancient Egypt and Rome:
- Pigment suspended in beeswax; applied hot with heated tools
- Extraordinary durability: Fayum mummy portraits (1st–3rd c. CE, Roman Egypt) survive
  with uncanny freshness after 1800 years — the wax seals and protects the pigment
- A separate portrait tradition from the canonic Egyptian style: Roman province of Egypt
  fuses Egyptian mummy burial practice with Roman-Greek portrait realism — a hybrid
  object at the intersection of two visual cultures

## Common Confusion Points

**Lascaux vs Altamira vs Chauvet**: Three separate sites. Altamira (Spain) was first
major Paleolithic cave discovered (1879, dismissed as forgery for decades). Lascaux
(France, 1940) became famous mid-20th century. Chauvet (France, 1994) is oldest known
and arguably most technically sophisticated — inverting the expected progress narrative.

**Kouros does not equal Apollo**: Early kouros figures are not identified as Apollo.
They are generic ideal youth figures used for votive and funerary purposes. Attribution
requires attributes (lyre, laurel, bow, quiver).

**The Greek originals problem**: Most canonical Greek bronze statues are lost. What
survives are Roman marble copies made for patrician collectors. Marble copies of bronzes
are structurally compromised (marble cannot span gaps that bronze can — hence support
struts under raised arms). Virtually all study of Greek sculpture is study of Roman copies.

**Egyptian frontality is not incompetence**: Egyptian artists could paint naturalistic
animals (hunting scenes, sacred birds). The frontality of the human figure is a
deliberate convention for ritual efficacy, not a limitation of skill.

**Polykleitos's original**: The Doryphoros survives only in Roman copies. We do not
have the bronze original. The same is true of the Discobolus of Myron and nearly every
other famous Greek sculpture.

**Encaustic vs tempera**: Encaustic uses wax binder, applied hot. Egg tempera uses
egg yolk binder, applied at room temperature. Both precede oil paint by centuries.

**Fayum portraits**: These are not Egyptian in style — they are Greco-Roman portrait
paintings inserted into Egyptian mummy wrappings. They represent cultural fusion, not
Egyptian continuity.
