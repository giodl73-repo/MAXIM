# 02 — The Refrigeration Cycle

## Evaporation, Compression, Condensation — How AC and Heat Pumps Move Heat

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Core insight: refrigeration doesn't create cold — it moves heat from where you don't want it to where you don't mind it; air conditioner moves heat from inside → outside; heat pump (heating mode) moves heat from outside → inside (even cold outside air contains significant heat energy); this is why heat pumps can deliver more energy than they consume (COP > 1)
- **Refrigerant phase change physics**: refrigerant boiling point determined by pressure; low pressure → low boiling point; evaporator is low pressure → refrigerant boils at ~40°F (absorbing heat from 70°F indoor air); condenser is high pressure → refrigerant condensing at ~100°F (rejecting heat to 90°F outdoor air); the compressor creates the pressure differential that makes this possible
- **The four steps of vapor compression cycle**:
  1. **Evaporator** (indoor coil for AC, outdoor coil for heating mode heat pump): low-pressure liquid refrigerant expands and boils; absorbs heat from surrounding air (heat of vaporization); leaves as low-pressure vapor; indoor air crosses coil → cooled and dehumidified (moisture condenses on cold coil → condensate drain)
  2. **Compressor**: pumps low-pressure vapor to high-pressure vapor; does work (electrical energy input); raises pressure and temperature of refrigerant; reciprocating (older/small), scroll (dominant residential — quieter, more efficient), screw (commercial), centrifugal (large commercial); compressor is the "heart" of the system and the most expensive component
  3. **Condenser** (outdoor coil for AC, indoor coil for heating mode heat pump): high-pressure hot vapor cools and condenses to liquid; rejects heat to outdoor air via condenser fan; leaves as high-pressure liquid
  4. **Expansion device**: reduces high-pressure liquid to low-pressure liquid/vapor mixture; thermostatic expansion valve (TXV) or electronic expansion valve (EEV) — modulates flow to maintain superheat; orifice/fixed restrictor in simpler systems
- **P-H (pressure-enthalpy) diagram (Mollier diagram)**: key tool for refrigeration analysis; x-axis = enthalpy (BTU/lb), y-axis = pressure (PSIA); saturation curve (two-phase region inside dome); cycle traces as rectangle on this chart (evaporating along bottom, compressing up right side, condensing along top, expanding down left side); compressor work = enthalpy rise during compression; refrigerating effect = enthalpy absorbed in evaporator; COP = refrigerating effect / compressor work
- **Superheat and subcooling**: superheat = how many degrees above boiling point the vapor is at evaporator outlet (ensures no liquid enters compressor); target 10-15°F; low superheat = risk of liquid slugging compressor; high superheat = low efficiency, less cooling; subcooling = how many degrees below condensing temperature the liquid is at condenser outlet; target 10-15°F; ensures only liquid enters expansion device; measurement allows refrigerant charge diagnostics
- **Refrigerant charge**: correct refrigerant amount critical; low charge (leak) → low suction pressure, high superheat, inadequate cooling, compressor overheats; high charge (overcharged) → high head pressure, high subcooling, compressor flooding; charging method: superheat method (fixed orifice systems), subcooling method (TXV systems), manufacturer's charging chart
- **Two-stage and variable-speed (inverter-driven) compressors**: single-stage = on/off (100% capacity or off); two-stage = 65% or 100% (less short cycling, better dehumidification); variable-speed inverter compressor = modulates continuously 25-100% (matches load, maximum efficiency, best humidity control, SEER2 ratings up to 26 residential); inverter compressors dominate modern mini-split/heat pump systems
- **EER, COP, SEER**: EER (Energy Efficiency Ratio) = BTU/h cooling / watts input at fixed conditions; COP (Coefficient of Performance) = heat energy moved / work input (dimensionless, >1 for heat pumps); SEER/SEER2 (Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio) = total cooling season BTU / total watt-hours (seasonal integrated measurement, more realistic than EER); SEER2 uses new test conditions (2023) making numbers ~15% lower than old SEER
