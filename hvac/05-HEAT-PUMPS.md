# 05 — Heat Pumps

## COP > 1, Cold Climate, Mini-Splits, Geothermal

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **The fundamental physics**: heat pumps don't create heat — they move it; moving heat from cold to hot requires work (2nd law of thermodynamics), but the work required is much less than the heat moved; outdoor air at 20°F contains enormous amounts of thermal energy (absolute zero is -459°F — 20°F is 479°F above absolute zero); COP = heat delivered / electrical work input; COP of 3 = 3 BTU of heat delivered per BTU of electricity consumed (300% "efficiency"); gas furnaces max out at 98% AFUE (1.0 COP equivalent); heat pumps typically 200-400% in moderate temperatures
- **Air-source heat pump (ASHP) — basic**: reverse refrigeration cycle; heating mode: outdoor unit = evaporator (absorbs heat from outdoor air), indoor unit = condenser (releases heat indoors); cooling mode: outdoor unit = condenser, indoor unit = evaporator; reversing valve switches modes; four-way valve is HVAC-speak for reversing valve
- **Balance point**: temperature at which heat pump output equals heat loss of building; below balance point, auxiliary heat (resistance strips) supplements; traditional ASHP balance point ~25-30°F; below balance point, COP drops, resistance heat is less efficient; traditional ASHP impractical in very cold climates
- **Cold-climate ASHP (ccASHP)**: engineered for cold weather performance; variable-speed/inverter compressor operates at very high speeds for cold conditions; enhanced vapor injection (EVI) or intermediate-injection technology; Mitsubishi Hyper Heat, Bosch IDS, Daikin Aurora, Fujitsu Halcyon lines; rated at -13°F to -22°F operation; COP ~1.5-2.0 at 0°F (still more efficient than electric resistance, often competitive with gas at low temperatures); NEEP cold-climate database tracks tested performance
- **HSPF and HSPF2**: Heating Seasonal Performance Factor; total heating season BTU / watt-hours consumed; integrated seasonal metric for heat pump heating efficiency; minimum federal standard 8.2 HSPF (legacy), 7.5 HSPF2 (2023 new test procedure); high-efficiency models 12-14 HSPF2; HSPF2 test conditions more representative of real cold-climate operation
- **Mini-split (ductless) systems**: outdoor unit ("condenser") connected to one or more indoor units ("air handlers") via refrigerant line set (3/8" liquid + 5/8" suction + control wiring); no ductwork; each indoor unit conditions one zone independently; wall-mounted cassette, ceiling cassette, floor console, concealed ducted options; ideal for room additions, renovations, garages, basements; SEER2 up to 42 available; loss of efficiency in very cold climates if not cold-climate rated; multi-zone (one outdoor, multiple indoors) vs single-zone
- **Ducted heat pump systems**: central air handler with refrigerant coil; drops into existing forced-air system (replaces gas furnace or AC); "add-on" heat pump: works with existing gas furnace (switchover thermostat selects heat pump above ~30°F, gas below); or full heat pump replace furnace + AC; requires compatible air handler and properly sized ductwork
- **Ground-source heat pump (GSHP / geothermal)**: ground or water as heat source/sink instead of air; ground temperature constant ~50-55°F year-round below frost line (excellent heat source in winter, cooler than summer air for heat rejection); dramatically higher COP (3.5-5.0 average) than air-source; loop types:
  - Closed-loop horizontal: 2-6 feet deep trenches, 400-600 ft of pipe per ton; large lot required
  - Closed-loop vertical: boreholes 150-300 ft deep, ~200-250 ft per ton; smaller lot, higher drilling cost
  - Closed-loop pond/lake: coiled pipe sunk in pond if available; lowest cost if applicable
  - Open-loop: pump groundwater through heat exchanger, return to ground; high COP but water rights and reinjection required
  - GSHP cost: high upfront installation (especially vertical bore) but lowest operating cost; 26% federal tax credit (IRA 2022); 5-10 year payback typical
- **Heat pump water heaters (HPWH)**: uses heat pump to heat domestic water; COP 3.5-4.0; much more efficient than electric resistance; requires unconditioned or semi-conditioned space (basement/garage — draws heat from surroundings); noise (fan and compressor); Rheem ProTerra, A.O. Smith Voltex, Stiebel Eltron; $300 federal tax credit (IRA 2022); excellent choice when electricity available and water heating is significant load
