# 04 — Heating Systems

## Furnaces, Boilers, AFUE, Condensing, Electric

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Gas furnace anatomy**: heat exchanger (combustion gases in, room air over exterior → never mix); induced draft blower (creates combustion airflow); burners; igniter (hot surface igniter replaced standing pilot in 1990s); gas valve; limit switch (high-temp safety cutoff); pressure switch (proves draft); variable-speed or multi-stage air handler blower; condensate drain (condensing furnaces only)
- **AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency)**: percent of fuel energy converted to useful heat over heating season; includes cycling losses; federal minimum: 80% non-condensing for furnaces (some climate exceptions); 80% furnace = 20% lost up flue; 96% condensing = 4% lost; measure for gas furnaces and boilers; not the same as COP (which measures heat moved, not converted)
- **Non-condensing vs condensing** (the most important furnace distinction):
  - Non-condensing (80% AFUE): flue gases exit at ~300-400°F (too hot to condense water vapor); B-vent (metal double-wall flue) or Category I (negative pressure, non-condensing); cannot use PVC flue; installation anywhere flue can be routed out (side or top)
  - Condensing (90-98% AFUE): extracts so much heat that flue gases exit at ~100-120°F → water vapor condenses in secondary heat exchanger → condensate (slightly acidic) drains to drain; PVC or CPVC flue (2" or 3" direct-vent or power-vent); two pipes: combustion air intake + exhaust; can be installed in tight spaces; condensate neutralizer required in some municipalities (neutralizes acidic condensate before drain)
  - The 5°F crossover: for every 5°F outside temperature drop, efficiency advantage of condensing increases; in northern climates (heating season in single digits) condensing pays back faster; in mild climates (heating season rarely below 35°F) payback is longer
- **Boilers** (water/steam heat distribution):
  - Cast iron sectional boilers: assembled from sections (can fit through door); slow to heat up, high mass; very durable; atmospheric vent typical; residential and light commercial
  - Steel boilers: lighter, faster response
  - Condensing mod-con (modulating-condensing): stainless steel heat exchanger; modulating burner adjusts output 25-100% to match load; outdoor reset control; AFUE 95-98%; PVC flue; eliminates short cycling; most efficient option; brands: Navien, Triangle Tube, Viessmann, Weil-McLain Ultra; requires low return water temperature for condensing operation
  - Steam boilers: one-pipe (steam up, condensate returns by gravity as steam cools) vs two-pipe (separate steam supply and condensate return); traps; older urban housing; maintenance intensive; no thermostat at individual radiators in one-pipe
- **Electric resistance heating**: 100% "efficient" (all electricity → heat) but at electrical prices, operating cost high vs gas; baseboard heaters (240V resistance element + natural convection); radiant ceiling panels; heat strips in air handler; used as backup/supplemental heat in heat pumps; appropriate when electricity is cheap, heating loads small, or gas not available
- **Oil furnaces**: #2 fuel oil (similar to diesel); oil burner (high-pressure spray gun ignited by transformer spark); AFUE 80-90%; Northeast US historic reliance; conversion to natural gas or heat pump increasingly common as oil prices volatile; oil storage tank maintenance and leak liability
- **Wood and pellet systems**: wood stoves (non-HVAC), pellet stoves (automated fuel feed, thermostatic), wood gasification boilers (high-efficiency, catalytic or non-catalytic), outdoor wood boilers (isolated from house fire risk; long pipe run to house); EPA Phase 2 rules (2020) — strict emission limits for wood stoves and heaters
- **Radiant ceiling and radiant panel**: electric infrared panels (high-temperature, efficient if occupants in direct line of sight); low-watt-density radiant ceiling (hydronic); warm ceiling is comfortable (radiant exchange with occupants); distinct from high-temp infrared heaters (industrial/outdoor patios)
