# Reporting and Writing: Inverted Pyramid, Narrative, Data Journalism

## The Writing Landscape

Journalism writing is constrained by three forces: structure (which organizational form fits this story), speed (deadline pressure limits revision), and clarity (complex information for general audiences). Different story types require different forms — the inverted pyramid for breaking news, the narrative arc for features, the wall-style for analysis.

```
STORY FORM SELECTION
====================

  WHAT TYPE OF STORY IS THIS?
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BREAKING NEWS → Inverted Pyramid                                │
  │  ("Mayor resigns amid corruption investigation")                 │
  │                                                                  │
  │  FEATURE / HUMAN INTEREST → Narrative / Hourglass                │
  │  ("Inside the last week of the city's oldest bookstore")         │
  │                                                                  │
  │  ANALYSIS / EXPLAINER → Wall Street Journal Formula              │
  │  ("Why inflation persists despite Fed rate increases")           │
  │                                                                  │
  │  PROFILE → Biographical narrative                                │
  │  ("How a refugee from Haiti became the city's top cardiac        │
  │   surgeon")                                                      │
  │                                                                  │
  │  DATA STORY → Context-forward + visualization                    │
  │  ("School test scores fell in 87% of districts this year")       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Inverted Pyramid

The inverted pyramid is the default structure for news stories. It places the most newsworthy information first, allowing editors to cut from the bottom and readers to get the key facts quickly.

```
INVERTED PYRAMID STRUCTURE
===========================

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ██████████████████████████████████████████████████████████     │
  │  ████████████  LEAD — Most newsworthy information  ████████     │
  │  ██████████████████████████████████████████████████████████     │
  │                  (Who, what, when, where, why, how)             │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
       ▼
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ████████████████████████████████████████████████████      │
  │  ████ ELABORATION — Second most important details ████     │
  │  ████████████████████████████████████████████████████      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
       ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ████████████████████████████████████████████████    │
  │  ████ BACKGROUND / CONTEXT ████████████████████      │
  │  ████████████████████████████████████████████████    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
       ▼
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ██████████████████████████████████████    │
  │  ████ SECONDARY DETAILS ███████████████    │
  │  ██████████████████████████████████████    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────┘
       ▼
  ┌────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ████████████████████████      │
  │  ████ BACKGROUND DETAIL ████   │
  │  ████████████████████████      │
  └────────────────────────────────┘

  ORIGINS: Telegraph era — transmission could fail at any point;
  most important information had to come first to survive partial
  transmission. Structure survived because it also matches how
  time-pressed readers actually read news.

  ADVANTAGES:
  • Editors can cut from bottom without destroying story
  • Readers know the story from the first paragraph
  • Search engine friendly (keywords in lead)

  DISADVANTAGES:
  • No drama or suspense — conclusion before story
  • Can feel flat for complex, nuanced stories
  • Rewards speed over depth
```

## The Lead

The lead (first sentence/paragraph) is the most critical writing in the story. It must answer the most important questions immediately.

```
LEAD CONSTRUCTION
==================

  THE 5W+H LEAD (hard news):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  WHO:   The actor (subject) of the story                         │
  │  WHAT:  The action or event (verb and object)                    │
  │  WHEN:  Time (but not first word — leads never start with time)│
  │  WHERE: Location (briefly, not before the news)                  │
  │  WHY:   Cause or motivation (briefly if known)                   │
  │  HOW:   Method (briefly if newsworthy)                           │
  │                                                                  │
  │  EXAMPLE:                                                        │
  │  "Mayor James Chen resigned Thursday, citing personal health     │
  │   reasons, two weeks after the city council opened a formal      │
  │   investigation into his campaign spending."                     │
  │                                                                  │
  │  WHO:   Mayor James Chen                                         │
  │  WHAT:  resigned                                                 │
  │  WHEN:  Thursday                                                 │
  │  WHY:   "citing personal health reasons" (stated) /              │
  │         investigation (context that complicates the stated why)│
  │  WHERE: [implied — city hall context established previously]     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  LEAD PROBLEMS TO AVOID:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BURIED LEAD: Most newsworthy info in paragraph 3 or later       │
  │  LABEL LEAD: Just a noun phrase, no verb ("The resignation of    │
  │   Mayor James Chen...")                                          │
  │  ATTRIBUTION LEAD: Quote at the start without context            │
  │  NUMBER DUMP: Multiple statistics in first sentence              │
  │  "IT WAS A..." lead: Weak, vague, says nothing                   │
  │  BLIND LEAD: Uses pronouns before establishing subjects          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Attribution Standards

```
ATTRIBUTION IN NEWS WRITING
============================

  SAID vs. CLAIMED vs. ALLEGED:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SAID: Neutral attribution for direct statements                 │
  │   "The governor said the plan would create 5,000 jobs."          │
  │   (Most common; doesn't imply truth or falsehood)                │
  │                                                                  │
  │  CLAIMED: Implies the reporter has some doubt, or the claim      │
  │   is contested or unverifiable                                   │
  │   "The company claimed its product cured arthritis."             │
  │                                                                  │
  │  ALLEGED: For criminal accusations before conviction             │
  │   "Smith allegedly struck the officer." / "The alleged thief."   │
  │   Legal protection: Distinction between allegation and fact      │
  │                                                                  │
  │  ACCORDING TO: Preferred for reports, documents, officials       │
  │   "According to the audit, $2 million was unaccounted for."      │
  │                                                                  │
  │  REFUSED TO COMMENT: Accurate report of non-response             │
  │   "X did not respond to multiple requests for comment."          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ATTRIBUTION POSITION:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Short quotes: Attribution at end → "The system is broken,"      │
  │   said the director.                                             │
  │  Long quotes: Attribution after first sentence of quote          │
  │  NEVER: "Said the director" → "the director said" (AP style)     │
  │  NEVER: Compound-verb attribution → "said and noted" (use one)   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Nut Graf

The nut graf (nutshell paragraph) is the paragraph that tells readers why the story matters — the "so what." It is essential in narrative and feature writing, where the story doesn't open with the news directly.

```
NUT GRAF FUNCTION
==================

  STORY STRUCTURE WITH NUT GRAF:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ANECDOTAL OPENING (draws reader in with specific scene)         │
  │  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── │
  │  NUT GRAF: "Maria's story is one of thousands. Since January,    │
  │   more than 10,000 city residents have applied for emergency     │
  │   housing assistance, a 40 percent increase from last year,      │
  │   city records show. Despite record numbers, city officials      │
  │   have offered few solutions beyond extending existing           │
  │   waitlists."                                                    │
  │  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── │
  │  ELABORATION (the story fills in with evidence, voices, data)    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  NUT GRAF REQUIREMENTS:
  ① States the central claim of the story (explicitly)
  ② Establishes stakes (why this matters, what's at risk)
  ③ Provides scale (how widespread, how significant)
  ④ Appears within first 5–7 paragraphs (readers won't wait longer)
```

## Narrative Story Structures

### The Hourglass Structure

```
HOURGLASS STRUCTURE
====================

  INVERTED PYRAMID (hard news summary first)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Lead: What happened (news summary)  │
  │  Key facts: Casualties, officials    │
  │  Context: Background                 │
  └──────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓ TURN (explicit narrative pivot)
  NARRATIVE CHRONOLOGY (tell the story)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "It began Tuesday morning when..."  │
  │  Chronological narrative             │
  │  Quotes, detail, scene               │
  └──────────────────────────────────────┘

  Use when: Breaking news that also has a compelling
  human story to tell. Gets news to time-pressed readers
  but also tells the full story for those who continue.
```

### The Wall Street Journal Formula

```
WALL STREET JOURNAL FORMULA (for analysis/features)
======================================================

  SPECIFIC ANECDOTE (hooks the reader with a real person/scene)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "For 27 years, Sandra Williams kept her retirement savings      │
  │   at First National Bank. Last month, she moved it all out."     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓
  NUT GRAF (pivots from specific to universal story)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "Depositors like Williams are part of a quiet exodus from       │
  │   regional banks, according to new Federal Reserve data..."      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓
  BODY (evidence, expert voices, data, scope)
          ↓
  RETURN TO ANECDOTE (circle back to Sandra Williams)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "Williams says she has no plans to return. 'I just don't        │
  │   trust them the way I used to,' she said."                      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Advantages: Emotional resonance + factual evidence combined
  Works for: Complex policy, economic trends, social changes
```

## Data Journalism

Data journalism applies computational analysis to large datasets to find stories invisible to traditional reporting.

```
DATA JOURNALISM WORKFLOW
=========================

  DATA ACQUISITION
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  FOIA request for databases (not just documents)                 │
  │  Public databases: Census, BLS, BEA, state data portals          │
  │  Scraped data: Web scraping of public information                │
  │  Purchased data: Commercial data aggregators                     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓
  DATA CLEANING
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Government data is often messy: inconsistent naming,            │
  │   duplicate records, OCR errors in scanned documents             │
  │  Tools: OpenRefine, Python pandas, Excel/Google Sheets           │
  │  Document all cleaning decisions (reproducibility)               │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓
  ANALYSIS
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Aggregation: Totals, means, medians, distributions              │
  │  Comparison: Year-over-year, geographical, demographic           │
  │  Geospatial: Map patterns (geocoding, GIS)                       │
  │  Statistical: Significance tests, regression (with caution)      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓
  VERIFICATION
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Expert review of methodology                                    │
  │  Subject interview: Is the pattern explained by other factors?   │
  │  Peer review within newsroom (another data journalist checks)    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          ↓
  VISUALIZATION + PRESENTATION
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Principle: Show what the data actually says, not what           │
  │   makes the most dramatic chart                                  │
  │  Choose chart type for information type (see below)              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Avoiding Misleading Statistics

```
COMMON DATA JOURNALISM ERRORS
================================

  CHERRY-PICKING DATES:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Starting a trend chart at a peak or trough to make the trend    │
  │  look dramatic → always show full available time series          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  BASE RATE FALLACY:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "Crime in District X up 100%" → from 2 incidents to 4           │
  │  Always report absolute numbers AND rates/percentages            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CORRELATION ≠ CAUSATION:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Statistical correlation is always only described as such        │
  │  "Associated with" not "caused by"                               │
  │  Expert review required before any causal claim                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  TRUNCATED Y-AXIS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Bar charts with non-zero y-axis make small differences look     │
  │  enormous → start bar charts at zero                             │
  │  (Line charts can legitimately start non-zero for trend focus)   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  INAPPROPRIATE AVERAGE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Mean distorted by outliers → skewed distributions need median   │
  │  Example: "Average CEO pay is $15 million" — median is $5M       │
  │  Always ask: Is the distribution skewed? Report both if so.      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Datelines and House Style

```
STRUCTURAL CONVENTIONS
=======================

  DATELINE: City and state (or country) at start of story
  PARIS — President Emmanuel Macron announced...
  WASHINGTON — The Supreme Court ruled...
  Use only when reporter was physically present in that location.

  AP STYLE KEY RULES (applied in most US news outlets):
  • Numbers: Spell out one through nine; numerals 10 and above
  • Ages: Always numerals: "a 7-year-old boy"
  • Dates: Tuesday, not "last Tuesday" (avoid relative time)
  • Percent: "percent" (not %) in most prose contexts
  • Titles: Capitalize before name, lowercase after
    "President Biden" / "Joe Biden, the president"
  • States: Abbreviate only when following city name; AP abbreviations
    (not postal codes): Calif., not CA; Mass., not MA

  ACTIVE VOICE: News writing strongly prefers active voice
  PASSIVE: "The report was released by the agency."
  ACTIVE: "The agency released the report."
  (Passive appropriate only when subject unknown or irrelevant)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Story Type | Structure | Lead Type |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Breaking news (fire, vote, resignation) | Inverted pyramid | Hard news 5W+H |
| Feature on a social trend | WSJ formula | Anecdotal → nut graf |
| Crime narrative | Hourglass | Summary news → chronological narrative |
| Policy analysis | WSJ or explanatory | Nut graf-first or scene |
| Data story | Data-forward + visualization | Statistical finding |
| Profile | Narrative | Scene or anecdote |

## Common Confusion Points

**The inverted pyramid is not the best structure for features.** It is optimized for one thing: letting readers stop early with maximum information. For stories requiring emotional engagement, context, or nuance, other structures work better. Lead with the inverted pyramid only when the news value is so obvious it must come first.

**Attribution at the end of long quotes.** "The chairman said" after a 200-word block quote is disorienting — readers have forgotten who's speaking. Interrupt after the first sentence of long quotes: "blah blah blah," the chairman said. "More blah blah blah."

**The nut graf is not optional in long-form.** A story that opens with four paragraphs of scene-setting and never explicitly states what the story is about loses readers rapidly. The nut graf must appear by the fifth paragraph at the very latest. "Why am I reading this?" must be answered explicitly.

**Data journalism requires expert review before publication.** Computational journalists can make statistical errors. Before publishing data findings, have a statistician or subject-matter expert review the methodology. The story's credibility depends on the analysis being sound — and errors in data stories can be politically significant.
