# Soil Formation: Parent Material, Weathering, CLORPT Factors

## The Big Picture

Soil forms at the interface of the lithosphere and the biosphere through the simultaneous action of weathering (breaking rock down) and pedogenesis (building soil structure up). Formation operates on timescales of 100 to >100,000 years, but the key insight is that loss rates under cultivation commonly exceed formation rates by 10–100×.

## Section 1 — Parent Materials

The starting material determines the inherited mineralogy, texture, pH, and initial CEC of a soil.

### Types of Parent Material

```
  RESIDUAL PARENT MATERIAL:
  Formed in place by weathering of underlying bedrock
  Soil inherits bedrock mineralogy directly
  Examples:
    Granite --> coarse-textured, low pH, K-rich, low Ca
    Limestone --> fine-textured, high pH, high Ca, forms Alfisols/Mollisols
    Basalt --> fine-textured, high Mg/Fe/Ca, fertile (Andisols if volcanic)
    Shale --> fine-textured, variable; often high Al, moderate fertility
    Sandstone --> coarse-textured, low CEC, well-drained

  TRANSPORTED PARENT MATERIAL (depositional):
  +-----------+  +-------------------+  +----------------------+
  | Glacial   |  | Alluvial (stream) |  | Aeolian (wind)       |
  | Till /    |  | Floodplain        |  | Loess: silt deposited|
  | Outwash   |  | deposits: sand,   |  | by wind; deep, fine, |
  |           |  | silt, gravel      |  | highly fertile       |
  | Mixed;    |  | Well-sorted;      |  | Chinese Loess Plateau|
  | unsorted; |  | stratified;       |  | US Corn Belt loess   |
  | variable  |  | often fertile     |  | (Mollisols)          |
  +-----------+  +-------------------+  +----------------------+
  +-----------+
  | Organic   |  Peat, muck: Histosols; O material > 30 cm
  +-----------+  Glacial lake deposits: fine-textured lacustrine sediments
```

## Section 2 — Weathering Types

Weathering is the breakdown of rock and primary minerals into smaller particles and secondary minerals. Three categories operate simultaneously.

### Physical Weathering

```
  PHYSICAL WEATHERING MECHANISMS:

  1. FROST ACTION (freeze-thaw):
  Water fills rock fractures --> freezes --> expands 9% by volume
  --> Fractures widen and propagate
  Most effective in: arctic/alpine; many freeze-thaw cycles/yr
  Creates: blocky angular particles (colluvium)

  2. THERMAL EXPANSION/CONTRACTION:
  Daily temperature cycling in arid environments
  Differential expansion of mineral grains --> surface spalling (exfoliation)

  3. UNLOADING (pressure release):
  Overlying rock removed by erosion
  Rock expands upward --> sheet joints form parallel to surface
  --> Characteristic dome shapes (Half Dome, Yosemite)

  4. WETTING AND DRYING:
  Clay minerals expand when wet, contract when dry
  Repeated cycles --> surface cracking; particle separation

  5. BIOTURBATION:
  Tree roots penetrate fractures; growth widens them (root wedging)
  Burrowing animals mix and fragment material
  Earthworms: ingest and excrete 10–40 t/ha/yr; fragment mineral grains

  PRODUCT: Smaller particles; same mineralogy; increases surface area
  --> Exposes more mineral surface for chemical weathering
```

### Chemical Weathering

```
  CHEMICAL WEATHERING MECHANISMS:

  1. DISSOLUTION:
  Soluble minerals dissolve in water
  CaCO3 + CO2 + H2O --> Ca²⁺ + 2HCO3⁻  (limestone weathering)
  Dissolution rate: karst landscapes; caves; carbonate soils

  2. HYDROLYSIS (most important for silicates):
  H⁺ + OH⁻ from water attack mineral structure
  KAlSi3O8 + H2O + CO2 --> Al(OH)3 + K+ + H2SiO4 + CO2
  (K-feldspar --> kaolinite + K released + silicic acid)
  Rate controlled by: temperature, pH, mineral surface area, drainage

  3. OXIDATION:
  O2 in water oxidizes Fe2+ to Fe3+ (rust-colored soils)
  2FeS2 + 7O2 + 2H2O --> 2Fe2+ + 4SO4²- + 4H+  (pyrite oxidation)
  Acid mine drainage: sulfide rock exposure + O2 + water = H2SO4 production

  4. CARBONATION:
  CO2 + H2O --> H2CO3 (carbonic acid; weak acid)
  Carbonic acid accelerates hydrolysis of silicates
  Primary pathway for converting CO2 to bicarbonate (long-term C cycle)

  5. CHELATION:
  Organic acids (from roots, decomposition) complex Fe3+, Al3+
  --> Dissolve and mobilize normally insoluble minerals
  Important in podzolization (spodosol formation)
```

### Biological Weathering

```
  BIOLOGICAL WEATHERING:

  LICHENS:
  Pioneer colonizers of bare rock
  Produce oxalic acid + other organic acids
  --> Etch mineral surfaces; release Ca, K, Mg
  Surface area under lichen: 10–100× chemical weathering rate vs. bare rock

  PLANT ROOTS:
  Rhizosphere pH often 1–2 units lower than bulk soil (CO2, organic acids)
  Root exudates: citric acid, malic acid chelate Fe3+, Al3+
  Physical: root penetration; root growth pressure > 1 MPa

  MICROORGANISMS:
  Bacteria oxidize Fe2+ (Thiobacillus, Acidithiobacillus) and sulfur
  Fungi produce organic acids; lichen association
  Mycorrhizal fungi: weathering hyphae mechanically and chemically access mineral P

  EARTHWORMS AND SOIL FAUNA:
  Ingest soil; grind mineral particles internally (gizzard)
  Excrete as aggregated casts: smaller average particle size
  Net effect: accelerate both physical and chemical weathering rates
```

## Section 3 — CLORPT in Detail

### Climate Effects on Soil Development

```
  CLIMATE DETERMINES SOIL DEVELOPMENT TRAJECTORY:

  TEMPERATURE EFFECT:
  Weathering rate approximately doubles per 10°C temperature increase
  (van't Hoff rule; applies to many chemical reactions)
  Tropical soils: 10–100× faster weathering than arctic soils
  --> Tropical soils (oxisols, ultisols): deeply weathered, low primary minerals
  --> Arctic soils: thin, unweathered, primary minerals persist

  PRECIPITATION EFFECT:
  High precipitation: leaching dominates
  --> Soluble cations (Ca2+, Mg2+, K+, Na+) removed
  --> Residual Fe/Al oxides (red-orange colors)
  --> Low pH; low CEC; kaolinite + gibbsite (1:1 clays)

  Low precipitation (arid): evaporation > leaching
  --> Soluble salts accumulate (CaCO3, CaSO4, NaCl)
  --> High pH; often calcareous; Bk horizon (calcic)

  CLIMATE-SOIL ORDER CORRELATION:
  Arctic/subarctic tundra:    Gelisols, Inceptisols, Histosols
  Boreal conifer forest:      Spodosols (podzols)
  Temperate deciduous forest: Alfisols, Ultisols
  Temperate grassland:        Mollisols (most fertile agricultural soils)
  Mediterranean/semi-arid:    Alfisols, Aridisols
  Tropical wet:               Oxisols, Ultisols, Andisols (volcanic)
  Desert:                     Aridisols, Entisols
```

### Time Effects and Chronosequences

```
  PEDOGENESIS TIMELINE (temperate, humid climate):

  t = 0 yr:      Fresh mineral material (glacial till, alluvium, volcanic ash)
  t = 100 yr:    Biological activity begins; first hint of A horizon (Entisol)
  t = 1,000 yr:  A horizon darkened by organic matter; slight structure (Inceptisol)
  t = 10,000 yr: E horizon forming (eluviation); clay moving to B (Inceptisol --> Alfisol)
  t = 50,000 yr: Distinct Bt (argillic) horizon; Fe-Al redistribution
  t = 100,000 yr: Highly developed Bt; possible fragipan; Ultisol in humid tropics
  t = 1 Myr+:    Deeply weathered; oxisol-like; primary minerals largely consumed

  CHRONOSEQUENCE METHOD:
  Compare soils of known different ages (same climate, parent material, relief)
  Hawaiian volcanic flows: perfect natural experiment
  Basaltic flows: t=0 to t=4 Myr; tracks full weathering sequence
  Results: Ca2+ depleted by 2,000 yr; P sorbed by Al/Fe by 10,000 yr;
           virtually no primary minerals remain after 1 Myr
```

## Section 4 — Secondary Minerals (Products of Weathering)

```
  WEATHERING PRODUCT HIERARCHY:
  PRIMARY MINERALS (rocks) --> SECONDARY MINERALS (soils)

  PRIMARY: Quartz, K-feldspar, Na-feldspar, Ca-feldspar, olivine,
           pyroxene, mica (muscovite, biotite), hornblende
           --> Resistance order: Quartz (most resistant) > K-feldspar >
               Na-feldspar > Ca-feldspar > olivine (least resistant)

  SECONDARY CLAY MINERALS:
  +---------------------+------------------+-----------+---------+
  | Mineral             | Type             | CEC (meq/ | Where   |
  |                     |                  | 100 g)    | formed  |
  +---------------------+------------------+-----------+---------+
  | Smectite (montmor.) | 2:1 expanding    | 80–150    | Moderate|
  |                     | clay             |           | leaching|
  | Vermiculite         | 2:1 non-expand.  | 100–150   | Moderate|
  | Illite              | 2:1 non-expand.  | 10–40     | Variable|
  | Chlorite            | 2:1:1            | 10–40     | Moderate|
  | Kaolinite           | 1:1 non-expand.  | 1–15      | High    |
  |                     | clay             |           | leaching|
  | Gibbsite Al(OH)3    | Al hydroxide     | 0–5       | Very    |
  | Goethite FeOOH      | Fe oxyhydroxide  | 0–5       | highly  |
  | Hematite Fe2O3      | Fe oxide         | 0–5       | weathered|
  +---------------------+------------------+-----------+---------+

  2:1 clays (smectite): 2 silica sheets sandwich 1 alumina sheet
  --> Can expand between layers when wet; high shrink-swell; high CEC
  1:1 clays (kaolinite): 1 silica + 1 alumina; no interlayer expansion; low CEC
```

## Section 5 — Pedogenic Processes

The processes that build soil profile differentiation from undifferentiated parent material:

```
  PEDOGENIC PROCESSES:

  ADDITION:
  Organic matter (litter, roots, root exudates)
  Aeolian input (dust, pollen, volcanic ash)
  Colluvial additions from upslope

  TRANSLOCATION (within profile):
  ELUVIATION: removal of material from upper horizon (E = elluviated)
    Types: leaching of cations, clay translocation, podzolization (Fe/Al/OM complex)
  ILLUVIATION: accumulation in lower horizon (B = illuviated)
    Argillic (Bt): clay accumulation
    Spodic (Bhs): organic matter + Fe + Al accumulation
    Calcic (Bk): CaCO3 accumulation
    Oxic (Bo): Fe/Al oxide accumulation

  TRANSFORMATION:
  Weathering of primary to secondary minerals
  Humification of organic matter (SOM formation)
  Reduction/oxidation (redox) changes

  LOSS:
  Leaching to groundwater
  Erosion to streams
  Gaseous losses (N2, N2O, CO2, CH4)
  Crop harvest (nutrient export)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why is limestone parent material more fertile than granite? | Limestone weathers to Ca2+, Mg2+, HCO3-; maintains high pH; higher CEC; granite releases K+ but is acidic and coarse |
| Why are tropical soils often nutrient-poor? | Highly weathered (millions of yr); all primary minerals consumed; only Fe/Al oxides and kaolinite remain; nutrients held in biomass, not mineral soil |
| Why are loess-derived soils (Mollisols) so fertile? | Fine silt particle size + carbonate minerals + grassland OM accumulation = high CEC + high SOM + favorable pH |
| What drives podzol (spodosol) formation under boreal conifers? | Acidic conifer litter + chelating organic acids mobilize Fe/Al from E horizon → Bhs accumulation |
| What chemical weathering reaction drives the long-term carbon cycle? | Silicate hydrolysis consumes CO2 to form bicarbonate; transported to ocean; precipitated as CaCO3 over millions of years |
| How fast do soils form vs. how fast do they erode under cultivation? | Formation: ~0.01–0.1 mm/yr; erosion under cultivation: 1–10 mm/yr → net loss in most row-crop agriculture |

## Common Confusion Points

**Chemical weathering produces secondary minerals, not just ions.**
When K-feldspar weathers, it doesn't just release K+ — it forms kaolinite and other secondary clay minerals as residues. The clay mineralogy of a soil is the product of weathering history, not original parent material texture.

**Young alluvial soils can be highly fertile despite minimal pedogenesis.**
Entisols on young alluvium (floodplain deposits) may have very little profile development but inherit fresh mineral nutrients from upstream erosion. The Nile delta, Ganges-Brahmaputra floodplain, and Sacramento Valley are fertile precisely because annual flooding deposits fresh mineral-rich sediment.

**Biological weathering rates are difficult to overstate.**
In the rhizosphere, soil pH can be 2 units lower than bulk soil, weathering rates 10–1000× faster than in bare inorganic conditions. Mycorrhizal fungi mechanically drill into minerals with hyphae under enormous pressure. The mineral nutrient supply rate in most soils is ultimately biologically mediated.

**Soil formation is not unidirectional.**
Erosion, cultivation (mixing), and catastrophic events (volcanic burial, glaciation) reset pedogenesis. A soil with 10,000 years of development can be reset to effectively zero by severe erosion exposing subsoil.
