# Soil Microbiology: Bacteria, Fungi, Mycorrhizae, Soil Food Web

## The Big Picture

A gram of fertile agricultural soil contains 10⁸–10⁹ bacterial cells, 100–200 m of fungal hyphae, thousands of nematodes, and vast numbers of protozoa and arthropods. This community drives all nutrient cycling, builds soil structure, defends plants from pathogens, and regulates the atmosphere via CH₄ and N₂O. Managing soil biology is managing soil productivity.

```
SOIL FOOD WEB — ENERGY FLOW

  SOM + Plant Residue + Root Exudates
  (primary energy source)
          |
          v
  BACTERIA + FUNGI (decomposers; primary consumers)
     |          |
     |          +--- FUNGAL FEEDERS (nematodes, mites, collembolans)
     |
     v
  PROTOZOA (flagellates, amoebae, ciliates)
     |       Graze bacteria; mineralize N in the process
     |
     v
  NEMATODE PREDATORS (feeding on protozoa + other nematodes)
     |
     v
  MITES + COLLEMBOLA (microarthropods)
  ENCHYTRAEIDS (potworms)
     |
     v
  EARTHWORMS + MILLIPEDES + BEETLES
  (macrofauna; physical soil engineers)
     |
     v
  SMALL INVERTEBRATE PREDATORS (centipedes, predatory beetles)
          |
          v
  BIRDS, MAMMALS, MOLES (consume soil invertebrates)
```

## Section 1 — Soil Bacteria

### Numbers and Biomass

```
  BACTERIAL ABUNDANCE:
  Typical agricultural soil: 10⁸–10⁹ cells/g (10⁸ = 100 million)
  Forest organic layer: 10⁹–10¹⁰ cells/g
  Subsoil (> 50 cm): 10⁵–10⁷ cells/g (decreasing with depth rapidly)

  BACTERIAL BIOMASS:
  Typical: 150–500 kg C/ha in top 15 cm
  Active fraction: only 1–5% of cells actively metabolizing at any time
  Most are dormant (energy limitation; not dead)

  BACTERIAL CELL SIZE: 0.5–5 µm; much smaller than fungal hyphae
```

### Functional Groups

```
  KEY BACTERIAL FUNCTIONAL GROUPS:

  HETEROTROPHS (organic C oxidizers):
  Most soil bacteria; decompose SOM, plant residues
  Major genera: Arthrobacter, Bacillus, Pseudomonas, Streptomyces
  Aerobic when O2 available; facultative anaerobes in microsites

  CHEMOLITHOTROPHS (inorganic compound oxidizers):
  Nitrifiers: Nitrosomonas + Nitrospira + Nitrobacter
    NH4+ --> NO2- --> NO3- (nitrification; aerobic; critical for N cycling)
  Sulfur oxidizers: Thiobacillus, Acidithiobacillus
    FeS2 + H2SO4 (acid mine drainage)
  Iron oxidizers: Gallionella, Leptothrix

  NITROGEN FIXERS (N2 --> NH4+):
  Free-living: Azotobacter (aerobic), Clostridium (anaerobic)
               Cyanobacteria (Anabaena -- some associated with free-living)
               Rate: 1–5 kg N/ha/yr (low; significant only over long time)
  Symbiotic: Rhizobium, Mesorhizobium, Bradyrhizobium (in legume root nodules)
             Rate: 50–300 kg N/ha/yr (economically significant)
             Frankia (actinorhizal plants: alder, sea buckthorn, bayberry)
             Rate: 20–150 kg N/ha/yr

  DENITRIFIERS:
  Pseudomonas, Paracoccus, Thiobacillus denitrificans (anaerobic)
  NO3- --> NO2- --> NO --> N2O --> N2
  Require: anoxic microsites, NO3-, organic C substrate
  Hot spots: wet, aggregated soil where O2 diffusion is limited

  SULFATE REDUCERS (anaerobic):
  Desulfovibrio, Desulfobacter
  SO4²- + organic C --> H2S + CO2 + H2O
  Produces: H2S (toxic; rotten egg smell in waterlogged soils)
```

### Rhizosphere Effects

```
  RHIZOSPHERE = zone of intense biological activity around roots
  Extends: ~1–3 mm from root surface
  Bacterial density: 10–100× higher than bulk soil
  Reason: root exudates provide energy-rich C substrates

  ROOT EXUDATE TYPES:
  Sugars (glucose, fructose): direct bacterial energy
  Organic acids (citric, malic, oxalic): chelate Fe, Mn, P
  Amino acids: N source for rhizosphere bacteria
  Phenolics: signaling for symbioses (rhizobia, mycorrhizae)
  Mucilage (polysaccharides): aggregate stabilization; lubrication

  RHIZOSPHERE PRIMING EFFECT:
  Root exudates stimulate microbial decomposition of OLD soil organic matter
  Even in well-fertilized soils, roots "prime" mineralization
  Mechanism: fresh C supply activates dormant decomposers
  Effect: up to 300% increase in SOM decomposition rate in rhizosphere
  Agriculture implication: plant roots drive N mineralization timing
```

## Section 2 — Soil Fungi

### Fungal Biomass and Ecology

```
  FUNGAL BIOMASS:
  Agricultural soil: 300–1500 kg C/ha in top 15 cm
  Forest floor: 500–5000 kg C/ha (dominant over bacteria in forests)
  Hyphae length per gram: 100–200 m (agricultural); up to 1,000 m (forest)

  FUNGI vs. BACTERIA:
  BACTERIA dominate: high-N substrates (young leaves, manure), moist soils,
                     cultivated/disturbed soils
  FUNGI dominate:    low-N substrates (wood, lignin, recalcitrant OM),
                     well-structured undisturbed soils, forests

  FUNGAL FUNCTIONAL GROUPS:
  +-- Saprotrophic: decompose dead organic matter (wood, litter)
      Basidiomycetes (white-rot, brown-rot fungi): lignocellulose
      Ascomycetes: diverse substrates
  +-- Mycorrhizal: mutualistic with plant roots (see Section 3)
  +-- Pathogenic: diseases of plants and animals
  +-- Predatory: some capture nematodes (Arthrobotrys: constricting rings)

  WHITE-ROT vs. BROWN-ROT:
  White-rot: degrades BOTH cellulose AND lignin
             Uses peroxidase enzymes; produces CO2 + H2O
             Net: complete mineralization; pale bleached wood remains
  Brown-rot: degrades ONLY cellulose (leaves lignin)
             Produces brown crumbly residue (brown carbon in soil)
             Important source of recalcitrant soil C
```

### Glomalin and Aggregate Stability

```
  GLOMALIN:
  Glycoprotein produced by arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi
  Coats fungal hyphae; released into soil upon death
  Strongly hydrophobic: stabilizes soil aggregates
  GRSP (Glomalin-Related Soil Protein): 1–100 mg/g soil depending on type
  Estimated 10–15% of soil C may be glomalin-derived in some soils
  Relationship: AM fungi biomass --> glomalin --> aggregate stability
  Management: no-till preserves hyphal networks --> more glomalin
              tillage disrupts hyphae --> less glomalin --> poorer aggregation
```

## Section 3 — Mycorrhizal Associations

Two dominant types: ectomycorrhizal (EM) and arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM), with fundamentally different biology.

### Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungi (AMF)

```
  AMF CHARACTERISTICS:
  Phylum: Glomeromycota
  Species: ~220 described (remarkably few for their ecological importance)
  All obligate symbionts: cannot grow without a plant host
  Distribution: ~80% of all vascular plant species form AM associations
  Including: most agricultural crops (maize, wheat, soybean, tomato, potato)
  Not AM: Brassicaceae (cabbage family), Chenopodiaceae, some Proteaceae

  INFECTION MECHANISM:
  Spore germinates; hypha grows toward root
  Strigolactone exudate from P-deficient roots signals the fungus
  Hyphopodium forms on root surface; intracellular penetration
  ARBUSCULE forms inside cortical cell:
    Branched tree-like structure maximizing surface area
    Exchange site: P from fungus --> plant; C (sugar) from plant --> fungus
  VESICLE forms: lipid storage organ within root or soil

  NUTRIENT ACQUISITION:
  Hyphae explore soil volume 10–100× larger than roots alone
  Hyphae only 2–5 µm diameter; penetrate pores inaccessible to roots (> 100 µm)
  P uptake: hyphae access P-depleted zones; release organic acids + phosphatases
  Typical P delivery: 40–80% of plant P via AM pathway in P-limited soils
  Also: Zn, Cu, N (limited), water

  COST TO PLANT:
  4–20% of total plant photosynthate transferred to fungus
  Higher cost when P is abundant (plant reduces AM colonization)
  Plant regulates colonization: high-P suppresses AM colonization
```

### Ectomycorrhizal Fungi (EMF)

```
  EMF CHARACTERISTICS:
  Phyla: Basidiomycota + Ascomycota (both represented)
  Species: ~6,000–20,000 estimated (much higher diversity than AM)
  NOT obligate: some can live saprotrophically
  Host: primarily trees of temperate/boreal forests
  KEY HOSTS: Pinus, Picea, Abies, Larix, Quercus, Fagus, Betula, Eucalyptus

  INFECTION MECHANISM:
  Fungal hyphae WRAP around root tip: MANTLE (sheath)
  Hyphae grow BETWEEN (not into) cortical cells: HARTIG NET
  No penetration of cell membranes (unlike AM arbuscules)
  Root tip modified: swollen, branched or unbranched "mycorrhizal root"

  NUTRIENT ACQUISITION:
  More complex organic N mineralization than AM fungi
  Can produce proteases, chitinases: access N from organic matter
  Particularly important in N-limited boreal forests
  Exploit patchy nutrient distribution in forest organic layer
  P acquisition: similar to AM but through different enzyme portfolio

  FRUITING BODIES:
  Many EM fungi produce macroscopic fruiting bodies
  Boletus, Cantharellus (chanterelle), Amanita, Russula, Tricholoma
  Truffles (Tuber spp.): EM fruiting bodies of oak/hazel

  SPECIES SUCCESSION:
  Early colonizers: fast-growing; "early succession" EM genera (Laccaria, Inocybe)
  Late succession: long-lived; complex; Piloderma, Suillus, Cenococcum
  Diverse EMF community = forest nutritional resilience
```

### AM vs. EM Comparison

| Feature | Arbuscular Mycorrhizal (AM) | Ectomycorrhizal (EM) |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Fungal phylum | Glomeromycota | Basidiomycota + Ascomycota |
| Host plants | ~80% vascular plants | Mostly trees (temperate/boreal) |
| Cell penetration | Yes (arbuscules inside cells) | No (Hartig net between cells) |
| Hyphal structure inside root | Arbuscule + vesicle | Hartig net |
| Root tip morphology | Unchanged externally | Swollen, mantled |
| Primary nutrient delivered | P (mostly) | P + complex organic N |
| Dominant ecosystem | Tropics, agriculture | Temperate + boreal forests |
| Species richness | ~220 sp. | ~6,000+ sp. |

## Section 4 — Soil Food Web

The soil food web is the community of organisms connected through feeding relationships and decomposition.

```
  SOIL FOOD WEB — FUNCTIONAL GROUPS AND TROPHIC LEVELS:

  TROPHIC LEVEL 1 (Primary producers and C substrates):
  Plant roots, root exudates, fresh plant litter
  Algae (surface soils), cyanobacteria (biological soil crusts)

  TROPHIC LEVEL 2 (Primary decomposers/consumers):
  Bacteria (10⁸–10⁹/g): decompose soluble C; mineralize N
  Fungi (100–200 m hyphae/g): decompose structural C (lignin, cellulose)
  Protozoa (10⁴–10⁵/g): flagellates + amoebae bacterivores
  Algal feeders (surface):

  TROPHIC LEVEL 3 (Secondary consumers):
  Bacterivorous nematodes (10²–10³/g): Rhabditis, Acrobeles
  Fungivorous nematodes: Aphelenchoides, Ditylenchus
  Fungivorous collembola (10⁵/m²): Onychiurus, Folsomia
  Fungivorous mites (Oribatida): Scheloribates, Oppiella

  TROPHIC LEVEL 4:
  Predatory nematodes (Mononchus, Labronema)
  Predatory mites (Mesostigmata): Hypoaspis, Gamasida
  Predatory beetles (ground beetles, rove beetles)
  Enchytraeids (feed on fungi + bacteria)

  ECOSYSTEM ENGINEERS (not strictly trophic):
  Earthworms: ingest 10–40 t soil/ha/yr; creates bioturbation + casts
  Termites: decompose wood + build mounds (tropical)
  Ants: move materials; create macropores; farm fungi (leafcutter)
```

### N Mineralization via Protozoa

The microbial loop: bacteria immobilize N in biomass; protozoa graze bacteria and excrete excess N as NH₄⁺.

```
  MICROBIAL LOOP:
  Plant N demand: requires mineral N (NH4+, NO3-)
  Bacteria:       immobilize N in biomass (C:N ratio ~ 6:1)
                  if C:N of substrate < 25: net N mineralization
                  if C:N > 25: net N immobilization (N tied up)
  PROTOZOA:       graze bacteria (C:N of bacteria ~ 6:1)
                  Protozoa C:N ~ 6:1 also; excess N excreted as NH4+
  NET EFFECT:     1 protozoan grazing event releases ~0.7 NH4+ per bacterial cell
  In pot experiments: protozoa increase plant N uptake 30–60%
                       (microbial loop makes locked N accessible to plants)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What drives high vs. low F:B ratio (fungal:bacterial)? | High F:B in forest/no-till/well-structured soils (fungi dominate); low F:B in cultivated/disturbed/wet soils (bacteria dominate) |
| Why does tillage reduce aggregate stability? | Destroys fungal hyphae and glomalin; disrupts earthworm channels; exposes SOM to decomposition; reduces binding agents |
| What is the AM fungal arbuscule? | Branched tree-like intracellular structure; the exchange interface for P (fungus) and C (plant) |
| Why can't AM fungi grow without a plant host? | Obligate symbionts; cannot synthesize their own lipids; depend on plant C supply |
| What is the rhizosphere priming effect? | Root exudates stimulate microbial decomposition of old SOM; up to 300% increase in mineralization near roots |
| What is the microbial loop? | Bacteria immobilize N; protozoa graze bacteria; excrete excess N as NH4+; makes N plant-available |
| Which mycorrhizal type processes complex organic N? | EM fungi (boreal/temperate trees); produce proteases; access N from forest organic matter |

## Common Confusion Points

**Soil bacteria numbers are impressive but most are inactive.**
Of 10⁸–10⁹ bacteria per gram, typically 1–5% are actively metabolizing. The rest are dormant, limited by energy (C) availability. "Awakening" dormant bacteria with fresh C inputs (tillage, organic matter addition) explains the "tillage flush" of N mineralization after plowing.

**Mycorrhizal inoculants from the garden center often do not work.**
Commercial AM fungal inoculants are often low-viability, wrong species, or applied in conditions that suppress colonization (high P). Most soils already have functional AM fungal communities. The best way to support mycorrhizae is to reduce tillage and P fertilizer rates, not to add inoculants.

**Fungi and bacteria are competitors, but decomposition requires both.**
Bacteria decompose low C:N substrates (young leaves, manure) rapidly. Fungi decompose high C:N substrates (wood, straw, lignin) that bacteria cannot. A diverse, balanced microbial community decomposes the full spectrum of organic inputs. Bacterial-dominated soils cannot decompose recalcitrant organic inputs efficiently.

**Earthworm biomass is not always a good sign.**
In deep, compacted soils, high earthworm density can actually increase bulk density because their casting compresses the soil. Anecic (deep burrowing) earthworms (Lumbricus terrestris) generally improve structure; endogeic (shallow mixing) worms may be less beneficial in some contexts.
