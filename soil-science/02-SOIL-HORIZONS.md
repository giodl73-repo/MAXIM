# Soil Horizons: Master Horizons, Profiles, and USDA Soil Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

A soil profile is the vertical sequence of layers (horizons) from surface to parent material. Horizons are the product of pedogenic processes acting over time on parent material. Reading a profile is reading the history of that soil — climate, vegetation, hydrology, disturbance — encoded in color, texture, structure, and chemistry.

```
SOIL PROFILE — MASTER HORIZONS

  SURFACE
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  O  ORGANIC HORIZON        Oi: fresh litter (identifiable)
     (above mineral soil)   Oe: partially decomposed (fibric/hemic)
                            Oa: fully decomposed (sapric = muck)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A  MINERAL TOPSOIL        Dark color from SOM; biologically active
     (A horizon)            Ap = plowed layer (agricultural); loses structure
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  E  ELUVIATED HORIZON      Light color; leached of Fe, Al, clay
     (E horizon)            Common in Spodosols, Alfisols (podzolic soils)
     (albic = white/pale)   Not all soils have E
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  B  SUBSOIL                Illuviation or alteration zone
     (B horizon)            See subordinate designations below
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  C  PARENT MATERIAL        Slightly or un-weathered material
     (C horizon)            Retains parent material structure
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  R  BEDROCK                Consolidated, continuous rock
     (R horizon)            Cannot be dug with hand tools
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  DEPTH
```

## Section 1 — Master Horizon Descriptions

### O Horizon (Organic)

```
  O HORIZON = organic material > 20% organic C (by weight)
  (> 30% organic C for mucky material)

  Located at or above mineral soil surface
  Present in: forests, peatlands, wetlands
  Often absent in: cultivated fields, grasslands (OM incorporated into A)

  SUBDIVISIONS:
  Oi (fibric): > 2/3 plant material recognizable; fresh litter
  Oe (hemic): 1/3 to 2/3 recognizable; partially decomposed
  Oa (sapric): < 1/3 recognizable; highly decomposed; dark

  ECOLOGICAL ROLE:
  Provides organic C to A horizon via leaching and bioturbation
  Mycorrhizal fungi concentrated here
  Key thermal insulation layer in permafrost soils
```

### A Horizon (Mineral Topsoil)

```
  A HORIZON = mineral horizon at or near surface
  Darkened by organic matter accumulation (humus coats mineral grains)
  Most biologically active mineral horizon

  COLOR: dark brown to black (high SOM) -- Munsell value < 3.5, chroma < 3.5
  ORGANIC MATTER: 1–15% in most agricultural soils; > 20% in Mollisols
  STRUCTURE: granular or crumb (most fertile); platy in compacted soils
  ROOTING: most roots concentrated here

  SUBTYPES:
  Mollic epipedon: dark, thick (>18 cm), high base saturation (>50%); Mollisol criterion
  Umbric epipedon: like mollic but low base saturation (acidic)
  Anthropic epipedon: modified by plowing and fertilization
  Ap (plow layer): top 15–20 cm disturbed by cultivation; sharp lower boundary
```

### E Horizon (Eluviated)

```
  E HORIZON = zone of eluviation (loss of material upward to downward)
  Materials lost: clay, Fe, Al oxides, organic matter (via chelation)
  Materials remaining: quartz and other resistant primary minerals
  Result: pale, bleached appearance; low clay content; high sand/silt fraction

  COLOR: pale; gray, white, yellowish-gray (Munsell chroma < 3)
  TEXTURE: coarser than overlying A and underlying B (clay removed)
  PRESENT IN: Spodosols (podzols), Alfisols with strong eluviation
  ABSENT IN: most tropical soils, Mollisols, Vertisols

  ALBIC MATERIAL: horizon completely depleted of Fe/Al oxides;
                   white/pale color; often just above Bhs in Spodosol
```

### B Horizon (Subsoil — Illuviation/Alteration)

The B horizon is defined by alteration, not simply position. Subordinate letter symbols identify what has happened.

```
  B HORIZON SUBORDINATE DESIGNATIONS:

  Bt  = accumulation of translocated silicate clay (argillic)
        Clay films (argillans/cutans) visible on peds and in pores
        Common in: Alfisols, Ultisols, Mollisols

  Bhs = accumulation of humus + Fe + Al complexes (spodic)
        Dark colored; cemented possible; podzol B horizon
        Common in: Spodosols (boreal, sandy acid parent)

  Bs  = accumulation of Fe/Al without humus
  Bh  = humus accumulation alone

  Bk  = accumulation of CaCO3 (calcic)
        White powdery or nodular calcium carbonate
        Common in: Aridisols, semi-arid Mollisols

  Bo  = accumulation of Fe/Al sesquioxides (oxic)
        Red-brown; very low CEC; kaolinite + gibbsite
        Common in: Oxisols (tropics)

  Bw  = structural development (cambic); colored differently than C
        Slight weathering but no strong accumulation
        Common in: Inceptisols (young soils)

  Bg  = gleying (reduction under saturation)
        Gray, olive-gray colors (reduced Fe); mottles of orange-red
        Common in: Gleysols, poorly drained Inceptisols

  Bx  = fragipan: brittle, dense; brittle when dry; roots difficult
        to penetrate; common in Ultisols and some Alfisols of E USA
```

### C and R Horizons

```
  C HORIZON = slightly or unweathered parent material
  Retains structure of original parent material
  Some chemical alteration but no pedogenic structure
  Not always present (if erosion removed A+B, or bedrock near surface)

  Ck = C with CaCO3 (calcareous parent material)
  Cr = partly weathered, "rippable" bedrock (saprolite)

  R HORIZON = consolidated bedrock
  Cannot be broken by hand; continuous rock
  May be fractured (Rf) or massive
```

## Section 2 — Pedon, Polypedon, and the Soil Individual

```
  PEDON:
  Smallest 3-dimensional sampling unit of a soil
  Defined to include full lateral variation of all horizons
  Area: typically 1–10 m² (just large enough to sample representative profile)
  Depth: to R, or 2 m, whichever comes first

  POLYPEDON:
  Group of contiguous, similar pedons
  = the mapping unit of soil surveys (often = a series or phase)

  SOIL SERIES:
  The most specific (lowest) level in the Soil Taxonomy system
  Named after geographic location of type location (e.g., Norfolk sandy loam)
  ~22,000 soil series in the USA

  SOIL HORIZON vs. SOIL LAYER:
  Horizon: naturally occurring, pedogenically produced
  Layer: any depth increment, artificial (for sampling)
```

## Section 3 — Catena (Toposequence)

A catena is a regular sequence of soils that differ from each other in their characteristics as a result of variations in relief (topography) while parent material and climate are the same.

```
  CATENA CROSS-SECTION (humid temperate glacial landscape):

  SUMMIT           SHOULDER    BACKSLOPE    FOOTSLOPE    TOESLOPE
  (nearly level)   (convex)    (linear)     (concave)    (nearly level)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      ↑ well drained              ↓ moderately drained  ↓↓ poorly drained

  SOIL CHARACTERISTICS VARY ALONG CATENA:

  SUMMIT:          Alfisol / Mollisol; well-drained; maximum leaching
                   Deep A; strong Bt; oxidized B (brown/red)

  SHOULDER:        Thinner soil; often eroded A; carbonate deeper

  BACKSLOPE:       Variable; erosional vs. depositional position

  FOOTSLOPE:       Colluvial additions from upslope; slightly wetter;
                   deeper A horizon from added organic matter

  TOESLOPE:        Wetter; gleyed B horizon (Bg); gray colors;
                   may be Gleysol or Histosol in depression center
                   Possible seasonal saturation --> hydric soil

  WHY CATENA MATTERS FOR MANAGEMENT:
  Soil type changes every 50–200 m across a landscape
  Yield maps in precision agriculture show this pattern clearly
  Crop stress in wet hollows (anaerobic) vs. dry shoulders (drought)
  N2O emissions highest in wet footslope (denitrification hotspot)
```

## Section 5 — Diagnostic Horizons

Soil Taxonomy relies on precisely defined diagnostic horizons for classification. The most agriculturally important:

```
  EPIPEDONS (surface diagnostic horizons):

  Mollic: Dark; thick (>18 cm); base sat. > 50%; SOM > 1–2% -- grassland soils
  Ochric: Light-colored; most common default epipedon
  Anthropic: Human-modified; plowing, heavy fertilization
  Plaggen: Man-made; centuries of manure application; > 50 cm deep; European
  Histic: Organic; wet conditions; > 20–30% C; > 40 cm thick at surface

  SUBSURFACE DIAGNOSTIC HORIZONS:

  Argillic (Bt): Clay accumulation; clay films on ped surfaces; defines Alfisols, Ultisols
  Spodic (Bhs): Organic + Fe + Al illuviation; defines Spodosols
  Oxic (Bo): Sesquioxide dominance; very low CEC (< 16 meq/100 g clay); defines Oxisols
  Calcic (Bk): CaCO3 accumulation; defines Aridisols
  Cambic (Bw): Color/structure change; no illuviation; Inceptisol criterion
  Natric: Argillic + > 15% sodium saturation (sodic soils; dispersed structure)
  Fragipan: Brittle; dense; restricts roots and water movement
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the most agriculturally productive soil order? | Mollisols — thick dark mollic epipedon; high SOM; high base saturation; temperate grasslands |
| What horizon characterizes podzolization (boreal forests)? | E horizon (bleached, clay-depleted) + Bhs (dark Fe/Al/OM accumulation) |
| What diagnostic horizon is required for an Alfisol? | Argillic (Bt) horizon with base saturation > 35% |
| Why are Oxisols low in CEC despite being clay-rich? | Dominated by kaolinite (1:1 low CEC) and Fe/Al oxides (near-zero CEC); all 2:1 high-CEC clays weathered away |
| What does a Bg horizon indicate? | Gleying — periodic or permanent saturation with reducing conditions; hydric soil; gray from Fe reduction |
| What is a catena? | Toposequence — series of soils varying systematically with topographic position under same climate and parent material |

## Common Confusion Points

**Horizon depth is not standardized.**
The O horizon may be 1 cm or 30 cm; the A horizon 5 cm or 80 cm. Depth ranges are determined by pedogenic processes and site history. Do not assume "the A horizon is 20 cm deep" — always measure at each profile.

**B horizon is not defined as "below A."**
The B horizon is defined by its diagnostic characteristics (illuviation, structural development, color change, etc.) — not by position. In an eroded soil, the Bt may be the surface horizon. In a deep alluvial profile, the B may not exist.

**Soil color in field is measured under moist conditions in standard light.**
Munsell color is recorded both moist and dry. The moist color is more diagnostic. Colors are given as three components: hue (e.g., 10YR), value (brightness), and chroma (color intensity): "10YR 3/2" = dark grayish brown.

**The 12 soil orders are not mutually exclusive globally.**
All 12 orders occur on every continent except Antarctica (which lacks soils; ice). The order map shows dominant order by area but all orders occur within most temperate and tropical countries.
