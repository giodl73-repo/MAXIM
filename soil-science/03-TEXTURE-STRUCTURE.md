# Soil Texture and Structure: Sand/Silt/Clay Triangle, Aggregates

## The Big Picture

Soil texture (the permanent ratio of sand, silt, and clay particles) and soil structure (the arrangement of those particles into aggregates) are the two most important physical properties governing water movement, aeration, workability, and productivity. Texture is inherited from parent material and weathering; structure is built by biology and can be destroyed by tillage.

```
TEXTURE vs. STRUCTURE — THE ESSENTIAL DISTINCTION

  TEXTURE:                                STRUCTURE:
  Permanent; parent material-derived      Temporary; biologically built
  Sand + silt + clay percentages          Arrangement of particles into aggregates
  Cannot be changed without soil removal  Can be improved or destroyed
  Determines upper limit of fertility     Determines actual water/air movement
  "The hand you're dealt"                 "How you play the hand"

  BOTH control:
  Porosity, water retention, drainage, aeration, root penetration, erodibility
  Texture sets the range; structure modifies within that range
```

## Section 3 — Soil Texture Analysis Methods

### Hydrometer Method (Standard)

Based on Stokes' Law: rate of particle settling in water depends on particle diameter squared.

```
  STOKES' LAW:
  v = (2r²(ρp - ρf)g) / (9η)

  Where:
    v  = settling velocity (m/s)
    r  = particle radius (m)
    ρp = particle density (~2.65 g/cm³ for soil minerals)
    ρf = fluid density (~1.0 g/cm³ for water)
    g  = gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s²)
    η  = fluid viscosity (temperature-dependent)

  SETTLING TIMES AT 20°C:
  Sand (0.1 mm radius = 0.05 mm, fine sand):  ~ 30 seconds to settle 10 cm
  Silt (0.001 mm radius = 2 µm upper):          ~ 4 hours to settle 10 cm
  Clay (< 0.001 mm, < 2 µm):                  > 8 hours at 10 cm depth

  HYDROMETER TEST:
  Disperse soil (destroy aggregates with sodium hexametaphosphate)
  Put in 1 L cylinder; stir; measure hydrometer reading at:
  40 sec (sand removed; reading = silt + clay remaining in suspension)
  2 hr (silt also settled; reading = clay remaining)
  Calculate: %sand, %silt, %clay from readings
  Plot on triangle to determine textural class
```

### Field Texture (Ribbon Test)

```
  FIELD ASSESSMENT BY FEEL:
  Wet a small amount of soil; manipulate between thumb and forefinger

  SAND:    Individual grains visible; gritty; no plasticity; no ribbon
  LOAMY S: Mostly gritty; slight cohesion; very short ribbon < 1 cm
  SANDY L: Gritty; can squeeze a short ribbon (1–2 cm)
  LOAM:    Slightly gritty + slightly silky; smooth; 2–3 cm ribbon
  SILT L:  Silky/soapy; slightly plastic; 2–3 cm ribbon (breaks easily)
  CLAY L:  Plastic; sticky when wet; 3–5 cm ribbon
  CLAY:    Very plastic; very sticky; long ribbon > 5 cm; polishes when pressed
```

## Section 4 — Soil Structure: Aggregate Types

Soil structure is the arrangement of primary particles (sand, silt, clay) and organic matter into secondary units called peds or aggregates.

```
  AGGREGATE TYPES (PED SHAPES):

  GRANULAR:         CRUMB:              BLOCKY:
  +----+            +-------+           +----------+
  | o  |  1-5 mm   | ~o~o  |  1-5 mm  |  ______  |  5-50 mm
  | o  |  spherical | ~o~o  |  porous  | |      | |
  +----+            +-------+           +----------+
  Plowed horizon    Organic-rich A       Subsoil B horizon
  Common in well-   Common in forest     Angular or subangular
  cultivated soils  topsoil              blocky (ABK vs. SBK)

  PRISMATIC:        COLUMNAR:           PLATY:
  |       |         |  __  |            ═══════════
  |  3-20+|  cm    | |  | |            ─────────── 1-10 mm
  | prisms|         | |  | |  rounded   ─────────── layers
  |       |         |  ‾‾  |   top      horizontal
  Subsoil B         Natric (sodium)      Compacted soil
  Vertisols         horizons             Plowpan; E horizon

  KEY: Granular/crumb = BEST for plant growth (porous, well-aerated)
       Prismatic/blocky = normal subsoil (not bad)
       Platy = PROBLEMS (compaction, poor drainage, restricted roots)
```

### Aggregate Stability

Aggregate stability measures resistance to slaking (collapse when wetted) and dispersion (clay detachment under raindrop impact).

```
  AGGREGATE STABILITY TESTS:

  WET SIEVING: Place air-dried aggregates (4-8 mm) on 0.25 mm sieve;
               oscillate in water for 5 min;
               Stable aggregate % = fraction retained / initial weight × 100

  SLAKING TEST: Drop aggregates onto water surface; observe:
  Stable: aggregate holds intact (> 10 sec before any collapse)
  Unstable: immediate dispersion, turbid water

  FACTORS THAT INCREASE STABILITY:
  +-- Organic matter (humus coats particles; acts as glue)
  +-- Fungal hyphae (physical binding; glomalin protein from AM fungi)
  +-- Earthworm casting (mucus + mechanical processing)
  +-- Drying cycles (clay platelet re-arrangement)
  +-- Fe/Al oxide cementing (tropical soils; very stable)

  FACTORS THAT DECREASE STABILITY:
  +-- Tillage (physical disruption of aggregate bonds)
  +-- Sodium (Na+) dispersion (destroys aggregates; sodic soils)
  +-- Wetting too rapidly (air entrapment; slaking)
  +-- Loss of organic matter
  +-- Traffic compaction (shear forces break aggregates)
```

## Section 5 — Porosity: Macropores, Mesopores, Micropores

```
  PORE SIZE CLASSIFICATION:

  MACROPORES:    > 0.08 mm diameter
  Function:      Gravity drainage; earthworm channels; root channels
  Water content: Empty when at field capacity
  Air:           Provide gaseous exchange when drained

  MESOPORES:     0.008–0.08 mm
  Function:      Capillary water (plant available at FC)
  Water:         Held at tensions > 10 kPa
  Air:           Empty as soil dries beyond FC

  MICROPORES:    < 0.008 mm
  Function:      Tightly held water (available to bacteria but not to plants below PWP)
  Water:         Held at tensions > 1,500 kPa (PWP)
  Air:           Rarely air-filled

  TOTAL POROSITY:
  Sandy soil:    ~35–40% pore space by volume (fewer, larger pores)
  Clay soil:     ~45–60% pore space (more, smaller pores)
  Organic matter-rich soil: up to 70% (highly porous aggregated structure)

  BULK DENSITY:
  BD = dry soil mass / total volume (includes pores)
  Sandy soil BD: ~1.6 g/cm³
  Loam BD:       ~1.3 g/cm³
  Clay BD:       ~1.1–1.2 g/cm³
  Organic soil:  ~0.3–0.6 g/cm³
  Compacted:     > 1.7 g/cm³ (limits root growth)
  Soil particle density (Dp): ~2.65 g/cm³ (constant for minerals)
  Total porosity = 1 - (BD / Dp)
```

## Section 6 — Tillage Effects on Structure

```
  TILLAGE IMPACTS ON SOIL STRUCTURE:

  CONVENTIONAL TILLAGE (moldboard plow + disk):
  [Primary tillage] Inversion to 20–30 cm depth
  --> Destroys surface aggregates
  --> Buries residue; kills surface biological community
  --> Exposes fresh mineral soil; initially nutrient-accessible
  --> Creates compacted plowpan at bottom of tillage depth
  --> Over years: macropore reduction; earthworm population decline

  REDUCED TILLAGE (chisel plow, disk only):
  [Partial disturbance] Some surface disturbance; no inversion
  --> Moderate aggregate preservation
  --> Some residue at surface

  NO-TILL:
  [No soil disturbance] Seed directly into undisturbed surface
  --> Aggregates intact; surface biological community preserved
  --> Residue layer on surface: thermal buffer; erosion protection
  --> Earthworm populations: 10–50× higher than conventional (multi-year)
  --> Macropore channels preserved: rapid drainage through biological channels
  --> TRADE-OFF: More herbicide required (residue + weed pressure management)
                  May require adaptation period (5–10 yr for full benefit)

  STRUCTURE RECOVERY AFTER CONVERSION TO NO-TILL:
  Bulk density improvement: 3–5 yr for topsoil; 10+ yr for full profile
  Aggregate stability improvement: 2–5 yr
  Earthworm recovery: 3–10 yr depending on initial population
  Macropore restoration: 5–15 yr
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Which texture holds the most plant-available water? | Silt loam — large surface area without too-tight micropores; holds maximum capillary water |
| Which texture drains fastest? | Sandy; macropores dominate; water moves by gravity rapidly |
| What creates granular structure? | Biological activity: earthworms, fungal hyphae (glomalin), organic matter; aggregation by root exudates |
| What destroys granular structure fastest? | Tillage; also sodium (Na+) dispersion in sodic soils; raindrop impact on bare surface |
| What is bulk density and why does it matter? | Mass/volume ratio including pores; > 1.6 g/cm³ in loam = compaction restricting root growth |
| What does a platy structure indicate? | Compaction (surface traffic, tillage compaction below plow depth); poor aeration and drainage |
| How do you determine texture in the field without a lab? | Ribbon test: feel for grittiness (sand), silkiness (silt), plasticity and stickiness (clay) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Soil texture cannot be improved by adding sand to clay.**
This is a persistent misconception. Adding moderate amounts of sand to clay produces a mixture resembling concrete — the clay fills the voids between sand grains without improving drainage. To improve clay soil, add organic matter (which builds aggregates and improves structure), not sand.

**High clay content does not equal poor drainage permanently.**
A clay soil with excellent granular or prismatic structure can have rapid drainage through macropore channels between aggregates. A sandy soil with a compacted plowpan can have poor drainage. Structure trumps texture for actual water movement in many cases.

**No-till does not reduce compaction in all cases.**
Without deep-rooted cover crops or biological activity to create macropores, no-till surface compaction from wheel traffic can be worse than with tillage (which mechanically disrupts compaction). The no-till system requires biological activity to maintain structure.

**Texture triangle classes are field classifications, not precise chemical categories.**
The 12 textural classes are practical working categories with somewhat fuzzy boundaries. The actual boundary between "clay loam" and "clay" is not ecologically sharp. For precision work (water retention modeling, hydrology), use measured clay%, not class names.
