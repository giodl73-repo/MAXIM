# Soil Science — Pedology, Chemistry, Biology, and Management: The Full Landscape

## The Big Picture

Soil is the thin interface between rock and life — the "critical zone" where geology, biology, chemistry, and hydrology intersect. It is not dirt. It is a structured, living system that supports >95% of terrestrial food production, stores more carbon than all living vegetation plus the atmosphere combined, and filters the water supply for billions of people.

## The Critical Zone Concept

The critical zone is the thin layer from vegetation canopy to the base of fresh groundwater circulation — the zone where rock, soil, water, air, and living organisms interact to regulate Earth's surface environment.

```
CRITICAL ZONE ARCHITECTURE:

  ABOVE GROUND BIOMASS
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  LITTER / ORGANIC HORIZON (O)  : 0–5 cm
  TOPSOIL (A horizon)           : 0–30 cm      <-- most biologically active
  SUBSOIL (B horizon)           : 30–100 cm    <-- accumulation zone
  WEATHERED PARENT MATERIAL (C) : 0.5–5 m      <-- active weathering front
  SAPROLITE (partially weathered): up to 10–30 m
  FRESH BEDROCK (R)             :              <-- ultimate mineral source
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  GROUNDWATER ZONE               : depth variable

  KEY RATES:
  Soil formation: ~0.01–0.1 mm/yr (most soils) to ~1 mm/yr (young volcanic)
  Soil erosion (unprotected cultivated): ~1–10 mm/yr
  --> Most agricultural soils are losing soil faster than it forms
  Biological activity: concentrated in top 15–30 cm
```

## Soil Formation Factors (CLORPT)

Hans Jenny's state factor model (1941): soil = f(climate, organisms, relief, parent material, time)

```
  CLORPT FACTORS:

  C = CLIMATE
      Temperature: controls weathering rates, organic matter decomposition
      Precipitation: drives leaching, clay formation, CaCO3 movement
      P/PET ratio: humid vs. arid soil development trajectories

  L = ORGANISMS
      Plants: litter input, root exudates, bioturbation
      Animals: earthworms, insects, burrowing mammals (pedoturbation)
      Microbes: organic matter decomposition, N fixation, weathering

  O = RELIEF (topography)
      Slope aspect: N vs. S facing = different moisture, temperature, vegetation
      Slope position: summit, backslope, footslope (catena concept)
      Drainage: convergent hollows accumulate water; ridges shed water

  R = PARENT MATERIAL
      Mineralogy: granite vs. limestone vs. basalt vs. glacial till
      Texture: sandstone vs. shale vs. loess
      Determines CEC, pH, nutrient suite, texture baseline

  P = TIME
      Young soils (< 1,000 yr): dominated by parent material character
      Mature soils (10,000–100,000 yr): leaching + weathering evident
      Old soils (> 1 Myr): highly weathered; oxisols/ultisols
      Most agricultural soils: 10,000–100,000 yr since glaciation
```

## Soil Classification Overview

```
USDA SOIL TAXONOMY — 12 ORDERS (by formation environment):

  GELISOLS:     Permafrost within 2 m; Arctic, Antarctic, high altitude
  HISTOSOLS:    Organic soils (>30 cm organic material); peat bogs, fens
  SPODOSOLS:    Highly leached; acid; Bhs horizon; boreal forest; podzols
  OXISOLS:      Highly weathered; low CEC; Fe/Al oxides; humid tropics
  VERTISOLS:    High shrink-swell clays; crack when dry; black cotton soils
  ARIDISOLS:    Dry; evaporation > precipitation; CaCO3 accumulation
  ULTISOLS:     Heavily leached subtropical; argillic horizon; humid SE USA
  MOLLISOLS:    Thick dark mollic epipedon; grassland; high fertility (corn belt)
  ALFISOLS:     Moderate leaching; argillic horizon; temperate forest; fertile
  INCEPTISOLS:  Young, weakly developed; arctic to tropics; diverse
  ENTISOLS:     Minimal development; young; alluvium, dunes, steep slopes
  ANDISOLS:     Volcanic parent material; high P retention; low bulk density
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Soil organic matter (SOM) and soil organic carbon (SOC) are not the same.**
SOM = all organic compounds in soil; SOC is the carbon fraction. Convention: SOC × 1.72 ≈ SOM (based on assumption that organic matter is ~58% carbon). Modern soil carbon assessments report SOC in Mg C/ha or kg C/m².

**"Dirt" and "soil" are different.**
Dirt is displaced soil — soil out of context, in the wrong place. Soil is a structured, living system with specific horizon development and ecological function. The distinction matters for management: disturbed soil (subsoil exposed by construction) has none of the functional properties of topsoil.

**CEC is not the same as nutrient content.**
High CEC means high capacity to hold cation nutrients (Ca2+, Mg2+, K+, NH4+); it does not mean those nutrients are present. A high-CEC soil can be nutrient-deficient. CEC is a structural property; nutrient content requires separate measurement.

**"Degraded soil" often looks identical to healthy soil on the surface.**
Compaction, P depletion, salinization, and organic matter loss are largely invisible from surface appearance. Full assessment requires horizon examination, laboratory analysis, and biological characterization.
