# Nutrition — Overview

## The Big Picture

Nutrition is **applied biochemistry at the organism level** — how ingested molecules are digested, absorbed, transported, metabolized, and used or stored. The field sits at the intersection of biochemistry, physiology, epidemiology, and public health. Its central challenge: humans are complex adaptive systems, not controlled reactors, and most evidence is observational.

```
FOOD (complex mixture of macronutrients + micronutrients + phytonutrients + water)
     │
     ▼
DIGESTION & ABSORPTION
     │  Mouth: salivary amylase (starch → maltose), mastication (surface area)
     │  Stomach: HCl (pH 1.5–3.5) + pepsin (protein denaturation + cleavage)
     │  Small intestine: pancreatic lipase / amylase / proteases
     │                   bile salts (emulsification of fats)
     │                   brush border enzymes (maltase, lactase, sucrase)
     │  → absorbed: monosaccharides, amino acids, fatty acids, glycerol,
     │               monoacylglycerols, vitamins, minerals, water
     ▼
ROUTING BY SOLUBILITY
     │  Water-soluble nutrients (sugars, AAs, water-soluble vitamins):
     │    enterocytes → portal vein → liver (first-pass metabolism)
     │  Fat-soluble (FAs, fat-soluble vitamins A/D/E/K):
     │    reassembled as chylomicrons → lymphatics → thoracic duct →
     │    subclavian vein → systemic circulation (bypasses liver)
     ▼
LIVER: CENTRAL METABOLIC HUB
     │  Glycolysis / gluconeogenesis / glycogenesis
     │  β-oxidation / lipogenesis / VLDL/LDL assembly
     │  Amino acid catabolism / transamination / urea cycle
     │  Ketone body synthesis during prolonged fasting
     ▼
SYSTEMIC DISTRIBUTION & UTILIZATION
     ├── Glucose → brain (obligate), RBCs (anaerobic), muscle (working)
     ├── Fatty acids → heart + resting muscle (preferred fuel)
     ├── Ketone bodies → brain (fasting), heart (efficient substrate)
     └── Amino acids → muscle protein synthesis, gluconeogenesis, NT synthesis
     ▼
STORAGE OR EXCRETION
     ├── Glycogen: liver (~100g), muscle (~400g) — rapid mobilization
     ├── Triglyceride: adipose tissue — bulk energy store
     ├── Protein: no dedicated store; catabolize muscle in prolonged starvation
     └── Excess water-soluble vitamins/minerals → urine
```

## Nutrient Taxonomy

```
NUTRIENTS
├── MACRONUTRIENTS (gram-scale; primary energy and structural substrates)
│   ├── Carbohydrates  4 kcal/g
│   │   ├── Monosaccharides: glucose, fructose, galactose
│   │   ├── Disaccharides: sucrose (Glc+Fru), lactose (Glc+Gal), maltose (Glc+Glc)
│   │   ├── Polysaccharides: starch (α-1,4 linked; amylose + amylopectin)
│   │   │                    glycogen (highly branched, α-1,6 + α-1,4)
│   │   └── Dietary fiber: cellulose (β-1,4 — not human-digestible)
│   │                      pectin, FOS, inulin — fermented by gut bacteria → SCFAs
│   │
│   ├── Proteins  4 kcal/g
│   │   ├── 20 amino acids; 9 essential (cannot be synthesized):
│   │   │   His, Ile, Leu, Lys, Met, Phe, Thr, Trp, Val
│   │   ├── Conditionally essential (under stress): Arg, Gln, Gly, Cys, Tyr, Pro
│   │   └── Functions: structural (collagen, actin, myosin), enzymatic,
│   │                  signaling (insulin, GH), immune (antibodies), transport (Hb)
│   │
│   └── Fats (Lipids)  9 kcal/g
│       ├── Saturated FA (SFA): no double bonds; C12:0 to C22:0
│       │   Palmitic (C16:0), stearic (C18:0) — dominant in animal fat
│       ├── Monounsaturated FA (MUFA): 1 double bond
│       │   Oleic acid (C18:1 ω-9) — olive oil, avocado, majority of most fats
│       ├── Polyunsaturated FA (PUFA): ≥2 double bonds
│       │   omega-3: ALA (C18:3) from plants; EPA (C20:5) + DHA (C22:6) from fish
│       │   omega-6: Linoleic acid (C18:2) from vegetable oils → AA (arachidonic)
│       │   Essential: ALA + LA — humans lack Δ12 and Δ15 desaturase
│       └── Trans FA: geometrically trans double bonds
│           Industrial (partially hydrogenated): atherogenic — raises LDL, lowers HDL
│           Ruminant (vaccenic acid, CLA): different metabolism, less harmful
│
├── MICRONUTRIENTS (milligram to microgram scale; catalytic and regulatory)
│   ├── Vitamins (13 essential)
│   │   ├── Fat-soluble (stored; toxicity possible): A, D, E, K
│   │   └── Water-soluble (minimal storage; daily need): C + 8 B-vitamins
│   │       B1 (thiamine), B2 (riboflavin), B3 (niacin), B5 (pantothenic),
│   │       B6 (pyridoxine), B7 (biotin), B9 (folate), B12 (cobalamin)
│   └── Minerals
│       ├── Macrominerals (>100 mg/day): Ca, P, Mg, Na, K, Cl, S
│       └── Trace minerals (<100 mg/day): Fe, Zn, Cu, Se, I, Mn, Cr, Mo, F
│
└── PHYTONUTRIENTS (non-essential; bioactive plant compounds)
    ├── Polyphenols: flavonoids (quercetin, EGCG), stilbenes (resveratrol), lignans
    ├── Carotenoids: β-carotene (pro-vitamin A), lycopene, lutein/zeaxanthin
    └── Glucosinolates: cruciferous vegetables → indoles + isothiocyanates
        (broccoli, Brussels sprouts, wasabi — bitter defensive compounds)
```

## Energy Accounting

| Macronutrient | Energy density | Primary pathway |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Carbohydrate | 4 kcal/g | Glycolysis → pyruvate → acetyl-CoA → TCA → OXPHOS |
| Protein | 4 kcal/g | Transamination + deamination → gluconeogenesis or TCA |
| Fat | 9 kcal/g | β-oxidation → acetyl-CoA → TCA → OXPHOS |
| Alcohol | 7 kcal/g | ADH → acetaldehyde → ALDH → acetate → acetyl-CoA |
| Fiber | ~2 kcal/g | Gut bacteria → short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate) |

```
RESPIRATORY QUOTIENT (RQ) = VCO₂ / VO₂

  RQ = 1.00 → pure carbohydrate oxidation
  RQ = 0.70 → pure fat oxidation (less CO₂ per O₂)
  RQ = 0.83 → mixed substrate (typical resting state)
  RQ > 1.00 → net de novo lipogenesis (excess CHO → fat, extra CO₂ produced)

Measurement: indirect calorimetry (metabolic cart) in clinical/research settings
Practical: RQ shifts toward fat during fasting, toward CHO after meals
```

## The Epidemiology Problem

The central methodological challenge of nutrition science: we can rarely run the experiments we need.

```
HIERARCHY OF EVIDENCE (strongest → weakest for causal claims)

1. Mechanistic / in vitro / animal studies
   └── Establish biochemical plausibility; rarely translate directly to humans

2. Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)
   └── Gold standard, BUT hard in nutrition:
       ✗ Years-long interventions needed for chronic disease endpoints
       ✗ Cannot blind food allocation (participants know what they eat)
       ✗ Compliance degrades over years; free-living subjects cheat
       ✗ Surrogate endpoints (LDL, CRP) ≠ clinical outcomes (MI, death)
       ✗ Individual variation enormous (same diet, very different responses)

3. Prospective cohort studies (e.g., Nurses' Health Study, EPIC)
   └── Large N, long follow-up, BUT:
       ✗ Dietary recall via FFQ (food frequency questionnaire) is poor proxy
       ✗ Healthy user bias: vegetable eaters also exercise, sleep, don't smoke
       ✗ Residual confounding: can never measure everything
       ✗ Relative risks in cohort nutrition studies are typically small (HR 1.1–1.3)
          → easily explained by confounding

4. Cross-sectional studies, ecological correlations
   └── Weakest; generates hypotheses only
   └── Ancel Keys' 6-country plot (SFA → CHD) was ecological — but launched
       decades of policy

Most nutrition "facts" in public discourse come from cohort studies.
The biochemistry is solid; the population-level causation claims are often shaky.
```

## McGovern Report (1977) — Dietary Guidelines Politics

The first US Dietary Goals (1977 Senate Select Committee on Nutrition, chaired by Senator McGovern) established the template for all subsequent dietary guidelines:

- Reduce dietary fat (especially saturated fat) → reduce heart disease
- Increase carbohydrates to 55–60% of calories
- Basis: Ancel Keys' diet-heart hypothesis (observational + metabolic studies)
- Industry response: meat and egg industries lobbied successfully; "reduce consumption of meat" became "choose lean meat"
- Consequences: food industry reformulated products — fat out, sugar in
- Subsequent evidence: fat-heart relationship weaker than believed; carbohydrate quality matters enormously

This history is why you need to distinguish:
- Mechanistic biochemistry (well-established)
- Epidemiological associations (correlation, confounded)
- Policy recommendations (influenced by industry, politics, and incomplete science)

## Common Confusion Points

**Correlation in cohort studies ≠ causation**
The diet-heart hypothesis (saturated fat → LDL → heart disease) originates from observational data, supported by metabolic studies. The subsequent RCTs (Women's Health Initiative, PREDIMED, etc.) have produced genuinely mixed results. The relationship exists but is more conditional and nuanced than the 1977 guidelines implied.

**"Carbs cause insulin spikes" is true but the framing misleads**
Insulin is a normal anabolic hormone. Postprandial insulin peaks are not inherently harmful. The pathology is chronic fasting hyperinsulinemia (insulin resistance) driven by excess energy intake + sedentary lifestyle. A banana causes an insulin response; so does protein. The context — energy balance, metabolic health, activity — determines whether this matters.

**"Essential" = the body cannot synthesize it, not that it's critical in high quantity**
Essential fatty acids (ALA, LA) must come from food because mammals lack specific desaturase enzymes. Non-essential nutrients can become "conditionally essential" under physiological stress, illness, or genetic variants (e.g., cysteine becomes essential when methionine is deficient).

**Calories in/calories out is correct but incomplete**
The energy balance identity is thermodynamically exact. What it doesn't capture: how macronutrient composition affects satiety hormones (GLP-1, ghrelin), thermic effect of food (protein ~25–30%, CHO ~6–8%, fat ~2–3%), and gut microbiome — all of which affect the "calories in" side. CICO is true; "only CICO matters" is oversimplified.

**Supplements rarely replicate whole-food effects**
Beta-carotene supplements increased lung cancer risk in smokers (CARET trial) despite dietary beta-carotene appearing protective. The food matrix — phytonutrients, fiber, co-nutrients, physical structure — creates effects that isolated compounds don't replicate. This is why RCTs of single supplements so often fail.
