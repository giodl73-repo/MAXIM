# Dietary Patterns

## The Big Picture

Individual nutrients (vitamin C, omega-3, fiber) are studied in isolation, but people eat food, not nutrients — and foods combine into patterns that have synergistic, antagonistic, and emergent effects not predictable from components. The dietary patterns approach evaluates whole eating patterns against health outcomes, and is increasingly considered more robust evidence than single-nutrient intervention trials. The major lesson of 50 years of nutritional epidemiology: **the quality and source of foods matters more than individual macronutrient ratios**.

```
DIETARY PATTERNS — EVIDENCE HIERARCHY:

  STRONGEST EVIDENCE:
    Mediterranean diet:
      PREDIMED RCT (2013, retracted + re-published 2018):
      → Primary cardiovascular event prevention; ~30% relative risk reduction
      → Specific: EVOO arm and nuts arm vs control; strong effect
      → One of few large RCTs in dietary pattern research

    DASH diet:
      Multiple RCTs: consistently lowers blood pressure 8–12 mmHg systolic
      Mechanism understood (K+, Mg++, Ca++ physiology)

  GOOD OBSERVATIONAL EVIDENCE:
    Mediterranean: cancer, cognitive decline, T2DM, all-cause mortality
    Whole-food plant-based: cardiovascular risk, T2DM, cancer (specific sites)
    Blue Zones: longevity clusters (observational; confounders significant)

  STRONG NEGATIVE EVIDENCE:
    Ultra-processed food (NOVA 4):
      Multiple large cohort studies + 1 RCT (Hall 2019): clear associations
      with obesity, T2DM, CVD, cancer, all-cause mortality

  CONTESTED / INSUFFICIENT:
    Carnivore, OMAD, specific timing approaches: insufficient RCT evidence
    Vegan vs omnivore: observational evidence confounded by healthy user bias

MACRO-FRAMEWORK: WHAT ALL EVIDENCE-BASED PATTERNS SHARE:
  1. High vegetable, legume, whole grain, and fruit intake
  2. Minimal ultra-processed food
  3. Moderate to high fiber (25–40g/day)
  4. Healthy fat sources (nuts, olive oil, fish)
  5. Limited added sugar + refined carbohydrates
  → The "details" (meat yes/no, dairy, specific fats) matter far less than these 5 commonalities
```

## Mediterranean Diet

### PREDIMED Trial

```
PREDIMED (Prevención con Dieta Mediterránea):

  Design: multi-center RCT in Spain; n=7,447 high-cardiovascular-risk adults
  Duration: 4.8 years median follow-up

  Groups:
    1. Mediterranean diet + extra virgin olive oil (EVOO, ≥4 tbsp/day)
    2. Mediterranean diet + mixed nuts (30g/day: walnuts/hazelnuts/almonds)
    3. Control: low-fat diet advice (minimal active intervention)

  PRIMARY ENDPOINT: MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events)
    = MI + stroke + cardiovascular death

  RESULTS (2013 NEJM → 2018 re-analysis after methodological critique):
    EVOO arm: 30% relative risk reduction in MACE vs control (HR=0.69, 96% CI 0.53-0.91)
    Nuts arm: 28% relative risk reduction (HR=0.72, 96% CI 0.54-0.96)

  RETRACTION AND RE-PUBLICATION (2018):
    Original: some sites used cluster randomization improperly
    Re-analysis with corrected statistical approach: effect sizes held up
    → PREDIMED results remain the strongest dietary RCT evidence for CVD prevention

MEDITERRANEAN DIET COMPOSITION:
  CHARACTERISTIC FOODS (daily/weekly):
    Daily: EVOO (≥4 tbsp), vegetables (≥3 servings), fruits (≥3 servings),
           legumes (≥3/week), nuts (≥3/week), whole grains
    Weekly: fish (≥3/week), white meat > red meat
    Monthly: red meat (low), processed meat (minimal)
    Moderate: red wine (1–2 glasses/day with meals — optional)

  AVOIDS:
    Butter/margarine, cream, pastry; commercial sweets; SSB; processed meats

MECHANISMS:
  EVOO polyphenols (oleocanthal, hydroxytyrosol):
    Oleocanthal: COX-1/COX-2 inhibitor (like ibuprofen, lower dose)
    Hydroxytyrosol: antioxidant; reduces LDL oxidation
  Omega-3 from fish + ALA from walnuts → EPA/DHA pathway → anti-inflammatory
  Fiber from legumes/whole grains → microbiome SCFAs → LPS reduction
  Polyphenols (fruits, vegetables, wine) → endothelial function, NO bioavailability
  Mediterranean pattern vs components: synergy — not just olive oil or just fish
```

## DASH Diet

```
DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):
  Developed in 1990s; multiple NHLBI-funded RCTs

COMPOSITION:
  Fruits and vegetables: 8–10 servings/day
  Dairy (low-fat): 2–3 servings/day
  Whole grains: 6–8 servings/day
  Fish, poultry, legumes: increased
  Red/processed meat: limited
  Sodium: 1500–2300 mg/day (DASH-Sodium extension)
  Added sugar: minimal

EVIDENCE:
  Appel 1997 (NEJM): vs typical American diet → −11 mmHg systolic; −5.5 mmHg diastolic
    (In hypertensive: −11/−6; in normotensive: −6/−3)
  DASH-Sodium trial: combining DASH + low Na → additional BP reduction
  Effect equivalent to one antihypertensive medication
  Consistent across many RCTs and meta-analyses

MECHANISM:
  POTASSIUM (4700 mg/day target in DASH):
    Facilitates renal Na+ excretion (natriuresis)
    Na+/K+-ATPase: intracellular K+ → Na+ pump → cells maintain volume
    Vasorelaxation: K+ → hyperpolarize smooth muscle → vasodilation
    Modern diet: ~2500 mg/day; DASH: 4700 mg/day → large gap

  MAGNESIUM (~500 mg/day):
    Ca2+-ATPase activator: muscle relaxation (including vascular)
    eNOS cofactor: endothelial NO synthesis
    ~45% of US adults below RDA for Mg

  CALCIUM from dairy (1250 mg/day):
    Reduces vascular smooth muscle contraction
    May increase urinary Na+ excretion

  REDUCED SATURATED FAT:
    LDL-C reduction → endothelial function

DASH for weight loss:
  Dietary quality improves but DASH not primarily calorie-restricted
  Combined with caloric restriction: more effective for metabolic syndrome
  OMNI-HEART variant: replaced some carbohydrate with protein or MUFA
    → Protein arm: additional −1.4 mmHg systolic vs DASH
    → MUFA arm: similar + lipid improvement
```

## Blue Zones

```
BLUE ZONES (Dan Buettner, National Geographic, 2000s–):

FIVE REGIONS:
  1. Sardinia, Italy (Nuoro province): male longevity especially
  2. Okinawa, Japan: female longevity; historically high centenarian rate
  3. Loma Linda, California: Seventh-day Adventist community; plant-based
  4. Ikaria, Greece: Mediterranean island; low dementia rates
  5. Nicoya Peninsula, Costa Rica: low midlife mortality

COMMON FACTORS (Buettner's Power 9):
  Natural movement: daily moderate activity, not structured exercise
  Purpose (Ikigai/Plan de vida): sense of meaning
  Down-shifting: stress reduction practices
  80% rule (Hara hachi bu — Okinawa): stop eating at 80% full
  Plants (mostly plant-based): legumes central; minimal meat
  Wine (moderate): some zones, not Loma Linda or Adventists
  Belonging: religious/community affiliation (all zones)
  Loved ones first: multigenerational households; family connection
  Right tribe: social networks of healthy behaviors

EPIDEMIOLOGY:
  Sardinian study: centenarian cluster in mountain villages; genetic factors (ACE variant)
    + diet (goat milk, flat bread, legumes, low calorie) + community
  Okinawa centenarian study: significant; traditional Okinawan diet (pre-1960s)
    vs post-1960s Westernized diet → dramatically shorter lives in younger cohorts
  Loma Linda: Adventist Health Study-2 (AHS-2) → large epidemiological study
    Vegetarian Adventists: 7+ years longer life vs non-vegetarian Americans
    BUT: Adventists also don't smoke, drink, controlled lifestyle → powerful confounder

METHODOLOGICAL CONCERNS:
  Survivorship bias: those who died are not present in current populations
  Healthy cohort effect: rural isolation → preserved traditional practices among survivors
  Demographic data quality: historical records in Sardinia, Costa Rica unreliable
    → Some "longevity" may be age exaggeration (Saul Justin Newman, 2019)
    → Newman: strong correlation between poor birth record quality and Blue Zone status
    → "Blue Zones" may be statistical artifacts of bad 19th-century record keeping
  Confounder pile-up: caloric restriction + activity + social connection + genetics
    + smoking (rare) + alcohol (moderate or none) + stress → impossible to isolate diet
  → Blue Zones are suggestive but cannot isolate dietary causation
  → Best use: qualitative inspiration for lifestyle elements known to promote health
```

## Vegan and Vegetarian Diets

```
TYPES:
  Vegan: no animal products at all
  Lacto-ovo vegetarian: includes dairy and eggs
  Pescatarian: includes fish (not red meat/poultry)
  Flexitarian: mostly plant-based with occasional animal products

HEALTH EVIDENCE:
  EPIC-Oxford, AHS-2, other large cohorts:
    Vegetarians vs meat eaters: 20–30% lower CVD risk; lower T2DM; lower some cancers
    Vegans vs vegetarians: further reduction but also more nutrient gaps
    Confounders: vegetarians typically: more educated, non-smoking, more active, health-conscious
    → "Healthy user bias" is significant; hard to isolate diet from lifestyle

KEY NUTRIENT RISKS IN VEGAN DIET:

  VITAMIN B12:
    Exclusively from animal products (bacteria produce it; concentrated in animal tissue)
    Plants: no reliable dietary B12 (algae contain B12 analogs that may block actual B12)
    Risk: B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anemia + peripheral neuropathy + cognitive decline
    → ALL vegans MUST supplement B12 or consume B12-fortified foods
    → 1000 μg/day cyanocobalamin (every other day) or 100–250 μg/day for vegans
    → Methylcobalamin not proven superior despite marketing

  VITAMIN D:
    Limited in all dietary patterns; synthesis from UV exposure primary source
    D3 (cholecalciferol) — from lanolin (sheep wool); most supplements
    D2 (ergocalciferol) — vegan; slightly less potent at raising 25(OH)D levels
    Vegan D3: available from lichen-derived sources
    Deficiency: common regardless of diet; supplement regardless

  OMEGA-3 (EPA/DHA):
    Marine omega-3 (EPA, DHA): exclusively from fatty fish and some algae
    ALA (flaxseed, chia, walnuts): converts to EPA/DHA, but poorly (~5–15%)
    → Vegans: low EPA and DHA without supplementation
    → Risk: cardiovascular benefit of omega-3 is EPA/DHA, not ALA
    → SUPPLEMENT: algae-derived DHA (where fish get it anyway); 200–300 mg DHA/day

  IRON:
    Heme iron (animal): 20–30% absorption; not inhibited by phytates
    Non-heme iron (plant): 1–10% absorption; inhibited by phytates, polyphenols
    → Vegans need ~1.8× more dietary iron than omnivores to absorb same amount
    → Consume: lentils, tofu, tempeh, fortified cereals; with vitamin C (enhances non-heme)
    → Risk: iron deficiency anemia (especially menstruating women)

  ZINC:
    Phytates in plant foods reduce zinc absorption ~50%
    Plant sources: legumes, nuts, whole grains
    → Vegans: may need ~50% more dietary zinc; soaking/fermenting legumes reduces phytate

  CALCIUM:
    Dairy is the most bioavailable source; plant calcium is partially absorbed
    Good plant sources: tofu (calcium-set), fortified plant milks, bok choy, kale, almonds
    Oxalate-high foods (spinach, beet greens): low calcium absorption despite high content

IODINE:
  Seaweed (highly variable content; can also be excessive), iodized salt
  Dairy (from iodine-based teat disinfectants and cattle feed): significant in UK/EU
  Many plant milks: not iodine-fortified
  → Iodine often overlooked in vegan supplementation guidance
```

## Time-Restricted Eating

```
TIME-RESTRICTED EATING (TRE) / INTERMITTENT FASTING:

  TYPES:
    TRE: all eating within defined daily window (8h, 10h, 12h)
    16:8: 16h fast, 8h eating window (most studied)
    5:2: 2 days/week ~500 kcal; 5 days ad libitum
    ADF (alternate day fasting): alternating low-cal and ad-libitum days
    OMAD (one meal a day): extreme; 23h fast; insufficient evidence

CIRCADIAN ALIGNMENT:
  Human metabolism is time-dependent (circadian rhythm):
    Insulin sensitivity: higher in morning; lower in evening
    Glucose tolerance: best ~1h after waking; worst ~8–10h later
    Core body temperature: peaks afternoon; lowest early morning
    Digestive enzyme secretion: follows circadian rhythm

  EARLY TRE (eTRE): eating window in morning/midday vs. evening
    Sutton 2018: eTRE (8h, 6am–2pm) in pre-diabetic men → −38% insulin levels;
                 −11 mmHg systolic; −3.6% glucose → without caloric restriction
    RATIONALE: eating aligned with circadian insulin sensitivity

  LATE TRE (eating in the evening): less beneficial or negative
    Body's circadian rhythm: processes food better in daylight hours
    Evening eating → elevated post-prandial glucose/insulin → worse metabolic outcomes
    Cross-sectional: night-shift workers → higher metabolic disease rate (circadian disruption)

EVIDENCE:
  Weight loss: multiple RCTs → TRE produces ~0.5–1.5 kg more weight loss than control
               Mechanism: reduces eating window → usually reduces total caloric intake
               When calories equated: weight loss difference disappears (Lowe 2020, NEJM Evid.)
  Metabolic: independent of weight loss effects less clear; eTRE may improve insulin sensitivity
  Lean mass: concern that prolonged fasting → muscle catabolism
             TRE + adequate protein + resistance training → lean mass preserved (current evidence)
  CALORIC RESTRICTION IS THE MECHANISM FOR MOST OUTCOMES:
    Most TRE weight loss is explained by eating less
    TRE is a time-based strategy that for many people → natural caloric restriction
    Not a metabolic miracle independent of energy balance
```

## Ultra-Processed Food (UPF)

```
NOVA CLASSIFICATION (Monteiro, JAMA 2019):

  NOVA 1: Unprocessed or minimally processed foods
           Fresh fruits, vegetables, meat, fish, eggs, plain milk, dried beans, plain grains
           Minimal processing: cleaning, cutting, freezing, pasteurizing, drying

  NOVA 2: Processed culinary ingredients
           Pressed oils, butter, sugar, salt, flour — extracted from NOVA 1 foods
           Used in cooking; not eaten alone

  NOVA 3: Processed foods
           Canned vegetables with salt, cheese, cured meats, freshly baked bread
           Simple combination of NOVA 1 + 2

  NOVA 4: Ultra-processed foods (UPF):
           Industrial formulations with ingredients not found in home kitchens
           Contains 5+ ingredient classes from:
             → Hydrolyzed proteins, modified starches, hydrogenated fats
             → High-fructose corn syrup, inverted sugars
             → Flavoring agents (natural and artificial), flavor enhancers
             → Emulsifiers (lecithin, carrageenan, polysorbate 80)
             → Colorants (caramel, tartrazine)
             → Thickeners, humectants, anti-foaming agents
           Examples: mass-produced bread, breakfast cereals, nuggets, chips,
                     packaged snacks, reconstituted meat products, flavored yogurts, sodas

EVIDENCE:

  COHORT ASSOCIATIONS:
    Large cohorts (NutriNet-Santé, UK Biobank, SUN Study):
    UPF intake: higher total mortality, CVD, cancer, T2DM, depression, IBD, cognitive decline
    Dose-response: each 10% increase in UPF intake → 14% higher total mortality
    NutriNet 2019: UPF → +12% cancer risk; particularly breast cancer
    Confounders: UPF intake correlates with poverty, time pressure, education → hard to separate

  HALL 2019 RCT (NEJM):
    Study design: 20 subjects; ultra-processed vs unprocessed diet; 2 weeks each, crossover
    Controlled: matched for energy, sugar, fat, fiber, macronutrients presented
    RESULT: ad libitum intake → UPF diet: subjects ate +508 kcal/day MORE
             → Weight gain +0.9 kg (UPF arm) vs −0.9 kg (unprocessed arm)
    Mechanism for overconsumption unclear:
      Texture: UPF softer → faster eating → less time for satiety signals
      Palatability: engineered taste → reduced satiation
      Fiber removal → faster gastric emptying → faster refeeding
      Disrupted gut microbiome → altered satiety hormones?

  MECHANISMS PROPOSED:
    Hyperpalatability engineering: fat + sugar + salt combinations optimize hedonic response
    Texture modification: soft → chew less → eat faster → exceed satiety threshold
    Nutrient displacement: UPF crowds out nutrient-dense food (not enough calories elsewhere)
    Emulsifiers: disrupt mucus layer → dysbiosis (animal evidence; human unclear)
    Additives: some artificial colors → behavioral effects (children); others disputed

LIMITATIONS OF NOVA:
  NOVA 4 is heterogeneous: diet soda = NOVA 4; whole-grain bread with lecithin = NOVA 4
  → Not all NOVA 4 foods are equally harmful
  Association not causation: UPF consumption correlates with poverty, stress, food environment
  → Structural factors may be the real drivers of poor health outcomes
  Reformulation challenge: manufacturers add fiber/reduce sugar → does it become NOVA 3?
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Diet question | Evidence-based answer |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Best RCT evidence for CVD prevention | Mediterranean + EVOO or nuts (PREDIMED) |
| Best evidence for blood pressure | DASH diet; specifically K+, Mg++ mechanism |
| Are Blue Zones proven diet evidence? | No — observational; severe confounders; possible data quality issues |
| B12 for vegans: supplement or food? | Must supplement — no reliable plant source |
| Best omega-3 for vegans | Algae-derived DHA supplement (200–300 mg/day) |
| TRE and weight loss: is it the timing? | Mostly caloric reduction; eTRE may have circadian benefits |
| What unifies all healthy dietary patterns? | Vegetables/legumes/whole grains, low UPF, adequate fiber, healthy fats |
| UPF and overeating: proven mechanism? | Hall RCT shows effect; mechanism unclear (texture, palatability, GI speed) |

## Common Confusion Points

**The Mediterranean diet is a pattern, not a food**
"Olive oil is healthy" is a much weaker claim than "the Mediterranean dietary pattern reduces cardiovascular events." The PREDIMED effect is from the whole pattern plus olive oil or nuts — not from either component in isolation. Studies on individual foods extracted from dietary patterns often fail to replicate the pattern-level effects.

**DASH lowers blood pressure regardless of weight loss**
DASH studies were designed to test dietary composition independent of weight change. The blood pressure effect of DASH is observed in studies where caloric intake was maintained constant. It's not just "eating less processed food and losing weight" — the specific micronutrient composition (K+, Mg++, Ca++) has mechanistic effects on vascular physiology independent of weight.

**Blue Zones may not exist in the way popularized**
Saul Newman's 2019 paper found that Blue Zone regions correlate strongly with poor birth record quality — raising the possibility that "supercentenarians" are people who outlived their birth records. The Okinawan longevity advantage was real historically but has largely disappeared in younger cohorts who adopted Western diets. The popular framing should be treated skeptically; the underlying health behaviors (plant-based, low calorie, community, activity) may be valid without requiring extraordinary longevity claims.

**"Vegan" is not automatically healthy**
A diet of white bread, chips, Oreos, and soda is technically vegan. The health benefits associated with vegetarian/vegan diets in cohort studies are heavily confounded by the overall health consciousness of those who choose plant-based eating. The benefits observed likely reflect whole-food plant-based eating, not merely animal-product exclusion.

**Time-restricted eating is not a bypass of caloric balance**
When calories are equated in controlled trials, metabolic differences between TRE and regular eating patterns are modest. The real-world benefit of TRE is that restricting the eating window usually causes people to eat less. This is a valid strategy for some people, but it should not be marketed as a metabolically distinct pathway to fat loss independent of energy balance.
