# Fats

## The Big Picture

Dietary fat is the most energy-dense macronutrient (9 kcal/g), the primary fuel at rest, and the source of essential fatty acids and fat-soluble vitamins. It's also the most politically charged topic in nutrition: the 1977 fat-reduction hypothesis, 40 years of low-fat dietary guidelines, and subsequent evidence that upended the simple narrative.

```
FAT SYSTEM — LANDSCAPE VIEW
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FATTY ACID TYPES:
  SFA (saturated)       MUFA (monounsaturated)      PUFA (polyunsaturated)
  Palmitic C16:0        Oleic C18:1 ω-9             ω-6: LA, AA
  Stearic C18:0         (can synthesize via Δ9-des)  ω-3: ALA, EPA, DHA
  Myristic C14:0                                     Trans FA: industrial (harmful)
         │                      │                           │
         └──────────────────────┼───────────────────────────┘
                                ↓ DIGESTION / ABSORPTION
  Dietary fat → bile emulsification → pancreatic lipase → monoglycerides + FFA
  Enterocytes → re-esterify → TG → package with ApoB-48 → CHYLOMICRONS → lymph → blood

                                ↓ LIPOPROTEIN TRANSPORT
  Chylomicron TG → hydrolyzed by LPL at tissues → chylomicron remnants → liver
  Liver → VLDL (ApoB-100) → LPL hydrolysis → IDL → LDL (cholesterol-enriched)
  LDL → cells via LDL receptor; excess → oxidized LDL → macrophage foam cells

  HDL: reverse cholesterol transport (load: peripheral → liver)
  ApoB: 1 per atherogenic particle → ApoB = particle count = best atherogenic marker

                                ↓ ATHEROGENESIS RISK
  LDL-C: total cholesterol in LDL particles (can mislead: particle size matters)
  LDL-P / ApoB: particle count (better predictor; captures sdLDL burden)
  Small dense LDL (sdLDL): more oxidizable; longer circulation; penetrates arterial wall
  sdLDL driver: high-carb / insulin resistance → shift to small particles

                                ↓ CLINICAL MARKERS + DECISIONS
  ApoB > LDL-C: captures discordant high-risk patients (T2DM, metabolic syndrome)
  TG: driven by VLDL; reduced by omega-3 (4g/day); elevated by fructose/refined carbs
  Replace SFA with PUFA or MUFA (not refined carbs) → CVD benefit
  Trans FA (industrial): raise LDL-C + lower HDL-C → worst of both worlds; now banned
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

```
FATTY ACID STRUCTURE:

  CH₃─CH₂─CH₂─...─CH₂─COOH
  │   │         │   │   │
  ω   ω-1       ω-3  2   1  ← carbon numbering from carboxyl (IUPAC)
  end                       ← omega (ω) numbers from methyl end

SATURATED (no double bonds):
  CH₃─(CH₂)ₙ─COOH
  Solid at room temperature (high melting point from dense packing)

MONOUNSATURATED (1 double bond):
  CH₃─...─CH=CH─...─COOH
  Liquid at room temperature; cis configuration bends chain → lower Tm

POLYUNSATURATED (≥2 double bonds):
  Multiple cis double bonds → very low melting point; liquid even at low temperature
  More susceptible to oxidation (more double bonds = more oxidizable)
```

## Saturated Fatty Acids (SFA)

```
SATURATED FATTY ACIDS — CHAIN LENGTH MATTERS:

Short-chain SFA (SCFA, C4–C6):
  Butyrate (C4): colonocyte fuel; produced by gut bacteria from fiber
  From: butter, some dairy
  Metabolism: directly absorbed → portal vein (not packaged in chylomicrons)

Medium-chain SFA (MCFA, C8–C12):
  Caprylic (C8), capric (C10), lauric (C12)
  From: coconut oil (~65%), palm kernel oil, MCT oil
  Metabolism: rapidly absorbed; not re-packaged as chylomicrons
              → portal vein → liver → preferentially oxidized for energy
              → less tendency to be stored; faster availability
              → MCT oil used therapeutically (ketogenic diet, malabsorption)
  Lauric acid (C12): raises both LDL and HDL; antimicrobial properties;
    metabolized partly like MCFA; often classified with long-chain

Long-chain SFA (LCFA, C14–C22):
  Myristic (C14): from palm oil, dairy; strongly raises LDL-C
  Palmitic (C16): most abundant SFA in nature; from palm oil, meat, dairy
    Raises LDL-C; increases LDL particle number
  Stearic (C18): from animal fat, cocoa butter
    Does NOT raise LDL-C — rapidly desaturated to oleic acid in liver
    → Exception to "SFA = atherogenic" generalization
    Cocoa butter (mostly stearic + oleic) considered neutral for lipids

CARDIOVASCULAR EPIDEMIOLOGY:
  The simple claim "SFA → LDL → heart disease" was the basis for guidelines
  Meta-analyses of SFA replacement:
    Replace SFA with refined carbohydrates → no benefit or worse
    Replace SFA with PUFA (especially omega-6) → reduced CHD events
    Replace SFA with MUFA → neutral to mildly beneficial
  → The problem is what you replace SFA with, not just removing SFA
```

## Monounsaturated Fatty Acids (MUFA)

```
OLEIC ACID (C18:1 ω-9): dominant MUFA in diet

Sources: olive oil (~73%), avocado (~71%), high-oleic sunflower/canola, almonds, cashews
         Animal fat: beef tallow (~43%), lard (~47%), duck fat (~50%)

METABOLISM:
  MUFA are not essential (can synthesize from saturated FA via Δ9-desaturase)
  Incorporated into cell membranes and triglycerides
  Good oxidative stability (one double bond → less oxidation than PUFA)

CARDIOVASCULAR EFFECT:
  Replacing SFA with MUFA: modestly reduces LDL-C
  Replacing carbohydrates with MUFA: reduces TG, raises HDL, little LDL change
  Mediterranean diet (high in olive oil = high MUFA): consistent cardiovascular benefit
    PREDIMED trial (2013, n=7,447): Mediterranean diet + olive oil or nuts
    → 30% relative risk reduction in MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events)
    vs low-fat diet control
    → Only large RCT with hard cardiovascular endpoints in whole-diet context

OLEIC ACID AND CELL MEMBRANES:
  High membrane MUFA → intermediate fluidity
  MUFA in LDL → oxidation-resistant → possibly less atherogenic than PUFA-rich LDL
  Olive oil polyphenols (oleocanthal, hydroxytyrosol) — anti-inflammatory
  → Some Mediterranean benefit may be polyphenol-mediated, not only MUFA
```

## Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids (PUFA)

### Omega-6 Family

```
OMEGA-6 PUFA:

Linoleic acid (LA, C18:2 ω-6):
  ESSENTIAL: humans lack Δ12 desaturase to make it
  Sources: vegetable oils (soybean 51%, corn 55%, safflower 73%)
           nuts, seeds
  Most abundant PUFA in modern Western diet (~6–8% of calories)

LA → AA (arachidonic acid, C20:4 ω-6):
  Elongation + desaturation: LA →(Δ6-des)→ GLA(C18:3) →(elong)→ DGLA(C20:3) →(Δ5-des)→ AA

ARACHIDONIC ACID (AA):
  Major structural component of cell membranes (especially neural)
  Precursor to eicosanoids (pro-inflammatory pathway):
    COX pathway: AA → PGE₂ (prostaglandins), TXA₂ (thromboxanes)
    LOX pathway: AA → LTB₄ (leukotrienes)
    → Inflammatory mediators
  → NSAIDS (aspirin, ibuprofen) inhibit COX → reduce AA-derived eicosanoids
  → AA-derived eicosanoids are not unconditionally bad — they mediate
    normal immune responses; the problem is excess/imbalance

Modern omega-6 intake:
  Pre-agricultural estimate: ~2–4% of calories
  Current Western: 6–8% of calories
  Mostly increased from industrial vegetable oils replacing animal fats
```

### Omega-3 Family

```
OMEGA-3 PUFA:

Alpha-linolenic acid (ALA, C18:3 ω-3):
  ESSENTIAL: humans lack Δ15 desaturase
  Sources: flaxseed (~53%), chia (~60%), hemp (~18%), walnuts (~9%)
            canola oil (~11%), soybean oil (~7%)
  Conversion to EPA/DHA: very inefficient in humans
    ALA → EPA: ~5–10% conversion
    ALA → DHA: ~2–5% conversion (even lower)
    → Plant-based omega-3 is a poor substitute for fish oil

Eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, C20:5 ω-3):
  Anti-inflammatory eicosanoid production:
    COX pathway: EPA → PGE₃ (weak), TXA₃ (weak)
    LOX pathway: EPA → LTB₅ (weak, anti-inflammatory)
  → Competes with AA for eicosanoid pathway → reduces AA-derived inflammation
  Sources: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, herring, sardines), fish oil, algae oil

Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, C22:6 ω-3):
  Critical for brain and retinal structure: ~40% of neuronal membrane PUFA
  Particularly critical during fetal development and infancy
  Sources: same as EPA
  RBC DHA membrane content: used as biomarker of long-chain ω-3 status

OMEGA-3 CARDIOVASCULAR EVIDENCE:
  High-dose EPA (Vascepa, 4g/day) + statin: REDUCE-IT trial (2018)
    25% relative risk reduction in MACE in high-risk patients
    Mechanism: possibly EPA's direct anti-inflammatory + anti-aggregatory effects
                possibly displacement of AA from membranes
  Standard fish oil supplements: ASCEND, VITAL trials — neutral for CVD events
    → 1–2g/day fish oil supplements appear insufficient for primary prevention
    → High-dose pharmaceutical-grade EPA may be different
  Triglyceride reduction: omega-3 potently reduce TG (~20–50% at 4g/day)
    PCSK9 + statin → best LDL reduction; omega-3 best for TG reduction
```

### Omega-3:6 Ratio

```
OMEGA-3:OMEGA-6 RATIO DEBATE:

Pre-agricultural estimate: ~1:1 to 1:4 ratio
Modern Western diet: ~1:15 to 1:20 (heavily omega-6 dominant)

Proposed problem:
  High omega-6 → high AA → pro-inflammatory eicosanoid dominance
  Low omega-3 → insufficient EPA/DHA to compete

CRITIQUE:
  The ratio is a heuristic, not a biochemical mechanism
  Absolute EPA/DHA intake matters more than ratio
  High LA intake does NOT strongly increase AA if calories are at maintenance
    (LA → AA conversion is limited by downstream enzyme capacity)
  More important: are you eating enough EPA/DHA from fish? (most people: no)
  → Focus on absolute EPA/DHA intake (≥500mg/day from 2 fish servings/week)
    rather than obsessing over exact omega-6 reduction

PRACTICAL:
  Reduce industrial seed oils if very high (soy/corn/safflower-based cooking oils)
  Increase fatty fish 2+/week (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring)
  ALA from walnuts/flax useful but not substitutable for EPA/DHA
```

## Trans Fatty Acids

```
CIS vs TRANS:
  Natural double bonds in unsaturated FA are CIS (H atoms on same side → kinked chain)
  TRANS double bonds: H atoms on opposite sides → straight chain (like saturated)
  → Different physical and metabolic properties

INDUSTRIAL TRANS FATS (ARTIFICIAL):
  Source: partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils (invented ~1911)
  Why created: convert liquid vegetable oils → solid fats (shortening, margarine)
               increase shelf life; cheaper than butter for commercial baking

  Major trans FA: elaidic acid (trans-octadecenoic, C18:1t)

  METABOLIC EFFECTS (worst of both worlds):
    ↑ LDL-C (like SFA)
    ↓ HDL-C (unlike SFA, which raises HDL)
    ↑ LDL particle number (small, dense LDL — more atherogenic)
    ↑ Systemic inflammation (CRP, IL-6)
    ↑ Coronary heart disease risk: strongest food–CHD relationship found in epidemiology

  Regulatory action:
    US FDA: mandatory trans fat labeling (2006)
    FDA: determined PHVO (partially hydrogenated vegetable oil) = not GRAS (2015)
    US food supply: effectively eliminated by ~2018
    Global: WHO target to eliminate industrial trans fats by 2023
    Status: still present in some developing world food supply

RUMINANT TRANS FATS (NATURAL):
  Vaccenic acid (C18:1t11): produced by bacteria in cow/sheep rumen
  Conjugated linoleic acid (CLA): also produced ruminally
  Both found in beef fat and dairy at ~2–5% of fat
  Metabolic effects: different from industrial — CLA has potential anti-inflammatory effects
    Vaccenic acid: converted to CLA in human tissues; appears neutral or beneficial
  → "Trans fat" on a food label from cheese or beef ≠ same health concern as industrial
```

## Cholesterol Metabolism and ApoB

### The Cholesterol-Lipoprotein System

```
LIPOPROTEINS: packages for transporting hydrophobic lipids in aqueous blood

                    SIZE (nm)    DENSITY       PRODUCED BY
  Chylomicrons      100–1000     < 0.94        Intestine (dietary fat)
  VLDL              30–80        0.94–1.006     Liver (endogenous TG export)
  IDL               25–35        1.006–1.019    VLDL → LDL conversion
  LDL               18–25        1.019–1.063    IDL processing
  HDL               5–12         1.063–1.210    Liver/intestine (reverse transport)

LIPOPROTEIN JOURNEY:
  Dietary fat → chylomicrons in lymph → circulation
  Chylomicron TG hydrolyzed by lipoprotein lipase (LPL) at tissues
  Chylomicron remnants → liver

  Liver packages TG + cholesterol as VLDL
  VLDL TG hydrolyzed by LPL → IDL
  IDL further hydrolyzed → LDL (cholesterol-enriched)
  LDL → delivers cholesterol to all cells via LDL receptor
  Excess LDL → oxidized → taken up by macrophages → foam cells → atherosclerosis
```

### LDL Particle Size Nuance

```
LDL HETEROGENEITY:
  LDL is not a single particle type:

  Large, buoyant LDL (pattern A):
    Larger particles; cholesterol-rich
    Less atherogenic per particle
    Associated with higher HDL, lower TG

  Small, dense LDL (sdLDL, pattern B):
    Smaller, denser particles; protein-rich relative to cholesterol
    More atherogenic: more easily oxidized; slower clearance; longer circulation time;
                      penetrates arterial wall more easily
    Associated with: metabolic syndrome, high TG, low HDL, insulin resistance
    Diet effect: high-carbohydrate diets → shift toward sdLDL
                 dietary fat (MUFA + SFA) → shift toward large LDL

CLINICAL MEASUREMENT:
  LDL-C (standard): measures total LDL cholesterol, not particle number
  LDL-P (NMR): counts actual LDL particles (more predictive in some analyses)
  sdLDL-C: direct measurement of small, dense fraction
  ApoB: one ApoB molecule per atherogenic particle → best single marker
        (measures VLDL + IDL + LDL + Lp(a) all together)
```

### ApoB — The Best Atherogenic Marker

```
APOB:
  Apolipoprotein B-100: single structural protein on LDL, VLDL, IDL, Lp(a)
  One ApoB per atherogenic particle → ApoB = count of atherogenic particles

  Why ApoB > LDL-C:
  Two patients with same LDL-C of 130 mg/dL:
    Patient A: 1000 large LDL particles (low particle count, low ApoB)
    Patient B: 1500 small dense LDL particles (high particle count, high ApoB)
    Same LDL-C, very different atherogenic risk

  ApoB-lowering: primary target in aggressive lipid management
    Statins: ↓ LDL-C ~30–50% + ↓ LDL-P + ↓ ApoB
    PCSK9 inhibitors: ↓ LDL-C 50–60% + ↓ ApoB dramatically
    Ezetimibe: blocks intestinal cholesterol absorption; small ↓ ApoB
    Omega-3 (4g): ↓ TG dramatically; neutral/slightly ↑ LDL-C (but ↓ VLDL and particle count)

LDL-C vs ApoB discordance (common situations):
  Low LDL-C but high ApoB:
    Metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance
    Many small LDL particles → low cholesterol per particle → LDL-C underestimates risk
    → ApoB correctly captures high risk
  High LDL-C but low ApoB:
    Hypothyroidism; high-fat, very-low-carb diets; familial hypercholesterolemia pattern
    Large LDL particles → high cholesterol per particle → LDL-C overestimates risk
    → ApoB correctly captures lower risk
```

## Essential Fatty Acids

```
ESSENTIAL FATTY ACIDS (EFA): must be consumed; cannot synthesize

1. LINOLEIC ACID (LA, C18:2 ω-6)
   Required: ~4–10g/day for adults
   Deficiency: extremely rare in modern diets (vegetable oils ubiquitous)
   Deficiency signs: dermatitis, hair loss, impaired wound healing
   → Practically never an issue with any mixed diet

2. ALPHA-LINOLENIC ACID (ALA, C18:3 ω-3)
   Required: 1.1g/day (women), 1.6g/day (men) per DRI
   Deficiency: rare but possible on extremely low-fat diet lacking plant omega-3
   Signs: skin lesions, neurological abnormalities
   → ALA itself is easy to meet with flax/chia/walnuts
   → Downstream EPA/DHA from ALA: very limited conversion — separate concern

EFA-derived compounds:
  AA (from LA): essential structural and signaling functions (not just inflammatory)
  EPA + DHA (from ALA or diet): critical for neural + cardiovascular function
  → EPA/DHA deficiency is common despite adequate ALA
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Fat strategy |
|------|-------------|
| Reduce atherogenic risk (high ApoB) | Reduce sdLDL: lower refined carbs; increase MUFA/fish |
| Maximize anti-inflammatory | Increase EPA+DHA (fatty fish 2×/week or high-dose fish oil) |
| Replace saturated fat appropriately | Replace with MUFA (olive oil) or omega-3 PUFA — not refined carbs |
| Reduce TG | High-dose omega-3 (4g/day pharmaceutical); reduce refined carbs, fructose |
| Ketogenic diet fat composition | Emphasize MUFA (olive, avocado) over excessive palmitic acid |
| Heart-healthy single dietary change | PREDIMED: 4 tbsp olive oil/day OR 30g mixed nuts/day |
| Eliminate trans fat | Avoid: partially hydrogenated oils in processed/bakery foods |
| MCT for rapid energy (therapeutic) | MCT oil; also used in ketogenic epilepsy management |

## Common Confusion Points

**Saturated fat is not monolithic — stearic acid is neutral**
Stearic acid (C18:0, abundant in beef tallow and cocoa butter) does not raise LDL-C. It's rapidly desaturated to oleic acid in the liver. The SFA-LDL relationship primarily applies to palmitic (C16:0) and myristic (C14:0). Lumping all SFA together obscures important distinctions.

**"Cholesterol in food" vs "cholesterol in blood" are weakly linked**
Dietary cholesterol (eggs, shellfish) has minimal effect on LDL-C in most people. The liver down-regulates cholesterol synthesis to compensate for dietary intake. ~75% of blood cholesterol is synthesized endogenously. The 300mg dietary cholesterol limit was removed from 2015 US guidelines. Exception: hyperresponders (FH) and some with ApoE4 genotype.

**LDL-C lowering ≠ ApoB lowering in all cases**
Some interventions reduce LDL-C while raising LDL particle number (e.g., very-low-carb diets can increase LDL particle count in some individuals even as LDL-C rises, particularly with large particle shifts). Measuring both LDL-C and ApoB (or LDL-P) captures more of the risk picture.

**Vegetable oils are not uniformly health-promoting**
Industrial seed oils (soybean, corn, safflower) are very high in omega-6 linoleic acid, are highly refined, and produce oxidized lipid byproducts when heated. This doesn't make them equal to trans fats, but olive oil, avocado oil, and unrefined coconut oil have different properties and stability profiles. The type of vegetable oil matters.

**HDL-C is not "protective cholesterol" in a simple sense**
High HDL-C is associated with lower CHD risk in observational studies, but raising HDL-C pharmacologically (CETP inhibitors) has not reduced cardiovascular events in trials. HDL function (reverse cholesterol transport capacity) matters more than particle count. HDL-C as a target for therapy has not been validated.
