# Minerals

## The Big Picture

Minerals are **inorganic elements essential for physiological function** — structural (bone, teeth), regulatory (enzyme cofactors, signaling), and transport (oxygen, electrons). Unlike vitamins, minerals cannot be synthesized or chemically converted; they must be absorbed from food in their elemental or ionic form. Bioavailability — the fraction actually absorbed — is where most mineral nutrition complexity lives.

```
MINERAL CLASSIFICATION:

MACROMINERALS (>100 mg/day required):
  Calcium (Ca)     — bone, muscle contraction, signaling
  Phosphorus (P)   — ATP, nucleic acids, bone mineral
  Magnesium (Mg)   — 300+ enzyme cofactor; ATP activation; electrical
  Sodium (Na)      — primary extracellular cation; osmolarity; nerve
  Potassium (K)    — primary intracellular cation; nerve, muscle
  Chloride (Cl)    — HCl (stomach acid), extracellular anion
  Sulfur (S)       — amino acids (Met, Cys), glutathione

TRACE MINERALS (<100 mg/day required):
  Iron (Fe)        — oxygen transport (heme), electron transport (OXPHOS)
  Zinc (Zn)        — 300+ enzymes; immune; wound healing; protein synthesis
  Copper (Cu)      — SOD, ceruloplasmin, ferroxidase, connective tissue
  Selenium (Se)    — glutathione peroxidase, thyroid deiodinase
  Iodine (I)       — thyroid hormones (T3, T4)
  Manganese (Mn)   — SOD (Mn-SOD), gluconeogenesis enzymes
  Chromium (Cr)    — insulin signal potentiation (disputed)
  Molybdenum (Mo)  — xanthine oxidase, sulfite oxidase
  Fluoride (F)     — tooth enamel hardening
```

## Macrominerals

### Calcium

```
BODY DISTRIBUTION:
  99% in bone and teeth (as hydroxyapatite, Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂)
  1% in blood and cells — tightly regulated [Ca²⁺] = 1.1–1.3 mmol/L serum
  Intracellular: 0.1 µmol/L → 1,000× gradient between extra- and intracellular

REGULATION (calcium homeostasis):
  Parathyroid hormone (PTH):
    Responds to falling serum Ca²⁺
    → Kidney: increases Ca reabsorption; activates 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D
    → Bone: osteoclast activation → bone resorption → Ca release
    → Net: raises serum Ca²⁺
  Calcitonin (thyroid):
    Responds to rising serum Ca²⁺
    → Inhibits osteoclasts; increases renal Ca excretion
    → Net: lowers serum Ca²⁺ (minor role in adults)
  Vitamin D (1,25(OH)₂D):
    → Intestine: upregulates TRPV6 + calbindin → Ca absorption
    → 60% absorbed with adequate D vs 10–15% without

FUNCTIONS:
  Structural: bone and tooth mineral
  Muscle contraction: Ca²⁺ binds troponin → actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling
  Neurotransmitter release: Ca²⁺ influx → vesicle fusion → NT exocytosis
  Cell signaling: second messenger; calmodulin activation
  Coagulation: multiple steps in clotting cascade require Ca²⁺

BONE REMODELING:
  Osteoblasts: deposit new bone matrix (osteoid → mineralize with HA)
  Osteoclasts: resorb bone, dissolve HA with H⁺
  Continuous remodeling: entire skeleton replaced every ~10 years
  Peak bone mass: 25–30 years; declining after (especially in women post-menopause)
  Osteoporosis: bone mass below fracture-risk threshold
    Estimated density: DXA scan, T-score (vs young adult reference)
    Prevention: adequate Ca + D + weight-bearing exercise in youth/middle age

DIETARY SOURCES AND ABSORPTION:
  Best absorbed: dairy (30–32% absorption), fortified foods
  Plant sources: variable — some high Ca plants have poor absorption:
    Spinach: 480mg Ca/100g but only 5% absorbed (oxalate chelation)
    Kale: 135mg Ca/100g, 40–50% absorbed (low oxalate)
  DRI: 1000 mg/day (19–50 yr); 1200 mg/day (51+ women; 71+ men)

TOXICITY (hypercalcemia):
  Usually from: hyperparathyroidism, malignancy, vitamin D toxicity
  Signs: nausea, weakness, polyuria, kidney stones, calcification
  UL from supplements: 2,000–2,500 mg/day
```

### Phosphorus

```
FUNCTIONS:
  Bone mineral: hydroxyapatite (paired with Ca in ~1:1.7 molar ratio Ca:P)
  Energy: phosphate backbone of ATP, ADP, AMP; creatine phosphate
  Nucleic acids: DNA and RNA backbone (phosphodiester bonds)
  Cell membranes: phospholipid head groups
  Signaling: protein phosphorylation; cAMP, cGMP second messengers
  Acid-base buffer: H₂PO₄⁻/HPO₄²⁻ system in plasma

DEFICIENCY:
  Rare in normal diet — phosphorus in virtually all foods
  Risk: extreme malnutrition; parenteral nutrition without P
  Refeeding syndrome: giving carbohydrates to severely malnourished →
    massive cellular uptake of P → severe hypophosphatemia → cardiac arrest

EXCESS:
  CKD patients: kidneys can't excrete P → hyperphosphatemia
  Elevated PTH response → bone resorption → vascular calcification
  Dietary restriction of P in CKD: avoid processed foods (high phosphate additives)

High phosphate additives in ultra-processed foods:
  Inorganic phosphate salts (more bioavailable than organic phosphate in meat)
  → processed cheese, deli meats, colas, bakery products
  → CKD patients must read labels
```

### Magnesium

```
FUNCTIONS:
  Enzyme cofactor: >300 enzymes require Mg²⁺
  ATP activation: MgATP²⁻ is the active form of ATP in most enzymatic reactions
    → glycolysis, oxidative phosphorylation, DNA replication/repair, protein synthesis
  Membrane potential: modulates Na/K-ATPase; affects NMDA receptor (Mg block)
  Bone: ~60% of body Mg in bone (structural role; buffer for serum Mg)
  Parathyroid: Mg required for PTH secretion → Mg deficiency → functional hypoparathyroidism

DEFICIENCY:
  Subclinical deficiency common: ~45% of US population below EAR
  Causes: low dietary intake (processed food diet low in Mg); gut disease;
          alcoholism; type 2 diabetes (glycosuria loses Mg); diuretics
  Symptoms: muscle cramps, tremor, cardiac arrhythmias, anxiety, constipation
            hypokalemia refractory to K supplementation (Mg needed for K retention)

DIETARY SOURCES: nuts, seeds, leafy greens, legumes, whole grains, dark chocolate
  Mg refined out of white flour; highly processed foods very low in Mg

CARDIOVASCULAR:
  Epidemiological: low serum Mg → higher risk of AF, hypertension, sudden death
  IV Mg: standard treatment for torsades de pointes, pre-eclampsia eclampsia prevention
  Oral supplementation: limited evidence for CVD prevention but commonly used

FORMS OF SUPPLEMENTS:
  Mg oxide: highest Mg content but poor absorption (~4%)
  Mg citrate: well absorbed; laxative at high doses
  Mg glycinate/bisglycinate: well absorbed; minimal GI effects
  Mg L-threonate: claimed better CNS penetration; some cognitive research interest

RDA: 310–420 mg/day; UL (from supplements only): 350 mg/day
```

### Electrolytes: Sodium, Potassium, Chloride

```
ELECTROLYTE PHYSIOLOGY:

SODIUM (Na⁺): primary extracellular cation
  ECF [Na⁺]: 135–145 mEq/L; maintained by kidneys
  Regulation: ADH (antidiuretic hormone) regulates water to maintain [Na⁺]
              Aldosterone regulates Na reabsorption directly
  Functions: osmolarity; action potentials; Na-coupled transporters (SGLT1, etc.)
  Deficiency (hyponatremia): headache → confusion → seizure → death
  Excess (hypernatremia): thirst, concentrated urine; severe: coma
  Dietary excess: blood pressure (Na-sensitive hypertension ~50% of hypertensives)
  Intake: average US ~3,400 mg/day; target <2,300 mg/day (AHA <1,500 for high risk)
  Sources: processed food (~70% of US intake), restaurant, table salt

POTASSIUM (K⁺): primary intracellular cation
  ICF [K⁺]: 140–150 mEq/L vs ECF ~4–5 mEq/L
  Na/K-ATPase: maintains gradient; requires 40% of resting energy in neurons
  Resting membrane potential: determined largely by K⁺ gradient
  Functions: action potentials; muscle contraction; aldosterone regulation
  Hypokalemia: muscle weakness, paralysis, cardiac arrhythmia
  Hyperkalemia: life-threatening; slowed cardiac conduction → VF → death
               common in CKD, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors
  Dietary: DRI 3,400–4,700 mg/day; typical US intake ~2,500 mg/day
  Sources: fruits (bananas, avocado, potatoes, tomatoes), legumes, dairy
  K:Na ratio inversely associated with blood pressure and stroke risk
```

## Trace Minerals

### Iron

```
BODY IRON DISTRIBUTION:
  Total: ~3–5g in adult
  Hemoglobin: ~65% (functional, in RBCs)
  Myoglobin: ~10% (muscle O₂ storage)
  Storage (ferritin, hemosiderin): ~20–25% (liver, spleen, bone marrow)
  Transferrin-bound (transport): ~0.1% (small but critical pool)

IRON ABSORPTION:
  Duodenum is primary absorption site
  HEME IRON (from myoglobin + hemoglobin in meat):
    Absorbed intact through HCP1 transporter
    Bioavailability: 15–35% — relatively high; not affected by inhibitors
    Sources: red meat, poultry, fish
  NON-HEME IRON (Fe³⁺ from plant/dairy):
    Must be reduced to Fe²⁺ by duodenal cytochrome B (DcytB)
    Absorbed via DMT1 transporter
    Bioavailability: 2–20% — highly variable
    ENHANCERS: vitamin C (reduces Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺), heme iron (promotes non-heme absorption)
    INHIBITORS:
      Phytate (phytic acid): in whole grains, legumes → chelates iron
      Polyphenols: tea, coffee (tannic acid), red wine
      Calcium: competes with Fe for absorption at high doses
      Oxalate: in spinach, beets

IRON REGULATION:
  Hepcidin: liver-produced hormone; master regulator of iron homeostasis
    Iron replete → high hepcidin → degrades ferroportin → reduces absorption + export
    Iron deficient → low hepcidin → increases absorption
    Inflammation → high hepcidin → "anemia of chronic disease"
    (iron locked up even if stores present → iron unavailable for RBC production)

DEFICIENCY (most common nutritional deficiency worldwide):
  3 stages:
    1. Iron depletion: reduced serum ferritin (store depletion; no symptoms)
    2. Iron-deficient erythropoiesis: low transferrin saturation; microcytic RBCs
    3. Iron-deficiency anemia: low Hb; pallor, fatigue, exercise intolerance
  Global: 2 billion iron-deficient; ~800 million iron-deficiency anemia
  High risk: children, premenopausal women, vegetarians, frequent blood donors,
             gastrointestinal disease (celiac, IBD)
  Diagnosis: CBC (low Hb, MCV, MCHC) + ferritin (low in depletion, but elevated in inflammation)
             serum iron + TIBC + transferrin saturation

TOXICITY:
  Hereditary hemochromatosis: HFE gene mutation → low hepcidin → iron overload
    → liver cirrhosis, cardiomyopathy, diabetes, bronze skin
    Treatment: regular phlebotomy (blood removal)
  Acute iron poisoning: children → accidental ingestion
  Oxidative stress: free Fe²⁺ participates in Fenton reaction → OH• → tissue damage
  Ferroptosis: iron-dependent regulated cell death (GPx4 deficiency → lipid peroxidation)
```

### Zinc

```
BODY ZINC: ~2–3g total; no dedicated storage pool → daily intake critical

FUNCTIONS:
  Structural: finger domains (zinc finger proteins → transcription factors, nuclear receptors)
             zinc-containing enzymes: >300 enzymes use Zn catalytically
  Immune: T-cell maturation, lymphocyte proliferation, NK cell activity
          Zn deficiency → lymphopenia → increased infection susceptibility
  Wound healing: required for collagen synthesis, cell proliferation
  Taste and smell: Zn in gustin (taste buds); deficiency → dysgeusia (altered taste)
  Reproduction: required for testosterone synthesis; sperm motility

ABSORPTION:
  20–40% from mixed diet; ZIP4 transporter
  Metallothionein: cytoplasmic Zn-binding protein; sequesters excess Zn
  INHIBITORS: phytate (major; molar phytate:Zn ratio determines absorption)
              calcium at high doses
  ENHANCERS: meat, fish (phytate absent; also contain Zn-binding peptides after digestion)
  Bioavailability: oysters 30–60%; beef 20–30%; legumes 10–15%; cereals 10–20%

DEFICIENCY:
  Subclinical widespread globally; overt deficiency in developing world
  Signs: growth retardation in children; immune dysfunction; delayed wound healing;
         acrodermatitis (skin rash); alopecia; hypogonadism; night blindness
  Acrodermatitis enteropathica: genetic Zn malabsorption (ZIP4 mutation)
  DRI: 8–11 mg/day; UL: 40 mg/day (high Zn → copper displacement)

SUPPLEMENTATION:
  Zinc lozenges (zinc acetate/gluconate): reduce cold duration ~1 day if started early
  Mechanism: Zn ions may block rhinovirus attachment to nasal epithelium
  Must start within 24h of symptom onset; throat/mouth zinc concentration needed
```

### Selenium

```
FUNCTIONS:
  Selenoprotein P: selenium transport protein in plasma
  Glutathione peroxidases (GPx1, GPx4): reduce H₂O₂ + lipid peroxides
    → antioxidant defense; GPx4 prevents ferroptosis
  Thioredoxin reductase (TrxR): reduces oxidized thioredoxin → broad antioxidant
  Iodothyronine deiodinases: convert T4 → T3 (active thyroid hormone)
    → Se deficiency → reduced T3 conversion → hypothyroid symptoms even with adequate iodine
    → Kashin-Beck disease (joint disease) in combined I + Se deficiency (China)

SELENOPROTEINS: 25 human selenoproteins; contain selenocysteine (Sec, 21st amino acid)
  Encoded by UGA codon (normally stop codon) with SECIS element in 3'UTR
  → Most complex protein synthesis: requires specialized Sec-tRNA

STATUS ASSESSMENT:
  Serum selenoprotein P: best functional marker; reflects tissue status
  Plasma selenium: reflects recent intake

GEOGRAPHIC VARIATION:
  Soil selenium content varies dramatically → dietary Se depends on soil
  Low-Se regions: Finland (historically), China (Keshan disease area), NZ, parts of UK
  High-Se regions: Great Plains (US), Argentina
  Keshan disease: endemic cardiomyopathy in China → selenium supplementation eliminates it

DEFICIENCY:
  Keshan disease: dilated cardiomyopathy (children in low-Se China)
  Kashin-Beck disease: osteoarthropathy (combined I + Se deficiency)
  Impaired immune function

TOXICITY (selenosis): relatively narrow therapeutic window
  > 400 µg/day supplementation → nail and hair loss, garlic breath, nausea
  Selenate in water > 1000 µg/L → overt toxicity
  UL: 400 µg/day
  SELECT trial: Se + E supplements → null/possible harm for cancer prevention
               (contradicted earlier case-control studies)

RDA: 55 µg/day; best sources: Brazil nuts (vary wildly, 1 nut = 50–300+ µg),
     seafood, organ meats, meat
```

### Iodine

```
THYROID HORMONES: iodine is uniquely required for their synthesis

T4 (thyroxine): 4 iodine atoms per molecule; prohormone
T3 (triiodothyronine): 3 iodine atoms; active form (5× more potent)
Conversion: T4 → T3 by deiodinase enzymes (selenium-dependent)

THYROID FUNCTION:
  TSH (pituitary) → thyroid gland → T4/T3 synthesis and secretion
  T3/T4 → nuclear receptor → regulate >500 genes
  Functions: basal metabolic rate, growth, neurodevelopment, temperature regulation

DEFICIENCY:
  Goiter: enlarged thyroid gland (hyperplasia compensating for low iodine)
  Hypothyroidism: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, mental slowing
  CRETINISM (prenatal severe deficiency): most preventable cause of intellectual disability
    Maternal iodine deficiency → fetal brain cannot develop normally
    → Irreversible: proper brain development requires T3 in second trimester
    → Global burden: ~500,000 severely affected births/year in deficient regions
  WHO: iodine deficiency remains #1 preventable cause of brain damage globally

IODIZED SALT: WHO/UNICEF intervention; reduced goiter dramatically
  Morton Salt (US): 45 µg I per gram salt → 2,250 µg/day at 5g salt consumption
  Many regions: mandatory iodization has nearly eliminated endemic cretinism

GOITROGENS: substances that interfere with thyroid iodine uptake
  Cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, kale, cabbage): glucosinolates → thiocyanates
  → Block iodine organification at high intake
  Clinical significance: minimal with adequate iodine intake; cooking reduces goitrogenic activity
  Soy: may also inhibit thyroid peroxidase
  → Practical: no issue with normal mixed diet and adequate iodine

TOXICITY:
  Excess iodine (Wolff-Chaikoff effect): acutely suppresses thyroid
  Persistent excess → Jod-Basedow phenomenon: hyperthyroidism in predisposed individuals
  UL: 1,100 µg/day; supplement doses of 150–250 µg safe for most
```

## Bioavailability: The Critical Variable

```
FACTORS AFFECTING MINERAL BIOAVAILABILITY:

DIETARY INHIBITORS:
  Phytate (phytic acid, inositol hexaphosphate):
    Found in: whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds (protective seed compound)
    Chelates: Fe, Zn, Ca, Mg → insoluble complexes → not absorbed
    Molar phytate:Zn ratio > 15:1 → significant Zn inhibition
    Reduction strategies:
      Fermentation (sourdough, tempeh): phytase breaks down phytate
      Soaking + discarding water: leaches some phytate
      Germination/sprouting: activates plant phytase
      Cooking: some heat-labile phytase activity

  Oxalate:
    Found in: spinach, beets, Swiss chard, rhubarb
    Chelates Ca, Fe → reduced absorption from high-oxalate sources
    Spinach Ca absorption ~5% vs kale ~50% (low oxalate)
    Kidney stones in susceptible individuals from high-oxalate diet

  Polyphenols:
    Tea and coffee tannic acids: form complexes with non-heme Fe
    Coffee: reduces Fe absorption ~40% if taken with meal
    Timing: separate coffee/tea from iron-rich meals or supplementation by 1+ hour

DIETARY ENHANCERS:
  Vitamin C (ascorbic acid):
    Reduces Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ → better absorbed by DMT1
    100–200mg with a meal → doubles non-heme iron absorption
    Also promotes Fe release from storage proteins
    Clinical use: take iron supplement with orange juice

  Meat factor (MPF — meat protein factor):
    Peptides from meat digestion enhance non-heme iron absorption
    →"Meat-factor effect": adding meat to plant-based meal improves Fe + Zn absorption
    Mechanism: partially understood; enhances DMT1-mediated transport

  Fermentation:
    Reduces phytate → improves mineral bioavailability from plant foods
    Traditional fermented foods (injera, idli, tempeh, sourdough) have better mineral release

PHYSIOLOGICAL FACTORS:
  Iron status: low iron → upregulate absorptive machinery (DMT1, DcytB, ferroportin)
              → absorption increases with deficiency (homeostatic)
  Age: vitamin D-dependent Ca absorption declines with age + menopause
  Gastric acid: needed for Fe³⁺ reduction; PPI use → impaired Fe absorption
  Pregnancy: increased absorption of Fe, Ca, Zn in third trimester
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Concern | Mineral action |
|---------|---------------|
| Iron deficiency anemia | Heme iron sources (meat); vitamin C with non-heme; check ferritin + TIBC |
| Calcium absorption from plants | Choose low-oxalate sources (kale > spinach); adequate vitamin D |
| Maximize non-heme iron from vegan diet | Vitamin C with every iron-rich meal; avoid tea/coffee with meals |
| Magnesium deficiency (cramps, arrhythmia) | Increase nuts, seeds, leafy greens; supplement Mg glycinate |
| Zinc on plant-based diet | Soak/ferment legumes; consider supplementation |
| Iodine on vegan diet | Use iodized salt; consider supplement (seaweed variable and unreliable) |
| Selenium adequacy globally | Brazil nuts (1/day); OR seafood; supplement 100-200µg if concerned |
| Potassium and blood pressure | Fruits (bananas, avocado), potatoes, legumes; low-sodium diet simultaneously |

## Common Confusion Points

**Spinach is not a good calcium source despite high calcium content**
The oxalate in spinach chelates calcium so effectively that only ~5% of spinach calcium is absorbed. Kale, broccoli, and bok choy have lower total calcium but 40–60% bioavailability. This matters especially for vegans calculating calcium intake.

**Ferritin is not a reliable iron status marker in the presence of inflammation**
Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant — it rises during inflammation regardless of iron stores. A person with iron deficiency + infection/inflammation can have normal or elevated ferritin. The complete iron panel (ferritin + serum iron + TIBC + transferrin saturation + CRP) provides better picture. Transferrin saturation < 16% is more sensitive for deficiency in inflammatory states.

**Calcium supplementation and cardiovascular risk is genuinely uncertain**
Some meta-analyses suggest high-dose calcium supplementation (> 500mg/day in supplement form) may increase cardiovascular events. Dietary calcium does not show this association. Current guidance: get calcium from diet when possible; if supplementing, keep doses moderate (500mg at most per sitting, ideally 500mg/day or less); combine with vitamin K2.

**Iodine and thyroid: both deficiency and excess can cause goiter**
Deficiency → goiter (thyroid enlarges to capture more iodine). Excess iodine initially suppresses thyroid (Wolff-Chaikoff effect), but chronic excess can also cause thyroid dysfunction and goiter in predisposed individuals (Jod-Basedow). The therapeutic window is relatively wide for most people, but those with pre-existing thyroid disease are more sensitive.

**The 40mg/day zinc upper limit is due to copper displacement, not zinc toxicity**
High supplemental zinc (> 40mg/day) competes with copper absorption (same DMT1 transporter) → copper deficiency → anemia, neuropathy. Zinc supplementation at therapeutic doses for colds (>80mg lozenges) is safe short-term but shouldn't be continued for weeks.
