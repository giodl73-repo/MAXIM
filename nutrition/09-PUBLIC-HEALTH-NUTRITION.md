# Public Health Nutrition

## The Big Picture

Public health nutrition operates at the intersection of biochemistry, epidemiology, economics, and politics. Individual nutritional science asks "what should a person eat?" — public health nutrition asks "how do populations actually eat, why, and what structural interventions improve outcomes?" The answers are often uncomfortable: the barriers to healthy eating are not primarily knowledge gaps but economic, environmental, and political structures that create incentives for poor dietary choices.

```
PUBLIC HEALTH NUTRITION — LEVELS OF ANALYSIS:

  INDIVIDUAL:
    Knowledge, skills, preferences, cultural practices
    Autonomy-based interventions: education, labeling, counseling
    Limited effect alone: people generally know vegetables are healthier than chips

  COMMUNITY/ENVIRONMENT:
    Food environment: what's available, affordable, accessible
    Food swamps: density of fast food vs. grocery stores
    Food deserts: geographic lack of access to fresh food
    Interventions: zoning, subsidized grocery programs, corner store conversion

  SYSTEMIC/POLITICAL:
    Agricultural policy: which crops receive subsidies
    Trade policy: global food system structure
    Industry influence: lobbying on dietary guidelines, marketing restrictions
    Pricing: sugar taxes, SNAP (food stamp) restrictions, school lunch standards

GLOBAL BURDEN:
  UNDERNUTRITION:
    829 million chronically hungry (FAO 2021)
    149 million children under 5: stunted (chronically low height-for-age)
    45 million children: wasted (acutely low weight-for-height)
    Undernutrition contributes to 45% of all child deaths under 5

  MICRONUTRIENT DEFICIENCIES ("Hidden hunger"):
    Affect 2+ billion people; often no visible symptoms until severe
    Iron deficiency: most common (>1 billion affected)
    Iodine deficiency: 1 billion at risk; 50 million with cognitive impairment
    Vitamin A deficiency: leading cause of preventable blindness in children

  OBESITY AND OVERNUTRITION:
    2.5 billion overweight; 890 million obese (2022 WHO data)
    "Double burden": undernutrition + obesity coexist in same countries/households
    Fastest growth: low/middle income countries adopting Western food systems
```

## Undernutrition

### Kwashiorkor and Marasmus

```
KWASHIORKOR (Protein Deficiency):

  Etymology: Ga language (Ghana): "disease of the displaced child" (when second child born)
  Cause: adequate caloric intake but severe protein deficiency
    → Classic scenario: child weaned to starchy gruel (cassava, plantain) when new sibling born
    → Calories = adequate; protein = near-zero; amino acid insufficiency

  PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
    Protein deficiency → low plasma albumin → low oncotic pressure
    → Fluid shifts from vascular to interstitial → edema (pitting edema, ascites)
    → Paradox: edematous child may look "fat" → "sugar baby" → late recognition

  SIGNS:
    Edema: bilateral pitting edema of feet/legs, then generalized
    "Flaky paint" skin: depigmentation, peeling, ulceration
    Hair changes: depigmentation (reddish = "flag sign"), easily plucked, straight
    Fatty liver: impaired VLDL synthesis → fat accumulates in hepatocytes
    Apathy, irritability, loss of appetite
    Moonface: facial edema

  LAB: Hypoalbuminemia (<3.0 g/dL); hypoproteinemia; low transferrin; low pre-albumin

MARASMUS (Total Caloric Deficiency):

  Cause: severe deficiency of both calories AND protein
    → Starvation in the setting of inadequate total food access
    → Often: chronic food insecurity; maternal malnutrition; infant diarrhea causing malabsorption

  PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
    Total starvation → body catabolizes fat stores, then muscle → extreme wasting
    No edema (plasma proteins maintained better; catabolism provides amino acids)

  SIGNS:
    Severe muscle wasting: "skin and bones" appearance; ribs visible
    Loose skin folds (loss of subcutaneous fat)
    Growth retardation: low weight-for-height AND low height-for-age
    Hunger: alert, anxious, irritable (unlike Kwashiorkor apathy)
    No edema; no skin/hair changes

MARASMIC KWASHIORKOR:
  Both features: edema + wasting
  Most severe form; worst prognosis

MANAGEMENT (WHO F-75/F-100 protocol):
  Phase 1 (stabilization): F-75 formula (low protein); treat infection; electrolytes
  Phase 2 (rehabilitation): F-100 (high protein/energy); transition to food
  NEVER: high-protein refeeding first → refeeding syndrome (fatal electrolyte shifts)
  → Hypophosphatemia from insulin surge + cellular uptake depletes phosphate
  → Cardiac failure if refeeding too fast
```

### Global Child Malnutrition

```
MALNUTRITION METRICS:

  STUNTING (low height-for-age, <-2 SD below median):
    Chronic, cumulative undernutrition → irreversible after age 2
    Consequences: permanent cognitive impairment; reduced adult earning capacity
    149 million children globally (2022); Sub-Saharan Africa + South Asia dominant
    Critical window: first 1000 days (conception to age 2)

  WASTING (low weight-for-height, <-2 SD):
    Acute undernutrition; can be reversed
    45 million children globally; acute crisis indicator

  UNDERWEIGHT (low weight-for-age): composite indicator; less used now

  SAM (Severe Acute Malnutrition): MUAC < 115mm OR weight-for-height Z < -3 SD
    Life-threatening; requires therapeutic feeding

INTERVENTIONS:
  RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food): Plumpy'nut (peanut paste, milk, sugar, oil + vitamins)
    Community-based treatment (no hospitalization needed)
    Transformed treatment of SAM: 90% recovery rate with RUTF vs <50% hospital-based historically
  Breastfeeding: exclusive for 6 months → single most effective intervention for infant mortality
  Micronutrient supplementation: see below
  Fortification: salt iodization; flour iron + folic acid; rice vitamin A
```

## Micronutrient Deficiencies

### Iodine Deficiency

```
IODINE DEFICIENCY DISORDERS (IDD):

  GLOBAL BURDEN:
    1 billion people at risk of iodine deficiency
    ~50 million with cognitive impairment from IDD
    Leading preventable cause of brain damage in children worldwide

  THYROID PHYSIOLOGY:
    Iodine (I⁻) → trapped by thyroid → oxidized to I₂ → bound to thyroglobulin → T4/T3
    Triiodothyronine (T3): active form; ~4× more potent than T4
    Thyroxine (T4): prohormone; converted to T3 in peripheral tissues
    Thyroid hormones: essential for fetal brain development and myelination

  DEFICIENCY CONSEQUENCES (by severity):
    Mild: goiter (thyroid gland enlargement; compensatory TSH stimulation)
    Moderate: subclinical hypothyroidism; reduced IQ (~10-15 points in population studies)
    Severe during pregnancy: cretinism
      → Neurological cretinism: severe intellectual disability, deaf-mutism, motor deficits
      → Myxedematous cretinism: profound hypothyroidism; dwarfism; less cognitive damage
      → IRREVERSIBLE if not treated before birth/early infancy
    Iodine deficiency in pregnancy: most devastating; all maternal iodine is needed for fetal brain

  IODIZED SALT:
    David Marine (1920s): first systematic iodine supplementation
    Michigan trial (1924): goiter rate fell from 40% to <1% with iodized salt
    Universal salt iodization (USI) campaign: 90% of world's salt now iodized
    Success: global cretinism near-eliminated; goiter rates dramatically reduced
    Still problematic: refugee camps, conflict zones, remote communities

ASSESSMENT:
  Population: urinary iodine concentration (UIC) in spot urine samples
    Sufficient: 100–299 μg/L (median in population)
    Deficient: <100 μg/L
    Excess: >300 μg/L (can also cause thyroid problems — Jod-Basedow)
  Individual: TSH + FT4 + thyroglobulin
```

### Iron Deficiency

```
IRON DEFICIENCY — MOST COMMON NUTRIENT DEFICIENCY WORLDWIDE:

  BURDEN: >1 billion people iron deficient; 300-500 million with iron deficiency anemia (IDA)
  Disproportionately: pre-menopausal women, pregnant women, young children

  STAGES:
    Stage 1 (storage depletion): serum ferritin falls; no functional impairment yet
    Stage 2 (transport depletion): serum iron falls; TIBC rises; hemoglobin normal
    Stage 3 (functional deficiency/IDA): hemoglobin falls → anemia
      → Microcytic, hypochromic anemia (small, pale red cells; low MCV, MCH)

  CONSEQUENCES:
    Children: cognitive impairment; behavioral problems; delayed motor development
              (partly reversible with iron treatment if early enough)
    Adults: fatigue; reduced work capacity; reduced cognitive function
    Pregnancy: maternal IDA → premature birth; low birth weight; maternal mortality

  TREATMENT:
    Oral iron: ferrous sulfate/gluconate/fumarate; 150-200mg elemental iron/day
    GI side effects: constipation, nausea (take with food; divided doses)
    IV iron (modern): Ferric carboxymaltose; now first-line if GI intolerance or severe
    → IV: more expensive but better tolerated; rises Hb faster
    Transfusion: only if Hb <7 with symptoms or <8 in high-risk

  PREVENTION:
    Fortification: wheat flour, maize meal → iron + folic acid (most cost-effective)
    Supplementation: routine iron + folate in pregnancy (WHO recommendation)
    MNP (multiple micronutrient powder): sprinkle on complementary foods for infants
    Dietary: promote heme iron (meat, fish); vitamin C with non-heme iron meals;
             reduce tea/coffee with meals; soaking/fermentation to reduce phytates
```

### Vitamin A Deficiency

```
VITAMIN A DEFICIENCY (VAD):

  BURDEN: 190 million preschool children; 19 million pregnant women
  Leading cause of preventable childhood blindness globally

  CLINICAL PROGRESSION:
    Mild: night blindness (first symptom — rod photoreceptors need rhodopsin = retinal)
    Moderate: Bitot's spots (foamy, white deposits on conjunctiva; keratin accumulation)
    Severe: xerophthalmia (dry eye; corneal damage) → corneal ulceration → blindness
    → Corneal scarring: irreversible blindness; affects 250,000–500,000 children/year
    Immune impairment: VAD → increased severity of measles, diarrheal disease, malaria
    → Half of all under-5 deaths in VAD-endemic areas could be prevented

  VITAMIN A SUPPLEMENTATION (VAS):
    High-dose capsules twice-yearly (WHO recommendation; age 6mo–5yr)
    200,000 IU retinol (every 4–6 months)
    Cost: ~$1–2 per child per year
    Impact: 24% reduction in all-cause mortality; 28% reduction in diarrhea mortality
    One of most cost-effective interventions in global health (Copenhagen Consensus)

  GOLDEN RICE:
    Biofortified rice expressing β-carotene (provitamin A) — yellow/golden color
    Developed: USAID-funded, 1999 (Ingo Potrykus + Peter Beyer)
    Opposition: Greenpeace + GMO opponents blocked introduction for 20+ years
    Philippines: first regulatory approval 2021; commercial cultivation beginning
    Bangladesh: approved 2024
    → Controversy: legitimate GMO safety concerns vs. calculated campaign to delay
       during which hundreds of thousands of children went blind
```

## The Obesity Epidemic

```
GLOBAL OBESITY TRENDS:

  1975: world obesity rate ~4% (adults)
  2022: 16% of adults obese; 43% overweight (WHO)
  US: 42% obese; 74% overweight (NHANES 2022)
  China: 6% obese (2020); fastest growing; 50 million obese adults

MECHANISMS (why has obesity increased so dramatically?):

  FOOD ENVIRONMENT:
    Caloric density increased: portion sizes; cheap fats/sugars; restaurant/takeaway culture
    UPF proliferation: engineered for palatability; displaces whole foods
    Energy density vs. satiety: UPF = high kcal/g + poor satiety signaling
    Marketing: $11B/year on food advertising in US; 80% for UPF + fast food

  DECREASED PHYSICAL ACTIVITY:
    Sedentary work (knowledge economy)
    Car-dependent urban design
    Screen entertainment
    Decline of active transport (walking, cycling)

  BIOLOGICAL:
    Set point theory: body defends a weight range via metabolic adaptation + appetite
    Adipokines: fat tissue secretes leptin (satiety), adiponectin → dysregulated in obesity
    Leptin resistance: obese individuals: high leptin but brain leptin receptors desensitized
    Gut microbiome: obese dysbiome → may extract more energy from food (Turnbaugh 2006)
    Epigenetics: intrauterine programming → offspring of obese mothers → higher obesity risk

SOCIAL DETERMINANTS:
  Income: food insecurity paradox → cheaper calories from high-calorie foods
    → Low-income neighborhoods: dollar store + fast food > grocery stores
    → Healthy diet costs ~$1.50/day more than unhealthy diet (Drewnowski)
  Education: less time for cooking; less knowledge of food preparation
  Stress: cortisol → increased appetite; especially high-fat/high-sugar foods
  Shift work: circadian disruption → metabolic dysfunction + weight gain

MEDICATIONS:
  GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide = Ozempic/Wegovy; tirzepatide = Mounjaro):
    Originally: T2DM medication
    Weight loss: 15–22% body weight reduction in RCTs (unprecedented pharmacologically)
    Mechanism: GLP-1 receptor in brain → appetite suppression; delayed gastric emptying
    Impact: potentially reframes obesity as treatable medical condition vs. willpower failure
    Concern: side effects (nausea, rare pancreatitis); cost ($1,000/month); long-term safety
```

## Food Environment

```
FOOD SWAMPS AND FOOD DESERTS:

  FOOD DESERT:
    Geographic area with limited access to affordable, nutritious food
    USDA definition: urban = >1 mile; rural = >10 miles from supermarket
    ~23.5 million Americans in food deserts (USDA Food Access Research Atlas)

  FOOD SWAMP:
    Area saturated with high-calorie, low-nutrient food outlets
    Fast food restaurants + convenience stores overwhelm any healthy options
    Research (2017, Cooksey-Stowers): food swamps stronger predictor of obesity than food deserts

  RESEARCH CONTROVERSY:
    Initial enthusiasm: food deserts → obesity → remedy = add supermarkets
    Later studies (Allcott 2019, QJE): supermarkets added to food deserts →
      NO significant change in dietary behavior
    → Dietary patterns didn't change when access improved
    → Conclusion: DEMAND (preferences, budget, time, culture) ≠ SUPPLY (access)
    → "Access" model alone is insufficient; structural factors include income, price, time

FOOD PRICING:
  Energy density vs. cost: cheap energy = refined grains, fats, sugars
    $/1000 kcal: white bread $0.22; chips $0.24; kale $11.61; blueberries $8.52
  Healthy eating costs more per calorie but may not cost more per meal if structured well
  Drewnowski's finding: people in poverty maximize kcal per dollar → rational in context
  SNAP (food stamp program): $6/person/day in 2023
    → $6 can buy healthy food; but requires: time, knowledge, cooking infrastructure, mental bandwidth
    → All of which low-income households have less of (poverty tax on cognitive resources)

SCHOOL NUTRITION:
  National School Lunch Program (US): 30 million children; 5 billion meals/year
  Impacts: children's entire vegetable/fruit intake often depends on school meals
  "Smart Snacks" rules: restrict competitive foods sold during school day
  Breakfast programs: strongest evidence — breakfast availability → better cognitive performance
  → School meal quality has improved since 2010 Healthy Hunger-Free Kids Act; still contested
```

## Dietary Guidelines and Political Economy

```
US DIETARY GUIDELINES (updated every 5 years; last: 2020–2025):

POLITICAL ECONOMY:
  Process: USDA + HHS appoint Dietary Guidelines Advisory Committee (DGAC)
  DGAC: independent nutrition scientists → evidence review → report
  Political step: USDA + HHS convert DGAC report to actual guidelines
  → Here: industry lobbying modifies recommendations

HISTORICAL EXAMPLES OF INDUSTRY INFLUENCE:

  1977 McGovern Report (Senate Select Committee):
    Original language: "decrease consumption of meat" (based on saturated fat evidence)
    Beef industry lobbied → changed to "choose meats that will reduce saturated fat intake"
    → Structural change: language shifted from food to nutrient
    → Led to nutrient-level thinking rather than food-level recommendations
    → "Saturated fat" instead of "meat" → allows reformulated processed products to comply

  Sugar Industry (JAMA Internal Medicine, Kearns 2016):
    Leaked internal documents: Sugar Research Foundation funded Harvard researchers
    to downplay sugar-heart disease link and redirect blame to fat (1960s)
    → Shaped decades of dietary guidelines toward low-fat approach

  Low-Fat Era (1980s–2000s):
    Dietary guidelines focused on reducing fat → food industry responded with low-fat products
    → Often replaced fat with refined carbohydrates and sugar
    → "Snackwell's" phenomenon: fat-free cookies with same calories
    → May have contributed to obesity epidemic (refined carbs + palatability of fat-free food)

  Eggs: for decades recommended to limit (dietary cholesterol → serum cholesterol)
    2015 DGAC: removed dietary cholesterol limit (evidence changed → dietary cholesterol
    doesn't directly raise serum cholesterol in most people)
    Egg industry lobbying: had fought the restrictions for years; finally vindicated by evidence

  Sodium: current guideline = <2300 mg/day (1 tsp)
    Average US intake: ~3400 mg/day
    Industry lobbied heavily against lower targets
    American Heart Association: recommends <1500 mg/day for most adults

REFORMING THE PROCESS:
  PROPONENT: Base guidelines on food patterns (Mediterranean, DASH) rather than nutrients
  → Avoids industry gaming of individual nutrient categories
  → Currently: 2020-2025 guidelines include more food-level advice (reduce red/processed meat)
```

## Supplementation Evidence Summary

```
EVIDENCE-BASED SUPPLEMENTATION:

  STRONG EVIDENCE — SUPPLEMENT:
    Folic acid (400 μg/day): periconceptional → prevents neural tube defects
      95%+ prevention of NTDs; mandatory recommendation for all reproductive-age women
    Vitamin D (1000–2000 IU/day): most adults deficient; bone health; immune
    B12 for vegans: see earlier module — mandatory
    Iron in deficiency: treat identified iron deficiency; not routine in all
    Iodine in pregnancy: 150 μg/day supplement if not using iodized salt
    Omega-3 (DHA) for vegans: 200–300 mg algae DHA; cardiovascular + fetal brain

  MODERATE EVIDENCE — CONTEXT-SPECIFIC:
    Calcium (1000–1200 mg/day): only if dietary intake inadequate
      Combined calcium + D: fracture prevention in elderly (USPSTF: recommended)
      Calcium alone: does NOT reduce fractures (meta-analyses)
      High-dose calcium supplements: possible ↑ cardiovascular risk (controversial)
    Magnesium (300–400 mg/day): if deficient (common); sleep, blood pressure
    Zinc: only if deficient; excess → copper deficiency
    Vitamin K2 (MK-7 form): with vitamin D for bone + cardiovascular; weak evidence

  EVIDENCE AGAINST — DO NOT SUPPLEMENT:
    Vitamin E (high dose): ↑ all-cause mortality in meta-analyses; pro-oxidant
    Vitamin A (high dose): ↑ lung cancer in smokers (CARET trial, 1996);
                            teratogenic in excess in pregnancy
    Beta-carotene: same concern as A for smokers
    Antioxidant cocktails (A, C, E): no benefit; possible harm in high doses
    Selenium (excess): toxic; U-shaped curve — only supplement if deficient
    Fish oil (EPA/DHA in general population): ASCEND (2018), VITAL (2019):
      no CV event prevention in general population; benefit only in high-risk/deficient

  INSUFFICIENT EVIDENCE:
    Probiotics (specific strains for specific conditions): varies; see gut microbiome module
    Resveratrol, NMN, quercetin: preclinical evidence; no human evidence of longevity benefit
    Collagen peptides: some joint pain evidence; GI absorption to collagen unclear
    Multivitamins (general): no benefit on hard outcomes in adequately nourished adults
      (exception: elderly with poor dietary diversity; developing world)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Kwashiorkor and marasmus can coexist and are not opposites**
The classic teaching distinguishes them (edema vs. wasting), but in practice, marasmic kwashiorkor combines both. A child with severe acute malnutrition (SAM) may present on a spectrum. The WHO management protocol (F-75/F-100) applies to both; the dangerous error is aggressive early refeeding in either condition — refeeding syndrome kills.

**Iron deficiency ≠ anemia**
Iron deficiency has three stages. Anemia only appears in the final stage when hemoglobin falls. Cognitive impairment and fatigue can occur in Stage 1–2 (storage and transport depletion) before anemia is detectable by complete blood count. A normal hemoglobin does not rule out iron deficiency — ferritin testing is required for early detection.

**Iodine deficiency is a solved problem — except where it isn't**
Universal salt iodization has been one of public health's great successes: cretinism is now rare in most countries. But iodine deficiency persists wherever: iodized salt programs aren't universal (conflict zones, remote areas), populations avoid iodized salt (low-sodium dieters, pure sea salt users, some organic food consumers), or dietary sources are inadequate. Thyroid deficiency in pregnancy from mild iodine deficiency is still a meaningful contributor to cognitive outcomes in high-income countries.

**Food deserts are real but not the whole story**
The food desert concept captured a genuine inequality in geographic food access. The complication is that studies adding grocery stores to food deserts have generally not changed dietary behavior. This doesn't mean access doesn't matter — it means access is necessary but not sufficient. Price, cooking time, cultural food preferences, income for equipment, and food knowledge all interact. Interventions that only address physical access will have limited impact.

**Dietary guidelines are influenced by industry but not captured by it**
The US dietary guidelines have been influenced by agricultural lobbying — this is documented in industry internal communications. But the guidelines are not simply industry propaganda: they do recommend reducing red/processed meat, limiting sodium and added sugars, and increasing vegetables. The industry's influence tends to soften the recommendations at the margins, delay adoption of new evidence, and maintain nutrient-level language that allows product reformulation rather than food-level changes.
