# Proteins

## The Big Picture

Protein is the most functionally diverse macronutrient — structural material, catalysts, hormones, antibodies, neurotransmitter precursors, oxygen carriers. From a dietary standpoint, the key questions are: what quality, what quantity, and what timing?

```
PROTEIN SYSTEM — LAYERED VIEW
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INTAKE LAYER:
  Animal proteins              Plant proteins
  (complete; DIAAS 0.9–1.2)   (often incomplete; DIAAS 0.4–0.9)
  high leucine (~8–10.5%)      lower leucine (~6–8%)
         │                              │
         └──────────┬───────────────────┘
                    ↓ DIGESTION
  Pepsin (stomach) → trypsin/chymotrypsin (pancreatic) → brush-border peptidases
  Output: free amino acids + di/tripeptides → portal vein

                    ↓ AMINO ACID POOL
  Liver:     transamination, urea cycle, gluconeogenesis, NEAA synthesis
  Muscle:    protein synthesis (requires mTOR activation)
  Gut:       enterocyte fuel (glutamine)
  Immune:    glutamine + arginine

                    ↓ mTOR / MPS SIGNAL
  Leucine + resistance exercise → Rag GTPase → mTORC1 → S6K1 + 4E-BP1
  → ribosome assembly → muscle protein synthesis
  Leucine threshold: ~2–3g/meal to saturate signal
  → maps to ~25–40g high-quality protein or ~30–40g+ plant protein

                    ↓ QUALITY METRICS (PDCAAS / DIAAS)
  PDCAAS: capped at 1.0; uses fecal digestibility (less accurate)
  DIAAS:  uses ileal digestibility; uncapped; picks limiting AA
  Practical: DIAAS < 0.75 = incomplete; requires complementation

                    ↓ ANIMAL vs PLANT (see section below)
  Animal: complete AAs; high leucine; heme iron; B12; EPA/DHA
  Plant: complementary pairs needed (grains + legumes); ferment to reduce phytate

                    ↓ AGING / SARCOPENIA
  Anabolic resistance → higher dose needed → 1.2–2.0g/kg in older adults
  Combined with resistance exercise → partially overcomes resistance

                    ↓ REQUIREMENTS BY GOAL
  Sedentary RDA: 0.8 g/kg (minimum)
  Resistance training: 1.6–2.2 g/kg
  Cutting: 2.2–3.1 g/kg (preserve LBM)
  Older adult: 1.2–2.0 g/kg
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

```
PROTEIN INTAKE
     │
     ▼
DIGESTION (pepsin → trypsin/chymotrypsin → carboxypeptidases/aminopeptidases)
     ↓
FREE AMINO ACIDS + DI/TRIPEPTIDES in portal blood
     │
     ├── Liver: transamination, deamination, urea cycle, gluconeogenesis
     │          non-essential AA synthesis
     │
     └── Peripheral tissues:
         Muscle: protein synthesis (myofibrillar, mitochondrial, connective tissue)
         → mTORC1 activation required for synthesis signal
         → leucine is the key trigger amino acid

         Gut: enterocytes prefer glutamine as fuel
         Immune cells: glutamine + arginine critical for function
         Liver: arginine → urea cycle → urea excreted by kidney

NITROGEN BALANCE:
  N in (protein / 6.25 = g protein) vs N out (urine + feces + skin + sweat)
  Positive: growing, anabolic state → net protein accretion
  Negative: catabolic (illness, starvation, insufficient intake) → muscle loss
  Zero (equilibrium): maintenance
```

## The 20 Amino Acids

```
AMINO ACID CLASSIFICATION:

ESSENTIAL (cannot synthesize; must obtain from diet — 9):
  Histidine (His, H)   — hemoglobin; histamine precursor
  Isoleucine (Ile, I)  — BCAA; glucogenic+ketogenic; muscle fuel
  Leucine (Leu, L)     — BCAA; key mTOR activator; ketogenic
  Lysine (Lys, K)      — collagen (with hydroxyproline); carnitine synthesis
  Methionine (Met, M)  — SAM donor; sulfur source; first AA in translation
  Phenylalanine (Phe, F)— tyrosine precursor; catecholamine pathway
  Threonine (Thr, T)   — mucins, immunoglobulins
  Tryptophan (Trp, W)  — serotonin + melatonin precursor; least abundant in diet
  Valine (Val, V)      — BCAA; glucogenic; muscle metabolism

CONDITIONALLY ESSENTIAL (essential under stress/illness/rapid growth):
  Arginine (Arg, R)    — urea cycle, NO synthesis; essential in growth
  Cysteine (Cys, C)    — from Met; essential when Met limited; glutathione
  Glutamine (Gln, Q)   — most abundant in blood; gut/immune fuel; becomes
                          essential during illness, surgery, critical illness
  Glycine (Gly, G)     — collagen (1/3 of collagen AAs); becomes limiting when
                          glycine demand high (collagen synthesis, detox)
  Proline (Pro, P)     — collagen (with hydroxyproline); conditional
  Serine (Ser, S)      — phospholipid head groups; serine proteases
  Tyrosine (Tyr, Y)    — from Phe; catecholamines, thyroid hormones, melanin

NON-ESSENTIAL (synthesize from glucose + nitrogen):
  Alanine, Asparagine, Aspartate, Glutamate

BRANCHED-CHAIN AMINO ACIDS (BCAA: Leu + Ile + Val):
  Unique: metabolized primarily in muscle, not liver (unlike most AAs)
  → BCAA supplementation directly available to muscle
  → 35–40% of dietary essential AAs are BCAA
```

## Protein Quality Metrics

### PDCAAS — Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score

```
PDCAAS = (mg of limiting essential AA per g of test protein) /
          (mg of same AA per g of reference pattern) × digestibility

Reference pattern: WHO/FAO 2-to-5-year-old pattern (most demanding)
Digestibility: fecal true digestibility from rat studies
Score capped at 1.0 (100%)

PDCAAS of common proteins:
  Whey protein: 1.0 (100%)
  Casein: 1.0
  Egg white: 1.0
  Soy protein: 1.0
  Beef: ~0.92
  Pea protein: ~0.73 (limiting: methionine)
  Wheat gluten: 0.25 (limiting: lysine)
  Rice protein: ~0.59 (limiting: lysine)

Limitations of PDCAAS:
  • Fecal digestibility includes bacteria, mucus → overestimates true absorption
  • Capping at 1.0 loses information about excess AAs
  • Reference pattern may not be ideal for adults
```

### DIAAS — Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score (WHO, 2013)

```
DIAAS = (mg of digestible dietary indispensable AA per g of dietary protein) /
         (mg of same dietary indispensable AA per g of reference protein) × 100

Key improvements over PDCAAS:
  1. Ileal digestibility (from ileum, not feces) — more physiologically accurate
     → excludes bacteria-modified AAs in colon
  2. NOT capped at 100% — high-quality proteins can exceed 100
  3. Uses three reference patterns: infants, children 6mo-3yr, >3yr+adults
  4. Scores individual AAs; picks lowest (most limiting)

DIAAS of common proteins (adults):
  Whey protein concentrate: 1.09 (limiting AA: Trp, but all above 1.0)
  Milk protein: 1.18
  Egg: 1.13
  Casein: 1.03
  Beef: ~1.0
  Soy protein isolate: 0.90 (limiting: Met+Cys)
  Pea protein: 0.82 (limiting: Met+Cys)
  Cooked rice: 0.59 (limiting: Lys)
  Cooked whole wheat: 0.45 (limiting: Lys)

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  DIAAS score < 0.75 = incomplete protein source (requires complementation)
  For mixed diets, complementation occurs across the day — not within each meal
  (the "complementary protein at every meal" requirement is outdated)
```

## Muscle Protein Synthesis — mTOR and Leucine

### The mTORC1 Pathway

```
MUSCLE PROTEIN SYNTHESIS SIGNAL PATHWAY:

LEUCINE (and other BCAAs) enter muscle
     │
     ▼
Rag GTPase + Ragulator complex sense leucine at lysosomal membrane
     │
     ▼
mTORC1 (mechanistic Target Of Rapamycin Complex 1) activated
     │
     ▼
S6K1 + 4E-BP1 phosphorylation
     │
     ├── S6K1 → activates ribosomal S6 → translation
     └── 4E-BP1 release → eIF4E → cap-dependent translation initiation
                                  → ribosomes assemble on mRNA → protein synthesis

PARALLEL PATHWAY (exercise):
  Resistance exercise → IGF-1 → PI3K → Akt → mTORC1
  → synergizes with leucine → greater MPS than either alone

INHIBITORS of mTORC1:
  Rapamycin (rapalogs): therapeutic mTOR inhibition (transplant, cancer)
  Energy deficit: AMPK activation (energy sensor) → inhibits mTORC1
  → Fasting, extreme caloric restriction suppresses MPS even with protein
```

### The Leucine Threshold

```
LEUCINE THRESHOLD CONCEPT:

  Muscle Protein
  Synthesis Rate
       ↑
       │            ████████  plateau
       │          ██
       │        ██
       │      ██
       │    ██   ← leucine threshold (~2–3g leucine per meal)
       │  ██
       └──────────────────────────► Leucine dose per meal
          0.5  1   2   3   4g

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:
  ~2–3g leucine per meal is needed to maximally stimulate MPS
  This corresponds to ~25–40g high-quality protein (whey: ~10% leucine)
  Spreading protein across 3–5 meals maximizes MPS stimulation per day
  → Four 25–40g protein meals > one 100g protein meal for MPS

LEUCINE CONTENT OF PROTEINS:
  Whey: 10.5% (highest)
  Casein: 9.0%
  Beef/chicken: ~8%
  Soy: 7.7%
  Wheat: 6.8%
  Rice: 7.6%

  Why plant proteins need more grams:
  Lower leucine per gram + lower digestibility + lower DIAAS
  → need ~1.3–1.5× the dose of plant vs animal protein to achieve same MPS
```

### Plant Protein Complementation

```
LIMITING AMINO ACIDS BY SOURCE:
  Grains (rice, wheat, corn): low LYSINE (and often Thr)
  Legumes (beans, lentils, soy, peas): low METHIONINE (+ Cys)
  Nuts/seeds: varies; often low Lys

CLASSICAL COMPLEMENTARY PAIRS:
  Rice + beans: grain (high Met, low Lys) + legume (high Lys, low Met) = complete
  Corn tortilla + black beans: Mexico (traditional)
  Hummus + pita: chickpea + wheat
  Peanut butter + whole grain bread: legume + grain
  Lentil soup + whole grain: staple of Middle Eastern diets

MODERN UNDERSTANDING:
  Don't need to eat complements at same meal — liver maintains free AA pool
  (~4–6 hour window across meals is sufficient)
  A varied plant diet across the day = adequate essential AA coverage
  Pure grains-only or legumes-only diets are limiting — but realistic
  plant-based diets easily meet AA requirements
```

## Whey vs Casein Kinetics

Both from milk (~20% whey, ~80% casein). Very different digestion rates.

```
WHEY PROTEIN:
  Soluble in acid → not clotted by stomach → rapidly emptied
  Peak blood AA concentration: ~60–90 minutes post-ingestion
  Returns to baseline: ~3 hours
  → "fast protein": acute MPS spike
  → Better for post-exercise (acute repair window)
  → High leucine content (~10.5%)

CASEIN PROTEIN:
  Clotted (coagulated) by stomach acid → gel → slow digestion
  Peak blood AA concentration: ~4–5 hours
  Sustained elevated AAs: 5–7 hours
  → "slow protein": sustained anti-catabolic effect
  → Better for overnight or between-meal protein synthesis maintenance
  → Lower leucine (~9%) but sustained delivery

PRACTICAL:
  Post-workout: whey → rapid AA delivery for acute MPS
  Before bed: casein → sustained AA supply overnight (anti-catabolic)
  Micellar casein (native): slowest; casein hydrolysate: faster than intact
```

## Protein in Aging — Sarcopenia

Sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) is one of the most clinically significant consequences of inadequate protein intake in aging.

```
MECHANISM OF AGE-RELATED MUSCLE LOSS:

  Aging → reduced anabolic sensitivity
  → same leucine dose produces less MPS response ("anabolic resistance")
  → need higher protein intake to maintain nitrogen balance
  → baseline MPS rate not reduced; response to feeding is blunted

TIMELINE:
  Peak muscle mass: ~25–35 years
  ~1% muscle loss per year after 40 (accelerates after 60)
  ~30–40% total muscle mass lost by age 80 if sedentary
  → Sarcopenia: predictor of falls, hospitalization, mortality

PROTEIN REQUIREMENTS IN AGING:
  Standard RDA: 0.8g/kg/day (insufficient for older adults)
  Research consensus: 1.2–1.6g/kg/day for older adults to maintain muscle
  Some guidelines recommend 1.6–2.0g/kg for those with resistance training
  Distribution: at least 3 meals × 25–40g (enough to clear leucine threshold)

LEUCINE SUPPLEMENTATION:
  Adding leucine to lower-quality protein meals raises them to threshold
  → Leucine-enriched plant protein can approach animal protein effectiveness
  → Leucine + HMB (β-hydroxy β-methylbutyrate, leucine metabolite) research ongoing

RESISTANCE EXERCISE + PROTEIN:
  Resistance training restores anabolic sensitivity in older adults
  → Makes protein more effective for MPS
  → The "anabolic resistance" of aging is partially overcome by exercise
  → Combined intervention (protein + exercise) >> either alone
```

## Common Confusion Points

**RDA ≠ optimal intake**
The 0.8g/kg RDA is the minimum to prevent protein deficiency in sedentary adults. It is not the optimal intake for muscle maintenance, aging, or athletic performance. Evidence consistently supports higher intakes (1.2–2.2g/kg) for most active adults.

**"Complete protein" at every meal is outdated advice**
The liver maintains a free amino acid pool for 4–6 hours. You don't need to eat complementary proteins at the same meal — across the day is sufficient. This matters for vegans eating diverse plant foods.

**Excess protein does not become muscle**
Extra dietary protein above what's needed for MPS is oxidized (some to glucose via gluconeogenesis) or excreted as urea. You cannot hypertrophy by eating 4g/kg protein if training stimulus and total calories are not sufficient. The building block analogy only works if construction (training signal) is happening.

**Animal protein does not harm kidneys in healthy people**
The concern about protein and kidney damage originates from studies of patients with existing kidney disease (where protein restriction is appropriate) and from rat studies using casein at extreme doses. In healthy adults with normal kidney function, high protein intake (2–3g/kg) has not been shown to reduce GFR or increase kidney damage markers. Different situation in CKD.

**Soy protein is not estrogenic in humans at normal dietary doses**
Soy isoflavones (phytoestrogens) bind estrogen receptors weakly (10,000–100,000× weaker than estradiol). Clinical studies of soy protein consumption do not show feminization, gynecomastia, or reproductive effects in men at typical dietary doses. The concern comes from extrapolating from high-dose animal studies.
