# Vitamins

## The Big Picture

Vitamins are **organic compounds required in small amounts for normal physiological function** that the body cannot synthesize in adequate quantities. The 13 essential vitamins are divided by solubility, which determines absorption, storage, and toxicity risk.

```
VITAMIN CLASSIFICATION:

FAT-SOLUBLE (A, D, E, K):
  Absorbed with dietary fat via chylomicrons → lymph → circulation
  Stored in liver (A, D, K) and adipose tissue (E)
  Can accumulate → TOXICITY POSSIBLE with excess supplementation
  No daily requirement — periodic consumption sufficient if stores adequate
  Exception: daily vitamin K turnover is rapid; stores modest

WATER-SOLUBLE (B-complex × 8, + Vitamin C):
  Absorbed directly in GI tract → portal vein
  Limited storage (except B12 — liver stores 3–5 years' supply)
  Excess excreted in urine → TOXICITY rare (exceptions: B6 neuropathy, niacin flush)
  Daily or frequent intake generally needed

VITAMINS AT A GLANCE:
  A (retinol)        — vision, immune, epithelial differentiation
  D (calciferol)     — calcium homeostasis, bone, immune modulation
  E (tocopherol)     — antioxidant, membrane integrity
  K (phylloquinone / menaquinone) — coagulation, bone calcification
  B1 (thiamine)      — glycolysis/TCA coenzyme (TPP)
  B2 (riboflavin)    — electron carrier (FAD, FMN)
  B3 (niacin)        — electron carrier (NAD+, NADP+)
  B5 (pantothenic)   — coenzyme A component
  B6 (pyridoxine)    — amino acid transamination (PLP coenzyme)
  B7 (biotin)        — carboxylation reactions (acetyl-CoA carboxylase)
  B9 (folate)        — one-carbon transfers, DNA synthesis (methyl-THF)
  B12 (cobalamin)    — myelin synthesis, methionine cycle
  C (ascorbic acid)  — collagen synthesis, antioxidant, iron absorption
```

## Fat-Soluble Vitamins

### Vitamin A (Retinol)

```
FORMS:
  Preformed vitamin A: retinol, retinal, retinoic acid — from animal foods
  Provitamin A: beta-carotene, alpha-carotene, beta-cryptoxanthin — from plants
               beta-carotene → 2 retinol (theoretical); actual conversion ~12:1 from food

METABOLISM:
  Dietary retinol → retinyl esters → packaged in chylomicrons → stored in liver
  beta-carotene: absorbed as intact molecule in some individuals; cleaved to retinal by BCO1
  BCO1 gene: polymorphisms in ~40% of people → poor beta-carotene conversion
             → vegans/vegetarians with BCO1 variant at risk for deficiency

FUNCTIONS:
  Vision:
    11-cis-retinal binds opsin → rhodopsin (rod pigment) — night vision
    Photobleaching: retinal isomerizes cis→trans → activates phototransduction cascade
    Regeneration: all-trans-retinal recycled back to 11-cis → visual cycle
    Deficiency: night blindness (earliest sign); progresses to corneal ulceration,
    xerophthalmia (dry eye), irreversible blindness — leading cause of preventable
    childhood blindness globally

  Gene expression:
    Retinoic acid (RA) → nuclear receptors (RAR/RXR) → regulate transcription
    → epithelial differentiation, immune development, embryogenesis
    → EXCESS RA during pregnancy: teratogenic (neural tube defects)
      → Isotretinoin (Accutane) use requires pregnancy prevention protocols

  Immune function:
    Required for mucosal epithelial integrity → barrier function
    Deficiency → increased susceptibility to infections (measles mortality)
    WHO: vitamin A supplementation in deficient children → measles mortality -50%

DEFICIENCY:
  Endemic in low-income countries with low animal product consumption
  Golden Rice: beta-carotene in rice → biofortification approach
  Global: ~250 million preschool children deficient

TOXICITY (hypervitaminosis A):
  Preformed retinol: toxic at chronic doses > ~10,000 IU/day in adults
  Signs: headache, liver damage, hair loss, bone pain, birth defects
  Polar bear liver: ~500,000–5,000,000 IU/100g — traditional warning
  Beta-carotene: non-toxic (carotenodermia — orange skin — at very high intake,
                 not dangerous; but β-carotene supplements → lung cancer in smokers)

RDA: 700–900 µg RAE/day (RAE = retinol activity equivalent)
UL: 3,000 µg RAE/day (preformed vitamin A only)
```

### Vitamin D (Calciferol)

```
FORMS:
  Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol): synthesized in skin from 7-dehydrocholesterol + UVB
  Vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol): from UV-irradiated ergosterol (fungi/yeast)
  D3 > D2 in raising serum 25(OH)D — D3 preferred for supplementation

SYNTHESIS AND ACTIVATION:
  Skin: 7-dehydrocholesterol + UVB (290–315nm) → pre-vitamin D3 → D3
        Adequate synthesis: 10–20 min noon sun on arms+legs, fair skin, summer
        Insufficient: winter >35°N latitude; dark skin (melanin blocks UVB);
                      sunscreen; age (decreased skin 7-dehydrocholesterol)

  Liver: D3 → 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D, calcidiol) — major circulating form
         25(OH)D is what's measured for vitamin D status (blood test)
         Half-life: ~15 days; best marker of adequacy

  Kidney: 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D (calcitriol, active form)
          Regulated by PTH, calcium, phosphate → tight endocrine control
          1,25(OH)₂D is the biologically active hormone

CALCIUM HOMEOSTASIS (primary function):
  Calcitriol binds vitamin D receptor (VDR) in:
    Small intestine: increases TRPV6 + calbindin → Ca absorption (60% with D; 10–15% without)
    Kidney: reduces Ca excretion
    Bone: at adequate levels, promotes mineralization; at high doses, mobilizes Ca
    Parathyroid: suppresses PTH → reduces bone resorption

  DEFICIENCY CONSEQUENCES:
    Children: rickets — deficient mineralization → soft, bowed leg bones, costochondral rosary
    Adults: osteomalacia — demineralized bone, bone pain, muscle weakness
    Secondary hyperparathyroidism → bone resorption → osteoporosis

NON-SKELETAL FUNCTIONS:
  VDR is expressed in virtually every cell type → widespread gene regulation
  Immune modulation: reduces autoimmunity risk (MS, T1DM association with low D)
  Muscle function: myopathy from severe deficiency
  Cancer: observational association with numerous cancers — RCT evidence weak
  Cardiovascular: observational association — large RCTs (VITAL) show null effect

STATUS CATEGORIES (25(OH)D, nmol/L):
  < 30:    Severe deficiency (rickets/osteomalacia risk)
  30–50:   Deficiency (secondary hyperparathyroidism)
  50–75:   Insufficiency (suboptimal bone health)
  75–125:  Adequate (optimal for bone; most experts' minimum target)
  > 125:   Potential excess (toxicity risk, though not common at this level)
  > 250:   Toxicity: hypercalcemia, kidney damage, calcification

GLOBAL DEFICIENCY:
  ~40% of US adults below 50 nmol/L
  Risk groups: dark skin, limited sun exposure, obesity, elderly, malabsorption
  Supplementation: 1,000–2,000 IU/day is safe and effective to maintain sufficiency
  Therapeutic for severely deficient: 50,000 IU/week for 8–12 weeks (supervised)
```

### Vitamin E (Tocopherol)

```
FORMS:
  8 forms: α, β, γ, δ-tocopherol + 4 tocotrienols
  α-tocopherol: preferentially retained by liver (transfer protein specific to α)
                → primary functional form in blood and tissues

FUNCTION: FAT-SOLUBLE ANTIOXIDANT
  Membrane phospholipids contain PUFA → susceptible to lipid peroxidation chain reactions
  α-tocopherol: located in membrane bilayer → donates H to lipid peroxyl radicals
  Interrupts chain reaction → regenerated by vitamin C (ascorbate) or glutathione
  → First line of defense against membrane lipid oxidation

OTHER FUNCTIONS:
  Modulates platelet aggregation
  Possible gene expression effects (independent of antioxidant)

DEFICIENCY:
  Rare in humans with adequate fat absorption
  Premature infants: can develop hemolytic anemia + retinopathy
  Fat malabsorption syndromes (Crohn's, cystic fibrosis, cholestasis)
    → ataxia, peripheral neuropathy (neurological symptoms from membrane damage)
  Genetic: abetalipoproteinemia → no chylomicrons → no fat-soluble vitamin absorption

SUPPLEMENTATION PARADOX:
  Observational: high dietary vitamin E associated with lower CVD, cancer
  RCTs: high-dose α-tocopherol supplements showed no benefit and possible harm
    HOPE trial: vitamin E 400 IU + heart failure patients → increased HF risk
    ATBC trial: alpha-tocopherol + smokers → no lung cancer protection
  Explanation: high-dose α-tocopherol displaces other tocopherols (especially γ)
               γ-tocopherol neutralizes different free radical species (NO-derived)
               γ:α ratio matters; supplements disrupt the natural balance

RDA: 15 mg/day (α-TE); UL: 1,000 mg/day (from supplements only)
```

### Vitamin K

```
FORMS:
  K1 (phylloquinone): leafy greens (spinach, kale, broccoli)
                      primarily goes to liver for coagulation
  K2 (menaquinones): fermented foods (natto, certain cheeses), animal liver;
                     also synthesized by gut bacteria (contribution unclear)
    MK-4 (short-chain): converted from K1 in tissues; extrahepatic activity
    MK-7 (long-chain): natto; long half-life → better accumulation in bone/artery
    MK-8, MK-9, MK-13: various fermented foods

FUNCTIONS:
  Coagulation factors (K1 primary role):
    γ-carboxylation of glutamate → Glu → Gla (gamma-carboxyglutamate)
    Carboxylated factors: II (prothrombin), VII, IX, X, protein C, protein S, protein Z
    Without vitamin K → decarboxylated inactive clotting factors → hemorrhage
    Warfarin mechanism: inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase → depletes active K
    Reversal: vitamin K1 competes with warfarin effect

  Bone calcification (K2 primary role):
    Osteocalcin: bone matrix protein requiring K-dependent carboxylation
    Carboxylated osteocalcin binds hydroxyapatite → bone matrix organization
    Undercarboxylated osteocalcin = marker of K insufficiency
    MK-7 supplementation → improved osteocalcin carboxylation
    Fracture reduction: mechanistic support; RCT evidence in Japanese population

  Vascular calcification prevention:
    Matrix Gla protein (MGP): K-dependent protein in arterial wall
    Carboxylated MGP → inhibits arterial calcification
    Uncarboxylated MGP: marker of arterial calcification risk
    → K2 may protect against vascular calcification; evidence accumulating

DEFICIENCY:
  Adults: rare except with warfarin, fat malabsorption, liver disease
  Newborns: all newborns born with low K → vitamin K injection at birth standard care
             prevents hemorrhagic disease of the newborn (VKDB)
  Antibiotic treatment: reduces gut bacteria K2 synthesis (marginal contribution)

K1 vs K2 controversy:
  K1 and K2 have different tissue distribution and half-lives
  K1: short half-life (~6h); primarily hepatic coagulation
  MK-7: long half-life (~72h); better extrahepatic (bone, artery) distribution
  Clinical relevance: for bone and vascular outcomes, K2 (especially MK-7) may be
  more relevant than K1; for anticoagulation reversal, K1 is standard

RDA: 90–120 µg/day (total K); no established UL (no known toxicity)
```

## Water-Soluble Vitamins

### B-Vitamin Coenzymes — The Theme

B-vitamins primarily function as **coenzyme precursors** — they are phosphorylated or modified into active coenzyme forms that enable enzymatic reactions.

```
B-VITAMIN COENZYME FORMS AND ROLES:

B1 (thiamine)    → TPP (thiamine pyrophosphate)
                   Pyruvate dehydrogenase (pyruvate → acetyl-CoA)
                   Alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase (TCA)
                   Transketolase (pentose phosphate pathway)
                   Critical: high-carbohydrate oxidation; brain/nerve tissue

B2 (riboflavin)  → FAD, FMN
                   Electron carriers in OXPHOS (Complex I, II, III)
                   Fatty acid oxidation; amino acid metabolism

B3 (niacin)      → NAD⁺, NADP⁺
                   Most critical coenzymes in metabolism
                   Accepts hydride (H⁻) in: glycolysis, TCA, β-oxidation
                   Donates hydride for: biosynthesis reactions
                   NADP⁺: anabolic reactions (lipogenesis, biosynthesis)
                   High-dose niacin: raises HDL, reduces TG (pharmacological)

B5 (pantothenic) → Coenzyme A (CoA)
                   Central to all CoA-requiring reactions:
                   Acetyl-CoA, succinyl-CoA, malonyl-CoA
                   Essentially every metabolic pathway

B6 (pyridoxine)  → PLP (pyridoxal phosphate)
                   Transaminases: amino acid ↔ keto acid
                   Decarboxylases: AA → amines (dopamine, serotonin, GABA synthesis)
                   Glycogen phosphorylase; sphingolipid synthesis

B7 (biotin)      → carboxylation coenzyme
                   Acetyl-CoA carboxylase (FA synthesis)
                   Pyruvate carboxylase (gluconeogenesis)
                   Propionyl-CoA carboxylase
                   Avidin (egg white protein) binds biotin → deficiency if eaten raw

B9 (folate)      → THF (tetrahydrofolate); active form: 5-methyl-THF
                   One-carbon transfers; de novo purine synthesis; dTMP synthesis
                   5-methyl-THF → methionine synthesis (transfers methyl to B12)
                   → DNA synthesis; cell division
                   Neural tube defect prevention (first trimester supplementation critical)

B12 (cobalamin)  → methylcobalamin, adenosylcobalamin
                   Methionine synthase: 5-methyl-THF → THF + methionine
                   (B12 deficiency → folate trap → functional folate deficiency)
                   Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase: propionate metabolism
                   Myelin sheath synthesis and maintenance
                   Neurological: subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord
```

### Deficiency Diseases — The Classic Cases

```
SCURVY (Vitamin C deficiency):
  Mechanism: vitamin C = ascorbic acid = cofactor for prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases
             → hydroxylation of collagen proline and lysine residues
             → cross-linking of triple helix → collagen stability
  Without C: collagen not properly cross-linked → unstable → degrades
  Signs:
    • Perifollicular hemorrhages, corkscrew hairs (weakened connective tissue)
    • Swollen, bleeding gums (gum collagen)
    • Poor wound healing
    • Joint pain (collagen in joints)
    • Fatigue (mitochondrial carnitine synthesis also requires C)
  Historical: scurvy killed more sailors than combat; lemon juice prevented it (Cook, 1776)
  Latent period: 4–12 weeks of deprivation
  Modern: rare; seen in elderly, food insecurity, fad dieters (carnivore diet has some risk)
  RDA: 75–90 mg/day; UL: 2000 mg/day (GI upset at high doses)

RICKETS / OSTEOMALACIA (Vitamin D deficiency):
  See Vitamin D section above

BERIBERI (Thiamine B1 deficiency):
  Two forms:
    Wet beriberi: cardiovascular — heart failure, edema
                  pyruvate dehydrogenase failure → lactate buildup; cardiac dysfunction
    Dry beriberi: peripheral neuropathy — weakness, wasting, sensory loss
  History: endemic where polished white rice = primary calorie (milling removes B1)
           Eijkman (chickens) and Hopkins discovered dietary origin → Nobel Prize 1929
  Today: alcoholism (alcohol interferes with B1 absorption/utilization)
          Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome: acute Wernicke (ophthalmologic, ataxia) →
          chronic Korsakoff (memory disorder) — thiamine emergency in alcohol intoxication

PELLAGRA (Niacin B3 deficiency):
  "4 Ds": Diarrhea, Dermatitis, Dementia, Death
  Dermatitis: symmetric, sun-exposed skin → casal's necklace (around neck)
  Dementia: frontal lobe involvement
  GI: inflammation
  History: endemic in American South early 20th century (corn diet, low niacin)
           Goldberger proved dietary cause (rejected germ theory) 1915–1920
           Corn niacin: bound as nialysin → not bioavailable without alkali processing
           Nixtamalization (lime treatment): traditional Mexican corn processing releases niacin
           → pellagra in European corn consumers, not in Mesoamerican populations
  Today: seen in alcoholism, Hartnup disease (tryptophan absorption defect)

PERNICIOUS ANEMIA (B12 deficiency — most common B12 deficiency cause):
  Pernicious anemia: autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells
    → absence of intrinsic factor (IF) — required for B12 absorption in terminal ileum
    → B12 malabsorption despite adequate dietary B12
    Treatment: IM (intramuscular) B12 injection bypasses GI absorption
               or high-dose oral (passive diffusion without IF at very high doses)
  B12 deficiency consequences:
    Megaloblastic anemia: B12 needed for methionine synthase → without B12 →
                          folate trap (folate stuck as 5-methyl-THF) → no THF for DNA synthesis
                          → large, immature red blood cells (megaloblasts)
    Subacute combined degeneration: demyelination of posterior + lateral spinal cord
                                    → sensory loss, weakness, ataxia, cognitive decline
    Irreversible if untreated
  Vegan risk: B12 only in animal products; all vegans need supplementation

NEURAL TUBE DEFECTS (Folate deficiency):
  Spina bifida, anencephaly
  Critical window: days 21–28 post-conception (often before pregnancy known)
  Folate supplementation: 400 µg/day before conception reduces NTD risk ~70%
  Mandatory folic acid fortification: US since 1998 → NTD prevalence reduced ~35%
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Concern | Key information |
|---------|----------------|
| Vegan diet adequacy | B12 must supplement; vitamin D common deficiency; omega-3 EPA/DHA from algae oil |
| Sun exposure and vitamin D | 10-20 min noon summer sun adequate for most; supplement 1000-2000 IU/day if limited |
| Pregnancy | Folate 400µg/day before conception; D; avoid excess vitamin A (preformed retinol) |
| Fat-soluble vitamin toxicity risk | A and D at high supplement doses; E controversial at high dose; K very safe |
| Drug-nutrient interaction | Warfarin ↔ vitamin K (stable intake preferred); methotrexate ↔ folate |
| Beriberi prevention | Avoid prolonged milled white rice as sole staple; supplement in at-risk alcoholism |
| Pellagra prevention | Nixtamalization of corn; varied protein intake (tryptophan → niacin) |
| Population most at risk for D deficiency | Dark skin + indoor work + high latitude + elderly + obese |

## Common Confusion Points

**Vitamin D from supplements requires cofactors to function**
Vitamin D3 supplementation is most effective when magnesium is adequate (magnesium is required for multiple vitamin D activation steps and for VDR function). Vitamin K2 (MK-7) is increasingly co-supplemented with D to direct calcium to bone rather than arteries. Neither changes the fundamental need for D in deficiency, but they affect the response.

**B12 deficiency can be masked by folate supplementation**
High folate intake can correct megaloblastic anemia from B12 deficiency while neurological damage continues unchecked. This is why high-dose folate supplementation in the elderly is a concern — it may mask B12 deficiency and delay diagnosis until neurological symptoms are irreversible.

**Vitamin C does not prevent colds**
Large RCTs have confirmed: regular vitamin C supplementation does not reduce the incidence of the common cold in the general population. It may modestly reduce duration (~8%) and severity. In extreme physical stress (marathon runners, arctic expeditions) there may be a small prevention benefit. The 1970s Pauling claims were not replicated.

**Vitamin K and warfarin: stable intake, not avoidance**
Patients on warfarin are often told to avoid vitamin K-containing foods. The better advice: maintain consistent vitamin K intake. Warfarin dosing is calibrated to a patient's vitamin K intake level. Wildly fluctuating intake → wildly fluctuating INR. Consistent moderate intake is manageable; elimination is unnecessary and creates nutritional problems.

**Beta-carotene from supplements is not equivalent to beta-carotene from food**
CARET and ATBC trials: synthetic beta-carotene supplements increased lung cancer risk in smokers. Food-based beta-carotene does not show this effect. Explanation: single high-dose supplement form vs mixed carotenoids in food matrix; possible pro-oxidant effect of synthetic beta-carotene in high oxidative stress (smoking) environment.
