# Gut Microbiome

## The Big Picture

The human gut harbors approximately 38 trillion microorganisms — roughly equal to the number of human cells — encoding 150× more unique genes than the human genome. These microbes are not parasites but **metabolic partners**: they digest substrates our enzymes cannot, produce essential metabolites, train the immune system, and communicate with the brain. The microbiome is increasingly recognized as a metabolic organ with systemic effects on health and disease.

```
GUT MICROBIOME — OVERVIEW:

  Location:       Primarily large intestine (colon): 10¹¹ bacteria/mL
                  Small intestine: 10³–10⁷ /mL (far fewer)

  Composition:    Bacteria (~99% of biomass) + Archaea + Fungi (mycobiome) + Viruses (virome)
                  ~1,000–2,000 species in a healthy adult gut
                  Dominated by 4 phyla:
                    Firmicutes:   ~50–65% (Lactobacillus, Clostridia, Ruminococcus, Faecalibacterium)
                    Bacteroidetes: ~20–30% (Bacteroides, Prevotella, Akkermansia)
                    Proteobacteria: ~1–5% (E. coli, Klebsiella) — more = dysbiosis marker
                    Actinobacteria: ~1–3% (Bifidobacterium)

  Weight:         ~0.2 kg (wet weight)
  Genome size:    ~9 million unique genes (vs 22,000 human protein-coding genes)

FUNCTIONAL ROLES:
  Carbohydrate fermentation → short-chain fatty acids (energy, signaling)
  Protein metabolism → amino acids, indoles, phenols (beneficial + toxic)
  Bile acid transformation → secondary bile acids (regulates host metabolism)
  Vitamin synthesis → K₂, B₁₂, folate, biotin (partial supply)
  Immune training → educates mucosal + systemic immune system
  Barrier maintenance → colonocyte nutrition; tight junction regulation
  Gut-brain axis → neurotransmitter precursors, vagus nerve signals
```

## Gut Flora Diversity

### Diversity Metrics

```
DIVERSITY METRICS:
  α-diversity (within-sample):
    Species richness: number of distinct taxa
    Shannon entropy: accounts for richness + evenness
    High α-diversity = more distinct species, more evenly distributed
    → Generally: higher α-diversity = healthier (more resilience, functional redundancy)

  β-diversity (between-sample):
    UniFrac distance: phylogenetically weighted difference between communities
    Bray-Curtis dissimilarity: abundance-based
    PCoA plots: visualize community separation
    → Microbiome composition varies dramatically between individuals

DIVERSITY DETERMINANTS:
  Diet: strongest modifiable determinant
    Diverse plant fiber intake → higher species diversity
    Western diet (low fiber, high fat/sugar) → reduced diversity
    Mediterranean diet → increased Bifidobacterium, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii

  Age:
    Infant: colonized during/after birth (vaginal vs C-section differences)
    Adult: stable but unique composition
    Elderly: reduced diversity; shift toward Proteobacteria; less Bifidobacterium

  Antibiotics: major diversity disruptor
    Broad-spectrum antibiotics → acute collapse of diversity
    Recovery: partial; some taxa may not recover for months or years
    Repeated courses: cumulative loss

  Geography + culture: significant differences across populations
    Hadza (Tanzania): extremely high diversity; 100% fiber-rich diet
    Western populations: significantly lower diversity
    → "Industrialized microbiome": one consequence of Western lifestyle

FIRMICUTES/BACTEROIDETES RATIO (F/B ratio):
  Original claim (2006 Turnbaugh/Gordon): obese mice: higher F/B ratio; lean mice: lower
  Implication: F/B ratio predicts obesity
  CURRENT STATUS: heavily contested and mostly abandoned as a simple marker
    → Human data: inconsistent; no consistent direction of association with obesity
    → Ratio varies with diet, geography, individual, sample type
    → Most current researchers: F/B ratio is not a useful clinical metric
    → More informative: specific species abundance (Akkermansia, F. prausnitzii)
```

### Keystone Species

```
KEYSTONE MICROBIOTA:

  Faecalibacterium prausnitzii:
    Most abundant commensal in healthy adults (~5–15% of total)
    Major butyrate producer
    Anti-inflammatory: produces anti-inflammatory compounds (MAM, butyrate)
    Marker: depleted in Crohn's disease, IBD, colorectal cancer, depression
    → Its absence is one of the most reproducible dysbiosis signatures

  Akkermansia muciniphila:
    ~1–4% of gut bacteria; mucus layer resident (degrades mucin glycoproteins)
    Inverse correlation with obesity, T2DM, metabolic syndrome
    Mechanism: strengthens intestinal barrier (outer membrane protein Amuc_1100)
    First human probiotic RCT (2019): pasteurized Akkermansia → improved metabolic markers
    Now available as commercial probiotic

  Bifidobacterium spp.:
    Dominant in breast-fed infants; ferment HMOs (human milk oligosaccharides)
    Adults: 3–6% of total; higher in non-Western populations
    Well-studied probiotic genus; IBS and infant health evidence strongest

  Ruminococcus gnavus / torques:
    Mucus-associated; elevated in IBD + IBS
    Marker of compromised mucosal barrier

  Clostridium difficile:
    Pathobiont: normally suppressed by competition
    After antibiotics: opportunity → overgrowth → C. diff colitis
    → Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT): 90%+ effective for recurrent C. diff
      (best validated microbiome intervention)
```

## Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs)

```
PRODUCTION:
  Fermentation of dietary fiber (non-digestible carbohydrates) by anaerobic bacteria
  Primary SCFAs: acetate (C2), propionate (C3), butyrate (C4)
  Relative proportions: ~60:20:20 (acetate:propionate:butyrate) in normal fermentation

  Primary producers:
    Butyrate: Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia, Butyrivibrio, Clostridiales
    Propionate: Bacteroides, Akkermansia, Dialister
    Acetate: Bacteroides, Bifidobacterium (most gut bacteria produce some)

BUTYRATE — PRIMARY ENERGY SUBSTRATE FOR COLONOCYTES:

  Functions:
    Colonocyte fuel: ~70% of energy for colon epithelium comes from butyrate
      → Deficiency → colonocytes starve → mucosal atrophy → barrier dysfunction
    Histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor: epigenetic regulation → anti-cancer
      → Inhibits proliferation + promotes differentiation + apoptosis in cancer cells
    Treg induction: butyrate induces regulatory T cells → gut immune tolerance
    Anti-inflammatory: inhibits NF-κB signaling in colonocytes → less IL-6, TNF-α
    GPR41/43 agonist: activates G-protein coupled receptors on enteroendocrine cells
      → Stimulates GLP-1, PYY secretion → satiety signaling

  Butyrate and colorectal cancer (Warburg effect paradox):
    Cancer cells: aerobic glycolysis → don't oxidize butyrate for energy
    Butyrate accumulates in cancer cells → acts as HDAC inhibitor → pro-apoptotic
    Normal colonocytes: oxidize butyrate → don't accumulate it
    → Butyrate is selectively toxic to CRC cells; protective to normal cells

PROPIONATE — LIVER GLUCONEOGENESIS:
  Absorbed → portal circulation → liver
  Substrate for hepatic gluconeogenesis (significant in ruminants; modest in humans)
  Reduces lipogenesis → may improve insulin sensitivity
  GPR41 agonist → stimulates PYY release (satiety)
  Cross-talk with gut microbiome: propionate produces → signals back to host

ACETATE — SYSTEMIC EFFECTS:
  Most abundant SCFA; most escapes to systemic circulation
  Peripheral tissues (muscle, brain): taken up and oxidized for energy
  GPR43 on adipocytes: inhibits lipolysis → fat storage (controversial)
  GPR41 in peripheral nervous system: modulates sympathetic activity
  Brain: crosses BBB; hypothalamic acetate → reduced appetite (animal data)
  Also: substrate for propionate/butyrate production via cross-feeding
```

## Prebiotics and Probiotics

### Prebiotics

```
PREBIOTIC DEFINITION (Gibson & Roberfroid, 1995; updated 2017):
  "A substrate that is selectively utilized by host microorganisms conferring a health benefit"
  → Must be: resistant to digestion, fermentable, selectively stimulate beneficial bacteria

MAJOR PREBIOTIC TYPES:

  FOS — Fructooligosaccharides (short-chain inulin):
    Degree of polymerization: 2–8 units
    Sources: chicory, leek, asparagus, garlic, onion, banana
    Selectivity: Bifidobacterium, some Lactobacillus (can metabolize short-chain fructans)
    Effect: bifidogenic (increases Bifidobacterium); butyrate production
    Dose: 5–15g/day for measurable effect

  Inulin (long-chain FOS):
    DP: 2–60 units; mostly >10 (long-chain vs FOS)
    Primary source: chicory root (commercial inulin extraction)
    Fermented more slowly, more distal in colon → better for whole-colon fermentation
    Bifidogenic + Roseburia/F. prausnitzii stimulation → butyrate production

  Resistant Starch (RS):
    Type RS1: physically inaccessible starch (whole grain, seeds)
    Type RS2: native granules with crystalline structure (raw potato, green banana)
    Type RS3: retrograded starch (cooked-then-cooled potato, rice) → recrystallizes
    Type RS4: chemically modified starch
    Fermentation: Ruminococcus champanellensis, Bacteroides, Bifidobacterium
    → Major butyrate substrate → colonocyte protection
    → Rs3 (cooked-cooled) easy to incorporate: leftover potato salad, cold rice

  GOS — Galactooligosaccharides:
    Present in human milk (HMOs include GOS-like structures)
    Commercial: produced from lactose by β-galactosidase
    Strong bifidogenic effect; used in infant formula
    Well-studied for: immune effects, IBS, infant microbiome

  Pectin:
    Found in fruit cell walls (apple, citrus)
    Gel-forming; fermented to propionate and acetate
    Bifidogenic; may reduce LDL (bile acid sequestration)
```

### Probiotics

```
PROBIOTIC DEFINITION:
  "Live microorganisms that, when administered in adequate amounts, confer
  a health benefit on the host" (FAO/WHO 2001)

  STRAIN SPECIFICITY IS CRITICAL:
  Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG: well-evidenced for acute diarrhea prevention
  Lactobacillus acidophilus (generic): evidence much weaker
  → You cannot generalize "Lactobacillus" — effects are strain-specific
  → Label must specify: genus species strain (e.g., Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG)
  → "Probiotic yogurt" may not contain clinically studied strains

WELL-EVIDENCED USES:

  Acute infectious diarrhea (children):
    L. rhamnosus GG, L. reuteri, Saccharomyces boulardii
    Meta-analyses: reduce duration by ~1 day; reduce severity
    Strongest evidence base in probiotic literature

  Antibiotic-associated diarrhea (AAD):
    S. boulardii + L. rhamnosus GG: level 1 evidence
    Reduces AAD risk ~50%; reduces C. diff risk in outpatients
    Give during AND after antibiotic course

  IBS (irritable bowel syndrome):
    Mixed evidence; some specific strains effective
    VSL#3 (multi-strain): improves IBS symptoms (some trials positive)
    L. plantarum 299v: abdominal pain reduction (moderate evidence)
    Not all IBS patients respond; subtypes matter

  Recurrent C. difficile:
    FMT (fecal microbiota transplant — technically "stool transplant"):
    NOT a probiotic, but the most effective microbiome intervention
    90–95% cure rate vs <35% for antibiotics
    Mechanism: restoration of diversity → competitive exclusion of C. diff

EVIDENCE LIMITATIONS:
  Most studies: short-term (4–12 weeks); small N; different strains; different doses
  Survivability: probiotics must survive gastric acid + bile → reach colon alive
    → Enteric-coated; high CFU count; refrigerated
  Colonization: transient — probiotics don't permanently colonize in most cases
    → Must be taken continuously for effect to persist
  Quality: CFU counts on label often not verified; many products below stated dose
```

## Gut-Brain Axis

```
GUT-BRAIN AXIS COMPONENTS:
  Enteric nervous system (ENS): ~500 million neurons in gut wall
    "Second brain" — processes information locally without CNS input
    Controls: peristalsis, secretion, blood flow
    Communicates with CNS via vagus nerve + spinal cord

  VAGUS NERVE (CN X):
    80–90% afferent (gut → brain)
    Transmits: gut mechanosensory, chemosensory, metabolic signals
    Carries: SCFA signals, immune signals, endocannabinoid signals
    Microbiome → gut epithelium → enterochromaffin cells → vagus → brainstem
    → Influences mood, satiety, anxiety, HPA axis

  SEROTONIN (5-HT):
    ~95% of body's serotonin: synthesized and stored in gut enterochromaffin cells
    Regulates: gut motility, secretion
    Microbiome influence: specific bacteria stimulate enterochromaffin 5-HT synthesis
      (Clostridiales, Ruminococcaceae)
    Gut 5-HT does NOT cross BBB → not directly the "mood serotonin"
    BUT: microbiome → gut 5-HT → vagus → brainstem → modulates CNS serotonin indirectly

  HPA AXIS (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal):
    Microbiome-derived signals → immune cells → cytokines → hypothalamus → stress axis
    Germ-free mice: exaggerated stress response; normalized by colonization
    Human data: dysbiosis associated with elevated cortisol (correlation, not causation)

  NEUROTRANSMITTER PRECURSORS:
    Tryptophan → serotonin (gut) + kynurenine + indoles
      Microbiome modulates tryptophan metabolism → affects kynurenine/IDO pathway
      → Kynurenine pathway: activated in depression; inflammation diverts tryptophan
    GABA: Lactobacillus spp. produce GABA → enteric neurons → vagus → brain
    Dopamine precursors: L-DOPA produced by some gut bacteria → systemic

DYSBIOSIS AND MENTAL HEALTH:
  Depression:
    Meta-analyses: 12 studies → F. prausnitzii depleted, Coprococcus depleted in depression
    Coprococcus → produces DOPAC (dopamine precursor) → correlation with wellbeing
    FMT from depressed donors → depressive behavior in recipient rats (animal model)
    Human FMT for depression: trials underway; preliminary positive signals

  Anxiety:
    L. rhamnosus JB-1: reduced anxiety-like behavior in mice via vagus (Bravo 2011)
    Human trials: mixed; L. helveticus R0052 + B. longum R0175 (Lallemand) → reduced anxiety
      in RCT; replicated partially

  CAUSALITY PROBLEM:
    Most human data is observational: depressed people may eat differently
    → Dysbiosis could be consequence, not cause, of mental illness
    Animal models (germ-free, FMT): suggest causality
    Mechanisms increasingly plausible but human causal evidence still limited
```

## Dysbiosis Links

```
DYSBIOSIS DEFINITION:
  Imbalance in gut microbiome composition and/or function
  → Loss of beneficial taxa; increase in pathobionts
  → Often: reduced diversity, reduced butyrate production, leaky gut

LEAKY GUT (Intestinal Permeability):
  Tight junctions between enterocytes: controlled barrier
  Disrupted by: dysbiosis (specifically reduced butyrate → less tight junction support)
  Result: bacterial products (LPS = lipopolysaccharide) enter portal circulation
  LPS → TLR4 receptors → innate immune activation → low-grade systemic inflammation
  → "Metabolic endotoxemia" (Cani 2007): elevated plasma LPS in high-fat diet mice
  → Found in: obesity, T2DM, NAFLD, metabolic syndrome, some IBD

IBD (INFLAMMATORY BOWEL DISEASE):
  Crohn's disease + Ulcerative Colitis
  Dysbiosis role: not causal alone (genetics required); dysbiosis amplifies inflammation
  Consistent findings:
    ↓ Firmicutes diversity (especially F. prausnitzii)
    ↑ Proteobacteria (E. coli, Klebsiella)
    ↓ Bacteroidetes in UC
  Treatment:
    FMT for UC: ~30% remission in RCTs (less effective than C. diff)
    Exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN) for Crohn's: induces remission (mechanism partly microbiome)

OBESITY:
  Turnbaugh 2006 (Gordon lab): transferring obese mouse microbiome to germ-free mice → more fat gain
  Human data: obese individuals have: ↓ diversity, ↓ Akkermansia, ↓ F. prausnitzii
  BUT: most associations are correlations + confounded by diet
  Mechanism proposed: microbiome extracts ~10% more energy from same food
  FMT from lean to obese humans: improved insulin sensitivity in one small trial;
    not replicated consistently → effect smaller than hoped

TYPE 2 DIABETES:
  Dysbiosis marker: reduced butyrate producers; increased LPS-producing Proteobacteria
  Akkermansia: inversely correlates with T2DM; mechanism = improved gut barrier
  Clinical trials of specific probiotics in T2DM: modest effects on HbA1c
  Metformin: modifies microbiome (increases Akkermansia!) — some T2DM benefit may be microbiome-mediated

COLORECTAL CANCER:
  Fusobacterium nucleatum: found enriched in CRC tissue; promotes CRC growth in animal models
  Mechanism: binds E-cadherin; activates Wnt signaling; creates immunosuppressive environment
  Used as early detection marker (stool F. nucleatum PCR) in some CRC screening protocols
  Bacteroides fragilis (enterotoxigenic): B. fragilis toxin → promotes CRC via STAT3/NF-κB
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Best prebiotic for butyrate production | Resistant starch (RS3 = cooked-cooled potato/rice; RS2 = green banana) |
| Probiotic with most evidence for any indication | L. rhamnosus GG + S. boulardii for AAD/traveler's diarrhea |
| Most effective microbiome intervention overall | FMT for recurrent C. diff (90%+ cure rate) |
| Does gut serotonin affect mood directly? | No — doesn't cross BBB; indirect via vagus; kynurenine pathway more direct |
| How to increase microbiome diversity | Increase dietary fiber diversity: 30+ different plant foods/week |
| Is F/B ratio a useful health marker? | No — not consistently predictive in humans; abandoned as clinical metric |
| Dysbiosis association with obesity | Real but not cleanly causal; diet confound large; microbiome change modest |
| Akkermansia: why valuable? | Mucosal barrier reinforcement; inversely correlated with metabolic disease |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Probiotic" on a food label means almost nothing clinically**
The FDA does not require clinical evidence for probiotic food claims in the US. A yogurt labeled "probiotic" may contain any strain at any CFU count, potentially dead before consumption. Clinical probiotic evidence is strain-specific and dose-specific. If recommending probiotics for a clinical indication, specify the genus-species-strain designation and use products with documented viability at expiration.

**Gut serotonin and brain serotonin are different pools**
95% of serotonin is in the gut — this is a real, well-established fact. But gut-derived 5-HT does not cross the blood-brain barrier. The brain synthesizes its own serotonin (raphe nucleus). The microbiome-mood connection is indirect: via vagal nerve signaling, tryptophan metabolism diversion to kynurenine, and immune/inflammatory mediators — not via serotonin transport from gut to brain.

**Dysbiosis is a description, not a diagnosis**
There is no single "healthy" microbiome composition — it varies enormously between healthy individuals (more than between many disease groups). "Dysbiosis" describes a shift from an individual's baseline or from a population reference — not a specific microbial fingerprint. The same composition might be normal for one person and dysbiotic for another.

**Fecal microbiota transplant works for C. diff; evidence is much weaker elsewhere**
FMT cures recurrent C. diff at 90%+ — this is one of the most striking results in modern medicine and is FDA-approved (2023). For IBD, obesity, and other conditions, results have been mixed, modest, or not replicated. The success in C. diff reflects a specific mechanism (competitive exclusion of a single pathogen); the same approach does not translate straightforwardly to multifactorial diseases.

**Resistant starch content changes with cooking and cooling**
Raw potato starch is RS2 (native crystalline structure → resistant). When cooked, it gelatinizes → becomes digestible (not resistant). When cooled, some re-crystallizes as RS3 → resistant again. This is why cold potato salad has more resistant starch than hot boiled potatoes — the same food, different cooking state. The same applies to rice, pasta, and legumes: cooling after cooking increases RS3 content.
