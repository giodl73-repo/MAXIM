# Harmony & Chords

## The Big Picture: From Intervals to Functional Harmony

```
HARMONIC HIERARCHY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INTERVAL    Two pitches simultaneously → consonance/dissonance

CHORD       3+ pitches → triad (root/3rd/5th) or extended

CHORD TYPE  Quality determined by interval structure:
              Major / Minor / Diminished / Augmented / Suspended

INVERSION   Root not in bass → changes stability/voice leading

7TH CHORD   Triad + stacked 3rd → richer color, more momentum

EXTENSIONS  9th / 11th / 13th → jazz/pop harmonic color

ROMAN NUMS  Scale-degree labeling → describes harmonic FUNCTION

FUNCTION    Tonic / Pre-Dominant / Dominant → tension/resolution arc
              T    PD              D    → T

PROGRESSION Chain of chords with directed motion → musical narrative
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Triads

A triad = root + third + fifth. Four qualities:

```
TRIAD STRUCTURE (above root C):

Major (M):     C  E  G    = M3 + m3 = P5 total  → bright, stable
Minor (m):     C  E♭ G    = m3 + M3 = P5 total  → darker, stable
Diminished (°):C  E♭ G♭   = m3 + m3 = ♭5 total  → tense, unstable
Augmented (+): C  E  G♯   = M3 + M3 = ♯5 total  → ambiguous, tense

NOTATION:
  C   = C major    (no symbol = major)
  Cm  = C minor
  C°  = C diminished
  C+  = C augmented
  Csus2 = suspended 2nd (C D G — no 3rd, has M2 instead)
  Csus4 = suspended 4th (C F G — no 3rd, has P4 instead)
```

### Diatonic Triads in Major

Built on each scale degree of C major:

```
SCALE DEGREE    CHORD    QUALITY
I     C E G     C        Major
ii    D F A     Dm       Minor
iii   E G B     Em       Minor
IV    F A C     F        Major
V     G B D     G        Major
vi    A C E     Am       Minor
vii°  B D F     B°       Diminished
```

**Pattern in all major keys:** M m m M M m °

### Diatonic Triads in Natural Minor

Built on each scale degree of A natural minor:

```
SCALE DEGREE    CHORD    QUALITY
i     A C E     Am       Minor
ii°   B D F     B°       Diminished
♭III  C E G     C        Major
iv    D F A     Dm       Minor
v     E G B     Em       Minor (not dominant — no leading tone)
♭VI   F A C     F        Major
♭VII  G B D     G        Major
```

**Note:** In harmonic minor (raised 7th → G♯), the v becomes V (E G♯ B = major), and vii° becomes vii° (G♯ B D = diminished leading-tone triad). This is why composers use harmonic minor — to get V and vii° in minor.

## Seventh Chords

Add another stacked third above the triad → seventh chord. The 7th above the root determines the type:

```
TYPE              SYMBOL   TRIAD  + 7TH    EXAMPLE (root C)
Major 7th         Cmaj7    Major  + M7     C E G B
Dominant 7th      C7       Major  + m7     C E G B♭
Minor 7th         Cm7      Minor  + m7     C E♭ G B♭
Minor-Major 7th   CmM7     Minor  + M7     C E♭ G B
Half-Diminished   Cø7      Dim    + m7     C E♭ G♭ B♭
  (also: Cm7♭5)
Diminished 7th    C°7      Dim    + d7*    C E♭ G♭ B♭♭(=A)
Augmented 7th     C+7      Aug    + m7     C E G♯ B♭
Augmented maj7    Cmaj7♯5  Aug    + M7     C E G♯ B

*diminished 7th = interval of 9 semitones = ♭♭7 (enharmonically = M6)
 C°7: C-E♭-G♭-B♭♭(=A) — all minor thirds stacked
```

**The dominant seventh (V7)** is the most structurally important: the M3 (B in G7) wants to resolve up to C (tonic), and the m7 (F in G7) wants to resolve down to E — converging voice leading into the tonic triad. This is the engine of tonal harmony.

### Diatonic Seventh Chords in Major (C major)

```
I    Cmaj7   = C E G B        major seventh
ii   Dm7     = D F A C        minor seventh
iii  Em7     = E G B D        minor seventh
IV   Fmaj7   = F A C E        major seventh
V    G7      = G B D F        dominant seventh (ONLY dominant 7th diatonically)
vi   Am7     = A C E G        minor seventh
vii° Bø7     = B D F A        half-diminished (minor 7th flat 5)
```

## Extensions: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths

Continue stacking thirds beyond the 7th. The extension numbers "wrap around" the octave:

```
CHORD TONES IN A 13th CHORD (every diatonic note):
  Root  3rd  5th  7th  9th  11th  13th
    C    E    G    B    D    F     A
    1    3    5    7    9    11    13

NAMING: just the highest extension used
  C9     = C7 + 9th    = C E G B♭ D
  Cmaj9  = Cmaj7 + 9   = C E G B D
  Cm9    = Cm7 + 9     = C E♭ G B♭ D
  C11    = C9 + 11th   = C E G B♭ D F  (11th often omitted in practice — clashes with M3)
  C13    = C11 + 13th  = C E G B♭ D F A

ALTERED extensions (jazz):
  ♭9 = 1 semitone above root (minor 9th) = very tense
  ♯9 = 3 semitones above root = "the Hendrix chord" (E7♯9 = E G♯ B D G♮)
  ♯11 = augmented 11th = Lydian flavor on dominant (C E G B♭ D F♯)
  ♭13 = lowered 13th = replaces 5th in altered chords (C E G♭ B♭ D♭ E♭)

C7alt (altered dominant): uses ♭9, ♯9, ♯11, ♭13 in various combinations
  Maximum tension before resolution to tonic
```

## Inversions and Figured Bass

**Root position:** Root is in the bass.
**1st inversion:** 3rd in the bass. More unstable than root position — wants to resolve.
**2nd inversion:** 5th in the bass. Even more unstable — limited contexts.
**3rd inversion (7th chords):** 7th in the bass. Strong tendency to resolve.

```
C major triad inversions:
  Root:         C E G    (root C in bass) → ⁵₃ in figured bass
  1st inv:      E G C    (3rd E in bass)  → ⁶₃ or just ⁶
  2nd inv:      G C E    (5th G in bass)  → ⁶₄

G7 seventh chord inversions:
  Root:         G B D F  (root G in bass) → ⁷
  1st inv:      B D F G  (3rd B in bass)  → ⁶₅
  2nd inv:      D F G B  (5th D in bass)  → ⁴₃
  3rd inv:      F G B D  (7th F in bass)  → ⁴₂ or ²

MOST COMMON INVERSIONS:
  I⁶  = 1st inversion of tonic — used for passing motion
  V⁶  = 1st inversion of dominant — softer approach to tonic
  I⁶₄ = 2nd inversion of tonic — CADENTIAL ⁶₄, precedes V7→I at cadences
  V⁴₂ = 3rd inversion of V7 — bass moves to tonic by step (F→E in key of C)
```

## Roman Numeral Analysis

Chord labeling relative to the key — reveals function regardless of specific pitches:

```
ROMAN NUMERAL CONVENTIONS:
  Uppercase = major quality (I, IV, V, II, VI, VII)
  Lowercase = minor quality (i, ii, iii, iv, v, vi, vii)
  ° = diminished
  + = augmented
  Superscript:
    7 = added seventh
    maj7 = added major seventh
    6, ⁶₃, ⁶₄ = inversions
    9, 11, 13 = extensions

EXAMPLES in C major:
  I → IV → V → I   : tonic-subdominant-dominant-tonic (basic progression)
  I → vi → IV → V  : "I-vi-IV-V" (50s progression — "Heart and Soul")
  ii → V → I       : the jazz ii-V-I cadence

EXAMPLES in A minor:
  i → iv → V7 → i  : minor ii-IV-V-i
  i → ♭VII → ♭VI → V: Andalusian cadence (flamenco)
  i → ♭VI → ♭III → ♭VII: "Minor descending" (many pop songs)
```

## Chord Function: T / PD / D

Every chord in tonal harmony has a **functional role**:

```
FUNCTION MAP (in C major):
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  TONIC (T)           PRE-DOMINANT (PD)      DOMINANT (D)     │
│  I, I⁶, vi, iii      ii, ii⁶, IV, IV⁶       V, V⁷, vii°      │
│                       II (secondary)          V of X         │
│                                                              │
│  Stable, at rest      Moving, heading          Tense, needs  │
│  "home base"          toward dominant          resolution    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Normal flow: T → PD → D → T
  Can skip PD: T → D → T
  Can stay at T: I → vi → I
  Can extend D: V → vii° → V7 → I
  Cannot go backward: D → PD is unusual, creates retrogression
```

## The ii–V–I Cadence

The most important progression in jazz, the backbone of all functional harmony:

```
In C major:   Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7
Roman:        ii7 → V7 → Imaj7
Function:     PD  → D  → T

WHY IT WORKS:
  G7 (dominant): B (M3, leading tone) wants → C
                 F (m7)                wants → E
                 These two voices converge from above and below into C major
  Dm7 prepares the G7 with stepwise motion in all voices

In minor (A minor): Bø7 → E7 → Am(maj7)
Roman:              iiø7 → V7 → i
  E7 requires the raised 7th (G♯) from harmonic minor
```

## Secondary Dominants

Any major chord can be preceded by its own V7, which temporarily "tonicizes" it:

```
SECONDARY DOMINANT: V7 of V, V7 of IV, V7 of ii, etc.

In C major:
  Normal V:    G7 → Cmaj7
  V/V:         D7 → G   (D major-minor 7th, the dominant of G)
  V/IV:        C7 → F   (C major-minor 7th — requires B♭, not in C major)
  V/ii:        A7 → Dm  (A dominant 7th)
  V/vi:        E7 → Am  (E dominant 7th)

NOTATION: V7/V, V7/IV, V7/ii, V7/vi, etc.
  The slash means "dominant of" — read V7/V as "five-seven of five"

The secondary dominant borrows the raised 7th from the tonicized chord's
  own major scale, creating a temporary sense of that chord as tonic.
  The move briefly leaves the key and returns → chromatic enrichment
```

## Deceptive Cadence

```
V resolves somewhere other than I:

V → vi  : DECEPTIVE cadence (most common)
  G7 → Am in C major
  The vi substitutes for I (shares 2 of 3 tones: Am = A C E, C = C E G)
  Creates surprise, continuation of phrase when I is expected

Other "deceptive" resolutions:
  V → ♭VI  : common in minor (E7 → F in A minor)
  V → IV6  : Brahms favored this surprise
  V → I⁶   : lands on 1st inversion tonic (slightly evasive)
```

## Modal Mixture (Borrowed Chords)

Borrowing chords from the parallel minor (or major) into the current key:

```
In C MAJOR, borrowing from C MINOR (parallel):
  ♭III: E♭ major chord → borrowed
  ♭VI:  A♭ major chord → common dark color in pop
  ♭VII: B♭ major chord → Mixolydian sound, very common in rock
  iv:   F minor chord  → poignant pre-dominant
  ii°:  B diminished chord (from minor)
  i°7 / vii°7 (dim7): fully diminished from harmonic minor

Common pop/rock progressions using mixture:
  I → ♭VII → IV → I   : "La Bamba," "Twist and Shout" — ♭VII = Mixolydian
  I → ♭VI → ♭VII → I  : "In My Life" (Beatles), countless others
  I → iv → I           : the "minor iv" — hauntingly beautiful
  I → ♭VI → ♭VII → i  : common minor-ending

The ♭VII chord (B♭ in C major) is among the most common borrowed chords in
rock and pop — it appears so frequently it barely feels "borrowed" anymore.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want... | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| Standard tension-resolution | ii–V–I |
| Brighten a minor passage | Raise the 7th (harmonic minor) → V chord |
| Dark color in major | ♭VI or ♭VII (modal mixture) |
| Hauntingly chromatic | Minor iv in major key |
| Tonicize a chord | Secondary dominant (V7 of that chord) |
| Surprise at cadence | Deceptive (V → vi) |
| Jazz sophistication | Add 9ths, 11ths, 13ths |
| Maximum altered tension | C7alt (♭9, ♯9, ♯11, ♭13) |
| Stable tonic in jazz | Imaj7 (not just I) |
| Label chords universally | Roman numerals relative to key |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Chord" vs "harmony":** A chord is a specific simultaneity. Harmony is the study of how chords relate and move over time. A single chord has no harmonic function in isolation — only context establishes function.

**Major 7th vs dominant 7th:** Cmaj7 has a M7 above root (B♮). C7 (dominant 7th) has a m7 (B♭). The dominant 7th creates tension because of the tritone between the 3rd and 7th (B♭ and E in G7). The major 7th is stable and consonant.

**The ♭7 in Mixolydian:** B♭ appears in C Mixolydian, making the I chord a natural dominant 7th (C-E-G-B♭). This is why rock and blues in a major key can use the "flat seven chord" — the mode is implicitly Mixolydian.

**Extensions: the 9th, 11th, 13th ARE 2, 4, 6:** The 9th is the same note class as the 2nd; the 11th is the 4th; the 13th is the 6th. The higher numbers indicate they appear above the 7th (in the second octave of the chord stack). Practically: a C9 chord contains a D but voiced above the B♭.
