# History Arc

## The Big Picture

Photography's history is the history of making light permanent — first in hours, then minutes, seconds, fractions. Each technical breakthrough democratized photography further: from daguerreotypes requiring trained operators and fixed studios, to the Brownie's "you press the button, we do the rest," to the smartphone where computation substitutes for every technical skill. The arc is not purely one of improvement; each era created distinctive aesthetics that later generations have tried to recover.

```
PHOTOGRAPHY HISTORY — TIMELINE:

  1826  Niépce: 8-hour exposure on pewter — first photograph
  1839  Daguerre: daguerreotype publicly announced (August 19 — World Photography Day)
  1839  Talbot: calotype (negative-positive) announced; different paradigm
  1851  Archer: wet collodion — 10-second exposures; revolution in speed
  1861  Maxwell: first color photograph (additive projection, tartan ribbon)
  1871  Maddox: gelatin dry plate — freed from darkroom tent
  1884  Eastman: flexible roll film
  1888  Kodak #1: roll film; mail-in processing; "You press the button"
  1900  Kodak Brownie: $1 camera; mass photography begins
  1907  Lumière Autochrome: first commercial color process
  1913  Leica prototype (Barnack): 35mm cine film in still camera
  1925  Leica Model I: commercial 35mm; handheld documentary photography
  1932  Ansel Adams: f/64 Group; straight photography; zone system
  1935  Kodachrome: first modern color film (3-layer; K-14 process)
  1936  35mm SLR (Exakta) precursor; 1954: Asahi Pentax — modern 35mm SLR
  1947  Land: Polaroid instant photography
  1948  Hasselblad 1600F: Swedish medium format SLR
  1963  Instamatic: Kodak 126 cartridge; easier loading
  1975  Sasson (Kodak): first digital camera prototype (0.01 MP, 23 seconds)
  1985  Minolta Maxxum 7000: first production autofocus SLR
  1991  Kodak DCS-100: first commercial digital SLR
  2000  Sharp J-SH04 (Japan): first camera phone
  2007  iPhone: capacitive touch; real UI; begins smartphone camera era
  2010  Instagram: social distribution defines the purpose of photography
  2016  Google Night Sight + Pixel: computational photography mainstream
  2022+ AI generation: Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, Midjourney — image synthesis without camera
```

## The Inventors: 1826–1851

### Niépce and the First Photograph

```
JOSEPH NICÉPHORE NIÉPCE (1765–1833):

THE VIEW FROM THE WINDOW AT LE GRAS (c.1826):
  Process: bitumen of Judea (light-sensitive asphalt) on pewter plate
  Camera obscura: focused image of window view at Niépce's estate
  Exposure time: approximately 8 hours (sun appears to illuminate both sides of
    courtyard in the image — moved from east to west during exposure)
  Development: polished with lavender oil + white petroleum → removes unexposed areas

  IMAGE: very dim; barely visible without enhancement
  → Modern digital enhancement reveals courtyard + buildings + trees
  → Oldest surviving photograph of a real scene

  CONTEXT: Niépce was trying to fix camera obscura images permanently
    Had experimented for 10+ years before this result
    Problem: bitumen requires very long exposure times → impractical for portraits
```

### Daguerre vs. Talbot

```
DAGUERREOTYPE (Louis Daguerre, 1839):

PROCESS:
  Silver-plated copper sheet → fumed with iodine → AgI (light-sensitive layer)
  Expose in camera: latent image formed (Gurney-Mott mechanism)
  Develop: fumes of heated mercury → HgAg amalgam at exposed sites → visible image
  Fix: sodium thiosulfate ("hypo") → remove unexposed AgI → image permanent
  Result: direct positive image; unique; no copies possible

PROPERTIES:
  Resolution: extraordinary (50+ lp/mm possible); detail rivals modern film
  Tone: continuous; no grain → "mirror with memory"
  Exposure time: first years: 15–30 minutes in direct sunlight (impractical for portraits)
    By 1841: improvements → 1–3 minutes → portraiture practical
    By 1850: ~10 seconds with better lenses + faster plates
  Size: common sizes: 1/6 plate (8×7cm), 1/4 plate (10×8cm), whole plate (21×17cm)

UNIQUE CHARACTERISTICS:
  No negative → unique object (like a painting); cannot be copied
  Laterally reversed image (mirror image) unless prism used
  Must be tilted under light to see image (reflection-based; not transmission)
  Very fragile: dust or touch destroys surface
  Often hand-colored (miniature painters adapted to daguerreotype)

CALOTYPE (William Henry Fox Talbot, 1841):

  Paper negative: sensitized with silver iodide + silver nitrate
  Expose → calotype paper turns darker (Ag⁰ forming at exposed sites)
  Develop: gallic acid → amplifies latent image (Talbot discovered development before Daguerre announced)
  Fix: sodium thiosulfate → permanent
  Print: make contact print from paper negative → positive paper print

PROPERTIES:
  Resolution: much lower than daguerreotype (paper fibers scatter light → soft)
  Tone: inherent paper texture + soft focus → painterly aesthetic
  FUNDAMENTAL ADVANTAGE: multiple prints from one negative → reproducibility
    → Prototype for all subsequent photography (negative-positive process)
  Exposure time: similar to daguerreotype

THE GREAT PATENT DISPUTE:
  Daguerre: French government bought his process → free to the world
  Talbot: patented calotype aggressively in UK → suppressed commercial use
  → Britain fell behind Europe and America in photography partly due to Talbot's patents
  → Fox Talbot is the "inventor of photography" in the intellectual sense (process + printing)
    but Daguerre "won" in contemporary impact and public imagination
```

### Wet Collodion — Civil War Photography

```
WET COLLODION (Frederick Scott Archer, 1851):

PROCESS:
  Collodion (gun cotton dissolved in ether + alcohol) poured on glass plate
  → Dip in silver nitrate → AgI + AgBr forms in collodion matrix
  Expose while still wet (3–15 minutes working time!)
  Develop immediately: pyrogallic acid → silver image in collodion
  Fix: sodium thiosulfate or potassium cyanide
  Result: glass negative (ambrotype — backed with black = appears positive)
         or tintype (on iron — ferotype)

PROPERTIES:
  Exposure time: 5–15 seconds in good light → portraits, street scenes practical
  Sensitivity: ~10× faster than calotype; comparable to slow modern film
  Resolution: excellent (glass base; no paper fibers)
  Cost: much cheaper than daguerreotype

LIMITATIONS:
  Must be coated → exposed → developed while wet (15 min window)
  → Photographer must carry complete darkroom → "wet plate wagon"
  → Large portable tent darkrooms in field
  → Civil War: Brady, Gardner, O'Sullivan → historic documentation
                But: could only photograph static scenes (troop movements, battlefields
                after battle, not during → too slow + logistically impossible)

CIVIL WAR DOCUMENTATION:
  Mathew Brady: organized teams; sent Gardner, O'Sullivan into field
  Alexander Gardner: more field photography; left Brady 1863; published own album
  Timothy O'Sullivan: extreme field conditions; photographs of Gettysburg dead
  → These photographs: first comprehensive visual documentation of a modern war
  → "The Dead of Antietam" (Brady gallery, 1862): public sees war dead for first time
    → Enormous psychological impact; photography as anti-war statement
    BUT: often staged (Gardner moved bodies; posed scenes at Gettysburg)
```

## Democratization: 1871–1935

### Dry Plate and Roll Film

```
GELATIN DRY PLATE (Richard Leach Maddox, 1871):
  Replaced collodion with gelatin (protein) as silver halide binder
  Dry plate: could be manufactured, stored, used days later → no field darkroom!
  Manufacturing: plates made in factories → distributed to photographers
  → Freed photography from the wet-plate mobile darkroom

  George Eastman: started manufacturing dry plates 1880 → highly successful
  Eastman improvements: flexible nitrocellulose film roll (1884)
    → Replace glass → much lighter; can be loaded in daylight-loading cartridge

KODAK #1 (1888):
  "You press the button, we do the rest"
  100-exposure roll film camera; amateur operation
  After exposing: send entire camera to Kodak → developed, printed, camera reloaded
  Price: $25 (high for 1888) → reduced over time

KODAK BROWNIE (1900):
  Price: $1; film: 15¢ per roll
  Simple box camera; fixed focus; single shutter speed
  Design: Frank Brownell; named from Palmer Cox's "Brownies" cartoon characters
  → Brought photography to mass market; over 1 million units in first years
  → "Snapshot culture" begins: recording family life, vacations, everyday moments
  → The beginning of photography as social medium rather than professional art/document
```

### Color Photography Milestones

```
EARLY COLOR:
  Maxwell, 1861: additive synthesis — photographed tartan ribbon through R, G, B filters
    → Project three images simultaneously → color (imperfect; not truly panchromatic)
    → Demonstration of color principle; not practical

  AUTOCHROME LUMIÈRE (1907):
    First commercially successful color process
    Potato starch grains: R, G, B dyed; spread on glass plate; topped with panchromatic emulsion
    Result: mosaic color screen + B&W emulsion → additive color
    Characteristics: soft; impressionistic; muted colors; warm palette
    → Used by: Steichen, Stieglitz, early photojournalists
    Production: 1907–1935 (millions of plates made)

KODACHROME (1935):
  Leopold Godowsky Jr. + Leopold Mannes ("God and Money") → developed at Kodak
  First modern 3-layer integral tripack color film
  Each layer sensitive to different primary (with filter layers)
  IMPORTANT: couplers NOT in film (unlike all later films)
  → Couplers introduced in K-14 development process → 14-step complex processing
  → Only Kodak could process (control of couplers in chemistry)
  → Extraordinary archival stability: properly stored Kodachrome slides: 100+ years

  Visual qualities:
    Rich saturation; distinctive reds; fine grain; low ISO (64 typical)
    "Kodachrome moment": warm, saturated, memory-like rendering
    Paul Simon: "You give us those nice bright colors / those greens of summers..."
  Discontinued: 2010 (last processing lab: Dwayne's Photos, Parsons KS)
```

## Technical Revolutions: 1925–1980

### 35mm and Handheld Photography

```
LEICA M SERIES (Barnack prototype 1913; Model I commercial 1925):
  Oskar Barnack: used 35mm cinema film → compact camera
  Motivations: Barnack asthmatic → couldn't carry large format; wanted lightweight
  Design: rangefinder focusing; accurate; compact; no mirror box
  Initial resistance: serious photographers scoffed at "miniature" format
  → Erich Salomon: used Ermanox then Leica → candid portraits of diplomats/politicians
    → Founded photojournalism genre (available light; small camera; unposed)
  → Henri Cartier-Bresson: decisive moment philosophy; Leica as extension of eye
  → Leica normalized: small, portable, inconspicuous as tool for documentary

MAGNUM PHOTOS (founded 1947):
  Cartier-Bresson, Robert Capa, David Seymour, George Rodger
  First photographer-owned cooperative agency
  → Photographers retain copyright; agents sell rights
  → Defined photojournalism ethics + aesthetics of 20th century
```

### Polaroid Instant

```
POLAROID (Edwin Land, 1947 invention; 1948 first sale):

LAND'S INSIGHT:
  His daughter asked: "Why can't I see the picture now?"
  → Land worked out complete system in conceptual sketch in one day (January 21, 1945)
  → Commercial development: 3 years

CHEMISTRY (peel-apart prints, SX-70 integral):
  Peel-apart (1948–1972):
    Negative + positive sheet + pod of reagent (alkali + developer + thickener)
    Pull through rollers → pod bursts → reagent spreads between sheets
    Processing occurs between the sheets (diffusion transfer)
    Peel apart after ~1–2 minutes → discard negative → keep positive

  SX-70 INTEGRAL (1972 — revolutionary):
    All-in-one unit: no waste, no peeling
    10-exposure cassette; motor ejects print; develops in light on the surface
    Opacification layer: prevents further exposure as print exits camera
    Processing: 3–5 minutes in room temperature
    → Became cultural icon; Eames chairs + SX-70 = 1970s aesthetic

CULTURAL IMPACT:
  Instant photography: first medium where photographer could verify result immediately
  → Useful for commercial (client approval), ID photos, medical, Polaroid parties
  → Warhol: hundreds of Polaroids as preliminary sketches; subjects as social ritual
  → "Shake it like a Polaroid picture" (OutKast): actually bad advice — shaking causes
     unevenness in processing; let it develop face-down in darkness

DECLINE AND BANKRUPTCY:
  Digital cameras: killed the "instant" advantage → 2001 bankruptcy, 2008 closure
  → Polaroid Originals (now Polaroid): restarted; new 600/SX-70 film compatible
  → Fujifilm Instax: enormous contemporary market; most popular instant today
```

## The Digital Transition: 1975–2010

### Kodak's Paradox

```
KODAK'S DIGITAL INVENTION AND SUICIDE:

SASSON'S FIRST DIGITAL CAMERA (1975):
  Steve Sasson, Kodak engineer: built first digital camera from image sensor component
  Fairchild CCD: 0.01 megapixel (100×100 = 10,000 pixels)
  Storage: magnetic cassette tape; 23 seconds to store one image
  Readback: specialized screen
  Size: bread box

  Sasson presented to Kodak management:
  Response: "That's cute, but don't tell anyone about it"
  → Executives' concern: digital cameras would cannibalize film sales
  → Film: Kodak's entire profit center; $1B/year profit from film in the 1990s

THE KODAK TRAP (Innovator's Dilemma):
  Kodak invented digital photography; had all the technology; had the brand
  → Couldn't commercialize it because:
    Each digital camera sold → one fewer film user → direct profit loss
    Film was 75% gross margin; camera hardware was lower margin
    → Digital = destroying their profit engine with lower-margin business
  → Every business case showed digital was a bad deal for Kodak

  Simultaneously: Sony, Canon, Nikon, then Samsung and Apple
    → Had nothing to lose from film → invested in digital aggressively

TIMELINE OF DECLINE:
  1991: Kodak DCS-100 (first DSLR, $13,000) — Kodak invented it but couldn't scale
  1994: Apple QuickTake 100 (partnership with Kodak) — consumer digital
  2001: Kodak profit peaks; digital camera sales surpassing film for first time
  2004: Kodak exits camera business entirely; focuses on printing
  2012: Kodak files Chapter 11 bankruptcy
  2013: Emerges from bankruptcy (much smaller company)

LESSON:
  Kodak is the canonical Innovator's Dilemma (Christensen, 1997) case
  → Disruption came from below (cheap digital) → Kodak couldn't respond
  → Rational management of existing business + rational rejection of disruption = death
```

### Digital Sensor Race

```
DIGITAL CAMERA WARS (1995–2010):

  CONSUMER:
    1994: Apple QuickTake 100 — VGA resolution; 8 frames in memory
    1995: Casio QV-10 — first with color LCD screen (huge UX change)
    1997: Olympus D-600L — 1.4MP; mainstream quality threshold beginning
    1999: Nikon Coolpix 950 — 2.1MP; "good enough" moment for many consumers

  PROFESSIONAL:
    1999: Nikon D1 — first affordable pro DSLR ($5,500; 2.7MP)
    2002: Canon EOS-1Ds — first full-frame DSLR (11.1MP; $8,000)
    2004: Canon 20D (8.2MP, $1,500) — crossing quality threshold for enthusiasts
    2008: Nikon D90 — first DSLR with video recording (major market shift)

  SMARTPHONE:
    2000: Sharp J-SH04 (Japan, 0.1MP) — first camera phone
    2007: iPhone — single fixed lens; 2MP; but UI + connectivity > quality
    2010: iPhone 4 — 5MP; 720p video; HDR — serious photography
    2012: Nokia 808 PureView — 41MP; computational zoom; peak of dedicated phone cameras
    2016: iPhone 7 Plus — dual camera + portrait mode; phone photography revolution
    2020+: computational photography overtakes optical in mainstream

FILE FORMAT WARS:
  JPEG vs RAW: Canon, Nikon, Sony all had proprietary RAW formats
  → 2004: Adobe DNG (Digital Negative) — open format proposal
  → Nikon, Canon: refuse to adopt DNG; maintain proprietary NEF, CR2/CR3
  → Most RAW software reads all formats; interoperability solved via software, not standards
```

## The Instagram Era and Beyond

```
SOCIAL PHOTOGRAPHY (2010–present):

  INSTAGRAM (launched 2010; Facebook acquired 2012 for $1B):
    Key innovation: square format + filters → aesthetic democratization
    Filters: Earlybird, Valencia, Lo-Fi → applied 1970s film aesthetics to phone photos
    → The "look" became more important than technical quality
    → Defined visual culture of the 2010s: high contrast, warm tones, shallow DOF

  SMARTPHONE CAMERA WARS:
    DXOMark scores: manufacturers compete on sensor benchmarks
    Computational photography arms race:
      Google Pixel Night Sight (2018) → industry benchmark for low light
      Huawei P30 Pro (2019) — periscope telephoto + low light
      iPhone 12 Pro — LiDAR; ProRes; Night Mode portraits
      Samsung Galaxy S22 Ultra — Space Zoom 100×; 108MP sensor
    → 2020+: all major phones within 10% of each other; computation > optics

  AI PHOTOGRAPHY (2022+):
    Stable Diffusion (open source), DALL-E 3, Midjourney:
    Text → image; no camera required
    → "Photography" increasingly a subset of "visual image creation"
    → Authenticity crisis: photographic document no longer reliable evidence
    → Photojournalism standards: cameras/originals required; AI generation banned
    → Wedding/commercial: AI enhancement of real photos = accepted; AI replacement = contested

FILM REVIVAL:
  Film sales: declining 1990–2010 → stabilizing then growing 2015+
  Kodak: relaunches Ektachrome (2018); increases Portra production
  Fujifilm: only major manufacturer not to exit film completely
  35mm film: +200% growth in sales 2016–2022 (mostly 18–35 year olds)
  → Authenticity (physical process); aesthetic (grain, color rendering);
    intentionality (no instant review, finite frames); social distinction

THE ARCHIVAL PARADOX:
  Film era: prints and negatives survive if stored well → Ansel Adams negatives still printable
  Digital era:
    Proprietary formats: older RAW files may not be readable in 20 years
    Storage: HDDs fail; USB drives degrade; CDs delaminate; servers close down
    Overabundance: trillion+ photos per year; how to curate?
    → Archivists' recommendation: print physical + DNG + multiple backup copies
    → Film originals: 100+ year survival with proper storage
    → Digital originals: uncertain beyond 20–30 years without active management
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Era | Key capability | Key limitation |
|-----|---------------|----------------|
| Daguerreotype (1839–1860) | Extraordinary detail | Unique; no copies; toxic mercury |
| Calotype (1841–1855) | Multiple prints from negative | Soft; paper texture; patented |
| Wet collodion (1851–1880) | Speed (seconds); resolution | Mobile darkroom required |
| Dry plate/roll film (1871–1935) | Store + ship film; factory production | Low color capability; large camera |
| Kodachrome (1935–2010) | Saturated color; archival stability | Complex processing; Kodak only |
| 35mm SLR (1954–2005) | Versatility; system; image quality | Cost; film cost; lab required |
| Digital SLR (1991–2020) | Instant feedback; no film cost | Mirror complexity; bulk |
| Mirrorless (2008–present) | Silent; small; computational | EVF lag (historical); learning curve |
| Smartphone (2010–present) | Always available; sharing | Small sensor; physics limits |

## Common Confusion Points

**Daguerre "won" but Talbot invented modern photography**
The daguerreotype had better image quality and dominated the market for portraits 1839–1855. But Talbot's calotype — negative + positive — is the conceptual basis of all subsequent photography (film, digital). Every film photograph ever made works on Talbot's principle. Every digital sensor that captures data for later processing follows the same logic. The daguerreotype was a dead end aesthetically and technically.

**Kodak invented digital photography and still went bankrupt**
This is one of the most important case studies in technology disruption. Kodak had the technology, the brand, the distribution, the customer relationships, and the engineering talent. They failed because digital cannibalized film, their core profit source, and they couldn't cannibalize themselves. The lesson is that rational management of an existing business can rationally avoid the innovation that would save it.

**Film is not objectively inferior to digital at all tasks**
At equivalent cost (medium format digital vs. 4×5 film), large format film can exceed digital in certain characteristics. More importantly, film's physical process, its characteristic grain, color rendering, and the constraint of finite frames creates different behaviors and aesthetics. The "film revival" is not nostalgia alone — it's a response to the infinite, consequence-free, screen-mediated nature of digital photography.

**The "decisive moment" is a translation artifact**
Cartier-Bresson's book was titled "Images à la Sauvette" (Images on the Sly / Stolen Images) in French. The English publisher retitled it "The Decisive Moment." The phrase comes from Cartier-Bresson's introductory quote from Cardinal de Retz — but it became the defining phrase of photojournalism philosophy in a way the French title wouldn't have. The English title has shaped how his work has been interpreted more than the French original.

**Smartphone computational photography is not "cheating"**
Every photograph since the 1880s has involved optical, chemical, or digital processing. The question of what counts as "photography" has always been contested. Ansel Adams' zone system involved extensive darkroom manipulation. Every printed newspaper photograph was retouched before 1970. Multi-frame computational photography is a new form of processing — the argument about authenticity is a legitimate aesthetic discussion, not an objective technical distinction.
