# Theater and Performance — Overview and Landscape

## The Big Picture

Theater is the art of live performance before an audience. It is distinct from recorded performance (cinema, television) by its liveness — the irreducible present-tense co-presence of performers and audience. Performance studies expanded this definition to include any behavior that is "performed" in a broader sense — from ritual to political speechmaking to everyday social behavior.

```

             Thespis (first actor, 534 BCE — tradition)
             Aeschylus introduces 2nd actor

500-400 BCE  Classical period: Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes
             City Dionysia festival (civic institution)
             Theater of Dionysus, Athens (14,000 seats)

400-100 BCE  Hellenistic period: New Comedy (Menander)
             spread of Greek theater throughout Mediterranean

200 BCE-400  Roman theater: Plautus, Terence (comedy)
             Seneca (tragedy for reading, not performance)
             Mime and pantomime (popular performance)

400-900      Collapse of professional theater in Western Europe
             Church forbids theatrical performance (400s+)
             Folk performance traditions persist

900-1400     Liturgical drama: tropes, mystery plays
             Quem Quaeritis — earliest surviving text
             Cycles: York, Chester, Coventry (200+ plays)
             Commedia dell'arte emerges (c. 1550)

1550-1700    Renaissance theater: Elizabethan (1576+)
             Professional public theater (Globe, 1599)
             Spanish Golden Age (Lope, Calderon)
             Italian perspective scenery (Serlio 1545+)
             Opera origins: Florentine Camerata (1598)

1700-1800    French neoclassicism + Enlightenment
             Italian opera seria, buffa
             English sentiment and comedy

1800-1900    Romanticism, melodrama, the "well-made play"
             Naturalism: Ibsen, Strindberg, Chekhov
             The proscenium arch becomes dominant

1900-1960    Expressionism, symbolism, Brecht's epic theater
             Theatre of the Absurd (Beckett, Ionesco)
             Stanislavski system, Method acting

             Postmodern theater (Wilson, Foreman)
             Immersive theater (Punchdrunk, Sleep No More)
```

## What Makes Theater "Live"

The liveness question is not trivial — it is the theoretical center of performance studies:

```
LIVENESS — THE THEORETICAL DEBATE
------------------------------------
Philip Auslander "Liveness" (1999):
  Challenges the simple claim that live performance
  is "original" and recording is derivative.
  Argues: our sense of "liveness" is itself shaped
  by mediatization. We compare live performance
  to recorded performance; the live event is
  experienced through the lens of the recorded.
  There is no pure, unmediated "live" event.

Peggy Phelan "Unmarked" (1993):
  The opposing view: performance's power is in
  its DISAPPEARANCE. Performance's only life is
  in the present. It cannot be saved, recorded,
  documented without becoming something other than
  performance.
  "Performance's only life is in the present."

The practical stakes:
  What is lost when a stage production is filmed?
    Peripheral vision, spatial immersion, the breath
    of the actor, the sense of shared risk, the
    possibility that something will go wrong.
  What is preserved?
    Script, blocking, lighting design, the specific
    performances. Documentation, not performance.

RESTORED BEHAVIOR (Schechner):
  All performance is "twice-behaved behavior" —
  it is behavior stored, rehearsed, and re-enacted.
  Even "spontaneous" performance follows patterns.
  This connects to the question of "live vs. recorded":
  if all performance is stored and repeated, the
  distinction between live and recorded is a matter
  of degree and context, not category.
```

## Theater and the Social

Theater has a social function that goes beyond entertainment:

```
THEATER AS CIVIC INSTITUTION
------------------------------
Athens (5th c. BCE):
  The City Dionysia was a STATE festival — performances
  were civic acts. Tribute from allied cities was displayed.
  Generals poured libations. The orphans of war dead
  were paraded before the performance.
  The plays WERE civic deliberation in dramatic form.
  Tragedy asked: what are the limits of human knowledge?
  Of divine justice? Of political power?

Medieval Mystery Cycles:
  Performed by craft guilds (guild = trade association).
  The Shipwrights' Guild performed Noah's Ark.
  The Crucifixion was performed by the Pinners (nail-makers).
  Theater as civic pedagogy: the Bible enacted for
  an illiterate population.

Brecht's political theater (20th c.):
  Explicitly: theater as instrument of political
  consciousness-raising. "Don't weep; change the world."

Theater of the Oppressed (Boal, 1970s):
  Forum theater: a scene of oppression is staged;
  the audience can stop it, replace the protagonist,
  try a different action. Rehearsal for revolution.
  Applied in community organizing, prisons, hospitals.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Theater" vs. "theatre"**
Both spellings are correct; "theatre" is standard in UK/Australia; "theater" in US. The word derives from Greek "theatron" (viewing place). No meaningful distinction.

**Drama (text) vs. Theater (performance)**
Drama is the written text; theater is the performed event. Many plays work as literary texts (read); great plays also work in performance. The distinction matters when a text written for performance is taught only as literature (Shakespeare read vs. Shakespeare staged are different experiences).

**Performance studies is not the same as theater studies**
Theater studies focuses on the theatrical art form. Performance studies (associated with Richard Schechner, the NYU program he founded) is interdisciplinary — it uses theater, anthropology, linguistics, and sociology to study all forms of performed behavior. Theater is a subset of its object; a political rally is also in its purview.

**The "fourth wall" is a specific metaphor**
The "fourth wall" refers to the invisible wall between performers and audience in proscenium theater — the three walls of the set plus the imaginary one at the front. "Breaking the fourth wall" means acknowledging the audience. This only works as a metaphor in proscenium staging; in a thrust stage or round, the concept doesn't apply cleanly.
