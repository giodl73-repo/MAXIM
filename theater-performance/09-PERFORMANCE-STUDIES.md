# Performance Studies

## The Big Picture

Performance studies emerged as an academic field in the 1970s (associated with Richard Schechner at NYU and the journal TDR/The Drama Review) as an expansion of theater studies to encompass all forms of "performed" behavior — from ritual to sport to political rallies to everyday social interaction. Its intellectual roots are in anthropology (Victor Turner), linguistics (Austin's speech act theory), and theater practice.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

+------------------------------------------------------------------+

## Layer 1: Victor Turner — Social Drama and Liminality

Victor Turner (1920–1983) was a British anthropologist who studied Ndembu ritual in Zambia and developed concepts that became foundational for performance studies.

### The Social Drama

```
TURNER'S SOCIAL DRAMA
-----------------------
Turner observed that social conflict follows a
recognizable four-phase structure — a "social drama":

Phase 1: BREACH
  A violation of the normative rules that govern
  social relations. A breach of social contract.
  Can be deliberate (rebellion) or inadvertent.

Phase 2: CRISIS
  The breach deepens; schism opens in the community.
  It cannot be ignored. The community must respond.

Phase 3: REDRESSIVE ACTION
  The community applies recognized procedures to
  address the crisis:
  - Informal: gossip, mediation, shaming
  - Formal: legal/judicial processes
  - Ritual: ceremony, sacrifice, public performance
  The redressive phase is where PERFORMANCE emerges —
  the community performs its own values to resolve the breach.

Phase 4: REINTEGRATION OR SCHISM
  Either the breach is healed (reintegration; the community
  is reconstituted) or the schism becomes permanent
  (recognition of irreparable breach; division).

TURNER'S INSIGHT:
  Theater and ritual are the same kind of activity —
  they are the REDRESSIVE PHASE formalized.
  Tragedy reenacts and performs the community's breach
  and potential reintegration.
  Greek tragedy functioned exactly as the social drama
  formalized and made public.
  Courts of law are social dramas.
  Political elections are social dramas.
  Corporate restructurings are social dramas.
```

### Liminality and Communitas

Van Gennep (1909) described rites of passage as three-phase: separation, liminality, reincorporation. Turner developed the concept of liminality:

```
LIMINALITY
-----------
"Limen" = Latin for threshold.
The liminal phase is the BETWEEN:
  You have left one state (childhood, bachelorhood,
  civilian life) but not yet arrived at the next
  (adulthood, marriage, soldier).

  During liminality:
  - Normal structures are suspended
  - The initiate is "neither here nor there"
  - Normal hierarchies may be inverted
  - Anything can happen (that is the point)
  - Transformation is possible

  Examples:
  - Wedding ceremony (between single and married)
  - Military boot camp (between civilian and soldier)
  - Carnival (between ordinary time and its suspension)
  - Theater (between everyday life and the performance)
  - Hazing rituals (between outsider and member)

COMMUNITAS:
  In the liminal phase, a different kind of social bond
  emerges: communitas. Not a structured hierarchy but
  a direct, unmediated connection between human beings.
  "We are all, for this moment, equal."

  Turner contrasts:
  Structure (the everyday social hierarchy,
    roles, statuses, rules) vs.
  Communitas (the undifferentiated togetherness
    possible in the liminal phase).

  Performance generates communitas between performers
  and audience. The theater audience shares communitas
  during the performance — temporarily freed from
  their social roles and statuses, united in the
  shared experience.

LIMINOID:
  Turner distinguished liminal (obligatory ritual in
  traditional society) from liminoid (optional, peripheral,
  leisure activities in industrial/post-industrial society).
  Theater, art, carnival in modern societies are liminoid —
  we choose them; they are at the edges of social life;
  they can be more subversive precisely because they are
  optional.
```

## Layer 2: Schechner's Restored Behavior

Richard Schechner (b. 1934) is the institutional founder of performance studies as an academic discipline. His conceptual contribution is the concept of "restored behavior":

```
RESTORED BEHAVIOR
-----------------
Schechner's key concept: all performance is "twice-behaved
behavior" — behavior that has been stored, trained,
rehearsed, and re-enacted.

"Restored behavior is living behavior treated as a
film director treats a strip of film. These strips of
behavior can be rearranged or reconstructed; they are
independent of the causal systems (personal, social,
political, technological) that brought them into existence."

TYPES OF RESTORED BEHAVIOR:
  1. Theatrical performance: obviously rehearsed and repeated
  2. Ritual: actions performed in prescribed ways
  3. Sports: trained movements within rules
  4. Social behavior: daily routines, greetings, ceremonies
     (we "perform" our social roles)
  5. Dreams: Turner included dreams as performances of
     a kind; Schechner is more cautious here

THE IMPLICATIONS:
  1. There is no single "original" performance.
     Every performance is a restoration. Even the
     "first" performance was based on rehearsal.

  2. "Authentic" behavior and "performed" behavior
     are not categorically different.
     The actor performing grief and the person grieving
     at a funeral are both performing — one more
     consciously, both drawing on stored behavioral repertoire.

  3. "Live vs. recorded" distinction is complicated.
     If all behavior is stored and restored, the live
     performance is already a form of playback.

  4. Culture is transmitted through restored behavior.
     Ritual, ceremony, dance, theater are all mechanisms
     for storing and transmitting cultural behavior across generations.

CONNECT: memory systems
  Before writing: ritual and performance ARE the memory system.
  The oral epic is stored behavior. The Noh play is stored behavior.
  The Catholic Mass is stored behavior.
  Performance is an archive — a different kind of archive
  from writing, but an archive.
```

### Environmental Theater

```
SCHECHNER'S ENVIRONMENTAL THEATER
------------------------------------
Schechner's 6 Axioms (1968):
  1. The theatrical event is a set of related
     transactions between performers and audience.
  2. All the space is used for performance; all the
     space is used for audience.
  3. The theatrical event can take place either in
     a totally transformed space or in a "found" space.
  4. Focus is flexible and variable.
  5. All production elements speak their own language.
  6. The text need be neither the starting point nor
     the goal of a production.

The Living Theatre (Julian Beck and Judith Malina):
  Politically engaged; performances as political events.
  Audience and performers share same space.
  Paradise Now (1968): performers circulated through
  audience; audience could participate or leave;
  "the performance is a journey" — no fixed beginning,
  middle, end.

The Grotowski connection:
  Jerzy Grotowski's "Poor Theater" principle:
  Theater needs NOTHING except an actor and a spectator.
  Strip away sets, costumes, music, lighting — the actor's
  body is sufficient and all else is supplementary.
  This is the theatrical minimum, defining the essential.
  (Grotowski and Schechner were friends and mutual influences)
```

## Layer 3: Speech Act Theory and Performativity

### Austin: Performative Speech Acts

J.L. Austin's **How to Do Things with Words** (1962) introduced the distinction that became central to performance studies:

<!-- @editor[bridge/P2]: Austin's performative speech act theory has a direct structural parallel to type theory and formal verification that's worth noting for this learner — a performative is an utterance whose semantic content IS its execution; felicity conditions are preconditions/invariants; an infelicitous performative is a precondition failure. The distinction constative/performative maps roughly to denotational vs. operational semantics: a constative describes a world-state; a performative changes it. Any MIT TCS graduate will recognize the formal structure immediately; naming the parallel would make this section richer in one sentence. -->
```
AUSTIN'S SPEECH ACT THEORY
----------------------------
CONSTATIVE vs. PERFORMATIVE utterances:

CONSTATIVE: a statement of fact that can be true or false.
  "The cat is on the mat." (True or false; can be checked)

PERFORMATIVE: an utterance that DOES SOMETHING rather
  than describing something.
  "I name this ship The Queen Mary." (The naming IS the act)
  "I now pronounce you husband and wife." (The pronouncing IS the marriage)
  "I promise to pay you." (The promising IS the obligation)
  "I hereby declare war." (The declaring IS the declaration)

These utterances cannot be true or false — they are
FELICITOUS (successful) or INFELICITOUS (failed).

FELICITY CONDITIONS for a performative:
  1. There must be an accepted conventional procedure.
  2. The circumstances must be appropriate.
  3. The procedure must be executed correctly.
  4. The participants must have the appropriate intentions and feelings.
  5. The participants must follow through.

If these conditions are not met, the performative is VOID.
  "I now pronounce you husband and wife" said by
  someone with no authority → not married.
  "I promise" said insincerely → hollow promise
    (but interestingly: still a promise in some sense)

Austin later realized ALL utterances have performative aspects
(illocutionary force) — the distinction constative/performative
is not clean. This led him to the full speech act theory:
  Locutionary act: the act of saying something
  Illocutionary act: what you do IN saying it (warn, promise, etc.)
  Perlocutionary act: what happens AS A RESULT (the effect)
```

### Butler: Gender Performativity

```
JUDITH BUTLER'S GENDER PERFORMATIVITY
----------------------------------------
Gender Trouble (1990), Bodies That Matter (1993)

Butler's thesis:
  Gender is not a pre-existing fact that one expresses.
  Gender is PRODUCED through repeated, stylized acts.
  There is no gender "essence" behind the performance;
  the performance IS gender.

The citation model (drawing on Austin and Derrida):
  Performative speech acts work through CITATION —
  they cite prior speech acts. "I pronounce you
  husband and wife" works because it cites all the
  prior times this phrase (or its equivalent) was
  used in the appropriate institutional context.

  Similarly: gender acts cite prior gender acts.
  "Feminine" behavior is a citation of a code of
  femininity; "masculine" behavior cites a code of masculinity.
  There is no original; every citation cites a citation.

IMPLICATIONS:
  If gender is performance, it can be SUBVERTED through
  a different kind of performance.
  Drag, for example, makes the citational structure visible:
  a man in drag CITES femininity while making visible
  that it is a citation. This denaturalizes what
  normally passes as natural.

  Butler is NOT saying: "gender is just a costume you can
  change freely." The citations are compelled; failing
  to perform gender correctly has real social consequences.
  The compulsion is real even if the foundation is not.

THE PERFORMANCE STUDIES CONNECTION:
  Butler draws on performance theory (Austin, Derrida,
  Schechner) to argue that gender is produced through
  repeated performance. Performance studies theorists
  (especially feminist and queer scholars) have used
  Butler's framework to analyze how performance can
  naturalize or denaturalize social norms.
```

## Layer 4: Goffman — The Social Performance

Erving Goffman (1922–1982) developed a sociology of everyday life using theatrical metaphor:

```
GOFFMAN'S DRAMATURGICAL ANALYSIS
----------------------------------
The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life (1959)

Goffman's claim:
  Social life is structured like theatrical performance.
  We present carefully managed versions of ourselves
  to different audiences in different settings.

FRONT STAGE vs. BACK STAGE:
  Front stage: the performance space; where we present
    our managed social self; appropriate behavior is
    performed.
  Back stage: where we relax from performance; where
    the "real" self (if there is one) can emerge;
    where costumes are changed and props are stored.

  Examples:
    A restaurant: the dining room is front stage
    (waiters perform hospitality); the kitchen is back
    stage (the performance is prepared and dropped).

    A hospital: the doctor's bedside manner is front stage;
    the staff room discussion of the patient is back stage.

PROPS, COSTUMES, SETTING:
  Goffman uses this theatrical vocabulary literally:
  physical objects (props) support the performance;
  clothing (costume) signals identity; the spatial
  arrangement of the room (setting) structures interaction.

IMPRESSION MANAGEMENT:
  We constantly manage what impression others form of us.
  This is not deception — it is the normal structure of
  social interaction. All social interaction involves
  a degree of performance.

  Disruptions: when the performance breaks down
  (spilling something, forgetting a name, saying the
  wrong thing in the wrong context) → embarrassment,
  remediation, face-saving strategies.

CONNECT: leadership and management
  For a VP-level reader:
  Executive communication IS front-stage performance.
  The "all-hands" meeting, the board presentation, the
  team standup — these are performances with audiences,
  props, settings, and managed impressions.
  Goffman's framework makes this explicit and analyzable
  rather than implicit and unreflective.
```

## Layer 5: Immersive Theater and Contemporary Performance

```
IMMERSIVE THEATER
-----------------
The audience moves through a designed space and may
interact with performers, explore at their own pace,
and experience different narrative fragments.

Punchdrunk (UK, founded 2000 by Felix Barrett):
  The dominant contemporary immersive company.
  Sleep No More (New York, 2011-present):
    Based on Macbeth; set in a multi-floor, multi-room
    "hotel" (the McKittrick Hotel, actually a warehouse).
    Audience wears white masks; performers do not.
    The story unfolds simultaneously in many spaces.
    Audience chooses where to go; no two experiences
    are identical.
    ~400 performers; ~100,000 objects in the set.

  THE FORMAL PROPERTIES:
    - No fixed beginning/middle/end for any one audience member
    - The "show" is non-linear; you construct your version
    - Performer:audience ratio much higher than conventional theater
    - The audience is anonymous (masked); the performers are "real"
    - The spatial design is total; every room is designed in depth
    - Duration: 3 hours; audience can stay or leave

  Compare: hypertext narrative (you choose your path);
  open-world game (you explore at your own pace);
  both analogies have been used.

DURATIONAL PERFORMANCE:
  Marina Abramovic (b. 1946): the most prominent live art practitioner.
  The Artist Is Present (MoMA, 2010): Abramovic sat in the
    atrium for 3 months during museum hours. Visitors could
    sit across from her and make eye contact.
    736 hours total; 1,500 people sat across from her.
    "The piece is not about showing; it is about presence."
  The body as the medium; duration as the form;
  the encounter between artist and viewer as the content.
```

## Performance Studies and the "Live vs. Recorded" Debate

```
THE LIVENESS DEBATE — SUMMARY
--------------------------------
Phelan (1993): Performance's only life is in the present.
  It cannot be saved, recorded, documented.
  Attempting to document performance produces something ELSE.
  The ontology of performance = presence + disappearance.

Auslander (1999): The "live" is not pure.
  Our sense of liveness is shaped by mediatization.
  We compare live performance to recorded performance.
  The live event is experienced through the lens of the record.
  "Liveness" is itself a construct.

Schechner: All performance is restored behavior.
  Therefore: all performance is already a form of record.
  The live/recorded distinction is a matter of degree.

THE DIGITAL AGE COMPLICATION:
  Live-streaming: a performance is "live" but also
    recorded and distributed. Which is it?
  Zoom theater: COVID forced theaters online.
    Is online theater "theater" at all?
    What is lost? What (if anything) is preserved or added?
  VR performance: immersive but not in a shared physical space.
    New form? Or deficit simulation of theater?

No consensus; the debate continues and is generative.

## Common Confusion Points

**Performance studies is not theater studies**
Theater studies focuses on theatrical art. Performance studies uses theater as one example among many of a broader category: any behavior that can be analyzed as "performance." A court trial, a political rally, a religious ritual, a job interview, a football game — all are in performance studies' purview.

**"Performative" in Butler ≠ "performative" in Austin**
Austin's performative speech act is an utterance that does something (a promise, a declaration). Butler's gender performativity is the production of gender through repeated, citational acts. They share the core idea (doing creates being rather than being expressing itself in doing) but the specific applications are different. Butler explicitly credits Austin but transforms the concept.

**Goffman's theatrical metaphor is not reductive**
Saying "social life is performance" does not mean it is fake, shallow, or insincere. Goffman is describing the structure of social interaction, not condemning it. The managed presentation of self is how cooperation is possible — it is functional, not fraudulent.

**Liminality is not a permanent state**
Liminality is valuable precisely because it is temporary. Its power (transformation, communitas, freedom from structure) depends on the return to structure that follows. A permanent liminality would not be transformative — it would be anomie. Turner was aware that some social movements try to make liminality permanent (utopian communities, communes) and that this usually fails or produces pathological results.
