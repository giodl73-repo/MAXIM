# Opera

## The Big Picture

Opera is the union of music and drama in which the text is (usually entirely) sung. It is simultaneously a musical form, a theatrical form, and a social institution. Its 400-year history reflects successive attempts to balance the demands of music and drama — a tension that has never been finally resolved.

## Layer 1: Origins and Baroque Opera (1600–1750)

### The Camerata and Monteverdi

See theater-performance/03-RENAISSANCE-BAROQUE.md for the Camerata's founding of opera. Key extension here:

```
MONTEVERDI'S L'ORFEO (1607) — TECHNICAL ANALYSIS
---------------------------------------------------
The libretto: by Alessandro Striggio.
Five acts; the Orpheus myth.

MUSICAL INNOVATIONS:
  Word painting: when Orpheus sings of dying, the
    music descends; when he sings of the lyre, a lyre
    sounds. The music depicts what the text describes.
  Stile concitato ("excited style"): rapid, repeated
    notes for agitation — Monteverdi's invention.
    The first systematic musical emotional rhetoric.
  Orchestra: ~36 instruments (huge for the period).
    Different instrument groups for different realms:
    - Strings and flutes = the earthly pastoral world
    - Brass and trombones = the underworld (Hades)
    The instrument palette is the first systematic
    use of orchestral color for dramatic meaning.
    Compare: Wagner's Leitmotif system is this idea
    taken to its logical extreme.

THE PROBLEM OPERA DEFINES:
  When text and music compete, which wins?
  Monteverdi: music must serve the text (Camerata ideal).
  But: when a great singer has a great aria, the text
  is subordinated to the singer's display of virtuosity.
  This tension drives the entire history of opera.
```

### Baroque Opera — The Major Forms

```
OPERA SERIA ("serious opera")
------------------------------
The dominant form, ~1650–1780.
Texts: mythology, ancient history.
Structure: recitative (speech-like) + aria (song).

RECITATIVE:
  Sung speech; the text is clear; the action advances.
  Two types:
    Secco ("dry"): voice + harpsichord only.
      Fast, efficient text delivery.
    Accompagnato ("accompanied"): full orchestra.
      For more emotionally charged passages.

ARIA:
  A formal song; the action pauses while a character
  sings of their emotional state.
  DA CAPO ARIA (A-B-A' form):
    A: main material
    B: contrasting section
    A': return, with improvised ornamentation
    The singer added ornamentation on the DA CAPO.
    The score was a skeleton; the singer completed it.
    This is the opposite of later practice (full notation).

OPERA SERIA CONVENTIONS:
  3 acts; each character gets one aria per act.
  No ensembles (duets/trios etc.) of consequence — arias
  only. This is dramatically rigid but musically splendid.
  The castrato (castrated male soprano) was the vocal
  ideal — superhuman power and range.
  Handel wrote many of his best roles for castrato.

OPERA BUFFA ("comic opera")
----------------------------
Lower-class characters; contemporary settings;
social satire; ensemble finales (the "chain finale"
that ends each act — all characters onstage, multiple
tempos and keys).

Pergolesi's La Serva Padrona (1733): paradigm case.
  A maid manipulates her master into marrying her.
  Two singing characters + a mime; 50 minutes.
  The "Querelle des Bouffons" (1752-54): a pamphlet war
  in Paris about whether Italian buffa was superior to
  French serious opera (Rameau). It was really a dispute
  about Enlightenment values (naturalness vs. grandeur).
```

## Layer 2: Gluck's Reform and Mozart

### Gluck: The Drama Must Win

Christoph Willibald Gluck's "reform operas" (Orfeo ed Euridice, 1762; Alceste, 1767) attempted to subordinate music to drama:

```
GLUCK'S REFORM MANIFESTO (Alceste preface, 1769)
--------------------------------------------------
"I sought to restrict music to its true office of
serving poetry by means of expression... by a
beautiful simplicity; not wishing, for this purpose,
to pass over unnaturally the second [A'] part of an
aria where the text allows no variation of meaning."

Against: da capo aria's ornamentation.
Against: the castrato's vocal display for its own sake.
Against: the halt of drama for the aria.

FOR: continuous musical drama; the music serves the text.

This is the Camerata's original ideal restated.
But now as a reaction against what Baroque opera had become.

The reform cycle: opera keeps oscillating between
music-dominant (singer's vehicle, vocal display) and
drama-dominant (the music serves the text/scene).
Gluck: drama dominant.
Rossini (bel canto): music/voice dominant.
Wagner: attempts a synthesis but creates a new extreme.
Verdi: gradually moves toward drama.
20th c.: various solutions.
```

### Mozart: The Synthesis

```
MOZART'S OPERAS
---------------
Don Giovanni (1787): opera buffa but with a serious ending.
Le nozze di Figaro (1786): comic opera as political comedy.
Cosi fan tutte (1790): comedy about fidelity and illusion.
Die Zauberflöte (1791): singspiel (spoken dialogue + music).

MOZART'S ACHIEVEMENT:
  Character in music: each character in Don Giovanni has
  a distinctive musical language. Don Giovanni's music
  is seductive, mercurial, unstoppable. Donna Elvira's
  is overwrought, passionate. Leporello's is comic and
  practical. The characters SOUND different, not just
  act different.

  THE ENSEMBLE: Mozart was the master of the ensemble
  — scenes where multiple characters sing simultaneously,
  each with their own text and emotional state.
  The Act II finale of Figaro is 20 minutes of continuous
  music in which the plot complications multiply and
  resolve. Multiple characters with multiple agendas
  sing simultaneously in musical counterpoint.
  Musical structure = dramatic structure.

  This is the opposite of Opera Seria (one character,
  one aria) — it is musical-dramatic integration.
```

## Layer 3: Bel Canto and Italian Romantic Opera

```
BEL CANTO ("beautiful singing")
---------------------------------
Roughly 1810–1850.
Composers: Rossini, Bellini, Donizetti.

The vocal ideal:
  Legato (smooth, connected) phrasing.
  Ornamental runs, trills, coloratura (rapid passages).
  The human voice as the instrument above all others.
  Drama is secondary to the vocal display.

Rossini: The Barber of Seville (1816).
  Bel canto comedy; Figaro's rapid-fire patter arias.
  The Act I finale and Act II finale — comic machinery
  at maximum speed.

Bellini: Norma (1831).
  "Casta Diva" — the archetype of bel canto melody:
  long, arching, ornamental, requiring extraordinary
  breath control and tonal consistency.
  Bellini's melodies: Chopin said he learned to phrase
  from Bellini's melodic lines. Wagner said Bellini
  taught him the operatic long line.

Donizetti: Lucia di Lammermoor (1835).
  "Mad scene": Lucia enters covered in blood having
  killed her husband; the soprano sings an extended
  mad scene with a glass harmonica (eerie sound).
  The mad scene as operatic genre: emotional extremity
  justified as reason for extended display.
```

## Layer 4: Verdi — The Dramatist

Giuseppe Verdi (1813–1901) is the master of Italian opera and the greatest theater-maker in the form's history:

```
VERDI'S DEVELOPMENT
--------------------
Early Verdi (Nabucco 1842, Rigoletto 1851):
  Grand, powerful choruses (Nabucco: "Va pensiero" —
    became an Italian nationalist anthem)
  Sharp dramatic situations; driven librettos
  Baritone as villain: a Verdi innovation

Middle Verdi (Trovatore 1853, Traviata 1853, Aida 1871):
  La Traviata: a contemporary social story (a courtesan).
  The audience in 1853 saw people in modern dress
  dying of tuberculosis — scandalous contemporaneity.
  Verdi insisted; it was initially a failure (audience
  laughed at the fat soprano dying of consumption);
  it became perhaps the most-performed opera in history.

  Aida: large-scale spectacle (Egyptian setting) with
  intimate psychological focus. The triumphal march
  AND the father-daughter love duet. Verdi holds both.

Late Verdi (Otello 1887, Falstaff 1893):
  Collaboration with librettist Boito.
  The closest Italian opera comes to continuous music
  drama (approaching Wagner but remaining Italian).
  Falstaff: Verdi's last opera, written at 79.
  A comedy. Fugue at the end ("Tutto nel mondo e burla" —
  "All the world's a jest"). The fugue is a musical joke:
  formal, rigorous, inevitable — and about nothing.
  Verdi's final statement.
```

## Layer 5: Wagner — The Gesamtkunstwerk and the Leitmotif

Richard Wagner (1813–1883) is the most theoretically ambitious figure in operatic history. His theoretical writings (Opera and Drama, 1851; The Artwork of the Future, 1849) preceded his mature operas and provided their philosophical justification.

### The Gesamtkunstwerk

```
WAGNER'S GESAMTKUNSTWERK ("total artwork")
-------------------------------------------
Wagner's thesis:
  The separate arts (music, poetry, dance, visual art)
  are fragments of a lost total art (Greek tragedy).
  Opera as conventionally practiced keeps them separate
  (singer displays voice regardless of drama; libretto
  is subordinate to music). This is decadent.

  The Gesamtkunstwerk would UNIFY all the arts under
  a single dramatic-musical-visual conception.
  Music, text, staging, gesture, costume all serve
  a single dramatic intention.

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:
  Wagner wrote his own librettos (poems, actually).
  He designed his theater (Bayreuth Festspielhaus, 1876).
  He specified staging, lighting, orchestra placement.
  The sunken, covered orchestra pit at Bayreuth:
    The orchestra is invisible to the audience.
    Music seems to arise mysteriously from below the stage.
    The "mystic abyss" between audience and stage.
    A deliberate immersive design: you cannot see the
    machinery; you are in the dream.
    Compare: Brecht exposed the machinery; Wagner buried it.
    Opposite design philosophies.
```

### The Leitmotif System

The Leitmotif system is Wagner's most technically precise innovation and the most directly relevant to a technically-minded reader:

```
THE LEITMOTIF SYSTEM
---------------------
A Leitmotif is a short musical theme — a melody,
a harmonic progression, a rhythm — associated with a
character, an object, a concept, or an emotion.
When the theme sounds, the association is activated —
even if the character is absent from the stage.

THE RING CYCLE (Der Ring des Nibelungen):
  4 operas: Das Rheingold (1h), Die Walkure (3.5h),
  Siegfried (4h), Gotterdammerung (5h)
  Total: ~15 hours of music (performed over 4 days)

  Wagner identified over 100 Leitmotifs in the Ring.
  Key examples:
  - The RING itself: rising arpeggio, slightly sinister
  - VALHALLA (the gods' fortress): noble brass, stately
  - FATE: descending chromatic line, heavy, inevitable
  - SIEGFRIED'S HORN: heroic, ascending, open intervals
  - THE SWORD: bright, major, sudden
  - WOTAN'S FRUSTRATION: chromatic, unstable
  - LOVE (various forms): different leitmotifs for
    Siegmund/Sieglinde love, Siegfried/Brunnhilde love, etc.

INFORMATIONAL FUNCTION:
  The Leitmotif system functions as a TAGGING SYSTEM.
  When you hear the Ring motif during a scene where
  no one is talking about the ring, you know the
  ring is present in the situation (perhaps as a
  subconscious factor).
  When Wotan sings and the Fate motif sounds,
  you know his will is constrained by necessity
  even if he's saying something else.

  The orchestra comments on and contradicts what
  the characters say and do.
  It provides a SECOND LAYER OF NARRATIVE.

  Analogy: the musical score is like metadata —
  it tags the semantic content with additional
  information not present in the surface text.
  Wagner is running two simultaneous information
  streams (vocal/textual and orchestral/motivic)
  that cross-reference each other constantly.

THE ENDLOS MELODIE ("endless melody"):
  Wagner replaces the number opera (aria + recitative)
  with continuous music. The music flows without
  stopping for applause. There are no "songs" to
  cheer. The opera is a CONTINUOUS MUSICAL DRAMA.
  This requires a new kind of attention from the audience.
```

## Layer 6: After Wagner — Reactions and New Directions

```
VERISMO (1890s)
---------------
Italian composers (Puccini, Mascagni, Leoncavallo)
react against Wagner's mythology and Verdi's historical
grandeur by setting operas in contemporary working-class
settings.

Mascagni's Cavalleria Rusticana (1890): Sicilian peasants
  jealousy and murder in 70 minutes. Brutal, direct.
Leoncavallo's Pagliacci (1892): circus performers; the
  play-within-a-play as a murder scene.
Puccini: La Boheme (1896), Tosca (1900), Madama Butterfly
  (1904). The individual, intimate, tragic.

DEBUSSY: Pelleas et Melisande (1902)
  Maeterlinck's symbolist play set to music.
  Anti-Wagnerian in almost every way:
  - Minimal melodic material (no memorable tunes)
  - Orchestral understatement (not the Wagnerian wave)
  - Characters who speak in half-finished sentences
    (and the music mirrors this — incomplete, ambiguous)
  - Symbolism (what do things mean is not explained)
  A strange, beautiful, singular work.

RICHARD STRAUSS: Salome (1905), Der Rosenkavalier (1911)
  Salome: expressionist (Strauss before "Expressionism"
    as a term). The "Dance of the Seven Veils"; Salome
    kisses the head of John the Baptist.
    Simultaneously erotic, horrifying, and musically lush.
  Der Rosenkavalier: nostalgic, Viennese waltz opera.
    Both exist; Strauss was stylistically omnivorous.

BERG: Wozzeck (1925)
  12-tone and atonal opera (though Berg's 12-tone is
  more tonal than Schoenberg's).
  Wozzeck: a simple soldier oppressed by all around him
  until he murders his wife and drowns.
  Each scene uses a different classical musical form:
    Suite, Rhapsody, Military March, Passacaglia, Rondo —
    formal structures applied to expressionist content.
    The disconnect between form and content IS the point.

BRITTEN: Peter Grimes (1945)
  The opera that re-founded English opera.
  The "sea interludes" — orchestral pieces between
  scenes that depict both the literal sea and the
  psychological world of the characters.

JOHN ADAMS: Nixon in China (1987), Doctor Atomic (2005)
  Minimalist opera (repeating musical patterns, slowly
  evolving). Historical events as operatic subject.
  Doctor Atomic: the Trinity nuclear test. J. Robert
  Oppenheimer's aria "Batter my heart" (Donne's sonnet)
  before the bomb test is one of the great contemporary
  operatic moments.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Opera and musical theater are different forms**
Opera: almost entirely sung; classical (bel canto or operatic) singing technique; no amplification; serious institutional heritage. Musical theater: combines singing and spoken dialogue; microphone amplification; popular music vocal technique; developed from 20th-c popular forms. The boundary has blurred (Sondheim, Hamilton) but the distinction between operatic and musical theater conventions is real.

**Wagner's operas are not about the Leitmotifs**
The Leitmotif is an analytical tool (applied retrospectively by Wagner and critics) and a compositional method. The operas work for audiences who cannot identify a single Leitmotif by name. The themes work subliminally; the associations build through repetition and variation. You don't need to know that the "Renunciation of Love" motif is playing to feel its effect.

**"Verismo" does not mean "realistic"**
Verismo (Italian: truth/realism) is a specific late-19th-c Italian operatic style emphasizing contemporary working-class settings and emotionally direct (often violent) scenarios. Puccini is associated with verismo but his operas are dramatically stylized, not naturalistically realistic.

**Recitative is not the unimportant part**
The conventional wisdom is: recitative (speech-like) advances the plot; the aria (song) is the important part. In opera buffa and in Mozart, the recitatives are often where the drama is most alive — the plot turns, the characters reveal themselves, the fun happens. The aria pauses and reflects. Both matter.
