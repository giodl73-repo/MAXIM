# Modern Drama

## The Big Picture

Modern drama (roughly 1870–1960) is defined by successive rejections: naturalism rejected romantic melodrama; expressionism rejected naturalism; Brecht rejected Aristotelian empathy; the Absurdists rejected logical plot progression. Each new form is a response to the failure of the previous one to capture "the truth" of human experience in a world after Darwin, Freud, Marx, and two World Wars.

## Layer 1: Naturalism — The Theater of Social Reality

### Ibsen: The Founder

Henrik Ibsen (1828–1906) is the origin of modern drama in the same sense that Darwin is the origin of modern biology: everything after him is shaped by the terms he set.

```
IBSEN'S FORMAL INNOVATIONS
----------------------------
1. THE WELL-MADE PLAY TURNED AGAINST ITSELF
   The bien fait (Scribe, 1820s-50s) was a mechanical
   formula: meticulous exposition, a letter or document
   that creates crisis, a "scène à faire" (obligatory
   scene of confrontation), a tidy denouement.
   Ibsen uses this structure but fills it with real
   psychological and social consequence. The "tidy"
   conclusion is no longer available.
   A Doll's House ends not with resolution but with
   a door slam that opens onto the unknown.

2. RETROSPECTIVE STRUCTURE
   Ibsen's plays are about the PAST catching up to the present.
   The action is discovery: what happened before the
   play began? The "play" is the revelation.
   Rosmersholm, Ghosts, Hedda Gabler, The Master Builder —
   all structured around the irruption of suppressed
   past into a present that cannot contain it.
   "The past never stays past" — this is Ibsen's thesis.

3. SUBTEXT: WHAT IS NOT SAID
   Characters talk around what they mean.
   Hedda Gabler never says she envies Lovborg's freedom.
   She talks about "vine leaves in his hair" — an image,
   not a statement. Ibsen invented the subtext that
   Chekhov developed and Pinter made into a style.

4. THE PROBLEM PLAY
   Drama as vehicle for social analysis:
   A Doll's House (1879): the institution of bourgeois marriage
   Ghosts (1881): hereditary syphilis and moral hypocrisy
   An Enemy of the People (1882): expert knowledge vs. mob
   The Wild Duck (1884): the destructive "ideal demand"
```

### Strindberg: The Psychological Interior

August Strindberg (1849–1912) pushed naturalism into the interior and then beyond it:

```
STRINDBERG — TWO PHASES
------------------------
NATURALIST PHASE:
  Miss Julie (1888): class and sex as forces destroying
  both characters. Stage directions insist on maximum
  naturalism: no intermission, no painted flats, real
  props, kitchen as setting, complete psychological
  motivation.
  The preface is a manifesto for naturalist drama.

  SUBTEXT AS POWER:
  Julie and Jean cannot say what they mean to each other.
  Everything is displaced — into class status games,
  sexual teasing, the story of the bird. The final
  scene: Julie goes out to kill herself, hypnotized
  by Jean's voice. Motivation is simultaneously
  naturalistic and mythically over-determined.

EXPRESSIONIST/DREAM PHASE:
  The Father (1887): paranoid male protagonist
  A Dream Play (1901): "The Author has tried to
  reproduce the disconnected but apparently logical
  form of a dream. Anything can happen; everything is
  possible and probable."
  — characters merge, scenes transform without transition,
    dream logic replaces causal logic
  Miss Julie (1888 naturalism) → A Dream Play (1901
    anti-naturalism): Strindberg traversed the full range.
```

### Chekhov: The Theater of Atmosphere

Anton Chekhov (1860–1904) is the most paradoxical of the naturalists: his plays are full of people trying to speak and failing, trying to act and failing, trying to mean and not quite managing.

```
CHEKHOV'S DRAMATURGY
----------------------
"If there's a gun on the wall in Act I, it must go off
by Act III." — often attributed to Chekhov.

BUT CHEKHOV'S ACTUAL PRACTICE IS OFTEN THE OPPOSITE:
  The gun doesn't go off (or does, and changes nothing).
  Marriages are proposed and rejected without consequence.
  Characters announce departures they never make.
  Vershinin and Masha love each other — it changes nothing.
  The three sisters never get to Moscow.

THE NON-EVENT as the dramatic event:
  Conventional drama: anticipation → climax → resolution
  Chekhov: anticipation → (pause) → life continues
  The "dramatic" event happens offstage if at all;
  what's onstage is the atmosphere before and after.

KEY FEATURES:
  Ensemble: no protagonist; all characters equally weighted
  Lyric structure: mood and atmosphere over plot
  Subtext: constant (character says one thing, means another)
  Pauses: Chekhov specifies them; they are structural
  MKHV (Moscow Art Theater, Stanislavski): created the
    performance style that realized Chekhov's vision —
    slow tempo, psychological truth, ensemble work.
    The naturalist stage direction was Chekhov's;
    the acting method was Stanislavski's.
```

## Layer 2: Expressionism — The Inner World Distorted

German Expressionism (c. 1910–1925) rejected the outer world of naturalism for the inner world of emotion and vision. The stage distorts reality to reveal subjective truth.

```
EXPRESSIONISM — FORMAL FEATURES
---------------------------------
Not the realistic world but THE WORLD AS A CHARACTER
EXPERIENCES IT:
  Distorted sets: angles, disproportionate furniture
  Harsh, unnatural lighting: strong shadows, colored gels
  Stylized movement: mechanical or distorted
  Language: telegraphic, intense, exclamatory
  Characters: types or symbols (The Father, The Worker,
    The Banker) not psychological individuals

KEY TEXTS:
  Frank Wedekind: Spring Awakening (1891) — sexuality,
    authority, repression, suicide among adolescents.
    Considered "immoral" for decades; finally staged
    1906; became a 2006 rock musical.

  Georg Kaiser: From Morn to Midnight (1912) — a bank
    clerk embezzles, seeks experience, dies.
    The "stations" structure: a sequence of episodes
    (like stages of a journey or the Stations of the Cross)
    Influence of the Strindberg dream play.

  Ernst Toller: Man and the Masses (1921) — written
    in prison after the failed Bavarian Soviet Republic.
    Revolutionary themes; expressionist staging.

RELATION TO PSYCHOLOGY:
  Expressionism emerged simultaneously with psychoanalysis.
  Both explore the distorted, symbol-laden inner world.
  The stage as dream is both Strindberg's metaphor and
  Freud's analytic territory.
```

### Pirandello: Identity and Illusion

*Six Characters* raises a systems-level authority question: who owns the canonical representation? The author (schema designer), the character (the data), the actor (the runtime), or the director (the orchestrator)? This is the same conflict as competing authoritative sources in a layered system — and Pirandello's answer is that no layer has final authority, only competing claims.

Luigi Pirandello (1867–1936) occupies a unique position — neither naturalist nor expressionist, but a theorist of the theater's own nature:

```
PIRANDELLO — METATHEATER
--------------------------
Six Characters in Search of an Author (1921):
  Six characters interrupt a theater rehearsal.
  They claim to exist as characters but have no
  author to write their story.
  The Director tries to stage their drama; the
  characters protest that the actors' interpretations
  are WRONG.
  Who has authority over a character's truth?
  The author? The character? The actor? The director?

  METALEPSIS in performance:
  Characters exist at one narrative level;
  the theater company at another.
  Pirandello crosses them — characters invade
  the frame of the rehearsal.

Henry IV (1922):
  A man falls from a horse during a costume parade
  and wakes up believing he IS Henry IV.
  When he recovers sanity, he chooses to REMAIN
  in the role of Henry IV because the performance
  of identity is more bearable than the chaos of reality.
  "All the world's a stage" pushed to its limit:
  if all identity is performance, there is no self
  beneath the role.
```

## Layer 3: Brecht's Epic Theater

Brecht's theory and practice is covered in literature/03-DRAMA.md. Here, the theatrical specifics:

```
BRECHT'S THEATRICAL PRACTICE
------------------------------
GESTUS: not an individual character's "emotion" but
  the social attitude, the class relation, the
  historical position made visible in gesture and stance.
  The "social gesture" (gesellschaftliche Gestik):
  how a character holds their body shows their
  position in the social order.

  Example: the bowing gesture of a servant to a master
  is not just subservience — it is a class relation
  made physically manifest.

THE HALF-CURTAIN:
  Brecht used a half-curtain (knee-to-waist height) that
  partially concealed scene changes but made them visible.
  Stagehands moved set pieces in view of the audience.
  The machinery of illusion is exposed.

BANNERS AND PROJECTIONS:
  Scene titles projected onto the stage or on placards.
  "The year is 1943. A Wehrmacht officer enters."
  The audience knows the outcome before the scene;
  they watch HOW, not what.

MUSIC (Kurt Weill, Hanns Eisler):
  Songs interrupt action; they comment, argue, generalize.
  The Threepenny Opera (1928): songs "interrupt"
  the audience's involvement — you have to think about
  what you just heard, not feel it and move on.
  Music as critical interruption, not emotional reinforcement.

MAJOR TEXTS:
  Mother Courage and Her Children (1939):
    Courage profits from war while losing her children to it.
    Brecht intended her as a negative example — a capitalist
    figure who cannot see. Many audiences find her
    sympathetic: the V-Effekt here "failed" in a way
    that reveals something about the limits of his theory.

  The Good Person of Szechwan (1941):
    A good person cannot survive in a capitalist world
    without a cruel alter ego. The split persona demonstrates
    the structural impossibility of individual goodness
    in a bad system.

  The Caucasian Chalk Circle (1944):
    Bertolt Brecht at his most Shakespearean (and
    his best in terms of theatrical playability).
```

## Layer 4: Theatre of the Absurd

### Beckett: The Existential Void in Dramatic Form

```
SAMUEL BECKETT (1906–1989)
---------------------------
Waiting for Godot (1953 premiere, Paris):
  Vladimir and Estragon wait for Godot. He does not come.
  A boy arrives each act: Godot will come tomorrow.
  Act II repeats Act I with minor variations.
  Lucky and Pozzo appear in both acts (transformed in II).
  "Nothing to be done." / "They do not move."

FORMAL ANALYSIS:
  CIRCULAR STRUCTURE: the play ends where it begins.
    "Shall we go?" "Yes, let's go." They do not move.
    The final stage direction repeats the first position.
    Nothing has changed; everything has been.

  VAUDEVILLE STRUCTURE:
    Vladimir and Estragon are a cross-talk comedy team.
    Their hat and boot routines, their misunderstandings,
    their reliance on each other — these are music hall,
    Laurel and Hardy, Abbott and Costello.
    But the comedy is now in service of existential despair.
    "We always find something, eh Didi, to give us the
    impression we exist?"

  LANGUAGE AS BOTH TOOL AND FAILURE:
    Characters talk endlessly but do not communicate.
    Language is the only activity available; it is
    demonstrably inadequate. Both things are true
    simultaneously.

Endgame (1957):
  Hamm is blind, cannot move; Clov can move but cannot sit.
  They are trapped in a room (the skull? the world's end?).
  "Finish" is the first and last word.
  More concentrated, more despairing than Godot.

Happy Days (1961):
  Winnie is buried to her waist (Act I), then to her neck
  (Act II). She talks cheerfully through it all.
  "Oh this is a happy day, this will have been another
  happy day!" Beckett's darkest comedy.

The Irish connection:
  Beckett wrote first in French (to strip away the rhetorical
  facility of his native English); then self-translated.
  Writing in a second language as a form of foreignization:
  make it strange; resist the easy idiom.
```

### Ionesco: The Comic Absurd

```
EUGENE IONESCO (1909–1994)
----------------------------
The Bald Soprano (La Cantatrice Chauve, 1950):
  A play that literally has no plot.
  Mr. and Mrs. Smith talk. Mr. and Mrs. Martin arrive.
  The Fire Chief arrives. Language breaks down.
  The Smiths and Martins exchange lines and positions.
  The play restarts from the beginning.

  Language as EMPTY RITUAL:
  "The English prime their pistols to be able to shoot
  the rabbits and eat well... it is better to lay eggs
  in the country than in the city, it is more difficult..."
  Conversation reduced to phatic exchange without content.
  The social function of language (maintaining connection)
  severed from the referential function (transmitting meaning).

Rhinoceros (1959):
  Everyone in a small town turns into rhinoceroses.
  Berenger is the last to refuse.
  Easy to read as: fascism (it was written in that context).
  But also: conformism in general. The terror is how
  EASY the transformation is, how REASONABLE it looks,
  how lonely resistance becomes.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Naturalism is not "realistic staging"**
Naturalism is a theoretical program: the stage should represent social reality with scientific accuracy, as Zola applied the scientific method to the novel. "Realistic staging" (using real props, real sets) is one consequence, but the program is larger: drama should investigate social and biological determinism.

**Brecht's V-Effekt does not mean "no emotion"**
Brecht did not want audiences to be unaffected. He wanted them to maintain critical judgment ALONGSIDE emotional response. Mother Courage is emotionally devastating — that is the point. The V-Effekt is not emotional anesthesia but the maintenance of a reflective distance that coexists with emotional engagement.

**The Absurdists were not nihilists**
The Theatre of the Absurd (Esslin's label) depicts a world without inherent meaning, but the plays are not nihilistic in the sense of advocating despair or inaction. Beckett continues to write (Winnie: "Old Style! [she smiles] There is so much to be thankful for"). The persistence of the human in absurd conditions is itself a kind of affirmation.

**Harold Pinter is not just "pause-oriented"**
Pinter's famous pauses and silences are a technique, not the substance. His plays are about menace, power, and the way language is used to dominate and exclude. The Birthday Party (1958), The Homecoming (1965) are political dramas of power and humiliation in which the pauses are the moments where power shifts.
