# Roman and Medieval Theater

## The Big Picture

Roman theater adapted and transformed Greek models; medieval theater largely reinvented theatrical performance from scratch after the near-total collapse of professional theater in late antiquity. These two periods bracket the gap — what survived, what was lost, and how theatrical performance re-emerged through the Church's own rituals.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|         ROMAN AND MEDIEVAL THEATER — LANDSCAPE                   |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  ROMAN (200 BCE–400 CE)        MEDIEVAL (900–1550 CE)           |
|  ---------------------         ----------------------           |
|  Plautus and Terence: comedy   Quem Quaeritis trope             |
|  Seneca: tragedy for reading   Liturgical drama (church)        |
|  Mime and pantomime            Mystery/miracle/morality plays   |
|  Spectacle: circus, gladiators Guild performance (cycles)       |
|  Permanent stone theaters      Market square performance        |
|                                Commedia dell'arte (c. 1550+)    |
|                                                                  |
|  THE COLLAPSE                                                    |
|  ~400 CE: Church condemnation of theater begins                  |
|  476 CE: Fall of Western Rome                                    |
|  Professional theater largely disappears in West                 |
|  Performance survives as popular and folk form                   |
|  Church re-invents theatrical performance from liturgy           |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Layer 1: Roman Comedy — Plautus and Terence

Roman comedy is the direct ancestor of European romantic comedy through to Shakespeare and beyond. The plots, character types, and structural conventions were transmitted almost intact.

### Plautus (c. 254–184 BCE)

```
PLAUTUS — CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------
20 surviving comedies (of ~130 attributed)
Adapted Greek New Comedy (Menander's plots)
  but made them coarser, more farcical

KEY FEATURES:
  Stock characters (fixed types that persist through history):
    Senex (old man) — foolish or lecherous father
    Adulescens (young man) — in love, financially dependent
    Servus callidus (clever slave) — the plotting engine,
      often the most intelligent character
    Meretrix (courtesan) — love interest with complications
    Leno (pimp/slave-dealer) — the villain
    Miles gloriosus (braggart soldier) — cowardly, vain
    Parasitus (hanger-on) — lives by flattery and invitations

  PLOT TYPE (almost universal):
    Young man loves courtesan (or other "unavailable" woman)
    Father controls the money
    Clever slave devises scheme to get money from father
    for son to buy woman's freedom
    Father is outwitted; celebrates OR son is revealed
    to be a citizen (the woman's citizen status
    removes the complication)

  FORESHADOWING SHAKESPEARE:
    These stock types → Italian commedia → Elizabethan
    The clever servant (Puck, Touchstone, Feste)
    The braggart soldier (Falstaff, Parolles)
    The young lover (Romeo, Lysander)
    All have Roman comedy DNA.
```

### Terence (c. 195–159 BCE)

```
TERENCE — IN CONTRAST TO PLAUTUS
----------------------------------
6 comedies survive (all of them)
More refined, less farcical than Plautus
Admired in the Middle Ages and Renaissance for
  his "pure Latin" — used as a school text

KEY DIFFERENCES FROM PLAUTUS:
  Double plot structure: two pairs of lovers/problems
    simultaneously — creates parallels and contrasts
  More psychological character motivation
  Less reliance on farce and slapstick
  Prologues used to address critics (meta-theatrical)

ANDRIA (166 BCE): The first double-plot structure.
  Two young men, two women, two sets of problems,
  converging resolution. Terence's innovation of
  the double plot was enormously influential:
  Shakespeare's subplot-weaving comes through this tradition.

HECYRA (Mother-in-Law):
  Unusual: no scheming slave, no farcical climax.
  A realistic domestic comedy about a marriage in trouble.
  Failed twice in performance (audience left for competing
  attractions — a gladiatorial show, a tightrope walker).
  Terence records this in his prologue, bitterly.
```

### Seneca and Tragedy for Reading

```
SENECA (c. 4 BCE–65 CE) — CLOSET DRAMA
-----------------------------------------
9 tragedies on Greek mythological subjects
(Medea, Phaedra, Oedipus, Thyestes, Trojan Women, etc.)
BUT: probably written for READING and private performance,
not for public stage production.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
  Senecan tragedy was what Renaissance scholars had
  when they wanted to revive "classical" tragedy.
  Greek tragedies were largely unknown in the West
  until the 15th century (Byzantine manuscripts).
  Seneca's tragedies were available throughout the
  medieval period (he wrote in Latin, not Greek).

SENECAN FEATURES (which became neo-classical "rules"):
  Five-act structure
  Revenge and bloodbath plots (Thyestes — cannibalism;
    Medea — infanticide; Atreus's revenge)
  Stoic rhetoric: long speeches on fate, death, virtue
  Violence reported by messenger OR depicted on stage
    (contrast: Greek convention kept violence offstage)
  The influential "Senecan ghost": shade from the underworld
    opens the play, demands revenge (Hamlet's ghost = Senecan)

ELIZABETHAN SENECAN INFLUENCE:
  Kyd's Spanish Tragedy (1587) — first revenge tragedy
  Marlowe's Tamburlaine — Senecan rhetoric
  Shakespeare: Hamlet's ghost, the revenge structure,
    the moral rhetoric are all Senecan
```

## Layer 2: Roman Spectacle — The Other Tradition

Roman theatrical culture was not only comedy and tragedy. The popular forms were often more important:

```
ROMAN POPULAR PERFORMANCE
--------------------------
MIME:
  Short comic scenes from daily life
  No masks (unlike Greek/tragedy)
  Women could perform (unique in ancient theater)
  Improvised and scripted; some famous mime texts survive
  (Herodas's Mimiambi, though these may be literary)
  Enormously popular throughout Roman period

PANTOMIME (pantominus — "all imitating"):
  Not what we call pantomime today.
  A solo dancer who performed mythological narratives
  through gesture and movement while a chorus sang and
  musicians played. No words from the dancer.
  The great performers were celebrities (like modern sports stars).
  Rivaled theater in popularity.

SPECTACLE (ludi):
  Circus races, gladiatorial combat, wild animal hunts (venationes)
  Naumachia (staged naval battles in flooded arenas)
  These competed directly with theatrical performance
  (Terence records this — his audience literally left for
  the gladiators)
  The Colosseum (opened 80 CE) seated 50,000-80,000 people.
  The Circus Maximus seated 250,000.
  Theater (5,000-15,000 seats) was outscaled by spectacle.
```

## Layer 3: The Collapse and Survival

```
THE DISAPPEARANCE OF PROFESSIONAL THEATER
-------------------------------------------
~300-400 CE:
  Christian church condemns theatrical performance.
  Actors (histriones) declared infames — legally
  without reputation; cannot testify in court;
  cannot marry a respectable citizen.
  Church councils repeatedly condemn attendance at theater.

313 CE: Edict of Milan — Christianity becomes legal
380 CE: Christianity becomes state religion
  This closes the festivals that supported theater.

476 CE: Fall of Western Rome
  The infrastructure collapses: no more state subsidy,
  no more urban leisure class to fund performances.

WHAT SURVIVED:
  Mime and popular performance survived at the margins:
    At fairs, markets, weddings, noble feasts
    Jongleurs (jugglers/performers), minstrels, acrobats
    The Church condemned them but could not eliminate them.

  Texts survived in monasteries:
    Terence read as Latin school text throughout medieval period
    Hrosvitha of Gandersheim (c. 935–1002): nun who wrote
    Christian comedies in imitation of Terence
    Her works are among the first medieval dramas, written
    in a monastery, not for public performance.

  Liturgical ritual preserved theatrical elements:
    Call and response between priest and choir
    Reading of the Passion narrative as dialogue
    These become the seed of liturgical drama.
```

## Layer 4: Medieval Liturgical Drama

### The Quem Quaeritis Trope

```
QUEM QUAERITIS (c. 925 CE)
----------------------------
The earliest surviving liturgical dramatic text.
Found in the Regularis Concordia (Winchester, c. 965-75)
but probably in use earlier.

The text (for Easter Matins):
  Priest (representing the angels at the tomb):
    "Quem quaeritis in sepulchro, o Christicolae?"
    ("Whom do you seek in the tomb, O Christians?")

  Choir (representing the three Marys):
    "Jesum Nazarenum crucifixum, o Caelicolae."
    ("Jesus of Nazareth, the crucified, O heavenly ones.")

  Priest: "Non est hic; surrexit, sicut praedixerat.
    Ite, nuntiate quia surrexit de sepulchro."
    ("He is not here; he is risen, as he foretold.
    Go, announce that he has risen from the tomb.")

The Resurrection announced and enacted at the altar.
Priests acting out the scene; the altar is the tomb;
the Easter candle is the risen Christ.

THIS IS THEATER:
  Dialogue between characters
  Acting (not just chanting)
  Costumes (liturgical vestments = costume)
  A "set" (the altar)
  An audience (the congregation)
  A narrative being performed

Theater grew from this liturgical seed.
```

### Mystery Plays: The English Cycles

```
MYSTERY/MIRACLE CYCLES (13th-16th c.)
---------------------------------------
"Mystery" = from Latin "misterium" (trade/guild secret),
not "mystery" in the modern sense.

The cycles: large-scale productions of biblical narrative
  from Creation to Last Judgment, performed by
  trade guilds in English towns.

MAJOR ENGLISH CYCLES:
  York Cycle: 48 plays (most survive)
  Chester Cycle: 24 plays
  N-Town Cycle (East Anglia): 41 plays
  Towneley/Wakefield Cycle: 32 plays

PERFORMANCE:
  On pageant wagons: mobile stages that moved through
  the town, stopping at fixed stations.
  Each guild performed its assigned play at each station.
  Audiences stayed in one place; the plays came to them.
  The whole cycle could take 1-3 days.

GUILD ASSIGNMENTS:
  The Shipwrights built and performed Noah's Ark
  The Bakers performed the Last Supper
  The Pinners (nail-makers) performed the Crucifixion
  The assignment was functional/symbolic/ironic

THE WAKEFIELD MASTER:
  Anonymous playwright who revised the Towneley cycle.
  "The Second Shepherd's Play" (Secunda Pastorum):
  Comic subplot where a shepherd steals a sheep and
  hides it in a crib pretending it is a newborn baby.
  The three shepherds "adore" the swaddled sheep.
  Then the scene shifts to the real Nativity.
  This is sophisticated dramatic irony — the comic
  inversion (the sheep as false Christ) frames and
  intensifies the genuine Adoration that follows.

MORALITY PLAYS:
  Allegory rather than biblical narrative.
  Abstract virtues and vices as personified characters.
  EVERYMAN (c. 1510): the most famous surviving morality.
    Everyman is summoned by Death. He asks his friends
    (Fellowship, Kindred, Goods) to accompany him to
    the judgment. One by one they refuse or abandon him.
    Only Good Deeds accompanies him at the end.
    The allegory is transparent; the drama is surprisingly
    powerful.
```

## Layer 5: Commedia dell'Arte

Commedia dell'arte (c. 1550–1750) is one of the most influential theatrical forms ever: it established professional theater in Europe, created the stock types that persist through Moliere, Goldoni, and into Chaplin and beyond.

```
COMMEDIA DELL'ARTE — THE SYSTEM
----------------------------------
"Arte" = of the guild/profession.
These were PROFESSIONAL actors (arte = trade guild),
touring Italy and then Europe with their company.

NOT scripted plays — SCENARIOS:
  A scenario was a plot outline (beginning, turning points,
  ending) posted backstage. Actors improvised dialogue,
  speeches, jokes (lazzi) within this framework.
  The result: FLEXIBLE, RESPONSIVE theater.

STOCK CHARACTERS (MASCHERE):
  Each actor specialized in one character, which they
  played their entire career.

  ZANNI (servants, comic):
    Arlecchino (Harlequin): acrobatic, foolish, crafty
      Patchwork costume, half-mask (black nose, large eyebrows)
    Pedrolino (later Pierrot): naive, melancholy, lovesick
    Brighella: cunning, cruel, street-smart zanni
    Pulcinella: hunchbacked, violent, comic — origin of Punch

  VECCHI (old men):
    Pantalone: miserly old Venetian merchant
      Red costume, long nose, money obsession
    Il Dottore: pedantic Bolognese lawyer or doctor
      Black costume, speaks Latin nonsense

  INNAMORATI (young lovers):
    No masks. Beautiful costumes. Real names (Isabella, Flaminio)
    Speak in literary Italian (vs. the servants' dialect)

  CAPITANO: braggart soldier. Plautus's miles gloriosus
    in Italian dress. Boasts endlessly; cowardly.

LAZZI: rehearsed comic bits inserted into any scenario
  A lazzo might be: Arlecchino's acrobatic fall, a
  swordfight routine, a repeated misunderstanding,
  a dialogue about flies. Pre-packaged comedy modules.

INFLUENCE:
  Moliere saw commedia → his character comedy
  Goldoni reformed it (wrote full scripts, not scenarios)
  Gozzi defended it against Goldoni
  Into English pantomime (Harlequinade tradition)
  Chaplin's Tramp = Arlecchino in English film
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Pantomime" in Roman context is not British Christmas pantomime**
Roman pantomime was a solo dance artist performing mythological narratives wordlessly — a high art form with celebrity performers. British "panto" derives from the 18th-century Harlequinade tradition (a commedia-derived entertainment). They share the name only accidentally.

**Commedia dell'arte was not entirely improvised**
The scenarios provided plot structure; the lazzi were rehearsed. Improvisation operated within significant constraints. The "improvisation" was the filling in of dialogue and comic business within a pre-structured frame — more like jazz improvisation over changes than pure free improvisation.

**Morality plays are not boring moralizing**
Everyman is dramatically effective because the allegory IS the story — when Fellowship says "I will not go with you to that journey," you feel the abandonment. The abstract names make the structure visible, not the emotional impact less.

**The mystery plays were not crude folk theater**
The Second Shepherd's Play demonstrates sophisticated dramatic technique: the comic subplot parallels the sacred narrative, the inversion (sheep as false infant) frames and intensifies the real Nativity, and the shift in register from comic to devotional is dramatically managed. This is not naivete — it is craft.
