# Acting Theory and Training

## The Big Picture

Acting theory is the systematic study of how performance is produced — how an actor creates a character, maintains consistency across performances, and produces an audience experience. Modern acting theory begins with Stanislavski's systematic approach (late 19th c.) and has branched into multiple traditions ranging from the strictly psychological (Method) to the rigorously physical (Lecoq, Suzuki) to the technique-focused (Meisner).

+------------------------------------------------------------------+

|  units and objectives, through-line, emotional memory           |

## Layer 1: Stanislavski's System — The Formal Decomposition

Konstantin Stanislavski (1863–1938) developed his system over decades of practice at the Moscow Art Theater. It is a formal methodology — a structured decomposition of the acting task into analyzable components.

### The System — Core Concepts

```
STANISLAVSKI SYSTEM — STRUCTURED DECOMPOSITION
------------------------------------------------
An actor approaches a role by working through a hierarchy
of analytical/imaginative tasks:

1. GIVEN CIRCUMSTANCES
   All the facts of the play: time, place, events preceding
   the play, social relationships, historical context.
   The given circumstances are the environment within which
   the character makes choices.
   Like: the constraints of a problem before solving it.

2. THE MAGIC "IF"
   "If I were in these circumstances, what would I do?"
   The actor does not pretend to BE the character;
   they imagine themselves IN the character's situation.
   This is not a loss of self but a transfer of self into
   imaginary circumstances.
   The "if" converts the character's reality into the
   actor's imagination: activates the actor's will and
   creativity.

3. UNITS AND OBJECTIVES (bits/tasks)
   Divide the play into UNITS (beats/sections) — each unit
   is defined by a single objective: what does the character
   WANT in this moment?

   Objectives must be:
     - Expressed as an infinitive verb: "to get sympathy,"
       "to prevent him leaving," "to dominate"
     - Active (not passive): "to want tea" = bad;
       "to make her get the tea" = good
     - Achievable within the unit

4. SUPER-OBJECTIVE and THROUGH-LINE OF ACTION
   The SUPER-OBJECTIVE is the character's overall want
   across the entire play.
   The THROUGH-LINE OF ACTION (supertask) is the continuous
   thread of desire that connects all units to the
   super-objective.
   Like: a recursive goal hierarchy where each unit
   objective serves the super-objective.

   Example: Hamlet
     Unit objective: "To find out whether the Ghost is real"
     Super-objective: "To avenge my father and restore justice"
     Stanislavski: even the most passive-seeming scene has
     an objective; the actor's job is to find it.

5. EMOTIONAL MEMORY (affective memory)
   Recall past personal emotional experiences to fuel
   the character's emotions in the scene.
   Controversial: Strasberg developed this into the
   Method (see below); Stanislavski himself backed away
   from it in his late work, preferring "physical actions."

6. PHYSICAL ACTIONS (late Stanislavski)
   The Method of Physical Actions: start with DOING,
   not FEELING. Physical action induces emotion.
   "The body leads; the emotions follow."
   If you perform the action truthfully, the emotion
   will arise naturally.
   This is the foundation for Meisner's approach.
```

### The System as Structured Decomposition

```
ANALOGY FOR THE TCS READER
---------------------------
Stanislavski's system is a STRUCTURED DECOMPOSITION
of the acting problem:

The "problem" = produce truthful, repeatable,
  character-consistent behavior in imaginary circumstances

The decomposition:
  PLAY → ACTS → UNITS/BEATS
  Each unit has:
    - Given circumstances (environment/state)
    - Objective (desired state/goal)
    - Action (behavior to achieve goal)
    - Obstacle (what resists the action)
    - Result (whether objective achieved)

  This is:
  f(given_circumstances, objective) → action → outcome
  The actor is computing the optimal action given
  the state and the goal.

The THROUGH-LINE is the recursive structure:
  Unit objectives aggregate to scene objectives
  Scene objectives aggregate to act objectives
  Act objectives serve the super-objective
  Like: local optimization serving a global function

This is not a metaphor — the systematic decomposition
is the genuine form of the analysis. Stanislavski was
applying engineering-style thinking to the problem of
acting before "engineering thinking" was a cultural category.
```

## Layer 2: American Derivatives — The Great Schism

Stanislavski visited the US in 1923 with the Moscow Art Theater. The American theater establishment was transformed. Three major teachers developed his ideas in different directions:

```
THE THREE-WAY SPLIT
-------------------

STRASBERG (Lee Strasberg, 1901-1982; Actors Studio, NYC)
  → Emphasized EMOTIONAL MEMORY as primary tool
  → The actor digs into personal trauma to fuel character
  → Deep psychological truth at the cost of technique
  → Associated with: Marlon Brando, James Dean, Marilyn Monroe,
    Al Pacino, Robert De Niro, Dustin Hoffman
  → Criticism: actors lost in their own psychological material;
    the character is buried under the actor's personal emotion;
    the audience doesn't need your autobiography, they need
    the character's reality

STELLA ADLER (1901-1992; Stella Adler Studio)
  → IMAGINATION over memory
  → "The actor lives in the imagination, not in his past"
  → Use your imagination to create the character's
    circumstances vividly; don't raid your own traumas
  → Study: history, social circumstances, clothing, manners
    of the period — external research fuels imagination
  → Associated with: Marlon Brando (studied with Adler),
    Robert De Niro (also studied with Adler)
  → Adler directly contradicted Strasberg; she had studied
    with Stanislavski himself (Paris, 1934)

MEISNER (Sanford Meisner, 1905-1997; Neighborhood Playhouse)
  → LIVING TRUTHFULLY IN IMAGINARY CIRCUMSTANCES
  → The actor's attention must be on the OTHER ACTOR, not
    on their own emotional state
  → "Acting is reacting" — the character's response arises
    from genuinely listening and responding to the other
  → THE REPETITION EXERCISE: two actors repeat a simple
    observation to each other, responding to HOW it is said
    not what (content eventually abandoned; the exercise
    trains pure responsiveness)
  → Associated with: James Gandolfini, Grace Kelly, Steve McQueen,
    Jeff Goldblum, Diane Keaton
```

## Layer 3: Physical Approaches — Embodied Acting

Against the American psychologically-focused Method, a parallel tradition emphasizes the body as the primary instrument:

### Meyerhold's Biomechanics

```
VSEVOLOD MEYERHOLD (1874–1940)
--------------------------------
Stanislavski's student who rejected him.
Biomechanics (developed 1921+): a system of physical
training based on the principle that emotion follows
physical action (the opposite of "feel it first").

THE REFLEX ARC:
  A physical "etude" (exercise) has three phases:
  1. Otkaz: preparatory counter-movement (the windup)
  2. Posyl: the action itself
  3. Tochka: the stop/conclusion

  Physical economy: all unnecessary movement eliminated.
  Each gesture is precise, motivated, effective.

  Influenced by: biomechanics of industrial labor
  (Taylor's scientific management — efficient motion
  analysis), commedia dell'arte, and acrobatics.

  Political context: Meyerhold worked in the early
  Soviet period, designing a theater for the new
  workers' state. Physical precision → the efficient
  worker-actor. Biomechanics ≠ Taylorism but the
  industrial metaphor is not accidental.

  Fate: Meyerhold was arrested 1939, tortured, shot 1940.
  His wife was murdered in their apartment.
  His theater was closed by Stalin.
```

### Jacques Lecoq and the Neutral Mask

```
JACQUES LECOQ (1921–1999; Ecole Jacques Lecoq, Paris)
------------------------------------------------------
Influenced by: commedia, mime, Decroux's corporeal mime,
  Italian theater, sport, animal movement

THE NEUTRAL MASK:
  A plain leather mask that covers the face and removes
  all expression. The actor must express everything
  through the body.
  Purpose: discover "neutral" — a state of pure physical
  availability before character, before attitude, before
  emotion. A zero point.
  "The neutral mask is the beginning of everything."

BUILDING FROM NEUTRAL:
  From neutral: actors explore the physical world
  (elements: earth, fire, water, air; materials)
  Then: animals (observe and embody)
  Then: commedia half-masks (fixed types)
  Then: bouffon (grotesque social satire — the outcast
    who reveals the truth through deformity and excess)
  Then: clown (your own personal comic persona —
    the most difficult, found late in training)

THE LECOQ SCHOOL:
  No "Lecoq technique" to apply — Lecoq prepared actors
  for whatever style they would work in by developing
  physical intelligence, observation, and creativity.
  Graduates: Yasmina Reza (playwright), Geoffrey Rush,
  Complicite (Simon McBurney), Théâtre du Soleil.
```

### Suzuki Method

```
TADASHI SUZUKI (b. 1939; SCOT — Suzuki Company of Toga)
---------------------------------------------------------
Japanese theater director; method developed 1970s+

CORE PRINCIPLE: "Grammar of the Feet"
  The body's connection to the earth (lower body
  strength, stamped rhythms) is the foundation of
  all theatrical presence.
  Western training focuses on the voice and upper body.
  Suzuki: the lower body — the way you walk, stand,
  root yourself — is what projects presence in a theater.

TRAINING:
  Slowdown walking: extreme slow walking, each step
    placed with full weight and attention
  Stomping patterns: complex rhythmic sequences
  Working with gravity: the floor as partner
  Sudden acceleration and freeze
  Classical Japanese theater as model

EFFECT:
  Actors appear monumentally present on stage.
  Large theaters, physical theater, Greek tragedy
  (Suzuki has staged much Greek tragedy) benefit.
  The technique transfers well across cultures because
  it focuses on the universal physics of the body
  in gravity, not on cultural gesture.
```

## Layer 4: The Diderot Paradox — Feeling vs. Technique

Denis Diderot raised the fundamental acting question in **Paradox of the Actor** (1773):

```
THE DIDEROT PARADOX
--------------------
Question: Should the actor FEEL the character's emotions,
  or should the actor CONTROL technique while appearing to feel?

Diderot's answer: the greatest acting is CONTROLLED TECHNIQUE.
  The actor who truly feels their role is inconsistent —
  some nights the emotion is present, other nights absent.
  The actor who controls technique produces the effect
  of emotion reliably, night after night.

TWO POLES:
  FEELING (the internal approach):
    Stanislavski (emotional memory), Strasberg (Method)
    Authenticity; "truth"; risk of inconsistency
    The performance is "happening" for the first time

  TECHNIQUE (the external approach):
    Diderot; Brecht (demonstrating, not being)
    Reliability; repeatability; the audience's experience
    The performance is a crafted object

RESOLUTION:
  Most working actors and teachers say: both.
  Technique creates the structure within which emotion
  can arise safely. Emotion without technique is chaos.
  Technique without emotion is dead.
  Meisner: technique trains you to respond genuinely;
  then in performance you use the technique but the
  response is real.

Compare: a jazz musician's technique (scales, patterns)
enables spontaneous musical feeling. You cannot
improvise freely without mastery of the grammar.
The more internalized the technique, the freer the feeling.
```

## Layer 5: Audition and Practical Acting

```
PRACTICAL ACTING TOOLKIT (what working actors use)
----------------------------------------------------
OBJECTIVE: What do I want? (from the scene partner)
TACTIC: What am I doing to get it?
  → change tactics when the current one doesn't work

ACTIONING: verb-based analysis
  Every line = a transitive verb applied to your partner
  "I threaten you" / "I seduce you" / "I mock you"
  This keeps the actor active and transitive, not passive

OBSTACLES: What stands between me and my objective?
  External: what they do, the situation
  Internal: my own fear, contradictory desire

THE FIVE QUESTIONS:
  1. Who am I?
  2. Where am I?
  3. What time is it? (period, season, hour)
  4. What do I want?
  5. Why do I want it now?

PREPARATION:
  What happened immediately before the scene?
  The given circumstances of the immediate moment
  (Stanislavski: what are the "proposed circumstances"
  that produce this entrance?)

SCORE OF THE ROLE:
  A written or mental record of objectives, tactics,
  decisions across the whole play.
  Like: an annotated algorithm — each decision point
  has a recorded resolution that can be repeated.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"The Method" is not Stanislavski's system**
Stanislavski developed his system over 50 years and changed it significantly (moving away from emotional memory toward physical actions in his late work). "The Method" specifically refers to Lee Strasberg's version, which emphasizes emotional memory heavily. Stella Adler studied with Stanislavski directly and brought back a different account — she and Strasberg disagreed publicly.

**Acting training is not one thing**
The range from Strasberg (deep psychological immersion) to Lecoq (physical neutrality and clown) to Suzuki (lower body presence and stamped rhythm) to Brecht (social gesture and critical demonstration) covers a wide theoretical space. Most professional training programs combine elements rather than committing purely to one school.

**"Living truthfully" does not mean "being yourself"**
Meisner's formulation "living truthfully in imaginary circumstances" is sometimes misread as "just be natural." The "imaginary circumstances" are the key — you bring your real responses to a FICTIONAL situation. The work is creating a richly imagined fictional world and responding truthfully to it. This is not the same as doing nothing.

**The neutral mask is not silence or blankness**
The neutral mask is a tool for discovering physical expressiveness, not physical suppression. When all facial expression is removed, the actor must communicate through the whole body. Lecoq's "neutral" is not deadness — it is "a state of potential, of readiness, before any expression."
