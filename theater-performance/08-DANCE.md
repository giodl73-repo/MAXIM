# Dance

## The Big Picture

Dance is organized movement of the human body, usually in time with music, for aesthetic purposes. It is the oldest of the performing arts (predating writing by tens of thousands of years), the most universal (every human culture has it), and the most ephemeral (until notation and recording systems were developed, it existed only in performance and in the bodies of trained dancers).

## Layer 1: Ballet — Origins and Structure

### Court Origins

```
BALLET ORIGINS (1489-1670)
---------------------------
Began not as theater but as court entertainment:
  1489: Bergamo, Italy — elaborate banquet entertainment
    with "dances" between courses representing themes.
  Ballo: Italian for "ball game" or "dance."
  Balthasar de Beaujoyeulx: Ballet Comique de la Reine (1581,
    Paris) — considered the first "ballet" as a whole event
    (5.5 hours; court performance).

  Early ballet = spectacle for the court nobility:
    The AUDIENCE was also sometimes the PERFORMERS
    (noble guests danced in the interludes).
    Louis XIV (Sun King) was an avid dancer and
    performed in court ballets into his mid-20s.
    He founded the Academie Royale de Danse in 1661 —
    the first formal dance institution in the West.

  The classical ballet vocabulary (five positions,
  turned-out legs, the technical terms) was codified
  by the French court ballet tradition.
  Raoul Auger Feuillet published Choregraphie (1700) —
  the first notation system for ballet.
```

### Romantic Ballet

```
ROMANTIC BALLET (1830-1870)
----------------------------
A revolution in content, style, and technology:

CONTENT SHIFT:
  From court allegory and mythology to supernatural
  tales of sylphs, wilis, and spirits.
  The ballerina became the focus (not the male dancer).

TECHNOLOGIES THAT ENABLED IT:
  Pointe technique (dancing on the tips of the toes):
    Made possible by a reinforced tip in the slipper.
    Creates the appearance of weightlessness, ethereality.
    Marie Taglioni is credited with the first significant
    use of pointe work (La Sylphide, 1832).
  Gas lighting: enabled atmospheric effects (moonlight,
    forest at night) that the Romantic aesthetic required.

KEY WORKS:
  La Sylphide (1832): a Scottish man loves a supernatural
    sylph; when he catches her, she dies. First Romantic ballet.
  Giselle (1841): peasant girl dies of a broken heart;
    returns as a wili (spirit of a betrayed maiden);
    saves the man who broke her heart from the other wilis.
    Still performed; the ultimate Romantic ballet.
```

### The Imperial Russian School: Petipa

```
MARIUS PETIPA (1818-1910)
--------------------------
French-born, worked in Russia 1847-1903.
The most influential choreographer in ballet history.
Formalized the "classical ballet" we know today.

KEY STRUCTURES Petipa established:
1. The Grand Pas de Deux
   The centerpiece of classical ballet:
   - Adagio: ballerina and partner in supported turns,
             lifts, balances. Slow, expansive, display of
             line and partnership.
   - Male Variation: the male dancer's solo.
             Technical display: jumps, turns.
   - Female Variation: the ballerina's solo.
             Pointe work, turns, balances.
   - Coda: both together; virtuosic finale.
             Repeated pirouettes, jumps, chaîné turns.

2. Corps de ballet patterns
   Large groups of women dancing in geometric formations:
   lines, circles, chevrons, waves.
   Swan Lake's corps of 16-24 swans is the archetype.
   The corps as visual texture and structural rhythm.

KEY WORKS (with Tchaikovsky):
  Swan Lake (1877 original; Petipa/Ivanov revival 1895)
  The Sleeping Beauty (1890)
  The Nutcracker (1892)
  These three are the core classical ballet repertoire.
  Tchaikovsky's scores remain the gold standard of ballet music.
```

### Ballets Russes and 20th-Century Ballet

```
BALLETS RUSSES (Sergei Diaghilev, 1909-1929)
---------------------------------------------
The most important company in 20th-c ballet.
Diaghilev (impresario) assembled the best choreographers,
composers, and designers of the era:
  Choreographers: Fokine, Nijinsky, Massine, Nijinska,
    Balanchine
  Composers: Stravinsky (Firebird 1910, Petrushka 1911,
    Rite of Spring 1913), Prokofiev, Debussy, Ravel
  Designers: Picasso, Matisse, Cocteau

THE RITE OF SPRING (1913):
  Choreography: Vaclav Nijinsky
  Music: Igor Stravinsky
  Premiere: May 29, 1913, Paris — RIOT in the theater.
  The story: the chosen maiden dances herself to death
  in a pagan ritual.
  The music: rhythmically violent, harmonically brutal,
  no conventional melody. The famous 34-note rhythmic
  irregularity in the "Augurs of Spring."
  The choreography: turned-IN feet (opposite of classical
  ballet); hunched posture; angular, jerky movement.
  Everything that classical ballet prized, violated.
  The riot: legend (probably both pro and con factions
  in the audience; the accounts vary). Authentic: the
  work was genuinely disorienting.
  Now recognized as the most important musical event
  of the 20th century (with Schonberg's op. 11 Pieces
  as the other candidate).

BALANCHINE (George Balanchine, 1904-1983):
  Co-founder of New York City Ballet.
  "Neoclassical" ballet: pure movement, no narrative,
  the body in dialogue with music.
  "Ballet is woman" — the ballerina as the ideal vehicle.
  Apollo (1928), Serenade (1934), Agon (1957).
  The American ballet tradition flows from Balanchine.
```

## Layer 2: Modern Dance — The Body's Liberation

### Isadora Duncan

```
ISADORA DUNCAN (1877-1927)
--------------------------
Not a systematic theorist but a physical revolutionary.
Rejected: pointe shoes, corsets, the ballet vocabulary.
Wore: bare feet, flowing Greek-inspired tunics.
Danced to: Beethoven, Brahms, Wagner (music not written
  for dance — ballet had its own music or trivial scores).

Duncan's claim:
  Dance should arise from the natural movement of the body,
  particularly from the solar plexus (center of the torso)
  and the relationship to gravity.
  Barefoot contact with the earth.
  Movement inspired by nature (waves, trees, wind).

Her school: trained "Isadorables" — six girls she adopted.
Her legacy: she opened possibilities but founded no
  technique. Modern dance had to develop its own methods.
```

### Martha Graham — The Technique

```
MARTHA GRAHAM (1894-1991)
--------------------------
The systematizer of modern dance — she developed a
formal technique still taught worldwide.

GRAHAM TECHNIQUE — CORE PRINCIPLES:
  CONTRACTION AND RELEASE:
    The center of movement is the pelvis/lower torso.
    Contraction: the torso curves inward (like a hollow);
      the back rounds; it is the fundamental gesture
      of breath, pain, intensity.
    Release: the torso opens; the back extends; ease.
    These are the two poles between which all Graham
    movement oscillates.

  FLOOR WORK:
    Graham dancers work extensively on the floor
    (sitting, lying, falling) — not just vertical.
    The relationship to the floor is central.
    Contrast: ballet aspires to be airborne; Graham
    is rooted and earthbound.

  SPIRAL:
    The torso twists against the lower body; the hips
    face one way while the upper body faces another.
    Creates tension, complexity, expressiveness.

KEY WORKS:
  Lamentation (1930): a solo in a tube of jersey fabric.
    A figure of grief that cannot escape its own body.
  Appalachian Spring (1944): Copland score.
    Pioneer life, hope, restraint. The "Shaker" hymn.
  Cave of the Heart (1946): Medea myth.
```

### Merce Cunningham — Movement for Its Own Sake

```
MERCE CUNNINGHAM (1919-2009)
-----------------------------
Graham's student; rejected her expressionism.

CUNNINGHAM'S POSITION:
  Movement is not about expression or emotion.
  Movement is its own thing — it does not need to
  mean anything beyond itself.
  "Movement can be expressive only in the sense that it
  is human and therefore partakes of humanity."

CHANCE PROCEDURES:
  Cunningham used I Ching (chance operations) to determine
  the order of movements, sections, and combinations.
  He separated the DANCE from the MUSIC completely:
    Music (by John Cage and others) was composed
    independently; the dance was composed independently;
    they were performed simultaneously but neither
    "illustrated" the other.
    The audience could watch either; the relationship
    was discovered in performance.

  This is the opposite of Wagnerian Gesamtkunstwerk
  (all elements unified in a single vision).
  Cunningham: each element is autonomous; the
  relationship is accidental and discovered by the viewer.

INFLUENCE:
  Opened the door for postmodern dance.
  Collaboration with John Cage (music), Robert
  Rauschenberg and Jasper Johns (design).
```

## Layer 3: Postmodern Dance and the Judson Church

```
JUDSON DANCE THEATER (1962-1966)
---------------------------------
A collective of artists who performed at the Judson
Memorial Church in Greenwich Village, New York.

THE RADICAL QUESTION: What is dance?
  Can everyday movement be dance?
  Can tasks (carrying, walking, sleeping) be dance?
  Does dance require trained bodies?
  Does dance require music?
  Does dance require a theater?

ANSWERS FROM JUDSON:
  Steve Paxton: everyday movement is dance.
    Satisfyin' Lover (1967): 42 people walk across a stage.
    No trained dancers; no special movement; just walking.
    The question: when you frame ordinary movement as
    performance, what changes? The frame, not the content.

  Yvonne Rainer: Task-based movement.
    Trio A (1966): no climaxes, no held positions,
    continuous, even effort throughout.
    "No to spectacle, no to virtuosity, no to transformations
    and magic and make-believe, no to the glamour and
    transcendency of the star image, no to the heroic and the
    anti-heroic, no to trash imagery, no to involvement
    of performer or spectator, no to style, no to camp,
    no to seduction of spectator by the wiles of the
    performer, no to eccentricity, no to moving or being
    moved." — "No Manifesto" (1965)

CONTACT IMPROVISATION (Steve Paxton, 1972+):
  Two or more bodies in physical contact, sharing
  weight, following the physics of momentum and gravity.
  No fixed choreography; no prescribed roles.
  The point of contact generates the movement.
  Widely practiced globally; a genuinely new form.
```

### Tanztheater — Pina Bausch

```
PINA BAUSCH (1940-2009, Wuppertal Tanztheater)
------------------------------------------------
Synthesizes dance, theater, and repetition in a way
that is neither dance-with-theater nor theater-with-dance.

Bausch's method:
  Questions to her company: "What frightens you?"
  "What do you do when you are happy?"
  The company's responses become the material.
  The work is about human relationships, longing,
  aggression, ritual, and the gap between what we
  want and what we do.

FORMAL FEATURES:
  Repetition: actions are repeated many times,
    sometimes hundreds of times.
    Repetition changes meaning; what starts as natural
    becomes strange (compare Shklovsky's defamiliarization).
  Verbal text mixed with movement.
  Casual clothing alongside formal dress.
  The stage often filled with earth, water, leaves,
    carnations — the environment is itself a performer.

KEY WORKS:
  Cafe Muller (1978): Bausch herself dances blindly
    through a room full of chairs while a man
    desperately removes chairs before she hits them.
    A man and woman embrace; other characters keep
    pulling them apart; they are always reassembled.
    Repeated. The image of love and obstruction, endlessly.
  Kontakthof (1978): "Contact court" — adults in formal
    wear circle each other, touch, retreat.
    Later performed by senior citizens (2000) and teenagers (2008).
```

## Layer 4: Non-Western Classical Dance Forms

### Kathak

```
KATHAK (North Indian classical dance)
--------------------------------------
Origins: traveling storytellers (kathakas) who narrated
  mythological tales through movement, gesture, and song.
  Developed in the Mughal court: refined as a courtly art
  that absorbed Persian and Islamic elements.

CHARACTERISTICS:
  Intricate footwork: the ghungroo (ankle bells with
    many small bells) must be heard in every tap.
    The beats per minute and complexity of footwork
    patterns (tatkaar) rival flamenco.
  Rapid spins (chakkar): fast multiple pirouettes;
    a kathak dancer can do 64+ turns.
  Abhinaya: expressive mime of stories and emotions.
    Hand gestures (mudra), facial expressions, eye movement.
  Improvisation within structure: the dancer improvises
    rhythmic variations against a fixed tala (rhythmic cycle).
    The tabla (drum) and dancer improvise together —
    a conversation between percussion and footwork.

TWO SCHOOLS (gharanas):
  Lucknow gharana: courtly, soft, expressive.
  Jaipur gharana: vigorous, energetic, footwork emphasis.
```

### Noh Movement

```
NOH MOVEMENT (Japan, 14th c.+)
--------------------------------
Noh is a complete performance form combining dance,
drama, poetry, and music. The movement is its most
distinctive and misunderstood element.

EXTREME SLOWNESS:
  Noh performance moves at a fraction of naturalistic
  speed. A walk across the stage may take 10 minutes.
  This is not inefficiency — it is the aesthetic.

  Why slowness?
  MA (the interval, the pause):
    The key Noh concept. The meaningful empty space
    between sounds, between movements.
    "Noh is in the pauses." — Zeami Motokiyo
    (the founder of Noh theater, 14th c.)

  At this tempo, every tiny movement is amplified:
  the turning of a head, the raising of a hand.
  The audience reads the smallest gesture as large.

  The mask: Noh masks are fixed expressions that seem
  to change through the lighting and the dancer's
  micro-movements. Tilt slightly down: sadness.
  Tilt slightly up: joy. The performer does not move
  the face — the observer's perception shifts.

  MONOGUKAR ("objectifying"): Noh dancers do not
  "feel" the character — they embody the formal
  representation of the character. The craft is
  in the precision of the formal vocabulary.
  Compare: Diderot's paradox in Western acting theory.
```

## Dance Notation — How Movement Is Written Down

```
MOVEMENT NOTATION SYSTEMS
--------------------------
BENESH NOTATION (1955):
  Five-line staff (like music); the body represented
  on the staff; body positions notated in sequence.
  Used by the Royal Ballet and most classical ballet
  companies.

LABANOTATION (Rudolf Laban, 1928):
  More comprehensive than Benesh. Notates direction,
  level, duration, dynamic quality, body part used.
  Used for modern dance and ethnochoreological work.
  The staff is read from bottom to top (time moves up).

MOTIF NOTATION (Laban-based):
  Simpler than Labanotation; for less detailed purposes.

THE NOTATION PROBLEM:
  Dance notation is like programming: it describes
  a process that must be executed (embodied) to be
  real. The notation is not the dance, just as the
  score is not the music. The notation loses:
  - Exact dynamic quality
  - Individual performer's quality of movement
  - The feel of the space
  - What is NOT written (the between-movements transitions)

VIDEO/DIGITAL:
  Video documentation has mostly replaced notation
  as the primary preservation tool.
  But: video captures one viewpoint; it cannot capture
  the three-dimensional spatial design.
  Digital volumetric capture (motion capture) is
  the closest approximation to notational completeness.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Modern dance" does not mean "contemporary dance"**
"Modern dance" is a historical style (roughly Duncan to Graham to Cunningham, c. 1900–1960s) characterized by a break from ballet. "Contemporary dance" means dance made now. Contemporary dance can be ballet, modern, postmodern, hip-hop, or hybrid. They are not synonyms.

**Ballet was originally performed by men**
Until the Romantic era, the most important performers in ballet were men — the ballerina as star is a 19th-century development. Louis XIV danced leading roles in court ballets. In early ballet history, women danced in pantomime and lesser roles.

**Butoh is not just "Japanese performance art"**
Butoh is a specific form that emerged in Japan in the late 1950s (Tatsumi Hijikata and Kazuo Ohno). It is characterized by slow, controlled movement; white body paint; extreme physical transformation; and engagement with taboo and death. It emerged partly as a response to the bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, partly as a rejection of both Western modern dance and classical Japanese forms.

**Cunningham separating music from dance was not indifference to music**
Cunningham and John Cage were long-term artistic and personal collaborators. The separation was a principled aesthetic position: music and movement both exist in time, and their independent composition produces an unpredicted relationship in performance. This is not hostility to music but a different way of relating art forms — not hierarchy (music serves dance or dance serves music) but autonomous co-presence.
