# Renaissance and Baroque Theater

## The Big Picture

The Renaissance (roughly 1450–1600) and Baroque (1600–1750) periods produced the most theatrically diverse century and a half in history: the Elizabethan public theater in England, the Spanish Golden Age, Italian perspective scenery, and the invention of opera — all within 150 years. The printed text also transformed theater: Shakespeare's plays survived because of print; the theater itself remained oral and performative.

## Layer 1: The Elizabethan Public Theater

### Material Conditions (again, at more depth)

```
THE ELIZABETHAN THEATER SYSTEM (1576–1642)
--------------------------------------------
1576: James Burbage builds The Theatre (Shoreditch)
      — the first permanent purpose-built playhouse
      outside the City of London
      (City authorities were hostile; playwrights built
      outside city jurisdiction)

1599: The Globe built from The Theatre's timbers
      (when the Burbages' lease expired and the landlord
      was uncooperative; they literally dismantled the
      building and moved it across the Thames)

MAJOR PLAYHOUSES:
  The Theatre (1576-1599): first permanent playhouse
  The Curtain (1577): near The Theatre; used as backup
  The Globe (1599): Shakespeare's company; home of Hamlet,
    Othello, King Lear, Macbeth, The Tempest
  The Rose (1587): Marlowe's venue; excavated 1989
  The Swan (1595): illustrated in the van Buchell sketch
    (our primary contemporary visual document of interior)
  Blackfriars (indoor, 1608): indoor theater; wealthier
    audience; candlelight; more elaborate music/effects

INDOOR vs. OUTDOOR:
  Outdoor (Globe, Rose): standing groundlings, afternoon
  Indoor (Blackfriars, Phoenix): seated, evening, candles
    higher ticket prices, wealthier audience
  Shakespeare's late plays (The Tempest, Winter's Tale)
  show indoor theatrical effects — these were Blackfriars plays
```

### The Thrust Stage and Its Consequences

```
THE THRUST STAGE GEOMETRY
--------------------------
                     [TIRING HOUSE]
                      (backstage)
           +----------+-----+----------+
           |     backstage rooms        |
           | (costume, props, exits)    |
           +----------+-----+----------+
           |      STAGE DOORS          |
           |   Left         Right      |
           |                           |
     BOX   |    MAIN THRUST STAGE      |   BOX
           |    (projects into yard)   |
           |                           |
     BOX   +------+         +------+   |   BOX
                  |         |          |
                  |  YARD   |          |
    GALLERIES     | (stand) |          |  GALLERIES
    (three tiers) |  1d     |          |  (seated)
                  +----+----+          |
                       |               |
                       +--------  -----+
                           AUDIENCE
                        (surrounding 3 sides)

KEY CONSEQUENCE:
  The audience surrounds the stage on THREE sides.
  Soliloquy is addressed into THIS configuration.
  "To be or not to be" — Hamlet speaks to an audience
  that is literally around him.
  The aside, the soliloquy, the direct address:
  all are dramatically natural in this geometry.
  They become awkward in the proscenium theater.

NO SCENERY (primarily):
  A few props; costume signals location; words do the work.
  "This castle hath a pleasant seat" — we see only
  a stage; Malcolm's words create Inverness.
  The audience's imagination is the scenic design.

DAYLIGHT:
  Afternoon performances, outdoors.
  "Globe" is the world; the stage is the world.
  "All the world's a stage" is not just a metaphor —
  the physical theater made this claim literally.
```

### Shakespeare's Dramaturgy

```
SHAKESPEARE'S TECHNICAL INNOVATIONS
-------------------------------------
1. VERBAL SCENE-CREATION
   No scenery → the text creates all locations:
   "But soft, what light through yonder window breaks?
   It is the east, and Juliet is the sun."
   Juliet is standing on a balcony (probably an upper
   gallery above the stage door). Romeo describes moonlight.
   The words conjure the scene the theater cannot show.

2. LAYERED PROSE AND VERSE
   Verse (iambic pentameter): royalty, elevated moments
   Prose: comic scenes, lower-status characters,
          characters who have lost their reason
          (Ophelia's mad scenes are prose)
   Rhymed couplets: exits ("the time is out of joint" etc.),
          spells, songs
   The register shift IS dramatic information.

3. THE SOLILOQUY
   Direct address to the audience.
   Not a private thought overheard — a public confidence.
   Hamlet tells US what he's thinking; we are conspirators.
   This is only possible with a thrust stage (audience
   surrounds; the actor can look directly at them).
   The proscenium theater makes the soliloquy awkward
   (you're watching someone talk to himself in a box).

4. MULTIPLOT STRUCTURE
   Multiple plots at different social registers:
   Main plot (kings, nobles) + subplot (servants, clowns)
   The subplot comments on, inverts, and illuminates
   the main plot.
   King Lear: Lear's blindness to his daughters ::
             Gloucester's blindness to his sons
   The Tempest: Prospero's plots :: Caliban/Stephano subplot
```

## Layer 2: Christopher Marlowe and the Elizabethan Stage

```
CHRISTOPHER MARLOWE (1564–1593)
---------------------------------
Died at 29 in a tavern brawl (possibly espionage).
Established the forms Shakespeare inherited:

DOCTOR FAUSTUS (1592):
  Blank verse of immense power:
  "Was this the face that launch'd a thousand ships,
  And burnt the topless towers of Ilium?"
  The "mighty line" — Jonson's phrase for Marlowe's
  iambic pentameter.

  STRUCTURE:
  A scholar sells his soul for 24 years of magical power.
  Wastes most of the time on conjuring tricks.
  The final soliloquy ("Ah Faustus, now hast thou but
  one bare hour to live") — 59 lines of real-time
  countdown as midnight approaches.
  This is the most technically brilliant soliloquy
  before Shakespeare.

TAMBURLAINE (1587):
  First major success of blank verse on the English stage.
  Established: the conquering hero, Senecan rhetoric,
  the "overreacher" figure who transcends all limits.

EDWARD II (1592):
  The first tragedy of a historical English king.
  The first major treatment of same-sex desire on the
  English stage (Gaveston and Edward II).
  Source for Shakespeare's Richard II.
```

## Layer 3: The Spanish Golden Age

The Spanish Golden Age (roughly 1580–1680) is underrepresented in anglophone accounts of theater history but was comparable in richness to the Elizabethan:

```
SPANISH GOLDEN AGE THEATER
----------------------------
CORRAL DE COMEDIAS: the physical theater
  An open courtyard (corral) surrounded by houses
  whose upper-floor windows serve as boxes.
  A stage at one end; a covered awning.
  Women could attend (unlike Elizabethan theater).
  Women could perform (Italy and Spain had women actors
  earlier than England and France).

LOPE DE VEGA (1562–1635):
  Claimed to have written 1,500+ plays (400+ survive).
  "The Phoenix" — he set the terms of Spanish dramatic theory.
  His Arte Nuevo de Hacer Comedias (1609):
    Deliberately breaks classical unities: "When I have
    to write a comedy, I lock up the precepts with
    six keys and banish Terence and Plautus from my
    study... I write according to the art invented by
    those who sought the common voice."
  The comedia: a mixed form (comedy + tragedy) in 3 acts
    with honor plots, class conflict, and romantic intrigue.

CALDERON DE LA BARCA (1600–1681):
  La vida es sueño (Life Is a Dream, 1635):
    Segismundo, a prince imprisoned from birth (his
    horoscope predicted he'd be a tyrant), is freed
    for a trial. Behaves brutally; is put back to sleep.
    Told it was a dream. Released again; this time
    behaves with honor. Conclusion: "all of life is
    a dream, and dreams are dreams."
  The philosophical sophistication: is reality
  distinguishable from a sufficiently vivid dream?
  (Anticipates Descartes' evil demon argument, 1641)

AUTO SACRAMENTAL:
  One-act allegorical play on Eucharistic themes.
  Performed on Corpus Christi (the host feast).
  Calderon was the master of this form.
  All of Christian theology as a dramatic form.
```

## Layer 4: Italian Scenography — The Proscenium Revolution

While England and Spain built thrust stages, Italy developed a different theatrical architecture that would eventually dominate European theater:

```
ITALIAN PERSPECTIVE SCENERY
-----------------------------
Sebastiano Serlio's Architettura (1545):
  Three standard set types:
    Tragic scene: columns, arches, palaces
    Comic scene: ordinary houses, shops, taverns
    Pastoral scene: trees, landscape, grottos
  All using single-point perspective — the entire
  stage picture converges on one vanishing point.

CONSEQUENCE:
  The perfect viewing position is a SINGLE POINT:
  the seat where the perspective is correct.
  This becomes the prince's seat (the focal point of
  the court entertainment audience).
  The theater is redesigned around this geometry:
  proscenium arch (a frame) isolates the painted stage
  picture; the audience sits in front, not around.

THE PROSCENIUM ARCH
  The "picture frame" stage: audience in front, looking
  at a framed picture. Developed in Italian court theater
  and opera houses. Spread to France (17th c.) → England
  (after 1660 Restoration) → dominant form by 1700.

  The thrust stage → proscenium shift:
  THRUST:    audience surrounds stage on 3 sides
             actor cannot hide; soliloquy is direct address
             no scenery; verbal creation of place
  PROSCENIUM: audience faces stage like a painting
              actor can use wings/flies to hide/reveal
              scenery is painted perspective illusion
              soliloquy becomes awkward (who are you talking to?)

  This architectural shift CHANGES the dramatic form:
  Brecht's V-Effekt works by making the proscenium visible.
  You can only break the fourth wall if there IS a fourth wall.
```

## Layer 5: Opera Origins

### The Florentine Camerata

```
THE FLORENTINE CAMERATA (c. 1573–1587)
----------------------------------------
A group of Florentine humanists (Giovanni Bardi, host;
Vincenzo Galilei, father of Galileo; Giulio Caccini;
Jacopo Peri; Ottavio Rinuccini, poet) who met to
discuss the restoration of ancient Greek theater.

THEIR THEORY:
  Greek tragedy was SUNG throughout.
  The power of ancient tragedy came from music enhancing
  the text (not overpowering it as polyphony did).
  They would restore the union of music and drama by
  composing in "stile rappresentativo" (representational
  style): a single melody following the rhythms and
  inflections of speech, with simple accompaniment.
  The text must be clearly audible; music serves the text.

THE FIRST OPERAS:
  Jacopo Peri's Dafne (1598): first opera; text by Rinuccini;
    mostly lost. Private performance for the Camerata.
  Jacopo Peri's Euridice (1600): first surviving complete opera.
    For the wedding of Maria de' Medici and Henri IV of France.
    Mythological subject (Orpheus and Eurydice) — the choice
    was not accidental: Orpheus is THE story of music's power.

MONTEVERDI'S L'ORFEO (1607):
  The first GREAT opera. Mantua, 1607.
  The Camerata's ideas realized with genuine musical genius.
  Monteverdi uses the full range of early Baroque devices:
    Word painting (music illustrates the text's images)
    Affective melody (each character's affect has its own
    musical character — not yet the Leitmotif system,
    but individual musical personalities)
    Dramatic use of dissonance and resolution
  The TOCCATA that opens L'Orfeo is the first orchestral
  overture. The theater of orchestra = staged alongside text.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Shakespeare did not invent the blank verse drama**
Marlowe established blank verse as the dramatic medium; Shakespeare inherited and perfected it. The "mighty line" was Marlowe's; Shakespeare's genius was in what he did with the form, including when he broke it (prose for comic and mad scenes).

**The Globe was not the only or even the primary venue for Shakespeare**
The Globe (outdoor, 1599–1613) is the famous venue, but the Globe burned down in 1613. The Blackfriars (indoor) was arguably more important for Shakespeare's later career. The indoor theater enabled more elaborate music, sound effects, and atmospheric effects that shaped the late romances.

**Opera was invented as an attempt to REVIVE ancient theater, not to create a new form**
The Camerata thought they were restoring Greek dramatic practice. They were wrong about Greek theater being entirely sung — Greek tragedy had sung choral odes and spoken dialogue — but their misunderstanding produced one of the most important new art forms in Western music history.

**"Baroque" in theater means roughly 1600–1750, but the style varied enormously**
Baroque theater includes both the restrained classicism of French neoclassical drama (Racine, Corneille) and the spectacular excess of Italian opera seria. The common thread is the increasing sophistication of theatrical machinery, scenic design, and the growing importance of music in theatrical performance.
