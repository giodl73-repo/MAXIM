# Fingerspelling & Sign Language

## The Big Picture

Fingerspelling (using handshapes to spell words letter-by-letter) is a **subset** of sign language, not a synonym for it. American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete natural language with its own grammar, syntax, and phonology — fundamentally different from English. Fingerspelling is just one tool within it.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                SIGN LANGUAGE SYSTEM MAP                           │
│                                                                  │
│  FINGERSPELLING                                                  │
│  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│  │ One-handed (ASL, most European countries)                │   │
│  │ — 26 handshapes, one per letter                          │   │
│  │ — Used for: names, technical terms, initialisms          │   │
│  │ — Typically 10–20% of an ASL conversation               │   │
│  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤   │
│  │ Two-handed (BSL, Australian Auslan)                      │   │
│  │ — Both hands together form each letter                   │   │
│  │ — Completely different system from ASL                   │   │
│  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                                  │
│  FULL SIGN LANGUAGE                                              │
│  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│  │ ASL (American Sign Language)                             │   │
│  │ — Natural language, NOT English on the hands            │   │
│  │ — Spatial grammar (grammar encoded in 3D space)          │   │
│  │ — Completely distinct from BSL, LSF, etc.               │   │
│  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤   │
│  │ ~200+ distinct sign languages worldwide                  │   │
│  │ — BSL (British), LSF (French), DGS (German)             │   │
│  │ — ISL (International), BANZSL (Aus/NZ/British family)   │   │
│  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## American Manual Alphabet (Fingerspelling)

ASL fingerspelling uses 26 handshapes. Each is described below with an ASCII approximation and key description:

### Position Key for ASCII Diagrams

```
Viewing angle: as seen by the signer looking at their own palm (or: as seen by the viewer watching the signer)

Finger labels:
  T = thumb
  1 = index finger
  2 = middle finger
  3 = ring finger
  4 = pinky
  P = palm
  F = fist

States:
  O = rounded/curled (making 'O' shape)
  E = extended (pointing)
  B = bent (curled at middle joint)
  C = closed / touching
```

### The 26 Handshapes

```
A — Fist with thumb beside fingers (not tucked under)
    [1234 curled into fist, T alongside]
    "Closed fist, thumb up the side"

B — Four fingers together, extended upright; thumb tucked across palm
    [1234 straight together, T folded]
    "Flat hand, thumb tucked"

C — Curved hand like holding a cylinder (C-shape)
    [All fingers and thumb curved in a C]
    "Curved hand, open C"

D — Index up, other fingers curled to thumb (making a circle)
    [1 extended, 234 curled forming circle with T]
    "Index points up, others form circle"

E — All fingers bent at second joint, thumb tucked under
    [1234 bent/hooked, T underneath]
    "Claw/hooked hand"

F — Index and thumb touch to form circle; other three extended
    [1+T circle, 234 extended]
    "OK-like but 2/3/4 straight up"

G — Index points sideways (horizontal), thumb parallel to index
    [1 points horizontally, T parallel below it]
    "Horizontal gun shape pointing sideways"

H — Index and middle point together sideways (horizontal)
    [1+2 together pointing sideways, others curled]
    "Two fingers horizontal"

I — Pinky extended, others curled, thumb tucked
    [4 extended, 123T curled]
    "Pinky up"

J — Like I but trace a 'J' in the air (movement letter)
    [4 extended, then curves to make a J motion]
    "Pinky traces letter J"

K — Index and middle up, index angled slightly, thumb between
    [1 up slightly angled, 2 up, T between 1 and 2]
    "Like peace sign but with thumb between"

L — Index points up, thumb extends (L-shape)
    [1 up, T out = classic gun/L shape]
    "L-shape with index and thumb"

M — Three fingers (1,2,3) bent over thumb
    [123 bent/curved over T which is across palm]
    "Three fingers tucked over thumb"

N — Two fingers (1,2) bent over thumb
    [12 bent over T, 34 curled]
    "Two fingers tucked over thumb"

O — All fingers and thumb curved to meet at tips (O-shape)
    [All form a circle]
    "Full O with all fingertips touching thumb"

P — Like K but hand tilted downward so index points down
    [Same as K rotated so 1 points down/forward]
    "K-shape pointing down"

Q — Like G (pointing index) but pointing downward
    [Same as G rotated pointing downward]
    "G-shape pointing down"

R — Index and middle crossed (index over middle)
    [1 crossed over 2]
    "Crossed fingers (lucky fingers)"

S — Fist with thumb wrapped over top of fingers
    [1234 curled, T over top]
    "Closed fist, thumb across"

T — Fist with thumb between index and middle
    [1234 curled, T tucked between 1 and 2]
    "Thumb-up fist, thumb between fingers"

U — Index and middle together, pointing up
    [1+2 together vertical, 34 curled]
    "Two fingers together, pointing up"

V — Index and middle apart, pointing up (peace sign)
    [1 and 2 spread V-shape vertical]
    "V / peace sign"

W — Index, middle, ring fingers spread, pointing up
    [1,2,3 spread out vertically]
    "Three fingers spread upward"

X — Index hooked/curved (crooked finger)
    [1 bent at second joint, others curled]
    "Hooked/beckoning index finger"

Y — Thumb and pinky extended, other three curled
    [T and 4 extended out, 123 curled]
    "Hang-loose / shaka"

Z — Index draws a Z in the air (movement letter)
    [1 extended, traces Z shape in space]
    "Index draws letter Z"
```

**Movement letters** (J and Z): these require movement, not just a static handshape — J traces the letter J, Z traces the letter Z. In practice, native ASL users reading fingerspelling internalize these kinetic patterns.

## BSL Two-Handed Fingerspelling

British Sign Language uses **both hands**, making it visually distinct from ASL:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          BSL FINGERSPELLING — PRINCIPLES                      │
│                                                              │
│  Both hands cooperate to form each letter                    │
│  Active hand = dominant hand (right for right-handed)        │
│  Passive hand = non-dominant (left), often a reference       │
│                                                              │
│  Letter structure:                                           │
│  — Some letters: one hand touches specific finger of other  │
│  — Some letters: both hands form a shape together           │
│  — Some letters: position on passive hand matters           │
│                                                              │
│  Examples:                                                   │
│  A: Active index touches tip of passive index               │
│  B: Active hand touches base of passive index               │
│  C: Two curved hands facing each other (C shape)            │
│  D: Active index traces on palm of passive hand             │
│  E: Active index on tip of passive little finger            │
│                                                              │
│  ASL vs. BSL are MUTUALLY UNINTELLIGIBLE:                    │
│  — Different handshapes for most letters                     │
│  — Despite US/UK sharing English, the sign languages differ  │
│  — Historical independence: ASL descended from LSF (French), │
│    not from BSL                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### ASL Historical Origin

```
LSF (Langue des Signes Française, French Sign Language)
  ↓
Laurent Clerc brought LSF to America (1817)
  ↓
Combined with local Martha's Vineyard Sign Language
  ↓
ASL (American Sign Language) emerged

BSL (British Sign Language)
  ↓
Evolved independently in Britain
  ↓
BANZSL family: BSL + Auslan (Australia) + NZSL (New Zealand)
  are mutually intelligible (~70%)
```

This explains why ASL and BSL are completely different systems despite both being used by English-speaking communities. ASL and LSF (French) are more similar to each other than ASL and BSL.

## ASL Grammar — Key Principles

ASL is not English with hand gestures. It is a fully developed natural language with its own grammar. For an MIT-background reader: ASL is to English roughly as Mandarin is to English — shares a few borrowed words (fingerspelled), but completely independent syntax and structure.

### Topic-Comment Structure

ASL frequently uses **topic-comment** word order, different from English SVO:

```
English:  "I saw the movie yesterday"
ASL:      MOVIE ME SEE YESTERDAY  (topic first, then comment)
          or: YESTERDAY, MOVIE, ME SEE  (time → topic → comment)

The topic is established in space, then commented on.
This is analogous to Japanese/Korean topic markers (wa は).
```

### Spatial Grammar

The most distinctive ASL feature: **the signing space encodes grammar**.

```
3D space around the signer acts as grammar:

Left side ────────────────── Right side
  Subject/Referent A         Subject/Referent B

  JOHN [establish in space: John = left]
  MARY [establish in space: Mary = right]
  LEFT-GIVES-TO-RIGHT = "John gives to Mary"
  RIGHT-GIVES-TO-LEFT = "Mary gives to John"

The direction of the verb GIVE encodes both subject and object.
This is verb agreement via spatial reference — no separate
subject/object markers needed.
```

This is morphologically rich — far richer than English. A single verb movement can incorporate:
- Subject reference (where the verb starts)
- Object reference (where the verb ends)
- Aspect (how the movement is performed)
- Number (movement repeated = multiple times / multiple people)

### Non-Manual Markers (NMMs)

Facial expressions, mouth movements, eyebrow position, and head tilt are **grammatically required** in ASL — they are not emotional coloring:

```
NMMs and their grammatical roles:
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Eyebrows raised + slight lean forward: Yes/No question  │
│  "YOU DRIVE?" with raised brows = "Do you drive?"        │
│                                                          │
│  Eyebrows furrowed/down: Wh- question                   │
│  "YOU GO WHERE?" with furrowed brows = "Where did go?"  │
│                                                          │
│  Head nod while signing: topic marker                   │
│  HEAD SHAKE while signing: negation ("I don't...")      │
│                                                          │
│  Puffed cheeks: "large" modifier                        │
│  Sucked-in cheeks: "small/thin" modifier                │
│  Mouth pattern "CHA": intensifier ("really big/extreme")│
│                                                          │
│  Mouth patterns (mouthing): Some signs have associated  │
│  mouth movements borrowed from English (not all do).    │
│  Mouthing is not the same as lip-reading speech.        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Classifiers

ASL uses **handshapes as classifiers** — predicates representing a category of referent:

```
Flat hand = flat object (plate, paper, floor)
  Move flat hand in an arc = "the plate slid across the table"

Bent V = person or animal walking/running
  Move bent-V = "the person walked toward me"

1 (index up) = person standing
  Two index fingers approaching = "two people meeting"

Fist = round object (ball, tree trunk, pot)
  Fist moving forward = "the ball rolled"

Classifiers are like pronouns but for physical interaction.
They encode: shape of object, motion, location, orientation.
```

This is highly iconic — once understood, the connection between handshape and referent category is visually transparent. Compare to spoken language pronominalization which is arbitrary.

## Iconicity vs. Arbitrariness

A key question in sign linguistics:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  ICONICITY IN SIGN LANGUAGES                                  │
│                                                              │
│  Iconic signs: form resembles meaning                        │
│  TREE = one arm up (trunk + canopy shape)                    │
│  EAT = hand to mouth                                         │
│  SLEEP = head tilts to rested hand (sleeping posture)        │
│  TELEPHONE = Y-hand to ear                                   │
│  BOOK = two hands open like a book                           │
│                                                              │
│  Arbitrary signs: no visual resemblance to meaning           │
│  MOTHER = 5-hand, tapped on chin (arbitrary)                 │
│  FATHER = 5-hand, tapped on forehead (different convention)  │
│  THINK = 1 to forehead (semi-iconic: "thinking in head")    │
│                                                              │
│  ~30–50% of ASL signs have some iconic motivation.          │
│  Iconicity fades with historical compression (like English   │
│  etymology — "window" = "wind eye" in Old Norse, not         │
│  transparent today).                                         │
│                                                              │
│  Key point from linguistics: iconicity does not prevent a    │
│  system from being a language. ASL is a full natural language │
│  whether or not its signs resemble their meanings.           │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Signed Exact English (SEE) vs. ASL

An important distinction often confused:

```
ASL (American Sign Language):
  — Natural language, not based on English grammar
  — 500,000+ native signers in US
  — Own grammar, spatial syntax, classifiers
  — Different word order from English

SEE (Signed Exact English):
  — Invented system (1972), not a natural language
  — Follows English word order exactly
  — Adds invented signs for English grammatical morphemes
    (-ing, -tion, the, a, be, etc.)
  — Easier for hearing parents to learn (maps to English)
  — Controversial: Deaf educators debate its educational value
  — Considered by Deaf community as "not real signing"

PSE (Pidgin Signed English):
  — Hybrid: ASL vocabulary + English-influenced order
  — Used between hearing learners and Deaf signers
  — Natural contact language
```

## The Deafblind Manual Alphabet

For individuals who are both deaf and blind, communication is through **tactile fingerspelling**:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  DEAFBLIND MANUAL (Tactile Fingerspelling)                    │
│                                                              │
│  Signer forms ASL (or BSL) handshapes                        │
│  Receiver places their hand over the signer's hand           │
│  Receiver feels the handshape being formed                   │
│                                                              │
│  Speed: slower than visual reading (50–80 words/min)        │
│                                                              │
│  Alternative: Malossi code (Italy), Lorm alphabet (Germany) │
│  — Both map letters to touches on the palm of the hand       │
│  — Different from fingerspelling handshapes                  │
│                                                              │
│  Protactile ASL (ProTactile): emerging full communication    │
│  system developed by deafblind community, with touch-based  │
│  backchannel ("I understand" = tap on leg/arm)              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Common Confusion Points

**ASL fingerspelling ≠ ASL**. Fingerspelling is one small piece of ASL. A person who knows fingerspelling can spell English words. A person who knows ASL can hold a fluent conversation in a complete independent language. They're not interchangeable skills.

**ASL and BSL are unrelated**. Despite both countries speaking English, ASL and BSL are mutually unintelligible. A Deaf American signing to a Deaf Brit achieves no more communication than an English speaker trying to speak to a French speaker. They must both use ISL (International Sign), which is a contact pidgin.

**ASL is not "English with hands"**. ASL grammar differs fundamentally: spatial encoding, topic-comment word order, classifier predicates, non-manual markers as grammatical morphemes. An English sentence cannot be "translated" word-for-word into ASL and make grammatical sense — the grammar rules are different.

**Hearing people often write/type out fingerspelled words** (a-t-t-o-r-n-e-y) with hyphens between letters. In the Deaf community, fingerspelled lexicalized words (words borrowed into ASL via fingerspelling) are sometimes written in lower-case with a # prefix: #job, #back, #what. These are fingerspelled loan words that have developed distinctive movement patterns.

**"Signed Exact English" is controversial within Deaf education**. Research suggests children taught SEE may have lower ASL fluency than those exposed to natural ASL from birth. The Deaf community strongly advocates for natural language (ASL) exposure in early childhood, paralleling debates about oral vs. signing methods in deaf education history.
