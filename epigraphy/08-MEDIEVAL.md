# Medieval Inscriptions: Church Latin, Vernacular, Tomb Stones, Coins

## The Big Picture

```
MEDIEVAL EPIGRAPHIC LANDSCAPE (c. 500-1500 CE)
================================================

+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  LANGUAGE          CONTEXT              FUNCTION                 |
|  --------          -------              --------                 |
|  Latin             Church architecture   Dedication, relic        |
|  (dominant)        Tomb monuments        Memorial, identity       |
|                    Coins                 Authority, legitimacy    |
|                    Charters (sometimes)  Legal, boundary          |
|                    Reliquaries           Sacred identification    |
|                                                                   |
|  Vernacular        Tomb monuments        Local identity           |
|  (emerging         Graffiti              Personal presence        |
|   post-1000)       Heraldic              Clan/family identity     |
|                    Coins (late)          Regional authority       |
|                                                                   |
|  Mixed             Church programs       Hierarchy, teaching      |
|  Latin +           Memorial stones       Status + piety           |
|  vernacular                                                       |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

Medieval epigraphy is shaped by one dominant fact: **the Church controlled Latin literacy**. Inscriptions in Latin presuppose institutional contexts (church, court, monastery). Vernacular inscriptions emerge as markers of local identity and non-institutional authority, growing from the 10th century onward.

## Latin Inscriptions on Church Architecture

### Dedication Inscriptions

```
CHURCH DEDICATION INSCRIPTION TYPES
=====================================

FOUNDATION / DEDICATION:
  "This church was dedicated in honor of [saint]
   by [bishop's name] on [date]"
  Often on the main door lintel or apse arch
  Records:
    - Patron saint(s)
    - Bishop who consecrated
    - Date (sometimes exact: day, month, year, pope's year)
    - Donor name (if patron funded the building)

Formula (Romanesque):
  "ANNO DOMINI [YEAR] [MONTH] [DAY]
   DEDICATA EST HAEC AECCLESIA IN HONORE(M) [SAINT]
   A DOMINO [BISHOP] EPISCOPO [DIOCESE]"

Post-1150 Gothic buildings: more elaborate programs
  Arches inscribed with biblical verses
  Tympanum (semicircular zone above door) with inscription
  Identifying the scene depicted below

CONSECRATION vs. DEDICATION:
  Dedication: formal ceremony establishing sacred status
  Consecration: the rite of making holy (can be re-done
    if the building is "polluted" by violent death, etc.)
  Both produce inscriptions; dedicated = the event;
  consecrated = the status
```

### Relic Inscriptions

```
RELIC AND RELIQUARY INSCRIPTIONS
==================================

Relics were the physical remains or associated objects
of saints, kept in reliquaries (containers).

Inscription function:
  Identify what the relic is
  Authenticate the relic (who gave it, when, from where)
  Assert continuity of claim

Example type:
  "HIC EST DE LIGNO CRUCIS DOMINI NOSTRI IESU CHRISTI"
  "This is from the Wood of the Cross of Our Lord Jesus Christ"
  (On thousands of reliquaries claiming pieces of the True Cross)

Relic authentication chains:
  "These relics of Saint [X] were given by [bishop/abbot]
   to this church in the year of [pope/king] N"
  The inscription is the chain of custody document
  -- the medieval equivalent of provenance documentation

Medieval relic economy:
  Relics attracted pilgrims = income for the church
  Inscriptions were part of the authentication apparatus
  Forgery was common; inscriptions were forged accordingly
  Specialists today use inscription style/language/material
  to date and authenticate relic inscriptions
```

## Tomb Inscription Conventions

### Hic Iacet: The Standard Formula

```
LATIN FUNERARY FORMULA EVOLUTION
==================================

EARLY MEDIEVAL (500-1000):
  Simple slab with name only
  Cross + name (Chi-rho or plain cross)
  Pray for the soul formula appearing by 7th c.:
  "ORATE PRO ANIMA [NAME]" = "Pray for the soul of [name]"

HIGH MEDIEVAL (1000-1300):
  "HIC IACET [NAME] [TITLE/DIGNITY]
   QUI OBIIT [DATE]
   CUIUS ANIMA REQUIESCAT IN PACE" (or R.I.P.)
  = "Here lies [Name] [Title] who died [Date]
     Whose soul may rest in peace"

  OBIIT = died (3rd person singular perfect, obeo)
  HIC IACET = "here lies" (but coffin burials: stone above,
                           body below -- "here" is spatial)

LATE MEDIEVAL (1300-1500):
  Memorial brasses (in England, Low Countries):
    Engraved brass plate inlaid in stone floor
    Effigy (kneeling figure) + inscription around border
    More biographical: age, offices held, family connections
  Extended verse epitaphs:
    Warnings to the living ("I was as you are; you will be as I am")
    Requests for prayers ("Say a pater and ave for my soul")
    Spiritual claims ("Pray God his sins be forgiven")
```

### Memento Mori Inscriptions

```
MEMENTO MORI FORMULA
=====================

"Quod fuimus, estis; quod sumus, vos eritis"
= "What we were, you are; what we are, you will be"
[Tomb addresses the living directly]

Variants:
  "Sum quod eris; fueram quod es" (I am what you will be;
                                    I was what you are)
  These "speaking tomb" formulas appear across Europe
  from the 12th century; peak in 14th-15th c. (post-Plague)

Transi tombs (visible decay sculptures):
  14th-15th c. tomb monuments showing the deceased
  as a decomposing corpse (shroud, worms)
  Above: effigy of the living person in life
  Below: the decomposed body
  Inscription often identifies both states
  -> Visual + textual memento mori combined
```

## Vernacular Emergence in Stone

### Old English Inscriptions

```
OLD ENGLISH EPIGRAPHIC RECORD (7th-11th c.)
=============================================

The written record of Old English is dominated by manuscripts.
Epigraphic OE is rare. Key examples:

RUTHWELL CROSS (~7th-8th c., Dumfriesshire):
  "The Dream of the Rood" lines in Anglo-Saxon runic futhorc
  Mixed with Latin inscriptions in Roman script
  Latin program on stone; OE poem on margin in runes
  Status: one of the oldest literary OE texts

BEWCASTLE CROSS (~7th c., Cumbria):
  Runic futhorc inscriptions with royal name
  May commemorate King Alchfrith of Northumbria
  Latin + runic mixed program

ALFRED JEWEL (~880 CE):
  Gold, enamel, and rock crystal object
  Possibly a pointer for manuscript reading
  Inscription: "AELFRED MEC HEHT GEWYRCAN"
    = "Alfred ordered me made" (1st person object speaking)
  OE inscription, not Latin -- unusually direct royal claim

SEALSTONES AND METALWORK:
  More OE inscriptions on portable metalwork
  Than on stone (fragile Anglo-Saxon stone tradition
  relative to Continental stone tradition)
```

### Old French Inscriptions

```
OLD FRENCH EPIGRAPHIC RECORD (11th-13th c.)
=============================================

The emergence of vernacular in stone coincides with:
  Norman Conquest (1066): Anglo-Norman French as prestige language
  Troubadour culture: vernacular poetry gaining status
  Urban growth: non-Latinate merchant class developing

EXAMPLES:
  Norman England: some tomb inscriptions in Anglo-Norman
    from 12th c. onward
  France: regional variation:
    Langue d'oc (South): earlier vernacular use
    Langue d'oil (North): slightly later
  Generally: vernacular first in less formal contexts
    (trade inscriptions, graffiti, merchant marks)
    Latin retained for church and official contexts

TOURNAMENT HERALDRY:
  Heraldic devices on shields = visual inscriptions
  Identifying ownership and lineage
  Motto inscriptions in Latin AND vernacular
    (Latin: prestigious; vernacular: accessible to spectators)
```

## Medieval Coin Legends

Coins are a sub-discipline (numismatics) but important for epigraphy:

```
MEDIEVAL COIN LEGENDS
======================

FUNCTION:
  Identify the issuing authority (king, bishop, city)
  Assert legitimacy
  Date indirectly (whose reign = when minted)
  Specify the mint location

STANDARD LATIN FORMULAS:
  OBVERSE (front, portrait side):
    [NAME] REX ANGLORUM = "[Name] King of the English"
    [NAME] DEI GRATIA REX = "[Name] by God's Grace King"
    (DEI GRATIA: adopted universally after ~1100 CE)

  REVERSE (back, cross/symbol side):
    CIVI TAS (in two halves around cross): "city"
    EBORACI = "of York"
    Full: "CIVITAS EBORACI" = "[minted in the] city of York"

ABBREVIATION SYSTEM:
  Space on coins is minimal -> heavy abbreviation
  + = ET (and), abbreviation marks above letters
  The reading of medieval coin legends requires
  knowledge of the standard formula set

SIGNIFICANCE:
  Coin legends document which rulers claimed which titles
  and when
  Changes in titles (Rex Angliae -> Rex Anglorum -> Rex Angliae)
  have constitutional implications
  Coins are dateable by stylistic/metallurgical analysis
  -> anchor chronology for other finds
```

## Mason's Marks

```
MASON'S MARKS (MARQUES DE TACHERONS)
======================================

What they are:
  Personal signs cut by individual masons into stone blocks
  Appear on: cathedral walls, column shafts, floor stones
  Typically: geometric signs (cross, triangle, arrow, forked)
  Size: 5-15 cm typically
  Position: often hidden (inside joint, back of block)

Functions:
  PAYMENT: Mason marks their work so the master mason
    can verify quantity for payment purposes
    The mark is a tally system -- "X blocks by this mason"

  QUALITY CONTROL: Some marks indicate approved work
    (different from the worker's mark)

  RESPONSIBILITY: If a block fails, the mark identifies
    who cut it

GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION:
  Documented throughout Western Europe from ~1100 CE
  Romanesque period: mostly payment tallies
  Gothic period: marks become more elaborate and personal
  Some marks hereditary (father to son with modification)

COMPARISON WITH MODERN ANALOGY:
  Mason's marks are pre-modern quality management tagging
  -> Like a commit hash or a worker ID in a factory system
  Not linguistic inscription; but functional marking system
  Epigraphy's boundary with material culture studies

IDENTIFICATION:
  Mark dictionaries exist for major cathedrals
  Cross-site comparison can track mason migrations
  (Durham masons appear at Lincoln -> documented movement)
```

## Graffiti in Cathedrals: Medieval Prayer and Pilgrimage

```
MEDIEVAL CATHEDRAL GRAFFITI
=============================

Discovery context:
  In ultraviolet light, low-raking light (RTI),
  or simply careful examination:
  cathedral walls covered in medieval graffiti
  Previously overlooked as damage; now recognized as evidence

Norfolk Medieval Graffiti Survey (Matt Champion):
  Systematic survey of English parish churches
  100+ churches, ~3,000 medieval graffiti documented
  Results: graffiti is UNIVERSAL (every medieval church surveyed)

TYPES:
  DEVOTIONAL:
    Ships scratched near pilgrimage routes
      (Votive ships: prayer for safe voyage)
    Hands and feet: pilgrims tracing their hands
      in devotion to a saint's hand relic
    Faces: portraits of personal devotion
    Musical notation: identifying choir practice spots

  PERSONAL PRESENCE:
    Names and dates (many 14th-16th c.)
    Architectural sketches (masons planning?)
    Geometric figures (practice? play?)

  ARCHITECTURAL:
    Hexfoil: six-petal rose design
      Purpose debated: demon-trapping? decoration?
      Appears near doors and windows (threshold protection)

  PROTECTIVE / MAGICAL:
    Daisy wheels (apotropaic circles)
    VV marks (Virgo Virginum -- Virgin of Virgins)
    PM marks (Pace Maria?)
    Pentacles and stars

SIGNIFICANCE:
  Graffiti is the non-elite, non-institutional voice
  Counter to the official Latin program of the church
  Pilgrims scratched their ships; ordinary people
  marked their presence; folk protective signs
  exist alongside official sacred inscriptions
  -> Two overlapping epigraphic programs in the same space
```

| Formula | Expanded | Context |
|---------|---------|---------|
| HIC IACET | Here lies | Tomb inscription opening |
| OBIIT | Died [on date] | Memorial date marker |
| CUIUS ANIMA REQUIESCAT IN PACE | Whose soul may rest in peace | Funerary closing (became "R.I.P.") |
| ORATE PRO ANIMA | Pray for the soul of | Request for intercession |
| DEI GRATIA REX | King by God's Grace | Coin / royal title formula |
| DEDICATA EST | Was dedicated | Church dedication inscription |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Vernacular" inscriptions are rare before 1000 CE and become common only after 1200.** Latin was not just a prestige choice — it was the language of literate Christian culture. Vernacular written forms had to develop before vernacular inscriptions could proliferate.

**Mason's marks are not signatures.** They are a management accounting tool. The mason who cut a block marked it so the paymaster could count their output. The marks have no linguistic content.

**Medieval graffiti is not vandalism.** It is devotional and documentary practice. Scratching a ship in a church wall near a pilgrimage route was a form of votive offering — "I was here; pray for me." The modern dismissal of graffiti as damage has caused centuries of deliberate erasure of medieval evidence.

**R.I.P. is a Latin formula, not English.** "Rest in peace" is a back-translation of "Requiescat in Pace" (or the earlier "Cuius anima requiescat in pace"). The formula is medieval Latin; its English translation became familiar through the abbreviation.

**Medieval coin legends require specialist knowledge to read.** Abbreviations, missing vowels, damaged edges, and variant spellings make medieval numismatic epigraphy a specialist sub-field. Reading "CNVT REX AN" correctly as "Cnut King of the English" requires knowing the formula conventions.
