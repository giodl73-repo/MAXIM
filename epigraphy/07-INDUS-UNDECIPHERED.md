## Indus Valley Script: The Major Unsolved Problem

### Basic Facts

```
INDUS VALLEY SCRIPT FACTS
===========================

Period: 2600-1900 BCE (Mature Harappan phase)
Discovery: Harappa (1872), Mohenjo-daro (1920s)
Sites: 100+ sites with script; main concentration: Indus River valley
       (modern Pakistan and northwest India)
Material: primarily soapstone seals (~60% of corpus)
          some pottery, copper tablets, bronze tools

Corpus size: ~2,000 texts
Text length: SHORT -- average 5 signs; max ~26 signs
  This is a fundamental problem:
  Short texts have insufficient data for statistical analysis
  Cannot build a word frequency distribution
  Cannot identify grammatical elements

SIGN COUNT DEBATE:
  Reported range: 400-600 distinct signs
  Low count scholars: 400 (many "variants" = allographs = same sign)
  High count scholars: 600+ (each variant = different sign)
  The ambiguity matters:
    400 signs -> logo-syllabic (consistent with cuneiform type)
    600 signs -> possibly logographic + too many for alphabet
    This unresolved question affects every other hypothesis

DIRECTION: right-to-left (most texts)
  Some texts: boustrophedon or left-to-right
  Confirmed by: crowding at left end (writer ran out of space)
```

### Why It Remains Undeciphered

```
BARRIERS TO INDUS DECIPHERMENT
================================

1. NO BILINGUAL TEXT:
   Indus Valley had extensive trade contact with Mesopotamia
   Indus seals have been found at Mesopotamian sites
   BUT: no bilingual Indus-Akkadian inscription has been found
   -> Without a bilingual anchor, phonetic values cannot be
      extracted directly

2. UNKNOWN LANGUAGE:
   The Indus Civilization ended ~1900 BCE
   The language is not identified with confidence
   Candidates:
     - Proto-Dravidian (Iravatham Mahadevan's hypothesis)
       Dravidian languages (Tamil, Telugu, etc.) now spoken in
       South India; possibly descended from Indus language
     - Proto-Indo-Aryan (minority view)
     - Language isolate (no surviving descendants)
   Without knowing the language, phonetic values cannot
   be verified even if proposed

3. SHORT TEXTS:
   Average 5 signs per inscription = 1-2 words at most
   Statistical analysis requires word frequency distributions
   With texts of 5 signs, there is no meaningful frequency data
   Context (only seals = stamp impressions) is limited

4. CORPUS INTERPRETATION:
   Are the texts: names? titles? commodity labels?
   The seals were probably for trade certification
   ("this commodity belongs to / was certified by X")
   If so, the script may primarily encode proper names
   -> Even less linguistic information per text

5. DISPUTED EVIDENCE:
   Every proposed decipherment has been contested:
   - Subrahmaniam's proto-Dravidian readings (1970s): not accepted
   - Asko Parpola's detailed Dravidian analysis: not proven
   - Farmer, Sproat, Witzel (2004): propose Indus is NOT writing
     but a non-linguistic sign system (heraldic, religious)
     -> Disputed; most scholars still treat it as writing
```

### Statistical Approaches

```
INDUS STATISTICAL ANALYSIS
============================

Sign frequency distribution:
  A few signs appear very frequently (10-30% of texts)
  Many signs appear only once or twice
  This pattern is consistent with:
    - A writing system (function words + rare vocabulary)
    - A non-linguistic symbol system (common emblems + rare ones)
  The distribution alone does not distinguish between these

Rao et al. (2009, Science):
  Conditional entropy analysis
  Compared Indus sign sequences to:
    - Known writing systems (low conditional entropy)
    - Non-linguistic sequences (high conditional entropy)
    - Programmatic sequences like DNA (different pattern)
  Result: Indus conditional entropy resembles writing systems
  Critique (Sproat, Farmer): the analysis is flawed; does not
  distinguish logo-syllabic from non-linguistic sign systems

Current state: the question of whether it is writing is
  genuinely debated; most epigraphers treat it as writing
  but the debate is legitimate
```

## Linear A: Minoan Undeciphered

```
LINEAR A
=========

Period: ~1800-1450 BCE
Location: Crete and Aegean islands (Minoan civilization)
Discovered: Arthur Evans (same excavations as Linear B)
Corpus: ~1,500 texts; mostly short (administrative tablets)

RELATIONSHIP TO LINEAR B:
  Linear B (Mycenaean Greek, deciphered 1952) was adapted
  from Linear A by the Mycenaean Greeks who conquered Crete
  Many Linear B syllabic sign shapes derive from Linear A signs
  -> Linear B sign values can be READ into Linear A signs
  -> But the language is different!

PARTIAL READING:
  ~40 Linear A signs have known phonetic values
  (borrowed into Linear B with same shapes and values)
  These can be used to "read" Linear A phonetically

PROBLEM:
  Reading the sounds gives: words that are not Greek
  The language is not identifiable
  "Minoan" language = unknown; possibly isolate
  No known related language

WHAT CAN BE READ:
  Numbers and quantities (unambiguous)
  Some commodity words (shared with Linear B in context)
  Some personal names (partially)
  The administrative structure is clear (same as Linear B)
  The language content: not understood

STATUS: will remain undeciphered until a bilingual
        Linear A / known language text is found
        OR until Minoan language is identified
        (low probability for either)
```

## Proto-Elamite: Oldest Undeciphered Script

```
PROTO-ELAMITE
==============

Period: ~3300-2900 BCE (pre-dates Sumerian!)
Location: Khuzestan (SW Iran) and spreading east to Central Asia
Corpus: ~1,000 tablets (clay, like cuneiform)
Signs: ~1,000+ distinct signs (the most of any undeciphered system)

CHRONOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  Proto-Elamite appears as early as proto-cuneiform Uruk IV
  May be slightly EARLIER than Sumerian writing
  -> Writing may have been independently invented in Elam
     as early as or earlier than in Sumer
  -> OR Elamite trade contact with Uruk inspired the idea

RELATIONSHIP TO CUNEIFORM:
  Not a derivative of cuneiform
  Used similar clay tablet medium
  But: different signs, different administrative system
  Elamite traders were in contact with Sumer but developed
  their own writing independently (the evidence suggests)

WHY UNDECIPHERED:
  No bilingual text
  The "Elamite" language in later cuneiform is known
  (Behistun Inscription has Elamite column)
  BUT: proto-Elamite may not be the same language
       as later Elamite (1,000 years separate them)
  Sign count too high for known language pattern

2016: Oxford 5,000-Year Secrets project
  High-resolution photographs of all tablets
  MCMC (Markov chain Monte Carlo) sign frequency analysis
  Result: pattern consistent with numerical + commodity records
  Numbers and commodity counts can be read
  Linguistic content: still opaque
```

## Rongorongo: Easter Island

```
RONGORONGO
===========

Location: Easter Island (Rapa Nui)
Material: wooden tablets, carved with obsidian flake tools
Corpus: 25 surviving texts (all fragments or complete tablets)
Discovery: 1864 when Bishop Jaussen received a rope wrapped
           in a Rongorongo tablet as a gift
           Most tablets destroyed or lost by 1900 due to
           missionary activity, disease, and deportation

SIGN COUNT: ~120 base signs; ~1,500 compound signs
READING DIRECTION: boustrophedon-rotational
  Read left to right; at line end, rotate tablet 180 degrees
  Next line reads left to right (which was right-to-left before rotation)

WHY POSSIBLY INVENTED AFTER EUROPEAN CONTACT:
  No pre-contact tablet has been identified
  Hypothesis: Rapanui people saw Spanish colonial documents
  in 1770 and invented their own script
  Counter-hypothesis: independent invention, pre-contact
  (tablets used as firewood after smallpox epidemic reduced
  population; lack of pre-1864 survivors not evidence)

DECIPHERMENT ATTEMPTS:
  Metraux (1940s): some astronomical content identified
  Barthel (1958): proposed partial readings; not verified
  Fischer (1997): claimed astronomical/calendrical reading
    -> "Copulated" formula sequence -- widely rejected
  Current status: no consensus reading; possibly ritual/
    astronomical content; possibly astronomical almanac

CONNECTION TO RAPANUI LANGUAGE:
  Modern Rapanui (Polynesian language, related to Hawaiian)
  is still spoken (~3,000 speakers)
  But: island's population was decimated (from ~3,000 to ~110
  by 1877); cultural transmission was severely disrupted
  No living tradition of reading Rongorongo survived
```

## Voynich Manuscript: Medieval Puzzle

```
VOYNICH MANUSCRIPT
==================

Location: Beinecke Library, Yale University (MS 408)
Date: carbon-dated to 1404-1438 CE
Physical: vellum, ~240 pages (240 of ~272; some missing)
Language: "Voynichese" -- ~25-30 character alphabet

CHARACTERISTICS:
  Text appears to be a real language (not random):
    - Word length distribution consistent with natural language
    - Letter frequency distribution natural-looking
  Text appears NOT to be a known natural language:
    - No word matches any known European language
    - No Latin, Italian, Greek cognates identifiable

SECTIONS (by illustration type):
  Botanical: plants (mostly unidentifiable)
  Astronomical/astrological: zodiac, star diagrams
  Biological: bathing women in interconnected pools
  Cosmological: circular diagrams
  Pharmaceutical: labeled containers and plant roots
  Recipes/text: dense text with no illustrations

HYPOTHESES:
  1. Constructed language (earliest known, pre-Esperanto)
  2. Cipher of a known natural language
     -> Caesar cipher: too simple; ruled out
     -> Polyalphabetic: possible but no key found
  3. Polyglot cipher (multiple languages mixed)
  4. Meaningless (elaborate hoax): problematic because
     meaningful statistical patterns ARE present
  5. Unknown natural language (unknown script)

STATUS: no consensus; none of the many proposed
  decipherments has produced confirmed meaning
```

## Why Some Scripts Will Remain Undeciphered

```
NECESSARY CONDITIONS FOR DECIPHERMENT
=======================================

At least ONE of the following must be available:
  A) Bilingual or trilingual text with known language
  B) Identified language (even without bilingual)
     + Large corpus
  C) Known related language + pattern analysis

INDUS: Fails all three
  No bilingual. Language unknown. No related language confirmed.
  -> Will likely remain undeciphered without new discovery

LINEAR A: Partially has (B)
  Linear B sign values can be read in
  But the language is not Greek or any known language
  -> Will likely remain undeciphered without new discovery

PROTO-ELAMITE: Has limited (B)
  Later Elamite known but may not be the same language
  Numbers readable; linguistic content not
  -> Partial progress possible; full decipherment unlikely

RONGORONGO: Cultural break is the barrier
  The tradition of reading was lost (not the script design)
  If surviving speakers had maintained the reading tradition,
  it might be readable; the knowledge was simply lost

MORAL: Decipherment is not primarily about intelligence.
  It is about available information. Without a bilingual text
  or identified language, the best scholars in the world
  cannot crack an unknown script from statistical analysis alone.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Can't AI crack the Indus script?"** Not without a bilingual or identified language. AI can identify patterns, confirm it is writing-like, build probabilistic models — but it cannot produce a phonetic mapping without external constraints. Statistical analysis is necessary but not sufficient.

**Linear A is "almost" deciphered.** No, it isn't. The sounds of some signs are known (from Linear B borrowings). The language is completely unknown. Knowing the phonetic transcription of a language you don't know is not useful — you can read the sounds but not understand the meaning.

**The Voynich Manuscript may be a hoax.** This hypothesis cannot be dismissed. The statistical patterns might be produced by a clever forger. But they would have had to construct something that looks natural across every linguistic test applied over 600 years. The "elaborate Medieval hoax" hypothesis is not well supported by what is actually hard to fake.

**Farmer, Sproat, and Witzel's "Indus is not writing" claim is controversial.** Most Indus scholars disagree. But it is a serious scholarly argument, not a crank hypothesis. The issue is genuinely unresolved.

**Short texts are not solvable.** The Indus Valley corpus averages 5 signs. This is not enough data for any statistical method to produce confident results. The texts would need to be much longer, or many more texts found, or a bilingual discovered. None of these has happened in 150 years of excavation.
