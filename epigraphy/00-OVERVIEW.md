**Why inscriptions survive when papyri don't**: Stone, fired clay, and metal are chemically stable for millennia under the right conditions. Organic substrates (papyrus, vellum, bark) require dry conditions to survive. Most ancient texts were written on perishable media and are lost. Inscriptions on durable media are the survivors.

This creates **survivorship bias**: the epigraphic record overrepresents official, monumental, and public texts (decrees, dedications, funerary monuments) relative to the actual distribution of ancient writing.

## What Epigraphy Studies

```
INSCRIPTION TYPES BY FUNCTION
================================

PUBLIC / OFFICIAL:
  Laws, decrees, treaties (civic legislation)
  Census and tax records
  Boundary stones (property, territory)
  Military records (victory, casualty)
  Administrative documents (Mesopotamian clay tablets)

RELIGIOUS / DEDICATORY:
  Votive inscriptions (ex votos, thank-offerings)
  Temple dedications
  Ritual texts (curse tablets, binding spells)
  Sacred laws (access, purity rules)

FUNERARY:
  Epitaphs (who, when, by whom mourned)
  Sarcophagus inscriptions
  Tomb dedications
  Memorial stelae

PRIVATE / COMMERCIAL:
  Ownership marks (on pottery, tools)
  Trade marks (amphora stamps: origin, capacity, producer)
  Commercial accounts (tallies on tablets)

INFORMAL / GRAFFITI:
  Pompeii wall graffiti (electoral, erotic, banal)
  Medieval cathedral pilgrim marks
  Ownership scratches (graffiti on pottery)
  This category: most historically undervalued,
  most authentic voice of non-elite populations
```

## Script Typology: The Classification Problem

Writing systems are classified by their minimal phonological unit:

```
SCRIPT TYPOLOGY
================

LOGOGRAPHIC (or logosyllabic):
  One sign = one word or morpheme
  Examples: Sumerian (early), Chinese
  Sign count: hundreds to thousands
  Advantage: language-neutral (any speaker learns same sign)
  Disadvantage: large sign inventory to memorize

SYLLABIC:
  One sign = one syllable (C+V, or V alone)
  Examples: Linear B, Akkadian cuneiform (mixed)
  Sign count: 50-300 signs
  Advantage: smaller inventory
  Disadvantage: complex consonant clusters encoded awkwardly

ABJAD (consonantal alphabet):
  One sign = one consonant (vowels omitted or marked secondarily)
  Examples: Phoenician, Hebrew, Arabic
  Sign count: ~22-28 signs
  Assumption: reader supplies vowels from context
  Works well for Semitic languages (root = consonant pattern)

ALPHABET (full phonemic):
  One sign = one phoneme (consonant or vowel)
  Examples: Greek (first full alphabet), Latin, Cyrillic
  Sign count: ~20-33 signs
  Advantage: full phonemic representation
  Greek innovation: repurposed Semitic consonants as vowels

ABUGIDA (syllabic with default vowel + diacritics):
  Base character = consonant + inherent vowel
  Diacritics modify the vowel
  Examples: Devanagari, Thai, Ethiopic
  Neither purely syllabic nor alphabetic
```

## Why Epigraphy Matters

```
HISTORICAL EVIDENCE VALUE
==========================

DATABLE:
  Inscription on a building can date the building
  Dated coins anchor chronology
  Letter forms evolve systematically -> date by paleography

AUTHENTIC:
  Inscriptions are primary documents
  No copyist intermediary (unlike manuscripts)
  Forgeries exist but are detectable by material analysis

POLITICALLY UNFILTERED (sometimes):
  Official inscriptions reflect power's view
  Graffiti and informal texts reflect other voices
  Pompeii graffiti: most extensive record of vernacular
  Latin speech outside literary texts

LEGALLY BINDING ORIGINALS:
  Roman bronze law tablets = the law itself
  Athenian inscribed decrees = the decree itself
  Not copies -- the binding original

SURVIVAL WHERE ARCHIVES DON'T:
  When a palace burns, tablets in the fire may survive
  (clay fired harder)
  When libraries are sacked, stone inscriptions outside remain
  Sennacherib's palace: destruction preserved the clay tablets
  (Nineveh excavations: 30,000 tablet fragments)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Epigraphy vs. paleography? | Epigraphy: durable media (stone/metal/clay). Paleography: perishable media (papyrus/vellum). Overlap is real. |
| Why can't we decipher everything? | Need: sign inventory, bilingual text or known language, or statistical foothold. Without these, progress is very slow. |
| What script type is easiest to decipher? | Alphabetic with bilingual text (Rosetta Stone model). Syllabic next. Logographic hardest. |
| Are there still undeciphered scripts? | Yes: Indus Valley, Linear A, Proto-Elamite, Rongorongo, others |
| Best free Latin corpus? | EDCS (490,000+ entries) or CIL online |
| Best free Greek corpus? | PHI Greek Inscriptions (packhum.org) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Epigraphy and archaeology are not the same.** Archaeology studies material culture; epigraphy is the specialist study of inscribed texts on durable materials. All inscriptions are archaeological finds, but most archaeological finds are not inscriptions.

**Survivorship bias is severe.** The epigraphic record overrepresents stone, metal, and official public inscriptions. Most ancient writing was on perishable media and is lost. The inscriptions we have are not representative of all ancient writing.

**Dating by letter forms (paleographic dating) is approximate.** Letter forms evolve, but they evolve differently in different regions and social contexts. A "date range by paleography" is typically a span of 50-150 years, not a precise date.

**Decipherment is not translation.** Decipherment establishes the reading system — what sounds the signs represent. Translation then requires knowing the language. Linear B was deciphered (1952) before the Linear B texts were fully translated (because Mycenaean Greek was already known). Maya glyphs took longer because both script and language had to be worked out.

## Script / Material / Function Matrix

Which script on which material for which purpose? The intersection determines what survives and what is lost.

| Script Type | Typical Material | Primary Functions | Survival Rate | Example Corpus |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Cuneiform (logosyllabic)** | Clay tablet, stone | Admin records, royal annals, literature, letters | High (clay endures) | CDLI: 330,000+ |
| **Hieroglyphic (logosyllabic)** | Stone, painted plaster | Monumental, funerary, religious | High (dry climate + stone) | Temples, tombs |
| **Hieratic / Demotic** | Papyrus, ostraca | Admin, literary, personal | Low (organic) | Oxyrhynchus papyri |
| **Greek alphabet** | Stone, bronze, pottery | Decrees, dedications, laws, graffiti | Moderate (stone survives, bronze melted) | PHI: 190,000+ |
| **Latin alphabet** | Stone, bronze, lead | Laws, epitaphs, dedications, curse tablets | Moderate | CIL: 290,000+ |
| **Runic (alphabet)** | Stone, bone, metal, wood | Memorial, ownership, magic, casual | Low-moderate (wood lost, stone survives) | ~6,500 known |
| **Maya (logosyllabic)** | Stone, stucco, codex bark | Royal, calendrical, ritual | Low (codices destroyed, stone survives) | ~10,000 texts |
| **Indus (undeciphered)** | Steatite seals, pottery | Probably admin/trade | Moderate (seals endure) | ~4,000 objects |

```
MATERIAL SURVIVAL RULE OF THUMB
=================================

  Stone ──────── survives unless deliberately destroyed
  Fired clay ─── survives (accidental firing helps)
  Bronze ─────── usually melted for reuse (rare survivors)
  Lead ────────── survives (curse tablets, trade tags)
  Bone / ivory ─ survives in dry / cold conditions
  Wood ────────── almost always lost
  Papyrus ────── survives only in extreme dry (Egypt)
  Bark / leaf ── almost always lost
```
