## Greek Inscriptions: Categories

### Honorary Inscriptions

```
HONORARY INSCRIPTION STRUCTURE
================================

Purpose: award honors to benefactors, officials, athletes
Location: public spaces (agora, gymnasium, sanctuary)
Stone: marble (Athens), limestone (provinces)

Formula (Athenian decree pattern):
  "The demos (people) decided. [Tribe] presided.
   [Secretary] wrote. [Archon] [date].
   [Proposer] proposed:
   Seeing as [person] has done [good deeds] for the demos,
   it was voted to praise [person] and crown him with
   a golden crown of [amount] and to inscribe this stele
   and set it up on the Acropolis."

Prytaneion (citizen club): different formula
  "The Boule and Demos honor [person]"
  with specific privileges (dining rights, front seats)
```

The Athenian agora has produced the densest concentration of honorific inscriptions — the physical record of Athenian democratic procedure. Each inscribed decree is a vote count made permanent.

### Dedicatory (Votive) Inscriptions

```
VOTIVE INSCRIPTION PATTERN
============================

"[Name, son of Name, from place]
 dedicated [this object] to [deity]"

Minimum form:
  "Nikodromos to Athena"
  (3 words: dedicant + deity)

Extended form:
  "Alkibiades son of Kleinias of [tribe] Acamantis
   dedicated this to Athena having won [contest]
   in the [year] of [archon]"

Objects dedicated:
  Armor (after victory)
  Terracotta figurines (affordable votive)
  Bronze statuettes
  Stone reliefs (healing dedications to Asclepius)
  The object + inscription together = the dedication
```

The healing sanctuary of Asclepius at Epidaurus has produced some of the most extraordinary votive inscriptions — stone stelae recording miraculous cures in vivid narrative detail. These are the ancient equivalent of testimonials: "Aristagora of Troezen... had a tapeworm... and was healed."

### Funerary Inscriptions

```
GREEK EPITAPH CONVENTIONS
===========================

Archaic (7th-5th c. BCE):
  Simple name + patronymic on kouros/kore statues
  "I am the marker of Kroisos who died in the front rank"
  (First-person from the stele speaking)

Classical (5th-4th c. BCE):
  Athenian state burial inscriptions: lists of dead by tribe
  Private epitaphs: name + tribe + father
  Emoving verse epitaphs: "Do not pass by. Grieve.
    Here lies a girl, not yet a bride..."

Hellenistic (4th-1st c. BCE):
  More elaborate, biographical
  Age at death common
  Occupations mentioned
  Immigrant status noted
  (Delos: multilingual community, many foreign dedications)

Late antique (1st-4th c. CE):
  Mixing of Greek and Roman conventions
  Christian additions: chi-rho, "in peace"
  Family group burials
```

### Official Inscriptions: Athenian Democracy in Stone

```
ATHENS AGORA AS DEMOCRACY ARCHIVE
====================================

The Athenian agora produced:
  Decrees of the demos (thousands)
  Tribute lists (Athenian empire, 454-415 BCE)
    -> 300+ stone fragments listing tribute-paying cities
    -> Primary source for Athenian imperial administration
  Casualty lists (war dead by tribe)
  Ostracism shards (ostraka -- broken pottery with names)
    -> ~10,000 ostraka survive
    -> Athenian "vote to exile" mechanism (one per year)
    -> Kleon, Themistokles, Aristides ostraka found
    -> Some ostraka were pre-written by political operatives
       (batches of identical handwriting found at Kerameikos)

Athens decree format evolution:
  5th c.: heading, proposer's name, full formula
  4th c.: more abbreviated
  3rd c.: shorter still as bureaucracy developed

The Metroon (state archive):
  Documents inscribed in stone were put on display
  The inscription = the law (not a copy of a paper law)
  Stone is the primary; there may have been temporary
  wooden copies but stone is the legally binding form
```

## Latin Epigraphy: CIL and Scope

### Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum (CIL)

```
CIL HISTORY AND SCOPE
======================

Founded: 1853 (Theodor Mommsen, Berlin-Brandenburg Academy)
Volumes: 17 volumes, ongoing
Coverage: 290,000+ texts; target ~500,000
Organization: geographic (Vol. 1: earliest, Vol. 2: Spain, etc.)
Physical: enormous format (A2 paper, massive folio volumes)
Online: partially (EDH is more digitized; EDCS most complete)

Scale significance:
  Latin inscriptions = most densely documented ancient language
  More Latin epigraphic texts than from all other ancient
  languages combined
  From Britain to Mesopotamia; 3rd c. BCE to 6th c. CE
  Represents 700 years of Roman administrative presence
```

### Roman Inscription Types

```
ROMAN FUNERARY FORMULA
========================

D. M. (Dis Manibus) — "To the divine shades"
  Standard opening on Latin epitaphs from 1st c. CE
  Addresses the dead as divine/ancestral spirits

Full formula:
  D.M.S. (Dis Manibus Sacrum) = "Sacred to the divine shades"
  + Name of deceased (nominative or dative)
  + Age: vixit annis XX mensibus III diebus V
          "lived 20 years 3 months 5 days"
  + Dedicant: [name] [relationship] fecit/posuit
              "made/set this up"

HSE: Hic Situs Est — "Here lies"
SIT TIBI TERRA LEVIS (STTL) — "May the earth lie lightly on you"
VIXIT: "lived" (standard abbreviation V)
```

### Pompeii Graffiti: Vernacular Latin

Pompeii preserves ~11,000 inscriptions, including the largest body of non-elite Latin writing from antiquity:

```
POMPEII GRAFFITI CATEGORIES
=============================

ELECTORAL PROGRAMMATA (election notices):
  "I urge you to elect [name] as [office]"
  Posted on building facades, often by organizations
  "The fruit dealers ask you to elect Holconius..."
  Remarkable: shows organized political campaigning

GLADIATORIAL NOTICES:
  Upcoming arena events: date, sponsor, fighters
  "20 pairs of gladiators sponsored by A. Clodius Flaccus
   will fight at Pompeii on May 31-June 4..."
  Most sophisticated: wooden album (whiteboard) re-inscribed

PERSONAL GRAFFITI:
  Love, insults, declarations of presence
  "Successus the weaver loves Iris, the innkeeper's
   slave girl" (with rival response and counter-response)
  -- three-person dialogue in graffiti
  "I screwed the innkeeper's wife" (claims are common)
  "Gaius Pumidius Dipilus was here on 3 October"
  (oldest known "was here" inscription -- 78 BCE)

ALPHABETS AND WRITING PRACTICE:
  Children (and adults) practicing letter forms on walls
  Evidence of widespread literacy in urban Pompeii

LITERARY QUOTATIONS:
  Virgil's Aeneid lines quoted from memory
  Suggests Aeneid was memorized in schools
  (Not always accurately -- memory errors visible)
```

### Roman Legal Texts on Bronze

```
BRONZE TABULA (BRONZE TABLE) INSCRIPTIONS
==========================================

Laws, citizenship grants, veteran privileges on bronze:

Tabula Clesiana (Bronze): copies of laws posted publicly
Diplomata militaria: military discharge certificates
  -> Bronze = permanent; bronze = forgeable (known problem)
  -> Issued in pairs, one sealed, one open; compared

Lex Malacitana (Malaga, Spain, ~81 CE):
  Municipal law granting town rights
  Complete text for running a Roman municipium
  -> Price of grain, election procedures, court jurisdiction

Tabula Bantina (late 2nd c. BCE):
  Oscan language law on bronze
  -> Shows non-Latin communities using Roman legal form

Bronze archives:
  Rome: Capitoline temples held bronze originals
  Provinces: copies posted in main town forum
  The posted copy is not secondary -- it is authoritative
```

## Athenian Tribute Lists

A specific epigraphic dataset worth knowing:

```
ATHENIAN TRIBUTE LISTS (Attica, 454-415 BCE)
==============================================

Background:
  After 478 BCE: Athenian-led Delian League vs. Persia
  454 BCE: league treasury moved from Delos to Athens
  Custom: 1/60 of each member's tribute dedicated to Athena
  1/60 portion inscribed on stone stelae on Acropolis

Recovery:
  286 stone fragments recovered from Acropolis excavations
  Assembled by Meritt, Wade-Gery, McGregor (1939-1953)
  "Athenian Tribute Lists" (ATL) 4 volumes

Information content:
  Tribute-paying allies listed by geographic panel
  Amount paid (1/60 fraction)
  Annual record, 454-415 BCE
  Gaps correspond to events (Sicilian expedition years)

Historical value:
  Primary source for size of Athenian empire
  Maps of member states
  Evidence of tribute changes (increase = punitive)
  Evidence of revolts (absence from list)
  This is the financial statement of the Athenian empire
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Inscription type | Standard formula | What it records |
|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Greek honorific | "The demos praised X and crowned him..." | Democratic award, benefactor honors |
| Greek votive | "[Name] dedicated to [deity]" | Religious act, social display |
| Greek funerary | "I am the marker of X who died..." | Identity, tribal affiliation, age |
| Latin funerary | "D.M.S. [name] vixit annis X" | Age, DM dedication, dedicant |
| Latin milestone | "To the Emperor X... N miles from Y" | Road infrastructure |
| Electoral notice | "[Organization] urges you to elect X..." | Political campaigning |
| Military diploma | "Emperor X to soldier Y who served Z years..." | Citizenship + discharge rights |

## Common Confusion Points

**The CIL is not fully digitized.** The EDCS (Epigraphik-Datenbank Clauss-Slaby) has 490,000+ Latin inscriptions and is free online — more comprehensive than the CIL digital access. PHI covers Greek.

**Pompeii graffiti are not vandalism.** They are a documentary resource for non-elite Latin and Roman urban life. The Romans also had official painted inscriptions (programmata) on building facades — writing on walls was not inherently informal.

**Ostraka are not ballots.** Ostracism worked by vote: citizens wrote the name of who they wanted exiled on a pottery shard. A quorum of 6,000 was required; the person with the most votes was exiled for 10 years. The pre-written batches found at Kerameikos suggest organized political machine operation, not spontaneous democratic participation.

**Bronze inscriptions were prestigious but forgeable.** Military discharge diplomas (diplomata militaria) were valuable property documents. Forgeries are known; the dual-copy system (sealed + open) was an anti-forgery measure.

**"Official" inscriptions are not neutral documents.** They represent the view of the authority that commissioned them. Athenian casualty lists, for example, may not include all dead — they record the honored dead, not all dead.
