# Runic Scripts: Elder Futhark, Younger Futhark, Anglo-Saxon Futhorc

## The Big Picture

```
RUNIC SCRIPT FAMILY
====================

ELDER FUTHARK (2nd - 8th c. CE)
  24 runes
  Proto-Germanic language
  Earliest Germanic writing
       |
       +------------------+------------------+
       |                                     |
       v                                     v
YOUNGER FUTHARK (8th-12th c.)         FUTHORC (5th-11th c.)
  16 runes                              28-33 runes
  Old Norse (Viking age)                Old English / Anglo-Saxon
  REDUCED from 24                       EXTENDED from 24
  (paradox: language more complex;      Added letters for OE sounds
   script simpler)                      Northumbrian variant: 33 runes

GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION:
  Elder Futhark: Scandinavia, northern Europe
  Younger Futhark: Scandinavia, Viking world (Iceland to Russia)
  Futhorc: England, some on continent

SURVIVAL MEDIA:
  Stone (runestones): most survive
  Metal objects: fibulae, weapons, amulets
  Bone, antler: personal items
  Wood: mostly lost (organic decay)
  Rarely: manuscript (runes in Anglo-Saxon manuscripts)
```

The name "futhark" (and "futhorc") comes from the first six letters of the runic alphabet: F-U-T-H-A-R-K — analogous to "alphabet" from Greek alpha-beta.

## Elder Futhark: The Original Runic Alphabet

### Sign Inventory and Names

```
ELDER FUTHARK (24 runes, standard transliteration)
====================================================

ROW 1 (Aett 1 -- Freyr's eight):
  f  u  th  a  r  k  g  w
  F  U  TH  A  R  K  G  W

ROW 2 (Aett 2 -- Hagall's eight):
  h  n  i  j  ei  p  z  s
  H  N  I  Y  EI  P  Z  S

ROW 3 (Aett 3 -- Tyr's eight):
  t  b  e  m  l  ng  d  o
  T  B  E  M  L  NG  D  O

Sign design principles:
  No horizontal strokes (hard to carve along wood grain)
  All lines are vertical or diagonal
  Simple = fast to carve
  Each rune has a NAME (runic poem tradition):
    F-rune: "Fehu" (cattle/wealth)
    U-rune: "Uruz" (aurochs/strength)
    TH-rune: "Thurisaz" (giant/thorn)
    etc.

PHYSICAL CARVING:
  Typically carved along (not across) the grain
  Inscription reads left-to-right (most common),
    right-to-left, or boustrophedon (alternating)
  No word spaces in early inscriptions
  Bind runes: two runes merged into single carved sign
    (space-saving; also used for magical/aesthetic purposes)
```

### Origin Question

```
RUNIC ORIGIN HYPOTHESES
========================

MAINSTREAM: derived from North Italic / Latin script(s)
  Best supported by:
    - Some sign shapes similar to North Italic alphabets
      (Raetic, Lepontic inscriptions from Alpine region)
    - Germanic tribes in contact with these scripts 2nd-1st c. BCE
    - Latin influence on Elder Futhark sign values

ALTERNATIVE: derived from Latin alone
  Latin was the most visible Roman script to Germanic tribes
  Some signs have clear Latin parallels (A, B, R, T)

ALTERNATIVE: derived from Greek
  Some scholars: Etruscan/Greek route via Goths in Danube region
  Less supported by geographic and chronological evidence

CONCLUSION: No consensus; derivation from an Italic
  script (possibly Raetic or via Etruscan) is most likely

EARLIEST KNOWN ELDER FUTHARK INSCRIPTIONS:
  Meldorf fibula (~50 CE): debated (may be Latin)
  Vimose comb (~160 CE): "harja" (personal name?)
  Gallehus horns (~400 CE): "ek hlewagastiz holtijaz
                              horna tawido"
  = "I, Hlewagastiz son of Holt, made this horn"
  -> Longest early Elder Futhark sentence
```

## Elder Futhark Inscription Purposes

Runic inscriptions served multiple purposes, not primarily literacy:

```
ELDER FUTHARK USES
===================

1. OWNERSHIP MARKS:
   "[Name] owns this"
   Most common use: identifying property
   Weapons, tools, jewelry with owner's rune-name
   Simpler than writing: just the owner's name carved

2. MEMORIAL INSCRIPTIONS:
   "[Name] made/carved/gave this in memory of [Name]"
   Appearing on runestones from mid-first millennium
   Pre-runestone period: memorial on objects (not freestanding stones)

3. MAGICAL / APOTROPAIC:
   Rune names have semantic content:
     Fehu (F) = wealth -> carve F for wealth
     Tiwaz (T) = the god Tyr -> carve for victory in battle
   Repeated rune: alu (unclear meaning but very common)
   "alu" = possibly magical formula, charm
   Rune sequences as binding spells

4. MANUFACTURE MARKS:
   "X made this" on object of craftsmanship
   Quality claim? Ownership? Possibly both

5. CRYPTIC / MAGICAL SEQUENCES:
   Some inscriptions are not clearly meaningful
   Possible: magical purpose; possible: formulaic
   The alu example: not translatable as a word in
   any Germanic language but appears 60+ times
```

## Younger Futhark: The Viking Age Script

### The Reduction Paradox

```
YOUNGER FUTHARK REDUCTION
==========================

Old Norse (Viking period) was MORE phonologically complex
than Proto-Germanic:
  - More distinct vowels
  - More consonant distinctions
  - New sounds developed

Yet the runic script REDUCED from 24 to 16 signs.
ONE SIGN now represents multiple sounds:
  t/d: same rune
  k/g: same rune
  b/p/v: same rune
  i/e: same rune

Why? Two hypotheses:
  1. PRAGMATIC: runes were primarily for practical purposes
     (ownership, memorial). Ambiguity was acceptable
     because context disambiguated.
  2. REFORM: deliberate simplification for faster carving
     on the expanded runestone memorial tradition

Result: reading Younger Futhark is harder than Elder
  Multiple possible readings for many inscribed words
  Context and knowledge of Old Norse required
```

### Runestone Tradition

```
VIKING AGE RUNESTONES
======================

Period: primarily 8th-12th c. CE
Distribution: Scandinavia concentrated (~3,000 in Sweden alone)
  Sweden: 2,500+ runestones (most in the world)
  Denmark: ~250
  Norway: ~50
  England (Norse): ~30 (Scandinavian settlers)
  Russia: scattered (Varangian trade route)

Standard memorial formula (Swedish):
  "[Name] raised this stone in memory of [Name]
   his [relationship]
   He [died / was killed / went east / was in X]
   May God / Christ bless his soul"

Key: Christian and pagan elements mixed through 10th-11th c.
Most runestones are CHRISTIAN memorial stones
The "pagan" association of runes is largely a 19th c. myth

JELLING STONES (Denmark, ~960-985 CE):
  Harald Bluetooth's stones
  Small Jelling stone: Harald commemorates his parents
  Large Jelling stone: "Harald who won for himself all of
    Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian"
  -> First mention of "Denmark" as a unified entity
  -> First definitive evidence of Danish Christianization
  -> The Bluetooth wireless logo is Harald Bluetooth's runic initials
```

## Anglo-Saxon Futhorc

```
FUTHORC EXTENSIONS
==================

Base: 24 Elder Futhark runes
Added: runes for Old English sounds not in Proto-Germanic
  OS/OE rune: the "ash" rune (ae sound)
  AC rune: the "oak" rune (o/a variation)
  AESC rune: the front-vowel A
  YR rune: y sound
  IOR rune: unclear
  EAR rune: e+ar diphthong
  etc. -- up to 33 in Northumbrian variant

PURPOSE:
  Old English had sounds that 24 Elder Futhark runes
  could not represent adequately
  The futhorc was EXPANDED to cover them
  (opposite direction from Younger Futhark reduction)

FUTHORC USAGE:
  Primarily in England; 5th-11th c. CE
  Less common than Latin script (which was dominant
    due to Christian church/monastic literacy)
  Runic inscriptions in OE are rare relative to Latin
  Main uses: ownership marks, jewelry, crosses, coins

NOTABLE FUTHORC INSCRIPTIONS:
  Ruthwell Cross (~7th-8th c. CE):
    Large stone cross, Dumfriesshire, Scotland
    Runic inscription = portions of "The Dream of the Rood"
    (Old English religious poem -- the cross speaks)
    Same poem also in Vercelli Book (manuscript)
    Runestone + manuscript versions allow cross-checking

  Franks Casket (~700 CE):
    Whale-bone box, Northumbrian
    Mixed runic and Latin inscriptions
    Scenes from Roman, Jewish, Christian, Germanic traditions
    "Fisch" story in runes = origins of the whale-bone material
```

## Rune Masters and Literacy

```
RUNE MASTER (runristare) STATUS
=================================

Runic literacy was NOT widespread general literacy.
It was a specialized skill:
  Some inscriptions signed: "X risted (carved) these runes"
  The carver is identified as a specialist
  Clients commissioned runestones from rune masters

Evidence for specialized literacy:
  Quality varies enormously across runestones
  Some: elegant, error-free, well-spaced
  Some: poorly spaced, mirror signs, errors
  -> Range from expert to barely competent

MAGIC SPECIALISTS vs. MONUMENT CARVERS:
  Early period (Elder Futhark): small objects, magical use
    -> Specialized knowledge of rune names/powers implied
  Viking period (Younger Futhark): runestones, memorials
    -> Primarily a writing system; magical use secondary
    -> Monument industry = commercial skill, not esoteric

19TH-CENTURY ROMANTICISM:
  The Germanic/Norse revival (Romanticism) created a mythology
  of runic as deeply magical/mystical/esoteric
  This is NOT the scholarly picture:
    Medieval Icelanders used runes as an alphabet
    alongside Latin; no special mystical status
    The "magical" inscriptions are a subset, not the whole
```

## Common Confusion Points

**The reduction from 24 to 16 runes is genuinely paradoxical.** The language became more complex; the script became simpler. The best explanation is that Viking-age runes served primarily practical monument and ownership functions where precision was less critical than carving speed.

**Most Viking-age runestones are Christian memorial monuments.** The association of runes with paganism is a Romantic-era myth. The runestone tradition peaked in the 10th-11th centuries — precisely when Scandinavia was Christianizing. Most runestones invoke Christ or God.

**Bluetooth is named after Harald Bluetooth.** Harald Bluetooth (Harald Blatan), Danish king ~958-987 CE, unified Denmark and promoted Christianity. The wireless Bluetooth technology logo combines his runic initials (H and B in Younger Futhark). The technology was developed in Scandinavia by Ericsson.

**Not all runic inscriptions are meaningful text.** Some are collections of rune names (cryptic/magical). Some are alphabetic practice runs. Some are simply decorative. "Reading" a runic inscription requires distinguishing these categories.

**The Ruthwell Cross is one of the oldest Old English literary texts.** It predates most Old English manuscripts. The poem "The Dream of the Rood" — where the cross narrates the Crucifixion — appears in runic form on the cross centuries before the manuscript version. This shows that runic was used for literary composition in the Anglo-Saxon tradition.
