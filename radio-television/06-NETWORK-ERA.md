# Network Television Era: Big Three, Murrow/Cronkite News, Sitcoms, Color Transition, Nielsen Ratings

## The Big Picture

The network television era (roughly 1948-1980) was a period of unprecedented cultural homogeneity: three networks reached 90%+ of TV households simultaneously, sharing the same schedule. Nothing before or since has approached this level of simultaneous mass audience. Understanding the mechanics of this oligopoly explains both its power and its vulnerability to disruption.

```
NETWORK ERA STRUCTURE

AUDIENCE CONCENTRATION:
  1955-1980: ABC + CBS + NBC combined share: 90-95% of primetime viewing
  One popular show: 30-40% of all TV households simultaneously
  CBS Sunday Night (1960s): Bonanza could reach 40M viewers
  Today's top shows: 8-12M viewers (fragmented audience)

THREE-NETWORK OLIGOPOLY:
  Entry barrier 1: FCC licenses (limited spectrum)
  Entry barrier 2: affiliate relationships (decades to build)
  Entry barrier 3: O&O stations in major markets (owned and operated)
  Entry barrier 4: production/distribution infrastructure
  Entry barrier 5: advertiser relationships + rating services

  NO FOURTH NETWORK for 30 years:
  DuMont Network: tried 1946-1955; failed (couldn't get major affiliates)
  Fox Network: succeeded 1986 (with Fox-owned stations + satellite)
  Why Fox succeeded: UHF stations available; paid affiliates well;
                     targeting specific underserved demographics (youth)

PROGRAMMING SCHEDULE:
  Daytime: soap operas, game shows (cheap production)
  Prime time: 8-11 PM ET (most valuable; expensive production)
  Late night: news, Johnny Carson/Tonight Show
  Weekend mornings: children's programming
  Network: rarely owned content; licensed from studios
  Studios: produced shows; sold license to network; owned syndication rights
```

## The Big Three Networks

```
ABC, CBS, NBC: COMPARATIVE HISTORY

NBC (National Broadcasting Company):
  Parent: RCA until 1986, then GE, then Comcast (2011)
  Founded: 1926 (radio), TV: 1939 (experimental), 1944 (commercial)
  First TV network in US (RCA/Sarnoff investment)
  O&O stations: major market flagships
  History:
    1940s-50s: dominant (first mover, RCA technology, NBC radio stars)
    1960s: lost lead to CBS (entertainment strategy)
    1970s: weak (rural strategy while urban advertisers wanted urban audience)
    1980s: "Must See TV" (Cosby, Cheers, Family Ties, Seinfeld)
    Acquisition: GE 1986; Comcast 2011

CBS (Columbia Broadcasting System):
  Parent: William Paley family until 1995, then Westinghouse, then Viacom
  Founded: 1928 (radio), TV: 1941 (experimental)
  Strategy: quality + mass appeal + news prestige
  History:
    1950s: news leadership (Murrow), comedy stars raided from NBC
    1960s: dominant (rural, middle-America appeal)
    1970s: #1 ratings; "Tiffany Network" (quality reputation)
    1980: "Rural purge" undid this (advertisers wanted young urban)
    Late 1970s-80s: lost to ABC, then NBC recovered
    2000s: "CSI" era comeback; strong in news (60 Minutes)

ABC (American Broadcasting Company):
  Parent: originally owned by Edward Noble, then Capital Cities, then Disney (1995)
  Founded: 1943 (forced NBC Blue sale)
  History:
    1950s-60s: weakest of three; early deals with Disney (Disneyland show)
    1970s: "Jiggle TV" + Roots + Monday Night Football -> #1 ratings
    1976-77: first full season at #1 (won with entertainment; lost news)
    1980s: struggling again
    1995: Disney acquisition; strength in entertainment and sports (ESPN connection)

OWNERSHIP CURRENT (2024):
  NBC -> Comcast/NBCUniversal
  CBS -> Paramount Global (Shari Redstone)
  ABC -> Walt Disney Company
  All: now subsidiaries of much larger conglomerates
  Broadcast networks: declining piece of conglomerate value
  Streaming (Peacock, Paramount+, Disney+): more investment attention
```

## Broadcast News: Murrow and Cronkite

```
BROADCAST JOURNALISM STANDARDS

EDWARD R. MURROW (1908-1965):
  CBS News; career 1935-1961
  London Blitz broadcasts (1940-41): established his stature
  Post-WWII: radio to television transition
  See It Now (1951-58): documentary news program

  MCCARTHY EPISODE (March 9, 1954):
  Joseph McCarthy: Senator, anti-communist investigations (1950-54)
  Murrow: devoted "See It Now" entirely to McCarthy footage
  Let McCarthy condemn himself from his own footage and statements
  Then Murrow's commentary:
  "The line between investigating and persecuting is a very fine one
   and the junior Senator from Wisconsin has stepped over it repeatedly"

  Impact: first time national media directly challenged McCarthy
  McCarthy demanded reply time: given it; his reply further damaged him
  Army-McCarthy Hearings followed (1954): McCarthy discredited
  McCarthy censured by Senate December 1954

  CONTROVERSY:
  Some credit Murrow with ending McCarthy's career
  Reality: more complex (Army hearings + Republican disenchantment were key)
  But: Murrow demonstrated journalism's institutional power
  Paley reaction: supportive publicly, annoyed privately (advertiser complaints)
  See It Now: canceled 1958 (eventually)

  Murrow's farewell speech (1958):
  "This instrument [television] can teach, it can illuminate; yes, and it
   even can inspire. But it can do so only to the extent that humans are
   determined to use it to those ends. Otherwise it is merely wires and light
   in a box."
  Prescient criticism of television's commercial trajectory

WALTER CRONKITE (1916-2009):
  CBS Evening News anchor 1962-1981
  "Most Trusted Man in America" (1972 Opinion Research poll)
  Why trusted: deliberate objectivity, careful sourcing, calm delivery
              represented CBS News institutional credibility

  KEY MOMENTS:
  Kennedy assassination (November 22, 1963):
    Cronkite: on-air when confirmed
    Removed glasses, paused, voice broke slightly
    "The President of the United States is dead."
    Gave Americans shared experience of collective grief
    Live news: transformative; ~100M people watching

  Vietnam "Editorial" (February 27, 1968):
    After Tet Offensive: Cronkite traveled to Vietnam
    Returned; gave rare personal commentary:
    "To say we are closer to victory today is to believe...
     the optimists who have been wrong in the past... we are
     mired in stalemate"
    LBJ reportedly said: "If I've lost Cronkite, I've lost middle America"
    Whether LBJ said this: disputed; but represented the effect
    Cronkite's credibility: political fact

  SPACE COVERAGE:
  Apollo program: Cronkite covered every mission
  Moon landing (July 20, 1969): Cronkite's stunned reaction
  "Oh boy! Whew! Boy!"
  250M people watching (US + international)
  Largest TV audience to that point
```

## Television Genres and Format Development

```
GOLDEN AGE TV GENRES

SITCOM (SITUATION COMEDY):
  Format: ~22-30 minutes, recurring characters, laugh track
  "Three-camera": multiple cameras, live studio audience (or recorded)
  Structure: setup, complication, resolution within one episode
  "Escapist": laugh track trains audience response; formulaic but reliable

  EVOLUTION:
  1950s: I Love Lucy (Desi Arnaz invented: film the show vs live broadcast)
    Lucy: first to use three cameras + film + laugh track simultaneously
    Invented: "rerun" market (filmed episodes could be shown again)
    Before Lucy: live TV = one-time; film = expensive; Arnaz solved this

  1960s: Beverly Hillbillies (rural humor), Bewitched (suburban fantasy)
  1970s: All in the Family (Lear) - social commentary acceptable
         Mary Tyler Moore - professional woman protagonist
  1980s: Cosby Show - Black middle class normalcy; massive ratings
  1990s: Seinfeld - anti-sitcom sitcom; "show about nothing"
  2000s: Office (single camera, mockumentary)

EPISODIC DRAMA:
  ~48-60 minutes; recurring cast; plot usually resolved per episode
  Western: Gunsmoke (1955-1975: 20 seasons, longest-running US primetime drama)
  Medical: Ben Casey, Dr. Kildare
  Legal: Perry Mason
  Police: Dragnet, Adam-12
  Continuity: minimal (each episode standalone for syndication value)

SOAP OPERA (DAYTIME):
  Continuous narrative, ongoing storylines
  Never resolves: keeps audience coming back
  Budget: cheap (interior sets, small cast, fast production)
  General Hospital (1963+), Days of Our Lives (1965+)

MINISERIES:
  Limited run of 2-8 episodes on consecutive nights
  Roots (1977): 8 nights, final episode 100M viewers (largest US TV audience)
  Roots: demonstrated miniseries format could capture mass audience
```

## Color Television Transition

```
COLOR TV TRANSITION IN US (1953-1967)

CONTEXT:
  1950: RCA and CBS both had color TV systems
  CBS: field-sequential system (wheel with color filters)
       Incompatible with existing B&W sets
       Better picture quality
  RCA: compatible system (existing B&W sets show B&W)
       Inferior initial quality
  FCC initially (1950): approved CBS system

RCA COUNTER-STRATEGY:
  Sarnoff refused to manufacture B&W sets that could receive CBS color
  RCA lobbied: "CBS system will strand millions of B&W sets"
  Korean War (1951): FCC froze TV station allocations, froze color
  1953: FCC reversed, approved NTSC (RCA-compatible) color standard
  Market strategy: RCA outmaneuvered CBS via lobbying + market power
  This is why we have NTSC color (compatible but inferior initial quality)

SLOW COLOR ADOPTION:
  1954: RCA CT-100: first consumer color TV, $1,000 ($11,000 in 2024$)
  1955-1960: very few color sets sold; too expensive; little color content
  1960: only ~2% of US homes had color TV
  1961: NBC starts broadcasting in color more consistently (RCA owned NBC)
        Sarnoff used NBC to drive color set sales (vertical integration logic)
  1964: Tokyo Olympics: NBC broadcasts in color (watershed)
  1965: ~3% of US homes had color
  "Color transition year": 1965-1967
    1965-66 season: NBC switches to all-color prime time
    CBS and ABC follow immediately
    Color set sales: 6.5M (1966), 9M (1967), 12M+ (1968)
  1972: more color than B&W sets in US homes for first time
  1980s: B&W TVs essentially disappeared from main household use

PRODUCTION IMPLICATIONS:
  Color requires: more lighting (orthochromatic problems gone, but color needs light)
  Sets: must be built for color (no more grey tones that "read" on B&W)
  Wardrobe: carefully chosen colors (some colors look wrong or bleed on early NTSC)
  Color correction: new technical role on live programs
  Tape: color required higher-quality video tape technology
```

## Nielsen Ratings: The Measurement System

```
NIELSEN RATINGS: HOW THEY WORKED

ARTHUR C. NIELSEN SR. (1897-1980):
  Founded Nielsen Company 1923
  1936: applied ratings methodology to radio
  1950: extended to television

PURPOSE:
  Networks need to know: how many people watch each show?
  Advertisers need to know: how many people in their target demographic?
  Without ratings: advertising is blind; cannot price time slots

SAMPLING METHODOLOGY:
  You cannot measure every TV in America simultaneously
  Sample: ~5,000-40,000 household panel (representative of 125M+ households)
  Statistical inference: measure sample; extrapolate to total population

METERED HOUSEHOLDS:
  "People Meters" (1987+): electronic devices attached to TV
  Records: every channel changed, times on/off
  Audience member: enters ID code when they start watching
  Reports: nightly to Nielsen over phone line (later internet)

DIARY SYSTEM (older; still used in smaller markets):
  Household: keeps paper diary of viewing
  Sweeps months: February, May, July, November
  "Sweeps" period: all markets measured; rest of year: samples only
  Consequence: networks program strongest content during sweeps
  Viewers: got better shows 4 months/year
  Rest of year: more reruns, weaker content

WHAT RATINGS MEASURE:
  Rating: % of total TV households watching
  Share: % of households using TV at that time, watching show
  HUT: Households Using Television (denominator for share)
  Demographic ratings: 18-49, 25-54, women 18-49 etc.
  CPM: cost per thousand viewers (advertising price basis)

A "rating point":
  1 rating point = 1% of ~125M US TV households = 1.25M households
  30.0 rating = 37.5M households (Roots, 1977)
  Modern top show: 7-8 rating = 8-10M households

WHY DEMOGRAPHICS MATTER MORE THAN TOTAL VIEWERS:
  Advertisers: want specific buyers of their products
  18-49: peak earning + spending years; advertiser's sweet spot
  65+: brand loyal, less likely to switch; advertisers pay less for them
  Rural, older viewers: CBS strength in 1970s
  Urban, younger viewers: advertisers wanted this demographic
  Result: CBS canceled top-rated rural shows (1971 "Rural Purge")
          even though they had more total viewers
          because advertisers paid more for urban demographic
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Higher ratings always kept shows on the air."** Ratings are filtered through demographics. A show with 30M viewers of mostly elderly viewers might be canceled while a show with 10M viewers of 18-49 year olds is renewed — because advertisers pay 4-5x more per viewer in the 18-49 demographic. CBS's "Rural Purge" is the clearest example: canceled shows with millions of viewers because the viewers weren't in the right demographic.

**"Murrow ended McCarthyism."** Murrow's "See It Now" episode was important but not singular. The Army-McCarthy hearings (April-June 1954), Joseph Welch's "Have you no sense of decency?" confrontation, and growing Republican discomfort with McCarthy were larger factors. Murrow demonstrated journalism could challenge power; the political/institutional factors did the actual work.

**"Networks owned the shows they aired."** In the classical network era, networks licensed shows from independent studios (Warner Bros, Universal, independent producers). The studios owned the shows, could syndicate reruns, and sold international rights. Networks paid license fees for first-run. The fin-syn rules (Financial Interest and Syndication rules, 1970) explicitly prevented networks from owning or syndicating most programming. After fin-syn repeal (1991-95), networks vertically integrated: ABC = Disney = produces its own shows. This consolidation created the current conglomerate structure.

**"Live TV means everything was spontaneous."** Live television was staged and scripted, with extensive rehearsal. "Live" meant transmitted in real-time rather than pre-recorded and delayed. Ed Sullivan rehearsed acts all day Sunday before the Sunday evening live broadcast. News anchors read from scripts or teleprompters. Live = real-time transmission, not improvisation.
