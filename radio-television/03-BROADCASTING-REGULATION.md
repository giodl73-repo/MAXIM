# Broadcasting Regulation: Radio Acts 1912/1927, FCC 1934, Public Interest Standard, ITU, Fairness Doctrine

## The Big Picture

Broadcasting regulation is market design for a shared public resource. The core problem: spectrum is a public good (non-excludable in physics, finite), but private entities profit from using it. Regulation determines who gets access, on what terms, and what obligations come with that access. The regulatory decisions made in the 1920s-1930s created the media structure that shaped American culture for 70+ years.

```
REGULATORY ARCHITECTURE

INTERNATIONAL LAYER:
  International Telecommunication Union (ITU)
  Founded: 1865 (as International Telegraph Union)
  Role: coordinate spectrum allocation between nations
        set technical standards for interoperability
  Radio regs: adopted 1906 (first Berlin convention)
  Spectrum: divided into frequency bands, allocated to services
            (fixed, mobile, broadcast, satellite, etc.)
  ITU allotments: each nation gets frequencies to assign nationally
  No enforcement against sovereign nations (except by treaty/diplomacy)

NATIONAL LAYER (US):
  Radio Act 1912
  Radio Act 1927 (creates FRC)
  Communications Act 1934 (creates FCC, still in force)
  Telecommunications Act 1996 (major update)

STATE/LOCAL:
  Franchise agreements (cable TV)
  Limited role vs federal (broadcast = interstate commerce)

PRIVATE:
  Industry self-regulation (MPAA, NAB)
  Network standards and practices (network censors)
  Station policies

KEY PRINCIPLE: Spectrum is owned by the public
               Licenses: temporary permission to USE spectrum
               Not ownership; revocable if public interest not served
               Broadcasters: 1st Amendment protection BUT conditional
                             (not pure speech right; platform obligation)
```

## Radio Act of 1912

```
RADIO ACT OF 1912

CONTEXT: Titanic disaster, April 15, 1912
  Titanic sank; 1,517 people died
  Wireless operators: sent distress signals
  SS California: only 20 miles away, but its operator had gone to bed
  Amateur operators: crowded distress frequencies with traffic
  Californian: might have rescued more if radio watch maintained

  Congress: passed Radio Act of 1912 just months later

KEY PROVISIONS:
  1. LICENSING: All radio stations must be licensed
     Secretary of Commerce issues licenses
     But: had to issue them (no discretion to refuse)
     Problem: no authority to deny license -> no control over congestion

  2. PRIORITY FREQUENCIES:
     Ships: must monitor 500 kHz (international distress frequency)
     Distress signal: SOS (· · · — — — · · ·) protected
     Continuous wave for distress (over spark gap)

  3. AMATEUR ALLOCATION:
     Amateurs: restricted to wavelengths below 200 meters
               (i.e., above 1.5 MHz — considered useless at the time)
     Later discovered: short waves great for long-distance (sky wave)
     Amateurs: accidentally given the most valuable spectrum
               -> drove development of HF band
               -> amateur operators (hams) pioneered techniques
                  used in commercial radio

FAILURE:
  Courts: Secretary of Commerce cannot deny licenses
  Cannot control: location, power, frequency (within band)
  1925: ~5,700 licensed stations in US
  Interference: widespread, severe
  Federal courts: Commerce Secretary cannot impose order
  New law required

TITANIC PARALLEL:
  The Titanic disaster created the political will for regulation
  Same pattern: disaster -> regulation in:
    Financial crisis 2008 -> Dodd-Frank 2010
    Enron 2001 -> Sarbanes-Oxley 2002
    Regulation follows visible failure, not theoretical prevention
```

## Radio Act of 1927 and the FRC

```
RADIO ACT OF 1927

CONTEXT:
  1926: Zenith Radio case
    Zenith moved to frequency not licensed, at different hours
    Court: Secretary of Commerce cannot enforce restrictions
    Commerce Secretary Hoover: "No law"
    Result: "breakdown of radio regulation" (Hoover's words)
    Stations: moved to unauthorized frequencies, increased power
    Radio: chaos; listeners couldn't hear anything clearly

  Congress: Radio Act of 1927

KEY FEATURES:

1. FEDERAL RADIO COMMISSION (FRC):
   5-member commission appointed by President, confirmed by Senate
   Jurisdiction: all interstate and foreign radio communication
   Authority: could DENY, revoke, or modify licenses
   Fixed term licenses: 3 years (renewable)

2. PUBLIC INTEREST STANDARD:
   Licenses granted "if public convenience, interest, or necessity will be served"
   No definition of "public interest" in the Act
   Intentionally vague: regulatory flexibility
   FRC/FCC: must interpret "public interest" case by case

3. SPECTRUM AS PUBLIC PROPERTY:
   "No license shall be construed to create any right
    beyond the terms, conditions, and period of the license"
   Explicit: licensee does not own spectrum
             Government can take back, refuse to renew

4. NATIONAL BROADCASTING:
   1928 "Great Reallocation":
   FRC reviewed all ~700 station licenses
   Cleared: low-power, educational, religious stations
   Assigned: clear channels to powerful national stations
   Result: NBC and CBS dominate; independent stations reduced
   Controversial: favored commercial interests over educational

CLEAR CHANNEL STATIONS:
  50,000 watt high-power stations on exclusive frequencies
  Nighttime: cover entire US via sky wave (AM ground wave + sky wave)
  WGN Chicago, WSB Atlanta, WLW Cincinnati: still operate
  These assignments from 1928 FRC reallocation persist today
  "Clear Channel" company name: named after this regulatory privilege
```

## Communications Act of 1934 — The FCC

```
COMMUNICATIONS ACT OF 1934

CONTEXT:
  FDR New Deal era: consolidating regulatory agencies
  Radio Act 1927: FRC handles radio
  Interstate Commerce Commission: handles telephone (common carrier)
  Confusion: different agencies, overlapping jurisdictions

  Communications Act: created FCC, consolidated all wire and radio
                      communications under one agency

FCC STRUCTURE:
  7 commissioners (later reduced to 5), Presidential appointment
  Senate confirmation, staggered terms
  No more than majority from one party (bipartisan)
  Chair: designated by President
  Bureaus: Wireline, Wireless, Media, Consumer & Governmental Affairs,
           International, Public Safety

JURISDICTION:
  Title I: General provisions
  Title II: Common carriers (telephone, telegraph, later broadband)
  Title III: Radio (broadcast)
  Title VI: Cable (added 1984)
  Various additional titles since 1934

PUBLIC INTEREST STANDARD (carries over from 1927):
  License condition: station must serve "public interest, convenience,
                     and necessity"
  FCC interpretation has varied dramatically over decades:
    1940s-1970s: strict (content obligations, editorializing rules)
    1980s+: deregulatory (market = public interest)
    2000s: indecency enforcement increases post-Super Bowl 2004
  Regulatory pendulum: swings with administration ideology

STRUCTURE OF BROADCASTING REGULATION:
  Technical regulation: transmitter power, frequency, antenna height
                        FCC engineers and broadcast engineers negotiate
  Ownership regulation: limits on who can own how many stations
                        Cross-ownership rules
  Content regulation: limited by 1st Amendment
                      But: broadcast (public spectrum) = less protection
                      vs cable/internet (private infrastructure) = more

BROADCAST vs CABLE vs INTERNET:
  +------------------+------------------+------------------+
  | BROADCAST        | CABLE            | INTERNET         |
  | (licensed)       | (franchised)     | (unregulated)    |
  +------------------+------------------+------------------+
  | Public spectrum  | Private wires    | Private/common   |
  | FCC licensed     | Municipal franchise                 |
  | Public interest  | Must-carry rules | Net neutrality   |
  | obligations      | Rate regulation  | debate only      |
  | Content limits   | No content limits| No content limits|
  | Scarcity rationale| No scarcity     | No scarcity      |
  +------------------+------------------+------------------+
```

## ITU and International Spectrum Coordination

```
ITU: INTERNATIONAL TELECOMMUNICATION UNION

HISTORY:
  1865: International Telegraph Union (Paris)
        Standardize telegraph protocols between nations
  1906: First International Radiotelegraph Convention (Berlin)
        First international radio coordination
        Required: ships carry radio, monitor distress frequencies
  1932: ITU formed (merged telegraph + radio organizations)
  1947: ITU becomes UN specialized agency
  1965: Telecommunications in name (adding "Tele")
  2024: 193 member states

STRUCTURE:
  Radiocommunication Sector (ITU-R):
    World Radiocommunication Conference (WRC): every 3-4 years
    Radio Regulations: international treaty (binding)
    Spectrum allotments: which services get which bands globally

  Standardization Sector (ITU-T):
    Technical standards (modulation, protocols, interfaces)
    Many standards you use: ITU-T H.264 (video), G.711 (voice codec)

SPECTRUM ALLOCATION TABLE:
  ITU divides world into 3 regions:
  Region 1: Europe, Africa, Middle East, Russia
  Region 2: Americas
  Region 3: Asia-Pacific

  Each frequency band: allocated to specific services
  Allocation: "primary" or "secondary" service
  Primary: can operate freely (within ITU limits)
  Secondary: cannot interfere with primary; must accept interference

  Example: 2.4 GHz (WiFi):
  ITU allocation: ISM (Industrial, Scientific, Medical) unlicensed
  Secondary status in most regions
  Must accept: microwave oven interference (same band!)

INTERNATIONAL COORDINATION:
  New satellite in orbit: must coordinate with ITU
  New broadcast station near border: must not interfere with neighbors
  Agreements: bilateral or multilateral treaties
  Enforcement: diplomatic (no ITU police)
  Reality: major powers ignore inconvenient allocations sometimes
           (Russia, China use spectrum outside formal allocations at times)
           US NTIA coordinates federal use; FCC coordinates commercial
```

## The Fairness Doctrine

```
FAIRNESS DOCTRINE (1949-1987)

ORIGIN:
  1929: FRC: "...a broadcasting station can be...
        a reasonably balanced discussion of the problems of public interest"
  1949 FCC Report: "Editorializing by Broadcast Licensees"
    Formalized as regulatory policy
    Required: broadcast licensees (1) cover controversial public issues
              and (2) give competing viewpoints fair opportunity to respond

TWO COMPONENTS:
  1. DUTY TO COVER: stations must air controversial issues
     (Can't avoid controversy by ignoring it entirely)
  2. DUTY TO AIR OPPOSING VIEWS: present contrasting perspectives
     Not equal time (that's the Equal Time Rule, different)
     "Fair" time for major alternative viewpoints

RED LION BROADCASTING v FCC (1969):
  Supreme Court upheld Fairness Doctrine constitutionally
  Rationale: spectrum scarcity
    "Unlike newspaper publishers, broadcasters cannot publish
     competing views through multiple media... The licensee has no
     constitutional right to a license or to monopolize a radio frequency."
  Scarcity rationale: because spectrum is limited, those who use it
                       have obligations ordinary speakers don't have
  This ruling: still cited; still valid law
  BUT: FCC chose to stop enforcing (not ruled unconstitutional)

REAGAN-ERA REPEAL (1987):
  Reagan FCC (Mark Fowler, chair 1981-1987):
  "Television is just another appliance. It's a toaster with pictures."
  Deregulatory philosophy: market determines programming quality
  1987: FCC voted to abolish Fairness Doctrine
  Rationale: (1) No longer necessary; (2) May actually suppress speech
             (stations avoid controversial topics to avoid obligations)
  Congress: passed legislation codifying Doctrine -> Reagan vetoed

IMMEDIATE CONSEQUENCE:
  1988: Rush Limbaugh's radio program begins national syndication
  Before 1987: controversial one-sided political programming risked
               triggering Fairness Doctrine complaints
  After 1987: no obligation
  Talk radio: exploded (AM radio found new life after FM took music)
  Fox News (1996): could take partisan position without regulatory exposure
  Direct line: abolishing Fairness Doctrine -> partisan media landscape

DEBATE:
  Pro-repeal: chilling effect on speech; government shouldn't determine fairness
  Pro-doctrine: concentrated media ownership = few voices; public interest requires balance
  Current: no federal fairness doctrine; some states have informal expectations
           FCC has not shown interest in restoring it (since Reagan era)
```

## Equal Time Rule vs Fairness Doctrine

```
EQUAL TIME RULE (Section 315 of Communications Act)

DIFFERENT FROM FAIRNESS DOCTRINE:
  Equal Time: applies ONLY to political candidates
  Fairness Doctrine: applied to all controversial public issues

EQUAL TIME RULE:
  If station sells or gives time to one legally qualified candidate
  Must offer equal time to all other candidates for same office
  Not just major-party candidates: all legally qualified candidates
  Applies to: sale of time, free appearances

EXEMPTIONS:
  Bona fide news interview (Meet the Press)
  Bona fide news coverage (spontaneous event coverage)
  Bona fide news documentary
  On-the-spot coverage of news events

  These exemptions: why stations can cover candidates without
  having to give equal time to all opponents

IMPLICATIONS:
  Entertainment programs: once included
    "I Love Lucy" episode: Ricky Ricardo endorses candidate
    -> all other candidates could demand equal time
    -> stations avoided ANY political content in entertainment
  Syndicated reruns: candidate appears in old show -> equal time issues
  Ronald Reagan: movie actor, governor -> his old films could trigger rules
    (FCC ruled his older films exempt as "news documentaries" effectively)

STILL IN FORCE: Section 315 remains law
  Each election cycle: stations manage candidate appearances carefully
  Presidential debates: special exemptions (CPD sponsorship = news event)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"The FCC can regulate internet content."** The FCC regulates licensed broadcasters and common carrier networks. Internet content is not subject to FCC content regulation. The indecency rules (prohibiting explicit content outside safe harbor hours 10pm-6am) apply to over-the-air broadcast licensees — not cable, not streaming, not internet. This is why HBO can show nudity and violence that NBC cannot (in theory).

**"The Fairness Doctrine required equal time."** No. Equal Time (Section 315) requires equal time for political candidates. The Fairness Doctrine required fair coverage of controversial public issues with reasonable opportunity for opposing viewpoints — much more vague and harder to enforce. They are distinct rules that are frequently confused.

**"The FCC abolished the Fairness Doctrine because the Supreme Court required it."** The Supreme Court upheld the Fairness Doctrine in Red Lion (1969). The FCC voluntarily chose to stop enforcing it in 1987 based on a policy judgment, not a constitutional requirement. The Supreme Court has not ruled the Fairness Doctrine unconstitutional. Congress could reinstate it; the FCC could reinstate it; neither has shown interest.

**"Spectrum auctions mean the government sold spectrum."** Spectrum auctions sell licenses to use specific spectrum bands for specific purposes and durations — not the spectrum itself. The spectrum remains public property. The auction winner gets a license (exclusive right to transmit on those frequencies in that geography for that period) — not ownership. Licenses can be revoked and can expire.
