# Radio's Golden Age: Network Radio 1920s-1950s, NBC/CBS/ABC, Soap Operas, War of the Worlds 1938

## The Big Picture

Radio's golden age (roughly 1926-1955) was the first mass electronic media era. For the first time in history, a single performer could reach 30 million Americans simultaneously, in their homes, at a scheduled time. This changed politics, advertising, entertainment, and the nature of public experience. The business model invented for radio — national advertiser-funded network broadcasting — remained the template for television and shaped American culture for 70 years.

```
RADIO'S GOLDEN AGE TIMELINE

1920  KDKA Pittsburgh: first commercial broadcasting
      First broadcast: election results, Harding vs Cox
      November 2, 1920

1922  WEAF New York (AT&T): first paid commercial
      "Toll broadcasting": Queensboro Corporation real estate ad
      August 28, 1922: 15 minutes, $50

1925  National Radio Conference: voluntary networking
      Stations linked by telephone wire

1926  NBC FOUNDED (National Broadcasting Company)
      RCA + GE + Westinghouse joint venture
      David Sarnoff: driving force
      Two networks within NBC:
        NBC Red (flagship), NBC Blue (secondary)

1927  CBS FOUNDED (Columbia Broadcasting System)
      William Paley: buys failing network for $400,000 (age 27)
      Paley's model: affiliate stations keep local rights
                     vs NBC: owned-and-operated stations
      CBS grows: affiliate relationships better for expansion

1933-1945: ROOSEVELT'S FIRESIDE CHATS
      FDR: used radio to speak directly to public
      30 addresses, ~60-90M listeners each
      "Direct to citizen" bypassing press filter
      First president to fully exploit radio as medium

1938  WAR OF THE WORLDS (October 30, 1938)
      Orson Welles: Mercury Theatre on the Air
      Caused panic (disputed extent)
      Demonstrated: radio's authority, public suggestibility

1943  NBC BLUE NETWORK FORCED SALE
      FCC: prohibited owning two networks
      NBC Blue -> sold -> becomes ABC (American Broadcasting Company)

1950s TV ARRIVES
      Prime-time radio audiences: decline
      Radio: reinvents as music + news + local (while TV takes drama/comedy)
      FM stereo (1961): drives radio reinvention in 1970s+
```

## The Network Business Model

```
NETWORK RADIO MODEL: HOW IT WORKED

STRUCTURE:
  +------------------+     +------------------+     +--------------+
  |  NATIONAL        |     |  AFFILIATE       |     |  NATIONAL    |
  |  NETWORK         |     |  STATIONS        |     |  ADVERTISERS |
  |  (NBC/CBS)       |     |  (local markets) |     |              |
  |                  |<--->|                  |<----|              |
  | Provides programs|     | Carry programs   |     | Buy time     |
  | via AT&T long    |     | Sell local time  |     | during       |
  | lines            |     | Keep local ads   |     | programs     |
  +------------------+     +------------------+     +--------------+

NETWORK ECONOMICS:
  Network: produces programming, sells national ad time
  Affiliates: clear (transmit) the national feed, get:
    (a) free national programming (saves local production cost)
    (b) small network payments for clearing time
    (c) local ad time during national shows (very valuable)

  Why affiliates loved the deal:
  Without network: must produce 18 hours/day of local programming
  With network: national programming fills schedule; only local segments needed
  National programs: much higher quality and budget than local could afford

ADVERTISER SPONSORSHIP:
  Before networks: spot advertising (buy one announcement)
  Network era: PROGRAM SPONSORSHIP
  Advertiser sponsors entire program:
    "The Jack Benny Program, brought to you by Jell-O"
    Advertiser: owns the show, controls content
    Advertiser: can pull show, change content, fire talent
    "General Motors Theatre" — advertiser is producer

  This created: sponsor-driven content
  Sponsors avoided: controversy, anything that might offend
  Result: very safe, middle-American content
  Change: magazine-format advertising (1950s-60s):
    Networks take editorial control; advertisers just buy time
    Content decisions: separated from advertiser influence (somewhat)

RATING SYSTEM:
  C.E. Hooper (1934): radio ratings via telephone recall
  Ask: "Are you listening to the radio? What program?"
  Hooper rating: % of radio homes listening at time
  A.C. Nielsen (later): coincidental telephone + diary methods
  Ratings: determined: show survival, time slot, advertiser prices
  "Ratings point": 1% of total homes
  Top radio shows: 30-40+ ratings (30-40% of US homes listening)
```

## NBC and CBS: The Network Giants

```
NBC: NATIONAL BROADCASTING COMPANY (1926)

FOUNDING:
  RCA (Radio Corporation of America): patent pool + equipment sales
  David Sarnoff: general manager, later president
  NBC: subsidiary of RCA
  Capitalization: $2M from RCA + GE + Westinghouse
  First broadcast: November 15, 1926 (inaugural gala)

TWO NETWORK STRUCTURE:
  NBC Red: flagship; best time slots; highest-rated shows
  NBC Blue: secondary; sustaining programs; more experimental
  Same company, two programming services
  Affiliates: could take Red, Blue, or both

FCC 1943 RULING:
  "Chain Broadcasting" rules: prohibit owning two networks
  NBC: must divest one
  NBC Blue sold to Edward Noble for $8M (1943)
  Noble: renamed it ABC (American Broadcasting Company)
  ABC: "third network" for years; never caught NBC/CBS audiences
  Until: 1976-1977 season (Monday Night Football + Roots: ABC #1)

DAVID SARNOFF AND PATERNALISM:
  NBC's programming philosophy: uplift + entertainment
  Symphony orchestras, opera, serious drama
  But also: Jack Benny, Bob Hope, Burns & Allen (commercial hits)
  "Public interest" interpretation: high culture available to masses
  NBC Symphony Orchestra: Sarnoff personally recruited Toscanini (1937)
  Toscanini NBC broadcast: 10M+ listeners
  Contradiction: paternalistic cultural programming AND commercial lowest-common-denominator

CBS: COLUMBIA BROADCASTING SYSTEM

WILLIAM PALEY (1901-1990):
  Father: La Palina cigars (advertised on radio station)
  William Paley: 26 years old; buys Columbia Phonograph Broadcasting (1928) for $400K
  Within 1 year: turns around network

PALEY'S INNOVATIONS:
  1. AFFILIATE MODEL:
     CBS kept option to preempt affiliate time for network programs
     In exchange: paid affiliates to carry CBS programs
     NBC: affiliates were owned-and-operated OR paid nothing for clearances
     CBS: affiliates got paid + could sell local time
     Result: affiliates preferred CBS deals; CBS grew faster

  2. TALENT STAR STRATEGY:
     Paley recruited: Jack Benny (from NBC 1948) for $2.6M
     Why: high-bracket tax situation
     Paley structured: sale of corporation (capital gains) rather than income
     Saved Benny millions in taxes
     Result: Paley recruited dozens of NBC stars with same tax deal
     Jack Benny move: called "The Great Raid of 1948" in broadcasting circles

  3. NEWS STRATEGY (later, 1950s):
     Edward R. Murrow: CBS News as prestige brand
     Paley invested in news quality: unusual for commercial network
     CBS News reputation: lasted through Rather, Cronkite
```

## Radio Programming: The Golden Age Genres

```
GOLDEN AGE RADIO GENRES

SOAP OPERAS:
  Named: daytime serials sponsored by soap companies
  Procter & Gamble, Lever Brothers: soap manufacturers
  Target audience: housewives at home during day
  Programming logic: keep housewives listening (and buying soap)
  Content: ongoing serial melodrama, continuing storylines

  Origin: Irna Phillips ("The First Lady of Soap Opera")
  "Painted Dreams" (1930): first radio soap opera
  "The Guiding Light" (1937): radio -> television, ran until 2009 (72 years)
  "As the World Turns", "The Edge of Night": all began on radio

  P&G innovation: owned the shows themselves
  Advertiser-as-producer: P&G controlled content
  "Soap opera production" became P&G core competency
  They moved their shows to TV and kept producing them
  P&G: still produces some programming through subsidiaries

COMEDY VARIETY:
  Jack Benny Program (1932-1955): 21-year run
  Burns & Allen (1932-1950)
  Bob Hope Show (1938-1955)
  Format: weekly, ~30 minutes
  Cast: recurring characters, running jokes
  "Jack Benny's cheapness": 20-year running gag
  These shows: directly transplanted to television (same cast, same character)
  Radio comedy timing: developed conventions still used in sitcoms

DRAMATIC ANTHOLOGY:
  The Mercury Theatre on the Air (Orson Welles, 1938)
  Suspense (1942-1962)
  Lux Radio Theatre (1934-1955): Hollywood stars
  One-hour adaptations of films and plays
  Production design: all sound (no visuals)
  Sound designers: developed entire vocabulary of sound effects
                   that cinema and TV adopted

CRIME/DETECTIVE:
  Sam Spade, Philip Marlowe, The Shadow
  First-person narration (Chandler-esque)
  Sound: footsteps, doors, gunshots, telephone
  These conventions: directly influenced film noir cinematography
  and detective fiction narrative conventions

NEWS AND TALK:
  Walter Winchell: gossip + political commentary
  H.V. Kaltenborn: news analysis
  Boake Carter: controversial right-wing analysis
  Edward R. Murrow (CBS): WWII London broadcasts; changed news conventions
```

## War of the Worlds — Radio's Power Demonstrated

```
THE WAR OF THE WORLDS BROADCAST (October 30, 1938)

CONTEXT:
  Orson Welles: 23 years old; director, Mercury Theatre company
  Program: CBS "Mercury Theatre on the Air" (1938)
  No sponsor: CBS time filling; low-rated Sunday 8pm
  Format: dramatic radio adaptations
  That night: Halloween special — H.G. Wells's novel (1898)

THE BROADCAST:
  Opening: standard announcer credits, orchestra music
  8:00 PM: first 8 minutes = music, interrupted by "bulletins"
  Format: simulated news coverage of Martian invasion
    "Breaking news" format: interrupted dance music
    "Field reporters": coverage from Grover's Mill, NJ
    "Experts": interviewed as invasion progresses
  60 minutes: invasion, defeat of humans, aftermath

WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED:
  Most tuned in late: after 8:08 opener identifying it as drama
  Competing program (Edgar Bergen on NBC): 34x higher ratings
  Audience: relatively small; most heard drama from beginning

  BUT: some panic DID occur:
  People who tuned in after 8:08: missed disclaimer
  New Jersey roads: some fled toward family; reports of prayer
  Estimated: several hundred to a few thousand panicked
  National scale panic: vastly overstated by newspapers

WHY NEWSPAPERS EXAGGERATED THE PANIC:
  Newspaper industry: hostile to radio (competing for ad dollars)
  "Proof" that radio was dangerous, irresponsible: served their interest
  Stories: based mostly on unverified reports, theater
  Historian Jefferson Pooley and Michael Socolow (2013): documented
    that the panic was mostly manufactured by newspapers for competitive reasons

WHAT IT GENUINELY DEMONSTRATED:
  1. Radio = authority: news-format presentation was credible
     People conditioned to trust news broadcasts implicitly
     "Breaking news" format carries immediate credibility
     (Same vulnerability exploited: deepfake audio, 2020s)

  2. Radio = immediacy: theater/film audiences know it's fiction
     Radio news format: blurs fiction/reality
     Lesson: format convention shapes interpretation

  3. Regulatory aftermath:
     FCC: warned broadcasters against simulated news broadcasts
     Networks: prohibited "breaking news" format in drama
     This rule: still technically in force

  4. Historical myth-making:
     War of the Worlds panic: one of most repeated media myths
     Usually presented as: evidence of public credulity and media power
     Reality: smaller, more complex
     But the lesson drawn (media shapes belief) is real, even if
     the specific event was overstated
```

## Radio in World War II

```
RADIO AND WWII

FDR'S FIRESIDE CHATS:
  30 broadcasts 1933-1944
  Direct communication, bypassing press
  "Direct-to-citizen" communication pattern:
    FDR -> radio -> public
    Previously: President -> press release -> newspapers -> public
    FDR: cut out middleman, controlled message directly
  Average audience: 60-90 million (US population 130M)
  Comparable to: a modern president with 500M social media followers
  and nothing between them and the audience

  Content: explained New Deal policies, economic crisis, war progress
  Style: conversational, not oratorical (unlike radio speeches of era)
  Welles quote: FDR "the greatest radio voice in history"
  Impact: sustained public confidence through Depression and war

WAR OF INFORMATION:
  Both sides used radio for propaganda:
  Germany: Axis Sally (Mildred Gillars), Radio Berlin
  Japan: Tokyo Rose (various operators)
  US: Voice of America (1942-): official US government broadcasting
  UK: BBC World Service: began 1932, critical in WWII

  Shortwave: capable of international broadcast (sky wave)
  Government broadcasting: explicitly informational/propaganda
  First Amendment: doesn't apply to government speech (can be one-sided)
  Axis broadcasting: charged with treason after WWII in some cases

EDWARD R. MURROW (London, 1940-1941):
  CBS News: Murrow stood on rooftop during Blitz
  "This... is London"
  Described bombing, civilian experience, sound of war
  Live broadcasts: from bombed city to American living rooms
  Effect: moved US public opinion toward entering war
          (US: officially neutral 1940-1941)
  Changed: what journalism could be (eyewitness, atmospheric)
  Murrow standards: shaped CBS News through 1960s-70s
```

## Radio's Post-TV Reinvention

```
RADIO AFTER TELEVISION (1950s-1960s)

TELEVISION'S ARRIVAL (1948-1955):
  TV: takes radio's audiences in prime time
  Jack Benny: moved from radio to TV (1950)
  Most major radio drama/comedy: moved to TV
  Radio networks: tried to keep drama, failed
  Network radio audiences: collapsed by mid-1950s

RADIO REINVENTION:
  Couldn't compete: prime-time national drama/comedy (TV owns this)
  Could compete:
    Music: radio better than TV for music (no visual needed)
    News: radio portable (in car); TV not portable
    Local: community connection, local ads
    Youth: portable transistor radio (1954 Regency TR-1) -> teenagers
           could listen privately in bedroom or at beach

TRANSISTOR RADIO EFFECT (1954+):
  Transistors: replaced vacuum tubes
  Pocket-sized radios: cheap enough for teenagers
  Rock and roll: became radio's primary content
  DJ (disc jockey): new role — previously banned (musicians union)
  Payola scandal (1959-1960): record labels paying DJs for airplay
    Alan Freed: fired; Dick Clark: survived after testimony
    FCC: made payola illegal without disclosure

TOP 40 FORMAT:
  Todd Storz: developed "Top 40" format (1955)
  Play: 40 most popular songs in heavy rotation
  Short: news, weather, time
  Personality DJs: entertainment value
  This format: dominated AM radio 1955-1975

AM vs FM TRANSITION:
  1961: FCC mandates FM stereo standard
  1966: FCC: FM stations must air original programming
             (can't just simulcast AM)
  Late 1960s: FM "underground radio" — album-oriented, counterculture
              No playlist restrictions; entire album sides played
  1970s: FM overtakes AM for music listening
  AM: music dies; reinvents as talk, news, sports
  FM: music format becomes what AM was in 1955
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"The War of the Worlds broadcast caused mass panic."** This is one of the most persistent myths in media history. The "panic" was localized, small-scale, and substantially manufactured by newspaper coverage seeking to discredit radio. The broadcast had low ratings (most listened to NBC where Edgar Bergen and Charlie McCarthy were), and most who tuned in heard the opening disclaimer. The lesson about media credibility is real; the scale of the event was vastly exaggerated.

**"Network radio died when TV arrived."** Network radio drama and comedy essentially died (the shows moved to TV). But radio as a medium reinvented itself and remained a major industry. The local radio industry, music radio, news radio, and eventually talk radio all flourished after TV arrival. Radio's total advertising revenue actually increased through the 1960s and beyond — just from different genres.

**"Sponsors controlled network programming."** They did in the sponsor-as-producer era (1930s-1940s). The shift to magazine-format advertising (networks sell time; advertisers buy spots) in the late 1950s transferred editorial control to the networks. CBS News was able to run controversial documentaries (Harvest of Shame, Murrow on McCarthy) because the news division was insulated from sponsor pressure by Paley's deliberate policy — not because sponsors didn't try to object.

**"The Big Three radio networks are the same as the Big Three TV networks."** They are the same companies but with different histories. NBC and CBS moved successfully from radio to TV. ABC was the "third network" in radio (weak) and TV (weak) until the late 1970s. Mutual Broadcasting (fourth radio network, strong) never made it to TV at all. The radio network hierarchy didn't perfectly predict the TV network hierarchy.
