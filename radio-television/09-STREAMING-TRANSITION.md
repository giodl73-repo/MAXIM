# The Streaming Transition: Netflix Pivot 2007-2013, Streaming Wars, Cord Cutting, SVOD/AVOD/FAST Models

## The Big Picture

The streaming transition is the largest disruption in media distribution since the invention of television. It separates content from the broadcast schedule — from the specific time and frequency that defined radio and TV for 80 years — and replaces it with on-demand access to a catalog. The business model consequences of this separation are profound and ongoing. The streaming transition is not complete; the industry has not found a stable equilibrium.

```
STREAMING TRANSITION: PHASE MAP

PHASE 1: DVD + LIMITED STREAMING (1997-2010)
  Netflix: mail DVD (1997); streaming add-on (2007)
  Amazon: no video yet
  Hulu: launch 2008 (NBC/Fox joint venture, free with ads)
  iTunes: movie downloads (2006, buy not stream)
  Piracy: BitTorrent dominant for free content
  Model: supplement cable, not replace

PHASE 2: STREAMING BECOMES PRIMARY (2011-2018)
  Netflix: password sharing + cheap + lots of content
  Netflix original content: 2013 (House of Cards)
  Amazon Prime Video: 2011
  Cord cutting: begins in earnest (2013+)
  Cable: defensive (TV Everywhere - stream on device with cable sub)
  Mobile video: smartphones enabling anywhere viewing

PHASE 3: STREAMING WARS (2019-2022)
  Disney+: November 2019 ($6.99; 10M subscribers day 1)
  Apple TV+: November 2019
  HBO Max: May 2020 (later renamed Max)
  Peacock (NBC): July 2020
  Paramount+: March 2021
  Discovery+: 2021
  Total services: consumer facing ~40+ streaming options
  Subscriber fatigue: begins

PHASE 4: CONSOLIDATION + PROFITABILITY PRESSURE (2023+)
  Password sharing crackdown: Netflix (2023) -> subscriber surge
  Advertising tiers: Netflix adds ads (2022); Disney+ (2022)
  Bundling resumes: Disney Bundle (D+/Hulu/ESPN+); Comcast/Universal
  Mergers: Discovery + HBO Max = Max; Paramount+/Showtime
  Price increases: all services raise prices
  Subscriber churn: consumers rotate between services
  Debt: Netflix $14B+; Disney streaming losses ~$4B over pandemic era
```

## Netflix: The Pivot Company

```
NETFLIX HISTORY: THREE DISTINCT BUSINESS MODELS

MODEL 1: DVD MAIL (1997-2007)
  Reed Hastings + Marc Randolph: founded 1997
  Concept: DVD rental by mail vs Blockbuster's physical stores
  Innovation: no late fees (Blockbuster's primary irritant)
  Pricing: unlimited rentals for flat monthly fee (subscription)
  Operations: warehouses in each major city; overnight mail delivery
  Algorithms: Cinematch recommendation engine from early days
  Peak: 10M+ DVD subscribers by 2009

  BLOCKBUSTER RESPONSE: late and inadequate
  Blockbuster Online (2004): similar service, launched late
  Blockbuster in-store return: allow exchange at stores (brief advantage)
  Then: Blockbuster CEO ousted; service pulled back; company bankrupt (2010)
  Classic innovator's dilemma: incumbents can't cannibalize their core business

MODEL 2: STREAMING ADDITION (2007-2012)
  January 2007: Netflix adds streaming as free add-on to DVD subscribers
  Initial: 1,000 titles (limited rights)
  Technology: Flash video (early), then Silverlight, then HTML5
  Business case: delivery cost for DVD ($0.78/disk) vs streaming (~$0.01)
  Strategy: eventually transition DVD customers to streaming
  International expansion: Canada 2010, Latin America 2011, UK 2012

  TURNING POINT (2011):
  Netflix: split DVD and streaming into separate services ($7.99 each)
           Combined had been $9.99
  Result: instant backlash; 800,000 subscribers lost in one quarter
  Netflix stock: dropped 75% in 3 months
  "Qwikster" plan: rebrand DVD service (abandoned immediately)
  Lesson: transition too abrupt; consumers want bundle

MODEL 3: STREAMING ORIGINAL CONTENT (2013-present)
  February 1, 2013: House of Cards Season 1, all episodes at once
  Innovation 1: entire season at once (binge watching enabled/encouraged)
  Innovation 2: $100M commitment to 2 seasons before any aired
  Risk justification: Netflix had viewing data from 30M subscribers
    - Which content they watch
    - When they rewatch
    - What they watch after
    - Director/star performance correlation
  Data showed: Kevin Spacey + David Fincher + political drama = high predicted value

  BINGE MODEL:
  Weekly episode release: broadcast model; makes shared cultural event
  All-at-once release: Netflix model; maximizes subscriber capture
  Discussion: weekly = sustained weeks of social conversation
              all-at-once = intense 3-day burst then disappears
  Netflix chose: all-at-once (subscriber retention metric)
  Competition: some streamers return to weekly (HBO, Disney+)
  The choice reflects: subscriber LTV optimization vs cultural moment optimization
```

## Business Model Taxonomy

```
SVOD / AVOD / FAST: THE STREAMING MODEL MATRIX

SVOD (Subscription Video On Demand):
  Definition: monthly fee; no ads; unlimited access to catalog
  Examples: Netflix, Disney+, HBO Max, Amazon Prime Video (core)
  Revenue: subscription only
  Economics: CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) vs LTV (Lifetime Value)
  Churn: key metric; must keep subscribers or lose all revenue
  Content spend: high (must keep subscribers from canceling)
  Ad-free: no advertiser concerns about adjacency
  Advantages: predictable revenue; no advertiser dependency
  Disadvantages: subscriber acquisition cost high; ceiling on subscribers

  NETFLIX NUMBERS (2024):
    Subscribers: ~280M globally
    ARPU (Average Revenue Per User): ~$13/month (blended global)
    Content spend: ~$17B/year
    Profit: ~$5-7B net income
    Market cap: ~$250-300B

AVOD (Advertising-Supported Video On Demand):
  Definition: free (or cheap) access; ad-supported
  Examples: YouTube (free tier), Hulu (with ads), Peacock (free tier),
            Pluto TV, Tubi, Paramount+/Showtime with ads
  Revenue: advertising (CPM × views)
  CPM: cost per thousand impressions; streaming AVOD: $15-40 CPM
  vs broadcast TV: $25-50 CPM; vs Netflix ad tier: $30-65 CPM
  Targeting: streaming >> broadcast (individual-level data)
  Advantages: larger addressable audience (no subscription barrier)
  Disadvantages: need large scale for advertiser interest; complex ops
  Content spend: lower (because revenue limited by CPM × scale)

FAST (Free Ad-Supported Streaming TV):
  Definition: free; ad-supported; LINEAR SCHEDULE (like broadcast TV)
  Not: on-demand from catalog; you watch what's scheduled
  Examples: Pluto TV, Tubi, Samsung TV Plus, Peacock (some channels),
            LG Channels, Xumo
  Revenue: advertising
  Content cost: LOW (mostly licensed old content; not original)
  Tech: simulates TV channel (scheduled programming, no choose-your-own)
  Demographics: older viewers; secondary screens
  Growth: fastest-growing streaming segment 2021-2024

HYBRID (SVOD + AVOD):
  Netflix ad-supported tier: $6.99/month (vs $15.49 ad-free) (2022)
  Disney+ Basic (ads): $7.99/month (vs $13.99 ad-free)
  These tiers: introduced when subscriber growth slowed
  Economics: ad revenue + subscription = higher ARPU than either alone
             BUT: subscriber must accept ads = some won't pay premium

BUSINESS MODEL MAPPING TO SaaS:
  SVOD = pure subscription SaaS (Office 365 model)
  AVOD = freemium SaaS (Slack free tier with ads)
  FAST = free tool with advertising (search engine)
  Hybrid = tiered SaaS (Base/Pro/Enterprise with feature gates)
  Same economics; different content; same subscriber metrics
```

## Cord Cutting

```
CORD CUTTING: THE DATA

DEFINITION:
  "Cord cutting": cancel cable/satellite TV subscription
  "Cord never": never subscribed; streaming-only household
  "Cord shaving": reduce cable subscription tier (keep basic)

CABLE/SATELLITE SUBSCRIBER DATA:
  2011: 100.8M US pay TV households (peak)
  2015: 100.6M (plateau; decline beginning)
  2017: 96.0M
  2019: 88.7M
  2021: 78.6M
  2023: ~68-70M (estimated)
  Annual loss rate: 4-6M subscribers/year

  PAYING FOR CABLE: ~$100-200/month (package with internet)
  Cord cutting savings: ~$100/month
  Replacement cost: 3-5 streaming services at $10-15 = $30-75/month
  Net savings: ~$25-70/month
  But: bundle discount disappears (internet-only = often same price as bundle)

ECONOMICS OF CORD CUTTING (FOR CABLE COMPANIES):
  Lost: video subscription revenue (~$40/month margin)
  Retained: internet subscription revenue (~$35/month margin)
  Net: cable companies losing video revenue but retaining internet revenue
  Comcast response: internet subscribers growing while video subscribers fall
  Cable company = internet provider first; TV provider second (2024)
  "Triple play" (phone + cable + internet): dead; "double play" dying

TV EVERYWHERE (INDUSTRY DEFENSE):
  2010: Comcast, NBC launched TV Everywhere
  Concept: cable subscription includes online streaming (computer, tablet)
  Password = login to streaming apps (Hulu, HBO, etc.) with cable credentials
  Theory: cable subscription = all-access to streaming too
  Reality: subscribers still cancelled when better content went to streaming-only
  By 2019: TV Everywhere effectively failed to stop cord cutting

WHAT CABLE COMPANIES ARE NOW:
  Comcast: internet service provider (primary revenue) + Peacock (streaming)
  Charter: internet service provider + streaming
  Cox: internet service provider
  The "cable TV" business is dying; the "internet pipe" business is stable
```

## Algorithmic Programming

```
ALGORITHMIC PROGRAMMING vs SCHEDULED PROGRAMMING

SCHEDULED PROGRAMMING:
  Network/cable decides: you watch show X at 8 PM Tuesday
  All viewers: same show at same time
  Cultural effect: shared experience ("Did you see...last night?")
  Discovery: network marketing, press, word of mouth
  Algorithm: none; editorial decision-making

ALGORITHMIC PROGRAMMING (Netflix et al.):
  Netflix decides: for user A, show X first; for user B, show Y first
  Each user: different home page, different recommendations
  A/B testing: Netflix runs thousands of simultaneous tests
              Different thumbnails, different row order, different sequences
  Cold start: new user with no history -> demographics-based guess
  Established user: collaborative filtering (like you -> like this)

NETFLIX THUMBNAIL A/B TESTING:
  Same show: different thumbnail for different user segments
  A: action-oriented thumbnail for action fans
  B: romance-focused thumbnail for drama fans
  Same content; different entry point; higher click-through rate
  This is: same as landing page optimization in software products

ENGAGEMENT METRICS:
  Netflix primary metric: did subscriber watch enough to NOT cancel?
  Second metric: "completion rate" (started vs finished)
  Hours watched: proxy for satisfaction/retention
  Controversy: Netflix initially counted "started watching 2 minutes"
               Changed to: "completed 70% of an episode"
  Third-party verification: Netflix does NOT provide Nielsen-style ratings
                           Reluctantly releases some data (Top 10 lists)

FILTER BUBBLE IN TV:
  Algorithmic recommendation: shows you more of what you've watched
  Effect: reinforces existing taste; reduces discovery of unfamiliar
  Counter-argument: human editors at networks also limited to familiar genres
  But: personalization scale >> any editorial bias
  Netflix "Row 5" problem: best discovery content often buried

DARK PATTERNS IN STREAMING:
  Auto-play: next episode starts immediately (reduces decision point to stop)
  Post-credit scene: reward for staying through credits
  Cliffhanger episode endings: increase series completion rate
  "Limited series" labeling: creates completion compulsion
  All optimized for: engagement (hours watched) not necessarily enjoyment
```

## Streaming Economics and the Race to Profitability

```
STREAMING ECONOMICS: THE HARD NUMBERS

CONTENT SPEND (2023 estimates):
  Netflix: $17B
  Disney (all properties): $33B
  Warner Bros Discovery: $14B
  Amazon Prime Video: $9B
  Apple TV+: $7B (estimate; not disclosed)
  Paramount+/Peacock combined: $8B
  Total industry: ~$110-120B/year

  Compare: entire US film theatrical spend ~$10B production
  Streaming spends 10x more on content than theatrical
  (Streaming includes sports rights, news, etc. — not just drama/comedy)

SUBSCRIBER ACQUISITION COST (SAC):
  Netflix average SAC: ~$30-40 (cost to acquire one subscriber)
  Calculated as: marketing + promotion / new subscribers
  LTV must exceed SAC for business to work
  LTV = ARPU × average months × margin
  Netflix ARPU ~$13 × 24 months × 20% margin = ~$62 LTV
  Marginal: barely works; requires scale and low churn

PROFITABILITY STATUS (2024):
  Netflix: profitable, ~20% operating margin
  Disney streaming: became profitable Q4 2024 (after $4B+ losses)
  Peacock (NBC): still losing ~$2B/year
  Paramount+: losing money; merger discussions
  Amazon Prime Video: profitable (hidden in Prime bundle)
  Apple TV+: not disclosed; likely losing money (small)

  Pattern: only scaled platforms (Netflix, Disney, Amazon) profitable
           Smaller streamers: structurally difficult
           Path to profitability: more subscribers + higher prices + fewer shows

THE CONTENT SPENDING PROBLEM:
  Netflix: must keep content fresh to prevent churn
  Cancel rate after watching all desired content: high
  Solution: constant new content (high spend)
  But: viewers can't keep up with volume
  "Content paradox": too much to watch; hard to discover; still cancel

  Netflix viewership data (released 2023):
  Wednesday (2022): 150M household views in first 4 weeks
  Most shows: under 10M total views ever
  Power law distribution: few hits, many expensive misses
  Long tail: streaming has it, but most long-tail content viewed by very few

FUTURE STRUCTURE:
  Predictions (analyst consensus 2024):
  2-3 global scale streamers survive: Netflix + Disney+ + Amazon
  Mid-tier: merge or license content to winners
  FAST: grows as supplemental free option
  Cable: shrinks to 40-50M subscribers (hard sports/news fans)
  Theatrical: recovers partially (~$35B global by 2025?)
  No clear equilibrium yet: industry in active restructuring
```

## International Streaming Markets

```
INTERNATIONAL STREAMING: MARKET STRUCTURE

US: Netflix + Disney+ + Max + Peacock + Paramount+ + Amazon Prime
    Local: smaller local streaming services (FuboTV, etc.)
    Language: English dominant

EUROPE:
  Netflix + Amazon + Disney+ dominant (global)
  Public broadcaster streaming: BBC iPlayer, Arte, ARD (free, ad-free)
  Local subscription services: Sky/Now (UK), Canal+ (France), etc.
  Regulation: EU content quotas (30% of catalog must be European content)
  AVMSD (Audiovisual Media Services Directive): EU requires
    local content investment proportional to subscriber revenue
  Result: Netflix spending €1B+/year in Europe on local content

INDIA (largest streaming market by subscribers):
  JioCinema: free (Reliance Industries, effectively subsidized)
  Disney+ Hotstar: was dominant; JioCinema took cricket rights (2023)
  Amazon Prime Video: significant
  Netflix: relatively small (expensive for Indian market)
  Languages: 22 official languages; Hindi + regional
  Piracy: extremely high
  Price sensitivity: very high (Netflix India: $2-3/month)

CHINA:
  Netflix: not present (blocked)
  iQiyi (Baidu), Youku (Alibaba), Tencent Video: dominant
  Content: subject to CCP censorship; no Western content effectively
  Subscriber count: hundreds of millions (low ARPU)
  Technology: similar to Western SVOD/AVOD models

LANGUAGE AS MOAT:
  Netflix's Korean content strategy:
  Squid Game (2021): 142M households in 4 weeks
  First non-English title to reach #1 in US
  Result: massive expansion of Korean content investment
  Japanese anime: Crunchyroll (Sony subsidiary) specialized
  Spanish content: Netflix largest Spanish-language content producer

REGULATORY CHALLENGE:
  Each country: different content regulations, tax rules, local production requirements
  Australia: 20% local content requirement for streamers (proposed)
  Canada: C-11 (Online Streaming Act): streamers must fund Canadian content
  France: strict cultural protection laws
  Netflix: employs local production teams in 50+ countries
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Streaming companies are very profitable."** Netflix is profitable. Most other streaming services are not, or barely. Disney streaming became profitable only in late 2024 after years of losses. Peacock, Paramount+, and others are still losing money at scale. The "streaming" category includes both the profitable pioneer (Netflix) and many money-losing attempts to replicate its model. The industry's economics remain uncertain.

**"More content = better streaming service."** Content volume creates diminishing returns. Netflix releases ~100+ original series/films per month. Research on viewer behavior shows that very high volume creates "choice paralysis" and doesn't significantly improve retention vs. a well-curated smaller catalog of highly desired content. HBO's historical model (fewer, better shows) generated more subscriber loyalty per title than Netflix's volume approach.

**"Cord cutting replaces cable TV dollar-for-dollar."** The math rarely works out simply. Multiple streaming services + internet-only plan (often priced same as triple-play bundle) + sports app + news service can approach or exceed the cable bill that was being replaced. The real cord-cutting savings depend on how many services you subscribe to and whether you can live without live sports and local news (the remaining strongholds of cable value).

**"Netflix's recommendation algorithm is neutral."** Netflix's algorithm is optimized for a business outcome (subscriber retention / hours watched). It promotes content that the algorithm predicts will keep you watching, which is not the same as content that is most artistically valuable or that broadens your tastes. The algorithm is an engagement optimizer, not a cultural curator. Netflix's UI choices (autoplay, thumbnail testing) are explicitly designed to reduce the decision to stop watching.
