# Cable and Satellite: CATV Origins, CNN 1980, HBO Premium Model, DBS, Channel Proliferation

## The Big Picture

Cable and satellite television broke the three-network oligopoly by creating a new distribution architecture that bypassed spectrum scarcity entirely. The economics are completely different: cable is subscription-based with no spectrum constraint, so the model could support dozens then hundreds of channels. This single change — from 3 channels to 500 — transformed television from a mass medium into a niche medium, enabling everything from 24-hour news to premium content to today's streaming wars.

```
CABLE AND SATELLITE DISRUPTION PATTERN

BEFORE CABLE (1948-1972):
  3 networks + local stations = everything
  FCC spectrum scarcity: real constraint
  Business model: advertising-only
  Content: mass appeal (must not offend anyone = often bland)
  100% household reach potential: but audience splits 3 ways

CABLE ENTERS (1972-1985):
  New architecture: wired (not spectrum)
  No FCC spectrum constraint: unlimited channels (in theory)
  Business model: subscription + advertising
  Content: niche possible (small audience can support with sub fees)
  Penetration: gradual (requires wire installation to each home)

SATELLITE ENTERS (1975-1985):
  Distribution: single satellite serves entire country
  No need for coaxial cable to every home
  DBS (Direct Broadcast Satellite, 1994): dish on home -> direct
  Enabled: national niche networks without cable franchise

EFFECTS ON NETWORK TV:
  1975: Networks combined = 93% primetime share
  1980: 87%
  1985: 77%
  1990: 62%
  1995: 53%
  2000: 44%
  2010: 32%
  2020: <20% (traditional broadcast declining)
```

## CATV Origins — Community Antenna Television

```
CATV (COMMUNITY ANTENNA TELEVISION) ORIGINS

CONTEXT (1948-1952):
  TV: being received in cities near TV towers
  Problem: rural/suburban areas, hills, and valleys blocked signal
  TV set owners: couldn't receive any signal
  Economic problem: buy TV -> can't receive signal = useless

JOHN WALSON, SERVICE ELECTRIC (1948):
  Mahonoy City, Pennsylvania (coal mining region, mountains)
  Walson: appliance dealer selling TV sets nobody could receive
  Solution: erected tall antenna on mountain above the town
           strung cable from mountain to homes
  Each home: connected to cable -> received clear signal
  Charged: $100 installation + $2/month
  This is: first cable TV system

SIMULTANEOUS INVENTION: L.E. PARSONS (1948):
  Astoria, Oregon
  Same concept independently; received first cable system patent (disputed)

EARLY CATV FUNCTION:
  NOT: adding new channels
  JUST: retransmitting existing broadcast channels more clearly
  Community Antenna: one good antenna for whole community
  Economics: pay cable company once vs each home needing antenna

FIRST FCC RULING:
  FCC (1959): no jurisdiction over cable TV
  Rationale: not using spectrum (it's wires)
  Result: unregulated growth

REGULATORY BATTLE:
  Broadcasters: threatened by cable (why buy TV ads if people go to cable?)
  Broadcasters lobbied FCC to restrict cable
  FCC reversed: 1966 and 1972 cable regulations
  Required: cable must carry local broadcast stations (must-carry)
  Restricted: cable systems from importing distant signals that would
              duplicate local network affiliates
  Purpose: protect broadcast (and FCC's licensing power) from cable disruption
```

## HBO and the Premium Model

```
HBO: HOME BOX OFFICE (1972)

FOUNDATION:
  Time Inc. investment: Gerald Levin (later Time Warner CEO)
  First cable-only programming service
  November 8, 1972: first program on cable (Pennsylvania cable system)
  First program: a hockey game (New York Rangers vs Vancouver Canucks) + film

EARLY MODEL:
  Premium channel: pay extra beyond basic cable
  No commercials: subscription fee pays entirely
  Content: uncut movies and boxing
  Movies: unedited (no network censorship)
  Programming difference: uncut films; no commercial interruptions

SATELLITE DISTRIBUTION (1975): THE TURNING POINT
  HBO: first cable network to use satellite for national distribution
  September 30, 1975: "Thrilla in Manila" (Ali vs Frazier boxing match)
  Uplinked from New York; downlinked to cable headends nationwide
  Before: cable networks required expensive terrestrial microwave links
          (only worked where cable systems near each other)
  After: one satellite transponder = reach every cable system in country

  This is: the distribution platform insight
  One signal -> national reach -> makes national niche cable viable
  Without satellite: cable = local; with satellite = national

HBO GROWTH:
  1975: 287,000 subscribers
  1977: 1M subscribers
  1980: 8M subscribers
  1985: 14M+ subscribers
  Premium pricing: $10-12/month above cable basic (1980 = ~$30 in 2024$)

HBO IMPACT ON CONTENT:
  1980s: original films (not just acquired movies)
  1992: The Larry Sanders Show (first HBO original drama)
  1997: Oz (first HBO drama)
  1999: The Sopranos (transformed expectations for TV drama quality)
  2000s: The Wire, Deadwood, Curb Your Enthusiasm, Six Feet Under
  HBO became: "It's not TV. It's HBO" — brand = quality differentiation
  Network TV: compared unfavorably to HBO original content

HBO MODEL vs BROADCAST:
  Broadcast: free, advertiser-funded, must not offend, mass appeal
  HBO: subscription, no ads, can show anything, niche appeal possible
  Incentive structures: completely different
  HBO: best content -> subscribers stay; bad content -> they cancel
  Broadcast: most watched content -> high ratings -> ad revenue
  These different incentives: explain why HBO made The Sopranos
                              and broadcast made... Touched by an Angel
```

## Ted Turner and CNN

```
TED TURNER AND 24-HOUR NEWS

TED TURNER (1938-):
  Owns: WTBS Atlanta (UHF station, "superstation")
  WTBS: first cable superstation (satellite distribution to cable systems)
  1976: Turner took WTBS national via satellite (RCA Satcom 1)
  WTBS content: old movies, sports, syndicated reruns
  Broadcast networks: furious (Turner duplicating their programs via cable)

CNN (CABLE NEWS NETWORK), JUNE 1, 1980:
  Turner: "We won't be signing off until the world ends"
  First story: attempted assassination of civil rights leader Vernon Jordan
  First 24-hour news channel in world

  WHY IT WORKED:
  Previous news: 30 minutes/night (network evening news)
  CNN: any story, any time, continuous
  Breaking news: advantage over networks' scheduled broadcasts
  Gulf War (1991): CNN's moment
    Bernard Shaw, Peter Arnett: live from Baghdad as bombs fell
    Networks had no one there; CNN had prepared for this
    "CNN Effect": real-time war coverage changed diplomacy
    (Politicians could watch CNN to know what was happening same time
     as military briefings -- sometimes faster)

  CNN FOUNDING ECONOMICS:
  Budget: $30M first year
  Networks: called it "Chicken Noodle News"
  Network evening news: 50M+ viewers; CNN first year: 1-2M
  Turner: "I'm not gonna make any money" (first year)
  But: 24 hours of content at low cost + subscriber fees + ads

CNN COMPETITORS:
  Headline News (CNN 2): 1982 (Turner's second channel)
  MSNBC: 1996 (Microsoft + NBC joint venture)
  Fox News: 1996 (Rupert Murdoch, Roger Ailes)

  FOX NEWS LAUNCH CONTEXT:
  Rupert Murdoch: already owned: NY Post, WSJ, Fox Network, 20th Century Fox
  Thesis: CNN and MSNBC have liberal bias; huge conservative audience unserved
  Roger Ailes: Nixon political consultant; understood conservative audience
  Fox News: launched October 7, 1996
  Growth: 2001 overtook CNN in ratings; dominant cable news by 2002
  Business insight: partisan programming = loyal audience that doesn't leave
                    Compare: "engaged users" metric in social media
```

## Satellite Broadcasting

```
SATELLITE BROADCASTING TECHNOLOGY

COMMUNICATIONS SATELLITES:
  Arthur C. Clarke: predicted geostationary orbit communications (1945)
  Clarke orbit: 35,786 km altitude; period = Earth's rotation
  Satellite appears stationary from Earth
  One satellite: can see 1/3 of Earth's surface
  Three satellites: global coverage (except poles)

EARLY SATELLITE TV (TVRO): 1975-1990
  Early dish: 3-5 meters diameter (backyard dish)
  C-band: 3.7-4.2 GHz downlink
  Consumer: ranged farmers and early adopters who wanted more channels
  Networks' scrambling (1986): HBO, ESPN scrambled signals
    -> required: subscription + decoder box ($400-600)
    Catalyzed: paid satellite services

  C-band dishes: common in rural areas (still some in use)

DIRECT BROADCAST SATELLITE (DBS): 1994
  Ku-band: 11.7-12.7 GHz (higher frequency = smaller dish)
  Small dish: 18-inch (DirecTV oval dish) vs 3-meter TVRO dish
  High-powered satellite: 120 watts per transponder vs 8 watts (C-band)
  Result: small inexpensive dish usable everywhere

DirecTV (1994):
  GM/Hughes ownership
  Compressed digital signals: 150+ channels on 5 satellites
  Digital compression: MPEG-2 (first consumer DTV product)
  1994: 350,000 subscribers (first 6 months)
  2001: 11M subscribers (passed cable's largest operator, TCI/AT&T)
  Currently (2024): ~13M subscribers (at&t sold to Apollo 2021)

DISH Network (1996):
  Second DBS provider
  Different orbital slot, different format from DirecTV
  Price competition: drove down DirecTV costs

SATELLITE vs CABLE:
  SATELLITE advantages: available anywhere (rural areas; no cable wired)
                        more channels (no cable plant upgrade needed)
                        HD earlier (digital from day 1)
  SATELLITE disadvantages: one-way (no return path for internet until 2023 LEO)
                           weather sensitivity (heavy rain/snow)
                           no local channel carriage initially (resolved)
                           requires dish (apartment difficulties)

  CABLE advantages: two-way (internet + TV same wire)
                   no weather sensitivity
                   local access channels
  CABLE disadvantages: requires expensive infrastructure per street
                       franchise area monopoly
```

## Channel Proliferation and the Long Tail

```
CABLE CHANNEL PROLIFERATION (1979-2000)

KEY ENABLER: satellite distribution
  Before satellite: national cable channel requires expensive microwave
  After satellite: any channel can be national for ~$1M/year satellite time

CHANNELS LAUNCH:
  1977: C-SPAN (Congressional coverage, non-commercial)
  1979: ESPN (Entertainment Sports Programming Network)
        Originally: minor sports + slow-pitch softball
        ABC buys 15% stake (1984) -> major sports deals
  1980: CNN
  1981: MTV (24-hour music video)
        First video: "Video Killed the Radio Star" (Buggles)
  1982: Headline News (CNN 2), The Weather Channel
  1983: Discovery Channel, Lifetime, Bravo
  1985: A&E, Sci-Fi Channel
  1992: Cartoon Network, Comedy Central, Court TV
  1993: History Channel, Nickelodeon (full relaunch)
  1994: FX, TV Land
  1996: MSNBC, Fox News, Animal Planet, HGTV, Food Network
  2000+: Hundreds of channels, including digital-only tiers

ECONOMICS OF CABLE CHANNELS:
  Revenue model: subscriber fees + advertising
  Subscriber fees: cable operator pays per-subscriber fee to carry channel
    ESPN (2024): ~$10/subscriber/month (highest in industry)
    CNN: ~$0.70/subscriber/month
    Small channel: $0.05-0.20/subscriber/month
  Advertising: rates based on ratings + demographic match
  Bundle: cable operator packages all channels; subscriber pays one price

  MUST-CARRY vs RETRANSMISSION CONSENT:
  Broadcast stations: can require cable to carry them (must-carry)
  OR: negotiate retransmission consent fee (cable pays to carry)
  CBS (2019): charged ~$2/subscriber/month retransmission consent
  Broadcast networks: now effectively charging same as cable channels
  Total carriage payments: broadcasters received ~$12B/year (2022)

MTV EFFECT:
  Music videos: free promotion for record labels (labels provided them free)
  MTV: played them, didn't pay for content
  Business model: labels paid for MTV to exist (inverse of normal content cost)
  Labels: realized MTV drove record sales; invested heavily in videos
  Michael Jackson "Thriller" (1983): 14-minute video = major production
  MTV made: visual element essential for pop music success
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Cable TV was invented to offer more content."** Originally cable (CATV) was invented to solve a reception problem — poor over-the-air signal in mountains and rural areas. It was retransmitting existing broadcast channels more clearly, not adding new ones. The "more content" aspect only developed after satellite distribution made national cable programming economically viable (1975+).

**"CNN was immediately successful."** CNN had very low ratings for years after launch. The networks dismissed it. It became culturally important when breaking news events demonstrated its value — particularly the Gulf War (1991, 11 years after launch) which gave CNN its credibility moment. Commercial success and cultural importance are separate timelines.

**"Cable TV broke network monopoly through competition."** Cable supplemented but didn't directly compete with broadcast networks — it was a complementary distribution layer. What cable did was fragment the audience into niches, so the 93% primetime share of broadcast networks declined as viewers found their specific interests better served by dedicated channels (sports on ESPN, news on CNN, comedy on Comedy Central). Direct competition came later with streaming.

**"Fox News is a broadcast network like Fox."** Fox Broadcasting Company (Fox Network, 1986) is a broadcast television network with over-the-air affiliates, like NBC/CBS/ABC. Fox News Channel (1996) is a cable channel only available through cable/satellite/streaming subscriptions. Same corporate parent (News Corp/Fox Corporation, Rupert Murdoch), completely different businesses and regulatory frameworks. The broadcast Fox Network is licensed by FCC; Fox News cable channel is not.
