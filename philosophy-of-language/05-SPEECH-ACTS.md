# Speech Act Theory: Austin and Searle

## The Problem Speech Act Theory Solves

Classical philosophy of language focused on declarative sentences and their truth conditions. But most language does not describe — it commands, promises, warns, greets, marries, declares war, fires employees. Austin observed that a theory focused on description misses the majority of what language actually does.

Speech act theory answers: what are speakers DOING when they speak? The emphasis shifts from semantic content to pragmatic action.

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Austin: Performatives and Constatives

J.L. Austin (1911–1960), Oxford philosopher. His William James Lectures at Harvard (1955) were published posthumously as "How to Do Things with Words" (1962).

### The Initial Distinction

```
  CONSTATIVE UTTERANCES:
  Describe, report, state. True or false.
  "The cat is on the mat."
  "Water boils at 100°C."
  "The meeting starts at 9."
  These can be evaluated as TRUE or FALSE.

  PERFORMATIVE UTTERANCES:
  DO something in being said. NOT true or false.
  "I promise to meet you at noon."
  "I hereby declare you husband and wife."
  "You're fired."
  "I sentence you to five years."
  "I name this ship the HMS Victory."

  THE PARADOX:
  Performatives are not TRUE or FALSE.
  They are HAPPY (succeed) or UNHAPPY (fail/misfire).
  Saying "I promise to pay you" doesn't describe a promise —
  it IS the promise (the act of promising).
```

### Explicit vs Primary Performatives

```
  EXPLICIT PERFORMATIVE:
  Contains a first-person performative verb:
  "I promise...", "I warn...", "I order...", "I apologize..."
  The illocutionary force is stated explicitly.

  PRIMARY (IMPLICIT) PERFORMATIVE:
  The force is implicit:
  "I will be there" = I promise I will be there
  "There's a bull in that field!" = I warn you
  "Coffee?" = I offer you coffee
  Implicit performatives are far more common.
```

## Austin's Three Acts

Austin's mature framework replaces the constative/performative distinction with a three-way analysis of every utterance.

```
  EVERY UTTERANCE HAS (potentially) THREE ACTS:

  +---------------------------------------------------------+
  |  LOCUTIONARY ACT                                        |
  |  The act of saying something with definite sense and    |
  |  reference.                                             |
  |  = The semantic content, independent of context.        |
  |  "Shoot her" said by a director to an actor during      |
  |  filming = sensible utterance with reference to         |
  |  a female and a gun.                                    |
  +---------------------------------------------------------+
           |
           v
  +---------------------------------------------------------+
  |  ILLOCUTIONARY ACT                                      |
  |  What the speaker is DOING in making the utterance.     |
  |  The speech act itself: ordering, advising, warning,    |
  |  promising, permitting, prohibiting, asking, naming...  |
  |  Governed by CONVENTIONS and CONTEXTS.                  |
  |  "Shoot her" = an order (in this context)               |
  |  The ILLOCUTIONARY FORCE of the utterance.              |
  +---------------------------------------------------------+
           |
           v
  +---------------------------------------------------------+
  |  PERLOCUTIONARY ACT                                     |
  |  The EFFECTS the utterance has on the listener.         |
  |  Causing belief, persuading, deterring, embarrassing,   |
  |  amusing, frightening, consoling.                       |
  |  "Shoot her" → the actor shoots the target (effect)     |
  |  The speaker may or may not INTEND the perlocutionary   |
  |  effect; it is causal, not constitutive.                |
  +---------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Felicity Conditions

For a performative to succeed, certain conditions must hold. Austin calls violations "infelicities."

### The Three Groups

```
  A CONDITIONS (Procedure):
  A.1 A conventional procedure must exist for the act.
  A.2 The circumstances and persons must be correct.

  Example: "I hereby divorce you" — no such conventional
  procedure exists in most Western legal systems.
  → MISFIRE: the act has no effect. The divorce doesn't happen.

  B CONDITIONS (Execution):
  B.1 The procedure must be executed correctly.
  B.2 The procedure must be executed completely.

  Example: A marriage ceremony where the officiant says "Do you..."
  and the groom says nothing.
  → MISFIRE: incomplete execution, no marriage.

  Γ CONDITIONS (Sincerity):
  Γ.1 The persons must have the required thoughts/feelings.
  Γ.2 They must act accordingly afterward.

  Example: "I promise to come" said without intending to come.
  → ABUSE: the act is performed but hollow/insincere.
  The promise exists (conventions satisfied), but it is ABUSED.
  Contrast with misfires: the marriage DOES happen (legally)
  even if one party is insincere. The sincerity is breached,
  not the legal effect.

+----------------------------------------------------------------+
| INFELICITY TYPE | WHAT FAILS             | EFFECT             |
|-----------------|------------------------|--------------------|
| MISFIRE A.1/A.2 | No procedure or wrong  | Act void —         |
|                 | circumstances          | "doesn't come off" |
|-----------------|------------------------|--------------------|
| MISFIRE B.1/B.2 | Execution incorrect/   | Act void           |
|                 | incomplete             |                    |
|-----------------|------------------------|--------------------|
| ABUSE Γ.1/Γ.2   | Sincerity conditions   | Act performed but  |
|                 | violated               | hollow/abused      |
+----------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Austin's Collapse of the Constative/Performative Distinction

Austin reaches a surprising conclusion: the distinction he started with doesn't hold.

```
  PROBLEM: Every constative can be reformulated as a performative.
  "The cat is on the mat" = "I hereby state that the cat is on the mat."
  "I state" is a performative verb!

  CONVERSELY: Every performative has truth conditions in a way.
  "I promise to come" can be evaluated against sincerity conditions.
  Even the "happiness" of a performative has cognitive content.

  AUSTIN'S CONCLUSION (How to Do Things with Words, Lecture XII):
  The constative/performative distinction is not fundamental.
  All utterances are actions.
  Constatives are a SUBTYPE of speech acts — ones where the
  primary illocutionary force is to describe/assert.
  The three-part analysis (locutionary/illocutionary/perlocutionary)
  replaces the initial distinction.
```

## Searle: A Systematic Theory

John Searle (b. 1932) systematized Austin's insights into a full theory of speech acts.

### Illocutionary Acts: The Five Types

```
  ASSERTIVES (REPRESENTATIVES):
  Commit the speaker to the truth of a proposition.
  Direction of fit: WORDS → WORLD (describe it)
  Examples: affirm, assert, claim, deny, report, state, guess

  DIRECTIVES:
  Attempt to get hearer to do something.
  Direction of fit: WORLD → WORDS (make world match words)
  Examples: ask, command, dare, insist, order, request, suggest

  COMMISSIVES:
  Commit the speaker to a future course of action.
  Direction of fit: WORLD → WORDS
  Examples: offer, pledge, promise, swear, threaten, vow

  EXPRESSIVES:
  Express the speaker's psychological state.
  No direction of fit (not about truth or action)
  Examples: apologize, congratulate, thank, welcome, deplore

  DECLARATIONS:
  Bring about the state of affairs they name.
  Bi-directional fit: WORDS ↔ WORLD simultaneously
  Examples: appoint, christen, declare war, fire (dismiss), resign
  Require institutional context:
  "You're fired" only works if said by the right person in the
  right institutional context.
  "I hereby declare war" only if said by a head of state.
```

### Direction of Fit

```
  A key analytical concept:

  WORDS TO WORLD (assertives):
  Words must fit how the world is.
  "It is raining" is true if the world contains rain.
  Failure: the belief is false.

  WORLD TO WORDS (directives, commissives):
  The world must change to match the words.
  "Clean your room" = world should become clean.
  Failure: the order is not obeyed, the promise not kept.
  But it is NOT false — it is unfulfilled/disobeyed.

  DECLARATIONS: BOTH simultaneously.
  "I hereby declare you guilty" changes the institutional
  world AND reflects that change in words.
```

## Indirect Speech Acts

One of Searle's most important contributions.

```
  LITERAL MEANING:    "Can you pass the salt?"
                       = Question about ability
  ILLOCUTIONARY FORCE: REQUEST to pass the salt

  How does the hearer understand the request from the
  literally-phrased question?

  SEARLE'S ACCOUNT:
  1. The literal meaning is understood: are you able to pass salt?
  2. This is a trivially true question — hearer is obviously able.
  3. Conversational principle: speakers say relevant things.
  4. The relevant thing: a request for the salt.
  5. Therefore: I must be indirectly requesting.

  This analysis links to GRICE'S COOPERATIVE PRINCIPLE
  (see 08-PRAGMATICS.md).

  CONVENTIONALIZED INDIRECT ACTS:
  Some indirect acts are so common they are semi-conventionalized:
  "Can you..." → request (idiomatic)
  "Would you mind..." → polite request
  "Could you possibly..." → very polite request
  These no longer require full pragmatic inference — they are
  nearly lexicalized.

  POLITENESS THEORY (Brown & Levinson):
  Face-threatening acts (requests, criticisms, impositions)
  are softened by indirection to protect face.
  Indirectness = politeness marking.
  Direct commands are face-threatening.
  "Would you be able to perhaps consider closing the window?"
  = very face-threatening request wrapped in maximal indirection.
```

## Searle's Constitutive Rules

```
  REGULATIVE RULES:
  Regulate pre-existing activities.
  "Drive on the left."
  Traffic exists independently of this rule.
  Rules govern something that exists without them.

  CONSTITUTIVE RULES:
  X counts as Y in context C.
  Create the very activities they regulate.

  "Moving the rook forward counts as an illegal move in chess."
  Chess = CONSTITUTED by its rules. Without rules, no game.

  SPEECH ACTS AS CONSTITUTED ACTIVITIES:
  "Uttering 'I promise...' in appropriate circumstances counts
  as making a promise."
  There is no "natural" promising prior to the conventions.
  The conventions CONSTITUTE the act.

  THE FORM: X counts as Y in context C
  Uttering 'I promise φ' counts as committing to φ in context C
  (= speaker intends, hearer understands, both recognize this)

  INSTITUTIONAL REALITY:
  Searle's later work (Construction of Social Reality, 1995)
  extends this: all social/institutional facts are constituted
  by collective acceptance of "X counts as Y in C" declarations.
  Money, property, government, marriage — all constituted by
  collective acceptance.
  → Status functions assigned to physical objects by declaration.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| Performative | An utterance that performs an action rather than describes one |
| Locutionary act | The act of saying something with a definite sense and reference |
| Illocutionary act | What the speaker is doing in making the utterance (the force) |
| Perlocutionary act | The causal effect on the hearer |
| Felicity conditions | Conditions that must hold for a speech act to succeed |
| Misfire | Failure of A or B conditions — act simply doesn't occur |
| Abuse | Failure of Γ conditions — act occurs but is insincere |
| Assertive | Speech act committing speaker to truth of a proposition |
| Directive | Speech act attempting to get hearer to do something |
| Commissive | Speech act committing speaker to future action |
| Declaration | Speech act that brings about the state of affairs it names |
| Direction of fit | Words→World (assertions) vs World→Words (directives/commissives) |
| Constitutive rule | "X counts as Y in C" — rule that creates the activity |

## Common Confusion Points

**Illocutionary ≠ perlocutionary**: The illocutionary act is what the speaker IS DOING (warning, promising, requesting) — it is constituted by the utterance in context. The perlocutionary act is the EFFECT it has on the hearer (they become scared, they comply) — it is causal and contingent.

**Misfires vs abuses**: Misfires (wrong procedure, wrong person, incomplete execution) mean the act doesn't occur — you're not married if the officiant doesn't have authority. Abuses (insincerity) mean the act occurs but is hollow — a promise made insincerely is still a promise (you broke it).

**"Performative" in Austin ≠ "performativity" in Butler**: Austin's performatives are a specific type of utterance (I promise, I hereby name...). Butler's performativity is a theory of identity formation through repeated social acts. Same word, completely different theoretical context.

**Declarations require institutional power**: "You're fired" only works as a declaration (performative act that changes the world) if the speaker has the institutional authority to fire people. The same sentence said by someone with no authority is not a misfire — it is simply not a felicitous declaration.

**Speech acts are not just about spoken language**: Speech act theory applies to writing, signing, gestures, etc. — any communicative action. Written contracts, signed agreements, email terminations — all invoke the same framework. The "speech" in speech act is a term of art, not a restriction to spoken utterances.
