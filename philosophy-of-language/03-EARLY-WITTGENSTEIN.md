# Early Wittgenstein: Tractatus and Picture Theory

## The Book and Its Context

The Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (1921) is one of the strangest and most influential books in 20th-century philosophy. Written by Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889–1951) during and after WWI, published through Russell's support, it presented a complete system of logic, language, and the world in 75 pages. The book consists of numbered propositions (hierarchically structured) and ends with the famous statement: "What we cannot speak about we must pass over in silence."

The central claim: language can express what is the case (facts about the world) — but logic itself, ethics, aesthetics, and the nature of representation cannot be said; they can only be shown.

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                     TRACTATUS STRUCTURE                               |
|                                                                       |
|  WORLD                         LANGUAGE                               |
|  -----                         --------                               |
|  OBJECTS (Gegenstände)         NAMES (simple, unanalyzable)           |
|  Simple, unalterable           Name → Object (meaning = bearer)       |
|  Form the substance of world                                          |
|            ↓                              ↓                           |
|  ATOMIC FACTS (Sachverhalte)   ELEMENTARY PROPOSITIONS                |
|  Objects in determinate        Names concatenated                     |
|  combination                   "Picture" of possible atomic fact      |
|            ↓                              ↓                           |
|  STATES OF AFFAIRS (Tatsachen) PROPOSITIONS                         |
|  Existing atomic facts         Truth-functions of elementary propns   |
|            ↓                              ↓                           |
|  THE WORLD                     THE TOTALITY OF TRUE PROPOSITIONS      |
|  All facts (not things)        = the totality of natural science      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## The Picture Theory of Meaning

The core of the Tractatus. A proposition is a picture of a possible state of affairs.

### How Pictures Work

```
  A PICTURE has the same logical form as what it depicts.

  Consider a MODEL RAILROAD ACCIDENT used in court:
  - Toy cars represent real cars (by convention — like names)
  - Their SPATIAL ARRANGEMENT represents the spatial arrangement
    of real cars at the scene of the accident
  - The picture SHARES FORM with reality

  This is Wittgenstein's model:
  A proposition is like a picture.
  The elements of the proposition (names) represent objects.
  The ARRANGEMENT of elements in the proposition represents
  a possible arrangement of objects in the world.
```

### The Formal Account

```
  LOGICAL FORM:
  A picture can represent what it depicts because it shares
  a LOGICAL FORM with reality — the possibility structure
  of how objects can be combined.

  ELEMENTARY PROPOSITION:
  A fact (not just a list) — names in a concatenated relationship.
  "aRb" — a stands in relation R to b.
  This picturally represents: object a stands in relation R to object b.

  TRUTH CONDITIONS:
  An elementary proposition is TRUE  if the depicted state of affairs exists.
  An elementary proposition is FALSE if the depicted state of affairs does not exist.
  The proposition has SENSE (Sinn) = its truth conditions.
  The proposition has MEANING = the objects named by its constituents.

  A PROPOSITION SHOWS its sense — what would be the case if it is true.
  A PROPOSITION SAYS whether that state of affairs obtains.
```

### The Picture / World Correspondence

```
  PICTURE                         REALITY
  -------                         -------
  Name A         ←bijection→      Object a
  Name B         ←bijection→      Object b
  Relational     ←shared form→    Spatial/relational
  arrangement                     arrangement

  "aRb" is TRUE  iff  a R b in the world
  "aRb" is FALSE iff  a R b does NOT obtain

  THE PICTURE DOES NOT NEED TO BE TRUE TO HAVE SENSE.
  A false proposition still pictures something — a possible
  state of affairs that simply does not obtain.
  Sense ≠ truth.
```

## The Logical Structure of the World

Wittgenstein's ontology mirrors his logical theory.

### Objects, Atomic Facts, the World

```
  OBJECTS (Tractatus 2.01–2.02):
  Simple, unchangeable, unalterable.
  The "substance" of the world — what must exist for
  any representation at all to be possible.
  Objects are the possible constituents of atomic facts.
  Wittgenstein is deliberately vague about what objects ARE
  (sense data? physical particles? properties?).

  ATOMIC FACTS (Sachverhalte = states of affairs):
  Objects in determinate combination.
  "In a state of affairs, objects fit into one another
  like the links of a chain." (2.03)
  The world = the totality of existing atomic facts.

  THE WORLD is the totality of facts, not of things.
  Things (objects) can be enumerated, but the world
  is how things ARE ARRANGED, not just what things are.

  THIS MIRRORS LOGICAL STRUCTURE:
  Names = objects (atomic constituents)
  Elementary propositions = atomic facts (combinations)
  Complex propositions = truth-functions of elementary ones
  The totality of true propositions = the world
```

## Truth-Functions: The Logical Core

```
  ALL PROPOSITIONS ARE TRUTH-FUNCTIONS of elementary propositions.

  Logical constants (¬, ∧, ∨, →, ↔) are NOT parts of reality.
  They do not represent objects or facts.
  They are operations on propositions.

  "p ∧ q" is true when both p and q are true.
  But "∧" names no object, depicts no fact.
  It is a truth-functional operation.

  TAUTOLOGY: true under all truth-value assignments.
  (p ∨ ¬p): says nothing about the world.
  It is not a picture — it shows the logic of our representation.

  CONTRADICTION: false under all assignments.
  (p ∧ ¬p): impossible.

  TAUTOLOGIES AND CONTRADICTIONS ARE SENSELESS (sinnlos):
  Not false — they say nothing. They show the limits of logic.

  THIS IS THE SOURCE OF WITTGENSTEIN'S LATER INSIGHT:
  Logical truths are not discoveries about a Platonic realm.
  They are features of our representational system.
```

## The Limits of Language and the Unsayable

The most metaphysically ambitious part of the Tractatus.

```
  WHAT CAN BE SAID:
  Propositions of natural science (physics, chemistry, etc.)
  Everything that can be a picture of an atomic fact.
  "All the propositions of natural science" (Tractatus 4.11)

  WHAT CANNOT BE SAID (but is shown):
  The LOGICAL FORM that makes picturing possible.
  Ethics ("Good", "God", values)
  Aesthetics (the beautiful)
  The self (the metaphysical subject, not the empirical self)
  The meaning of life
  "How the world is" (facts) vs "that the world is" (mystical)

  The logical form of language cannot be stated within language —
  it is the precondition for saying anything at all.
  It can only be SHOWN by the structure of propositions.

  PROPOSITIONS ABOUT LOGIC:
  The sentences of logic (tautologies) do not describe logical form —
  they SHOW it. They are not pictures of facts.

  THE PHILOSOPHICAL PROPOSITIONS OF THE TRACTATUS ITSELF:
  Wittgenstein's famous ending: his own propositions are NONSENSICAL.
  They are "elucidations" — like a ladder to be kicked away once
  you have climbed to the correct view.
  "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." (7)
```

## The Show/Say Distinction

Central to understanding what the Tractatus is doing:

```
  SAYING (Sagen):                     SHOWING (Zeigen):
  What propositions SAY               What propositions SHOW
  = their sense                       = their form
  = what world-state they depict      = the logical structure
  = truth-evaluable content           = the preconditions for
                                        content
  EXAMPLES:
  "The rose is red"                   SHOWS subject-predicate form,
  SAYS that the rose is red           SHOWS that there are objects
  (true or false)                     and properties

  "p ∧ q → p"                         SHOWS the truth-functional
  SAYS nothing (tautology)            structure of logic
                                      Cannot be SAID — only shown

  "A object exists"                   CANNOT BE SAID
  NONSENSE as a proposition           (existence shown by the
  (not false, just not sayable)       presence of a name)

  WHAT CANNOT BE SAID:
  The form of representation itself.
  Ethics, aesthetics, the will, the self.
  The propositions of philosophy.

  THIS IS WHY PHILOSOPHY IS NOT A SCIENCE:
  Philosophy does not produce propositions — it clarifies
  the logic of propositions. It is an activity, not a doctrine.
```

## The Simple Objects Controversy

The Tractatus never specifies what the "simple objects" at the bottom of analysis are. This is deliberate.

```
  WITTGENSTEIN'S CLAIM: There MUST be simple objects.
  Argument: Propositions have sense independently of facts.
  For a proposition to have DEFINITE sense, analysis must terminate.
  If objects were complex, their nature would depend on facts,
  making sense dependent on contingent truths. Impossible.
  Therefore: there are simples — the substance of the world.

  What ARE the simples?
  Wittgenstein refuses to specify. They are logical presuppositions
  of the POSSIBILITY of representation, not empirical discoveries.

  LATER SELF-CRITIQUE (Philosophical Investigations):
  The demand for definiteness was a philosophical prejudice.
  The picture of language as built on atomic simples was wrong.
  Language does not require a bedrock of simples to function.
  This is Wittgenstein II's main target — his own earlier view.
```

## Influence on Logical Positivism

```
  VIENNA CIRCLE (Carnap, Schlick, Neurath, ~1920s–30s):
  Read the Tractatus closely.
  Adopted: the view that metaphysics consists of nonsense.
  Adopted: the idea that meaningful propositions = pictures of facts.
  DEVELOPED INTO: Logical positivism / verificationism.
  "The meaning of a proposition = its method of verification."
  (This is not in the Tractatus — it is the Vienna Circle's extension.)

  DIVERGENCE FROM WITTGENSTEIN:
  Vienna Circle: eliminate metaphysics as meaningless.
  Wittgenstein: metaphysics shows what cannot be said — not nonsense
  but an attempt to say the unsayable. The mystical is real,
  just not sayable.

  AYER: "Language, Truth and Logic" (1936) = popular version.
  CARNAP: Logical syntax of language (1934) = developed version.
  Both influence the logical tradition in philosophy of language and CS.
```

## Tractatus and Computing: The Connection

```
  WITTGENSTEIN'S PICTURE THEORY anticipates:

  1. DENOTATIONAL SEMANTICS:
     The meaning of a program = the mathematical function it computes.
     A program "pictures" a function (or a state transformer).
     Scott and Strachey's denotational semantics shares the idea
     that expressions are pictures of mathematical objects.

  2. TRUTH-TABLE METHOD:
     Wittgenstein invented the truth-table method (independently of Post).
     All propositional logic = truth-functions of atoms.
     The N-operator can express all truth-functions.
     This is related to NAND completeness in Boolean circuits.

  3. LOGICAL FORM AND TYPES:
     The "form" that names and facts share anticipates type constraints.
     A name can only stand for an object of compatible logical form.
     This is proto-typing.

  4. THE LIMITS OF FORMAL SYSTEMS:
     Wittgenstein's claim that the rules of logic cannot be stated in logic
     anticipates Gödel's incompleteness results (though Wittgenstein
     refused to accept Gödel's theorems as philosophically significant —
     a source of controversy).
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the picture theory? | A proposition is a picture of a possible state of affairs, sharing logical form with it |
| What does a proposition SAY? | Its sense — the state of affairs it depicts |
| What does a proposition SHOW? | Its logical form — the structure of representation itself |
| What is a tautology for Wittgenstein? | Senseless (not false) — shows the logic of representation, says nothing about the world |
| What are "objects" in the Tractatus? | Simple, unalterable constituents of possible facts — deliberately left unspecified |
| What cannot be said? | Logic's own form, ethics, aesthetics, the self, the metaphysical |
| Why does the Tractatus end in silence? | Its own propositions are elucidations, not pictures — the ladder to kick away |

## Common Confusion Points

**Senseless (sinnlos) ≠ nonsense (unsinnig)**: Tautologies and contradictions are sinnlos (senseless — they say nothing, depict no fact). Philosophical pseudo-propositions like "the self exists" are unsinnig (nonsense — they try to say what can only be shown). These are two different failures.

**The picture theory does not require visual resemblance**: A proposition is not a picture the way a photograph is. It shares LOGICAL FORM, not visual appearance. The arrangement of names in a proposition mirrors the possible arrangement of objects in a fact.

**The Tractatus does not say philosophy is impossible**: It says philosophy is an activity of clarification, not a body of doctrine. The pseudo-problems of traditional philosophy arise from misunderstanding the logic of language. Philosophy's job is to dissolve these confusions, not to solve them.

**Wittgenstein I ≠ logical positivism**: The Vienna Circle applied verificationism to dismiss metaphysics as literally meaningless. Wittgenstein I thought metaphysics showed what could not be said — the mystical was real but unsayable. He was no friend of the Circle's anti-metaphysical reductionism.

**The ladder metaphor**: Tractatus 6.54 — Wittgenstein says his own propositions are nonsensical; understanding them correctly means seeing through them and discarding them. The Tractatus is written to be self-undermining on reflection. This is intentional, not a flaw.
