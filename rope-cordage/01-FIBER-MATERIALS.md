# Fiber Materials: Natural and Synthetic Rope Fibers

## The Fiber Landscape

All rope starts as a fiber. The fiber determines everything downstream: the achievable strength, elongation behavior, chemical resistance, UV stability, and cost. Natural fibers dominated for 5,000 years; synthetic fibers have been displacing them since DuPont's nylon in 1938. Modern high-performance applications use exclusively synthetic.

```
FIBER CLASSIFICATION
====================

  NATURAL FIBERS (cellulosic or protein-based, finite staple length)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BAST FIBERS (plant stem):  Hemp, Flax, Jute                   │
  │  LEAF FIBERS (plant leaf):  Manila (abaca), Sisal, Phormium    │
  │  SEED FIBERS (seed hair):   Cotton                               │
  │  FRUIT FIBERS (husk):       Coir (coconut)                     │
  │  ANIMAL (protein):          Wool, Silk (rare in rope)          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SYNTHETIC FIBERS (continuous filament, polymer-based)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  COMMODITY SYNTHETICS:  Nylon (PA), Polyester (PET), PP, PE    │
  │  HIGH-PERFORMANCE:      UHMWPE, Aramid, LCP, PBO               │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  KEY DIMENSION:
  Natural fibers: staple (finite length, ~1–12 inches)
    → must be twisted to create yarn → hairy surface
    → moisture absorption → rot risk
  Synthetic filaments: continuous → no twisting needed for yarn
    → smooth surface → low friction → splice tension different
```

## Natural Fibers

### Hemp (Cannabis sativa)

Hemp was the dominant rope fiber from antiquity through the early 20th century — shipyard cordage, naval rigging, agricultural use.

```
HEMP FIBER PROPERTIES
======================

  Tensile strength:   300–900 MPa (wide range by quality/retting)
  Elongation at break: 2–4%
  Density:            1.4–1.5 g/cc (sinks in water)
  Water absorption:   Up to 30% weight (swells, weakens when wet)
  UV resistance:      Moderate
  Rot resistance:     Moderate (requires treatment or tarring)
  Chemical:           Degrades in strong acids and oxidizing agents

  Wet strength:       ~75–85% of dry strength (contrast with Manila: 80–90%)

  TREATMENT: Hemp rope for marine use was traditionally tarred (coal tar
  or pine tar) to resist rot and water absorption. Tarred hemp rigging
  has significantly improved longevity. Royal Navy standard: Stockholm tar.

  RETTING: Hemp fiber extraction = retting (controlled rotting of stems
  in water to loosen fiber from pith). Dew-retting vs. water-retting.
  Retting quality determines fiber strength and consistency.
```

### Manila (Abaca — Musa textilis)

Manila fiber comes from the leaf sheaths of the abaca plant (a banana relative from the Philippines). It outperforms hemp in most marine properties and was the preferred rope fiber from 1850 through the 1950s when nylon became available.

```
MANILA FIBER PROPERTIES
========================

  Tensile strength:   400–1,100 MPa (higher than hemp)
  Elongation at break: 2–4% (similar to hemp)
  Density:            1.45–1.50 g/cc (sinks)
  Water absorption:   Low compared to hemp; retains more strength wet
  Wet strength:       ~80–90% of dry (better than hemp)
  UV resistance:      Good
  Rot resistance:     Excellent among natural fibers (naturally resistant)
  Float:              No (sinks)

  WHY MANILA REPLACED HEMP FOR MARINE USE:
  • Better wet strength retention
  • Lower water absorption
  • Natural rot resistance
  • More uniform fiber quality
  • Available in large quantities from Philippines

  Manila was specifically chosen by US Navy as standard cordage
  through the 1940s. 1943: synthetic rope development accelerated
  due to Japanese occupation of Philippines cutting supply.
```

### Sisal (Agave sisalana)

```
SISAL FIBER PROPERTIES
=======================

  Tensile strength:   400–700 MPa
  Elongation at break: 2–3%
  Density:            1.45 g/cc
  Water absorption:   High; swells when wet (problematic)
  Rot resistance:     Moderate
  UV resistance:      Good

  USES: Baling twine (largest market), coarse rope, rugs
  NOT preferred for marine: excessive water absorption
  Cost: Cheapest of the major natural rope fibers
```

### Cotton

```
COTTON FIBER PROPERTIES
========================

  Tensile strength:   250–600 MPa (filament; rope considerably less)
  Elongation at break: 7–8%
  Density:            1.5–1.6 g/cc
  Water absorption:   Very high (25%+ weight); soft and absorbent
  UV resistance:      Poor
  Rot resistance:     Poor (requires treatment)

  USES: Soft tie lines, theatrical rigging (softness protects scenery),
        decorative rope, clothesline
  NOT structural: cotton rope has ~1/3 the strength of manila at
  equivalent diameter; never for load-bearing applications
```

### Jute (Corchorus species)

```
JUTE PROPERTIES
===============

  Tensile strength:   200–500 MPa (weakest major bast fiber)
  Elongation:         1.5–2%
  Density:            1.3–1.5 g/cc
  Water absorption:   Very high; loses strength when wet
  Rot resistance:     Very poor

  USES: Burlap fabric, twine, packaging; NOT structural rope
```

### Coir (Coconut Husk Fiber — Cocos nucifera)

```
COIR PROPERTIES
===============

  Tensile strength:   95–230 MPa (lowest of major natural fibers)
  Elongation:         30–40% (highest elongation of natural fibers)
  Density:            1.15–1.35 g/cc — near neutral in saltwater
  Water absorption:   Absorbs water but remains buoyant
  Rot resistance:     Excellent in saltwater (natural lignin content)
  UV resistance:      Good

  UNIQUE PROPERTY: Very high elongation (rope-grade: up to 40%)
  = excellent energy absorption for mooring/docking lines
  MARINE USE: Fender rope, dock lines where shock absorption wanted
              Historically: anchor rodes, tow lines
  Floats or near-neutral buoyancy: drift rope, line traps
```

## Synthetic Fibers: Commodity Grade

### Nylon — Polyamide 6 and 6.6

DuPont introduced Nylon 6.6 in 1938. It was the first truly high-strength synthetic textile fiber.

```
NYLON (PA 6.6 and PA 6) ROPE PROPERTIES
=========================================

  Fiber tensile strength: 800–1,000 MPa
  Rope elongation at break: 20–30% (highest of common synthetics)
  Rope density:          1.14 g/cc (sinks — barely)
  Water absorption:      3–9% by weight; strength drops ~15% wet
  UV resistance:         Good (better than PP, worse than polyester)
  Temperature resistance: Continuous service to 120°C; melts at 220°C
  Abrasion resistance:   Excellent
  Chemical resistance:   Poor in strong acids; good in bases

  ENERGY ABSORPTION: The 20–30% elongation means nylon rope absorbs
  enormous energy under shock load (energy = area under F-extension curve).
  This is WHY nylon is used for:
  • Anchor rodes (absorbs surge and wave shock)
  • Mooring lines (same reason)
  • Dynamic climbing rope (fall arrest — absorbs fall energy)

  NOT for:   Applications where stretch is unacceptable
             (halyards, sheets on racing yachts, winch lines)
```

### Polyester (PET — Polyethylene Terephthalate)

```
POLYESTER (PET) ROPE PROPERTIES
=================================

  Fiber tensile strength: 800–1,100 MPa
  Rope elongation at break: 10–15% (lower than nylon)
  Rope density:          1.38 g/cc (sinks)
  Water absorption:      0.4% (essentially waterproof)
  UV resistance:         Excellent (best of commodity synthetics)
  Temperature resistance: Continuous 135°C; melts at 260°C
  Abrasion resistance:   Very good
  Chemical resistance:   Excellent (acids, bases, solvents)

  KEY ADVANTAGE OVER NYLON:
  • Lower elongation → less stretch under load
  • Maintains strength when wet (no water absorption)
  • Better UV stability
  • Lower creep under sustained load

  USES: General purpose marine, sheets, halyards (not racing),
        mooring lines where stretch less wanted, static rigging,
        round slings for industrial lifting
```

### Polypropylene (PP)

```
POLYPROPYLENE (PP) ROPE PROPERTIES
====================================

  Fiber tensile strength: 300–500 MPa (weakest commodity synthetic)
  Rope elongation at break: 15–25%
  Density:                0.91 g/cc — FLOATS IN FRESH AND SALT WATER
  Water absorption:       None
  UV resistance:          POOR — degrades rapidly in sunlight
                          (without UV stabilizers, fails in 1–2 seasons)
  Temperature resistance: Softens at 90°C; melts at 165°C
  Abrasion resistance:    Moderate
  Chemical resistance:    Good (many acids and bases)

  PRIMARY REASON TO CHOOSE PP: It floats.
  Uses: water rescue throw bags, mooring lines to buoys, utility rope
        where buoyancy is required

  DO NOT USE for: life safety, extended UV exposure without replacement,
                  any application requiring strength retention over time
  IMPORTANT: UV-stabilized PP (carbon black or hindered amine
             light stabilizer) is much more durable but adds cost.
```

## Synthetic Fibers: High-Performance

### UHMWPE — Ultra-High Molecular Weight Polyethylene (Dyneema, Spectra)

```
UHMWPE PROPERTIES
==================

  Fiber tensile strength: 2,400–3,600 MPa
  Rope elongation at break: 3–4% (rope-level)
  Density:                0.97 g/cc (floats — only HPFS fiber that floats)
  Molecular weight:       3–6 million g/mol (vs. normal HDPE: 100,000–500,000)
  UV resistance:          Moderate — needs coating for outdoor use
  Temperature:            Service to ~70°C; strength degrades above 90°C
  Creep:                  LOW at ambient; significant above 70°C
  Chemical resistance:    Excellent (most acids, bases, solvents)
  Cut resistance:         Very high

  STRENGTH-TO-WEIGHT: 15× steel (wire rope) on breaking strength basis
  → replaces wire rope in weight-critical applications

  BUOYANCY: Density 0.97 g/cc → floats in seawater (1.025 g/cc)
  → unique among high-performance fibers

  SPLICE vs. SWAGE:
  UHMWPE cannot be swaged (too slippery, too stiff for conventional ferrule).
  Use:
  • Buried splice (lock stitch splice) — 95%+ efficiency
  • Thimble + splice to terminal
  • Brummel splice (for smaller diameter applications)
  NEVER use in a standard bowline or clove hitch for load-bearing —
  UHMWPE slips at knots under load (low coefficient of friction)

  DEGRADATION WARNING: UV degrades UHMWPE over time (turns yellow/brown,
  strength loss). Inspect color and test after significant outdoor use.
```

### Aramid Fibers (Kevlar, Technora, Twaron)

```
ARAMID FIBER PROPERTIES
========================

  Fiber tensile strength: 2,900–3,600 MPa (Kevlar 29/49, Technora)
  Rope elongation at break: 3.6% (Kevlar 29)
  Density:                1.44 g/cc (sinks)
  UV resistance:          POOR — degrades rapidly in UV (months)
  Temperature resistance: Stable to 480°C (dry); chars at 500°C+
  Abrasion resistance:    Moderate (cuts along fiber axis easily)
  Fatigue resistance:     Poor in bending fatigue (not flex-cycling)
  Chemical:               Stable to most solvents; degrades in UV

  Kevlar 29: General purpose aramid
  Kevlar 49: Intermediate modulus (stiffer) — aerospace
  Technora:  Co-polymer; better chemical resistance, hydrolysis resistance

  BEST USE CASES:
  • High-temperature environments (exhaust, engine room)
  • Cut-resistant applications (blade resistance)
  • Structural stay where UV protected (inside conduit or sheathing)

  NOT for: Dynamic loading (bending fatigue → fails rapidly)
           UV-exposed outdoor use (requires protective sheathing)
           Applications needing high elongation
```

## Buoyancy in Seawater

Seawater density: ~1.025 g/cc. Any fiber with density below 1.025 g/cc will float a dry rope in seawater.

| Fiber | Density vs. Seawater | Behavior |
|-------|---------------------|----------|
| Polypropylene (0.91) | Much less | Floats — actively |
| UHMWPE (0.97) | Less | Floats — barely |
| Nylon (1.14) | Greater | Sinks (when dry; absorbs water which helps) |
| Polyester (1.38) | Greater | Sinks |
| Aramid (1.44) | Greater | Sinks |

Floating rope matters for: water rescue throw lines (must stay at surface), drift lines, surface buoy mooring.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Application | Fiber Choice | Reason |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Dynamic climbing rope | Nylon | High elongation absorbs fall energy |
| Marine sheets and halyards | Polyester | Low stretch, UV stable, no water absorption |
| Mooring lines (surge absorption) | Nylon | Elongation absorbs dock surge loads |
| Water rescue throw line | Polypropylene | Floats — visibility + buoyancy |
| Weight-critical lifting sling | UHMWPE (Dyneema) | 15× steel by weight |
| Heat-resistant lashing | Aramid (Kevlar) | Stable to 480°C |
| Low-creep racing rigging | Vectran or UHMWPE | Near-zero creep under sustained load |
| Utility rope, indoor use | Polyester or nylon | Balanced properties |
| Historic reproduction | Hemp (tarred) | Authenticity for museum/heritage vessels |

## Common Confusion Points

**Manila is not hemp.** Manila (abaca) comes from a banana relative in the Philippines. Hemp comes from Cannabis sativa. They look similar as finished rope, but manila has better marine properties. Pre-synthetic "manila rope" is a distinct fiber, not a synonym for natural rope.

**UHMWPE "Dyneema" and "Spectra" are the same fiber.** Dyneema is DSM's brand (Netherlands); Spectra is Honeywell's brand (US). Same ultra-high molecular weight polyethylene. Different manufacturer, same polymer, similar properties.

**Nylon gets weaker when wet.** Nylon absorbs water (up to 9% by weight), which acts as a plasticizer, reducing tensile strength by ~15%. This needs to be accounted for in wet-service applications (marine, rescue). Polyester does not have this problem.

**PP rope has a service life issue.** UV degradation makes polypropylene rope brittle and weak without visible indication. A PP rope that looks fine may have lost 50% of its strength after 2 years of outdoor UV exposure. Inspect by bending — if stiff or crackles, discard. Annual replacement is standard practice for safety-critical PP rope.
