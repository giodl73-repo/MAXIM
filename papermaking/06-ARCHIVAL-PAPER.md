# Archival and Conservation Paper

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|             PAPER DEGRADATION LANDSCAPE                          |
|                                                                  |
|   ACID PAPER                     ALKALINE PAPER                  |
|   (pre-1980s most paper)         (modern standard)               |
|   ──────────────────             ──────────────────              |
|   pH 4.5–6.0                     pH 7.5–8.5                      |
|   Rosin-alum sized               AKD/ASA sized                   |
|   Lignin-containing in           Low-lignin (chemical pulp)      |
|   cheap grades                   CaCO3 buffer                    |
|   Lifespan: 50–200 years         Lifespan: 300–500+ years        |
|                                                                  |
|   The "slow fires" problem:      ISO 9706 / ANSI Z39.48-1992    |
|   ~80% of library holdings       standards define permanence      |
|   from 1850–1990 are on          requirements                    |
|   acidic, degrading paper                                        |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

The estimate: 30 million books in US libraries are "brittle books" — so
degraded they will crumble when handled within 50 years. This is an archival
crisis driven by chemistry, not physical damage.

## The Chemistry of Paper Degradation

### Acid-Catalyzed Hydrolysis

```
   PRIMARY DEGRADATION MECHANISM:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Cellulose: [C6H10O5]n, DP ~ 1000–1500 in paper

   H⁺ (acid) attacks β-1,4-glycosidic bond:
   –C-O-C– + H2O → –C-OH + HO-C–
   (chain scission — permanent break)

   Results in:
   ↓ DP → shorter chains
   ↓ Tensile strength → paper tears easily
   ↓ Flexibility → paper cracks and crumbles
   Embrittlement = "brittleness" of old newspapers / old books
```

Rate of hydrolysis follows Arrhenius equation:
- k = A · exp(–Ea / RT)
- Ea ~ 100–120 kJ/mol for cellulose hydrolysis
- Temperature effect: 10°C decrease in storage T → ~2× longer lifespan
- pH effect: decrease from pH 5.0 to pH 4.0 → ~10× faster degradation

```
   DEGRADATION RATE (approximate, paper at 20°C and 50% RH):
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   pH 4.5 (acidic): DP halves in ~50–100 years
   pH 6.5 (near neutral): DP halves in ~200–400 years
   pH 8.0 (alkaline + buffer): DP halves in ~500–1000+ years
```

### Sources of Acid in Paper

```
   1. ROSIN-ALUM SIZING (historical):
      ──────────────────────────────
      Al³⁺ hydrolyzes in water: Al³⁺ + 3H2O → Al(OH)3 + 3H⁺
      Continuous low-level acid generation over decades

   2. LIGNIN OXIDATION:
      ───────────────
      Lignin + UV + O2 → aromatic radicals → quinones → organic acids
      Groundwood (high lignin) paper: rapid acidification + yellowing

   3. ATMOSPHERIC POLLUTION:
      ────────────────────────
      SO2 + O2 + H2O → H2SO4 → sulfuric acid deposits on paper
      NOx: similar mechanism
      Historic archives in industrial cities: much higher degradation rates

   4. MIGRATION FROM OTHER MATERIALS:
      ──────────────────────────────────
      Acid from folder (non-archival), adjacent acidic paper
      Ink acids (iron gall inks, acidic printing inks)
      Plastic enclosures releasing plasticizers / acid gases
```

### Lignin-Specific Degradation

```
   LIGNIN CONTENT vs. DEGRADATION:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Groundwood (40% lignin): yellows in hours of UV, acid accumulates rapidly
   Newsprint: from white to yellow/brittle in 10–20 years
   Kraft pulp (<1% residual lignin): stable to UV, minimal acid generation
   Cotton (no lignin): most stable natural paper material

   LIGNIN + LIGHT (PHOTOTRANSFORMATION):
   ──────────────────────────────────────
   UV-A/B → phenyl radicals → guinone chromophores → yellow/brown
   Mechanism identical to wood darkening in sunlight
   Radiata pine: measurable yellowing in 30 min of sunlight exposure
   Kraft-bleached paper: very slow yellowing (residual lignin <0.05%)
```

## Standards for Permanent Paper

### ISO 9706 (International Standard for Permanence)

Published 1994, revised. Requirements for paper intended to last ≥500 years:

```
   ISO 9706 REQUIREMENTS:
   ───────────────────────
   1. pH (cold water extract):  ≥ 7.5
   2. Alkaline reserve:         ≥ 2% CaCO3 equivalent
      (buffer capacity to neutralize future acid formation)
   3. Kappa number:             ≤ 5 (residual lignin < 1%)
   4. Tear resistance:          ≥ 350 mN (tensile strength proxy)
   5. Folding endurance:        ≥ 150 double folds (Köster tester)
      (brittleness indicator)

   THE CaCO3 BUFFER:
   ──────────────────
   CaCO3 + H2SO4 → CaSO4 + H2O + CO2
   The alkaline reserve "consumes" acid as it forms
   2% CaCO3 equivalent provides ~20–50 years acid neutralization
   At pH maintained ≥ 7 → cellulose hydrolysis effectively stopped
```

### ANSI/NISO Z39.48-1992

US standard for permanent/durable library paper. Similar to ISO 9706:
- pH 7.5–10 (cold water extract)
- Alkaline reserve ≥ 2%
- Kappa number ≤ 5
- Meets ISO 9706 fold + tear requirements

Paper meeting these standards is marked with the infinity-within-circle symbol ♾
(the "permanent paper" symbol).

### Accelerated Aging Tests

```
   DRY HEAT AGING:
   ───────────────
   ISO 5630-3: 24h at 105°C
   Simulates ~25–50 years natural aging at 20°C, 65% RH
   Measure: folding endurance before/after

   MOIST HEAT AGING:
   ──────────────────
   ISO 5630-5: 72h at 80°C, 65% RH
   More aggressive hydrolysis conditions
   Better proxy for humid storage environments

   ACCELERATED AGING MODELS:
   ──────────────────────────
   Time equivalence: 1 day at 105°C ≈ 1–3 years at 20°C, 65% RH
   (Arrhenius model with Ea ~105 kJ/mol)
   Error: oxidation vs. hydrolysis mechanisms have different Ea
   → Accelerated aging not perfect proxy but standard industry practice
```

## Archive-Quality Fiber Sources

### Preferred Fiber for Archival Paper

```
   RANK OF FIBER SOURCES BY ARCHIVAL QUALITY:
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────
   1. Cotton linter / rag pulp:  DP ~5000–7000, near-zero lignin,
                                  pH-neutral, long fibers
                                  Used: banknotes, archive maps, certificates

   2. Acid-free bleached SW kraft: DP ~1000–1500, kappa < 5,
                                    alkaline-sized, CaCO3 buffer
                                    Used: ISO 9706 printing paper

   3. Acid-free HW kraft:         DP ~800–1200, kappa < 5, alkaline
                                    Used: archive boxes, folders

   4. Recycled alkaline fiber:    Variable quality, pH depends on source
                                    Lower DP than virgin
                                    Acceptable for file folders if alkaline

   AVOID for archival:
   - Groundwood / newsprint (lignin-rich)
   - Old acidic paper (pH < 7)
   - Paper with iron gall ink (accelerates degradation — separate category)
   - Bleached with elemental Cl2 (older stock) — residual Cl can cause damage
```

## Conservation: Treating Degrading Paper

### Mass Deacidification

For libraries with millions of degrading volumes, individual treatment is
impractical. Mass deacidification processes:

```
   BOOKKEEPER (Preservation Technologies):
   ─────────────────────────────────────────
   Spray/immersion with MgO particles dispersed in HFC carrier
   MgO + CO2 → MgCO3 (alkaline buffer deposited in paper)
   Batch capable: ~1,000 books/cycle
   Used: US Library of Congress, national libraries worldwide

   BÜCHERRETTUNG / DEZ (diethylzinc):
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Gas-phase treatment with Zn(C2H5)2
   Reacts with paper moisture + acids → ZnO + alkane
   Very effective but Zn compound is pyrophoric → safety risks
   Limited commercial use now

   BATTELLE / WEI T'O PROCESS:
   ──────────────────────────────
   Magnesium alkoxide in inert solvents
   Good penetration, commercial use in Scandinavian national archives
```

Mass deacidification outcome:
- Neutralizes existing acid (immediate benefit)
- Deposits alkaline buffer (long-term benefit)
- Does NOT restore mechanical strength (already-cleaved bonds stay cleaved)
- Target: stop further degradation, extend useful life 100–300 years

### Aqueous Deacidification (Single Sheets)

```
   CALCIUM HYDROXIDE BATH:
   ────────────────────────
   Ca(OH)2 in water (0.1–0.5% solution)
   Immerse paper 30–60 min
   Buffer deposited: CaCO3 (from atmospheric CO2 reaction)
   Good for single maps, prints, historical documents

   CALCIUM BICARBONATE:
   ─────────────────────
   CO2 dissolved in Ca(OH)2 → Ca(HCO3)2
   Less alkaline → safer for water-sensitive inks
   Better penetration into closed-pore paper
```

### Physical Conservation

```
   ENCAPSULATION:
   ───────────────
   Document sealed in polyester (Mylar/Melinex) envelope
   No adhesive contacts paper → fully reversible
   Provides physical support
   Note: does NOT deacidify — just protects from handling damage

   PHASED BOX (phase box):
   ────────────────────────
   Acid-free board enclosure, custom fit
   Microenvironment protection from dust, humidity fluctuations

   CELLULOSE TISSUE MENDING:
   ──────────────────────────
   Japanese tissue paper + wheat starch paste
   Repair tears and losses
   Fully reversible (water)
   Reversibility: gold standard for archive conservation
```

## Environmental Controls for Storage

```
   RECOMMENDED STORAGE CONDITIONS:
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Paper (general archive):
   Temperature: 15–18°C  (constant, no cycling)
   RH: 45–55%            (constant)
   Light: darkness (UV causes lignin oxidation and OBA fading)

   Photographic materials: more stringent (5–10°C, 30–40% RH)

   DEGRADATION RATES vs. CONDITIONS:
   ────────────────────────────────────
   20°C vs. 10°C: ~2× difference in degradation rate
   50% RH vs. 30% RH: slower at lower RH (less hydrolysis water)
   BUT: too dry (<30% RH) → embrittlement
   UV light: accelerates oxidative + photodegradative pathways

   RULE OF THUMB (Henderson):
   ────────────────────────────
   Every 10°C temperature decrease → 2× increase in paper lifespan
   Every 10% RH decrease → ~1.4× lifespan increase
   Combined: cool, dry, dark = maximum lifespan
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Archival need | Specification |
|--------------|---------------|
| Permanent paper purchase | ISO 9706 certified, pH ≥ 7.5, 2% alkaline reserve |
| Archive folder / box | Acid-free board (pH ≥ 7.5, no alum), buffered |
| Banknote / legal paper | Cotton 100% or cotton/linen blend |
| Treat acidic book collection | Mass deacidification (Bookkeeper or equivalent) |
| Single item conservation | Aqueous Ca(OH)2 or Ca(HCO3)2 treatment |
| Fragile document storage | Polyester encapsulation + acid-free box |
| Storage environment | 15–18°C, 45–55% RH, no UV exposure |
| Test paper permanence | ISO 5630 accelerated aging + fold/tear |

## Common Confusion Points

**Deacidification does not restore strength**: It neutralizes acid and deposits
alkaline buffer, stopping further hydrolysis. But cellulose chains already
broken don't re-form. A book deacidified at pH 4.5 will stop getting worse
but won't become flexible again. Mass deacidification is preservation, not
restoration.

**Acid-free ≠ archival forever**: "Acid-free" just means pH ≥ 7.0 at time of
manufacture. Without alkaline reserve and low lignin, an acid-free paper
could still become acidic over time (lignin oxidation, atmospheric SO2). ISO
9706 is the minimum meaningful permanence standard.

**Iron gall ink is a separate crisis**: Medieval and early modern documents
with iron gall ink (FeSO4 + tannic acid reaction product) are acidic and
catalytically oxidative. The ink itself attacks the paper at the ink-paper
interface. Requires specific treatment protocols (calcium phytate, etc.),
not just standard deacidification.
