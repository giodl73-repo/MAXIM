# The Paper Machine: Forming, Pressing, Drying

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              THE PAPER MACHINE — SECTION BY SECTION              |
|                                                                  |
|  HEADBOX → FORMING → PRESS → DRY END → FINISHING                 |
|              |          |       |          |                     |
|         Consistency  Water   Water      Calendering              |
|         0.3–1.0%    removal removal    Reeling                   |
|         1.0%→0.20%  20→45%  45→93%    Slitting                   |
|                                                                  |
|  STOCK PREP → fan pump → headbox → machine → finished roll       |
|                                                                  |
|  WIRE SECTION:   0.3–1.0% consistency → ~20% solids              |
|  PRESS SECTION:  20% solids → ~40–45% solids                     |
|  DRY END:        45% solids → 92–95% solids                      |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

Water removal by each section:
- Forming (gravity + vacuum): removes ~98% of the water that leaves the wire
- Pressing: cheapest water removal — no heat needed
- Drying: most expensive — uses steam to evaporate remaining water

**Energy imperative**: Always remove water mechanically before thermally.
Every kg of water removed by pressing costs ~1/10 of removing it by evaporation.

## Stock Preparation

Before the paper machine, pulp is prepared precisely.

```
   STOCK PREPARATION SEQUENCE:
   ──────────────────────────────
   PULP BALES or MARKET PULP + RECYCLED FIBER
         |
         | Repulping (hydrapulper): re-disperse dried pulp in water
         ↓
   REFINING (beating):
   Disk refiners — mechanical action on fiber
   Opens fiber surface → fibrillation → more bonding sites
   Cuts fiber length (controlled) → formation quality
   CSF (Canadian Standard Freeness): 600 ml (unbeaten) → 300–400 ml (refined)
   Lower CSF = more refined = stronger bond but slower drainage
         |
         ↓
   FIBER BLENDING:
   SW + HW blend to target properties
   Add fillers (CaCO3, clay, talc)
   Add wet-end chemistry (retention aids, sizing, wet strength)
         |
         ↓
   MACHINE CHEST → FAN PUMP → HEADBOX
   Consistency diluted to ~0.3–1.0% for forming
```

### Refining / Beating — The Critical Control

```
   FIBER BEFORE REFINING:         FIBER AFTER REFINING:
   ─────────────────────          ──────────────────────
   Round cross-section            Collapsed, ribbon-like
   Smooth surface                 Fibrillated surface (fine fiber hairs)
   Low surface area               High surface area
   Slow drainage                  Increased drainage... wait, no:
   Fast drainage (high CSF)       SLOWER drainage (more fibrils)
   Weak sheet                     Stronger sheet (more H-bond sites)

   REFINING EFFECT ON PROPERTIES:
   ────────────────────────────────
   ↑ refining:  ↑ tensile strength  ↑ burst  ↓ tear  ↓ bulk  ↓ opacity
   ↓ refining:  ↑ tear  ↑ bulk  ↑ opacity  ↑ softness (tissue)
```

## The Headbox

Controls the uniformity of fiber distribution across the machine width.

```
   HEADBOX FUNCTION:
   ──────────────────
   1. Uniform velocity profile across full width (up to 11 m)
   2. Turbulence to prevent fiber flocculation
   3. Deliver stock at correct jet/wire speed ratio (jet/wire = 0.95–1.05)

   JET/WIRE SPEED RATIO:
   ─────────────────────
   Jet faster than wire (>1.0): fibers align in machine direction (MD)
      → higher MD/CD tensile ratio
   Jet = wire speed:            random fiber orientation
      → more balanced MD/CD, better formation
   Jet slower than wire (<1.0):  fibers align in CD (rare, some specialties)

   STRATIFIED HEADBOX (multi-layer):
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Deliver different fiber furnishes to different layers of the sheet
   Simultaneously: fine HW fibers to surface + strong SW fibers inside
   → Good surface quality without sacrificing strength
   Used in: copy paper (HW surface/SW core), board grades
```

## Forming Section

### The Classic Fourdrinier

```
   FOURDRINIER WIRE SECTION:
   ──────────────────────────
   Headbox jet lands on wire (metal or plastic mesh, 80–120 mesh)
   Gravity drainage through wire
   Foils and hydrofoils: turbulence assists drainage
   Suction boxes: vacuum increases drainage
   Couch roll: final vacuum dewatering (suction press roll)

   DEWATERING ELEMENTS:
   ─────────────────────
   Table rolls:    low vacuum, gentle drainage
   Hydrofoils:     pulse drainage + maintain turbulence (formation)
   Suction foils:  higher vacuum
   Suction boxes:  high vacuum (20–50 kPa below atmospheric)
   Couch:          very high suction (50–60 kPa)

   RESULT: 1% → 20% solids on forming fabric
```

### Twin-Wire and Gap Formers

Single-wire Fourdrinier has two-sided difference: bottom (wire side) vs. top
(felt side). Modern approach: form between two wires.

```
   GAP FORMER (modern standard):
   ───────────────────────────────
   TWO forming fabrics closing gap around stock jet
   Simultaneous two-sided dewatering
   Benefits:
   - No one-sided drainage → better two-sidedness
   - Higher forming speed (up to 2,000 m/min tissue)
   - Better formation (fiber dispersion)
   - No top-wire installation over old Fourdrinier

   Commercial names: Bel Baie (valmet), Duoformer (Voith)
   Now: virtually all new PM use gap former or twin-wire over Fourdrinier
```

### Formation

"Formation" = the uniformity of fiber mass distribution in the sheet.
Measured quantitatively by beta-radiography or optical sensor.

```
   POOR FORMATION: "cloudy sky" appearance
   ─────────────────────────────────────────
   Fiber flocs visible as dense/thin areas
   Causes: high consistency, insufficient turbulence, wrong drainage rate

   GOOD FORMATION: uniform gray appearance
   ──────────────────────────────────────────
   Even basis weight distribution
   Better optical properties, strength uniformity, print quality

   FORMATION CONTROL:
   ───────────────────
   Headbox turbulence (micro-turbulence generators)
   Jet/wire speed ratio
   Forming board design
   Stock dilution
   Fiber beating level (shorter fibers form better but weaker)
```

## Press Section

Mechanical water removal — most energy-efficient phase.

```
   PRESS TYPES:
   ─────────────
   PLAIN PRESS ROLL:      water squeezed between two rolls + press felt
                          Nip pressure: 0.3–1.0 MPa (line load 100–500 kN/m)

   SUCTION PRESS ROLL:    drilled roll + vacuum inside draws water into felt
                          Better at high speeds

   EXTENDED NIP PRESS (ENP/shoe press):
   ─────────────────────────────────────
   Long nip (30–300 mm vs. <1 mm for roll nip)
   → More dwell time in nip at same pressure
   → More water expelled per nip pass
   → Typical solids increase: 38% (roll) vs. 50% (shoe press)
   Modern standard for kraft, board, and printing paper
```

### Press Felts

Press felts (now synthetic, historically wool) absorb water from sheet and
carry it away from the nip. Must be:
- Compressible (absorb water)
- Permeable (release water by vacuum suction on return loop)
- Dimensionally stable
- Non-marking

```
   PRESS FELT CONSTRUCTION:
   ─────────────────────────
   Base fabric: woven polyester/polyamide monofilament
   Batt: fine staple fibers needled onto base
   → Outer batt contacts paper (smooth, fine fiber)
   → Inner batt provides bulk for water storage
   Felt life: 2–6 weeks before replaced (cost: $5,000–50,000 each)
```

## Drying Section

Water remaining after pressing (~50–60%) must be evaporated.

### Multi-Cylinder Dryers

```
   STANDARD DRYING:
   ─────────────────
   Web passes alternately over upper and lower heated cylinders
   Steam inside cylinders: 100–160°C, 1.5–6 bar gauge
   Dryer fabric (synthetic) holds sheet against cylinder

   DRYER SECTION LAYOUT:
   ─────────────────────
   Pre-dryer: 45% → 92% solids
   Size press (or coating): 92% → apply size/coating → rewet
   After-dryer: re-dry to 94–95%

   HEAT ECONOMY:
   ──────────────
   Steam used: ~1.3–2.0 kg steam per kg water evaporated (multi-cylinder)
   Heat recovery: dryer vapor (wet air) → heat exchanger → preheat intake air
   Modern mills: closed hood, heat/vapor recovery reduces energy by 30–40%
```

### Through-Air Drying (TAD) — Tissue-Specific

```
   TAD PROCESS:
   ─────────────
   High-velocity hot air (160–200°C) blown THROUGH the web
   Very fast, high-capacity drying
   Web not compressed between cylinders → bulk and softness preserved

   Key tissue quality:
   TAD tissue: 3× bulk of conventionally dried tissue at same grammage
   → Soft, absorbent toilet tissue, hand towels
   TAD machines: higher capital cost, higher energy use per ton
   → Justified for premium tissue grades
```

## Calendar and Finishing

### Calendering

Smoothing and thickness reduction by mechanical pressure.

```
   SOFT CALENDAR:
   ───────────────
   Steel roll + soft polymer-covered roll
   Temperature (120–200°C) softens surface fibers
   → Surface smoothing without crushing bulk
   Used before coating for ink holdout

   HARD NIPS (traditional calendar stack):
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   Stack of 2–12 rolls (iron chilled, polished)
   High nip pressure → smooth surface + reduced caliper
   Result: high gloss, low bulk
   Used for supercalendered (SC) grades

   SUPERCALENDER:
   ───────────────
   Off-machine: separate calendar stack
   Steel + soft rolls alternating
   High speed, high pressure, steam spray
   → Very smooth, high gloss
   SC paper: Taber smoothness > 200s
   Used for: coated/uncoated grades for rotogravure printing
```

### Size Press

Applies starch or other binder to web surface between dryer sections.

```
   SIZE PRESS FUNCTION:
   ─────────────────────
   Apply 1–6 g/m² starch solution to both sides
   → Surface strength (prevents surface picking in printing)
   → Barrier properties
   → Smoothness improvement

   FILM PRESS (modern):
   ──────────────────────
   Metered film applied to transfer rolls → contact with web
   Better control than flooded nip design
   Can apply coating colors as well (rod metered, blade metered)
```

## Machine Speed, Width, and Scale

```
   PAPER MACHINE SCALE:
   ──────────────────────
   Tissue machine: 2,000 m/min, 5.5 m wide → 50,000–100,000 t/yr
   Printing paper: 1,400–1,800 m/min, 9–10 m wide → 300,000–500,000 t/yr
   Linerboard: 900–1,200 m/min, 8–10 m wide → 400,000–700,000 t/yr
   Newsprint: 1,500–2,000 m/min, 9–10 m wide → 200,000–400,000 t/yr

   PAPER MACHINE AS PROCESS CONTROL CHALLENGE:
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────
   Basis weight: ±1% variation tolerable, ±2% not acceptable
   Controlled by: headbox pressure, fan pump flow, fiber consistency
   MD variation: headbox flow stability
   CD variation: headbox dilution profiles (100+ control points across width)
   Moisture: CD moisture profile → actuated steam boxes, infrared dryers
```

## Real-Time Control System Bridge

The paper machine is a real-time continuous process control problem running at 1,000–2,000 m/min. The control architecture maps directly onto any SCADA or industrial automation system:

```
PAPER MACHINE CONTROL — FEEDBACK LOOP ARCHITECTURE
====================================================

CONTROLLED VARIABLES:
  Basis weight (g/m²) — primary quality variable
  Moisture content (%) — affects reel weight, customer spec
  Caliper (thickness, µm) — affects stack height, print nip
  Formation uniformity — affects print quality
  CD (cross-direction) profiles — variation across machine width

SENSOR LAYER (scanning measurement):
  Nuclear basis weight gauge: scan 1× per 15–30 sec (full width)
  Infrared moisture sensor: same scan
  Beta-backscatter caliper: same scan
  → Measurement delay: ~30 sec minimum (scan cycle)
  → Machine runs 500–1,000 m of paper between measurements

ACTUATOR LAYER (100+ control points):
  Headbox dilution water: 100–200 CD actuators control local
    fiber concentration → basis weight profile
  Steam boxes: CD moisture correction (heat → evaporate)
  Infrared dryers: section-level moisture trim
  Jet/wire speed ratio: MD/CD tensile balance

CONTROL ARCHITECTURE:
  Basis weight: headbox total flow → feedforward from refiner consistency
  CD basis weight: dilution actuators → pure feedback (slow loop)
  Moisture: dryer steam flow → feedforward; IR sensors → feedback (fast loop)

OPTIMIZATION PRINCIPLE:
  "Remove water mechanically before thermally"
  = always exhaust cheap methods before expensive ones
  Mechanical press: ~0.01 $/ton water removed (press nip, shoe press)
  Thermal drying:  ~1.50 $/ton water removed (steam, energy)
  Same principle: in any optimization, identify the cost hierarchy and
  exhaust the cheap option before committing to the expensive one —
  whether that's caching before computation, or pressing before drying.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Need to control | Lever |
|----------------|-------|
| MD/CD tensile ratio | Jet/wire speed ratio |
| Sheet formation uniformity | Headbox turbulence, jet speed, consistency |
| Basis weight profile (CD) | Headbox dilution actuators |
| Moisture profile (CD) | Infrared dryers, steam boxes |
| Press solids (improve drying efficiency) | Shoe press (extended nip) |
| Tissue softness/bulk | TAD instead of standard drying, minimal refining |
| Surface smoothness (print quality) | Soft-nip calendar, blade coating |

## Common Confusion Points

**Press section removes water most efficiently**: Steam drying costs ~5–8× more
per kg water removed vs. pressing. Every extra point of dryness from the press
section saves significant energy in the dryer. Modern shoe presses (50% solids
out) vs. old plain presses (38%) represent enormous energy savings.

**Formation ≠ basis weight**: Formation is the uniformity of fiber distribution
at the micro-scale (1–10 mm). Basis weight is the average weight per m². A paper
can have perfect average basis weight but terrible formation (visible clumps).
Good formation requires turbulence in forming — a balance with drainage speed.

**Two-sidedness is a forming artifact**: In a classic Fourdrinier, the bottom
wire side has finer fibers and fillers (washed through toward wire); the top
side is coarser. Coated papers hide this. Uncoated printing papers may show
two-sidedness in ink absorption. Twin-wire formers largely eliminate this.
