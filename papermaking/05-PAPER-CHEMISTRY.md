# Paper Chemistry: Sizing, Coating, and Finishing

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|             WET-END AND SURFACE CHEMISTRY                        |
|                                                                  |
|   WET END (added to stock before forming)                       |
|   ──────────────────────────────────────                         |
|   Sizing:      AKD, ASA        → water resistance               |
|   Wet strength: PAE, polyamide → strength when wet              |
|   Fillers:     CaCO3, clay     → opacity, brightness, cost      |
|   Retention:   PAM, C-PAM      → keep fillers in sheet          |
|   Drainage:    microparticles  → drainage rate                  |
|                                                                  |
|   SURFACE (applied at size press or coater)                     |
|   ──────────────────────────────────────────                     |
|   Starch sizing:   surface strength, barrier                    |

|   Optical brighteners (OBA): fluorescence → high brightness     |
|   Barriers:        PLA, starch, PE → food/liquid packaging      |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Internal Sizing: Making Paper Hydrophobic

### Why Sizing Matters

Pure cellulose is hydrophilic — hydroxyl groups absorb water rapidly.
Unsized paper: ink feathers, water soaks through, surface weakens when wet.
Sizing adds hydrophobic surface to fibers, controlling liquid penetration.

### Traditional Acid Sizing: Rosin + Alum

```
   ROSIN SIZING (historical, still used in some grades):
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Rosin (abietic acid) + NaOH → rosin soap
   Alum (Al2(SO4)3) added: Al³⁺ bridges rosin carboxylate to fiber
   Heat in dryer: hydrophobic resin precipitates on fiber surface

   CONDITION: pH 4.5–5.5 (acidic)
   PROBLEM: CaCO3 filler dissolves at pH < 7 → cannot use with fillers
             Paper itself becomes acidic → lignin + acid → degradation
             Archive life: 50–100 years before brittleness
```

### Alkaline Sizing: AKD and ASA

The shift to alkaline sizing (pH 7.5–8.5) from ~1980s enabled:
1. CaCO3 filler use (30% brightness improvement, cost savings)
2. Acid-free, archive-stable paper
3. Better paper machine runnability

```
   AKD (Alkyl Ketene Dimer):
   ──────────────────────────
   Reactive: cyclic ketene reacts with cellulose –OH → covalent ester bond
   Chemistry: β-ketoester bond with surface cellulose
   Added at wet end as dispersed emulsion (cationic)
   Cures in drying section (requires 24–48h off-machine for full hydrophobicity)
   Dosage: 0.1–0.5% on fiber
   Paper pH: 7.5–8.5 (alkaline)

   ASA (Alkenyl Succinic Anhydride):
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Reactive anhydride: reacts with cellulose –OH → half-ester bond
   Must be emulsified on-site (short shelf life)
   Faster reaction than AKD (sizing develops on-machine)
   Better with recycled fiber than AKD
   Used where fast cure needed (board machines)
```

### Starch Surface Sizing

Applied at the size press (between pre-dryer and after-dryer):

```
   FUNCTION OF SURFACE STARCH:
   ────────────────────────────
   1. Surface strength: prevents surface picking during printing
   2. Ink receptivity: modifies surface energy for ink
   3. Barrier: reduces air permeability, slight moisture barrier
   4. Smoothness: fills in surface voids

   STARCH TYPES:
   ──────────────
   Native corn starch: degraded by enzyme or thermal hydrolysis
   Modified (oxidized, cationic, anionic): tailored viscosity
   Application: 1–6 g/m² per side (starch solution 8–12% solids)
```

## Fillers

Mineral particles added to the pulp slurry to modify opacity, brightness,
and reduce fiber cost.

```
   FILLER COMPARISON:

   CaCO3 (Precipitated, PCC):
   ──────────────────────────
   Brightness: 96–98 ISO (highest)
   Refractive index: 1.59 (good light scattering)
   Form: rhombic, scalenohedral, aragonite (tailored)
   Particle size: 0.5–3 µm
   Requires alkaline papermaking (dissolves below pH 6)
   Use: printing/writing paper, coatings
   Cost: low

   KAOLIN CLAY (Calcined or Hydrous):
   ────────────────────────────────────
   Brightness: 80–93 ISO (hydrous), 92–96 (calcined)
   Refractive index: 1.56 (calcined higher)
   Platy shape → good print gloss, good barrier
   Acid-compatible
   Use: blade coatings, filled paper grades
   Cost: medium

   TALC (Mg3Si4O10(OH)2):
   ───────────────────────
   Brightness: 80–88 ISO
   Platy shape → good coverage, low abrasion
   Pitch control in mechanical pulp systems
   Use: newsprint, LWC paper
   Cost: low

   TiO2 (Titanium Dioxide, rutile):
   ──────────────────────────────────
   Brightness: 99+ ISO
   Refractive index: 2.76 (exceptional light scattering)
   Opacity: highest of any white pigment
   Use: specialty papers (Bible paper, cigarette), premium grades
   Cost: high ($2–4/kg vs. $0.15–0.25 for CaCO3)
```

### Retention: Keeping Fillers in the Sheet

Without retention aids, fine particles pass through the wire into white water.
Fine particles add cost without value if not retained.

```
   FILLER RETENTION CHALLENGE:
   ─────────────────────────────
   Untreated CaCO3 (1 µm): passes through 80-mesh wire easily
   Target: retain 70–90% of added filler in sheet

   MICROPARTICLE RETENTION SYSTEM:
   ─────────────────────────────────
   1. Cationic PAM (C-PAM, high MW): flocculates fine particles
   2. Anionic microparticles (bentonite clay, colloidal silica):
      adsorb onto C-PAM flocs → re-flocculate after shear disruption

   RESULT: improved retention + improved drainage (two-for-one)
   Used in most modern printing paper machines
```

## Wet Strength

Standard dry paper is held together by hydrogen bonds between cellulose fibers.
When wet, water disrupts H-bonds → paper disintegrates.

For grades that must function when wet (tissue, food wrapping, outdoor packaging,
tea bags, currency), chemical wet strength additives form covalent cross-links.

```
   POLYAMIDE-EPICHLOROHYDRIN (PAE) RESIN:
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   Cationic PAE reacts with carboxylate groups on fibers (at drying temperatures)
   Forms covalent ester/amide/azetidinium cross-links
   → Wet tensile: 15–40% of dry tensile (vs. 0–2% for normal paper)
   Used: tissues, towels, medical, outdoor bags

   WET STRENGTH MECHANISM:
   ─────────────────────────
   Dry: H-bonds hold sheet
   Wet: H-bonds disrupted by water
   PAE adds: covalent bonds that water cannot disrupt → wet strength retained

   REPULPABILITY PROBLEM:
   ──────────────────────
   PAE wet strength paper is harder to repulp in recycling:
   Need high pH, temperature, or oxidizing agent to break covalent bonds
   → Adds process cost for tissue recycling
```

## Optical Brightening Agents (OBA)

Paper's natural cellulose is slightly yellowish. Brightening options:

```
   BLEACHING (remove chromophore by oxidation): ceiling ~92–93 ISO brightness
   OBA (fluorescent whitening agent): adds fluorescent emission above 100%

   OBA MECHANISM:
   ───────────────
   Stilbene sulfonate derivatives adsorb onto cellulose
   Absorb UV (330–380 nm) → emit blue light (430–440 nm)
   Net effect: paper appears whiter/brighter than white
   Measured brightness: 95–115 ISO (>100 means UV fluorescent emission)

   LIMITATIONS:
   ────────────
   OBA fluorescence fades with UV exposure (outdoor use)
   Fades in washing (laundry — hence "bleaching" in detergents is OBA)
   Cannot use for archival paper: OBA indicates artificial brightening
     (though modern OBA is lightfast for most indoor archival applications)
   UV-B free light sources needed for true brightness measurement
```

## Coating

Paper coating applies a mineral pigment + binder layer to improve print quality.

### Coating Formulation

```
   STANDARD COATING FORMULATION:
   ───────────────────────────────
   PIGMENT (dominant, ~80 dry weight parts):
     PCC or GCC (ground CaCO3): 50–80 parts (brightness, cost)
     Kaolin clay:                10–30 parts (gloss, print quality)
     TiO2:                       0–5 parts (opacity premium)

   BINDER (adhesive, ~15 dry parts):
     SB latex (styrene-butadiene): 8–15 parts (gloss, bind)
     Starch: 3–8 parts (cost reduction, bind assist)
     PVA: specialty applications

   FUNCTIONAL ADDITIVES:
     Water retention agent (CMC): 0.2–0.5 parts
     Thickener (CMC, HMC): viscosity control
     Lubrication: calcium stearate (prevents roll sticking)
     Biocide: prevent bacterial growth in coating kitchen
     OBA: brightness boost in white pigment mix
```

### Coating Application Methods

```
   BLADE COATING:
   ───────────────
   Stock coated then blade scrapes off excess
   → Very smooth surface, precise coat weight
   Coat weight: 5–20 g/m² per side
   Speed: up to 1,600 m/min
   Grades: LWC (light-weight coated), WFC (wood-free coated)

   ROD METERING:
   ──────────────
   Metering rod (grooved or smooth) after pond applicator
   Lower coating stress on paper → better for weaker paper
   Used for: coated board, specialty

   CURTAIN COATING:
   ─────────────────
   Liquid curtain falls freely onto web
   No contact before landing → suitable for very sensitive substrates
   Enables simultaneous multi-layer application
   Used: specialty packaging, photo paper

   AIR KNIFE:
   ──────────
   Air jet removes excess coating
   Low coat weight, low gloss
   Used: lower-quality printing grades
```

### Coating Weight and Grades

```
   COAT WEIGHT (g/m² per side) → GRADE:
   ──────────────────────────────────────
   0           → Uncoated (UC) — copy paper, newsprint
   5–8 g/m²   → Light coated / matte (MFC, SC)
   8–14 g/m²  → LWC (Light Weight Coated) — magazines
   14–20 g/m² → MWC (Medium Weight Coated)
   18–25+ g/m²→ WFC (Wood Free Coated), cast-coated
```

## Calendering Effect on Coated Grades

```
   SOFT CALENDER:      gloss 25–40%  (matte coated grades)
   HARD NIPS 2-roll:   gloss 50–65%  (machine-finished)
   SUPER CALENDER:     gloss 70–80%  (high gloss magazine)
   CAST COATING:       gloss 85–92%  (photo-quality, specialty labels)
```

Cast coating: wet coated paper pressed against a highly polished chrome drum
while drying. Gives photographic mirror surface. Used for premium labels,
art prints.

## Barrier Coatings for Packaging

```
   MOISTURE BARRIER (WVTR):
   ─────────────────────────
   Polyethylene extrusion coating: good WVTR (milk carton standard)
   PVDC (polyvinylidene chloride): excellent WVTR, low coat weight
   Paraffin wax: old standard for frozen food boxes
   Modern: PLA, PHBV, starch-based (bio-based barriers — 06-ARCHIVAL, 09)

   OXYGEN BARRIER:
   ───────────────
   EVOH (ethylene vinyl alcohol): excellent O2 barrier at low humidity
   PVDC: good O2 barrier, all-humidity
   Al foil laminate: absolute barrier (all gases)
   Used: coffee pouches, medical packaging

   GREASE/OIL BARRIER (FCM — Food Contact Material):
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Fluorocarbon treatments (PFAS) — most effective, increasingly restricted
   Alternative: bio-based (starch, PLA, chitosan) barriers — lower performance
   Regulation: EU 2021 PFAS restriction in food contact materials
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Need | Chemistry |
|------|-----------|
| Water-repellent writing paper | AKD or ASA internal sizing |
| Acid-free (archival stable) | Alkaline sizing (AKD/ASA) + buffered with CaCO3 |
| Wet strength (tissue, outdoor) | PAE resin wet strength |
| High brightness print paper | PCC filler + OBA + blade coating |
| High opacity thin paper | TiO2 filler (Bible paper, cigarette) |
| Print gloss for magazines | LWC + super-calendering |
| Liquid packaging board | PE extrusion coating |
| Food packaging grease resistance | Fluorocarbon (being replaced) or bio-barrier |

## Common Confusion Points

**AKD cures slowly**: AKD takes 24–48h after drying to develop full
hydrophobicity. Fresh-cut paper or paper measured immediately after drying will
show low Cobb value (poor sizing) that improves over time. Paper mills must
account for this lag in quality testing.

**More filler ≠ stronger paper**: Fillers displace fiber, reducing fiber-fiber
bonds. Every 1% increase in filler content typically reduces tensile strength
by ~1–3%. This is the fundamental trade-off: brightness/cost from fillers vs.
strength. Retention aids + nanocellulose additions can partially compensate.

**OBA brightness > 100 ISO**: When you see ISO brightness values above 100%,
the paper contains OBA (fluorescent whitening agents). The measurement includes
UV-stimulated visible light emission. "True" reflectance is always ≤ 100%.
Archive papers should specify OBA-free and use D65 daylight or M2 illuminant.
