## Wood Chemistry: The Three Components

Wood is not just cellulose — it's a composite of three polymers:

```
   WOOD COMPOSITION (approximate, dry weight):

   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │  CELLULOSE         40–50%               │
   │  Structural, ordered, crystalline       │
   │  → The fiber we want                    │
   ├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
   │  HEMICELLULOSE     20–30%               │
   │  Branched, amorphous, mixed sugars      │
   │  Hardwood: xylan dominant               │
   │  Softwood: glucomannan dominant         │
   │  → Partially retained in pulp (aids bonding)│
   ├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
   │  LIGNIN            25–35%               │
   │  3D aromatic polymer, fills cell wall   │
   │  Binds fibers together                  │
   │  → Must be removed for white paper      │
   │  → Causes yellowing if retained         │
   ├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
   │  EXTRACTIVES       2–8%                 │
   │  Resins, fats, waxes, terpenes, tannins │
   │  → Cause pitch deposits in mill         │
   └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Lignin — The Enemy of White Paper

Lignin is a complex aromatic heteropolymer cross-linking cellulose and
hemicellulose in the cell wall. Its removal is the purpose of chemical pulping.

```
   LIGNIN STRUCTURE (simplified):
   ────────────────────────────────
   Phenylpropanoid units (coniferyl, sinapyl, p-coumaryl alcohols)
   Linked by C-O-C and C-C bonds (β-O-4, β-5, β-β, 5-5, etc.)
   MW: 5,000–20,000 g/mol
   Amorphous, hydrophobic

   WHY LIGNIN YELLOWS:
   ────────────────────
   UV + O2 → phenyl radical formation → quinone chromophores
   → yellow/brown color development
   Newsprint yellowing in ~hours of UV exposure
   Groundwood paper: high lignin → rapid yellowing
   Kraft paper (delignified): minimal yellowing
```

## Softwood vs. Hardwood: Fiber Differences

```
   SOFTWOOD TRACHEID:             HARDWOOD VESSEL + FIBER:
   ───────────────────            ───────────────────────────
   Length: 2–6 mm                 Vessel: 0.2–0.5 mm (short, wide lumen)
   Width: 20–60 µm                Fiber: 0.5–1.5 mm (short, narrow)
   Wall thickness: moderate       Wall thickness: thin (HW) to thick (HW)
   Lumen: variable by season      Lumen: variable

   LATEWOOD                       LUMEN COLLAPSIBILITY:
   ─────────                      ─────────────────────
   Thick wall → stiff fiber       HW fibers collapse easily on drying
   High MFA → high tensile        → ribbon-like → more contact area
   Less conformable               → better sheet formation and bonding
                                  SW fibers: more tubular → less contact

   RESULT:
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   SW kraft: long fibers, high tear + tensile (strength papers)
   HW kraft: short fibers, fine sheet structure, better surface
   Blend HW+SW: balance strength with surface quality (copy paper)
```

### Tree Species and Pulp Properties

| Species | Type | Fiber length | Key properties | Use |
|---------|------|-------------|----------------|-----|
| Pinus radiata / P. taeda | SW | 2.5–4.5 mm | High tear, long fiber | Liner, sack |
| Picea abies (spruce) | SW | 2–4 mm | Good strength, ECF-bleachable | P&W, board |
| Eucalyptus globulus | HW | 0.7–1.0 mm | Fine, smooth, fast-growing | Tissue, P&W |
| Betula pendula (birch) | HW | 0.8–1.2 mm | Fine sheet, Nordic specialty | P&W |
| Populus (aspen/hybrid) | HW | 0.6–1.0 mm | Fine, Canadian production | Newsprint, P&W |
| Acacia mangium | HW | 0.6–0.9 mm | Fast growing, SE Asia | Tissue, P&W |

### The Eucalyptus Revolution

Eucalyptus globulus (Portugal, Spain, Brazil, Chile, South Africa): rotates
in 7–12 years (vs. 25–40 years for softwood). Fiber length ~0.8 mm ideal for
tissue and printing paper. Brazil is the world's lowest-cost kraft pulp producer
due to Eucalyptus + tropical climate.

```
   EUCALYPTUS ECONOMICS:
   ──────────────────────
   Rotation age: 7 years (Brazil) vs. 25 years (Scandinavian softwood)
   Yield: ~40 m³/ha/yr (irrigated Brazil) vs. 5–8 m³/ha/yr (Scandinavia)
   Kraft yield: ~50% (similar to other hardwoods)
   Pulp cost advantage: often 30–50% below Scandinavian producers
```

## Cotton Fibers

### Cotton Linters

Cotton linters = short fibers (1–8 mm) attached to cotton seed after ginning
(longer staple fiber removed for textiles). High cellulose purity: 90–98%
(vs. 40–50% in wood). Minimal lignin.

```
   COTTON LINTER PROPERTIES:
   ──────────────────────────
   Cellulose content: 90–98%
   DP (degree of polymerization): 5,000–7,000
   Length: 1–6 mm
   Color: white to off-white
   No lignin → no yellowing risk
   Very clean → excellent for food contact, medical

   PROCESSING:
   ────────────
   Mild alkali cook (NaOH or Mg(OH)2 + H2O2)
   Bleach with H2O2 or ozone (no chlorine needed)
   Result: clean, long-fiber, highly bleachable pulp
```

### Cotton Rag

Post-consumer cotton and linen fabric scraps. Premium source.
Longer fibers than linters. Historically the dominant papermaking fiber.

Applications of cotton pulp:
- **Banknotes and security paper**: high tear, durability, tactile feel
- **Archival documents**: legal papers, certificates, archives
- **High-quality writing paper**: Crane's, Laid finish writing paper
- **Specialty: Cigarette paper, filter papers, bible paper (light + opaque)**

## Non-Wood Fibers

### Agricultural Residues

```
   SUGARCANE BAGASSE:
   ──────────────────
   Fibrous residue after sugar extraction
   ~75 million tonnes/year available globally
   Kraft or soda cooking → printing/writing paper
   Brazil, India, China, Thailand major producers
   Rind fiber: coarse → kraft grades
   Pith fiber: too short → typically removed

   WHEAT / RICE STRAW:
   ────────────────────
   ~800 million t/yr available globally
   Low fiber length (0.8–1.5 mm), high silica (straw ash)
   Silica blocks chemical recovery (cause of nozzle blockage)
   Soda process or OCC blend
   China: ~30% of paper uses straw in some regions

   BAMBOO:
   ───────
   Technically a grass, fast-growing (3–7 year rotation)
   Fiber length: 1.5–3 mm (intermediate SW/HW)
   Major use in South/Southeast Asia
   Kraft and semi-chemical pulping
```

### Bast and Leaf Fibers

```
   FLAX (Linum usitatissimum):
   ────────────────────────────
   Fiber from stem bark, 25–50 mm long
   Low lignin content — mild pulping sufficient
   Very strong paper — specialty currency, cigarette rolling paper

   HEMP (Cannabis sativa):
   ────────────────────────
   Stem bast fiber, 5–55 mm
   High cellulose, low lignin
   Industrial hemp production expanding (EU, N. America)
   Archival paper, specialty applications

   SISAL (Agave sisalana):
   ───────────────────────
   Leaf fiber, very long (1–1.5 m)
   Cut to papermaking length
   Tea bag paper, filter papers, specialty

   ESPARTO (Stipa tenacissima):
   ──────────────────────────────
   North African grass, historically important for British printing paper
   Fine, uniform short fibers — excellent for book paper
   Replaced by wood pulp since ~1970s
```

## Recycled Fiber

### Global Recycling Rates

```
   PAPER RECYCLING RATE BY REGION (2022 est.):
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────
   Germany: ~80%     Japan: ~83%     EU avg: ~72%
   USA: ~68%         China: ~49%     Global avg: ~58%
```

### Recovered Paper Grades

```
   GRADE 1: OCC (old corrugated containers)
   ─────────────────────────────────────────
   Most valuable, high volume
   Used for: testliner, corrugating medium, kraftliner
   Key property: fiber still relatively long and strong
   Used in: sack paper, liner board

   GRADE 2: Sorted Office Paper (SOP) / White Ledger
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
   High-grade white papers from offices
   Excellent brightness feedstock
   Used for: tissue, printing paper with deinking

   GRADE 3: Old Newspapers / ONP
   ─────────────────────────────
   Newsprint, magazines
   Shorter fibers, more contaminated ink
   Used for: newsprint with deinking, tissue

   GRADE 4: Mixed Paper
   ─────────────────────
   Mixed office and household
   Lowest value, highly variable
   Used: low-grade board, filling materials
```

### Fiber Shortening and Hornification

```
   VIRGIN SOFTWOOD KRAFT:      RECYCLED (multiple cycles):
   ──────────────────────       ─────────────────────────
   Fiber length: 2–4 mm         Fiber length: 0.6–1.5 mm (shortened)
   Flexibility: high            Flexibility: reduced (hornified)
   Swelling: high               Swelling: reduced
   Bonding: excellent           Bonding: reduced
   Drainability (CSF): 600+     Drainability (CSF): 350–450

   HORNIFICATION: irreversible fiber cell wall collapse on drying.
   Hydroxyl groups form inter-chain bonds → less re-swelling on re-wetting
   Each cycle: fibers shorten, hornify, lose bonding potential
   Practical limit: ~5–7 cycles before fiber too short/stiff
   → Requires virgin fiber addition to maintain product quality
```

### Deinking

Printed paper must be deinked before use in white paper grades.

```
   DEINKING PROCESS:
   ──────────────────
   1. PULPING: hot water (50–70°C) + NaOH + H2O2 + soap
      → disperses fibers, detaches ink particles

   2. SCREENING/CLEANING: remove staples, plastics, oversized particles

   3. FLOTATION: air bubbles + collector chemical (soap, fatty acid)
      → ink particles attach to bubbles → float off as froth
      → removes printing ink efficiently (offset, laser toner)

   4. WASHING: countercurrent washing removes fines and fillers

   5. BLEACHING: H2O2 or reducing agents → brightness increase

   FLOTATION vs. WASHING:
   ──────────────────────
   Flotation: removes ink but retains fiber fines → higher yield
   Washing: removes ink + fines → cleaner pulp, lower yield
   Most mills use both in sequence
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Need | Fiber choice |
|------|-------------|
| Maximum strength (sacks, liner) | Unbleached softwood kraft (SW-UKP) |
| Smooth bright printing surface | Bleached hardwood kraft (HW-BKP) |
| Tissue / softness priority | Short-fiber HW kraft (eucalyptus) |
| Banknotes, security documents | Cotton linter/rag + bast fiber blends |
| Low cost, moderate quality | OCC recycled or mechanical pulp |
| Archival / acid-free | Cotton linter or bleached SW kraft, alkaline sized |
| Cigarette paper | Flax/hemp specialty pulp |
| Maximum tensile (filter, tea bag) | Cotton or sisal specialty |

## Common Confusion Points

**Fiber length vs. fiber strength**: Long fibers give better tear resistance.
Short, collapsed fibers give better tensile (more contact = more hydrogen bonds).
Tissue is strong in tension despite short eucalyptus fibers because the fibers
collapse flat, maximizing contact area.

**Mechanical pulp vs. chemical pulp yield**: Mechanical (90–95%) vs. kraft
(45–50%). The "lost" 50% in kraft is lignin and some hemicellulose, extracted
into the black liquor. This is not waste — it's burned in the recovery boiler
for energy and chemical regeneration.

**"Tree-free" paper still has a carbon footprint**: Bamboo, kenaf, straw —
all require energy to pulp, bleach, and form. Agricultural fiber papers often
have chemical recovery challenges (straw silica) and may have higher water
use and chemical consumption than optimized wood kraft mills.
