# 01 — International Relations Theory

## Realism, Liberalism, Constructivism, English School

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Classical Realism (Morgenthau): human nature as power-seeking, national interest defined in terms of power, statesmen as managers of power; Politics Among Nations; statecraft vs morality
- Structural/Neorealism (Waltz): anarchy as systemic condition → self-help → power balancing; Theory of International Politics (1979); unit-level behavior explained by system structure; bipolar vs multipolar stability debate
- Offensive Realism (Mearsheimer): states maximize power (not just security), great powers seek regional hegemony, USA as offshore balancer, tragedy of great power politics; China rise as inherently destabilizing
- Security dilemma: state A arms for defense → state B perceives threat → arms → A less secure; offense-defense balance affects severity; Herz's concept (1951); nuclear weapons as stabilizing (make offense futile)
- Neoliberal Institutionalism (Keohane/Nye): institutions mitigate anarchy (provide information/reduce cheating/increase iteration); After Hegemony; complex interdependence; international regimes
- Democratic Peace Theory: Kant's perpetual peace → Doyle's empirical claim → Russett's evidence; causal mechanisms debate (institutional/normative/economic); critiques (selection, Gibler)
- Constructivism (Wendt): "Anarchy is what states make of it" (1992); ideational factors (norms/identities/culture) shape interests, not just material conditions; norm life cycle (emergence/cascade/internalization)
- English School (Bull/Wight/Watson): international society (not just system), shared norms/institutions/rules, pluralist vs solidarist society, humanitarian intervention tension with sovereignty
- Power transition theory: hegemonic stability, power transition war risk when rising power approaches hegemon parity; Thucydides Trap (Allison) — applied to US/China
- Current theoretical landscape: post-Cold War triumphalism → 9/11 → 2008 → Ukraine/Taiwan debates; theory testing vs theory development; foreign policy analysis as bridge
