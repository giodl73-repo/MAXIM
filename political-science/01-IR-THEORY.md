# International Relations Theory

## The Big Picture

IR theory is a family of competing frameworks that disagree on a central question:
what causes state behavior? Material power vs. institutions vs. ideas — and how much
does each variable explain? Each theory highlights a different causal mechanism.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    IR THEORY FAMILY TREE                              |
|                                                                      |
|  REALISM                LIBERALISM            CONSTRUCTIVISM         |
|  ───────                ─────────             ─────────────          |
```

## Section 1: Realism

### Classical Realism — Morgenthau

```
  MORGENTHAU: Politics Among Nations (1948)

  Central insight: human nature is power-seeking; politics is a struggle for power
  States pursue national interest defined in terms of power
  Moral principles cannot be applied to political action without distortion
  Statesmen must be prudent, not principled

  Key concepts:
  - National interest: what's good for the nation as a political unit
  - Balance of power: states check each other's power through alliances
  - Prudence: cardinal virtue of statecraft (know limits)
  - Tragedy: peace requires managing, not eliminating, power competition

  Critique: too focused on human nature → doesn't explain variation
  Why do some periods see more cooperation than others?
  Waltz's response: look at structure, not human nature
```

### Structural / Neorealism — Waltz

```
  WALTZ: Theory of International Politics (1979)

  The key structural insight: anarchy (absence of world government) forces states
  to provide for their own security — regardless of their intentions

  STRUCTURE → BEHAVIOR:

  Anarchy
    |
    v
  States must ensure own survival (no one else will)
    |
    v
  Self-help: states balance against threats, not bandwagon
    |
    v
  Security dilemma: arming for defense looks threatening to others
    |
    v
  Arms races, war, suboptimal outcomes EVEN when no state wants them

  POLARITY DEBATE:
  Waltz: Bipolarity > Multipolarity (fewer miscalculations, clearer deterrence)
  US-USSR cold war → stable despite risk; WWI multipolarity → catastrophe

  Critics: Cold War stability → nuclear weapons, not bipolarity per se
           Post-Cold War unipolarity actually quite stable

  UNIT-LEVEL HOMOGENEITY ASSUMPTION:
  All states are "like units" (security-seekers); domestic differences don't matter
  → leads to neoliberal critique: regime type matters!
```

### Offensive Realism — Mearsheimer

```
  MEARSHEIMER: The Tragedy of Great Power Politics (2001)

  Key departure from Waltz: states don't just seek security — they maximize power
  Relative power is what matters; regional hegemony is the goal

  Why maximize power?
  - Cannot know other states' intentions with certainty
  - States with greater power are safer → always want more
  - The logical endpoint: regional hegemony (no rival in your hemisphere)
  - US is the only genuine regional hegemon in history

  OFFSHORE BALANCING:
  Regional hegemons don't want others to become regional hegemons
  → Intervene to prevent it (not to spread values)
  US interventions: both World Wars, Cold War = preventing Eurasian hegemony

  CHINA IMPLICATIONS:
  China's rise is inherently destabilizing (same logic as all rising powers)
  US will try to contain China (as peer reviewed competing hegemon)
  China will try to dominate Asia (regional hegemony goal)
  Conflict probable — not because of bad intentions but structural logic
  "Tragedy": unavoidable even if both sides prefer peace

  Critiques: security maximization ≠ power maximization; misses nuclear effects;
             states regularly balance less than predicted
```

### The Security Dilemma

```
  HERZ (1951) → Jervis (1978) formal development

  State A builds defensive military capability
            |
            v
  State B cannot distinguish offensive from defensive
            |
            v
  State B perceives threat → arms in response
            |
            v
  State A less secure than before arming

  OFFENSE-DEFENSE BALANCE:
  If offense > defense (tanks crossing open plains):
    → Security dilemma severe (arming is provocative)
    → War more likely
  If defense > offense (rivers, mountains, nuclear weapons):
    → Dilemma mitigated (arming less threatening)
    → War less likely

  NUCLEAR WEAPONS AS STABILIZER:
  Secure second-strike capability → defense clearly dominant
  → Aggressor cannot prevent catastrophic retaliation
  → Security dilemma largely resolved (for nuclear states)
  → This is the logic of MAD stability (see 02-NUCLEAR-DETERRENCE)
```

## Section 2: Liberalism

### Neoliberal Institutionalism — Keohane

```
  KEOHANE: After Hegemony (1984)

  Starting point: accepts anarchy but asks — why do states cooperate anyway?

  ANARCHY DOESN'T PREVENT COOPERATION:
  Problem 1: Cheating (defection in Prisoners Dilemma)
  Problem 2: Information asymmetry (don't know other's intentions)
  Problem 3: Short time horizons

  INSTITUTIONS SOLVE THESE BY:
  ├── Providing information (transparency, monitoring)
  ├── Reducing transaction costs (established procedures)
  ├── Making defection costly (reputation mechanisms)
  ├── Lengthening shadow of the future (iteration)
  └── Embedding states in compliance expectations (lock-in)

  KEY INSIGHT: Regimes and institutions can persist even after the hegemon
  that created them declines (US-created institutions outlasted US hegemony peak)

  COMPLEX INTERDEPENDENCE (Keohane & Nye, 1977):
  Real world ≠ realist model
  - Multiple channels: state, society, multinational firms
  - No hierarchy of issues: economics as important as security
  - Military force less useful for many transactions
  When conditions hold → liberal predictions better than realist
```

### Democratic Peace Theory

```
  KANT (1795): Three conditions for "Perpetual Peace"
  1. Republican constitutions (citizens bear war costs → averse to war)
  2. Federation of free states (collective security institutions)
  3. Universal hospitality / cosmopolitan rights (commerce and openness)

  DOYLE (1983): Kant was right empirically
  Liberal democracies have not fought each other in modern history
  (defining "war" as >1000 battle deaths)

  RUSSETT & ONEAL (1997-2001): "Kantian Triangle"
  Each factor independently reduces conflict:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │       DEMOCRACY ────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
  │              |                                           |   │
  │              v                                           |   │
  │         Institutional                                    |   │
  │         accountability                                   |   │
  │              |                              REDUCED      |   │
  │       TRADE  v                              CONFLICT ←───┘   │
  │        |──> Mutual                                       │   │
  │        |    stakes                                       │   │
  │  INTERGOVERNMENTAL                                       │   │
  │  ORGANIZATIONS ──> Information + iteration               │   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CAUSAL MECHANISMS (which explains the pattern?):
  Institutional: democratic accountability makes leaders cautious
  Normative: democracies share norms against violence with peers
  Economic: trade creates mutual stakes against conflict
  Selection: democracies choose wars carefully (win rates are high)

  SCOPE CONDITION: Between democracies only
  Democracies vs. autocracies: no peace effect; democracies fight non-democracies frequently
```

## Section 3: Constructivism

### Wendt — "Anarchy Is What States Make of It" (1992)

```
  The foundational constructivist challenge to realism:

  REALIST CLAIM: Anarchy → self-help → security competition

  WENDT'S CHALLENGE:
  Anarchy is a structural condition, but it doesn't have one meaning
  States INTERPRET anarchy and construct identities/interests from it
  The "logic of anarchy" depends on the culture of that anarchy

  THREE CULTURES OF ANARCHY:
  ┌─────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Hobbesian       │ Enemies: treat each other as existential     │
  │                 │ threats; no limits on violence                │
  │                 │ (pre-state of nature, some IR subsystems)    │
  ├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Lockean         │ Rivals: compete but accept right to exist;   │
  │                 │ don't try to eliminate each other            │
  │                 │ (most current international relations)       │
  ├─────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Kantian         │ Friends: collective security, help each other │
  │                 │ against threats from outside the community   │
  │                 │ (NATO allies, EU members)                   │
  └─────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  IMPLICATION: Change is possible by changing the culture, not just power
  Material power is real; identity and norms are also real
```

### Norm Life Cycle (Finnemore & Sikkink, 1998)

```
  HOW NORMS EMERGE AND SPREAD:

  Phase 1: NORM EMERGENCE
  Norm entrepreneurs (states, IGOs, NGOs) advocate for new norm
  "Framing" — connect new norm to existing values
  Example: anti-slavery (Quakers → abolitionist states → then major powers)

  Phase 2: NORM CASCADE
  Norm crosses "tipping point" → rapid adoption by states
  Socialization and legitimation pressure ("naming and shaming")
  Not just material incentives — social pressure
  Example: landmines ban, chemical weapons norm

  Phase 3: NORM INTERNALIZATION
  Norm becomes taken-for-granted; no longer needs active promotion
  States follow automatically (why colonialism is gone as legitimate option)

  LIMITS: Norms can erode; great powers can violate norms at lower cost
  Russia 2022 violated territorial integrity norm → testing norm strength
```

## Section 4: English School

```
  BULL: The Anarchical Society (1977)

  Distinguishes:
  SYSTEM: states interact; effects are regular (realism)
  SOCIETY: states share rules, norms, institutions; expectations converge

  International society exists: "system of states" became "society of states"
  Common values: sovereignty, non-intervention, diplomatic norms, war limits

  PLURALIST vs SOLIDARIST:

  Pluralist (Bull): sovereignty and non-intervention primary
  States can hold different values; legitimacy through co-existence
  Humanitarian intervention = violation of international society norms

  Solidarist (Wheeler, later English School):
  States share deeper moral obligations across borders
  R2P (Responsibility to Protect) is consistent with international society
  Mass atrocities forfeit sovereign protection

  PRACTICAL RELEVANCE:
  Every humanitarian intervention debate is a pluralist-solidarist argument
  Kosovo (1999), Libya (2011): solidarist justification
  Iraq (2003): undermined English School framework
  Ukraine (2022): tested Westphalian sovereignty norms
```

## Section 5: Power Transition Theory

```
  HEGEMONIC STABILITY (Kindleberger/Gilpin):
  Stable international order requires a dominant power (hegemon) to provide
  public goods: open trade, stable currency, collective security
  US as post-WWII hegemon; Pax Americana; Keohane: "after hegemony" what?

  POWER TRANSITION (Organski/Kugler):
  War most likely when challenger approaches parity with dominant power
  Reason: dominant power resists decline; challenger demands redistribution
  Preventive war temptation: strike before challenger becomes peer

  THUCYDIDES TRAP (Allison, 2017):
  12 of 16 historical cases of rising power challenging dominant power = war
  Athens/Sparta in Peloponnesian War as archetype
  Modern application: US/China

  CRITICAL EVALUATION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Supporting: Germany/UK (WWI), Japan/US (WWII), Athens/Sparta   │
  │ Contradicting: US/UK late 19th century (peaceful transition)   │
  │                Soviet collapse without war                     │
  │ Key variable: nuclear weapons may have broken the historical   │
  │               pattern (nuclear great powers have not fought)   │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Waltz vs Mearsheimer**: both are neorealists but disagree on what states maximize.
Waltz: security (maintain position). Mearsheimer: power (maximize relative power).
The distinction matters for predictions: Waltz expects status quo behavior; Mearsheimer
expects expansionist behavior when opportunity permits.

**Constructivism is not idealism**: constructivists don't say ideas alone matter or
that goodwill can fix everything. They say interests are not given by material structure
alone — they're socially constructed. Power still matters; it just doesn't determine
interests by itself.

**English School is not liberalism**: English School accepts anarchy and the central
role of states. It doesn't expect institutions to overcome power politics. It identifies
a different mechanism: shared norms and a sense of community among states.

**Democratic peace is not "democracies are peaceful"**: democracies fight wars frequently.
The claim is specifically dyadic — pairs of democracies rarely fight each other.
The US-led "liberal order" has involved near-constant warfare against non-democracies.
