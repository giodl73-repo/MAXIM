# Geopolitics

## The Big Picture

Geopolitics examines how geography shapes power and strategy. The classical theorists
mapped the fundamental competitive logic of land power vs. sea power that still
structures great-power competition today — though technology has complicated but not
eliminated geographic constraints.

**Formal bridge:** Geographic constraints on power projection are structurally identical
to network topology constraints on system performance. Physical distance and terrain
impose latency and bandwidth limits on the delivery of force, just as network topology
imposes latency and bandwidth limits on message delivery. Chokepoints (Strait of Hormuz,
Malacca) are single points of failure in a network graph — high betweenness centrality
nodes whose disruption maximally degrades throughput. The heartland/rimland debate is
a graph theory argument about whether controlling the interior node or the perimeter
nodes gives higher leverage. Even the CAP-like trilemma appears in geopolitics: states
face tradeoffs between power projection range, local concentration, and sustainability
of extended commitments — you generally cannot simultaneously maximize all three.

## Section 1: Classical Geopolitical Theories

### Mackinder's Heartland (1904, revised 1919, 1943)

```
  "The Geographical Pivot of History" — Royal Geographical Society, 1904

  CORE ARGUMENT:
  The "pivot area" (Eurasia's heartland) is inaccessible to sea power
  → A land power dominating it could eventually dominate the world
  → Britain's historical advantage (sea power, circumnavigation) was ending
  → Railways enabled continental land power to project force like sea power

  THE FORMULA:
  "Who rules Eastern Europe commands the Heartland.
   Who rules the Heartland commands the World-Island.
   Who rules the World-Island commands the World."
  (World-Island = Eurasia + Africa combined land mass)

  1919 REVISION: After WWI, Mackinder saw Germany as the danger
  1943 REVISION: Midland Ocean (North Atlantic) + Midland Basin as counter

  MODERN RELEVANCE:
  - Russia remains the Heartland power → same containment logic
  - Ukraine as "gateway to the Heartland" — still contested 2022+
  - China's BRI reaching into Central Asia → Mackinderian ambition?
  - Critique: air power, nuclear weapons, satellites transcend geography
```

### Mahan's Sea Power (1890)

```
  MAHAN: The Influence of Sea Power upon History (1890)

  ARGUMENT: National greatness flows from sea power
  Sea power = commercial shipping + naval power + overseas bases
  Prerequisites for sea power: coastlines, natural harbors, naval tradition,
                               merchant marine, government will

  MAHAN'S INFLUENCE:
  - Theodore Roosevelt: built US Navy as great power instrument
  - Germany's Tirpitz: Mahanian thinking → naval arms race with UK → WWI
  - Japan: 1941 decision to expand Pacific → Mahanian strategy
  - China: PLAN expansion, island chains, Djibouti base → Mahanian playbook

  CHOKEPOINTS (Mahanian):
  Control these → control maritime trade
  (detailed below in Section 4)

  ISLAND CHAIN STRATEGY (US/Japan):
  First Island Chain: Japan → Taiwan → Philippines → US denial strategy
  Second Island Chain: Guam → Marianas → projection capability
  China's strategy: pierce the first island chain
```

### Spykman's Rimland (1944)

```
  SPYKMAN: The Geography of the Peace (1944)

  CRITIQUE OF MACKINDER:
  The rimlands (coastal zones of Eurasia) are more important than the heartland
  Why? They have the agricultural + industrial + population concentrations
  The heartland is vast but underpopulated and mostly harsh terrain

  "Who controls the Rimland rules Eurasia.
   Who rules Eurasia controls the destinies of the world."

  POLICY IMPLICATION: Containment
  → George Kennan's containment strategy (1946–1947) is Spykmanian
  → NATO (Western European rimland), Japan/Korea alliance (East Asian rimland)
  → Vietnam, Korea as contests for rimland control

  IRONY: Containment was sold as preventing Soviet hegemony;
  Spykmanian logic explains WHY those specific territories mattered
```

### Brzezinski's Grand Chessboard (1997)

```
  BRZEZINSKI: The Grand Chessboard (1997)

  Written after Cold War: how should US manage its unipolar moment?
  US = first truly global power; Eurasia = central arena

  EURASIAN PIVOT STATES (those that could tip the balance):
  Ukraine, Azerbaijan, South Korea, Turkey, Iran
  "If the United States is to remain the world's chief geopolitician,
   it must ensure that no single power or group of powers dominates Eurasia"

  UKRAINE AS PIVOTAL:
  "Ukraine, a new and important space on the Eurasian chessboard, is a
   geopolitical pivot because its very existence as an independent country
   helps to transform Russia... Russia without Ukraine ceases to be a
   Eurasian empire."
  → Putin's 2022 invasion vindicates Brzezinski's 1997 assessment

  "EURASIAN BALKANS": Central Asia + Caucasus
  Oil, instability, competing influences, ethnic conflicts
  The prize for BRI, Russian near-abroad, and US presence
```

## Section 3: Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)

```
  SCALE (2013–2025):
  ~150 countries signed MoU; $1+ trillion in loans/investments
  Infrastructure: ports, rails, roads, pipelines, fiber
  Sectors: transport, energy, digital (digital Silk Road)

  GEOPOLITICAL INTERPRETATION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ CHINA'S STATED GOALS:       STRATEGIC CRITICS' INTERPRETATION:│
  │ South-South cooperation     Create debt-dependent clients       │
  │ Mutual development          Build military logistics network    │
  │ Connectivity                Encirclement of India               │
  │ Trade facilitation          Dollar-alternative financial system │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  DEBT-TRAP DIPLOMACY DEBATE:
  Hambantota Port (Sri Lanka): 99-year lease to China after debt default (2017)
  → Widely cited as proof of debt-trap diplomacy
  Counter-argument (Brautigam): countries often willingly over-borrow;
    China rarely actually seizes assets; geopolitical leverage through
    economic dependence doesn't require formal control

  BRI FAILURES:
  Malaysia, Myanmar, Pakistan: renegotiated/cancelled projects
  COVID + rising US interest rates: developing country debt stress
  "BRI 2.0": smaller, more selective projects since 2020

  COUNTER-BRI INITIATIVES:
  US Blue Dot Network, G7 PGII (Partnership for Global Infrastructure)
  EU Global Gateway
  Limited success: private capital doesn't fund infrastructure in risky markets
```

### Indo-Pacific Strategy

```
  CONCEPT EVOLUTION:
  "Asia-Pacific" (Cold War) → "Indo-Pacific" (post-2017)
  Inclusion of Indian Ocean = includes India as strategic partner
  US INDOPACOM: renamed 2018 (from PACOM)

  QUAD (Quadrilateral Security Dialogue):
  US + Japan + Australia + India
  Revived 2017 after decade dormancy (founded 2007)
  Not a formal alliance: no mutual defense obligations
  Focus: supply chains, technology, infrastructure, humanitarian
  India's caveat: won't commit to direct China confrontation

  AUKUS (2021):
  US + UK + Australia
  Nuclear-powered submarine technology transfer to Australia
  (SSNs, not SSBNs — nuclear propulsion, conventional weapons)
  Major diplomatic incident: replaced French conventional sub contract
  Directly aimed at Pacific deterrence

  TAIWAN STRAIT:
  China claims Taiwan (One China policy) → PRC
  Taiwan operates as independent democracy (ROC) since 1949
  US: "strategic ambiguity" — sell arms, no formal commitment
  Xi's stated timeline: unification goal before 2049 (China's centennial)
  Risk calculus: cross-strait amphibious assault vs US intervention vs nuclear escalation
  TSMC factor: ~90% of advanced chips → semiconductors as deterrent

  SOUTH CHINA SEA:
  China's 9-dash line: claims ~90% of SCS (rejected by UNCLOS tribunal 2016)
  Artificial island-building: Spratly/Paracel reefs → military bases
  US FONOPs (Freedom of Navigation Operations): challenge territorial claims
  Claimants: Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, Taiwan
```

## Section 5: Arctic Competition

```
  WHY ARCTIC NOW:
  - Ice-free Arctic summers by 2040s (some estimates earlier)
  - Northern Sea Route (NSR): Europe-Asia via Arctic Ocean
    NSR: 35% shorter than Suez route (Hamburg-Yokohama ~23,000 km → ~15,000 km)
  - Resources: ~13% undiscovered oil, ~30% gas under Arctic seabed
  - Military: new domain for submarines, missiles, surveillance

  KEY ACTORS:
  Russia: Arctic frontage > all other Arctic nations combined;
          Murmansk nuclear submarine base; 40+ icebreakers (US: 2)
          NSR as "Russian territorial sea" (disputed by IMO)
  Norway/Denmark/Canada: sovereign claims, Coast Guard presence
  US: limited icebreaker capacity; McMurdo, Thule AFB
  China: "Near-Arctic State" (self-designation); invested in NSR access

  LEGAL FRAMEWORK:
  UNCLOS Article 76: extended continental shelf beyond 200nm EEZ
  Russia, Denmark, Canada all submitted overlapping continental shelf claims
  Arctic Council: scientific cooperation; Canada, Denmark, Finland, Iceland,
                 Norway, Russia, Sweden, US; Russia suspended post-2022

  MILITARY BUILDUP:
  Russia reopened Soviet-era Arctic bases; Novaya Zemlya, Franz Josef Land
  New Arctic brigades; hypersonic missile deployment
  NATO response: Norway GIUK gap surveillance, increased Arctic exercises
```

## Section 6: Sanctions as Geopolitical Tool

```
  SANCTIONS EVOLUTION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1990s: COMPREHENSIVE sanctions (Cuba, Iraq, Iran)              │
  │ Total import/export bans; hurt civilian population             │
  │ Result: humanitarian disasters; didn't change regime behavior  │
  │                                                                │
  │ 2000s: SMART/TARGETED sanctions                                │
  │ Individual-level (oligarchs, officials), asset freeze/travel ban│
  │ Less humanitarian damage; still limited effectiveness          │
  │                                                                │
  │ 2010s: FINANCIAL WARFARE                                       │
  │ SWIFT exclusion (Iran 2012, Russia 2022)                       │
  │ Correspondent banking restrictions                             │
  │ Dollar clearing denial                                         │
  │ Export controls (technology denial)                            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  RUSSIA 2022 CASE:
  Largest sanctions package in history:
  - 7 Russian banks excluded from SWIFT
  - Central Bank reserves frozen (~$300B)
  - Export controls: semiconductors, aviation, dual-use
  - G7 oil price cap ($60/barrel for maritime insurance/services)
  - Individual oligarch asset freezes

  RESULT: Russia's economy contracted ~2.5% in 2022 (less than expected),
  then grew ~3-4% in 2023-2024 (war economy + China trade + sanctions circumvention)
  Military production increased despite sanctions

  DE-DOLLARIZATION COUNTER-RESPONSE:
  Russia/China: trade in CNY and RUB
  BRI payment mechanisms
  mBridge (central bank digital currencies project: China, UAE, HK, Thailand)
  Saudi Arabia accepting non-dollar oil payment discussions
  Pace is slow (dollar share in reserves: ~60%, down from 72% in 2000)

  EXPORT CONTROLS — CHIPS:
  US October 2022 rules: block export of advanced chips + chipmaking equipment to China
  Huawei H20 chip restrictions 2024
  Dutch ASML: EUV lithography machine export ban extended
  Effect: China accelerating domestic chip development (SMIC, HiSilicon)
  → Partial success at slowing; not stopping China's capability development
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Mackinder was wrong about land power dominance**: nuclear weapons, air power, and
space-based ISR changed the calculus. The Heartland is no longer strategically unreachable
by sea powers. But the core insight — that Eurasia's geographic mass is the central
prize of great-power competition — remains structurally valid.

**BRI is not monolithic**: the Belt and Road Initiative has thousands of individual
projects across 150 countries with widely varying terms, quality, and strategic intent.
Some are genuinely developmental. Some are clearly strategic (Djibouti base). Many are
driven by Chinese state banks needing to deploy capital. Treating it as one unified
strategic plan overstates Chinese coherence.

**Strategic ambiguity on Taiwan is deliberate**: the US does not explicitly commit to
defend Taiwan. This is a feature, not a bug — it deters Chinese attack (they can't be
certain of non-intervention) and deters Taiwanese independence declarations (they can't
count on unconditional support). "Ambiguity" is a policy tool, not confusion.

**Sanctions effectiveness is conditional**: sanctions work best when multilateral,
when target has limited trade alternatives, and when paired with a clear off-ramp. Unilateral
US sanctions often fail because other countries continue trading. Russia's sanctions
resistance was enabled by China trade and war economy production shift.
