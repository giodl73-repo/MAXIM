# Political Science — Overview

## The Big Picture

Political science is the systematic study of power: who gets what, when, and how.
Its subfields differ more in scope than in method — all share an interest in how
actors pursue interests under constraints, often in the presence of conflict.

## Section 1: Levels of Analysis

Kenneth Waltz's three images framework — the most useful organizing device in IR:

```
  LEVEL 1 — INDIVIDUAL
  Leader psychology, perceptions, misperceptions, cognitive biases
  Explains: Hitler's expansionism, Gorbachev's reforms, miscalculation
  Critique: hard to generalize; cherry-picks leaders post-hoc

  LEVEL 2 — STATE/DOMESTIC
  Regime type, domestic politics, interest groups, bureaucracy
  Explains: democracies don't fight democracies, diversionary war,
            two-level games (Putnam), bureaucratic politics (Allison)
  Tools: selectorate theory, legislative veto players, rally-round-flag effects

  LEVEL 3 — SYSTEMIC
  Structure of international system — distribution of power, anarchy
  Explains: balance of power, polarity effects, hegemonic cycles
  Mechanism: anarchy → self-help → security dilemma → war as rational outcome
  Strongest claim: same systemic structure → similar behavior regardless of regime

  WHICH LEVEL WHEN:
  Puzzle: Why did WWI happen? All three:
    L1: Kaiser Wilhelm's personality/miscalculation
    L2: German domestic politics, mobilization schedule constraints
    L3: multipolarity, alliance rigidity, power transition
```

## Section 3: Power — Types and Instruments

```
  HARD POWER:
  Coercive — military force + economic sanctions + denial
  "Get others to do what they would not otherwise do through coercion"
  Instruments: armies, navies, export controls, blockades

  SOFT POWER (Nye, 1990):
  Attraction — values, culture, legitimacy, narrative
  "Get others to want what you want" through co-optation
  US instruments: Hollywood, universities, democracy brand
  China soft power: Confucius Institutes, development lending
  Critique: hard to measure, often overestimated by proponents

  SMART POWER:
  Combination of hard and soft; context-dependent instrument selection
  Clinton State Department doctrine; not a theory, a prescription

  SHARP POWER:
  Authoritarian influence operations using democratic openness
  Disinformation, elite capture, media infiltration
  Distinct from soft power: covert, manipulative rather than attractive

  ECONOMIC POWER:
  Market size → leverage (EU market access as bargaining chip)
  Financial dominance → coercion (dollar clearing, SWIFT)
  Technology dependence → chokepoints (semiconductors, rare earths)
```

## Section 4: The State

```
  WEBERIAN DEFINITION:
  "A human community that (successfully) claims the monopoly
  of the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory"
  — Max Weber, Economy and Society

  Three elements:
  1. Monopoly on force (de facto control)
  2. Legitimacy (accepted as rightful, not just coerced)
  3. Territory (bounded spatial jurisdiction)

  WESTPHALIAN SOVEREIGNTY (1648):
  Peace of Westphalia ended Thirty Years War
  Established: non-interference in internal affairs
  Principle: cuius regio, eius religio → extended to all state affairs
  Modern version: territorial integrity + legal equality of states
  Erosion: humanitarian intervention, R2P, ICC jurisdiction

  STATE STRENGTH SPECTRUM:
  Strong state ←────────────────────────→ Failed state
  (Scandinavia)                           (Somalia, Libya)
  Full bureaucratic    Partial control    No monopoly on force
  capacity, rule of    contested          Competing warlords
  law, tax capacity    legitimacy         No public services

  FAILED STATES vs WEAK STATES:
  Failed: literally no government with territorial control
  Weak: government exists but lacks capacity/legitimacy
  Most "failed state" problems = weak state capacity + legitimacy deficit
```

## Section 5: Democratic Peace Theory

The most robust empirical finding in IR — and the most debated:

```
  CLAIM: Democracies rarely (or never) go to war with each other

  INTELLECTUAL LINEAGE:
  Kant (1795) → "Perpetual Peace" — three conditions for peace:
    1. Republican constitutions → citizens bear war costs → want peace
    2. Federalism among free states (international institutions)
    3. Universal hospitality (open borders for commerce)

  Doyle (1983) → empirical claim: no wars between liberal democracies
  Russett & Oneal (1997) → "Kantian Triangle" — democracy + trade + institutions

  CAUSAL MECHANISMS DEBATE:
  Institutional: democratic accountability → leaders punished for war
  Normative: democracies internalize norms against violence with each other
  Economic: shared economic interests reduce war incentives
  Selection: democracies select more carefully into wars they think they'll win

  CRITIQUES:
  - Few democracies existed before 1900 → small N problem
  - Definition of "democracy" and "war" drives results
  - Selection effect: democracies in alliances → don't fight each other anyway
  - Democracies do fight non-democracies frequently
  - Kant's actual claim was about republics, not modern liberal democracies

  POLICY IMPLICATION: Democracy promotion as security strategy
  (Clinton/Bush/Obama doctrines) — controversial record
```

## Section 6: Methodology

```
  POSITIVIST vs INTERPRETIVIST:
  Positivist: social science can be like natural science; causal laws; prediction
  Interpretivist: meaning is constituted, not caused; thick description; understanding

  QUALITATIVE vs QUANTITATIVE:
  Case study    → process tracing, within-case causal inference, generalizability ↓
  Comparative   → MSSD/MDSD designs, controlled comparison
  Regression    → large-N, average effects, but identification problems
  Experiments   → RCT gold standard; field experiments in developing world
  Formal models → game theory, rational choice — precise but limited scope

  THE CREDIBILITY REVOLUTION:
  Parallel to economics: field experiments, natural experiments, RDD, DiD
  Key journals: APSR, AJPS, JOP → increasingly require pre-registration
  Problem: external validity vs internal validity tradeoff
  Legacy: vote for theoretical progress vs causal identification tradeoff ongoing

  PROCESS TRACING:
  Within-case method: traces causal mechanism from cause to effect
  Tests observable implications of proposed mechanism
  Distinguishes between competing causal stories
  Combines well with comparative case selection
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Anarchy ≠ chaos**: anarchy in IR means "no world government" — no authority above states.
The international system is anarchic in structure. This is not the same as disorder;
states create order through norms, treaties, and institutions within the anarchic structure.

**Realism ≠ pessimism**: realism is a structural theory claiming states must act to
maximize security under anarchy. It doesn't say war is inevitable or desirable — just
that the structure creates strong incentives for certain behaviors.

**Soft power ≠ propaganda**: propaganda is coercive information manipulation. Soft power
works through genuine attraction — others want what you have. Propaganda can undermine
soft power by creating cynicism about the source.

**Democratic peace ≠ democracies are peaceful**: democracies go to war frequently with
non-democracies. The claim is narrow: pairs of democracies almost never fight each other.
The US has been at war almost continuously since 1945 — with non-democracies.
