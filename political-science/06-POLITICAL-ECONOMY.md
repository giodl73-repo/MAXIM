# Political Economy

## The Big Picture

Political economy is where the logic of markets meets the logic of power. Trade creates
winners and losers within countries, not just between them. Financial flows cross borders
but face political resistance. Development paths depend on state capacity and policy
choices, not just comparative advantage. Understanding IPE (International Political Economy)
means holding economic and political logic simultaneously.

## Section 1: Trade Theory and Its Political Limits

### Classical and Neoclassical Trade Theory

```
  COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGE (Ricardo, 1817):
  Even if Country A is absolutely better at producing both goods X and Y,
  both countries gain by specializing in the good where they have comparative
  (relative) advantage and trading.

  Example:
  England: 100 hrs for cloth, 120 hrs for wine
  Portugal: 90 hrs for cloth, 80 hrs for wine
  Portugal has absolute advantage in BOTH
  But England's comparative advantage: cloth (ratio 100/120 vs 90/80)
  → England specializes in cloth, Portugal in wine → both gain

  HECKSCHER-OHLIN: Trade reflects factor endowments
  Capital-abundant countries export capital-intensive goods
  Labor-abundant countries export labor-intensive goods
  → Explains North-South trade pattern (in theory)

  STOLPER-SAMUELSON: Trade affects FACTOR DISTRIBUTION
  Trade = imports hurt the SCARCE factor domestically
  US imports labor-intensive goods → US unskilled labor hurt
  → Trade produces domestic losers → political resistance is rational
  → This is the distributional politics of globalization

  NEW TRADE THEORY (Krugman, 1979-1991):
  Scale economies + imperfect competition → intra-industry trade
  Germany and France both export cars to each other — why?
  → Specialization in varieties; scale economies allow diverse niche production
  → Path-dependence matters: who got there first matters
  → Strategic trade policy might make sense in some industries
```

### Political Constraints on Free Trade

```
  WHY FREE TRADE IS POLITICALLY DIFFICULT:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ CONCENTRATED LOSSES vs DIFFUSE GAINS                           │
  │ Gains from trade: small benefit per consumer ($50/yr)         │
  │ Losses from trade: large harm to specific industry workers     │
  │ → Industry workers organize, lobby; consumers don't           │
  │ → Political economy biased toward protection                  │
  │                                                                │
  │ INFANT INDUSTRY ARGUMENT (Hamilton/List):                      │
  │ New industries need protection to develop economies of scale  │
  │ before facing established foreign competition                  │
  │ East Asian developmental states used this successfully        │
  │ (Korea autos, Taiwan electronics, Japan steel)                 │
  │ Counter: how do you know which infant will grow up?           │
  │ Government may pick losers; protected industries may never grow│
  │                                                                │
  │ STRATEGIC TRADE POLICY:                                        │
  │ In imperfectly competitive markets, government subsidy to     │
  │ national champion can shift profit from foreign to domestic   │
  │ Boeing/Airbus: both governments subsidize; WTO dispute ongoing │
  │ Semiconductors: CHIPS Act, EU Chips Act, China strategy        │
  │                                                                │
  │ NATIONAL SECURITY (GATT Article XXI):                         │
  │ Countries can restrict trade for national security reasons    │
  │ Largely self-judging (WTO panel found limits in Russia-Ukraine │
  │ transit case 2019, but enforcement questionable)              │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Section 2: Global Value Chains

```
  GVC LOGIC:
  Production fragmented across countries; each stage done where most efficient
  "Factory World" emerged 1990s-2010s:

  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Design        Components      Assembly        Brand/Marketing   │
  │  (US, EU)  →   (Japan, Korea) → (China, Vietnam) → (US, EU)     │
  │                                                                   │
  │  iPhone example:                                                  │
  │  Design: Apple (California)                                       │
  │  OLED display: Samsung (Korea)                                    │
  │  Memory: SK Hynix/Micron (Korea/US)                              │
  │  Logic chip: TSMC (Taiwan design, Apple design)                   │
  │  Assembly: Foxconn (China, Vietnam, India)                        │
  │  Distribution: global supply chain                               │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  TRADE IN VALUE ADDED:
  Gross trade statistics overstate globalization
  Chinese phone "export" of $500 → Chinese value added ~$30
  Most value stays in US (design), Korea (components), Taiwan (chips)

  GVC DISRUPTION POST-2020:
  COVID: supply chain fragility exposed (semiconductors, PPE)
  Russia-Ukraine: energy supply chain disruption
  US-China competition: technology decoupling
  Response: "reshoring," "friendshoring," CHIPS Act, IRA

  POLICY SHIFT:
  2000s: "just-in-time" efficiency maximization
  2020s: "just-in-case" resilience + security
  → Industrial policy revival (US, EU, Japan) after 30yr hiatus
```

## Section 3: Economic Sanctions

```
  SANCTIONS TAXONOMY:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ COMPREHENSIVE: Total import/export ban on target country       │
  │ Cuba (US, 1962+), North Korea, Iran (2012-15)                  │
  │ Effect: hurts civilian population; limited behavioral change   │
  │                                                                │
  │ TARGETED/SMART: Specific individuals/entities                  │
  │ Asset freeze, travel ban, property confiscation               │
  │ SDN list (US Treasury OFAC)                                   │
  │ Evolution post-Iraq (humanitarian damage criticism)            │
  │                                                                │
  │ FINANCIAL: SWIFT exclusion, dollar clearing denial            │
  │ Most powerful modern instrument; cuts off from global finance │
  │                                                                │
  │ EXPORT CONTROLS: Deny specific technology/goods               │
  │ Chips, defense equipment, dual-use technology                  │
  │ US controls extraterritorial via FDPR (Foreign Direct Product  │
  │ Rule) — controls foreign goods made with US technology         │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  EFFECTIVENESS DEBATE:
  Hufbauer, Schott, Elliott (1990): sanctions succeed 33% of cases
  Critics (Pape, 1997): success rate much lower; ~5%; coding errors
  Consensus: sanctions rarely cause target to fully comply; more useful
  for signaling, coalition-building, raising costs

  CONDITIONS FOR EFFECTIVENESS:
  - Multilateral (target has few alternative partners)
  - Clear and achievable demands with off-ramp
  - Costly to sender (otherwise not credible)
  - Third-party sanctions (secondary sanctions) to close loopholes

  SECONDARY SANCTIONS:
  US sanctions reach beyond US jurisdiction:
  "If you trade with X, you lose access to US market/dollar system"
  Extremely powerful (dollar dominance) but alienates allies
  EU "blocking statute" → ineffective in practice (companies comply with US)

  RUSSIA 2022 CASE:
  Unprecedented scale: central bank reserves frozen, SWIFT exclusion
  Outcome: 2022 GDP -2.5% (less than expected), 2023 +3% (war economy)
  Oil price cap ($60) partially worked; Chinese/Indian imports kept revenue up
  Sanctions circumvention: parallel payment systems, third-country routing
  Long-term: accelerates dedollarization, alternative financial infrastructure
```

## Section 4: Currency and Monetary Politics

```
  BRETTON WOODS (1944-1971):
  Fixed exchange rates: all currencies pegged to dollar; dollar pegged to gold
  IMF: manage balance-of-payments problems within fixed system
  Advantage: stability; predictable exchange rates; reduced speculation
  Problem: "Triffin Dilemma" — US must run deficits to supply world with
           dollars; deficits erode confidence in gold convertibility

  NIXON SHOCK (1971):
  Nixon unilaterally closed gold window (ended dollar-gold convertibility)
  Smithsonian Agreement (1971), then floating rates (1973+)
  → World moved to managed float / flexible exchange rates

  DOLLAR HEGEMONY ("EXORBITANT PRIVILEGE"):
  Dollar = ~60% of global reserves; ~80% of global trade invoicing
  Benefits to US:
  - Can borrow in own currency → no currency risk on debt
  - Trade deficit sustainable (world needs dollars)
  - Seigniorage: world holds dollars → US gets zero-interest loan
  - Financial weaponization (SWIFT, asset freezes)

  MUNDELL-FLEMING TRILEMMA:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Choose any two:                                                  │
  │                                                                  │
  │  (1) Open capital account (free capital flows)                   │
  │  (2) Fixed exchange rate                                         │
  │  (3) Independent monetary policy                                 │
  │                                                                  │
  │ Bretton Woods: (2) + (3) → capital controls                     │
  │ Post-1970s: (1) + (3) → floating rates                         │
  │ Eurozone: (1) + (2) → no independent monetary policy           │
  │ China historically: (2) + (3) → capital controls               │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CURRENCY MANIPULATION:
  Deliberately undervaluing currency to boost exports
  China 2000s: sterilized capital inflows → kept RMB undervalued
  US Treasury "currency manipulator" designation: limited direct effect
  WTO rules don't clearly cover exchange rate policy (IMF does, weakly)
```

## Section 5: Washington Consensus vs Alternatives

```
  WASHINGTON CONSENSUS (Williamson, 1989):
  10 policy prescriptions for Latin American economic reform:
  1. Fiscal discipline
  2. Reorientation of public expenditure
  3. Tax reform
  4. Interest rate liberalization
  5. Competitive exchange rate
  6. Trade liberalization
  7. Liberalization of inward foreign direct investment
  8. Privatization
  9. Deregulation
  10. Secure property rights

  OUTCOMES: Mixed; some successes; major failures (Argentina 2001, Asian crisis)
  Critique: one-size-fits-all; sequencing matters; capital account liberalization
  before institutional development → crisis; ignored distributional consequences

  EAST ASIAN DEVELOPMENTAL STATE:
  Korea, Japan, Taiwan — rapid industrialization WITHOUT following Washington Consensus
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Industrial policy: government picked sectors to develop        │
  │ Directed credit: state banks channeled finance to target sectors│
  │ Export discipline: subsidies conditional on export performance │
  │ Trade protection: infant industry protection while developing  │
  │ Technology acquisition: import + reverse engineer + develop    │
  │ Strong state capacity: Weberian bureaucracy, not predatory     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  "Getting prices wrong deliberately" (Amsden) — the opposite of WC

  CHINA'S STATE CAPITALISM:
  Not Washington Consensus, not pure developmental state
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ SOEs in strategic sectors (energy, telecom, finance)           │
  │ Private sector in competitive sectors with party guidance      │
  │ Capital controls → domestic financial system insulated         │
  │ Export + FDI platform → absorbed technology                   │
  │ BRI → extend influence through investment                      │
  │ Now: "innovation-driven" → chips, EVs, AI, quantum            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Section 6: Resource Politics

```
  OIL:
  OPEC (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries): 13 members;
  OPEC+ includes Russia (since 2016); control ~40% of world production
  Oil shocks 1973 (Arab oil embargo), 1979 (Iranian revolution), 1990 (Gulf War)
  → Disruptions trigger recessions; demonstrate producer leverage

  PETROSTATES:
  Resource curse / Dutch Disease:
  Natural resource revenue → currency appreciation → manufacturing uncompetitive
  → Single export dependence → volatility, Dutch disease
  Political resource curse: revenue funds patronage → reduces accountability
  Norway exception: sovereign wealth fund (Government Pension Fund Global: >$1.7T)

  RARE EARTH CONCENTRATION:
  China dominates rare earth mining (~60%) AND processing (~85%)
  17 REEs critical for: EVs, wind turbines, electronics, defense
  Neodymium (magnets), Dysprosium (heat resistance), Lithium (batteries)
  China's response to trade disputes: rare earth export restrictions threat
  US, EU: rare earth supply chain diversification (Lynas, MP Materials, EU mines)

  WATER AS EMERGING CONFLICT DRIVER:
  Nile River: Ethiopia GERD dam → Egypt downstream dependence → potential conflict
  Jordan River: Israel/Palestine/Jordan
  Mekong: China dams → Vietnam/Cambodia/Thailand downstream
  No international treaty governing global water rights
  Climate change: glacier loss → reduced dry-season flow in major Asian rivers
```

## Section 7: Financial Globalization

```
  CAPITAL ACCOUNT LIBERALIZATION:
  1990s consensus: open capital accounts → investment flows → development
  Problems: "Sudden stop" — capital floods in → floods out → crisis
  Asia 1997: capital account + fixed exchange rate + short-term debt = crisis

  IMF RETHINK (Ostry, Ghosh 2010):
  Capital controls may sometimes be appropriate tools
  Not always distortionary; can prevent credit booms + crisis
  Reversal of strong 1990s position

  OFFSHORE FINANCIAL CENTERS:
  Cayman Islands, British Virgin Islands, Luxembourg, Ireland
  Tax treaty arbitrage, regulatory arbitrage, secrecy
  ~40-50% of global FDI passes through tax havens
  Panama Papers (2016), Pandora Papers (2021) exposed scale

  OECD PILLAR TWO (2021 agreement):
  Global minimum corporate tax: 15%
  136 countries agreed; implementation varies
  Aim: end race to bottom on corporate taxation
  Problem: numerous carve-outs, transition rules, enforcement gaps
  US implementation blocked in Senate (OECD deal requires domestic legislation)
  → EU implementing; some fragmentation

  G20 as Global Economic Governance:
  Replaced G7/G8 as principal forum after 2008 financial crisis
  Includes: 19 countries + EU = ~85% of global GDP
  Key forums: finance ministers + central bank governors (regular)
  Leaders summit: annual
  No formal decision-making power → political coordination + norms
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Comparative advantage doesn't mean what wins in practice**: comparative advantage
explains why trade is mutually beneficial. It doesn't determine which country will
actually develop which industries — that depends on capital, institutions, state policy,
and path dependence. The East Asian developmental states showed that industrial policy
can override comparative advantage with success.

**Capital controls were taboo; now they're not**: through the 1990s, IMF orthodoxy
strongly opposed capital controls. The Asian financial crisis and subsequent IMF research
shifted this. Controlled capital accounts (not fully closed) can coexist with development.
China's continued capital controls are an example of maintaining policy space.

**Dollar weaponization is powerful but self-limiting**: each use of the dollar as a
weapon accelerates efforts to build alternatives. Russia/China/India are building
alternative payment rails. The dollar's share of reserves has declined from 72% (1999)
to ~60% (2024). The process is slow because alternatives aren't yet liquid enough —
but the direction is established.

**Global value chains ≠ globalization is irreversible**: the post-2020 politics has
substantially reshaped GVC logic. CHIPS Act, EU Chips Act, IRA, and export controls
all reflect deliberate policy to re-shore strategic capabilities. "Friendshoring" —
moving production to politically aligned partners — is the new paradigm, even at
higher cost.
