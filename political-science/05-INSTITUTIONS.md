# International Institutions

## The Big Picture

International institutions are the infrastructure of international order. They reduce
the costs of cooperation, provide information, create expectations, and give states
mechanisms to coordinate on shared problems. They also reflect the power of their
creators and struggle when that power shifts.

**Formal bridge:** International institutions are distributed coordination middleware
for sovereign actors who cannot be compelled. The UN Security Council is a consensus
protocol with 5 blocking nodes (P5) — any one can veto, so binding decisions require
unanimous consent among the major powers plus a quorum of 9/15. WTO dispute settlement
is a formalized API for conflict resolution: states file complaints, panels adjudicate,
the appellate body (when functional) reviews, and losers face authorized retaliation
if they don't comply. The Appellate Body crisis is literally a quorum failure — US
blocked appointments until the AB dropped below 3 members and could no longer process
cases. NATO's consensus decision-making is a distributed write requiring all-node
agreement, which is why Turkey and Hungary can block decisions unilaterally.

## Section 1: UN Security Council

The most powerful body in international law — and the most dysfunctional:

```
  COMPOSITION:
  P5 (Permanent 5): US, UK, France, Russia, China — VETO POWER
  E10 (Elected 10): 2-year terms, regional rotation

  VETO MECHANICS:
  - Any substantive resolution requires 9/15 votes AND no P5 veto
  - Procedural matters: no veto applicable
  - Abstention ≠ veto (by convention since 1950)
  - "Double veto": veto determination of whether something is procedural

  CHAPTER VI (Pacific Settlement):
  Recommendations; conciliation; mediation; no enforcement
  Consent of parties required

  CHAPTER VII (Threats to Peace):
  Binding resolutions on all member states
  Can authorize force, sanctions, embargoes
  No consent required → binding without target state agreement

  REFORM DEBATES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ G4 (Brazil, Germany, India, Japan) → permanent member seats   │
  │ African Union → 2 permanent + 5 non-permanent seats           │
  │ "Uniting for Peace" (UNGA) res. 377 → bypasses SC when vetoed │
  │                                                                │
  │ Barrier: expanding P5 requires P5 consent                     │
  │ → Self-defeating structure; 75+ years of reform talk, no reform│
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  VETO RECORD (summary):
  US: most vetoes (primarily Israel/Palestine protection)
  Russia/USSR: historically most vetoes; weaponized Ukraine 2022
  China: increasing veto use as power grows
```

## Section 2: UN Peacekeeping and R2P

```
  PEACEKEEPING EVOLUTION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Traditional (Chapter VI): Lightly armed; interposition;        │
  │ consent-based; Cold War model; observe ceasefires              │
  │                                                                │
  │ Post-Cold War: More complex mandates; civilian protection;     │
  │ election monitoring; DDR (disarmament/demobilization/reintegration│
  │                                                                │
  │ Robust peacekeeping (Chapter VII element): MONUC/MONUSCO (DRC) │
  │ Force Intervention Brigade (offensive operations authorized)   │
  │                                                                │
  │ PROBLEMS: Lack of troops (states don't want to contribute);    │
  │ sexual abuse scandals; weak mandates vs strong militias;       │
  │ political will to deploy where most needed (Syria: none)       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  R2P (Responsibility to Protect):
  2005 World Summit Outcome, para. 138-139

  THREE PILLARS:
  1. Primary responsibility: each state has obligation to protect its population
  2. International assistance: help states build protection capacity
  3. Collective response: if state fails, international community may act

  FOUR CRIMES: Genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, ethnic cleansing

  IMPLEMENTATION RECORD:
  Libya 2011: SC Resolution 1973 authorized "all necessary measures"
    → NATO air campaign → Gaddafi overthrown
    → R2P "success" (prevented mass killing in Benghazi) but regime change outcome
    → Russia/China felt deceived; blocked all Syria R2P invocations after
  Syria 2011+: Russian veto blocked any Chapter VII action
    → Hundreds of thousands killed; R2P "failed"

  LESSONS: R2P requires P5 consensus; if any P5 has strategic interest → blocked
           Libya damaged R2P credibility; used as cover for regime change
```

## Section 3: WTO/GATT System

```
  GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, 1947):
  Not an organization — a treaty and a secretariat
  Purpose: reduce tariffs through multilateral negotiation rounds
  Eight rounds from 1947 to Uruguay Round (1986-1994)

  WTO (World Trade Organization, 1995):
  Replaced GATT as formal organization
  Extended coverage: goods + services (GATS) + intellectual property (TRIPS)

  KEY PRINCIPLES:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ MFN (Most Favored Nation): treat all WTO members equally        │
  │ → If you give one member a trade concession, give all          │
  │ Exceptions: FTAs/customs unions (Article XXIV), developing     │
  │ nations (Enabling Clause)                                      │
  │                                                                │
  │ NATIONAL TREATMENT: Treat domestic and imported goods equally  │
  │ once imported good is inside the border                        │
  │ → Can't tax imports higher than domestic equivalent            │
  │                                                                │
  │ BOUND TARIFF SCHEDULES: Each country commits to maximum tariffs │
  │ → Can lower below bound but not raise above                    │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  DISPUTE SETTLEMENT:
  DSB (Dispute Settlement Body) panels: first instance
  Appellate Body (AB): appeal; binding; auto-adoption in 60 days
  Authorization to retaliate: cross-sector sanctions possible

  APPELLATE BODY CRISIS:
  AB requires 3 of 7 members for a case
  US blocked all new appointments 2017-2023 (Trump + Biden)
  → AB fell below quorum 2019; cannot hear appeals
  → US argued AB was overreaching, creating law
  → MPIA (Multi-Party Interim Arrangement): alternative appeals among consenting WTO members
  → WTO dispute system effectively dysfunctional for major disputes

  DOHA ROUND (2001-???):
  Development agenda: agricultural subsidies, developing country access
  Never completed; effectively dead
  Bilateral/regional FTAs filled the vacuum (RCEP, CPTPP, EU deals)
```

## Section 4: IMF

```
  IMF MANDATE: International monetary stability; prevent balance-of-payments crises;
  macroeconomic surveillance; lender of last resort to sovereign borrowers

  KEY FUNCTIONS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ SURVEILLANCE (Article IV): Annual bilateral reviews of         │
  │ members' macroeconomic policies; IMF publishes WEO (World     │
  │ Economic Outlook), GFSR (Global Financial Stability Report)   │
  │                                                                │
  │ LENDING: Stand-By Arrangements (short-term), Extended Fund    │
  │ Facility (medium-term structural), Flexible Credit Line       │
  │ (precautionary, Mexico/Colombia/Poland)                        │
  │                                                                │
  │ SDRs (Special Drawing Rights): IMF reserve asset;             │
  │ basket of USD/EUR/GBP/JPY/CNY; allocated to members based     │
  │ on quotas; not a currency but a reserve claim                  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CONDITIONALITY DEBATE:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Washington Consensus conditionality (1980s-90s):               │
  │ Fiscal austerity + privatization + liberalization              │
  │ → Argentina 1999-2001, Asian crisis 1997: fiscal tightening    │
  │ in recession → deepened crises (Stiglitz critique)             │
  │                                                                │
  │ Post-2008 shift: IMF allowed capital controls (Ostry 2010);    │
  │ acknowledged growth effects of austerity; more nuanced         │
  │ → Greece conditionality still harsh (2010-2015)               │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  GOVERNANCE:
  Quota-based voting: quota ≈ GDP + trade + reserves
  US: ~17% → holds effective veto (85% supermajority for major decisions)
  China: ~6.4% (underrepresented by GDP)
  2010 quota reform: shift to emerging markets; delayed until 2016
  Executive Board: 24 directors; major powers have own seat
```

## Section 6: NATO

```
  FOUNDING (1949): 12 members; North Atlantic Treaty
  CURRENT (2024): 32 members (Finland 2023, Sweden 2024)

  ARTICLE 5 — THE CORE:
  "An armed attack against one or more [members] shall be considered
  an attack against them all... take such action as it deems necessary,
  including the use of armed force"

  NOTE: "Deems necessary" = NOT automatic military response
  Obligation is to consult and respond; what response is each member's choice
  Only invoked once: September 12, 2001 (9/11)

  NATO DECISION-MAKING:
  Consensus: every ally has veto (no majority voting)
  → Turkey blocked Finland/Sweden for 18 months
  → Hungary delayed Ukraine assistance packages

  INTEGRATED COMMAND:
  SACEUR (Supreme Allied Commander Europe): always an American four-star
  SACT (Supreme Allied Commander Transformation): shared command
  NATO HQ: Brussels
  Military headquarters: Mons, Belgium (SHAPE)

  2% GDP PLEDGE:
  Agreed at Wales Summit 2014 after Russia's Crimea annexation
  Target: defense spending ≥ 2% of GDP
  Compliance 2024: ~20 of 32 allies meet target (vs ~3 in 2014)
  Political significance exceeded practical significance
  US ~3.5%; UK ~2.3%; Germany ~2.1% (first time since Cold War)

  ENLARGEMENT LOGIC:
  Bucharest Summit 2008: Georgia and Ukraine "will become members"
  → No MAP (Membership Action Plan) given → ambiguous commitment
  → Russia views expansion as existential threat; invaded both
  → NATO committed in 2024 to Ukraine membership as "irreversible" but timing unclear
```

## Common Confusion Points

**UN Security Council resolutions are not all binding**: only Chapter VII resolutions
are binding on all member states. Chapter VI resolutions are recommendations. Most UNSC
resolutions are Chapter VI (or ambiguous "acting under Chapter VII" language that
intentionally blurs the line).

**NATO Article 5 is not automatic war**: the obligation is to treat an attack as an
attack on all and to take "such action as it deems necessary." Each member decides
what action to take. The credibility of the response depends on political will, not
treaty language. The treaty language is intentionally flexible.

**ECB vs IMF**: the European Central Bank manages eurozone monetary policy and is an EU
institution. The IMF is a global organization that manages balance-of-payments problems.
During the eurozone crisis, the "Troika" (IMF + ECB + European Commission) jointly
managed bailout programs — an unusual combination.

**WTO Appellate Body vs dispute panels**: panels (first instance) can still hear cases.
The AB (appellate review) cannot because US has blocked appointments. Countries can
appeal "into the void" (appeals with no body to hear them, effectively blocking final
binding outcomes). This is a structural crisis that makes WTO dispute settlement
increasingly dysfunctional for any party willing to appeal.
