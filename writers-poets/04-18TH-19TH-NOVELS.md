# 18th and 19th Century Novel — Defoe, Swift, Austen, Dickens, Flaubert, Balzac, Hardy

## The Novel Becomes Dominant

The 18th century produces the English novel as a recognizable form. The 19th century sees the novel become the dominant literary form across Europe, with a scale and social ambition it has never quite matched since.

```
THE NOVEL'S EMERGENCE (1700–1850):

  WHY THE NOVEL NOW?
  ─ Print culture and rising literacy (middle class, women).
  ─ Protestant culture: personal conscience, private reading.
  ─ New middle-class subjects: merchants, clerks, servants with
    ambitions — not aristocrats or heroes.
  ─ Philosophical empiricism: if knowledge comes from experience,
    fiction can educate by simulating experience.

  KEY FORMAL DEVELOPMENTS:
  ─ Defoe (1719): documentary realism; first-person narrator.
  ─ Richardson (1740): epistolary novel; extended interiority.
  ─ Sterne (1759): the anti-novel; narrative as digression.
  ─ Austen (1813): free indirect discourse; irony as structure.
  ─ Dickens (1836): serial publication; social panorama.
  ─ Flaubert (1857): le mot juste; style as moral stance.
  ─ Hardy (1878): tragedy in a contemporary setting.
```

## Daniel Defoe (c. 1660–1731)

Defoe is the first major English novelist in the sense of writing long prose fiction that presents itself as documentary:

```
  ROBINSON CRUSOE (1719):
  Presented as a genuine memoir: "The Life and Strange Surprising
  Adventures of Robinson Crusoe, of York, Mariner."
  No "written by Daniel Defoe" on the title page.

  THE CLAIM TO REALITY:
  Defoe's fiction adopts the conventions of documentary prose:
  ─ Specific dates and durations.
  ─ Detailed inventories (tools, food, materials).
  ─ Practical problem-solving reported matter-of-factly.
  ─ A narrator who claims only to record what happened.

  WHAT IT ESTABLISHES:
  The realistic novel's basic contract: "this could have happened."
  The narrative style that looks out the window rather than
  at the window — prose that pretends to transparency.
```

## Jonathan Swift (1667–1745)

Swift's Gulliver's Travels (1726) is a satire that achieves its effects by straight-faced adoption of Defoe's documentary style:

```
  GULLIVER'S TRAVELS:
  Book I (Lilliput): humans are small-minded — literally tiny.
  Book II (Brobdingnag): close examination reveals humans as
    repulsive — Gulliver is a pet, a specimen, a curiosity.
  Book III (Laputa): intellectuals have no practical sense;
    pure reason divorced from reality is sterile.
  Book IV (Houyhnhnms): rational horses are morally superior
    to Yahoo-humans. Or are they? Gulliver goes mad.

  THE SATIRE:
  The straight documentary style makes the satire hit harder:
  the narrator reports extraordinary things matter-of-factly,
  as if they were unremarkable. The reader must do the work
  of recognizing what is being targeted.

  THE AMBIGUITY OF BOOK IV:
  Gulliver concludes that humans are essentially Yahoos —
  bestial, irrational, filthy. He returns home and can't stand
  the company of his own family.
  Is Swift endorsing this? Or satirizing Gulliver's extremism?
  The scholarship is genuinely divided.
```

## Jane Austen (1775–1817)

Austen is technically the most accomplished novelist in the English language. Her surface of social comedy conceals — and is generated by — an extraordinarily precise ironic consciousness.

### Free Indirect Discourse

This is Austen's defining technique and her primary contribution to the novel form:

```
  FREE INDIRECT DISCOURSE:
  A way of narrating that adopts a character's voice and consciousness
  without markers (quotation marks, "she thought that").

  DIRECT SPEECH: "What a bore this party is," she thought.
  INDIRECT SPEECH: She thought that the party was a bore.
  FREE INDIRECT: What a bore this party was.

  The third example: is the narrator telling us the party is boring,
  or is this Elizabeth Bennet's thought reported in the third person?
  Both, simultaneously.

  WHAT THIS DOES:
  ─ Creates ironic distance: the narrator and character are
    superimposed but distinct. When we read about Emma's perceptions
    in free indirect discourse, we know Emma is wrong; the narrator's
    slight tonal distance signals it.
  ─ Creates intimacy: we are inside the character's consciousness
    while also able to observe it critically.
  ─ The form that 20th-century modernism will develop (Joyce,
    Woolf) is already fully present in Austen.

  EXAMPLE (Pride and Prejudice, opening):
  "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in
  possession of a good fortune must be in want of a wife."

  Who says this? Not Austen. Not Elizabeth Bennet.
  It's the voice of the social world — Mrs. Bennet's logic,
  Meryton's logic — stated with ironic distance. The "universal
  truth" is immediately revealed as local convention.
```

### The Novel of Manners

Austen's subject is always the same: educated women with limited options (no careers, almost no property rights, marriage as economic necessity) navigating a social world governed by convention. The dramatic tension: how do you maintain integrity (self-knowledge, honest judgment) in a world that punishes it?

```
  EMMA (1815): The most formally perfect of the novels.
  Emma Woodhouse is "handsome, clever, and rich" — and wrong
  about almost everything. She misreads Frank Churchill, invents
  a romance that doesn't exist, fails to see the one that does.
  Her education = learning to perceive correctly.

  THE FORMAL CHALLENGE:
  We are almost entirely inside Emma's misperceptions.
  Austen shows us what Emma sees without showing us what Emma
  misses. The reader should recognize Emma's errors before
  Emma does — but many readers don't.
  This requires extraordinary management of narrative
  perspective and information distribution.
```

## Charles Dickens (1812–1870)

Dickens is the first novelist to reach a mass audience — he published in serial installments in weekly and monthly magazines, with readerships in the hundreds of thousands.

### Serial Publication

```
  THE SERIAL FORMAT:
  ─ Published in monthly 20-part installments or weekly in magazines.
  ─ Each part: 2–3 chapters, cliffhanger ending.
  ─ Publication while writing: Dickens could (and did) adjust
    plot based on reader response.
  ─ Length: serial novels are long; the format encourages
    elaboration.

  WHAT THIS PRODUCES:
  ─ Strong plot construction (readers must want the next part).
  ─ Vivid, memorable characters (readers must recognize them
    instantly when they reappear months later).
  ─ Serial climaxes and revelations at regular intervals.
  ─ The double-plot: one plot for respectable readers,
    one for sensation seekers.

  DICKENS' CHARACTERS:
  The Dickensian character has a "tag" — a repeated phrase,
  manner, or physical detail that identifies them instantly.
  Micawber (Copperfield): "something will turn up."
  Uriah Heep: unctuous humility performed obsessively.
  Miss Havisham: frozen at the jilted moment.

  These are theatrical exaggerations, not psychologically
  realistic. They're closer to Jonson's "humour" characters
  than to Tolstoy's people. They work because they embody
  social types with concentrated force.
```

### Social Critique

Dickens was the first writer to make systematic social criticism — factories, workhouses, debtor's prisons, the legal system, schools — a primary subject of major fiction. Bleak House (1852–53) is a sustained attack on Chancery (the courts of equity). Hard Times (1854) attacks utilitarian political economy. Oliver Twist (1837) attacks the New Poor Law.

He didn't change these institutions directly, but he changed public attitudes toward them — which eventually changed policy.

## Gustave Flaubert (1821–1880)

Flaubert is the most technically self-conscious novelist of the 19th century — he agonized over every sentence, writing and rewriting for years, producing very few texts of extraordinary density and precision.

### Le Mot Juste

```
  "Le mot juste" — the precise word.

  Flaubert's theory: there is, for each thing to be expressed,
  one word that is correct — not approximately right, exactly right.
  The writer's task is to find it.

  LETTERS TO LOUISE COLET while writing Madame Bovary (1851–1856):
  "A good sentence in prose should be like a good line in poetry,
  unchangeable, as rhythmic, as sonorous."

  He spent weeks on a single page. He read aloud what he wrote
  to hear how it sounded ("gueuloir" = his shouting room).

  WHAT THIS PRODUCED:
  Madame Bovary has a prose texture unlike anything before:
  every sentence does work; no decoration; the narrative advances
  through precisely selected concrete detail.
```

### Madame Bovary (1857) — Free Indirect Discourse

```
  EMMA BOVARY: A doctor's wife in provincial Normandy who has
  read too many romantic novels and cannot reconcile her fantasies
  with her actual life. She has affairs, accumulates debt,
  is destroyed.

  THE TECHNIQUE: Free indirect discourse (style indirect libre).
  Flaubert was the theorist of the technique Austen had already
  practiced. He used it more systematically and self-consciously.

  "She went to the window. There was not a soul about. She leaned
  out and looked down. How cheerful the street below!"

  The last exclamation is Emma's consciousness, not the narrator's
  observation. We are inside Emma's romanticizing of ordinary life
  — and we know she's wrong because we've seen the street too.

  THE TRIAL FOR OBSCENITY (1857):
  Flaubert was tried (and acquitted) for Madame Bovary.
  The charge: the novel depicts adultery without condemning it.
  His defense: the novel condemns through the consequences and
  through the ironic treatment of Emma's romanticism.
  The prosecutor: the very style indirect libre creates moral
  ambiguity — we're inside Emma's seductions.
  They were both right.
```

### L'Éducation Sentimentale (1869)

The anti-Bildungsroman: Frédéric Moreau pursues a woman he desires for 30 years and achieves nothing. At the end, he and his friend agree that their best memory was an adolescent visit to a brothel where they ran away.

The novel's point is that sentimental education — the formation of feeling — is education in delusion. Flaubert's most savage book and his least read.

## Honoré de Balzac (1799–1850)

Balzac is the great encyclopedist of French society. La Comédie Humaine (The Human Comedy) is a project of 90 novels and stories using recurring characters to create a total portrait of French society from the Restoration through the July Monarchy.

```
  THE PROJECT:
  Balzac noticed that Buffon had classified animals — he wanted
  to do the same for social types (the banker, the journalist,
  the provincial lawyer, the Parisian nobleman).

  LA COMÉDIE HUMAINE:
  3 categories: Études de Moeurs (Studies of Manners),
  Études Philosophiques, Études Analytiques.
  Recurring characters: Vautrin (the criminal) appears in
  Père Goriot, Lost Illusions, and Splendors and Miseries
  of Courtesans. The same world, different angles.

  PÈRE GORIOT (1834): An old man sacrifices everything for
  daughters who abandon him. Lear structure in contemporary Paris.
  The young student Rastignac witnesses this and resolves to
  succeed in society by any means.
  "À nous deux maintenant!" (Just the two of us now!) —
  he cries at Paris from a hilltop, at the end. A declaration of war.
```

## Thomas Hardy (1840–1928)

Hardy attempted to write classical tragedy in a contemporary rural English setting — with the force of fate replaced by social structure, nature, and bad timing.

```
  THE TRAGIC STRUCTURE:
  Hardy's protagonists are typically people of natural gifts
  and moral seriousness who are destroyed by social forces
  they can't overcome (class, convention, religious hypocrisy)
  or by the indifference of nature/time.

  TESS OF THE D'URBERVILLES (1891):
  Tess is raped, tries to rebuild her life, falls in love,
  is abandoned when her husband learns her history, kills her
  rapist, is executed. Hardy's subtitle: "A Pure Woman."
  The irony: Victorian society called her "impure" for being
  raped. Hardy inverts this.

  THE NATURE WRITING:
  Hardy's Wessex (fictionalized Dorset) is not picturesque.
  Egdon Heath (Return of the Native) is described in the opening
  chapter as a landscape of indifference — "Haggard Egdon
  appealed to a subtler and scarcer instinct, to a more
  recently learnt emotion, than that which responds to
  the sort of beauty called charming and fair."
  The heath outlasts all the human activity in the novel.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Austen is about marriage and manners** — Austen is about epistemology — how do we know what we know, especially when social performance distorts everything? The marriage plots are the mechanism; the question is always: can this character learn to perceive accurately? Emma can; Marianne (Sense and Sensibility) eventually can; Anne Elliot (Persuasion) already does.

**Dickens is sentimental and melodramatic** — Yes, often. But Bleak House, Our Mutual Friend, and Great Expectations have structural and tonal complexity that goes well beyond melodrama. Great Expectations is both a critique of social aspiration and a psychologically acute study of self-deception.

**Flaubert hated Emma Bovary** — He famously said "Madame Bovary c'est moi" (Madame Bovary is me). He understood Emma's romanticism from the inside — he shared it, as a young reader who devoured Romantic novels. His contempt was self-directed. The irony in the novel is generated by intimacy, not contempt.

**Hardy is pessimistic** — He's not fatalistic in the sense of saying things can't be different. He's arguing that they shouldn't be as they are. Tess's destruction is a social crime. Jude the Obscure is a direct attack on the social structures that destroy working-class intellectual aspiration. The pessimism is moral critique.
