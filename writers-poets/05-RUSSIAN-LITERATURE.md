# Russian Literature — Pushkin, Gogol, Turgenev, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Chekhov

## The Russian Literary Context

Russian literature develops roughly 100 years later than Western European literature but compresses that development rapidly and produces some of the most significant works in any tradition. The context:

```
RUSSIAN LITERARY TIMELINE

  PUSHKIN (1799–1837):
  ─ Establishes Russian as a literary language.
  ─ In a culture dominated by French, Pushkin insists on Russian.
  ─ Creates all the major forms: lyric, verse novel, prose fiction, drama.

  GOGOL (1809–1852):
  ─ The ambiguous satirist — his satirical targets are never clear.
  ─ Dead Souls and The Overcoat define a realism that is also grotesque.

  THE GREAT PERIOD (1840–1880):
  ─ The 19th century novel reaches its greatest expression in Russia.
  ─ Turgenev: the Europeanized, elegiac observer.
  ─ Dostoevsky: psychological extremity; religious-philosophical depth.
  ─ Tolstoy: epic scope; moral intensity; the simplest and grandest style.

  CHEKHOV (1860–1904):
  ─ Invents the modern short story and the modern drama.
  ─ The ending that doesn't resolve; the subtext that says everything.

  SOCIAL CONTEXT:
  ─ Russian literary culture is inseparable from political censorship.
  ─ Every major writer had a troubled relationship with the state:
    Pushkin killed in a duel (arranged?); Dostoevsky sentenced to death
    (commuted); Tolstoy excommunicated; Chekhov navigated carefully.
  ─ The "intelligentsia" (Russian word) debated: Westernizers vs. Slavophiles —
    should Russia modernize on Western models or develop its own identity?
    This debate runs through all the major works.
```

## Alexander Pushkin (1799–1837)

Pushkin is the founding figure of modern Russian literature — the person who made Russian into a literary language capable of expressing everything. He died at 37 in a duel (widely believed to have been engineered by the court).

### Eugene Onegin (1825–1832)

A novel in verse — 14-line stanzas (the "Onegin stanza") with a specific rhyme scheme. The story: a dandy (Onegin) rejects a young woman's (Tatyana's) love; years later, she is married and socially successful; he falls in love with her; she rejects him.

```
  THE STRUCTURE:
  Onegin rejects Tatyana → Onegin kills his friend Lensky in a duel
  → Years pass → They meet again, she is a princess → He declares love
  → She says: "I love you, but I am married. I will remain faithful."

  THE IRONY:
  Onegin is the superfluous man — too sophisticated for genuine feeling,
  too bored for commitment. The first time, he couldn't see what
  Tatyana was. The second time, he wants what he no longer can have.

  PUSHKIN AND THE NARRATOR:
  The narrator constantly intrudes, commenting on the characters,
  addressing them, noting his own role in the story.
  "Eugene Onegin" is already metafiction — a novel about being
  a novel, about romantic conventions being inadequate to reality.

  NABOKOV'S TRANSLATION (1964):
  Nabokov spent years producing an extremely literal translation
  with 1000 pages of commentary. He argued you must sacrifice
  the verse to get the literal meaning. His target: previous
  translators who had "improved" Pushkin with poeticisms.
  This is still the most informative if unreadable translation.
```

## Nikolai Gogol (1809–1852)

Gogol is the most enigmatic of the Russian writers — his satire is never entirely sure of its target, and his career ended in a religious crisis that destroyed his creative life.

### Dead Souls (1842)

```
  THE PREMISE: Chichikov travels rural Russia buying "dead souls" —
  serfs who have died but are still registered on the census.
  He can then mortgage them as live property and get rich.

  THE SATIRICAL LOGIC:
  Dead souls = serfs (human beings reduced to census entries).
  Dead souls = the Russian landowners themselves — their moral emptiness.
  Dead souls = bureaucratic Russia — a society of fictions.

  THE CHARACTER TYPES:
  Manilov: sentimental dreamer, does nothing.
  Korobochka: petty, suspicious, hoarding.
  Nozdryov: reckless, lying, violent.
  Sobakevich: materialist, suspicious, everything is shoddy.
  Plyushkin: the miser, reduced to an object, surrounded by garbage.

  GOGOL'S PROCEDURE:
  These are not rounded characters — they are social-moral essences,
  comic grotesques. Each embodies a kind of spiritual deadness.
  Dead Souls was planned as a three-part Dante's Commedia:
  Inferno (the Russia Chichikov moves through) → Purgatorio
  → Paradiso. Only Part I and fragments of Part II were completed.
```

### The Overcoat (1842)

```
  THE STORY: Akaky Akakievich, a copying clerk, saves up
  and buys a new overcoat. It is immediately stolen. He petitions
  officials who don't help him. He dies. His ghost haunts
  Petersburg, stealing coats from officials.

  DOSTOEVSKY: "We all came out from under Gogol's Overcoat."
  (Probably apocryphal but widely quoted)

  What this means: the Overcoat established the template
  for the "little man" in Russian literature — the petty
  bureaucrat crushed by the system. It also established
  the grotesque-realistic mode that runs through Dostoevsky.

  THE TECHNIQUE:
  Gogol's prose alternates between pathos and absurdity so
  quickly that you can't maintain a stable emotional response.
  You laugh at Akaky; you feel for him; you laugh again.
  This tonal instability is the point: life under the Russian
  bureaucratic system is simultaneously tragic and ridiculous.
```

## Ivan Turgenev (1818–1883)

Turgenev is the most European of the Russian writers — elegant, restrained, elegiac. He lived much of his life in Paris and Baden-Baden. He was the first Russian writer to reach a large Western European audience.

### Fathers and Sons (Fathers and Children, 1862)

```
  THE NIHILIST BAZAROV:
  A young doctor who believes in nothing except empirical science.
  He rejects art, sentiment, tradition, religion, love.
  He uses people as test subjects; he dissects frogs; he is
  the most intellectually vital character in the novel.
  He dies of typhus contracted while performing an autopsy.

  THE NOVEL'S QUESTION:
  Is Bazarov right? Is the novel satirizing nihilism or endorsing it?
  Turgenev said he loved Bazarov. The radicals of the 1860s
  thought he was mocking them. Both sides were angry.

  THE TECHNICAL ACHIEVEMENT:
  Turgenev's "objective" method: no authorial intrusion,
  no direct moral commentary. Characters reveal themselves
  through action and dialogue. The technique Turgenev practiced
  Henry James later theorized.
```

## Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821–1881)

Dostoevsky is the supreme psychological novelist — not in the sense of depicting ordinary psychology but in the sense of exploring consciousness at extremes: humiliation, ecstasy, murder, compassion, theodicy, spiritual crisis.

### The Psychology of Underground

```
  NOTES FROM UNDERGROUND (1864):

  "I am a sick man... I am a spiteful man. I am an unattractive man.
  I believe my liver is diseased."

  The Underground Man is the first literary anti-hero:
  ─ Intelligent but unable to act.
  ─ Aware of his own irrationality but unable to overcome it.
  ─ Resents others; humiliates himself; is humiliated.
  ─ Argues that 2+2=4 is an insult — humans will act irrationally
    just to assert their freedom against determinism.

  THE PHILOSOPHICAL ARGUMENT:
  Against Chernyshevsky's "rational egoism": if you show people
  what is in their genuine interest, they will choose it.
  Underground Man: humans will choose against their interest
  specifically to prove they are not determined machines.
  "I'll do what I want, not what's rational."

  THIS IS THE COUNTER-ARGUMENT TO UTILITARIANISM:
  People value freedom of choice more than optimal outcomes.
  Dostoevsky makes this not as an argument but as a character.
```

### The Major Novels

```
  CRIME AND PUNISHMENT (1866):
  A student (Raskolnikov) kills a pawnbroker to prove he is
  a "Napoleon" — a person above ordinary moral law.
  He discovers he is not. The novel is his psychological
  breakdown and spiritual recovery.

  THE POLICE INSPECTOR PORFIRY:
  One of the great psychological confrontations in fiction.
  Porfiry knows Raskolnikov is guilty; Raskolnikov knows Porfiry
  knows. Their conversations are chess games of mutual disclosure.

  THE BROTHERS KARAMAZOV (1880):
  The murder of a father; his three sons as suspect.
  But the real subject:
  ─ Ivan's "Grand Inquisitor" chapter: the most powerful
    argument against God ever written, put in the mouth
    of a skeptic — not to endorse it but to face it.
  ─ Zosima's answer: active love, the specific person before you.
  ─ Alyosha as the embodiment of that answer.

  THE GRAND INQUISITOR (Book V):
  Ivan tells a story: Christ returns to 15th-century Seville.
  The Grand Inquisitor has him arrested. He explains: you gave
  humans freedom; they can't handle freedom; we gave them bread,
  miracle, and authority instead. We serve humans better than
  you did. He kisses the prisoner and says: go away.

  The "prisoner" (Christ) says nothing. This is the problem
  of theodicy as drama — unanswerable.
```

## Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910)

Tolstoy is the greatest realist novelist. Where Dostoevsky explores extremity, Tolstoy renders the full texture of life — its rhythms, its surfaces, its psychological gradations — with an authority no other writer has matched.

### The Technique

```
  TOLSTOY'S MORAL VISION:
  He believed that what is most true in human experience is
  the simplest and most universal — not intellectual argument
  but the simple feeling of a mother, the physical reality of
  suffering, the presence of death.

  His style follows this: his prose is transparent (compared
  to Flaubert's carefully wrought sentences, Tolstoy's seem
  to have no style at all — they simply describe).

  FREE INDIRECT DISCOURSE IN TOLSTOY:
  In Anna Karenina, we are successively inside Anna's consciousness,
  Levin's, Vronsky's, Dolly's, Kitty's — without a shift of
  narration. We understand all of them; we see each through
  the others' eyes.

  ANNA KARENINA (1877):
  Two parallel plots: Anna/Vronsky (love affair, destruction)
  and Levin/Kitty (ordinary domestic happiness found through work
  and honest living).
  The novel asks: what makes a life go well? Anna has everything
  (beauty, intelligence, passion) and destroys herself. Levin has
  doubt and imperfection and finds meaning through physical labor,
  marriage, and a moment of something like faith.
```

### War and Peace (1869)

```
  SCALE:
  ─ 580 characters, 1,300+ pages.
  ─ Covers Napoleon's invasion of Russia (1805–1815).
  ─ Alternates between: intimate psychological scenes (Natasha
    dancing, Prince Andrei dying) and philosophical essays
    (Tolstoy's theory of history).

  THE HISTORICAL THEORY:
  Great men do not make history — history is the aggregate
  of millions of individual actions, none decisive alone.
  Napoleon believed he commanded the battle of Borodino.
  Kutuzov (the Russian general) understood he commanded nothing —
  his genius was in recognizing what he could and couldn't control.

  THIS IS TOLSTOY'S ANTI-HEROIC THEORY OF HISTORY:
  No individual will drives large events. Causation is too
  distributed and complex for any individual to grasp, let alone
  direct. The great are great because they align with what
  was going to happen anyway.

  APPLICABLE TO COMPUTING:
  This is a distributed systems view of history — the emergent
  behavior of millions of agents, none dominant.
```

## Anton Chekhov (1860–1904)

Chekhov is the inventor of the modern short story and modern drama. He practiced medicine throughout his literary career; his medical eye — precise, diagnostic, unsentimental — shapes his fiction.

### The Chekhovian Short Story

```
  CHEKHOV'S PRINCIPLES:

  "The role of the artist is to ask questions, not to answer them."

  "If you show a gun in the first act, it must go off by the third."
  (Chekhov's Gun — every detail must be narratively significant)

  "Squeeze your prose to make it dense."

  WHAT HE REMOVED:
  ─ Narrative exposition (we are placed in medias res).
  ─ Explicit moral commentary (the reader draws conclusions).
  ─ Resolved endings (life doesn't resolve; his stories don't either).
  ─ Dramatic plot (something internal shifts; nothing external resolves).

  WHAT HE LEFT:
  ─ Atmosphere, mood, and physical detail.
  ─ Conversation that reveals character through what is not said.
  ─ The moment of recognition or realization.

  THE LADY WITH THE DOG (1899):
  A man and a woman have an affair during a vacation in Yalta.
  They part; he thinks he'll forget her; he can't. They meet in
  her city; they continue the affair; they realize they are in love.
  The story ends:
  "And it seemed as though in a little while the solution would
  be found, and then a new and splendid life would begin;
  and it was clear to both of them that they still had a long long
  road before them, and that the hardest part of it was only
  just beginning."
  No resolution. The story ends at the beginning of the problem.
```

### The Plays

Chekhov's four major plays (The Seagull, Uncle Vanya, Three Sisters, The Cherry Orchard) invented a form of drama that the 20th century inherited:

```
  TRADITIONAL DRAMA: Plot with rising action, climax, resolution.
  Decisive events. Clear motivation.

  CHEKHOV'S DRAMA:
  ─ Nothing decisive happens — or if it does, it happens offstage.
  ─ Characters talk about action instead of acting.
  ─ Multiple simultaneous conversations that don't connect.
  ─ The subtext is the real text.
  ─ Characters fail to communicate because they're all in their
    own worlds.

  UNCLE VANYA (1899):
  Vanya shoots at Serebryakov and misses — twice.
  This is the climactic action. He misses. Life continues.
  The professor leaves; Vanya and Sonya return to their work.
  "We shall rest." The most devastating ending in drama.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Tolstoy and Dostoevsky are the same tradition** — They're quite different. Tolstoy: classical, harmonious, epic scale, this-worldly. Dostoevsky: fragmentary, anguished, psychological, otherworldly. Tolstoy said of Dostoevsky that his books were "badly written" (though he admired some); Dostoevsky revered Tolstoy.

**Chekhov's characters are passive and boring** — Chekhov's characters are intensely active internally — longing, suffering, hoping, resigning themselves. What they lack is the ability to translate inner life into decisive external action. This is the human condition as Chekhov sees it, not a dramatic failure.

**Crime and Punishment is about guilt** — It's about ideology. Raskolnikov kills not from need or passion but from a theory — that great men transcend ordinary moral law. The novel demolishes this theory not through argument but through what happens to him psychologically. Dostoevsky's answer to harmful ideology is embodied, not abstract.

**Tolstoy's late life rejected his great novels** — He did. Tolstoy underwent a religious conversion in the 1870s and subsequently declared War and Peace and Anna Karenina morally worthless (because they celebrated the privileged class). He also renounced his copyrights, gave away his property, and tried to live as a peasant. His family found this difficult. He died fleeing his home.
