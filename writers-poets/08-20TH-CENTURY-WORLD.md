# 20th-Century World Literature — Borges, García Márquez, Camus, Beckett, Nabokov, Sartre

## The Global Novel

By the mid-20th century, the modernist experiment had been absorbed and was being used, challenged, and reinvented by writers working outside the Western European mainstream. The center of literary gravity shifted:

```
THE DISPERSAL OF MODERNISM:

  LATIN AMERICA:
  ─ Absorbed European modernism + Surrealism.
  ─ Added: indigenous and African mythological traditions,
    political urgency (colonialism, dictatorship, revolution).
  ─ The Boom (1960s–70s): García Márquez, Vargas Llosa, Cortázar,
    Donoso. Global readership; Nobel Prizes.
  ─ Precursors: Borges (Argentina, 1930s–1960s) — the intellectual
    foundation for the Boom.

  EUROPE (POST-WWII):
  ─ Existentialism (Sartre, Camus): what do you do after Auschwitz
    and the Bomb? How do you live without transcendent meaning?
  ─ Theatre of the Absurd (Beckett, Ionesco): theatrical form as
    expression of meaninglessness.
  ─ Nabokov: the émigré aesthete — formalist brilliance as
    ethical stance.

  AFRICA, ASIA, CARIBBEAN:
  ─ Decolonization movements produce literatures in English and
    French that address colonialism, identity, and tradition.
    (Chinua Achebe, Wole Soyinka, Naguib Mahfouz)
  ─ Treated in 09-CONTEMPORARY.md.

KEY FORMAL DEVELOPMENTS:
  ─ Magical realism: the impossibleas normal.
  ─ Metafiction intensified: Borges, Nabokov.
  ─ Minimal / absurdist drama: Beckett.
  ─ Philosophical fiction: Camus, Sartre.
  ─ Unreliable narrator radicalized: Nabokov.
```

## Jorge Luis Borges (1899–1986)

Borges is the writer who made it impossible to tell the difference between a philosophical essay and a short story — and argued this was the point.

### The Fictions

```
FICCIONES (1944) and EL ALEPH (1949) are collections of short
  prose pieces — Borges called them "fictions" because they
  are neither purely stories nor purely essays.

THE TECHNIQUE:
  ─ Borges pretends to be a scholar reviewing fictional books,
    or summarizing texts that don't exist.
  ─ "The Garden of Forking Paths" (1941):
    A spy story. The puzzle: why does the spy kill a man named
    Albert? Because the man is named Albert — and Albert is the
    name of the military target the spy needs to communicate.
    The murder is a message. Nested within: a discussion of
    Ts'ui Pên's labyrinthine novel in which every possibility
    is realized simultaneously. The novel = time itself.
  ─ "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" (1940):
    An encyclopedia entry about a fictitious world (Tlön) in
    which idealism is the operative metaphysics — no material
    objects, only mental ones. Tlön begins to replace reality.
    Borges's commentary: ideological systems are fictional
    worlds that replace the actual world.
  ─ "Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote" (1939):
    Menard writes the Quixote — not copies it, but recomposes it
    word for word as an independent work. The identical text
    means something different, because the context is different.
    The philosophical argument: meaning is not in the text but
    in the relation between text and context.
  ─ "The Library of Babel" (1941):
    A universe that is a library containing all possible books
    of 410 pages. All knowledge is there; nothing can be found.
    The library as metaphor for: language, the universe, the
    impossibility of systematic knowledge.
  ─ "The Lottery in Babylon" (1941):
    A society where a lottery determines all fates. Eventually
    the lottery controls everything, including negative outcomes
    and the lottery itself. A critique of systematic theology
    and determinism as formal games.

BORGES'S THEMES:
  ─ Time as labyrinth (not linear but branching or circular).
  ─ The map that becomes the territory (representation replaces
    the thing represented).
  ─ Infinity and self-reference (infinite libraries, mirrors,
    encyclopedias, eternal recurrence).
  ─ The unreliability of authorship and identity.
  ─ Philosophy done through narrative rather than argument.

BORGES AND MATHEMATICS:
  Borges was obsessed with Cantor's transfinite numbers (different
  sizes of infinity), Russell's paradoxes, Zeno's paradoxes.
  "The Garden of Forking Paths" = a combinatorial tree.
  "The Library of Babel" = the combinatorial explosion of language.
  His fictions are thought experiments in the tradition of
  mathematical philosophy.
```

## Gabriel García Márquez (1927–2014)

García Márquez invented the most widely imitated narrative technique of the 20th century: magical realism. But this term, as usually applied, misrepresents what he actually did.

### One Hundred Years of Solitude (1967)

```
THE NOVEL:
  Seven generations of the Buendía family in the fictional town
  of Macondo (modeled on García Márquez's hometown of Aracataca,
  Colombia). Covers roughly 100 years; ends with a hurricane that
  destroys the town and the family simultaneously.

MAGICAL REALISM — WHAT IT IS:
  The term comes from art criticism (Franz Roh, 1925; Alejo
  Carpentier's "lo real maravilloso").
  In García Márquez: impossible things happen and are reported
  with the same factual matter-of-factness as ordinary events.
  "Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel
   Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when
   his father took him to discover ice."
  The opening sentence. Ice = miraculous. The firing squad =
  ordinary. Both are reported with the same tone.

WHAT MAGICAL REALISM IS NOT:
  ─ Fantasy: fantasy creates an alternative world with its own
    rules. Magical realism presents the impossible in our world.
  ─ Surrealism: Surrealism seeks to disorient. Magical realism
    seeks to render a cultural reality — a world where the
    boundary between the living and the dead, between the natural
    and the supernatural, is genuinely porous.

THE CULTURAL ARGUMENT:
  García Márquez said he was writing how his grandmother told
  stories: extraordinary things reported in the same tone as
  ordinary things, without surprise. The Latin American oral
  tradition didn't separate the natural from the supernatural.
  Magical realism is realism for a culture with different
  ontological assumptions — not a literary trick but a
  different epistemology rendered in fiction.

THE POLITICAL DIMENSION:
  Macondo's history parallels Colombian and Latin American
  history: civil wars, banana company imperialism (United
  Fruit = United Fruit Company massacre, 1928), political
  corruption, and cyclical violence.
  The Buendía family's inability to escape their patterns
  = the region's inability to escape its history.
  The circular structure (the novel ends at its beginning)
  = the condemned repetition Pilar Ternera identifies:
  "The history of the family was a machine with unavoidable
   repetitions."

LOVE IN THE TIME OF CHOLERA (1985):
  Florentino Ariza loves Fermina Daza; she marries another man;
  Florentino waits 51 years, 9 months, and 4 days; her husband
  dies; Florentino approaches her again; she accepts.
  A novel about whether idealized love can survive contact with
  the real. Also about old age, sexuality, and mortality.
  Deliberately constructed as a Romantic novel — not ironic.
```

## Albert Camus (1913–1960)

Camus is the philosopher of the Absurd — but unlike Sartre, he was primarily a novelist. He refused the label "existentialist."

### The Absurdist Position

```
THE MYTH OF SISYPHUS (1942) — the philosophical essay:
  THE ABSURD: The human desire for meaning + the world's silence
    = absurdity. Not a property of humans alone, nor of the world
    alone, but of the confrontation between them.

  THE QUESTION: "There is but one truly serious philosophical
    problem, and that is suicide. Judging whether life is worth
    living amounts to answering the fundamental question of
    philosophy."

  THE WRONG ANSWERS:
  ─ Philosophical suicide (Kierkegaard, Chestov): the leap to
    faith. This is evasion — replacing the Absurd with something
    beyond reason.
  ─ Literal suicide: eliminates the confrontation but doesn't
    resolve it.

  THE RIGHT ANSWER: Revolt.
    To live in full consciousness of the Absurd, refusing
    consolation, refusing to reduce the tension.
    Sisyphus must be imagined happy — he has no hope, knows his
    fate, and embraces it. This is not optimism but lucid revolt.

THE STRANGER (L'Étranger, 1942):
  Meursault kills an Arab on a beach because the sun is too hot.
  He feels nothing at his mother's funeral. He is convicted
  and sentenced to death — not for the murder but for his failure
  to perform the expected emotional responses.
  The novel's argument: society requires a performance of feeling
  that has nothing to do with actual moral reality. Meursault's
  "crime" is that he refuses to lie about his inner states.

  THE STYLE:
  First-person narration; short declarative sentences; no
  emotional commentary; events reported as sensory facts.
  "Today, Maman died. Or maybe yesterday; I don't know."
  (First line.) The emotional detachment IS the prose style —
  not reported but enacted.

THE PLAGUE (La Peste, 1947):
  An epidemic in Oran. Dr. Rieux and others fight the plague
  without transcendent justification.
  The plague = Nazism (France under Occupation);
  the plague = absurdity itself (indifferent suffering).
  The lesson: solidarity — not because it works, not because
  God commands it, but because it's the only dignified response
  to shared mortality.
  "One must imagine Sisyphus happy" here becomes: one must care
  for the sick even knowing the cure is temporary and death
  is final.
```

## Samuel Beckett (1906–1989)

Beckett wrote in French (to avoid the facility of his native English) and then translated himself into English. He is the supreme minimalist of 20th-century literature.

### Waiting for Godot (1952)

```
THE PLOT: Two men (Vladimir and Estragon) wait for Godot.
  Godot does not come. They discuss leaving. They do not leave.
  Act II: the same, but the tree has leaves.

  This is not a vague summary — this is the play.

THE TECHNIQUE:
  ─ Vaudeville structure: the double act (Vladimir/Estragon)
    derives from the music-hall cross-talk act.
  ─ Repetition with variation: Act II exactly mirrors Act I
    with small differences that emphasize both change and stasis.
  ─ Non-sequitur dialogue: the characters talk past each other
    constantly; each is in their own private world while sharing
    the same physical space.

THE INTERPRETIVE PROBLEM:
  Beckett resisted all single-meaning interpretation.
  Who is Godot? Not God (Beckett denied it). Not Death.
  Not anyone specific. "If I knew who Godot was, I would have
  said so in the play."
  The point IS the waiting — the human condition of expecting
  something that will resolve meaning, and it never comes.

ENDGAME (1957):
  Hamm (blind, in a wheelchair) and Clov (can't sit down) in
  a room with two small windows and two garbage cans containing
  Hamm's parents (Nagg and Nell, legless).
  The play is the end of something — the world may be dying
  outside. The dialogue: chess moves. The title: endgame.
  The question: what is the point of continuing?
  Hamm: "The end is in the beginning and yet you go on."

BECKETT'S PROSE (TRILOGY: Molloy, Malone Dies, The Unnamable):
  The progressive dismantling of the novel:
  Molloy (1951): first-person narration by a decrepit man
    crawling toward his mother. Circularity, decomposition.
  Malone Dies (1951): man in a bed, waiting to die, inventing
    stories. The stories run out. He dies.
  The Unnamable (1953): no body, no location, no certainty
    about identity. Voice speaking, unable to stop:
    "I can't go on. I'll go on."
  The last sentence of the trilogy is the most famous ending
  in 20th-century fiction: "you must go on, I can't go on,
  I'll go on."
```

## Vladimir Nabokov (1899–1977)

Nabokov is the supreme stylist of 20th-century fiction in English — and he demonstrates that style is not merely surface but is the substance of ethical and epistemological positions.

### The Formal Programme

```
NABOKOV'S AESTHETIC:
  ─ He despised what he called "poshlost'" — kitsch sentimentality,
    vulgar authenticity, politically motivated art. He despised
    Dostoevsky (too sentimental), Freud (pseudoscience), Kafka
    (misread as allegory), Faulkner (bad writing).
  ─ He believed in the aesthetic object as its own justification.
    The pattern of the thing — how it is made — is the meaning.
  ─ Every reader is meant to notice how the novel is constructed.
    The hidden patterns (Nabokov's novels contain elaborate
    concealed structures) are part of the experience.

LOLITA (1955):
  Humbert Humbert, a pedophile, narrates his "love" for a 12-year-old
  girl (Dolores Haze / Lolita). He presents himself as a Romantic
  aesthete; the novel shows he is a predator who destroyed a life.

  THE FORMAL PROBLEM:
  We are entirely inside Humbert's perspective — his voice is
  brilliant, seductive, self-justifying. The novel requires
  the reader to see through the narrator while being in his mind.
  How?
  ─ Humbert's euphemisms signal evasion.
  ─ Dolores occasionally appears as herself through cracks
    in Humbert's narration — her actual suffering visible despite
    his attempts to aestheticize it.
  ─ The forward references to Dolores's death in childbirth at
    17 appear early — the outcome is visible from within the
    seductive narrative.
  The reader who is seduced by Humbert's prose is exactly who
  Humbert would seduce. The novel implicates its readers.

  THE STYLE:
  English was Nabokov's third language (Russian, French, then
  English). This outsider position gave him access to the language
  that native speakers don't have — he heard sounds, etymologies,
  connotations that familiarity conceals. Every sentence is
  deliberately audible.

PALE FIRE (1962):
  A 999-line poem by the (fictional) poet John Shade.
  A commentary on the poem by the (fictional) scholar Charles
  Kinbote.
  The commentary gradually reveals that Kinbote is not who he
  says, is obsessed with his own narrative, and has nothing to
  do with the poem he's annotating.
  The novel has no "main text" — the poem and commentary are
  equally present and equally unreliable.
  Possible reading: Shade wrote everything including Kinbote;
  Kinbote invented Shade; neither is "real" within the novel.

THE GIFT (1937–38, Russian):
  Nabokov's Russian masterpiece — a novel about a Russian writer
  in exile in Berlin writing a novel. The novel within the novel
  is a biography of Chernyshevsky (the utilitarian Dostoevsky
  attacked in Notes from Underground). The outer novel parodies
  and celebrates literary tradition simultaneously.
```

## Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980) — Fiction

Sartre is primarily treated in philosophy (09-20TH-CENTURY-CONTINENTAL.md) but his fiction makes philosophical positions dramatically concrete.

### Nausea (La Nausée, 1938)

```
THE NOVEL: Antoine Roquentin's diary in the fictional town of
  Bouville. He is researching the life of an 18th-century aristocrat.
  He begins to experience "nausea" — a sensation of the brute
  contingent existence of things, including himself.

THE PHILOSOPHICAL CONTENT:
  ─ The chestnut tree root: Roquentin looks at a chestnut tree
    root in a park and is overwhelmed by its sheer "thereness" —
    its existence without justification, without essence.
    This is the phenomenological insight of Being and Nothingness
    dramatized: existence precedes essence. The root exists first;
    its "meaning" is imposed afterward.
  ─ The "de trop" (superfluous): everything is superfluous —
    nothing has to be, and nothing has inherent purpose.
    This is freedom as nausea — the absence of determined meaning
    is terrifying as well as liberating.

THE ANTI-HUMANISM:
  The bourgeois inhabitants of Bouville ("Mudtown") are defined
  by their portraits in the town hall — they have "rights" to
  existence because they performed social functions.
  Roquentin has no such rights. He exists without justification.
  This is existentialist freedom encountered as dread.

SARTRE'S PLAYS AND STORIES:
  "No Exit" (Huis Clos, 1944): three people locked in a room
    together (Hell = other people). The famous line: "L'enfer,
    c'est les autres" — not misanthropy but a precise claim:
    other people's gaze constitutes my identity whether I want
    it or not. I cannot escape the "look" that defines me.
  "The Wall" (1939): prisoner awaiting execution invents a
    lie about his comrades' location; the comrades happen to
    be there; they are caught. The irony of existence: even
    arbitrary choices have consequences.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Borges is a writer of puzzles and games** — The labyrinths and puzzles in Borges are philosophical arguments conducted in fictional form. "The Garden of Forking Paths" is a serious argument about the nature of time. "Tlön" is a serious argument about how ideology replaces reality. The "game" surface conceals genuine philosophical work.

**Magical realism is an exotic Latin American style** — Magical realism is a precise response to a specific cultural situation: the co-presence of indigenous, African, and European ontologies in Latin America. The magic is not imported from fantasy tradition — it derives from oral traditions in which the supernatural is continuous with the natural. Applying the technique mechanically outside that cultural context produces decoration, not the original argument.

**Camus is a pessimist** — He explicitly argued against pessimism. Revolt against the Absurd — living in full consciousness of meaninglessness without suicide and without false consolation — is not pessimism but a form of defiant joy. Sisyphus is happy. The Plague ends with the determination to continue.

**Beckett is nihilistic** — Beckett's work persistently refuses pure nihilism. "You must go on, I can't go on, I'll go on." The refusal to stop — the persistence despite meaninglessness — is the moral center of his work. He is the opposite of the comfortably nihilistic; he makes continuation feel heroic.

**Nabokov's style is mere aestheticism** — Nabokov's style is his ethics. In Lolita, the reader who is seduced by the prose is complicit in Humbert's self-deception — the style IS the novel's moral argument. Nabokov insisted that art makes demands on the reader's attention and critical consciousness; passive consumption of beauty is a form of the poshlost' he despised.
