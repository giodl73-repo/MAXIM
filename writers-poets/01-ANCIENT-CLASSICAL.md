# Ancient Classical Literature — Homer, Sappho, Virgil, Ovid, Horace

## The Foundation of Western Literature

Ancient Greek and Latin literature established the forms and conventions that Western literature has been responding to ever since:

```
GREEK LITERARY FORMS (800–300 BCE)
  EPIC: Homer (Iliad, Odyssey) — long narrative poem, oral tradition
  LYRIC: Sappho, Alcaeus, Pindar — personal feeling in song
  DRAMA:
    Tragedy: Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides
    Comedy: Aristophanes (Old), Menander (New)
  HISTORY: Herodotus (narrative), Thucydides (analytic)

LATIN LITERARY FORMS (200 BCE – 100 CE)
  EPIC: Virgil (Aeneid) — deliberately literary, not oral
  LYRIC: Horace (Odes), Catullus, Tibullus, Propertius
  LOVE ELEGY: Ovid (Amores, Ars Amatoria, Metamorphoses)
  SATIRE: Horace, Juvenal, Persius
  PROSE: Cicero (rhetoric), Caesar (history), Tacitus (history)
```

The relationship between Greek and Latin is one of influence and transformation: Romans learned from Greek models, competed with them, and repurposed them for Roman purposes.

## Homer (c. 8th century BCE)

"Homer" is a name attached to the two great Greek epics. Whether this was a single person, a tradition of performances, or a school of poets is genuinely unknown. The Homeric Question (scholarly debate about the authorship and composition of the poems) has produced 200 years of scholarship without resolution.

### Oral Composition

The Iliad and Odyssey were originally composed and transmitted orally — memorized and performed by professional bards (aoidoi). This has major implications for how they're structured:

```
  ORAL FORMULAIC COMPOSITION (Milman Parry, 1930):

  Parry analyzed the repeated phrases in Homer ("wine-dark sea,"
  "rosy-fingered dawn," "swift-footed Achilles") and showed they
  are metrically shaped units — formulae that fit specific
  positions in the hexameter line.

  A bard composing in performance doesn't compose word by word —
  he composes formula by formula, choosing the metrically appropriate
  phrase from a large repertoire.

  "Wine-dark sea" (oinops pontos) fills an exact portion of the
  hexameter. When Homer needs to describe the sea in a particular
  metrical position, this phrase slots in.

  IMPLICATION:
  ─ Repetition is a compositional feature, not laziness.
  ─ The formulae are thematic tags — they carry accumulated meaning.
  ─ The bard shapes an inherited tradition, not a unique text.
  ─ The text we have is a transcription of a performance tradition,
    probably stabilized in Athens in the 6th century BCE.
```

### The Iliad

```
  BASICS:
  Setting: 10th year of the Trojan War (the full war is assumed known)
  Plot: The wrath of Achilles — its cause, consequences, and resolution
  Length: 15,693 lines in 24 books

  NOT A STORY OF THE WHOLE TROJAN WAR:
  The Iliad covers about 51 days of the war's final year.
  It begins with Achilles' quarrel with Agamemnon over a war prize
  (Briseis), and ends with the funeral of Hector — not with Troy's fall.

  THE STRUCTURE:
  Wrath (menis) — the opening word → loss of Briseis → withdrawal from battle
  → Patroclus fights in Achilles' armor → Patroclus killed by Hector
  → Achilles returns, kills Hector → Priam ransoms Hector's body
  → Funeral

  THE AMBIGUITY:
  Achilles is the greatest warrior but is also petulant, self-destructive,
  and brings disaster on his own people through his withdrawal.
  His aristeia (greatest exploit) comes at the cost of his dearest companion.
  He knows he will die young; the poem is about his choice to embrace that.

  HOMER'S GODS:
  The gods intervene constantly. This is not random — each god has
  particular interests and allegiances. The Trojan War is also a war
  among the Olympians. The gods are like the structural forces
  (fate, nature, social constraint) that determine what humans
  can and cannot do.
```

### The Odyssey

```
  BASICS:
  Setting: Poseidon's wrath; 10 years of wandering after Troy's fall
  Plot: Odysseus' return to Ithaca; his recognition and revenge
  Length: 12,109 lines

  THE STRUCTURE:
  ─ TELEMACHY (Books 1–4): Odysseus' son Telemachus seeks news.
  ─ ODYSSEUS' WANDERINGS (Books 5–12): told in flashback by Odysseus himself.
  ─ RETURN AND RECOGNITION (Books 13–24): disguise, test, massacre of suitors.

  THIS IS A DIFFERENT KIND OF EPIC:
  ─ The Iliad is about honor, community, and heroic death.
  ─ The Odyssey is about cunning, self-preservation, and homecoming.
  ─ Odysseus survives through intelligence (metis) not force (bia).
  ─ The Odyssey is the origin of the road-trip narrative; the
    journey-as-knowledge structure; the disguised-hero-tests-his-household.

  THE NARRATIVE SOPHISTICATION:
  ─ In medias res: begins with Odysseus on Calypso's island, year 10.
  ─ Embedded flashback (Books 9–12): Odysseus tells his own story
    to the Phaeacians — the first sustained first-person fictional narrative.
  ─ Multiple recognition scenes (dog Argos, nurse Eurycleia, Penelope)
    — graduated emotional climax.
  ─ Penelope's strategic delay (weaving/unweaving the shroud) matches
    Odysseus' cunning — she is the female counterpart.
```

## Sappho (c. 620–570 BCE)

Sappho was from Lesbos and wrote lyric poetry in the Aeolic dialect. Nine books of her poetry were known in antiquity; we have about 650 lines, mostly fragments, many recovered from papyrus in Egypt in the 19th–20th centuries.

### The Sapphic Stanza

Sappho invented the Sapphic stanza (used by Catullus and Horace):

```
  SAPPHIC STANZA (4 lines):
  Line 1-3: ─ ─ ─ ─  ─ ─ ─  ─ ─ ─
  (11 syllables: long-short-long-long / long-short-long / long-short-long-long)
  Line 4:   ─ ─ ─ ─  ─
  (5 syllables: long-short-long-long-long)

  This is not just metrical convention — the stanza shape creates
  a characteristic rhythm: three long lines of thought / one short
  conclusive line. A form that enacts contemplation and summary.
```

### Fragment 31 (Phainetai Moi)

```
  THE POEM: A speaker observes a man seated opposite a woman
  she desires. The man appears like a god. The speaker describes
  her own physical symptoms: heart fluttering, voice failing,
  tongue breaking, fire running under skin, blindness, ringing
  ears, sweat, trembling, pallor.

  WHAT SAPPHO INVENTED:
  ─ The first-person lyric speaker with a specific emotional situation.
  ─ The detailed phenomenology of desire — physical symptoms as
    the content of subjective experience.
  ─ The "blazon" of symptoms became the standard love-poem convention
    (Catullus, Petrarch, Shakespeare's "My mistress' eyes" — all
    respond to this inventory).
  ─ Longinus cited this poem in "On the Sublime" as the supreme
    example of psychological intensity.
```

Sappho's poems were school texts in Greece and Rome. Plato called her "the tenth Muse." The fragments are among the most powerful compressed poems in any language.

## Virgil (70–19 BCE)

Virgil is the supreme Latin poet and the pivotal figure connecting ancient and medieval literature. Dante chose him as his guide in the Divine Comedy. He was called by Tennyson "the lord of language."

### The Aeneid

The Aeneid (c. 29–19 BCE) is the official epic of the Roman Empire — commissioned (in effect) by Augustus, whose legitimacy traced back through Julius Caesar to Aeneas, Trojan hero and legendary founder of Rome.

```
  STRUCTURE:
  Books 1–6: The Odyssey (sea journey, adventures, underworld descent)
  Books 7–12: The Iliad (war in Italy, Aeneas vs. Turnus)

  THE HERO:
  Aeneas is "pietas personified" — dutiful, patient, subordinating
  personal desire to cosmic mission. He carries his father on his
  back (literally) and his household gods as Troy burns.
  He is NOT Achilles (passionate, self-willed) or Odysseus (cunning).
  He is the ideal Roman civic hero: puts duty to gods, family, and
  state above personal glory.

  THE TRAGEDY:
  Aeneas loves Dido, Queen of Carthage. He must leave her to found
  Rome. She kills herself. Aeneas descends to the underworld and
  sees her — she turns from him without a word.

  Virgil doesn't present this as triumphant. The cost of Aeneas'
  mission is enormous. Dido is the sympathetic figure.
  "Sunt lacrimae rerum et mentem mortalia tangunt." —
  "There are tears for things, and mortal matters touch the mind."
  This line (Book I) is perhaps the most quoted line in any language
  for capturing the pathos of inevitable loss.

  LITERARY VS. ORAL EPIC:
  ─ Homer: composed for oral performance, transmitted across generations.
  ─ Virgil: composed as written literature, deeply allusive to Homer.
  ─ Every major episode in the Aeneid echoes and responds to Homer.
  ─ This intertextuality is the point: Virgil is writing within
    the tradition, transforming it to Roman purposes.
  ─ Where Homer's gods are arbitrary and human, Virgil's are agents
    of fate working toward the destined glory of Rome.
```

## Horace (65–8 BCE)

Horace is the supreme lyric poet of Latin — master of the ode form and the epistolary poem.

```
  THE ODES (23 BCE):
  4 books of short lyric poems on love, friendship, wine, mortality, and Rome.

  "CARPE DIEM" (Odes I.11):
  The full line: "carpe diem, quam minimum credula postero"
  "Seize the day, trusting as little as possible in tomorrow."

  CONTEXT: Leuconoë is consulting astrologers to find out
  how long she will live. Horace says: don't ask (tu ne quaesieris).
  Whatever length Jupiter assigns, accept it. Strain your wines.
  Cut back hope to the measure of a short span.
  While we talk, envious time will have fled.
  Seize the day.

  This is not youthful hedonism — it is philosophical maturity.
  The recognition of mortality drives not despair but presence.
  The ode form (short, compressed, elegant) enacts the advice.

  "ARS POETICA" (epistle c. 19 BCE):
  The most influential text on literary theory in antiquity.
  Key principles: unity of form and content; decorum (each genre
  has its appropriate style); the purpose of poetry is to
  "delight and instruct" (aut prodesse aut delectare).
  This last formulation governed literary theory for 1500 years.
```

## Ovid (43 BCE – 17 CE)

Ovid is the most influential Latin poet for the Middle Ages and Renaissance. He was exiled by Augustus (8 CE, for unclear reasons — probably the Ars Amatoria and a scandal) to Tomis on the Black Sea, where he died.

### Metamorphoses

The Metamorphoses (8 CE) is a 15-book continuous narrative poem retelling Greek and Roman myths — all organized around the theme of transformation. It covers from the creation of the world to Julius Caesar's apotheosis.

```
  STRUCTURE: 15 books, 250 myths, no natural stopping points.
  Each story transforms into the next: shape-shifting matter.

  THE TRICK: Ovid takes the myths seriously enough to tell them
  skillfully, and not seriously enough to moralize. The gods
  are not awe-inspiring — they're petty, lustful, vain.
  The human figures are often more sympathetically drawn.

  TECHNOLOGY: Ovid's narrative technique is extraordinary.
  ─ Embedded narrative: stories within stories within stories.
  ─ Transformation as metaphor: identities are not fixed;
    everything changes.
  ─ Irony: the tone is detached, witty, occasionally sarcastic
    about the conventions it's deploying.

  INFLUENCE:
  The Metamorphoses is the single most important source for
  classical myth in the Middle Ages and Renaissance:
  Dante, Chaucer, Shakespeare, Titian, Bernini, Milton
  all draw directly on Ovid.
  It was the Latin text students knew best.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Homer invented the stories** — Homer composed versions of already-existing story traditions. The Trojan War cycle was widely known before Homer. What Homer did was shape, organize, and create the definitive literary versions. The stories themselves predate him.

**The Aeneid is simple propaganda** — The Aeneid celebrates Rome and Augustus, but it also presents the costs of empire with genuine sympathy. Dido, Turnus, Mezentius, Lausus — the Aeneid's "enemies" often inspire more pity than its hero. Virgil was at least ambivalent about the violence required to found Rome, and probably more than ambivalent.

**Sappho was a lesbian** — "Lesbian" (from Lesbos) is derived from her name; that's why the island gives the word. Her poems express intense desire for women. Her personal life, relationships, and social role (possibly the leader of a female chorus or education circle) are genuinely unknown beyond the poems.

**Ovid's Metamorphoses is just a myth compendium** — It's a structurally sophisticated literary work with a continuous thematic argument: everything changes, nothing stays the same, the universe is in constant transformation. The form (each story transforms into the next) enacts the content. It's not a reference book — it's an argument about mutability.
