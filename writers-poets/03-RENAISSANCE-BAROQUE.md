# Renaissance and Baroque Literature — Shakespeare, Cervantes, Milton, Montaigne

## The Context: Print, Humanism, and the Vernacular

```
THE CONDITIONS FOR RENAISSANCE LITERATURE:

  PRINTING PRESS (Gutenberg, ~1450):
  ─ Books become cheaper; literacy spreads; writers can address
    wider audiences and engage literary tradition systematically.
  ─ Texts can be distributed, corrected, and standardized.
  ─ The idea of the "author" (someone whose name attaches to a
    fixed text) becomes meaningful.

  HUMANISM:
  ─ Recovery of classical texts in better editions.
  ─ Rhetoric and eloquence as intellectual ideals.
  ─ The "uomo universale" — the person of broad cultivation.
  ─ Literature as a form of knowledge, not just entertainment.

  REFORMATION (1517):
  ─ Protestant translation of Bible into vernaculars.
  ─ Religious controversy drives prose style (polemic, sermon).
  ─ Milton's Paradise Lost is inconceivable without the Protestant tradition.

  NEW LITERARY FORMS:
  ─ The essay (Montaigne invents it, 1580)
  ─ The novel (Cervantes, 1605)
  ─ The full-length secular tragedy (Elizabethan/Jacobean drama)
  ─ The sonnet sequence (Petrarch → Sidney → Shakespeare)
```

## Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592)

Montaigne invented the essay — and with it, a new kind of knowledge: self-knowledge through sustained personal reflection.

### The Essays (Essais, 1580, 1588, 1595)

"Essay" comes from the French essai — an attempt, a trial. Montaigne describes himself as the subject and the object of his experiment:

```
  "I study myself more than any other subject. It is my metaphysics.
  It is my physics." — Essays, III.13

  WHAT THE ESSAYS DO:
  ─ Begin with an occasion (a classical text, an anecdote, a question).
  ─ Follow the mind's associative movements.
  ─ Arrive at personal reflection without systematic conclusion.
  ─ Acknowledge contradiction, uncertainty, change.

  "On Experience" (III.13) is his last and greatest essay:
  It moves from the impossibility of universal rules, through
  personal custom and habit, to the wisdom of living comfortably
  in the body — to being fully human rather than straining
  toward the superhuman.

  THE FORMAL INNOVATION:
  The essay is a form without a required conclusion.
  It mimics the movement of thought itself.
  Where philosophy demands systematic argument, the essay demands
  only sustained honest exploration.
```

### Skepticism and Self-Knowledge

Montaigne's motto: "Que sais-je?" — "What do I know?" He read the Pyrrhonist skeptics (Sextus Empiricus recovered in his era) and applied their suspension of judgment to his own life — not as philosophy but as practice.

This makes him the father of the modern personal essay, and the predecessor of much of what we call "creative nonfiction." Virginia Woolf's essays, David Foster Wallace's, James Baldwin's — all trace back to Montaigne's discovery that the self reflecting on the world is a legitimate literary subject.

## Miguel de Cervantes (1547–1616)

Don Quixote (1605, Part I; 1615, Part II) is regularly called the first modern novel. Whether or not this is exactly right, it is the first work of fiction that is self-consciously about fiction.

### What Don Quixote Actually Does

```
  SURFACE PLOT: An elderly Spanish gentleman has read too many
  chivalric romances and decides he is a knight errant.
  He rides out on a broken horse with a fat peasant as squire
  (Sancho Panza) and interprets the world through the lens of
  romance: windmills = giants, inns = castles, barmaids = noble ladies.

  THE COMPLICATIONS:

  1. IT'S FUNNY AND TRAGIC SIMULTANEOUSLY:
     Don Quixote's delusion is comic (he fights windmills).
     But his commitment to his ideal is admirable (he genuinely
     tries to be a knight, to serve justice, to protect the weak).
     Sancho Panza is a practical peasant who gradually absorbs
     some of his master's idealism.
     By Part II, we're not sure who is more deluded — the man
     who believes in fictional ideals or the world that has none.

  2. IT'S SELF-REFLEXIVE:
     Part I (1605) was a bestseller. In Part II (1615), other
     characters have read Part I and expect Quixote to behave
     as he did there. Quixote has become a character-in-the-world
     who is also a character-in-a-book.
     Also in Part II: someone published a fake Part II (by Avellaneda).
     Cervantes has Quixote encounter characters from the fake book
     and refute them.
     This is metafiction 400 years early.

  3. THE PHILOSOPHICAL QUESTION:
     What is the relationship between the stories we tell and
     the world we live in? Don Quixote makes narrative frames visible:
     the romances are "wrong" frames; but what's the "right" frame?
     Sancho's pragmatic world of eating and sleeping?
     The novel doesn't say.
```

### The Novel's Self-Awareness

Part I is narrated by "Cervantes" who claims to be translating from an Arabic source (Cide Hamete Benengeli) — an elaborate fiction of authenticity. The novel presents itself as a found document. This is the first major use of the unreliable/constructed narrator in fiction.

## William Shakespeare (1564–1616)

Shakespeare is the central figure of English literature — the writer who every subsequent writer in English (and many outside it) must reckon with. He wrote 37 plays, 154 sonnets, and two narrative poems.

### What Made Shakespeare Different

```
  HIS PREDECESSORS (Marlowe, Kyd, Lyly):
  ─ Dramatic verse can be powerful, but characters tend to be
    consistent (the Machiavel, the hero, the fool).
  ─ Moral positions are clear.

  SHAKESPEARE'S INNOVATION:
  ─ Characters change. Hamlet's famous indecision is itself dynamic.
    Prince Hal becomes Henry V through a transformation that is
    psychologically convincing.
  ─ Contradictory motivations coexist. Iago's reasons for hating
    Othello multiply and contradict each other — Coleridge called
    this "motiveless malignity."
  ─ Language is the character. The verse style of each major
    character is distinctive and shifts as the character shifts.
```

### The Problem Plays and Tragedies

```
  THE TRAGEDIES (c. 1600–1606): The peak.

  HAMLET (c. 1601):
  A prince is told by his father's ghost to revenge his murder.
  He delays. Why? No single answer satisfies — and the play
  was deliberately written so that none does.
  The character is a moving target: contemplative, then violent;
  philosophical, then crude; loving, then cruel.
  The play itself is formally unstable (different texts; famous
  soliloquies address the audience directly).

  KING LEAR (1605):
  An old king divides his kingdom between daughters based on
  flattery, drives away the honest daughter, is abandoned by
  the flatterers. Goes mad. Is reconciled with Cordelia.
  She dies. He dies.
  No redemption. The ending is not tragic in the classical sense
  (a flaw + its punishment); it is catastrophic — good and evil
  both destroyed by a process that doesn't differentiate.

  OTHELLO (1603):
  Manipulation, jealousy, race, and love. Iago convinces Othello
  that his wife is unfaithful. Othello kills her. This is wrong.
  The play explores how self-deception works — Othello convinces
  himself because he needs to, not because Iago's "evidence" is
  actually convincing.
```

### The Sonnets

```
  154 sonnets (published 1609), probably written c. 1593–1601.
  The sequence addresses two figures: a "Fair Youth" (unnamed, male)
  and a "Dark Lady" (unnamed, female).

  SHAKESPEARE'S INVERSIONS OF PETRARCH:
  The Petrarchan convention: the beloved is beautiful, the lover
  suffers devotedly.

  Shakespeare: "My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun" (130)
  — the Dark Lady is systematically un-beautified by all conventional
  blazon. The poem ends: "And yet, by heaven, I think my love as rare
  / As any she belied with false compare."
  The point: conventional blazon falsifies. His love is real, therefore
  it doesn't need conventional comparison.

  THE FAMOUS SONNETS:
  18 ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?") — time defeated by verse.
  29 ("When in disgrace with fortune") — remembered love as restoration.
  73 ("That time of year thou mayst in me behold") — autumn, age, death.
  116 ("Let me not to the marriage of true minds") — definition of love.
  129 ("Th'expense of spirit in a waste of shame") — lust as self-destruction.
```

## John Milton (1608–1674)

Milton is the last great Renaissance figure and the first great modern one. He was deeply involved in the English Civil War (1642–51), served Cromwell's government as Secretary for Foreign Tongues, and went blind before writing his masterpiece.

### Paradise Lost (1667)

```
  STRUCTURE: 12 books, approximately 10,000 lines of blank verse
  (unrhymed iambic pentameter).

  SUBJECT: The fall of Satan; the creation; the temptation and
  Fall of Adam and Eve.

  STATED PURPOSE: "To justify the ways of God to men."
  (Book I, line 26)

  THE OPENING IN MEDIAS RES:
  Book I opens in Hell — Satan and his fallen angels just defeated
  by God, lying in the burning lake. Satan rises, rallies the troops,
  builds Pandaemonium. This is the epic's first sustained action.

  WHY SATAN IS THE MOST POWERFUL FIGURE:
  Satan's speeches are the most rhetorically brilliant in the poem.
  "Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven" is technically evil
  but memorably defiant. Satan is the figure of heroic individualism —
  the Romantic poets (Blake, Shelley) read Satan as the true hero.
  Milton's intention: Satan's rhetoric is brilliant but wrong.
  His grandeur is real; his cause is corrupt.

  THE PROTESTANT THEOLOGICAL ARGUMENT:
  ─ God gives Adam and Eve free will — they must be able to sin
    or their obedience is meaningless.
  ─ Satan's temptation appeals to their desire for knowledge
    ("ye shall be as gods, knowing good and evil").
  ─ The Fall is fortunate (felix culpa) — it necessitates
    Christ's redemption, which brings a greater good.
  ─ Adam and Eve leave Eden hand in hand — "the world was all
    before them." Their future is open.
```

### The Blank Verse

Milton's blank verse is the supreme achievement in that form:

```
  VERSE PARAGRAPH vs. LINE:
  Milton doesn't use the line as the unit — he uses the paragraph.
  Sentences run across lines ("enjambment"), creating long
  syntactic arcs that carry the weight of epic narrative.

  "Of Man's First Disobedience, and the Fruit
  Of that Forbidden Tree, whose mortal taste
  Brought Death into the World, and all our woe,
  With loss of Eden, till one greater Man
  Restore us, and regain the blissful Seat,
  Sing Heavenly Muse..."

  The sentence delays the main verb ("Sing") for 6 lines,
  loading the subject with clauses. This is the Virgilian
  period structure — but in English.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Don Quixote is the first novel** — Murasaki Shikibu's The Tale of Genji (c. 1021) is a novel by any reasonable definition. Ancient Greek prose fiction (Longus' Daphnis and Chloe) qualifies. The claim about Don Quixote is specifically about the self-reflexive, self-aware European novel — the novel that knows it is a novel.

**Shakespeare wrote alone** — Shakespeare collaborated on several plays (with John Fletcher, probably with George Peele). The texts we have went through multiple editorial interventions between composition and the First Folio (1623, 7 years after his death). The "Shakespeare" we read is already a constructed artifact.

**Milton's Satan is the hero** — The Romantic misreading (Blake, Shelley). Milton's Satan is the most rhetorically powerful figure, but the poem carefully shows his self-deception and degradation as it proceeds. By Books IX–X, Satan is reduced to a serpent. The heroism is real but the cause is false. Blake said Milton was "of the devil's party without knowing it" — but Milton did know.

**Montaigne is just personal rambling** — The essays are formally constructed. "On Experience" moves from epistemological skepticism to bodily wisdom in a coherent argument. The wandering is strategic — it performs the point that knowledge is not systematic. This is form and content aligned, not absence of form.
