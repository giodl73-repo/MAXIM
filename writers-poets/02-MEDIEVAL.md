# Medieval Literature — Dante, Chaucer, Petrarch, Boccaccio

## The Medieval Literary Context

Medieval literature is not a single tradition — it spans roughly 1000 years, multiple languages, and radically different cultural contexts. The key move from ancient to medieval: literature becomes primarily religious in organization and purpose, and then — in the 12th–14th centuries — the vernacular languages (Italian, French, English, German) assert themselves against Latin.

```
MEDIEVAL LITERARY LANDSCAPE

  RELIGIOUS/ALLEGORICAL:
  Augustine (Confessions, City of God) — autobiography as spiritual journey
  Beowulf (c. 700–1000) — Old English epic; pagan + Christian overlay
  Chrétien de Troyes (c. 1180) — Arthurian romance; courtly love
  Divine Comedy (Dante, 1308–21) — allegorical journey; vernacular
  Piers Plowman (Langland, c. 1370) — English allegory

  SECULAR/LYRIC:
  Troubadour poetry (Occitan, c. 1100–1300) — courtly love lyric
  Minnesang (German, c. 1150–1300) — courtly lyric
  Petrarch (Italian sonnets, c. 1340) — secular love lyric
  Canterbury Tales (Chaucer, c. 1390) — vernacular social portrait

  PROSE NARRATIVE:
  Decameron (Boccaccio, 1353) — framed short fiction
  Malory, Le Morte d'Arthur (c. 1470) — Arthurian prose romance
```

## Dante Alighieri (1265–1321)

Dante is the defining figure of medieval Italian literature and one of the three or four greatest poets in any language. He was also a political figure, philosopher, and linguist who was exiled from Florence in 1302 and never returned.

### The Divine Comedy (Commedia, 1308–1321)

```
  STRUCTURE: 3 canticles × 33 cantos each + 1 introductory canto
  = 100 cantos total. Terza rima (aba bcb cdc...) throughout.
  ~14,000 lines.

  INFERNO (Hell): Dante + Virgil descend through 9 circles.
  PURGATORIO (Purgatory): Dante + Virgil ascend 7 terraces.
  PARADISO (Heaven): Dante + Beatrice ascend 9 heavenly spheres.

  THE JOURNEY: From the dark wood of error, through the
  recognition of sin, to purification, to union with God.

  ALLEGORY LEVELS (Dante's four levels, from his letter to Cangrande):
  1. Literal: a man journeys through the afterlife
  2. Allegorical: the soul's journey toward God
  3. Moral: how to escape sin and achieve virtue
  4. Anagogical: the soul's destiny in eternity
```

### Why the Vernacular Matters

Dante's choice to write in Italian (Florentine vernacular) rather than Latin was a programmatic political and philosophical decision. He argued for it in De Vulgari Eloquentia (On Vernacular Eloquence):

```
  THE ARGUMENT:
  ─ Latin is a fixed, artificial language (a grammatica).
  ─ Vernacular languages are living, natural — the primary
    human speech.
  ─ Italian has the potential for high literary use.
  ─ Poetry in the vernacular reaches more people and can
    express things Latin cannot.

  THE CONSEQUENCE:
  Dante writing the Commedia in Italian legitimizes Italian
  as a literary language. Petrarch and Boccaccio follow.
  Together, these three (the Three Crowns of Italian literature)
  establish the Florentine dialect as the basis of standard Italian.

  This is exactly what Chaucer does for English: write in the
  vernacular at the highest literary level, establishing a standard.
```

### The Contrapasso Principle

```
  CONTRAPASSO (counter-suffering): In the Inferno, each punishment
  mirrors the sin in kind — ironic justice.

  EXAMPLES:
  ─ The lustful are buffeted by eternal storm (they were governed
    by their passions in life; now a passion — wind — governs them).
  ─ The gluttonous lie in endless filth, guarded by Cerberus.
  ─ The wrathful fight each other in the River Styx.
  ─ Those who couldn't choose (the indifferent) endlessly chase
    a blank banner — they chose nothing, now they have nothing.
  ─ The traitors are frozen in ice (they were cold-hearted;
    now they're literally frozen).

  THEOLOGICAL STRUCTURE:
  The Inferno is not sadistic fantasy — it is a system of
  justice where punishment is appropriate to crime.
  Each circle illuminates the nature of the sin it punishes.
```

### The Philosophical System

The Comedy embeds a complete medieval cosmology (Aristotle + Neoplatonism + Christianity):
- Earth at the center; 9 celestial spheres; Empyrean beyond
- Hell underground; Purgatory as a mountain in the southern hemisphere
- Heaven as the celestial spheres in ascending order of proximity to God

Virgil represents natural reason — he can guide Dante through Hell and Purgatory but cannot enter Heaven (because he lived before Christ). Beatrice represents divine grace and revelation — she takes over in Paradiso.

## Geoffrey Chaucer (c. 1343–1400)

Chaucer is the first major English poet — he established English as a literary language with works of the highest technical sophistication.

### The Canterbury Tales (c. 1387–1400)

```
  FRAME: 30 pilgrims traveling from London to Canterbury.
  Each tells two tales each way = 120 planned. Actually:
  ~24 tales (some incomplete), in various stages of completion.

  THE FRAME AS SOCIAL PORTRAIT:
  The General Prologue is one of the great set-pieces of English literature:
  each pilgrim described in terms of their profession, appearance, habits,
  and moral character — with Chaucerian irony that lets readers see
  more than the narrator claims to see.

  THE VARIETY:
  The Knight's Tale: Chivalric romance (classical epic source).
  The Miller's Tale: Fabliau (comic, bawdy, working-class).
  The Pardoner's Tale: Moral exemplum with corrupt teller.
  The Wife of Bath's Tale: Feminist challenge to male authority.
  The Nun's Priest's Tale: Mock-heroic beast fable.

  THE DRAMATIC IRONY:
  The frame creates ironic distance: the Miller interrupts the Knight
  and tells a story that parodies it; the Reeve responds to the Miller
  with a counter-tale. The tales are "in character" for each teller —
  or pointedly not. The Pardoner confesses his own corruption,
  then asks for contributions to his fraudulent pardons.
```

### Technical Innovation

```
  IAMBIC PENTAMETER:
  Chaucer is the first English poet to use iambic pentameter
  systematically and brilliantly. Shakespeare inherits this.

  NARRATIVE RANGE:
  Chaucer could write: high romance, low comedy, religious allegory,
  philosophical dialogue, saint's life, beast fable — all within one
  work. This range is unmatched in English before Shakespeare.

  IRONY:
  Chaucer's "naive narrator" (who doesn't understand what he sees
  and praises rogues for being good at roguery) is a technical
  device for creating critical distance without explicit moral judgment.
  "I kan not glose, I am a rude man" — he says, with great art.
```

## Petrarch (Francesco Petrarca, 1304–1374)

Petrarch is the first modern secular lyric poet and the inventor of the literary persona. His Canzoniere (Songbook) — 366 poems mostly addressed to Laura — established the European sonnet tradition.

### The Petrarchan Sonnet

```
  FORM: 14 lines in iambic pentameter (Italian) or iambic pentameter (English)
  OCTAVE (8 lines): Rhyme scheme ABBAABBA — presents a problem,
                     situation, or argument.
  SESTET (6 lines): Various rhyme schemes — resolves, responds, complicates.
  THE VOLTA: The turn between octave and sestet, where the poem shifts.

  CONTENT CONVENTIONS:
  ─ Blazon: catalogue of the beloved's beauty (eyes, hair, neck, etc.)
  ─ The Laura-Laure pun: laurel branch (victory/poetry) = Laura's name
  ─ Love as paradox: "I find no peace and all my war is done"
    (translated by Wyatt)
  ─ The beloved is unattainable; her indifference causes suffering
  ─ The lover's self is constructed through the poems themselves

  INFLUENCE:
  Petrarchan sonnets → Wyatt and Surrey (English 1530s) →
  Sidney (Astrophel and Stella, 1591) →
  Shakespeare (154 sonnets, who then systematically inverts Petrarchan conventions)
```

### The Secular Lyric "I"

Petrarch is the first writer to present a secular, personal, non-religious inner life as the primary subject of literature. His poems don't teach doctrine or tell a narrative — they trace the shifting states of a consciousness in love.

This is the invention of the modern literary subject: not a character in a story, not an exemplum of a moral, but an "I" whose interior experience is the whole content.

## Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375)

Boccaccio is the first master of Italian prose fiction and the inventor of the novella form.

### The Decameron (1353)

```
  FRAME: 10 young Florentines flee the Black Death (1348) and
  retreat to a country villa for 10 days. Each tells one story
  per day = 100 novellas.

  THE STRUCTURAL INNOVATION:
  ─ The frame narrative is not just decoration — it creates a
    world in which storytelling itself is examined.
  ─ Different days have different themes (Fortune, Wit, Love).
  ─ The tellers comment on each other's stories.
  ─ The act of storytelling is a response to death: narrative
    as a form of survival against plague.

  THE TONE:
  Boccaccio's register ranges from tragic to comic to erotic
  to sentimental. His prose is supple, rhythmically varied,
  psychologically acute. This is the first major European
  prose fiction in the modern sense.

  INFLUENCE:
  Chaucer's Canterbury Tales follows Boccaccio's frame model
  (probably directly — Chaucer traveled to Italy twice and
  likely had access to the Decameron).
  Shakespeare sources several plots here (All's Well That
  Ends Well, Cymbeline).
  The novella form → the short story → all prose fiction.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Medieval" means dark and gloomy** — The Decameron is wickedly funny and cheerfully erotic. Chaucer's Miller's Tale is a comedy of cuckolding. The Troubadour poetry of Provence celebrates desire. Dante contains moments of great tenderness (Francesca and Paolo, Piccarda Donati). The medieval period produced a full range of literary registers.

**Dante is about the afterlife** — The Comedy is set in the afterlife but is about this life — specifically, the moral and spiritual life of a Christian soul and, by extension, Florence's political catastrophe (Dante saw his city's corruption as his civilization's failure). The real subject is human history and its relationship to divine justice.

**Petrarch invented the sonnet** — He perfected and popularized it, but the form originated with Giacomo da Lentini in Sicily around 1235. Petrarch's contribution was applying it systematically to a sustained portrait of interior experience and establishing the formal conventions (octave + sestet + volta) that Shakespeare would later inherit and transform.

**Chaucer's narrator is Chaucer** — The Canterbury Tales narrator ("Chaucer the pilgrim") is not Chaucer the poet but a character he constructs — a naive observer who misses obvious ironies. When the narrator praises the Pardoner's effectiveness, the reader is meant to see what the narrator doesn't. This is Chaucer the poet creating dramatic irony through a constructed persona.
