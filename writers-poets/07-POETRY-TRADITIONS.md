# Poetry Traditions — Blake, Keats, Whitman, Dickinson, Yeats, Eliot, Rilke

## What Poetry Does That Prose Cannot

Poetry exploits the full range of language's resources simultaneously: semantic content, sonic pattern, rhythm, line break, white space, and compression. Where prose can approximate these, poetry makes them structural.

```
THE RESOURCES OF POETRY:

  SEMANTIC:
  ─ Denotation: what the word means.
  ─ Connotation: what it carries — emotional, cultural, historical.
  ─ Ambiguity: multiple simultaneous meanings (poetry often requires this).

  SONIC:
  ─ Phonemic texture: which sounds, how often, in what pattern.
  ─ Rhythm: the pattern of stress (metrical) or weight (free verse).
  ─ Rhyme: end rhyme, internal rhyme, slant rhyme, eye rhyme.

  STRUCTURAL:
  ─ Line break: where the line ends is a syntactic and semantic event.
  ─ White space: silence and pause on the page.
  ─ Stanza: unit of organization (like a paragraph but formally structured).

  COMPRESSION:
  ─ Pound's definition: "poetry is news that stays news."
  ─ Poetry compresses; the reader expands. The gap between what is
    said and what is meant is where the poem lives.

MAJOR FORMS:
  Lyric: a single speaker in a single moment of feeling or thought.
  Ode: formal praise of a subject (Keats's odes).
  Sonnet: 14-line argument (Italian) or 14-line sequence (English).
  Elegy: mourning poem (Milton's Lycidas, Gray's Elegy, Tennyson's In Memoriam).
  Epic: long narrative (Homer, Virgil, Milton, Whitman's Leaves of Grass
        is an extended lyric in epic scope).
  Free verse: no fixed meter but intense attention to rhythm, sound, image.
  Dramatic monologue: a speaker addressing a silent listener (Browning,
        Eliot's Prufrock).
```

## William Blake (1757–1827)

Blake is the most difficult of the Romantics to place — he was already post-Romantic before the Romantic movement existed. He engraved, printed, colored, and sold his own books. He is a poet, visual artist, and prophet simultaneously.

### Songs of Innocence and Experience (1789, 1794)

```
THE STRUCTURE: Two matched sets of poems.
  Songs of Innocence (1789): The world as children see it — pastoral,
    protected, warm. God as shepherd; children as lambs.
  Songs of Experience (1794): The same world — or its counterpart —
    as fallen, exploited, trapped.

PAIRED POEMS:
  "The Lamb" (Innocence) / "The Tyger" (Experience):
  "Little Lamb, who made thee?" — God made you, gentle and meek.
  "Tyger Tyger, burning bright / In the forests of the night / What
   immortal hand or eye / Could frame thy fearful symmetry?"
  The Tyger is not answered. Blake doesn't say God made both lamb
  and tiger — the poem holds the question open. Is creation
  coherent? Can the same creator intend both?

"THE CHIMNEY SWEEPER" (two versions):
  Innocence version: a child sold into chimney sweeping consoles himself
    with dreams of angels releasing him. The poem is NOT celebrating this
    consolation — it's showing how ideology works: the child accepts
    suffering as spiritual preparation.
  Experience version: "And because I am happy, & dance & sing, / They
    think they have done me no injury." The child is now a tool of
    the state, church, and parents who "made up a heaven of our misery."

BLAKE'S SYSTEM:
  Blake developed his own mythological system (Urizen, Los, Orc, etc.)
  in the longer "Prophetic Books" (Milton, Jerusalem).
  Urizen = Reason as tyrant — the god of law, measurement, limitation.
  Los = the prophetic imagination, which fights Urizen.
  This is not eccentricity — it's systematic philosophical argument
  conducted in mythological form.

THE VISUAL DIMENSION:
  Blake's illuminated books must be seen, not just read. The images
  and text are a single unified work. Many copies differ in coloring
  and arrangement — each is a unique artifact.
```

## John Keats (1795–1821)

Keats died at 25. He wrote his odes in a period of a few months in 1819. He is the poet of sensation and mortality — the two are inseparable in his work.

### Negative Capability

```
KEATS'S TERM (from a letter to his brothers, December 1817):
  "Negative Capability, that is when man is capable of being in
  uncertainties, Mysteries, doubts without any irritable reaching
  after fact & reason."

  This is the capacity to hold opposing or unresolved ideas
  without needing to resolve them. Keats saw it as Shakespeare's
  supreme quality.

  The opposite: the "egotistical sublime" (Wordsworth's mode) —
  where the poet's self is the center and controller of the poem.

  THE ODES (1819) demonstrate Negative Capability:
  ─ "Ode to a Nightingale": the bird's song = escape from mortality.
    The speaker is drawn toward immortality/oblivion; the poem
    ends: "Was it a vision, or a waking dream? / Fled is that
    music: — Do I wake or sleep?" — resolution withheld.
  ─ "Ode on a Grecian Urn": "Beauty is truth, truth beauty" —
    the urn's claim. Is this wise or a cold abstraction?
    The poem holds both.
  ─ "To Autumn": the most perfectly executed of the odes.
    No argument; pure sensory presence of ripeness and decline.
    The season is static (suspended at its fullest) and in motion
    (declining). Both simultaneously.

KEATS'S STYLE:
  Sensuousness: every image is physical — smell, touch, taste,
    sound, temperature. The abstract is always grounded in sensation.
  Oxymoron as structure: "unravished bride of quietness"; "cold
    pastoral"; "for ever warm and still to be enjoyed."
  The ode form: Keats perfected the irregular ode (Pindaric model
    adapted) — stanzas of 10 lines, complex rhyme schemes that
    allow both expansion and compression.
```

## Walt Whitman (1819–1892)

Whitman invented American poetry — not just free verse, but the bardic mode: a "I" that is simultaneously personal and national.

### Leaves of Grass (1855–1892)

```
THE PROJECT: A single book, continuously revised and expanded
  throughout Whitman's life. The 1855 first edition is 12 poems.
  The "deathbed edition" (1891–92) has ~400.

THE CENTRAL TECHNIQUE: The long cataloguing line.
  "I celebrate myself, and sing myself,
  And what I assume you shall assume,
  For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you."

  The line unit is not metrical but breath-shaped.
  Repetition and parallelism replace rhyme and meter:
  "the carpenter dresses his plank... the mason is at work...
   the boatmen and clam-diggers arose early and stopped for me..."
  The catalogue = democracy as form. Every occupation, person,
  and place deserves equal formal weight.

"SONG OF MYSELF" (1855):
  Section 5: the mystical union of body and soul.
  Section 24: the poet as pure receptive sensory organ.
  Section 52: "I sound my barbaric yawp over the roofs of the world."
  The poem dissolves the boundary between self and world —
  "I contain multitudes."

"WHEN LILACS LAST IN THE DOORYARD BLOOM'D" (1865):
  Elegy for Lincoln. The three symbols: lilac (love/grief),
  star (Lincoln), hermit thrush (voice of poetry/death).
  The poem moves through grief toward acceptance of death
  as a universal condition — not personal but metaphysical.

WHITMAN'S SCOPE:
  His subject is always simultaneously: the self, the body, sex,
  democracy, America, death, the cosmos. These are not separate
  topics but aspects of the same vision: the individual body
  is the microcosm of the democratic nation is the microcosm
  of the universe.
```

## Emily Dickinson (1830–1886)

Dickinson wrote ~1,800 poems, published 10 in her lifetime. She sent poems to friends in letters; she sewed fascicles (hand-bound booklets) for herself. She is the great formal innovator of 19th-century American poetry.

### The Techniques

```
THE HYMN METER (common meter, long meter, short meter):
  Dickinson borrowed her base meter from Protestant hymnody
  (Isaac Watts's Hymns). Common meter: 8-6-8-6 syllables alternating.
  This means her poems can be sung to "Amazing Grace" and also
  to "The Yellow Rose of Texas" and "The Ballad of Gilligan's Island."
  The base form is familiar, communal, devotional.
  She distorts it: irregular stress, unexpected rhyme, em-dash breaks.

THE DASH:
  Dickinson's dashes are not punctuation — they are pauses, gaps,
  hesitations, simultaneities. They force the reader to hold
  multiple meanings without grammatical resolution:
  "I heard a Fly buzz — when I died —"
  The dash after "buzz" and after "died" creates hesitation within
  each line — the fly and death coexist in the same uncertain moment.

SLANT RHYME:
  Instead of full rhyme, Dickinson uses approximate rhyme:
  "noon" / "stone"; "room" / "storm"; "Day" / "Eternity."
  The near-miss creates unease — the expected resolution is withheld.

COMPRESSION:
  She removes articles, connectives, pronouns:
  "Tell all the Truth but tell it slant —
   Success in Circuit lies"
  Maximum information density. Every word carries weight.

THE SUBJECTS:
  Death (as visitor, as transition, as dissolution).
  Immortality (doubted, desired, uncertain).
  God (doubted — Dickinson was a skeptic in a Calvinist culture).
  Nature (precise observation + sudden metaphysical elevation).
  Consciousness (the self observing itself).

SELECTED POEMS:
  "Because I could not stop for Death —" (479): Death as a
    gentleman caller driving a carriage. The speaker dies without
    noticing; civilization recedes; Eternity is the destination.
  "I heard a Fly buzz — when I died —" (465): The moment of death
    is interrupted by a fly. The grand eschatological moment
    is punctured by the trivial physical world.
  "The Brain is wider than the Sky —" (632): consciousness exceeds
    the physical universe it contains.
  "Much Madness is divinest Sense —" (620): majority rules define
    sanity; disagreement is madness. The poet dissents.
```

## W.B. Yeats (1865–1939)

Yeats spans two eras: he began as a Romantic Symbolist and became a Modernist. His late poems (from The Tower, 1928, onward) are among the most technically accomplished in English.

### The Development

```
EARLY YEATS (1880s–1900s):
  The Celtic Twilight — misty, symbolic, Pre-Raphaelite influence.
  "The Lake Isle of Innisfree" (1888): pastoral escape; longing
    for a simpler Ireland. Beautiful but nostalgic.
  Yeats himself later rejected this mode.

MIDDLE YEATS (1910s):
  The Easter Rising (1916) forces a confrontation with political
  reality. "Easter 1916": the executions of the Irish revolutionaries
  become a meditation on how violence transforms ordinary men
  into myth. Refrain: "A terrible beauty is born."
  "The Second Coming" (1919):
    "Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold;
     Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world..."
  Written in the aftermath of WWI and Russian Revolution.
  The "rough beast" slouching toward Bethlehem — an anti-Messiah,
  the return of a violent cycle. (Yeats's system: history moves
  in 2,000-year "gyres" or spirals.)

LATE YEATS (1920s–1930s): THE MAJOR POEMS:
  "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928): old age, art as immortality,
    the escape from biological decay into the made thing.
    "That is no country for old men" — the body ages; the gold
    Byzantine mosaic does not. The speaker asks to be gathered
    into the "artifice of eternity."
  "Leda and the Swan" (1923): Zeus (as swan) rapes Leda;
    from this: Helen of Troy; from Helen: the Trojan War; from Troy:
    Western civilization. A moment of divine violence contains all
    of history. "Did she put on his knowledge with his power /
    Before the indifferent beak could let her drop?"
  "Among School Children" (1927): Yeats visits a school,
    thinks of his muse Maud Gonne, thinks about aging, arrives at:
    "How can we know the dancer from the dance?" — the question
    of whether art/being can be separated from the artist/body.

YEATS'S SYSTEM (A Vision, 1925, revised 1937):
  Yeats elaborated a cosmological system of gyres (interpenetrating
  spirals), phases of the moon (28 phases corresponding to human
  types), and historical cycles. The system is eccentric but
  it informs the symbolism of the late poems.
  Important: the poems work without knowing the system; knowing
  it adds depth but is not required.
```

## T.S. Eliot (1888–1965)

Eliot is the central figure of Anglo-American Modernist poetry. The Waste Land (1922) is the poem that defined what Modernist poetry was.

### The Waste Land (1922)

```
COMPOSITION:
  Written during Eliot's mental breakdown; finished in a
  Swiss sanatorium. Submitted to Ezra Pound, who cut it
  by roughly half — the version we have is Pound's edit.
  Pound's contribution: removing the narrative connective tissue.
  What remained: fragments.

THE FIVE SECTIONS:
  I. "The Burial of the Dead": Spring as cruel (forcing memory,
     desire from torpor). Fragments of memory, prophecy, social life.
  II. "A Game of Chess": Two scenes — upper-class woman in a
     perfumed room, nervous breakdown; working-class pub scene,
     closing time. Sterility at both ends of the social scale.
  III. "The Fire Sermon": The river Thames; a typist and a
     carbuncular young man have mechanical sex; Tiresias (who has
     been both sexes) observes.
  IV. "Death by Water": Ten lines. Phlebas the Phoenician drowns.
     A reminder of mortality that punctures the middle of the poem.
  V. "What the Thunder Said": drought; the journey to Emmaus;
     the approach to the Chapel Perilous; the thunder says:
     "Datta, Dayadhvam, Damyata" (Give, Sympathize, Control —
     from the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad). Rain.
     Closing line: "Shantih shantih shantih."

THE TECHNIQUE:
  ─ Allusion: ~50 languages and texts quoted or alluded to without
    explanation. (Eliot's Notes to the poem are themselves partly
    ironic.)
  ─ Juxtaposition: no transitions between fragments. The reader
    must make the connections.
  ─ Multiple voices: no stable speaker. Voices shift, intrude,
    overlap.
  ─ Mythological framework (Eliot cites Jesse Weston's From Ritual
    to Romance): the Grail legend / fertility myth / the Fisher King
    whose wound = the wasteland.

"THE LOVE SONG OF J. ALFRED PRUFROCK" (1915):
  The dramatic monologue as anxiety:
  "Let us go then, you and I, / When the evening is spread out
   against the sky / Like a patient etherized upon a table..."
  Prufrock is the modern anti-hero — unable to act, infinitely
  self-conscious, unable to ask the "overwhelming question."
  "Do I dare to eat a peach?" The bathetic domestic detail is
  the point: the epic scale of self-consciousness produces
  decisions about peaches.

FOUR QUARTETS (1943):
  Eliot's late masterpiece. Four poems named after places.
  The subject: time, redemption, the nature of language.
  "Burnt Norton" opens with the paradox of time:
  "Time present and time past / Are both perhaps present in time
   future / And time future contained in time past."
  Less allusive, more meditative than The Waste Land.
  Closer to mystical theology (Eliot converted to Anglo-Catholicism
  in 1927).
```

## Rainer Maria Rilke (1875–1926)

Rilke is the major German-language Modernist poet — and the poet for whom the question of what poetry is FOR was most urgent.

### Duino Elegies (1912–1922)

```
COMPOSITION: Begun at Duino Castle (Trieste) in 1912 when Rilke
  heard a voice in the wind. He wrote the first two elegies, then
  stopped for 10 years. Completed in a 2-week burst in 1922 at
  Muzot, Switzerland.

THE SUBJECT: What is the human place in the universe?
  The Angels (not Christian angels — Rilke's Angels are forces
  of complete being, beyond the human scale):
  "Who, if I cried out, would hear me among the order of Angels?"
  The Angels don't need us. They exist in pure intensity.
  Humans exist between animals (who live in the Open, unaware
  of their own mortality) and Angels (who are pure being).
  Humans are uniquely conscious of death — and this is both
  our limitation and our gift.

THE ARGUMENT (across the 10 elegies):
  We are not at home in the interpreted world.
  The dead are completed (no longer subject to loss).
  The task: to transform the visible world into the invisible
  through art — to hold things so intensely in consciousness
  that they are preserved in a different form.
  "Once each thing, only once. Once and no more. And we too,
   once. Just once. Never again. But to have been this once,
   even though only once: to have been of the earth, seems
   irrevocable."

RILKE'S STYLE:
  ─ Syntactically difficult: sentences suspend over long spans,
    build pressure, release late.
  ─ Phenomenologically precise: physical things are described
    with unusual attention — a jug, a bridge, a tree — because
    the physical is the site of the invisible.
  ─ The second person as a mode: Rilke often addresses entities
    directly (the Angel, a dead person, a tree) — this creates
    intimacy and urgency.

SONNETS TO ORPHEUS (1922) — written in the same two weeks:
  55 sonnets. Orpheus as the figure of poetic transformation —
  the poet who descends into death and returns.
  The "thing-poem" (Dinggedicht) technique from Neue Gedichte
  (1907–08): describe the thing from inside its own perspective.
  The famous panther poem: "His soft gaze, going past the bars,
  has grown so weary it can hold no more. / It seems to him there
  are a thousand bars / and behind the thousand bars, no world."
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Blake is a mystical eccentric** — Blake's system is philosophically serious: Urizen as Reason-as-tyranny, Los as Imagination-as-resistance, is a coherent critique of Enlightenment rationalism. The "mysticism" is a way of doing philosophy in mythological form. His critique of industrialism and organized religion in the "dark Satanic Mills" passage is pointed social criticism, not fantasy.

**Dickinson is a recluse writing greeting-card sentiments** — Dickinson withdrew from public life deliberately, but her poetry is intellectually rigorous and formally radical. Her slant rhymes and dash-broken syntax create formal instability that enacts the epistemological instability of her subjects (death, God, consciousness). She was well-read in science, theology, and poetry.

**The Waste Land is purely pessimistic** — It ends with "Shantih shantih shantih" — a peace benediction from the Upanishads. Eliot's own Notes say this is "a formal ending to an Upanishad." The poem moves from sterility and fragmentation toward (provisionally) the thunder's instruction: give, sympathize, control. Whether this is adequate consolation is left open.

**Yeats's system is crankery** — The A Vision system of gyres and phases is not separable from the poems — it generates the imagery ("the widening gyre," the phases of the moon). But the poems work as dramatic compressed arguments without needing the system. The system is scaffolding; the poems stand on their own.

**Rilke is vague and spiritual** — The Duino Elegies are philosophically specific: what is the human position relative to consciousness, mortality, and beauty? The "Angels" are not religious entities but a precise formal device — pure being against which human impure being is measured. Rilke's problem is the same one Heidegger (near-contemporary) addresses: what does it mean to be human in a world where Being is concealed?
