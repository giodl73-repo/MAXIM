# Writers & Poets — Overview

## The Big Picture

Literature is the art form that thinks. Where painting works through visual space and music works through time and sound, literature works through language — the same material as thought. This gives it a unique capacity: it can represent consciousness directly, render argument as narrative, and embed philosophy in character.

```
WHAT LITERATURE DOES THAT OTHER FORMS CAN'T:

  Free indirect discourse (Austen, Flaubert):
  "She thought, what a bore." — or are we told she thought that?
  The narrator and character fuse. We're inside and outside simultaneously.

  Stream of consciousness (Joyce, Woolf):
  The unedited flow of thought — associative, fragmented, temporally
  non-linear. Not what a character says but what they are.

  The unreliable narrator (Nabokov, Ishiguro):
  The narrator tells us what happened; the text implies otherwise.
  The reader must reconstruct the truth from the distortion.

  Metafiction (Borges, Nabokov):
  The fiction reflects on its own fictionality.
  Stories that are also arguments about the nature of stories.
```

## Chronological Map

```
ANCIENT (800 BCE – 100 CE)
  Homer (Iliad, Odyssey) — Greek epic, oral tradition
  Sappho — lyric poetry; the fragment
  Greek tragedy (Sophocles, Aeschylus, Euripides)
  Virgil (Aeneid) — Roman epic, literary (not oral) tradition
  Horace, Ovid — Roman lyric and narrative poetry

MEDIEVAL (500 – 1400 CE)
  Dante (Divine Comedy) — vernacular Italian, allegorical
  Chaucer (Canterbury Tales) — Middle English; social portrait
  Petrarch — Italian sonnet; the lyric "I"
  Boccaccio (Decameron) — prose fiction; the novella form

RENAISSANCE AND BAROQUE (1500 – 1700)
  Montaigne — the essay as form; subjective exploration
  Cervantes (Don Quixote) — the novel's origin; self-reflexive fiction
  Shakespeare — drama as philosophical poetry
  Milton (Paradise Lost) — the epic repurposed for Protestant theology

18TH AND 19TH CENTURY NOVEL
  Defoe, Swift, Sterne — English novel's emergence
  Austen — the novel of manners; free indirect discourse
  Dickens, Flaubert, Balzac, Tolstoy, Dostoevsky — the realist novel
  Hardy, James — realism's crisis

MODERNISM (1890 – 1940)
  Joyce (Ulysses, Finnegans Wake) — stream of consciousness
  Woolf — the lyric novel; consciousness as subject
  Proust (In Search of Lost Time) — memory and time
  Kafka — parable and absurdity
  Mann, Musil — the encyclopedic novel

20TH CENTURY WORLD AND POETRY
  Borges — philosophical fiction; the labyrinth
  Garcia Marquez — magical realism
  Beckett — the theater of failure; language's impossibility
  Nabokov — the novel as aesthetic game
  Morrison, Achebe, Rushdie, Coetzee — postcolonial literature
```

## Why Literary Form Matters

This directory focuses on what each writer actually did technically, not just what they wrote about:

```
  NARRATIVE TECHNIQUE:
  Who is narrating? From what temporal position?
  How reliable are they? What is the gap between their account
  and what actually happened?

  POINT OF VIEW (POV):
  First-person (I) — direct; limited to narrator's knowledge
  Third-person limited — one character's consciousness
  Third-person omniscient — narrator knows all
  Free indirect discourse — third-person but "infected" by
    character's voice/thought

  TEMPORALITY:
  Linear chronology (most fiction)
  In medias res (begin in the middle; flashback)
  Proust's involuntary memory (the past intrudes into the present)
  Joyce's single-day with all of history compressed

  LANGUAGE:
  Austen's irony: what the sentence says vs. what it means
  Joyce's puns: multiple meanings in single words
  Borges' precision: philosophical arguments in story form
  Morrison's syntax: fragmented to perform fragmented identity
```

## Key Terms Reference

| Term | What It Is | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Epic** | Long narrative poem of heroic deeds; invocation of muse; begins in medias res | Iliad, Odyssey, Aeneid |
| **Free indirect discourse** | Third-person narration that adopts character's voice without quotation marks | Austen, Flaubert |
| **Stream of consciousness** | Interior monologue following thought's associative flow | Joyce (Molly Bloom), Woolf |
| **Unreliable narrator** | Narrator whose account contradicts evidence elsewhere in text | Nabokov (Humbert Humbert), Ishiguro |
| **Metafiction** | Fiction that comments on its own fictionality | Borges, Nabokov, Calvino |
| **Magical realism** | Realistic narration of extraordinary events treated as ordinary | Garcia Marquez |
| **Villanelle** | 19-line poem with 2 refrains and specific rhyme scheme | Dylan Thomas "Do Not Go Gentle" |
| **Petrarchan sonnet** | 14 lines: octave (8) + sestet (6); problem + resolution | Petrarch, Shakespeare |
| **In medias res** | Beginning a narrative in the middle of the action | Homer (Iliad), most epics |
| **Ekphrasis** | Verbal description of a visual artwork within a literary text | Homer (Shield of Achilles), Keats ("Ode on a Grecian Urn") |

## Common Confusion Points

**Literature is easier than other arts** — Literature requires less technical training to access than music or visual art, but its formal sophistication is equally deep. Ulysses is technically as complex as a Bach fugue; Beckett's plays are as formally constrained as serial music. Accessibility of medium doesn't mean simplicity of form.

**The novel began with Cervantes or Defoe** — The novel as we know it has multiple origin points. Murasaki Shikibu's The Tale of Genji (c. 1021, Japan) is arguably the first psychological novel. The ancient Greek novel (Longus, Achilles Tatius) predates Cervantes by 1500 years. What's true: the specific form of the realistic social novel has roots in 18th-century England and France.

**Literary criticism = explaining what a text "means"** — The formalist tradition (New Criticism, Structuralism) focuses on how texts work, not what they mean. What point of view is used? How is time structured? What does the syntax do? These questions are more answerable and more illuminating than "what is the theme?"

**Modern poetry abandoned form** — Free verse is not formless. It has rhythm (without meter), line breaks (which are formal choices), sonic effects (alliteration, assonance), and structural organization. What it abandoned is the constraint of a fixed metrical scheme. This is like asking why modern architecture abandoned pilasters — the answer is that structural function replaced ornamental convention.
