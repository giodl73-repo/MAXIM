# White-Collar Crime: Sutherland and Beyond

## The Big Picture

White-collar crime is crime committed by persons of respectability and high social status in the course of their occupations. Sutherland's 1939 presidential address to the American Sociological Association transformed criminology by showing that the focus on street crime was a systematic bias -- elite crime was larger in economic impact, under-prosecuted, and theoretically explained by the same mechanisms.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                WHITE-COLLAR CRIME LANDSCAPE                      |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  DEFINITIONAL AXIS:                                              |
|  Sutherland (offender): High-status person in occupation         |
|  Edelhertz (offense):   Non-violent, financially motivated       |
|                                                                  |
|  OCCUPATIONAL            CORPORATE               STATE           |
|  (individual benefit)    (organizational benefit) (official abuse)|
|  ==================      ==================      ===============  |
|  Embezzlement            Price-fixing            Bribery          |
|  Insider trading         Securities fraud        Corruption       |
|  Tax evasion             Environmental crime     Police brutality |
|  Professional fraud      Product safety fraud    War crimes       |
|  Medicare fraud          Bank fraud              Genocide         |
|                                                                  |
|  SCALE: Total annual cost of white-collar crime in US:           |
|         ~$300-600 billion/year (vs. ~$16B for all street crime)  |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Sutherland's Founding Argument (1939-1949)

### The Core Challenge

```
  TRADITIONAL CRIMINOLOGY                SUTHERLAND'S CHALLENGE
  =======================                ======================

  Focus: street crime                    Focus: occupational crime

  Explains crime by:                     Points out:
  - Poverty                              - Corporate executives commit
  - Broken homes                           crime constantly
  - Mental illness                       - They are not poor, broken-
  - Immigrant status                       home, or mentally ill
  - Urban disorganization                - Yet they steal billions

                                         CONCLUSION:
                                         Existing theory is wrong
                                         OR the theory applies only
                                         to lower-class crime
                                         (itself a class-biased choice)
```

### Why White-Collar Crime Is Undercounted

```
  DETECTION:
    Requires expertise to identify
    Victims often don't know they've been victimized
    Complex financial flows obscure the crime

  REPORTING:
    Victims are often corporations (don't want publicity)
    Or diffuse (each customer loses $10, no one sues)
    Regulatory agencies handle, not police

  PROSECUTION:
    Expensive, complex, resource-intensive
    Defendants have excellent lawyers
    Juries unfamiliar with financial instruments
    Prosecutorial incentives favor convictions over
    challenging cases

  SENTENCING:
    White-collar defendants historically receive lighter
    sentences (though federal sentencing guidelines
    have partially corrected this)
```

## Typology of White-Collar Offenses

### By Target

| Type | Example | Scale | Detection |
|------|---------|-------|-----------|
| **Securities fraud** | Insider trading, pump-and-dump | $100M-billions | SEC investigation |
| **Bank/financial fraud** | Mortgage fraud, accounting fraud | $1M-billions | Bank examiners, SEC |
| **Tax evasion** | Offshore accounts, understated income | Millions | IRS audits |
| **Health care fraud** | Medicare billing fraud, upcoding | $60-80B/year in US | CMS audits |
| **Corporate fraud** | Enron, WorldCom, accounting | Billions | SEC, DOJ |
| **Environmental crime** | Illegal dumping, permit violations | Diffuse harm | EPA enforcement |
| **Bribery/corruption** | Government contracting, licensing | Systematic | FBI, IG |
| **Consumer fraud** | False advertising, product defects | Diffuse | FTC, class action |

### Fraud Triangle (Cressey)

Cressey studied embezzlers and found three necessary conditions:

```
  +------------------+
  |  OPPORTUNITY     |
  |  (access to      |
  |  trusted position|
  |  + weak controls)|
  |       /\         |
  |      /  \        |
  |     /    \       |
  |    / FRAUD\      |
  |   /________\     |
  | PRESSURE         RATIONALIZATION
  | (financial       (neutralization:
  |  need, debt,     "I'll pay it back"
  |  lifestyle)      "They owe me"
  |                  "Everyone does it")
  +------------------+

  Remove any vertex --> fraud less likely
  Corporate controls target OPPORTUNITY
  Financial wellness programs target PRESSURE
  Ethics training targets RATIONALIZATION
```

## Corporate Crime

Corporate crime is crime committed by and for the benefit of the organization rather than an individual. The organizational dimension creates distinctive challenges.

### Organizational Dynamics

```
  WHY DO ORGANIZATIONS COMMIT CRIMES?
  ====================================

  STRUCTURAL FACTORS:
  - Performance pressure from shareholders/executives
  - Diffusion of responsibility (nobody "did it")
  - Normalization of deviance (small violations escalate)
  - Organizational culture that rationalizes harm

  PROCESS:
  Senior executives set financial targets
  +
  Mid-managers face unreachable targets
  +
  Compliance costs reduce margin
  +
  Ethical corners get cut "just this once"
  +
  Violations escalate as they are unreported
  +
  Eventually: fraud, safety violations, pollution

  EXAMPLES:
  - Enron: mark-to-market accounting fraud; pressure to
    meet analyst expectations
  - Ford Pinto: calculated that payout < fix cost
  - Tobacco companies: concealed cancer research
  - Volkswagen: programmed emissions defeat devices
  - Boeing 737 MAX: concealed MCAS software from FAA
```

### Edwin Sutherland's Corporate Studies

Sutherland himself studied 70 large US corporations and found that every single one had violated the law (antitrust, false advertising, unfair labor practices). Average: 14 violations per corporation. Yet almost none resulted in criminal prosecution -- most resolved through regulatory consent decrees.

### Corporate Criminal Liability

```
  INDIVIDUAL vs. CORPORATE PROSECUTION
  =====================================

  Respondeat Superior doctrine:
    Corporation is liable for acts of employees
    acting in scope of employment and partly to
    benefit the corporation.

  "Too Big to Jail" critique:
    DOJ concerns that prosecuting TBTF banks could
    destabilize the financial system.
    Resulted in Deferred Prosecution Agreements (DPAs):
      - Corporation admits facts
      - Pays fine
      - Implements compliance program
      - Prosecution deferred / dropped if clean for X years
      - NO individual criminal conviction

  DPA critics: Creates expected value of crime > expected
               cost of crime for corporations.
               "The cost of doing business."
```

## Major Theoretical Accounts

### Differential Association (Sutherland)

Sutherland applied his own differential association theory:

```
  Business culture transmits:
  - Definitions favorable to law violation
    ("Everyone cheats on taxes")
    ("Regulators don't understand our business")
    ("Antitrust law is outdated")

  - Techniques for violation
    (how to structure transactions to avoid detection)
    (how to use subsidiaries to obscure)

  Executives learn criminal techniques from:
    - Mentors within firms
    - Industry networks
    - Accounting/legal advisors who push limits
```

### Routine Activity / Opportunity

```
  CORPORATE OFFENDING = OPPORTUNITY x MOTIVATION x
                        ABSENT GUARDIANSHIP

  Opportunity: trusted access to assets, information,
               regulatory complexity as cover

  Motivation: performance pressure, bonus structures
              that reward short-term gain

  Absent guardianship: weak boards, captured regulators,
                       compliant auditors

  Enron example:
    Opportunity: CFO structured off-balance-sheet SPEs
    Motivation: stock price, executive bonuses
    Absent guardian: Arthur Andersen captured; board waived
                     conflict-of-interest rules
```

### Neutralization Theory (Sykes and Matza, 1957)

White-collar offenders are adept at rationalizing violations before and during the act:

```
  NEUTRALIZATION TECHNIQUES
  =========================

  1. DENIAL OF INJURY
     "No one was really harmed"
     "Customers could afford it"
     "Insurance covers it"

  2. DENIAL OF VICTIM
     "They were doing the same thing"
     "The regulator is the real criminal"

  3. CONDEMN THE CONDEMNERS
     "Prosecutors are politically motivated"
     "Government is overreaching"

  4. APPEAL TO HIGHER LOYALTIES
     "I did it for the company"
     "I did it for my family"
     "Employees would have lost jobs otherwise"

  5. DENIAL OF RESPONSIBILITY
     "I didn't know"
     "My lawyers signed off"
     "Everyone else in the industry did this"
```

## Common Confusion Points

**White-Collar Crime vs. Occupational Crime vs. Corporate Crime**
White-collar (Sutherland): high-status person using occupational role. Occupational crime: any crime in course of employment regardless of status (embezzling cashier). Corporate crime: the organization benefits, not just individuals. These categories overlap but are analytically distinct.

**Regulatory Violation vs. Crime**
Not all corporate wrongdoing is crime. EPA permit violations can be civil, administrative, or criminal depending on intent and severity. The line matters: criminal prosecution requires mens rea (intent); regulatory violation does not.

**DPAs Are Not Exoneration**
Deferred Prosecution Agreements are controversial precisely because they admit corporate wrongdoing without a criminal conviction. Critics argue they enable executives to avoid accountability. Defenders argue they achieve more compliance reform than adversarial prosecution.

**"No Victim" Rationalization**
Securities fraud, insider trading, and price-fixing have diffuse victims who are individually unaware they are harmed. This does not mean there are no victims -- investors who traded without insider information lost real money. Diffuse victimization is one reason white-collar crime is systematically under-reported and under-prosecuted.
