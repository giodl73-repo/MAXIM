# Social Control and Labeling Theory

## The Big Picture

Social control theory asks the opposite question from strain theory: not "why do people commit crime?" but "why do most people NOT commit crime?" The answer is social bonds. Labeling theory inverts the question further: how does the criminal justice system itself produce criminality through the act of labeling?

## Hirschi's Social Bond Theory (1969)

*Causes of Delinquency* is the foundational text. Hirschi surveyed 4,000 California teenagers and showed that delinquency was inversely related to bonds to conventional society.

### The Four Bonds

```
  SOCIAL BOND THEORY
  ==================

  We are all motivated to offend (motivation is given).
  Crime is prevented by bonds to conventional society.

  ATTACHMENT          COMMITMENT
  ----------          ----------
  Emotional ties to   Investment in
  parents, teachers,  conventional activities:
  peers               education, career,
                      reputation
  "Would my parents   "What would I lose
  be ashamed?"        if caught?"

  INVOLVEMENT         BELIEF
  -----------         ------
  Time spent in       Acceptance of
  conventional        conventional moral
  activities:         norms
  sports, work,
  homework            "Is this wrong?"
  (no idle time
  for crime)

  Strong bonds on ALL four --> low delinquency
  Weak bonds on ANY --> elevated risk
```

### Empirical Support

Hirschi's survey generated strong correlational support: attachment to parents and school are the strongest predictors of non-delinquency. The effects held controlling for race and class, which was theoretically significant -- social bonds matter across structural positions.

**Key finding**: Peer attachment requires a qualifier. Attachment to *conventional* peers reduces delinquency. Attachment to *delinquent* peers may increase it (Sutherland's differential association mechanism). Bond theory handles the former; it needs supplementation for the latter.

## Gottfredson and Hirschi: Self-Control Theory (1990)

*A General Theory of Crime* argued that a single trait -- low self-control -- explains crime across the life course.

### Self-Control: The Construct

```
  LOW SELF-CONTROL PROFILE
  ========================

  Characteristics:
  - Impulsive (live for the moment)
  - Insensitive to others' pain
  - Risk-seeking (thrill over calculation)
  - Short-sighted (immediate reward focus)
  - Non-verbal (prefer action to talk)
  - Low frustration tolerance

  Source: Inadequate child-rearing before age 8-10
  (stable after childhood)

  Predicts:
  - Criminal offending
  - BUT ALSO: smoking, accidents, divorce, job instability,
    DUI, late debt payments -- all share low self-control
    signature
```

### The "Versatility" Argument

```
  Traditional criminology: different crimes need different explanations

  Gottfredson & Hirschi: One trait explains most crime

  +-----------------------------+
  |  LOW SELF-CONTROL           |
  +-----------------------------+
       |      |      |     |
       v      v      v     v
   Theft  Assault  Fraud  Drug
                          use

  All share: immediate gratification, low planning,
             indifference to consequences

  "Specialization" in crime is largely an artifact
  of classification, not reality.
```

### Critique: Tautology Problem

The main critique is circular definition: low self-control is both the cause and often identified by its effects (crime and analogous behaviors). Measuring the construct independently of its predicted outcomes is empirically challenging. The theory has strong predictive power but weak causal identification.

## Sampson and Laub: Age-Graded Social Control (1993)

*Crime in the Making* followed Glueck and Glueck's 1940s delinquent sample into adulthood. The key finding: criminality is not fixed by childhood self-control. Adult social bonds create turning points.

### Life-Course Framework

```
  CHILDHOOD/ADOLESCENCE          ADULTHOOD
  =====================          =========

  Structural factors:            Turning points:
  - Low SES                      - Marriage (quality matters)
  - Family disruption            - Employment (stable, quality)
  - School failure               - Military service
           |                     - Religious involvement
           v
  Weak social bonds
           |
           v
  Delinquency/crime              Strong adult bonds
           |                     --> Desistance
           |                         even for early starters
           +-------------------->

  Key: Adult bonds can OVERRIDE childhood disadvantage.
       Life-course is not deterministic.
```

### Social Capital and Crime

```
  MARRIAGE EFFECT:
  Not just being married -- QUALITY of the relationship.
  Strong marital bond = strong social capital = reduced offending.

  EMPLOYMENT EFFECT:
  Not just having a job -- JOB STABILITY and employer attachment.

  Mechanism: Adult bonds build stakes in conformity:
    "I have too much to lose to risk arrest."
    Recapitulates Hirschi's commitment element
    but shows it can develop in adulthood.
```

## Labeling Theory

### Lemert: Primary and Secondary Deviance (1951)

```
  PRIMARY DEVIANCE               SECONDARY DEVIANCE
  ================               ==================

  Any initial rule violation.    Deviance that develops
  Causes are varied and          in response to social
  not particularly interesting   reaction to primary deviance.
  to labeling theorists.

  You shoplift.                  Caught --> labeled "thief"
  You smoke marijuana.           --> treated as deviant
  You fight at school.           --> excluded from conventional
                                     roles (school, work, friends)
                                 --> accept deviant identity
                                 --> associate with other deviants
                                 --> criminal career

  The REACTION, not the act, is the primary object of study.
```

### Howard Becker: Moral Entrepreneurs (1963)

*Outsiders* is the key text. Becker showed that deviance is not a quality of the act itself but of the label applied by those with power to define and enforce rules.

```
  MORAL ENTERPRISE
  ================

  RULE CREATORS            RULE ENFORCERS
  (moral entrepreneurs)    (police, courts)
  - Crusading reformers    - Selective enforcement
  - Religious groups       - Discretionary labeling
  - Media campaigns        - Organizational interests
         |                         |
         v                         v
         RULES                DEVIANT LABEL
         (criminalization)    (applied selectively)
              |
              v
         WHO GETS LABELED?
         Not just who commits acts,
         but: who is most visible?
              who lacks power to resist label?
              who fits stereotypes?
```

**Key example**: Becker's marijuana study showed that police, judges, and media functioned as moral entrepreneurs -- their campaigns to criminalize marijuana were about rule-making by those with power, not objective harmfulness.

### Goffman: Stigma (1963)

```
  STIGMA TYPES (Goffman)
  ======================

  Tribal stigma:     Race, religion, nationality
  Physical stigma:   Disability, visible difference
  Character stigma:  Criminal record, addiction, mental illness

  STIGMA MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES:

  Passing:           Concealing stigmatized attribute
                     (ex-convict hiding record)

  Covering:          Known but minimized
                     (wheelchair user making it unremarkable)

  Identity politics: Challenging the stigma itself
                     (gay rights movement transforming
                     homosexuality from stigma to identity)

  Criminal record creates "spoiled identity" --
  master status that overwhelms other attributes.
```

## Conflict Theory and Critical Criminology

### Quinney: Social Reality of Crime (1970)

```
  QUINNEY'S PROPOSITIONS
  ======================

  1. Crime is defined by authorized agents of the state.
  2. Criminal definitions describe behaviors that conflict
     with the interests of dominant classes.
  3. Criminal definitions are applied by dominant classes
     to behaviors of powerless classes.
  4. Behavior patterns are developed in relation to
     criminal definitions.
  5. Conceptions of crime are constructed and diffused
     by dominant classes through communications media.
  6. The social reality of crime = definitions + actions
     in response to definitions.

  Implication: What counts as crime is a political decision,
               not a natural category.
```

### William Chambliss: Saints and Roughnecks (1973)

Classic ethnographic study: two teenage gangs from the same community. "Saints" (middle-class) committed as much delinquency as "Roughnecks" (lower-class) but were never arrested. Differential police attention, school response, and community perception produced divergent life outcomes.

```
  SAINTS (middle-class boys)    ROUGHNECKS (lower-class boys)
  ========================      ============================

  Same delinquency rate         Same delinquency rate
  Police: "sowing wild oats"    Police: "real criminals"
  School: leaders               School: troublemakers
  Community: "good kids"        Community: "bad kids"

  Outcome:                      Outcome:
  College, careers              Jail, dead-end jobs

  Lesson: Labeling is class-stratified.
          Criminal careers are partly manufactured
          by differential institutional response.
```

## Differential Association Theory (Sutherland, 1947)

Included here because it bridges social learning and social control -- it's not strain, not labeling, but informs both.

```
  SUTHERLAND'S 9 PROPOSITIONS (condensed)
  ========================================

  1. Criminal behavior is LEARNED (not inherited or invented).

  2. Learned in interaction with others in intimate
     personal groups (not media, not books).

  3. Learning includes: techniques, motives, drives,
     rationalizations, ATTITUDES.

  4. Person becomes criminal when definitions favorable to
     code violation EXCEED definitions unfavorable to it.

  5. Differential associations vary by:
     - FREQUENCY   (how often contact occurs)
     - DURATION    (how long)
     - PRIORITY    (how early in life)
     - INTENSITY   (prestige/emotional importance of source)

  Analogy: Criminal behavior is learned like any other behavior.
  Criminal values + techniques are transmitted through
  the same social learning mechanisms as any skill.
```

**Bridge to modern theory**: Differential association is the theoretical core of Akers' Social Learning Theory, which operationalized it empirically and is among the most empirically supported theories in criminology.

## Common Confusion Points

**Social Control vs. Social Control Theory**
"Social control" broadly means society's mechanisms for ensuring conformity (police, courts, norms, socialization). "Social control theory" specifically is Hirschi's argument that *bonds* prevent crime. Don't conflate the general concept with the specific theory.

**Labeling Doesn't Say Labels Cause All Crime**
Labeling theory is about the *amplification* of deviance through societal reaction. It does not claim that people are innocent victims of labels who would have reformed without them. The claim is more modest: formal processing and labeling make criminal careers more likely to persist.

**Self-Control Theory Is Not Deterministic**
Gottfredson and Hirschi say self-control is stable after childhood -- not that it is perfectly fixed. High-self-control people can still offend under sufficient provocation; low-self-control people can still avoid crime with sufficient bonds. The trait is probabilistic, not deterministic.

**Differential Association Is Not "Bad Companions"**
The folk version is "you become like who you hang out with." Sutherland's version is more precise: the balance of *definitions* (attitudes toward violation) that are transmitted matters, not simply peer delinquency levels. A highly religious friend transmitting strong anti-crime definitions outweighs casual criminal association.
