# Strain Theory and Anomie

## The Big Picture

Strain theories locate crime's cause in social structure rather than individual rationality. The core claim: society sets common goals but blocks legitimate access for some groups. That blocked aspiration creates strain, which motivates crime as an alternative route. Anomie is the societal condition where normative regulation collapses.

## Emile Durkheim: Anomie

Durkheim's concept predates modern criminology. He introduced anomie in *The Division of Labor* (1893) and applied it to suicide in *Suicide* (1897).

### Anomie as Deregulation

```
  NORMAL STATE                    ANOMIC STATE
  ============                    ============

  Normative integration:          Normative collapse:
  Society provides shared         Industrial disruption, rapid
  goals AND regulates             change, or economic crisis
  aspirations to match            dissolves the regulatory
  realistic means                 framework

  People know their "place"       Aspirations unlimited;
  in the social order             no satisfaction possible;
  (in a functional, not           normative guidance absent
  nostalgic sense)

  Result: Social solidarity       Result: Rising suicide rates,
                                  deviance, social breakdown
```

**Key Durkheim insight**: Crime itself is *normal* and *functional*. Every society has crime; it defines the moral boundaries of the community. The problem is excess -- anomic conditions produce too much deviance. The goal is not zero crime but optimal regulation.

### Mechanical vs. Organic Solidarity

```
  MECHANICAL SOLIDARITY        ORGANIC SOLIDARITY
  (pre-industrial)             (industrial/modern)
  ====================         ==================
  Shared values, religion,     Interdependence through
  beliefs                      division of labor
  Repressive law               Restitutive law
  Criminal law enforces        Civil/administrative law
  collective conscience        repairs relations
  Low anomie potential         HIGH anomie potential --
                               rapid change disrupts norms
```

## Robert Merton: Strain Theory (1938)

"Social Structure and Anomie" is probably the most cited article in criminology. Merton redeployed Durkheim's anomie concept to explain American crime patterns.

### The Core Model

```
  AMERICAN SOCIAL STRUCTURE
  ==========================

  CULTURAL GOALS                  INSTITUTIONALIZED MEANS
  (what everyone wants)           (legitimate paths to goals)
  +-----------------+             +--------------------+
  | Material success|             | Education          |
  | American Dream  |             | Hard work          |
  | Status/wealth   |             | Deferred gratif.   |
  +-----------------+             | Social connections |
           |                      +--------------------+
           |                               |
           +---------------+---------------+
                           |
                    SOCIAL CLASSES
                    =============
  Upper/Middle:    Goals reachable through legitimate means
  Lower/Working:   Goals promoted universally BUT
                   legitimate means are BLOCKED
                           |
                           v
                        STRAIN
                    (disjunction)
                           |
                           v
                  5 ADAPTATION MODES
```

### The Five Adaptations

```
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
| MODE        | GOALS | MEANS    | EXAMPLE                      |
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
| Conformity  |  +    |    +     | Working class person         |
|             |accepts|  accepts | pursuing education           |
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
| Innovation  |  +    |    -     | Drug dealer pursuing         |
| (crime)     |accepts| rejects  | money through crime          |
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
| Ritualism   |  -    |    +     | Bureaucrat going through     |
|             |rejects|  accepts | motions without ambition     |
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
| Retreatism  |  -    |    -     | Chronic alcoholic,           |
|             |rejects| rejects  | homeless person              |
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
| Rebellion   |  +-   |   +-     | Revolutionary; rejects both  |
|             |replace| replace  | and substitutes alternatives |
+-------------+-------+----------+------------------------------+
```

**Critical point**: *Innovation* is the mode Merton uses to explain crime. When legitimate means are blocked, people find illegitimate alternatives to reach culturally endorsed goals. The problem is not deviant values -- it is deviant *means* in service of *normal* goals.

### Why This Explains American Crime

```
  AMERICAN EXCEPTIONALISM (Merton's argument)
  ==========================================

  Most societies:
    Regulate aspirations to fit actual possibilities
    Teach people to want what they can reasonably get

  American Dream ideology:
    Insists ANYONE can succeed
    Success is ONLY measured by material achievement
    Failure = personal failure, not structural blockage
    No legitimate regulation of aspirations

  Result:
    Lower-class people absorb the success goal fully
    Blocked from legitimate means
    High innovation (crime) rates -- especially property crime
```

## Albert Cohen: Delinquent Subcultures (1955)

Cohen asked: why is lower-class juvenile delinquency *expressive* (vandalism, fights, status-seeking) rather than instrumental (theft for gain)? Merton's model predicts instrumental innovation -- Cohen explains non-utilitarian gang behavior.

### Status Frustration Model

```
  SCHOOL SOCIALIZATION
  ====================

  Middle-class measuring rod:
    Academic achievement
    Deferred gratification
    Verbal skill
    Respect for property
    Long-term planning

  Lower-class boys enter school lacking
  middle-class cultural capital
           |
           v
  STATUS FAILURE in school system
           |
           v
  STATUS FRUSTRATION (humiliation, rejection)
           |
           v
  REACTION FORMATION: Invert middle-class values
    - Non-utilitarian (vandalism for its own sake)
    - Malicious (hurting people for status)
    - Negativistic (opposite of mainstream)
    - Short-run hedonism
    - Group autonomy
           |
           v
  DELINQUENT SUBCULTURE
  (provides alternative status system)
```

**Cohen's contribution**: Crime can be expressive, not just instrumental. Subcultures create their own status systems when legitimate ones exclude.

## Cloward and Ohlin: Differential Opportunity (1960)

*Delinquency and Opportunity* extended Merton by arguing that illegitimate means are also differentially distributed. Not everyone who is blocked from legitimate success can access organized crime or lucrative illegal markets.

### Three Types of Delinquent Subcultures

```
  NEIGHBORHOOD STRUCTURE determines subculture type:

  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | CRIMINAL         | Stable, organized adult crime present    |
  | SUBCULTURE       | Youth can learn and enter criminal       |
  |                  | career ladder                            |
  |                  | = Income-generating crime, property      |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | CONFLICT         | Disorganized neighborhood                |
  | SUBCULTURE       | No organized crime to apprentice into    |
  |                  | Status via violence and fighting         |
  |                  | = Gang violence, turf wars               |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
  | RETREATIST       | Failed in both legitimate AND            |
  | SUBCULTURE       | illegitimate systems                     |
  |                  | Double failure                           |
  |                  | = Drug use, withdrawal                   |
  +------------------+------------------------------------------+
```

**Policy implication** (and direct influence): Cloward and Ohlin's framework directly influenced Lyndon Johnson's War on Poverty, particularly the "opportunity expansion" programs. Provide legitimate opportunity, reduce strain.

## Robert Agnew: General Strain Theory (1992)

Agnew expanded strain beyond Merton's goal-blockage model to cover all sources of negative affect, making the theory testable at the individual level.

### Three Sources of Strain

```
  MERTON'S STRAIN (macro):           AGNEW'S GST (micro):
  =======================            ==================

  Goal-blockage only                 THREE SOURCES:

                                     1. GOAL BLOCKAGE
                                        Failure to achieve
                                        positively valued
                                        goals (money, status,
                                        respect, autonomy)

                                     2. REMOVAL OF POSITIVE
                                        STIMULI
                                        Loss of valued things:
                                        job, relationship,
                                        home, family member

                                     3. PRESENTATION OF
                                        NEGATIVE STIMULI
                                        Exposure to aversive
                                        experiences: abuse,
                                        bullying, discrimination,
                                        victimization
```

### The Negative Affect Mechanism

```
  STRAIN (any source)
         |
         v
  NEGATIVE AFFECT: anger, frustration, depression, anxiety
         |
         v
  Pressure to take corrective action
         |
    +----+----+
    |         |
    v         v
LEGITIMATE  CRIME
COPING      (especially anger -> violence;
(therapy,    depression -> drug use;
 exercise)   desperation -> property crime)
```

### Conditioning Factors

Not all strain leads to crime. Factors that condition the strain-crime link:

```
  STRAIN MORE LIKELY TO PRODUCE CRIME WHEN:
  ==========================================
  - Strain is seen as unjust (not just unlucky)
  - Strain is high in magnitude
  - Coping resources are low (low self-control, poor social support)
  - Criminal alternatives are available and salient
  - Association with criminal peers (who model criminal coping)
```

## Messner and Rosenfeld: Institutional Anomie Theory (1994)

*Crime and the American Dream* argues that the US doesn't just create strain -- it has institutionalized anomie as a structural feature.

### The Core Argument

```
  AMERICAN INSTITUTIONAL STRUCTURE
  =================================

  ECONOMY                    dominates      ALL OTHER
  (market logic)             =========>     INSTITUTIONS
                                            Family
                                            School
                                            Polity

  Market values permeate everything:
    Family: time is money, work over family
    School: education as credential for market
    Polity: government serves economic growth

  Result:
    Non-economic institutions (family, school, civic org)
    DEVALUED and weakened as moral regulators
            |
            v
    Anomic condition:
    Success ethic dominates WITHOUT normative constraint
            |
            v
    High rates of serious crime
    (especially in comparison to comparable nations)
```

### International Comparative Evidence

IAT predicts nations with strong non-economic institutions (generous welfare states, civic culture, strong family policy) will have lower crime despite similar economic inequality. Scandinavian data supports this. The US is an outlier in crime rates among developed nations precisely because of institutional imbalance.

## Common Confusion Points

**Anomie vs. Strain**
Anomie (Durkheim) is a societal condition -- the breakdown of normative regulation. Strain (Merton) is an individual experience -- the disjunction between goals and means. Merton used anomie as the macro condition that produces individual strain, but the two concepts operate at different levels.

**Merton's Conformists Are Not Criminals**
The most common adaptation is conformity -- accepting both goals and legitimate means. Strain theory does not say everyone in the lower class is criminal. It says blocked opportunity increases the probability of innovative adaptation among those for whom it is most severe.

**Innovation Is Not Just Property Crime**
Merton's "innovation" covers any illegitimate means toward legitimate goals: drug dealing, fraud, corruption, political graft. White-collar crime fits the model -- executives using fraud to reach financial goals they could not reach legitimately. The model is not class-specific despite often being taught that way.

**GST and Domestic Violence**
Agnew's presentation-of-negative-stimuli source of strain directly explains why victimization predicts offending. Abused youth who are exposed to negative stimuli at home generate strain that -- absent adequate coping resources -- can produce delinquency. This is not excuse-making; it is mechanism identification.
