# Mass Incarceration as Policy Choice

## The Big Picture

The United States incarcerates at a rate 5-10x higher than comparable democratic nations. This is not a natural response to crime -- it is the result of specific policy decisions made from the 1970s onward. Understanding mass incarceration requires separating the causes (policy choices), the mechanisms (drug war + mandatory minimums + sentencing guidelines), and the consequences (individual, family, community, fiscal).

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|               US INCARCERATION LANDSCAPE                         |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  SCALE (2023 approx):                                            |
|  ~2 million incarcerated (jail + prison)                         |
|  ~1 in 100 adults                                                |
|  Rate: ~600 per 100,000 (Japan: 40; Germany: 70; UK: 130)        |
|                                                                  |
|  SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE:                                            |
|  Federal prisons:     BOP, 10% of population                     |
|  State prisons:       90% of population                          |
|  Local jails:         pretrial detention + short sentences        |
|  Immigration det.:    ICE detention (separate system)            |
|                                                                  |

|  Black Americans: 13% of US population, ~38% of prison pop.      |
|  Black men: 1 in 3 will be incarcerated in their lifetime        |
|  (vs. 1 in 17 for white men)                                     |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## The Historical Arc

```
  US INCARCERATION RATE (per 100,000)
  ====================================

  1925-1970:   ~100-110/100,000 -- stable for 50 years

  1970:        ~96/100,000
  1980:        ~139/100,000   <-- drug war begins
  1990:        ~297/100,000
  2000:        ~478/100,000
  2008:        ~504/100,000   <-- PEAK
  2020:        ~358/100,000   (COVID release + reform)
  2023:        ~380/100,000   (partial rebound)

  Why was it stable for 50 years?
  Bruce Western: When courts raised sentences, parole
  offset the increase; system was self-regulating.

  What changed in 1970s?
  Policy change, not crime change.
  (Crime rose in 1960s-1980s, but similar rises in
  other nations did not produce mass incarceration)
```

## Policy Drivers

### The War on Drugs

```
  DRUG WAR POLICY ESCALATION
  ===========================

  Nixon (1971): "War on Drugs" declared
    - Mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses
    - DEA created (1973)
    - Nixon aide John Ehrlichman later admitted:
      "We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be
      against the Vietnam War or Black, but by getting
      the public to associate the hippies with marijuana
      and Blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both
      heavily, we could disrupt those communities."
      (Atlantic, 2016)

  Rockefeller Drug Laws (1973, NY):
    15 years to life for possession of 4 oz. heroin/cocaine
    Inspired national wave of mandatory minimums

  Reagan/Bush escalation (1980s-1990s):
    Crack vs. powder cocaine 100:1 disparity (1986)
    Crack = 5g triggers 5-year mandatory minimum
    Powder = 500g triggers same sentence
    Crack markets predominantly Black; powder predominantly white
    (100:1 disparity reduced to 18:1 by Fair Sentencing Act, 2010)

  Three Strikes laws (California 1994 + others):
    Third felony conviction = 25 to life mandatory
    Felonies included non-violent offenses
    Some "strikes": stealing a slice of pizza (Leandro Andrade case)

  Federal Sentencing Guidelines (1987):
    Mandatory guidelines replacing judicial discretion
    Judges required to sentence within narrow ranges
    Enhanced sentences for drug quantity, weapon, role
```

### Truth in Sentencing

```
  TRUTH IN SENTENCING LAWS
  =========================

  Pre-1984: Parole boards could release inmates before
             full sentence served.
             Average served: ~50% of sentence.

  1994 Crime Bill: Federal incentive grants to states
             that require 85% of sentence served.
             Eliminated parole eligibility for many offenses.

  Effect: Even if sentencing didn't change, people stayed
          longer. Doubled the actual time served.

  Rational basis: "Truth" for victims about when release.
  Critique: Removes corrections system's ability to
            respond to rehabilitation, good behavior,
            prison overcrowding.
```

  =====================================

  Federal prison = mostly drug/guns/immigration

## Racial Disparities: Mechanisms

```
  RACIAL DISPARITY PRODUCTION SYSTEM
  =====================================

  ARREST DISPARITIES:
    Drug use is roughly equal across races.
    Drug arrests are racially concentrated (3-4x for Black).
    Why: Police concentrated in low-income Black neighborhoods
         for decades (historical investment in patrol there).
         Street drug markets more visible than indoor markets.
         Indoor (whiter) markets = lower arrest risk.

  PROSECUTION DISPARITIES:
    Prosecutors exercise wide discretion on charging.
    Studies: Black defendants charged with more serious
             offenses controlling for facts of case.
    Plea bargaining: Black defendants offered less favorable
                     plea deals in some jurisdictions.

  SENTENCING DISPARITIES:
    Federal: Crack/powder disparity (historical).
    Studies: Black men receive sentences 19.1% longer
             than white men for similar crimes (USSC 2017).
    Mechanisms: Criminal history scores (prior arrests
                accumulate more in over-policed communities),
                mandatory minimums (limit judicial discretion),
                pretrial detention (linked to worse outcomes).

  BAIL/PRETRIAL:
    Money bail system disadvantages poor.
    Poor disproportionately Black/Latino.
    Pretrial detention --> worse case outcomes.
```

## Consequences of Mass Incarceration

### Individual

```
  COLLATERAL CONSEQUENCES OF CONVICTION
  =======================================

  Legal bars/restrictions:
  - Voting (14 states: permanent disenfranchisement)
  - Public housing eligibility
  - Federal student loans (drug convictions)
  - Food stamps/SNAP eligibility
  - Public employment (civil service bars)
  - Professional licenses (nursing, teaching, law)
  - Gun ownership (federal bar)
  - Immigration status (deportation for LPRs)

  Employment:
  - "Ban the box" movement: delay criminal history Q
  - Audit studies: callback rate drop of 50% for
    felony conviction; Black applicants penalized even
    without record (adding race X record = severe)

  The "mark" of a criminal record (Devah Pager research):
    White with record = Black without record in callbacks.
```

### Family

```
  FAMILY EFFECTS OF INCARCERATION
  =================================

  Children with incarcerated parents:
  - More likely to develop behavioral problems
  - More likely to drop out of school
  - More likely to be incarcerated themselves
  (intergenerational transmission mechanism)

  "Concentrated disadvantage":
    Murray (1995): incarceration removes men from communities.
    Removes fathers, potential partners, workers.
    Changes sex ratio (fewer available men for marriage).
    Effects compound in high-incarceration neighborhoods.

  Harms are concentrated:
    Not dispersed equally.
    Some Black urban neighborhoods: 1 in 5 adult men
    incarcerated in any given year.
```

### Community

```
  COMMUNITY-LEVEL EFFECTS
  ========================

  SOCIAL CAPITAL:
    High incarceration disrupts community networks.
    Destroys employment connections, family structures.
    Reduces civic participation.

  CRIME PARADOX:
    Incarceration intended to reduce crime.
    But: concentrated incarceration can INCREASE crime.
    Returns from prison with criminal networks,
    no employment, and housing instability.
    Todd Clear: "coercive mobility" destabilizes neighborhoods.

  POLITICAL EFFECTS:
    Felon disenfranchisement removes voters from communities.
    Prison is counted in the district it's located
    (urban to rural population transfer effects).
    Democratic representation shifted.
```

## Fiscal Cost

```
  COST OF INCARCERATION (approximate US 2023):
  =============================================

  Annual cost per prisoner:
    Federal:  ~$42,000/year
    State:    ~$35,000/year (varies widely; CA > $100k)
    Jail:     ~$34,000/year

  Total annual correctional spending: ~$80-100 billion/year

  For comparison:
    Public K-12 per pupil spending: ~$13,000/year
    Public college tuition: ~$10,000/year

  "Prisonfare vs. Welfare":
    Wacquant: US has built prisonfare (mass incarceration
    as social policy for the poor) instead of welfare state.
    Mass incarceration is the successor to welfare reform
    as a means of managing poor urban populations.
```

## Alternatives and Reform

```
  REFORM POLICY OPTIONS
  ======================

  SENTENCING:
    - Repeal mandatory minimums
    - Restore judicial discretion
    - "Safety valve" provisions (allow departure for
      extraordinary circumstances)
    - Retroactivity for Fair Sentencing Act (Crack reduction)

  PRETRIAL:
    - Bail reform / risk-based assessment (replace money bail)
    - Expand pretrial services (supervision instead of detention)
    - New Jersey model: near-elimination of money bail (2017)
      Result: pretrial population fell; no safety increase

  DRUG POLICY:
    - Decriminalization (Portugal model: treat as health issue)
    - Drug courts (divert to treatment)
    - Marijuana legalization (reduces arrest volume)

  REENTRY:
    - "Ban the Box" in employment applications
    - Automatic record expungement after time
    - Housing first for people leaving prison
    - Education in prison (restored Pell grants 2023)

  PAROLE REFORM:
    - Reduce "technical violation" reincarceration
      (missing appointment, failed drug test)
    - ~25% of prison admissions are technical violations
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Violent Crime Did Not Drive Mass Incarceration**
Public perception is that mass incarceration is a response to violent crime. The data show: violent crime peaked in the late 1980s/early 1990s and has fallen since. Incarceration kept rising through the 2000s. Drug offenses drove the federal system; drug + mandatory minimums drove state systems.

**Incarceration vs. Crime Relationship Is Non-Linear**
At low levels of incarceration, incapacitating dangerous offenders reduces crime (incapacitation effect). At high levels, the marginal offender incapacitated is relatively low-risk (diminishing returns), while the community destabilization effects become significant. The US is clearly past the point of optimal marginal deterrence/incapacitation.

**Germany/Scandinavia Have Crime Too**
Lower incarceration rates do not mean utopian crime-free societies. Germany has serious organized crime, violent crime, and drug markets. The relevant comparison is: similar crime inputs produce vastly different incarceration outputs. This is not a crime difference -- it is a policy choice difference.

**Recidivism Is Not Fixed**
Common claim: recidivism is ~66% within 3 years. This measures *rearrest*, not reconviction or reincarceration. Many rearrests do not result in conviction. More importantly, recidivism varies enormously by offense type, age, program participation, housing stability, employment, and support services. It is not a fixed property of "criminals."
