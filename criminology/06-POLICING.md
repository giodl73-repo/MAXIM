# Policing: Strategies, Bias, and Reform

## The Big Picture

Policing is the front end of the criminal justice system -- the primary point of contact between state coercive power and the public. Policing strategy determines who gets arrested, for what, and where. Those decisions aggregate into the shape of the entire criminal justice system downstream.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                     POLICING LANDSCAPE                           |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  STRATEGIES                    ACCOUNTABILITY MECHANISMS         |
|  ----------                    -------------------------         |
|  Traditional patrol            Internal affairs                  |
|  Community policing            Civilian review boards            |
|  Problem-oriented              Civil liability (Section 1983)    |
|  Hot spots policing            Federal consent decrees           |
|  Intelligence-led              Body-worn cameras                 |
|  Predictive policing           Pattern-or-practice investigations|
|                                                                  |
|  RESOURCE ALLOCATION           REFORM DEBATES                    |
|  ===================           ==============                    |
|  Patrol (random)               Defund vs. reform                 |
|  Directed patrol               Police abolition                  |
|  Proactive policing            Co-responder models               |
|  Investigative units           Mental health diversion           |
|  Specialized units (drugs,     Camden NJ model                   |
|  homicide, gang)               Procedural justice                |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Historical Development

```
  ERA 1: POLITICAL (1840s-1920s)
  ==============================
  Police forces serve local political machines.
  Officers hired through patronage.
  Corruption endemic.
  Brutality tolerated toward immigrants, minorities.

  ERA 2: REFORM (1920s-1970s)
  ============================
  August Vollmer, O.W. Wilson modernize policing.
  Professional model: military hierarchy, centralization,
  technology (patrol cars, two-way radios), 911.
  Police = neutral law enforcement professionals.
  Separated police from political machines.
  BUT: distanced police from community.

  ERA 3: COMMUNITY (1980s-1990s)
  ===============================
  Response to rising crime + police-community tensions.
  Foot patrols, neighborhood officers.
  Problem-solving rather than just arrest.
  Requires community partnership.

  ERA 4: EVIDENCE-BASED (1990s-present)
  =======================================
  CompStat (NYPD 1993): data-driven accountability.
  Hot spots research (Sherman 1989): crime concentrates.
  Randomized experiments: what actually reduces crime?
  Tension: evidence-based can conflict with community trust.
```

## Policing Strategies

### Traditional Patrol (Random)

```
  RANDOM PREVENTIVE PATROL
  ========================

  Model: Officers patrol assigned beats.
  Theory: Visible police presence deters crime.

  Kansas City Preventive Patrol Experiment (1972-1973):
    Kelling, Pate et al.
    Three conditions:
    - Reactive (no patrol, only respond to calls)
    - Proactive (double/triple normal patrol)
    - Control (normal patrol)

    Result: No significant difference in crime, fear,
            or satisfaction across conditions.

  Implication: Random patrol is largely ineffective.
               Resources should be directed differently.
```

### Hot Spots Policing

```
  HOT SPOTS RESEARCH
  ==================

  Sherman & Weisburd (1989): 50% of crime in Minneapolis
  occurred in 3% of addresses ("hot spots").

  Crime concentration: Pareto principle applies.
  A few places generate most crime events.

  HOT SPOTS POLICING:
  Focus patrol/investigation on high-crime micro-places
  (addresses, blocks, intersections -- NOT neighborhoods)

  Evidence: Strong. Multiple randomized trials.
  Meta-analysis: 15-20% reduction in target area crime.
  Displacement: Partial but diffusion of benefits > displacement.

  DOSE-RESPONSE: How much patrol at hot spots?
  Optimal: 15 minutes patrol + absence + return
  (Koper Curve)
  Saturation: diminishing returns past ~15 min/2 hr intervals
```

### Problem-Oriented Policing (Goldstein, 1979)

```
  PROBLEM-ORIENTED POLICING (POP)
  ================================

  Traditional:                   Problem-Oriented:
  Respond to incidents           Identify underlying problems
  Repeat calls = repeat          producing repeat calls
  dispatches                     Tailor response to problem

  SARA MODEL:
  +----------+
  | SCAN     | Identify recurring problems
  +----------+
       |
       v
  +----------+
  | ANALYZE  | Understand causes and contexts
  +----------+
       |
       v
  +----------+
  | RESPOND  | Design targeted intervention
  +----------+
       |
       v
  +----------+
  | ASSESS   | Evaluate effectiveness
  +----------+

  Example: Bar closing fights
    Problem: Assaults at 2am near bar district
    Analysis: Same 3 bars, parking lot design, poor lighting
    Response: Last-call staggering, lighting, taxi service
    Not: more arrests of drunks
```

### Intelligence-Led Policing

```
  INTELLIGENCE-LED POLICING (ILP)
  ================================

  Core idea: Police decision-making driven by analysis
             of criminal intelligence, not just patrol
             tradition.

  CompStat (Bratton, NYPD, 1993):
    Computer statistics on crime
    Weekly accountability meetings
    Precinct commanders held accountable for crime trends
    Rapid deployment to emerging patterns

  CompStat's record:
    NYC crime fell dramatically in 1990s (but:
    national trend; many competing explanations)
    Stop-and-frisk soared under CompStat pressure
    (quantitative metrics create perverse incentives)
```

### Predictive Policing

```
  PREDICTIVE POLICING
  ===================

  PredPol (now Geolitica), ShotSpotter, COMPAS-style:
  Use historical crime data + algorithms to predict
  where crime will occur or who will re-offend.

  TECHNICAL CRITIQUE:
    Training data = historical arrest patterns
    Historical patterns = racially biased enforcement
    Output = directs police to already-policed communities
    Result: feedback loop amplifying existing bias

  VALIDITY CRITIQUE:
    Few rigorous evaluations
    RAND, academics find limited predictive validity
    beyond experienced officer judgment

  PROPRIETARY PROBLEM:
    Vendors refuse to share algorithms (trade secret)
    Defense attorneys cannot challenge basis for
    arrest/bail decisions
    Violates due process norms

  Chicago "Strategic Subject List" (heat list):
    Pre-crime identification of likely offenders
    ACLU: people on list received unwanted police contact
    without committing crimes
```

## Racial Bias in Policing

### Empirical Evidence

```
  DOCUMENTED DISPARITIES:
  ========================

  Stop-and-frisk (NYC pre-2013):
    88% of those stopped were Black or Latino
    88% of those stopped were innocent
    Despite: NYC ~44% Black/Latino

  Traffic stops:
    Multiple studies: Black motorists stopped at
    higher rates than white motorists
    Veil of darkness method: disparities larger in
    daylight (when race visible) than darkness

  Use of force:
    Incident-level studies: Black suspects more likely
    to experience force controlling for circumstances
    OR: no racial bias in lethal force after controlling
    for encounter rate (Fryer, 2016, contested)

  KEY METHODOLOGICAL DEBATE:
    Benchmark problem: What is the right denominator?
    - % of population? (ignores differential exposure)
    - % of all police contacts? (ignores why contacted)
    - % of armed suspects? (data quality issues)
    No benchmark is perfect; debate is genuine.
```

### Explanatory Frameworks

```
  INDIVIDUAL BIAS:
    Explicit racism by individual officers
    Implicit bias (IAT evidence)
    Training can address

  INSTITUTIONAL BIAS:
    Policies that disparately impact (stop-and-frisk)
    Geographic concentration of policing
    (Crime is in poor/minority areas because of
    structural inequality; policing concentrates there;
    arrests concentrate there)
    Drug war enforcement priorities

  STRUCTURAL BIAS:
    Poverty and race are correlated (historical causes)
    Crime rates higher in high-poverty areas
    Police concentrated in high-crime areas
    --> Arrest rates are racially disparate even without
        individual officer bias

    None of these frames exonerates the others.
    All three operate simultaneously.
```

## Use of Force

```
  FORCE CONTINUUM
  ===============

  Officer presence
       |
       v
  Verbal commands
       |
       v
  Soft empty-hand control (escort, grab)
       |
       v
  Hard empty-hand control (strikes)
       |
       v
  Non-lethal weapons (taser, OC spray, baton)
       |
       v
  Lethal force (firearm)

  Constitutional standard: "Objectively reasonable"
  Graham v. Connor (1989): Reasonableness from perspective
  of a reasonable officer on the scene, not 20/20 hindsight.

  Critics: Standard gives wide deference to officers;
  creates weak accountability for unjustified force.
```

## Accountability Mechanisms

```
  ACCOUNTABILITY ARCHITECTURE
  ============================

  +--------------------+      +------------------------+
  | INTERNAL AFFAIRS   |      | CIVILIAN REVIEW BOARD  |
  | (self-policing)    |      | (external oversight)   |
  +--------------------+      +------------------------+
  | Investigates       |      | Investigates            |
  | officer misconduct |      | complainants            |
  | historically weak  |      | usually advisory, not  |
  | (blue wall silence)|      | disciplinary           |
  +--------------------+      +------------------------+

  FEDERAL:
  Sec. 1983 Civil Rights Act claims: sue officer directly
  DOJ Pattern-or-Practice: AG can investigate agencies
    and impose consent decrees with judicial oversight
  Examples: LAPD (2000), Newark PD (2014),
            Baltimore PD (2017), Minneapolis (2023)

  BODY CAMERAS:
    Evidence: Mixed. Some studies show reduction in force
    and complaints. Others show minimal effect.
    Key issues: officer discretion on activation;
    footage access for public vs. officer review;
    metadata and facial recognition uses
```

## Reform Models

### Camden NJ Model (2013)

```
  CAMDEN COUNTY POLICE REFORM
  ============================

  Problem: Camden NJ -- one of most violent US cities.
           Police union contracts blocked accountability.
           Police did not live in community; poor relations.

  Solution: Disbanded city police department entirely.
            Created new county force from scratch.
            - Officers reapplied for jobs (without tenure)
            - New contract without problematic provisions
            - Community policing emphasis
            - Use-of-force policy revision
            - De-escalation training

  Result (contested):
    Crime did fall significantly
    Complaints against officers fell
    But: economic investment also increased
    Causation vs. correlation debate
```

### Procedural Justice (Tyler)

```
  PROCEDURAL JUSTICE THEORY
  ==========================

  Tom Tyler research: People comply with law not primarily
  because of deterrence but because they see law as LEGITIMATE.

  Legitimacy = procedurally fair treatment:
    - Voice (can I speak and be heard?)
    - Neutrality (are decisions made on facts, not bias?)
    - Respect (am I treated with dignity?)
    - Trust (are motives benevolent?)

  Implication: Police reduce crime more by being
  procedurally fair than by being aggressive.
  Fair treatment builds legitimacy; legitimacy drives
  voluntary compliance; compliance reduces crime.

  Evidence: Reasonably strong correlational;
  some randomized studies (BWC, body language scripts)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Strategy | Evidence Base | Best Use Case | Limitations |
|----------|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| Random patrol | Weak (KCPPE) | Visible reassurance | Inefficient |
| Hot spots | Strong | High-crime micro-places | Requires accurate data |
| Problem-oriented | Moderate-strong | Repeat call locations | Requires analysis capacity |
| Community policing | Moderate | Trust building, legitimacy | Hard to measure crime impact |
| Predictive policing | Weak | None endorsed without scrutiny | Feedback loop, bias risk |
| CompStat | Mixed | Accountability | Perverse incentives |
| Procedural justice | Moderate | Training, encounter style | Long-term, hard to measure |

## Common Confusion Points

**Defund vs. Abolition vs. Reform**
"Defund" means reallocate police budgets to other services (mental health, housing, social work) -- not eliminate police. "Abolition" is the more radical position that policing as currently constituted cannot be reformed. Reform advocates want internal improvements, civilian oversight, and training changes within the existing institution. These are empirically different positions.

**CompStat Success Is Contested**
The NYPD's 1990s crime decline is often attributed to CompStat and aggressive policing (Bratton/Giuliani). Crime fell comparably in cities that did not adopt CompStat. The national decline had multiple causes: end of crack epidemic, lead paint removal, demographic shifts, incarceration, and possibly abortion legalization (Levitt/Donahue). CompStat's independent contribution is unclear.

**Stop-and-Frisk Legal Standard**
Terry v. Ohio (1968): police can stop and frisk based on "reasonable suspicion" (lower than probable cause for arrest). NYC's aggressive use of this tool was found unconstitutional in Floyd v. City of New York (2013) not because stops are unconstitutional but because the program was applied with racial targeting.

**Use of Force "Objective Reasonableness" Standard**
Graham v. Connor's "objective reasonableness" is more permissive than many people assume. It does not require that officers make the best possible decision -- only a reasonable one by an officer in the situation. This standard creates wide deference and is a major point of contention in reform debates.
