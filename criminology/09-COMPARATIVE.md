# Comparative Criminology and International Systems

## The Big Picture

Comparative criminology examines crime and justice across nations to separate universal patterns from policy-contingent ones. The central lesson: incarceration rates, crime responses, and criminal justice practices are political choices, not natural outcomes of crime rates. Similar crime inputs produce radically different outputs depending on institutions, culture, and politics.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|           COMPARATIVE CRIMINOLOGY LANDSCAPE                      |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  WHAT VARIES ACROSS NATIONS:                                     |
|  - Incarceration rates (40-600+ per 100,000)                     |
|  - Crime definitions (marijuana, sex work, defamation)           |
|  - Justice system models (adversarial vs. inquisitorial)         |
|  - Sentencing philosophy (rehabilitation vs. punishment)         |
|  - Victim rights                                                 |
|  - Police organization (centralized vs. local)                   |
|                                                                  |
|  WHAT IS RELATIVELY UNIVERSAL:                                   |
|  - Age-crime curve                                               |
|  - Male overrepresentation                                       |
|  - Property crime > violent crime in volume                      |
|  - Urban concentration                                           |
|  - Correlation between inequality and violent crime              |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Justice System Models

### Adversarial vs. Inquisitorial

```
  ADVERSARIAL SYSTEM              INQUISITORIAL SYSTEM
  (US, UK, Canada, Australia)     (France, Germany, Italy, most
                                   of civil law world)
  =======================         ====================

  STRUCTURE:
  Judge = neutral referee         Judge = active investigator
  Prosecution vs. defense         Judge can call witnesses,
  Contest determines truth        order evidence
  Cross-examination central       Extensive pre-trial
                                  investigation

  PRE-TRIAL:
  Adversarial discovery:          Examining magistrate (juge
  Exchange documents,             d'instruction) leads
  depositions                     investigation
  Plea bargaining: >95%           Plea bargaining: limited
  of US cases resolve by plea     or absent in many systems

  TRIAL:
  Jury (US: 12, unanimous)        Judge-led panel
  Battle of competing             Inquisitorial examination
  narratives                      of facts

  PROSECUTORS:
  District Attorneys:             Career civil servants
  elected in most US states       politically insulated
  High political incentives       Lower political incentives
  for conviction/tough posture    for punitiveness
```

### Why Elected Prosecutors Matter

```
  US EXCEPTIONALISM: ELECTED PROSECUTORS
  =======================================

  Most of world: Prosecutors are career civil servants.
  US: DAs elected in ~2,300 jurisdictions.

  Political logic:
    "Tough on crime" is a winning electoral message.
    Acquittals = political liability.
    Plea deals at high rates = efficiency + win rate.
    No electoral incentive to prioritize rehabilitation.

  Reform prosecutors (Krasner in Philadelphia, Boudin in SF):
    Ran on progressive platforms (2016-2020 wave).
    Reduced prosecution of low-level offenses.
    Reduced pretrial detention.
    Result: Significant political backlash; some recalled.

  Implication: Criminal justice reform in US faces
  structural obstacle that European systems don't have --
  local electoral politics of crime.
```

## Punishment Philosophy: The Nordics

The Nordic countries are the reference case for low-incarceration, rehabilitation-focused justice.

### Norwegian Model

```
  NORWAY: CRIMINAL JUSTICE PHILOSOPHY
  =====================================

  INCARCERATION RATE: ~55/100,000 (US: ~600)

  PRINCIPLES:
  1. Rehabilitation is the primary goal.
     "Normalization principle": prison should
     resemble normal life as much as possible.

  2. Shortest effective sentence.
     Norway max sentence: 21 years (except terrorism).
     But: "preventive detention" (forvaring) can extend
     for continued risk.

  3. Open prisons:
     Bastoy island prison: minimum security farm.
     Inmates have keys to own rooms.
     Cook their own food.
     Work, education, therapy central.

  HALDEN PRISON (maximum security):
    Designed by architects to minimize stigma.
    No visible weapons.
    Officers eat with inmates.
    Music studio, woodworking, cooking school.
    Officers: 2-year university degree required.

  RECIDIVISM:
    Norway: ~20% reoffend within 2 years
    US: ~68% rearrested within 3 years
    (measures differ; but direction consistent)

  CRITICS:
    Anders Breivik killed 77 people; got 21 years.
    Comfortable prisons don't deter serious crime.
    Cultural homogeneity reduces comparability.
    Norway can afford it (oil wealth).
```

### Germany

```
  GERMAN CRIMINAL JUSTICE
  =======================

  RATE: ~70/100,000

  STRUCTURAL DIFFERENCES:
  - Judges are career civil servants (not elected)
  - Prosecutors are civil servants
  - No adversarial system; active judicial investigation
  - Plea bargaining exists but less dominant
  - Remand (pretrial) requires judicial order with
    specific legal grounds

  SENTENCING:
    Maximum sentence: 15 years (except life for murder)
    "Day fines" (Tagessatz): fines indexed to income
    (not flat amounts that are meaningless to rich)
    Suspended sentences and probation heavily used

  PRISON PHILOSOPHY:
    "Resocialization imperative" in Basic Law (constitution)
    Imprisonment must not destroy the ability to
    return to society. This is a constitutional requirement.

  YOUTH JUSTICE:
    Juvenile Justice Act (JGG): applies to 14-20 year olds
    Emphasis on education, counseling, community service
    Detention last resort for youth
```

### Inequality and Violence

```
  GINI COEFFICIENT vs. HOMICIDE RATE
  =====================================

  Strong positive correlation globally.
  Wilkinson & Pickett ("The Spirit Level", 2009):
    More unequal societies have higher crime, worse health,
    lower social trust, worse outcomes generally.

  Mechanism (multiple competing):
    1. Strain: inequality + success ideology = crime
    2. Social trust: inequality reduces trust/social capital
    3. Demonstration effect: visible wealth disparity
       creates resentment
    4. Institutional: inequality weakens state institutions
       (corruption, impunity)

  Important: Gun availability in US is a separate
  multiplier. Assault rates are similar across nations;
  homicide rates differ because of lethal weapon access.
```

## International Criminal Justice

### The International Criminal Court

```
  ICC (Rome Statute, 1998 / entered force 2002)
  ==============================================

  JURISDICTION:
    Genocide
    Crimes against humanity
    War crimes
    Crime of aggression

  NOT a general international criminal court.
  Only for the gravest crimes.

  COMPLEMENTARITY:
    ICC jurisdiction is residual.
    If national courts prosecute seriously --> ICC defers.
    ICC acts when national courts UNABLE or UNWILLING.

  MEMBERS: 124 states parties
  NOT MEMBERS: US, Russia, China, India
    US "unsigned" Rome Statute under Bush (2002)

  CRITIQUE:
    Mostly prosecuted African defendants.
    But: European and other atrocities not prosecuted.
    "ICC is for Africa" charge -- partially valid structurally.
    However: ICC has also indicted Putin (Ukraine) and
    Netanyahu/Sinwar (Gaza conflict).

  AD HOC TRIBUNALS:
    ICTY (Yugoslavia, 1993-2017): Mladic, Karadzic convicted
    ICTR (Rwanda, 1994-2015): genocidaires prosecuted
    SCSL (Sierra Leone), STL (Lebanon), others
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Lower Incarceration Does Not Equal Lower Crime**
Norway has low incarceration AND low crime. But the US has higher crime AND higher incarceration -- the incarceration does not explain away the crime difference. Crime rates depend on many factors (inequality, guns, social support systems) that are not reducible to incarceration policy.

**Cultural Comparison Objection**
Common claim: Nordic models work because of cultural homogeneity or wealth. Counterevidence: similar policies have worked in non-Nordic contexts (drug decriminalization in Portugal; sentencing reform in multiple US states showing reduced incarceration without crime increase). The cultural argument is partly valid but used as an excuse not to learn from comparisons.

**Adversarial vs. Inquisitorial Is Not Pure**
Modern legal systems mix features. Germany has adversarial elements; US has investigative magistrate-like elements (grand jury). The distinction is a heuristic for key structural differences, not a binary classification.

**The ICC Is Not a World Government**
The ICC has no enforcement power. It depends on states to arrest and transfer defendants. If the host country won't arrest, the ICC warrant is symbolic. This is why Putin can travel to some countries and not others. The ICC is an institution with normative force, not coercive power.
