# Desistance, Reentry, and Rehabilitation

## The Big Picture

**Empirical-methods note:** Desistance is uniquely hard to study because it is defined by the *absence* of events over time — you cannot confirm desistance from a single observation, only infer it from an extended period of non-offending. The methodological gold standard is longitudinal panel data following the same individuals from adolescence into adulthood, ideally with official records (arrest, conviction) supplemented by self-report interviews. The Glueck and Glueck dataset is legendary precisely because Sheldon and Eleanor Glueck tracked 500 Boston delinquents from the 1940s into adulthood, collecting interview and record data through the 1960s. Sampson and Laub rediscovered the archived data in the 1990s, coded it, and extended follow-up through age 70 (death records, criminal records, additional interviews) — giving criminology one of the longest longitudinal datasets in social science: 60+ years of observation on the same cohort. The dataset reveals patterns that shorter studies cannot: chronic offending through middle age, late desistance in the 60s, and the role of specific turning points only visible with long follow-up windows. For a researcher designing such a study today, the challenge is attrition (participants drop from the sample over decades), the endogeneity of turning points (does marriage cause desistance, or do desistance-prone individuals select into marriage?), and survivorship bias (those incarcerated long-term are unavailable for community observation). These are causal identification problems familiar from any long-run natural experiment.

Desistance is the process by which people stop offending. Understanding it is the foundation of rational rehabilitation policy. Most people age out of crime -- the question is what accelerates that process and what the justice system can do (or stop doing) to facilitate it.

## The Age-Crime Curve

The single most replicated finding in criminology: crime peaks in adolescence and early adulthood, then declines with age. This is true across cultures, historical periods, and crime types.

```
  AGE-CRIME CURVE
  ===============

  Rate of
  offending
     |
     |        ***
     |       *   *
     |      *     *
     |     *       **
     |    *          ***
     |   *               *****
     |  *                     ****
     | *                          ****
     |*                               *****
     +--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+---> Age
        10  15  20  25  30  35  40  45  50

  Peak: 16-24 depending on offense type
  Property crime peaks earlier (~16-17)
  Violence peaks slightly later (~18-24)
  White-collar crime peaks later (30s-40s -- requires
  occupational position)

  Gottfredson & Hirschi: Curve is universal and invariant.
  Self-control explains the decline (impulsivity decreases
  with brain maturation).

  Life-course theorists: Social bonds at key life events
  ACCELERATE the natural decline. Marriage, employment,
  military service can produce sharp drops even in high-risk
  individuals.
```

## Desistance Theories

### Sampson and Laub: Social Bonds and Turning Points

```
  SAMPSON & LAUB (1993, 2003)
  ===========================

  Following Glueck & Glueck's delinquent sample
  from childhood to age 70 (unique dataset).

  Key findings:
  1. Even high-rate chronic offenders desisted
     as adults -- determinism is wrong.

  2. QUALITY of social bonds matters:
     Marriage: Stable, committed relationships
     (not just being married -- high-conflict marriage
     does NOT produce desistance)
     Employment: Stable job with employer attachment
     (not just any job -- instability doesn't help)

  3. TURNING POINTS: Discrete events that change
     life trajectory:
     Marriage, military service, geographic move,
     new friendship, religious conversion,
     incarceration (sometimes, paradoxically)

  4. AGENCY matters: People make decisions within
     structural constraints. Same turning point
     produces desistance in some, not others.
     Active choice to "knife off" the past is required.
```

### Giordano: Cognitive Transformation

```
  GIORDANO, CERNKOVICH & RUDOLPH (2002)
  =======================================

  "Cognitive Blueprint for Change"

  Four cognitive shifts required for sustained desistance:

  1. OPENNESS TO CHANGE
     General receptivity to the possibility of
     transformation -- before any specific hook

  2. HOOKS FOR CHANGE
     Encountering specific opportunities
     (job, relationship, religious community)
     that serve as catalysts
     Same hook doesn't work for everyone

  3. ENVISIONING A REPLACEMENT SELF
     Developing a concrete alternative prosocial
     identity to replace criminal identity
     ("I want to be a father," "I want to be a
     tradesperson")

  4. REFRAMING PAST
     Seeing the former deviant lifestyle as
     unattractive, not just unavailable
     ("I don't want that life anymore")

  Key insight: Desistance requires more than opportunity;
  it requires identity change and cognitive reframing.
  This is why the same job offer produces desistance in
  one person but not another.
```

### Identity Theory (Maruna)

```
  MARUNA: "MAKING GOOD" (2001)
  =============================

  Studied 65 persistent offenders:
  30 desisted; 35 persisted.

  DESISTERS' NARRATIVES:
    "Redemption scripts":
    - Accept past self as real but atypical
    - Emphasize "true self" as good person
    - Past hardship = special understanding to help others
    - Often: become mentor, counselor, advocate
    Generativity -- giving back to community

  PERSISTERS' NARRATIVES:
    "Condemnation scripts":
    - Future determined by past
    - No agency
    - "What else can I do?"
    - Fatalistic; no alternative identity available

  Implication: Rehabilitation should support development
  of prosocial narrative and identity, not just
  skills training.
```

## Reentry: The First Year

The period immediately following release from prison is the highest-risk window. Most recidivism that occurs happens within the first year.

### Reentry Challenges Map

```
  DAY OF RELEASE
  ==============

  HOUSING:
    Released with: a bus ticket, $40-200 "gate money"
    No guaranteed housing.
    Options: family (if not barred by their lease),
             halfway house (often unavailable), shelter,
             street.
    HUD regulations: drug felons cannot live in
    Section 8 housing (some jurisdictions).
    Result: High rates of homelessness post-release.

  EMPLOYMENT:
    Most job applications: criminal history question
    Audit studies: 50% callback reduction for record.
    "Felon" checkboxes screened out before interview.
    Ban-the-box laws in some jurisdictions delay
    the question but don't eliminate it.

  SUPERVISION:
    Parole: Active supervision by officer.
    Conditions: report regularly, no weapons, no drugs,
                no association with known criminals,
                no leaving jurisdiction without permission.
    Technical violations: miss appointment, fail drug test
                         = automatic reincarceration.
    25% of prison admissions = technical violations.
    This is NOT a crime -- it is a supervision failure.

  SUBSTANCE USE:
    Many people incarcerated for drug offenses leave
    prison without treatment.
    Return to same environment + same triggers.
    Opioid overdose deaths peak in 1-2 weeks post-release
    (tolerance loss + return to use).
```

## What Works in Rehabilitation

### Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

```
  CBT FOR OFFENDERS
  =================

  Mechanism: Crime-supporting thinking patterns
             ("That's mine if I can take it";
              "Disrespect must be answered with force";
              "No one can tell me what to do")

  CBT addresses:
    - Cognitive distortions (unrealistic thinking)
    - Impulsivity / problem-solving deficits
    - Social perspective-taking
    - Anger management
    - Antisocial attitudes

  PROGRAMS:
    Moral Reconation Therapy (MRT)
    Thinking for a Change
    Reasoning and Rehabilitation (R&R)
    Aggression Replacement Training

  EVIDENCE: STRONG
    Meta-analyses: 8-15% reduction in recidivism
    Effect sizes larger for higher-risk offenders
    (Andrews & Bonta: "Risk-Needs-Responsivity" principle)
```

### Risk-Need-Responsivity (RNR) Model

The dominant evidence-based framework in corrections:

```
  RISK-NEED-RESPONSIVITY (Andrews & Bonta)
  ==========================================

  RISK PRINCIPLE:
    Match intervention intensity to offender risk level.
    High-risk offenders: intensive programs
    Low-risk offenders: minimal intervention
    (Giving intensive programs to low-risk offenders
     INCREASES recidivism -- the peer contamination problem)

  NEED PRINCIPLE:
    Target "criminogenic needs" -- dynamic risk factors
    that are both (a) correlated with reoffending and
    (b) changeable through intervention.

  The "Big Eight" criminogenic needs:
  +---------------------------+
  | 1. Criminal history*      | (* static -- cannot change)
  | 2. Procriminal attitudes  |
  | 3. Procriminal associates |
  | 4. Antisocial personality |
  | 5. Substance abuse        |
  | 6. Family dysfunction     |
  | 7. Employment problems    |
  | 8. Lack of education/     |
  |    leisure prosocial      |
  +---------------------------+

  RESPONSIVITY PRINCIPLE:
    Match intervention style to offender learning style.
    General: cognitive-behavioral approaches work across groups
    Specific: accommodate learning disabilities, mental health,
              age, gender, cultural factors
```

### Education in Prison

```
  CORRECTIONAL EDUCATION
  =======================

  PROGRAMS:
    GED (General Education Diploma) programs
    Post-secondary (college) programs
    (Federal Pell Grants for prisoners: eliminated 1994,
     restored 2023 via Second Chance Pell Act)

  EVIDENCE: MODERATE-STRONG
    RAND (2013): Education programs reduce recidivism
    by 43% (rearrest rate) vs. non-participants.
    Vocational training: 28% reduction.
    Cost-effective: education cheaper than incarceration
    (educational savings outweigh program costs).

  BARRIERS:
    Prison disrupts education (transfers, lockdowns).
    Educational programming cut in fiscal pressures.
    Stigma of "country club" prison narrative.
```

## Restorative Justice

```
  RESTORATIVE JUSTICE MODEL
  ==========================

  Traditional criminal justice:
    Crime = violation of state law
    Goal = punishment of offender
    Victim = witness

  Restorative justice:
    Crime = violation of people and relationships
    Goal = repair of harm
    All parties (victim, offender, community) participate

  FORMS:
    Victim-Offender Mediation: face-to-face
    Conferencing: family group conferencing (NZ model)
    Circles: indigenous-derived community circles

  EVIDENCE:
    Victim satisfaction: STRONG -- victims prefer it
    Offender recidivism: MODERATE -- some reduction
    Recommended for: juvenile cases, property crime,
                     some violent cases (consent required)
    Not recommended for: domestic violence (power imbalance)

  NEW ZEALAND MODEL:
    Youth justice system default: family group conferencing
    Dramatic reduction in formal prosecutions
    Maori community involvement central
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Desistance Is a Process, Not an Event**
People rarely stop offending abruptly. They go through cycles of offending and desisting, with each desistance period potentially longer. A single arrest after years of compliance doesn't mean desistance failed -- it's part of the process. This has implications for how technical violations are handled.

**Risk Principle Counterintuitive Direction**
The risk principle says: don't treat low-risk offenders intensively. This is counterintuitive -- surely more treatment is better? The evidence is clear: intensive programs for low-risk people increase recidivism, probably because they expose low-risk people to high-risk peers (peer contamination, labeling effects). Match intensity to risk.

**Recidivism Measure Matters Enormously**
Studies citing "67% recidivism rate" typically mean rearrest within 3 years. Reconviction rates are lower (~40%). Reincarceration rates are lower still (~30%). And these include technical violations. The appropriate measure depends on the policy question. A new serious crime is categorically different from a missed parole appointment.

### Drug Treatment Programs

```
  THERAPEUTIC COMMUNITIES (TC)
  ==============================

  Model: Total immersion residential treatment.
  Duration: 6-24 months (longer = better outcomes).
  Structure: Hierarchical community; peers as primary therapists.
  Address: Criminal thinking, substance use, prosocial identity.

  KEY EVIDENCE:
    CDATE (Correctional Drug Abuse Treatment Effectiveness):
    Meta-analysis of 78 studies: TC showed strongest effect sizes
    among all correctional drug treatments.

    Amity Prison TC (California):
    RCT with 3-year follow-up:
    TC completers: 27% reincarcerated vs. 75% control.
    Effect persisted only for TC graduates who entered
    community TC aftercare -- continuity of care critical.

  NOTE: "Therapeutic community" in prison ≠ TC aftercare.
  The two-stage model (prison TC → community TC aftercare)
  produces significantly better outcomes than prison TC alone.

  MEDICATION-ASSISTED TREATMENT (MAT)
  =====================================

  For opioid use disorder (OUD), MAT is the strongest
  evidence-based intervention available.

  METHADONE (full opioid agonist):
    Reduces illicit opioid use, overdose deaths, crime.
    Must be dispensed daily at licensed clinic (logistically burdensome).
    Meta-analyses: ~50% reduction in heroin use; crime reduction substantial.
    Reduces HIV transmission (shared needle use falls).

  BUPRENORPHINE (partial agonist, Suboxone):
    Can be prescribed by qualified physicians (no daily clinic visit).
    Ceiling effect: less overdose risk than methadone.
    More accessible; preferred for mild-moderate OUD.
    Similar effectiveness to methadone for retention and outcomes.

  NALTREXONE (opioid antagonist, Vivitrol extended-release):
    Blocks opioid receptors; no abuse potential.
    Monthly injection form improves adherence.
    Evidence: comparable to buprenorphine for motivated patients;
    worse for patients without strong external support (no agonist = harder).

  CRITICAL POLICY FACT:
    Most US jails and prisons do not offer MAT.
    People with OUD leave incarceration with zero tolerance.
    Opioid overdose death rate peaks 1-2 weeks post-release.
    MAT in correctional settings is evidence-based and cost-effective;
    its absence is a policy choice, not a resource constraint.
```

**What Works Is Not "Soft on Crime"**
CBT and education programs reduce recidivism -- they reduce future crime victims. That is the point of incarceration (or should be). The argument for evidence-based rehabilitation is not humanitarianism alone; it is crime reduction. Punitive measures that feel hard but don't reduce reoffending produce more future victims.
