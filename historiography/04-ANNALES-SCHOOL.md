# The Annales School: Longue Duree, Mentalites, Total History

## The Big Picture

The Annales school, founded in France in 1929, is the most influential historiographical movement of the 20th century. It attacked the Rankean tradition's obsession with political events (histoire evenementielle) and replaced it with social, economic, and geographic structures operating over long time spans. The school's most famous concept -- Braudel's longue duree -- is a framework for thinking about historical time as multi-layered, not as a single stream of events.

```
ANNALES: THREE GENERATIONS

  FIRST GENERATION (1929-1945): FOUNDING
    Lucien Febvre (1878-1956) + Marc Bloch (1886-1944)
    Journal: "Annales d'histoire economique et sociale" (1929)
    Program: revolt against event-history; structures over events
    Bloch: Feudal Society, Apologie pour l'histoire
    Febvre: problem-oriented history; mentalites

  SECOND GENERATION (1945-1968): BRAUDEL
    Fernand Braudel (1902-1985): institutional dominance
    Braudel: The Mediterranean (1949); Civilization and Capitalism (1979)
    Quantitative emphasis: price series, serial history
    "Total history": aspiration to capture all dimensions simultaneously

  THIRD GENERATION (1968-1989): MENTALITES AND FRAGMENTATION
    Jacques Le Goff, Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie, Pierre Nora
    Mentalites: collective attitudes, popular beliefs, symbolic systems
    Microhistory influence: scale shifts from large to individual
    Nora: "Lieux de memoire" (places of memory, 1984-1992)

  FOURTH GENERATION (1989+): UNCERTAIN
    Cultural turn absorbs Annales; global history challenges French focus
    Return to narrative under some
    No single dominant paradigm
```

## Marc Bloch (1886-1944): Method and Feudal Society

Bloch was arguably the more methodologically innovative of the two founders. His arrest and execution by the Gestapo in 1944 cut short the most influential practitioner of early Annales method:

```
BLOCH'S CONTRIBUTIONS

  FEUDAL SOCIETY (La Societe feodale, 1939-1940):
    Total history attempt: mental world (myths, collective memory) +
    material conditions (diet, climate, disease) +
    social structure (lordship, serfdom, knighthood) +
    political institutions (fiefs, homage, jurisdiction)
    NOT a political narrative of feudalism but a social analysis

  COMPARATIVE METHOD:
    Compare European feudalism with Japanese feudalism
    Both: fragmented sovereignty, personal bonds, warrior class
    Differences: reveal what is contingent vs. what is structural
    "Comparative history is the only way to distinguish the general from the local"

  APOLOGIE POUR L'HISTOIRE (unfinished, published posthumously 1949):
    Best introduction to historical method of the 20th century
    "What is history? The science of men in time"
    Method: observation through traces (vestiges); use all types of evidence
    Time: historians must think about both short and long time scales simultaneously
    Famous opening: "Tell me, Daddy -- what is history good for?"

  MATERIAL AND HUMAN DIMENSIONS:
    Royal touch: The Royal Touch (1924) -- study of the belief that kings
    could cure scrofula by touching; mentalite study avant la lettre
    Traces of past: landscape, place names, field systems as sources
    -> Archaeology, geography, linguistics all part of the historian's toolkit
```

## Braudel's Three Temporal Levels

Braudel's contribution to historiography: the claim that history operates simultaneously at three temporal levels with radically different speeds of change:

```
BRAUDEL'S THREE LEVELS OF TIME

  LEVEL 1: GEOGRAPHIC TIME (longue duree, very long duration)
    Scale: centuries to millennia
    Subject: geography, climate, ecology, resources
    Change rate: quasi-immobile (humans must adapt to it, not vice versa)

    THE MEDITERRANEAN (1949): begins with geography
    Part I: "The Role of the Environment"
      Mountains vs. coasts; sea routes; climate zones
      Mediterranean = a specific climatic/ecological unit
      This geography shaped everything: trade, settlement, religion, conflict

    Examples of longue duree structures:
      Mediterranean basin's summer drought and winter rain
      Alps and Pyrenees as barriers to north-south movement
      Steppe corridor as route for pastoral nomads
      Disease environments (malaria in low-lying coastal areas)

  LEVEL 2: CONJUNCTURAL TIME (medium duration, cycles)
    Scale: decades to a century
    Subject: economic cycles, population cycles, demographic trends
    Change rate: slow oscillations, often below contemporaries' awareness

    Price cycles: 16th century price revolution (silver influx from Americas)
    Population waves: plague mortality cycles and recovery
    Economic conjunctures: expansion and contraction
    Social structures: rise and fall of merchant classes

    These "conjunctures" shape what individual actions are possible
    You can't start a successful revolution in an economic upswing

  LEVEL 3: SURFACE TIME (events, evenements)
    Scale: days, weeks, years
    Subject: political events, battles, diplomatic negotiations, individual actions
    Change rate: fast; the "froth" on deeper waters

    Traditional political history: this level only
    Braudel's critique: events are the least causally important level
    "Events are dust"

  RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN LEVELS:
    Not deterministic: geography does not program events
    But: geographic and conjunctural constraints limit what events can achieve
    Event-level actors operate within structures they cannot see or change
    Historical explanation requires all three levels simultaneously
```

## The Mediterranean (1949): The Model Work

```
THE MEDITERRANEAN AND THE MEDITERRANEAN WORLD
IN THE AGE OF PHILIP II (2 volumes, 1949, revised 1966)

  STRUCTURE OF THE WORK:
    Part I: The Role of the Environment (geo time)
    Part II: Collective Destinies and General Trends (conjunctural time)
    Part III: Events, Politics, and People (event time)
    -> Order reverses narrative convention: slow first, fast last

  THE ARGUMENT:
    Philip II's decision to send the Armada against England (1588) is almost
    irrelevant to understanding the 16th-century Mediterranean
    The real history is: trade networks, silver from America, population growth,
    climate fluctuations, disease patterns, piracy, Ottoman-Habsburg confrontation
    These structural forces shape what Philip could do, far more than his decisions

  RECEPTION:
    Immediately recognized as a major work
    The Mediterranean as a spatial unit of analysis influenced "Atlantic history"
    and regional history worldwide
    Criticized: politics and agency disappear; no people in the landscape
    Criticized: too long (1200+ pages), structurally incoherent
    Feminist critique: women entirely absent
```

## Mentalites: Collective Attitudes and Symbolic Worlds

The third Annales generation shifted from structures to mentalities -- collective attitudes, beliefs, and symbolic systems that shape how people perceive and act in the world:

```
MENTALITES HISTORY

  CONCEPT:
    Mentalite (collective mentality) = shared, largely unconscious attitudes
    and frameworks through which a population understands the world
    Not individual psychology (that's social history's interest in class consciousness)
    Not official ideology (that's intellectual history)
    Somewhere in between: popular beliefs, ritual behavior, symbolic systems

  KEY WORKS:

    MONTAILLOU (Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie, 1975):
      Village in southern France, 1294-1324
      Source: Inquisition records against Cathar heresy (Jacques Fournier's register)
      Extraordinarily detailed: peasant beliefs, sexuality, daily life, village politics
      Methodology: read against the grain (inquisitor wanted to destroy heresy;
      historian uses records to recover the world the inquisitor was looking at)
      Published widely; major success: proved "total history" of a small community possible

    ATTITUDES TOWARD DEATH (Philippe Aries):
      L'Homme devant la mort (The Hour of Our Death, 1977)
      Traced changing attitudes toward death in Western culture from medieval to modern
      Medieval: "tamed death" (accepted, communal, familiar)
      Modern: "wild death" (denied, medicalized, hidden)
      Method: wills, tomb inscriptions, deathbed rituals, art

    THE MEDIEVAL IMAGINATION (Le Goff):
      Purgatory as historical concept (1981): purgatory invented in the 12th century
      Not a theological given: historical emergence of a belief
      Connects doctrine to social structure (class in afterlife?)
```

## Lieux de Memoire (Pierre Nora): Places of Memory

Nora's 7-volume project (1984-1992) represents the late Annales engagement with memory studies:

```
LIEUX DE MEMOIRE (PLACES/SITES OF MEMORY)

  ARGUMENT:
    France has lost "milieux de memoire" (living environments of memory --
    where the past is naturally transmitted through living practice)
    In their absence, "lieux de memoire" (constructed sites) emerge:
    archives, monuments, anniversaries, commemorations
    -> Deliberate memory as substitute for natural transmission

  METHODOLOGY:
    Study not the past events themselves, but the sites through which
    France symbolically represents its national past
    Examples: the Marseillaise, the Tricolor, Verdun, Versailles, the Tour de France
    Each site is analyzed: when it was "invented," what it symbolizes, how it changes

  SIGNIFICANCE:
    Invented traditions (Hobsbawm and Ranger: 1983) connection
    Memory studies as a field: launched by Nora's project
    "Heritage" industry analysis: what gets preserved, by whom, for whom

  CRITIQUE:
    Exclusively French national focus
    Elitist sites: not popular memory
    The "loss of living memory" narrative: nostalgic, possibly constructed
```

## Critiques of the Annales School

```
ANNALES CRITIQUES

1. NEGLECT OF POLITICS AND AGENCY:
   People disappear from structural history
   Decisions, intentions, consequences of individual action: irrelevant
   Political history = "epiphenomenon"
   Response: the "return to the event" (Furet on the French Revolution:
   Annales methods applied to political revolution)

2. STRUCTURAL DETERMINISM:
   Geography and conjuncture as near-deterministic
   Where is there room for counterfactual outcomes?
   Braudel: structures constrain but do not determine

3. METHODOLOGICAL IMPERIALISM:
   "Total history" as aspiration: impossible to achieve
   Long-duration analysis works better for some questions (climate, disease)
   than others (why did the French Revolution happen when it did, not earlier?)

4. EUROCENTRIC CORE:
   Most Annales work: France and Mediterranean
   "World history" Braudel writes in Civilization and Capitalism: still Western-centric
   Non-European worlds: appear as periphery to European core

5. FEMINIST AND POSTCOLONIAL:
   Women almost entirely absent from Annales history through the 1970s
   Non-European peoples: appear mainly as economic data (commodity prices,
   population statistics) not as historical agents
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Annales Concept | Historical Question It Answers | Limitation |
|-----------------|-------------------------------|------------|
| Longue duree | Why do some patterns persist for centuries? | Cannot explain why change happens when it does |
| Geographic determinism | How does ecology constrain historical possibilities? | Determinism ignores agency |
| Conjunctural analysis | How do economic cycles shape political options? | Cycle identification is retrospective |
| Mentalites | What did ordinary people believe? | Individual variation lost; hard to source |
| Serial history | What were the aggregate patterns in prices/population/crime? | Data quality; category changes |
| Lieux de memoire | How does national memory get constructed? | Focus on official/elite sites |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Annales" is not a single unified method** -- The Annales school spans three generations and encompasses Bloch's comparative method, Braudel's structural geography, Ladurie's mentalites, and Nora's memory theory. What unites them is the rejection of pure event-history and interest in social/economic/mental structures -- not a specific methodology.

**Longue duree is not just "long period"** -- Any narrative can cover centuries. Longue duree specifically refers to structures that change so slowly they appear nearly immobile within a human lifetime: geographic features, climatic cycles, ecological patterns. The key is the speed of change, not just the time span.

**Braudel was not a determinist** -- Despite the near-geological language about geographic time, Braudel explicitly denied that longue duree structures determine events. They set constraints within which agents operate, but do not specify outcomes. The three levels interact: events can shift conjunctures; conjunctures can alter long-term structures (slowly).

**"Mentalite" is collective, not individual** -- A mentalites study is not the same as psychological history of an individual or intellectual history of an elite thinker. It concerns the largely unconscious, shared frameworks that a population brings to experience. This makes sourcing difficult: the best sources are inadvertent (inquisition records, notarial records) rather than deliberate (philosophical treatises).
