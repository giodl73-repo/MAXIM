# Ancient and Medieval Historians

## The Big Picture

The historians of antiquity and the medieval period invented the fundamental conventions that Western and Islamic historiography still operate within: the question of evidence (what sources do you trust, and why?), the question of causation (why did events happen?), and the question of purpose (is history a guide to action, a record of memory, or a moral exemplar?). The answers differed radically between authors.

```
HISTORIOGRAPHICAL SPECTRUM IN ANTIQUITY

  HERODOTUS                    THUCYDIDES
  (484-425 BCE)                (~460-400 BCE)

  Subject: Persian Wars,       Subject: Peloponnesian War
           ethnography         (the war he lived through)
  Method:  Travel, interview,  Method: Eyewitness, critique of
           oral tradition       sources, rejection of myths
  Attitude: Curious, digressive Attitude: Analytical, severe
  Causation: Divine will,      Causation: Human nature, power,
             hubris, fate       fear, honor, interest
  Evidence: Accept many        Evidence: Critical: reject unless
           accounts            verified or plausible
  Goal:     Preserve memory,  Goal:     Timeless understanding
            entertain          of political/military dynamics
  Verdict:  "Father of History" "Father of Scientific History"
            vs. "Father of Lies" (modern consensus)
```

## Herodotus (c.484-425 BCE)

The Histories (Gk: historiai, inquiries) is the first major work of prose history in Western tradition. Its subject is the Persian Wars (490 and 480-479 BCE), but it opens outward into ethnographic surveys of Egypt, Scythia, Persia, Lydia, and the world as Herodotus encountered it in his travels.

```
HERODOTUS'S METHOD

  SOURCES: Oral testimony (autopsy = eyewitness; akoai = hearsay from others)
    Herodotus explicitly distinguishes: "I know this because I saw it" vs.
    "This is what the Egyptians told me" vs. "This is what the Persians say"
    -- a rudimentary but genuine source criticism

  CAUSATION: Multi-level
    Immediate: specific decisions, contingent events
    Background: hubris (transgression of proper limits)
    Divine: the gods ensure hubris is punished; cycles of rise and fall
    Pattern: Persian Empire's hybris -> divine nemesis -> defeat

  ETHNOGRAPHIC METHOD:
    Extended descriptions of non-Greek peoples (Scythians, Persians, Egyptians)
    Comparative: Greek customs compared to others without automatic Greek superiority
    Anthropological avant la lettre: customs are relative, not universal
    "Custom is king" (nomos basileus)

  SPEECHES: Reported as summaries, not claimed verbatim
    (less strict than Thucydides's protocol)

  WHY "FATHER OF LIES":
    Many Herodotean "facts" are false or exaggerated
    (Ants that dig gold in India; Nile source in Libya)
    BUT: his method of recording sources and acknowledging uncertainty
    is more sophisticated than critics allow
    Recent archaeology: validates some Herodotean claims dismissed as fanciful
```

## Thucydides (c.460-400 BCE)

The History of the Peloponnesian War is the most analytically rigorous ancient historiographical work and established standards that still define the discipline's self-image:

```
THUCYDIDES'S METHOD

  SOURCE CRITICISM:
    Explicit rejection of poetic traditions (Homer) as historical sources
    Methodological principle: only admit events where he was present
    or where he could cross-check surviving witnesses
    "Not the most pleasant to listen to, since there is no legendary element"

  SPEECHES:
    The famous problem: Thucydides admits he could not record speeches verbatim
    Protocol (1.22): "I have made the speakers say what seemed to me demanded
    of them by the various occasions, keeping as close to the general intent
    of what was actually said as possible"
    -> Speeches as analytical devices expressing what rational actors
       should have said in their situation
    -> Not fabrication but historical interpretation; still controversial

  CAUSATION:
    AITIA (immediate causes): Corcyrean affair, Potidaea, Megarian decree
    PROPHASIS (truest cause, underlying): "The growth of Athenian power
    and the fear this inspired in Sparta"
    -> First systematic distinction between proximate and underlying causes
    -> "Thucydidean trap" (Graham Allison): rising power vs. status quo power
       creates structural pressure toward war regardless of immediate triggers

  HUMAN NATURE:
    History recurs because human nature is constant
    "Useful for as long as human nature remains the same"
    -> Historical understanding = prediction tool for statecraft
    -> This claim is simultaneously his most influential and most contested

  LIMITATIONS:
    Athenian perspective (was Athenian general; exiled for failing at Amphipolis)
    Ends abruptly 411 BCE (war ends 404)
    Almost no economic or social history
    Dismisses most non-political causation
```

## Polybius (c.200-118 BCE)

Polybius was a Greek hostage in Rome who observed Rome's rise to Mediterranean dominance and wrote a universal history explaining how Rome conquered the world in 53 years:

```
POLYBIUS'S CONTRIBUTIONS

  PRAGMATIC HISTORY:
    History should train statesmen and generals
    "The best education and training for political life is the study of history"
    Eyewitness observation and participation in events preferred

  ANACYCLOSIS (constitutional cycle theory):
    Polybius derived from Plato a theory of constitutional change:
    Kingship -> Tyranny -> Aristocracy -> Oligarchy -> Democracy -> Ochlocracy
    (mob rule) -> back to Monarchy
    Rome's mixed constitution (consuls=monarchy, Senate=aristocracy,
    assemblies=democracy) explains its stability and success

  TYCHE (Fortune):
    Fortune works through rational causes, not random chance
    Polybius uses tyche to explain Roman success as a coherent story
    But also: good fortune can shift; Rome's empire may follow anacyclosis

  UNIVERSAL HISTORY:
    First explicit claim to write a history covering the whole known world
    (Mediterranean basin) under a single explanatory framework
    Precursor to world history as genre
```

## Livy (59 BCE - 17 CE)

Ab Urbe Condita (From the Founding of the City): 142 books (35 survive), covering Rome from Romulus through 9 BCE. Livy is the model for patriotic narrative history:

```
LIVY'S METHOD AND PURPOSES

  SOURCES: Earlier annalists, records destroyed in Gallic sack (390 BCE),
           monuments, oral tradition, oral tradition laundered through
           earlier Roman historians
  PROBLEM: Most of his sources are already literary; he cannot verify independently

  SPEECHES: Openly rhetorical and reconstructed; acknowledged as compositional

  PURPOSE:
    "Whether the record of the Roman people's doings was worth the labor it
    would cost me to write it I do not really know... but if I were not, I
    would find peace in recounting the virtues of great men of an earlier age"
    -> History as moral exemplar: maiores (ancestors) set standards
    -> Roman aristocratic ethos: the past judges the present

  FLAW AS HISTORIAN:
    Often works backwards from Roman legend (Romulus, early kings)
    Cannot distinguish myth from history; no skepticism about tradition
    Accuracy for pre-300 BCE Rome: very low
    Accuracy for post-300 BCE: much better (more surviving sources)
    Historical value: contemporary perspective; shows what Romans believed
```

## Sima Qian (145-86 BCE): Chinese Historiography

Sima Qian, Grand Historian (Taishi) of the Han court, established the canonical form of Chinese historiography that would persist for 2,000 years:

```
SHIJI (RECORDS OF THE GRAND HISTORIAN)

  Structure: 130 chapters, ~526,000 characters
  Format: ANNALS-BIOGRAPHIES (ji-zhuan):
    Benji (Annals): chronological account of rulers
    Biao (Tables): chronological tables
    Shu (Treatises): economics, music, astronomy, ritual
    Shijia (Hereditary Houses): feudal states and families
    Liezhuan (Biographies): prominent individuals

  COVERAGE: From Yellow Emperor through Emperor Wu of Han (~100 BCE)
  SOURCE CRITICAL: Sima Qian explicitly notes conflicting accounts and
  the limits of his evidence; close to Herodotus in epistemological honesty

  PERSONAL TRAGEDY:
    Defended a general who surrendered to Xiongnu (nomads)
    Emperor Wu had him castrated (alternatively: death or castration)
    Chose castration to complete the Shiji
    His personal voice enters the text: unusual for official history

  INFLUENCE: The ji-zhuan format became mandatory for subsequent official
    Chinese histories (Ershisi Shi = 24 Standard Histories)
    Sima Qian: equivalent canonical status to Thucydides in Western tradition
```

## Bede (672-735 CE)

The Venerable Bede, Northumbrian monk at Jarrow, wrote Ecclesiastical History of the English People (731 CE) -- the most important work of medieval English history and a model of critical method unusual for its time:

```
BEDE'S METHOD

  SOURCE USE:
    Explicit about sources and their limitations
    "I have not relied on any one author, but have drawn upon innumerable sources"
    Used: papal letters (obtained from Rome), regional church records,
    oral testimony from living witnesses, earlier chronicles

  CRITICAL DISTINCTIONS:
    Distinguishes: what he has verified vs. what he has heard
    Notes when sources contradict each other
    Compares testimony from multiple witnesses
    For a medieval monk: this is genuinely remarkable source criticism

  AD DATING SYSTEM:
    Bede popularized the Dionysius Exiguus system of reckoning from
    the Incarnation (Anno Domini)
    Before Bede: dating systems were regnal (year of a king's reign),
    indiction cycles (15-year fiscal period), or am (year from Creation)
    Bede's Ecclesiastical History used AD consistently throughout
    -> Became the standard for Christian Europe

  ECCLESIASTICAL FRAMEWORK:
    All causation is ultimately providential
    English conversion to Christianity = primary theme
    Pagan past = darkness; Christian present = light
    LIMITATION: Non-Christian perspective inaccessible to Bede's framework
    Social and economic history invisible to him
```

## Ibn Khaldun (1332-1406): Proto-Sociology

Abd al-Rahman ibn Khaldun wrote the Muqaddimah (Introduction, or Prolegomena) as the theoretical framework for his universal history (Kitab al-Ibar). It is arguably the most sophisticated work of historical theory before the 19th century:

```
IBN KHALDUN'S THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

  ASABIYYAH (group feeling / social solidarity):
    The cohesive force binding a tribal or social group together
    Strong asabiyyah: enables conquest and state-building
    Key thesis: dynasties rise from nomadic/tribal origins with strong asabiyyah
    -> conquer settled peoples with weak asabiyyah
    -> over 3-4 generations, settle, become luxurious, lose asabiyyah
    -> vulnerable to new nomadic group with strong asabiyyah
    -> cycle repeats

  CYCLICAL THEORY OF DYNASTIC CHANGE:
    Generation 1: founder, strong asabiyyah, austere
    Generation 2: consolidation, some luxury
    Generation 3: decline, luxury, bureaucratic
    Generation 4: dissolution, conquered
    Not deterministic: some dynasties break the cycle
    Historical evidence: Arab caliphates, Berber dynasties

  GEOGRAPHY AS DETERMINANT:
    Climate affects character: hot climates -> different temperament than cold
    Desert vs. agricultural environments -> different social organization
    Anticipates Montesquieu (Esprit des Lois 1748) by 350 years

  CRITIQUE OF SOURCES:
    Systematic list of reasons why historical accounts are distorted:
    1. Partisanship with opinions
    2. Reliance on transmitters without investigation
    3. Failure to understand context
    4. Baseless assumption that the truth is known
    5. Ignorance of how events conform to their context
    6. Desire for favor from great men
    7. Ignorance of human nature

  ECONOMIC ANALYSIS:
    Labor theory of value avant la lettre
    Population as economic determinant
    Cities and markets as products of civilization's complexity
    Proto-Keynesian: government spending stimulates economic activity
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet: What Each Historian Offers

| Historian | Distinctive Contribution | Still Relevant For |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Herodotus | Ethnographic openness; source labeling; multi-causal | Multicultural history; oral tradition; contingency |
| Thucydides | Proximate/underlying cause split; human nature constant; speeches as analysis | IR theory (Thucydidean trap); political realism |
| Polybius | Universal history; constitutional typology; military analysis | Institutional analysis; comparative politics |
| Livy | Narrative model; how myth and history interweave | National memory; propaganda; the uses of the past |
| Sima Qian | Annals-biographies format; biographical approach; personal voice | Chinese historiography; structure of official history |
| Bede | Source criticism; chronology (AD dating); institutional church history | Medieval sources; how chronology shapes narrative |
| Ibn Khaldun | Social cohesion theory; cyclical dynastic change; geography | State formation; rise-and-fall cycles; comparative history |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Father of History" vs. "Father of Lies"** -- The characterization of Herodotus as unreliable comes largely from his contemporaries (Thucydides's implicit dismissal) and later critics who applied later evidence standards retroactively. Recent scholarship (e.g., Fehling, West) shows Herodotean accuracy is better than often claimed, and his method of source attribution is sophisticated. Both characterizations are reductive.

**Thucydides's "truest cause" is structural, not conspiratorial** -- The claim that fear of Athenian power was the "truest cause" of the Peloponnesian War is a structural argument (power imbalance -> war tendency), not a claim that Spartans were secretly planning war while pretending otherwise. It predicts behavior from systemic position, not from individual motivation.

**Ibn Khaldun was not "ahead of his time" in isolation** -- The Muqaddimah is continuous with the Islamic scholarly tradition: Quran commentary, fiqh (jurisprudence), and earlier Islamic historians. His innovation was applying rational analysis systematically to social patterns rather than to divine will. He emerged from a specific intellectual context, not as a solitary anomaly.

**Medieval historiography is not all providential** -- Bede is providentialist. But medieval historians ranged widely: Otto of Freising wrote a philosophy of history influenced by Augustine; Byzantine chronographers were often quite secular in focus; Arabic historians (al-Tabari) compiled extensive source-critical documentation. Medieval = uniformly credulous is false.
