# Enlightenment Historiography: Reason, Progress, and Criticism

## The Big Picture

Enlightenment historiography (roughly 1680-1800) broke from Renaissance humanism and the chronicle tradition in three ways: it applied philosophical skepticism to historical sources, it substituted civilization for rulers and battles as the subject matter, and it embedded history within a master narrative of progress. The Enlightenment also produced its own critics (Vico, Herder) who challenged the universalism of the progress narrative before it had fully consolidated.

```
ENLIGHTENMENT HISTORIOGRAPHY MAP

  PHILOSOPHES (French):           BRITISH:                CRITICS:
  Voltaire: universal history     Hume: skepticism to     Vico: cycles, not progress
  (civilization = subject)        history (custom vs.     Herder: each culture its
  Montesquieu: comparative        reason as source)       own Geist; historicism
  political institutions          Gibbon: scholarship +   (counter-Enlightenment)
                                  irony + readable prose

  COMMON THREADS:
    1. Reason as method: apply philosophical skepticism to sources
    2. Secular causation: replace divine providence with human action
    3. Progress narrative: stadial theory (savagery -> barbarism -> civilization)
    4. Universal history: Europe not unique; China, Persia, India compared
    5. Source criticism: classical and biblical sources examined critically
```

## Voltaire (1694-1778)

Voltaire's historical works -- Siecle de Louis XIV (1751), Essay on the Customs and the Spirit of Nations (Essai sur les moeurs, 1756) -- established the genre of cultural history as distinct from political chronicle:

```
VOLTAIRE'S HISTORIOGRAPHICAL INNOVATIONS

  SUBJECT MATTER SHIFT:
    Traditional: rulers, battles, dynastic succession, treaties
    Voltaire: "I wished to understand what human society has been, what men
    have lived on, what has been the political foundation of their states,
    their arts, their customs, their laws"
    -> Civilization as historical subject

  UNIVERSALISM:
    Essai sur les moeurs begins with China (not Greece) -- revolutionary
    "We must know the condition of the rest of the world before writing
    about the small part of it we call Europe"
    Comparative framework: Chinese civilization as counter-example to European
    particularism

  ANTI-CLERICAL CRITIQUE:
    Systematic skepticism toward medieval Christianity as historical period
    "The dark ages" as period of clerical obscurantism (partially his framing)
    History of religion examined rationally, not reverentially
    -> Established the secular historiographical tradition

  LIMITATIONS:
    Progress narrative assumes European commercial civilization as destination
    His "universal history" is still quite Eurocentric in its standards
    Not an archival researcher: relies heavily on secondary accounts
    History as social criticism instrument: past weaponized for present
```

## Edward Gibbon (1737-1794)

The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire (6 volumes, 1776-1789) is the greatest work of English historical prose and a major historiographical achievement:

```
GIBBON'S METHOD AND ARGUMENT

  SCOPE:
    From the height of the Roman Empire (2nd century CE) to the fall of
    Constantinople (1453) -- 1,300 years
    Encompasses: Western Empire's fall, Eastern Empire's survival,
    barbarian kingdoms, early Islam, Crusades, Byzantine decline

  SOURCES:
    Extensive footnotes (500+ pages) citing primary sources in Latin,
    Greek, Arabic, French, Italian
    Genuine source criticism: notes where sources contradict each other
    Distinguished from Voltaire: Gibbon was an archival researcher

  CAUSATION:
    Famous formula: "The decline of Rome was the natural and inevitable
    effect of immoderate greatness... victory could not but hasten the fall"
    Secondary factor: "barbarism and religion"
    -> Christianity: undermined civic virtue; directed energy to otherworldly
       concerns; divided Empire through theological disputes
    -> This caused enormous controversy (Chapters 15-16 on Christianity)

  IRONY AS METHOD:
    Gibbon's literary irony distances reader from Christian apologists
    Irony as a way to criticize without explicit polemic
    Samuel Johnson: "His style is not English; it is Gibbonian"

  LIMITATIONS:
    Eastern Empire (which survived until 1453) treated as continuation of
    decline rather than distinct success story
    Economic history underdeveloped; gender essentially absent
```

## David Hume (1711-1776)

Hume's History of England (6 volumes, 1754-1762) applied his epistemological skepticism to English history:

```
HUME ON HISTORICAL KNOWLEDGE

  EPISTEMOLOGICAL BASE:
    Hume's empiricist skepticism (Treatise of Human Nature, 1739) applies to history
    We know past events only through testimony and documents
    Reliability of historical testimony degrades over time and transmission chains

  ON MIRACLES (Enquiry 1748 -- section 10):
    "No testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle unless the testimony be
    of such a kind that its falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact
    which it endeavors to establish"
    -> Rationalist principle for evaluating unusual historical claims
    -> Systematically undermines miraculous elements in historical tradition

  CUSTOM AND HABIT:
    Most of what we call "reason" is actually custom and habit
    Historical change: driven by psychology of human association, not rational design
    -> Counter to purely rationalist theories of progress

  HISTORY OF ENGLAND:
    Began with the Stuarts, extended backwards
    Tory in political sympathy: suspicious of Whig progressive teleology
    "Philosophical history": apply analytical frameworks, not just chronicle
```

## Giambattista Vico (1668-1744)

Vico's Scienza Nuova (New Science, 1725, revised 1730, 1744) is the most original work of Enlightenment historical theory, largely ignored until the 19th century:

```
VICO'S CONTRIBUTIONS

  VERUM-FACTUM PRINCIPLE:
    "The true is the made" (verum et factum convertuntur)
    We can only fully know what we have made (constructed)
    -> Mathematics: humans can know geometry because humans invented it
    -> Natural world: only God fully knows, because God made it
    -> Human history: humans can know, because humans made it
    -> Gives history epistemological standing separate from natural science
    Anticipates Dilthey's Verstehen (understanding) vs. Erklarung (explanation)

  THREE AGES (corso):
    Age of Gods: myth, religion, hieroglyphic writing
    Age of Heroes: epic, aristocracy, metaphoric language
    Age of Men: reason, democracy, vernacular prose
    RICORSO: after Age of Men, cycle can begin again (decline -> new barbarism)
    Not pure progress: counter to linear Enlightenment progress theory

  IMAGINATIVE UNDERSTANDING:
    To understand ancient peoples, must imaginatively enter their mental world
    Homer's epics: not authored by one poet but collective expression of heroic age
    Myth: primary form of historical evidence about ancient mentality
    Anticipates hermeneutics (Dilthey, Gadamer) and anthropological approach

  WHY IGNORED INITIALLY:
    Written in Italian; difficult prose
    Counter to dominant rationalist/progressivist assumptions
    Rediscovered by Michelet (translated 1827); influential on Isaiah Berlin
```

## Johann Gottfried Herder (1744-1803)

Herder's Ideas on the Philosophy of the History of Humanity (Ideen, 1784-1791) attacked Enlightenment universalism:

```
HERDER'S HISTORICISM

  CRITIQUE OF UNIVERSAL PROGRESS:
    Enlightenment: history has one destination (European rational civilization)
    Herder: each culture (Volk) has its own Geist (spirit) and internal standard
    There is no universal scale by which cultures can be ranked
    Chinese, African, Pacific Island cultures: not inferior; different expressions

  HISTORICISM:
    Each epoch must be understood on its own terms (Eigenheit, own-ness)
    Evaluate past by its own standards, not by present criteria
    Philosophical foundation of all later historical relativism
    Also: protects against anachronism and ethnocentrism

  LANGUAGE AND CULTURE:
    Language shapes thought; different languages = different ways of thinking
    Each Volk's language expresses its unique experience
    Anticipates Wittgenstein, Sapir-Whorf hypothesis

  TENSION WITH KANT:
    Herder was Kant's student but rejected his universalism
    Isaiah Berlin: Herder as inaugurating the "counter-Enlightenment" tradition
    Specific vs. universal; organic vs. mechanical; tradition vs. reason
```

## The Progress Narrative: Stadial Theory

Enlightenment historiography's master plot -- stadial (stage) theory -- ordered all societies on a single trajectory:

```
SCOTTISH STADIAL THEORY (Adam Smith, Adam Ferguson, John Millar)

  FOUR STAGES OF CIVILIZATION:
    1. Hunting and gathering: "savage" societies; communal, mobile
    2. Pastoralism: "barbarian" societies; property in herds; clan-based
    3. Agriculture: settled, landed property; class differentiation
    4. Commerce: specialized labor; market exchange; political freedom

  CONSEQUENCES:
    Property relations determine social organization (anticipates Marx)
    Each stage has characteristic institutions, laws, moral systems
    European commercial society = current peak; others = earlier stages

  CRITIQUES:
    Assumes single path: denies multiple routes to modernity
    Teleological: privileges European destination as natural end
    Ethnocentric: "savage" and "barbarian" = value-laden labels
    Methodologically flawed: extrapolates from contemporary "primitives"
    to describe ancient history

  LEGACY:
    Marx adopted structure while challenging the destination
    Provided framework for 19th-century colonial ideology
    ("civilizing mission" = bring backward peoples to commercial stage)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Gibbon's argument is not that Rome fell because of Christianity** -- Gibbon's formula names Christianity and barbarism as contributing factors but his main thesis is that the "immoderate greatness" of empire made decline inevitable. The chapters on Christianity analyze early Christianity's sociological structure; Gibbon was more subtle than his reputation for anti-Christian polemics suggests.

**Vico and Herder are not nihilistic relativists** -- Both argue for multiple paths and internal standards, not that all historical claims are equally valid. Vico believed his New Science was scientific. Herder disputed ranking cultures by a single standard, not the existence of universal human capacities.

**Stadial theory is not Marxism** -- Marx adapted and criticized stadialism. Scottish stages are driven by technology and population; Marx's stages are driven by class conflict. Marx also rejected the teleological inevitability of the Scottish commercial endpoint.
