# The Postmodern Challenge: Metahistory, Linguistic Turn, Limits

## The Big Picture

The postmodern challenge to history peaked in the 1970s-1990s. Its core claim: historical narratives are linguistic constructions, not recoveries of a pre-existing past. The historical text does not represent reality transparently; it constitutes a version of reality through the choices of narrative form, emplotment, and rhetoric. Taken to its strongest form, this seemed to make history indistinguishable from fiction. The challenge was real enough to generate a major disciplinary controversy, but the strongest version was rejected by most historians, who found a middle position between naive realism and wholesale relativism.

## Hayden White: Metahistory and Verbal Fiction

Hayden White's Metahistory (1973) is the most influential work of post-war American historical theory, and the most controversial:

```
WHITE'S STRUCTURE OF HISTORICAL CONSCIOUSNESS

  White analyzed 19th-century European historians and philosophers of history
  (Michelet, Ranke, Tocqueville, Burckhardt; Marx, Nietzsche, Croce, Hegel)
  to argue that each exhibits a predictable combination of four structural levels:

  THE FOUR LEVELS (and their options):

  1. MODE OF EMPLOTMENT (narrative form):
     How the historian's story is structured
     ROMANCE: hero struggles and triumphs
     COMEDY: temporary confusions resolved in harmony
     TRAGEDY: hero fails; world unchanged
     SATIRE: no resolution; absurdity of human striving

  2. MODE OF FORMAL ARGUMENT (explanatory strategy):
     How the historian explains what happened
     FORMIST: identify unique individuals and events (idiographic)
     ORGANICIST: integrate parts into a whole (holistic)
     MECHANICIST: cover-law explanation by general causal laws
     CONTEXTUALIST: explain events by their historical context

  3. MODE OF IDEOLOGICAL IMPLICATION:
     The historian's political stance
     ANARCHIST: present institutions are corrupt; utopian alternative
     CONSERVATIVE: organic change only; existing institutions valuable
     RADICAL: present institutions are evil; fundamental transformation needed
     LIBERAL: piecemeal reform; gradual progress

  4. MASTER TROPES (rhetoric):
     METAPHOR: identification of one thing with another (Romantic)
     METONYMY: part for whole; reductionism (Mechanicist)
     SYNECDOCHE: microcosm for macrocosm; integration (Organicist)
     IRONY: negation; no literal meaning adequate (Satiric)

  CONCLUSION:
    These four levels are "elective affinities" -- they tend to cohere:
    e.g., Romance + Formism + Anarchist + Metaphor often go together
    The choice is PRE-SCIENTIFIC: a "poetic" act that precedes evidence
    -> No objective basis for choosing among tropological schemes
    -> Historical representations are "verbal fictions" with no epistemological
       superiority to the literary forms they superficially resemble

  THE STRONG CLAIM:
    "A historical narrative is not only a reproduction of the events reported
    in it, but also a complex of symbols which gives us directions for finding
    an icon of the structure of those events in our literary tradition"
    -> Form is not a neutral vessel; it shapes content
```

## Michel Foucault: Genealogy and Power/Knowledge

Foucault's relation to historiography is oblique -- he was not a professional historian and did not engage closely with disciplinary debates -- but his influence on historical practice was enormous:

```
FOUCAULT'S HISTORICAL CONCEPTS

  L'ARCHEOLOGIE DU SAVOIR (1969) -- The Archaeology of Knowledge:
    Episteme: the unconscious rules that govern what can be said and known
    in a given period; constitutive of possible knowledge
    Not causes or conditions but constraints: what makes certain statements
    possible and others impossible
    Change between epistemes: ruptures, not evolution
    -> History is discontinuous; progress is an illusion imposed retrospectively

  GENEALOGY (Nietzsche's Herkunft / Entstehung concept, adopted by Foucault):
    Not origin (Ursprung: essence, pure beginning) but "descent" (Herkunft)
    and "emergence" (Entstehung)
    Genealogy: trace the body of accidents, power struggles, dominations
    that constitute what appears as a natural or universal phenomenon
    Example: Discipline and Punish (1975) -- not the history of prison
    reform as progress toward humane punishment, but the genealogy
    of how the disciplinary gaze became central to modern institutions

  POWER/KNOWLEDGE:
    "There is no power relation without the correlative constitution of a
    field of knowledge, nor any knowledge that does not presuppose and
    constitute at the same time power relations"
    Knowledge is not neutral with respect to power: it constitutes and
    is constituted by power relations
    The historian's own knowledge claims are implicated in power

  HISTORY OF THE PRESENT:
    Foucault uses history instrumentally: to destabilize the apparent
    naturalness of present institutions
    History shows contingency: things could have been otherwise
    Does not claim objectivity; claims genealogical utility

  RECEPTION IN HISTORY:
    Social historians: used "discourse" but mostly as synonym for "rhetoric"
    (not Foucault's technical sense)
    Gender historians: power/knowledge + body politics very influential
    Medical historians: "medical gaze" framework widely adopted
    Mainstream historians: mostly suspicious; too determinist, too presentist
```

## The Linguistic Turn

```
THE LINGUISTIC TURN IN HISTORICAL STUDIES

  CONCEPT: from Rorty (1967), but applied to history in 1980s-90s
  Language does not represent a pre-existing reality; it constitutes
  the objects it apparently describes
  "Things in themselves" are inaccessible; we only have language games

  APPLICATION TO HISTORY:
    Historical actors do not have interests/experiences that pre-exist language;
    their interests are constituted through available discursive frameworks
    Joan Scott's gender argument: gender as discourse, not natural fact
    Patrick Joyce: class is a discourse, not an objective position

  DEBATE IN ENGLISH HISTORY PROFESSION:
    Joyce (1991): Working-class identity is narratively constructed
    Thompson's tradition: actual material conditions and experiences exist
    independent of how they are narrated
    Lawrence Stone (1992): "History and Post-Modernism" exchange
    in Past and Present: sharp debate

  DERRIDA AND DECONSTRUCTION:
    "There is nothing outside the text" (il n'y a pas de hors-texte)
    -> Contexts are themselves textual constructions
    Trace: meaning is differential; all texts are relations of traces
    Applied to history: archives are texts; the past is only accessible
    through traces that are themselves texts
    Deconstructive historians: read for what texts exclude, suppress,
    contradict themselves on
```

## The Holocaust Challenge to Postmodernism

The Holocaust became the critical test case for the limits of narrative relativism:

```
HOLOCAUST AND HISTORICAL REPRESENTATION

  THE CHALLENGE FOR POSTMODERNISM:
    If historical narratives are verbal fictions with no epistemological priority
    over other narratives, what distinguishes the Holocaust historian from
    the Holocaust denier?
    Both are constructing narratives. Is there a principled basis for preferring one?

  SAUL FRIEDLANDER: "Probing the Limits of Representation" (1992) conference
    Can the Holocaust be represented at all? Are certain narrative forms improper?
    (Comedy? Fiction? Postmodern destabilization?)
    The "final solution" as "limiting case" for historical representation theory

  BEREL LANG: "Act and Idea in the Nazi Genocide" (1990)
    The Holocaust as moral event: description of it carries moral weight
    Postmodern "neutralization" of historical narrative is ethically problematic
    -> Some events are not equally available to all narrative framings

  HAYDEN WHITE'S RESPONSE:
    "Historical Emplotment and the Problem of Truth" (in Friedlander volume, 1992)
    Even for the Holocaust, emplotment is necessary
    The choice of tragic vs. comic vs. satiric emplotment is not arbitrary:
    some are more appropriate than others
    WHITE: conceded that appropriateness is real; but still tropological
    -> Partial concession to critics

  PRACTICAL IMPLICATION:
    Holocaust denial: not just a different narrative; factually wrong
    Courts have found Holocaust denial claims to be factually refuted
    (David Irving vs. Lipstadt libel trial, 2000)
    -> Evidence does constrain narratives; not all narratives are equal
```

## Responses: Practical Realism and the Defense of History

```
THE HISTORIANS' RESPONSE

  APPLEBY, HUNT, JACOB: "Telling the Truth about History" (1994)
    Position: "Practical realism"
    Accept: perspective is unavoidable; objectivity as "view from nowhere" impossible
    Reject: relativism that makes all accounts equally valid
    Argument: Intersubjective standards (peer review, source citation, argumentation)
    allow for progressive improvement in historical knowledge
    Democratic politics requires that some narratives be falsifiable
    (Holocaust denial must be refutable)
    Objectivity as regulative ideal, not achieved state

  RICHARD EVANS: "In Defense of History" (1997)
    More combative response to postmodernist challenge
    Evidence does constrain interpretation: some claims are simply false
    (Irving's claims about Hitler and the Holocaust demonstrably false)
    Narrative form does not determine truth value: same events can be
    emplotted differently; factual accuracy constrains all versions
    Ranke's ideal: not naive; we approach past reality through evidence
    even if never reaching it directly

  KEITH JENKINS: "Re-thinking History" (1991)
    Pro-postmodern position: history is always constructed by historians
    for present purposes; critical theory not optional
    But Jenkins is also clear: historians must be honest about their position

  PRACTICAL SYNTHESIS (current dominant position):
    1. Perspective is unavoidable: acknowledge it
    2. Evidence constrains interpretation: all accounts not equally valid
    3. Standards are communal, not individual: peer review, citation, argument
    4. Progress is real: some things we now know with more certainty than before
    5. Narrative form matters: but so do facts
```

## Common Confusion Points

**White did not say history is the same as fiction** -- White's claim was that historical narratives employ literary forms and that the choice of form is pre-scientific. He was not arguing that historians can make up facts. He was arguing about how facts are organized into coherent narrative wholes. The distinction between chronicle (list of facts) and narrative (causally connected story) was his starting point.

**Foucault was not a relativist** -- Foucault's genealogies make specific historical claims, supported by archival evidence. He argued for the power-constituted nature of knowledge, not that all knowledge claims are equally valid. His method: rigorous archival work plus genealogical framing. He explicitly denied being a relativist in late interviews.

**The linguistic turn did not end social history** -- Social history continued throughout and after the linguistic turn debates, incorporating cultural analysis where useful while maintaining commitment to material conditions and evidence. E.P. Thompson's tradition continues. The cultural turn added tools to the toolkit; it did not replace it.

**Holocaust denial and narrative relativism are different problems** -- White's argument was about the tropological pre-structuring of historical narratives, not about the factual content of specific claims. Holocaust denial is primarily a factual problem (denying documented events) not just a narrative problem. Evans's response to White correctly distinguishes these: narrative form matters, but factual accuracy is a separate (and binding) constraint.
