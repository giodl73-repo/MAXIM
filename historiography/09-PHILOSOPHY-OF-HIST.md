# Philosophy of History: Causation, Explanation, Periodization, Counterfactuals

## The Big Picture

Philosophy of history (as distinct from philosophy of historiography) asks: what is the logical structure of historical explanation? What makes a causal claim about the past valid? Can historical knowledge be objective? What is the status of periodization? Can counterfactuals be justified? These are the metahistorical questions that practitioners usually ignore in favor of doing history, but that become unavoidable when historians challenge each other's claims.

## Causation in History

Historical causation is not the same as physical causation. A physical law says: given conditions C, event E always follows. Historical explanations rarely invoke general laws; they cite specific conditions and mechanisms:

```
CAUSAL STRUCTURE IN HISTORICAL EXPLANATION

  NECESSARY CONDITION:
    "If not C, then not E"
    If the Habsburg Archduke Franz Ferdinand had not been assassinated,
    WWI would not have begun when it did
    -> Assassination: necessary condition for the specific July 1914 crisis

    LIMITATION: There can be many necessary conditions (breathing, no earthquake,
    a world in which states have armies...). Which are historically relevant?

  SUFFICIENT CONDITION:
    "If C, then E"
    History rarely offers sufficient conditions because complex events
    require many converging conditions -- no single condition is sufficient

  INUS CONDITION (Mackie 1965):
    "Insufficient but Necessary part of an Unnecessary but Sufficient condition"
    The assassination was: insufficient alone (not sufficient for war) but
    necessary within a broader sufficient set (alliance system + mobilization
    plans + nationalist tensions + imperial rivalries) that was itself
    sufficient for war but not the only possible sufficient set
    -> Best model for historical causation

  CAUSAL MECHANISM:
    Intermediate steps between cause and effect
    Not just: C -> E but: C -> M1 -> M2 -> ... -> E
    Example: Silver from Americas -> price revolution:
      Mechanism: increased money supply -> demand exceeds supply ->
      price inflation -> wage-price spiral -> social disruption
    "Analytic sociology" (Hedstrom, Ylikoski): middle-range theories
    specify mechanisms; avoid black-box causation

  LEVELS OF CAUSATION:
    Proximate (immediate): the assassination
    Intermediate: mobilization decisions, ultimatums, failures of diplomacy
    Underlying: alliance system, imperial competition, nationalist movements
    Structural: industrialization of warfare, telegraph enabling rapid mobilization
    All levels are real causes at different analytical scales
```

## Narrative Explanation vs. Covering Law

The most important debate in philosophy of history (1940s-1970s): Can history explain by citing general laws, or is narrative sufficient?

```
HEMPEL'S DEDUCTIVE-NOMOLOGICAL (DN) MODEL

  CARL HEMPEL (1942): "The Function of General Laws in History"
  Claims: all genuine explanations have the same logical structure:
    Covering law (L): All events of type C are followed by events of type E
    Initial conditions (IC): Event C1, C2, C3 occurred
    Explanandum: Event E occurred

  For history, laws might be: "whenever there is economic collapse plus
  political instability, revolutionary movements emerge"
  -> Napoleon's rise explained by DN model if appropriate laws exist

  HEMPEL'S CONCESSION: historical explanations are usually "explanation sketches"
  that implicitly invoke laws too imprecise to be stated explicitly

  CRITIQUE (William Dray):
    "Laws and Explanation in History" (1957)
    History does not use covering laws; it uses rational explanation
    To explain Napoleon's decision: reconstruct what Napoleon believed and valued,
    show that his decision was rational given those beliefs and values
    This is NOT subsumption under a law: it is showing that the action
    "made sense" from the agent's perspective

  NARRATIVE EXPLANATION (Gallie, Danto, White):
    Historical narrative is a legitimate explanatory form in itself
    Stories explain by showing how early events led to later ones through
    meaningful connections -- not through law subsumption
    "Narrative is not a description of events; it is an explanation of them"

  CURRENT STATUS:
    Hempel's DN model: rejected for history (no good historical laws)
    Dray's rational explanation: accepted for intentional action but
    doesn't explain structural/non-intentional historical change
    Narrative: accepted as legitimate but raises further questions
    (whose narrative? structured how?)
    Causal mechanism models (Hedstrom): best current framework for
    non-narrative structural explanation
```

## Counterfactual History

Can asking "what if?" questions be methodologically legitimate?

```
COUNTERFACTUAL ARGUMENTS IN HISTORY

  THE CASE FOR:
    Niall Ferguson (ed.): "Virtual History: Alternatives and Counterfactuals" (1997)
    Thesis: counterfactuals are epistemologically necessary for causal claims
    If you say "X caused Y," you are implicitly claiming "without X, no Y"
    -> Every causal claim contains a counterfactual claim
    Therefore: explicitly evaluating counterfactuals is more honest than
    disguising them in causal language

    EXAMPLE:
    Claim: "German invasion of Russia (1941) was the decisive cause of WWII outcome"
    Implicit counterfactual: "Without the invasion, Germany might have won"
    Better to make this explicit and evaluate it rigorously

  TYPES OF COUNTERFACTUALS:
    Close-possible-worlds: alter one contingent event; leave structure fixed
    "If Archduke Franz Ferdinand had taken a different route"
    More plausible: minimal change

    Far-possible-worlds: alter a structural feature
    "If industrialization had never occurred"
    Less tractable: too many interacting changes

  CONSTRAINTS ON LEGITIMATE COUNTERFACTUALS (Ferguson's criteria):
    1. Alter only contingent, not necessary, features of the historical situation
    2. The altered scenario must have been genuinely possible at the time
    3. Must deduce consequences using evidence, not imagination
    4. Avoid: "Hitler won" scenarios that multiply improbabilities

  THE CASE AGAINST (E.H. Carr, many others):
    "The historian does not deal in the might-have-been"
    Counterfactuals are inherently speculative; do not add knowledge
    They reveal historians' assumptions, not historical reality
    Danger: used to argue for or against present political positions

  WORKING RESOLUTION:
    Counterfactuals used as heuristic tools: expose assumptions, test claims
    They do not yield positive knowledge of "what would have happened"
    but they reveal the structure of causal arguments
    Explicit counterfactuals better than implicit ones: Ferguson is right
    that all causal claims contain them
```

## Periodization as Interpretation

```
THE PROBLEM OF PERIODIZATION

  Every historical period label is an interpretive act, not a natural division:
    "Middle Ages" (Enlightenment coinage: barbarous interval between
    classical antiquity and modern revival -- value-laden)
    "Renaissance" (19th-century invention: Burckhardt, Michelet)
    "Industrial Revolution" (was it gradual or revolutionary?)
    "Cold War" (when did it start? End with Berlin Wall or Soviet collapse?)

  WHO DECIDES:
    "Long 19th century" (Hobsbawm): 1789-1914 -- makes analytical sense
    "Short 20th century" (Hobsbawm): 1914-1991 -- WWI to Soviet collapse
    These are analytical constructs that serve a thesis

  FEMINIST PERIODIZATION:
    Standard periodization (Renaissance, Reformation, Enlightenment)
    based on changes in elite male intellectual/political life
    "Did women have a Renaissance?" (Joan Kelly 1977): article asks whether
    standard periodization applies when gender is the analytical lens
    Answer: women's status actually declined in the "Renaissance" period
    -> Standard periods may not be valid across different analytical categories

  GLOBAL PERIODIZATION:
    "Early modern": meaningful for Europe (1500-1800)
    Does "early modern" have the same meaning for China, India, West Africa?
    Chinese historians: different internal periodization (dynasties)
    -> Eurocentrism embedded in standard world history periodization

  PRACTICAL APPROACH:
    Acknowledge periods are interpretive constructs serving analytical purposes
    Be explicit about what makes a period: political change? Economic? Cultural?
    Different questions require different periodizations
    The period is a tool, not a container
```

## Historical Objectivity Revisited

```
THE OBJECTIVITY PROBLEM: COMPETING POSITIONS

  RANKEAN (naive realism):
    Documents give access to "how it actually was"
    Historian becomes transparent in the face of evidence
    CRITIQUE: Selection, interpretation, narrative all involve value choices

  RELATIVISM (Beard, Becker, postmodern):
    All history is relative to the historian's standpoint, period, culture
    No privileged vantage point for "the truth"
    CRITIQUE: Cannot distinguish Holocaust history from Holocaust denial
    Cannot explain why some historical claims are better justified

  THOMAS NAGEL'S "VIEW FROM NOWHERE":
    Objectivity requires a standpoint outside all particular perspectives
    For humans: impossible
    -> Perfect historical objectivity is therefore impossible

  SITUATED KNOWLEDGE (Sandra Harding, Donna Haraway applied to history):
    All knowledge is situated; there is no view from nowhere
    BUT: some positions are more revealing than others (marginalized
    standpoints may reveal what dominant standpoints normalize and hide)
    -> Partial but not therefore invalid

  PRACTICAL REALISM (current dominant position):
    1. Acknowledge standpoint: historian's perspective shapes questions, emphasis
    2. Evidence constrains: not all claims equally well-supported
    3. Intersubjective standards: peer review, source citation, argument
    4. Progress is real: historiography improves over time
    5. Objectivity as aspiration: "not the achieved result but the regulative ideal"
       (Megill "Historical Knowledge, Historical Error" 2007)

  THE BEST ACHIEVABLE:
    "Objectivity in history means providing the fullest possible
    account while acknowledging the limitations of perspective and evidence"
    Not: perfect correspondence to "what happened"
    But: best-justified inference from available evidence, communicated
    with acknowledgment of its limits
```

## Metanarratives and Their Crisis

```
LYOTARD AND GRAND NARRATIVES

  JEAN-FRANCOIS LYOTARD: "La Condition Postmoderne" (1979)
  "Postmodern condition" = incredulity toward metanarratives
  Metanarratives: grand unifying stories that legitimate knowledge:
    Enlightenment metanarrative: knowledge leads to human emancipation
    Marxist metanarrative: history is class struggle toward communism
    Christian metanarrative: salvation history from Creation to Last Judgment
    Progress metanarrative: human history as continuous improvement

  LYOTARD'S CLAIM:
    These metanarratives have lost credibility (after WWII, Gulag, Holocaust)
    The grounds for choosing among them are not available
    We are left with many small narratives (petits recits) with no
    metanarrative to legitimate or unify them

  IMPLICATION FOR HISTORY:
    If no metanarrative legitimates history, on what basis do we write it?
    Who decides which small narratives matter?
    The end of metanarratives = fragmentation without synthesis

  RESPONSES:
    Habermas: Enlightenment metanarrative is an unfinished project; critique
    of its failures does not require abandoning it
    Practical historians: continue to write analytical history without
    needing a metanarrative legitimation
    Post-colonial: the problem was always whose metanarrative was legitimated;
    this was not universal but particular

  THE PRAGMATIST RESOLUTION:
    Histories serve particular audiences and purposes
    They do not require ultimate legitimation from a metanarrative
    Multiple histories are possible; the test is whether they are
    well-supported by evidence and useful for the purposes they serve
    This is not relativism: evidence still constrains; some claims are false
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Standard Answer | Complication |
|----------|----------------|--------------|
| What caused X? | Cite necessary + sufficient conditions; specify mechanism | Only INUS conditions achievable; all causes are at multiple levels |
| Can covering laws explain history? | No (Hempel critique): only explanation sketches | Mechanisms replace laws; rational explanation for intentional acts |
| Are counterfactuals legitimate? | As heuristics only; expose causal assumptions | Cannot yield positive knowledge of alternative outcomes |
| Is periodization objective? | No: all periods are interpretive constructs | But some serve analytical purposes better than others |
| Can history be objective? | Not in Nagel's sense; yes in intersubjective sense | Progress is real; evidence constrains; not all accounts equal |
| Do metanarratives matter? | They are unavoidable but must be held lightly | Multiple metanarratives; test against evidence |

## Common Confusion Points

**Causation in history is not the same as causation in physics** -- Physics seeks laws that hold universally. History seeks specific causal mechanisms in particular configurations. The same causal factor (economic collapse) may produce revolution in one context and not in another because other conditions differ. INUS causation models this correctly; simple sufficient causation does not.

**Narrative explanation is not mere story** -- When Dray argued that rational explanation is legitimate in history, he was making a philosophical argument about the logical structure of intentional action explanation -- not advocating for anecdote over analysis. A rigorous narrative explanation specifies what the agent believed and valued, the options available, and why the chosen action was rational given those inputs.

**Counterfactual history is not alternative history fiction** -- Harry Turtledove's novels are entertainment. Robert Fogel's counterfactual economics (measuring the contribution of railroads to US growth by asking what growth would have been without them) is a recognized econometric technique. The difference is: Fogel used systematic evidence to constrain what "without railroads" would have meant; Turtledove imagines freely.

**"Postmodern" in historiography is not simply "anything goes"** -- The postmodern challenge raised legitimate methodological questions about narrative form, perspectivalism, and the constructed nature of historical categories. Most historians accepted the moderate version (perspective is unavoidable; narrative form matters) while rejecting the strong version (history = fiction = no standards). The working majority position is not postmodern relativism; it is practical realism with enhanced self-awareness about its own conditions of production.
