# Global and World History: Connected Histories, Provincializing Europe

## The Big Picture

Global history is the attempt to write history at planetary scale, escaping both the national(ist) container that dominates most academic history and the Eurocentric teleology that has dominated "universal" history since the Enlightenment. It emerged as a serious academic program in the 1990s, partly in response to globalization, partly as continuation of postcolonial critique. Its internal debates are about methodology (comparative vs. connected), about scale (world-system vs. microhistory), and about whether any genuinely non-Eurocentric history is currently possible.

## World Systems Theory (Wallerstein)

Immanuel Wallerstein's The Modern World-System (4 volumes, 1974-2011) is the most systematic attempt to theorize global economic history as a single structure:

```
WALLERSTEIN'S FRAMEWORK

  CORE CONCEPTS:
    The modern world-system originated in Europe in the "long 16th century"
    (1450-1640) as a capitalist world-economy
    Single economic system with political multiplicity (many states)
    structured in three zones:

    CORE: western Europe, then US/Japan
      Capital-intensive, high-wage, high-skill production
      Appropriates surplus value from periphery
      Protected by powerful state institutions

    SEMI-PERIPHERY: currently eastern Europe, Latin America, China
      Mix of core and periphery activities
      Political role: prevents direct core-periphery conflict
      (provides ladder between poles; occupies middle)

    PERIPHERY: Sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, etc.
      Labor-intensive, low-wage, resource extraction
      Weak states; unable to protect against unfavorable terms of trade

  HISTORICAL DYNAMICS:
    World-systems expand geographically over time (16th-19th century)
    Peripheral regions incorporated involuntarily through conquest/trade
    Hegemony: cycles of hegemonic power (Netherlands, Britain, US)
    Kondratieff waves: ~50-year economic cycles embedded in the system

  CRITIQUES:
    AGENCY: where is it? People only act out structural roles
    HISTORICAL VARIATION: non-Western economies had own internal dynamics
      before being incorporated -- not passive recipients
    EUROPE'S UNIQUENESS: system begins in Europe; this is assumed, not explained
    QUANTITATIVE DATA: many empirical claims contested
    OVERREACH: tries to explain too much with too simple a model
```

## Connected Histories: Sanjay Subrahmanyam

```
CONNECTED HISTORIES

  SANJAY SUBRAHMANYAM: "Connected Histories: Notes towards a Reconfiguration
  of Early Modern Eurasia" (Comparative Studies in Society and History, 1997)

  AGAINST DIFFUSIONISM:
    Traditional model: innovations originated in one place (usually Europe)
    and diffused outward
    "European miracle" literature: Europe's unique rise to modernity

  SUBRAHMANYAM'S ALTERNATIVE:
    Early modern period (16th-18th century): Eurasia was connected
    Multiple empires in the same period: Ming China, Mughal India, Ottoman,
    Safavid Persia, Habsburg Spain, Portuguese Estado da India
    They were not isolated developments; they interacted, influenced each other,
    responded to common pressures (early modern "conjuncture")

    "Messianism" across Eurasia in 1500-1600: millenarian expectations in
    different religious traditions simultaneously (Christian, Islamic, Hindu)
    Not coincidence: connected through trade and political interaction

  METHOD:
    "Connected history" (histoire connectee): trace actual connections
    (trade routes, diplomatic exchange, migration, shared intellectual
    sources) across cultural boundaries
    Not comparison of isolates (Weber's method) but study of actual links

  KEY CONTRIBUTION:
    Non-Western empires and peoples were not passive; they had their own
    complex dynamics and also shaped the European encounter
    The "rise of Europe" is a global phenomenon, not an internal European story
```

## Provincializing Europe: Chakrabarty

Dipesh Chakrabarty's Provincializing Europe: Postcolonial Thought and Historical Difference (2000) is the most philosophically sophisticated account of the problem with European universalism in historical categories:

```
CHAKRABARTY'S ARGUMENT

  THE PROBLEM:
    European social science categories (capitalism, class, democracy,
    secularism, civil society) are both:
    1. Indispensable: non-Western historians cannot avoid using them
    2. Inadequate: they were developed from and for European history
       and do not fit non-European experience without distortion

  "EUROPE" AS HYPERREAL:
    Not claiming Europe was unimportant; it clearly shaped global modernity
    But: historical categories derived from European experience have been
    universalized and presented as the standard against which all other
    histories are measured as deficient or incomplete
    -> Indian history = European history minus the good parts
    -> History of India always explained by what India lacked (feudalism
       not developing into capitalism; democracy not emerging from below, etc.)

  "HISTORY 1" AND "HISTORY 2":
    Following Marx: every capitalist process contains two histories:
    History 1: Abstract capital's universal logic (commodification, wage labor)
    History 2: Histories that are antecedent to capital and do not belong
               to capital's life process but which capital subordinates
    History 2 represents: non-European lifeworlds, kinship, community, religion
    Mainstream history ignores History 2; Chakrabarty insists on it

  NOT ANTI-UNIVERSALISM:
    Chakrabarty is not arguing that non-Western societies have nothing in
    common with Western experiences or that universal values are fake
    He is arguing: the particular European path to modernity has been
    mistaken for the universal path
    "Provincializing" = recognizing Europe as one region among others,
    not the model for all historical development

  INFLUENCE:
    Subaltern Studies connection: postcolonial theory in historical method
    Global history: all historians now must address how their categories
    were developed and for what tradition
    Criticism: what are the alternatives? Chakrabarty more diagnostic than prescriptive
```

## C.A. Bayly and Birth of the Modern World

```
C.A. BAYLY: "The Birth of the Modern World, 1780-1914" (2004)

  APPROACH: Global comparative/connected history
    Reject: nationalist and Eurocentric narratives
    Accept: genuine global history requires equal treatment of non-Western cases

  KEY ARGUMENTS:

  1. CONNECTED MODERNITIES:
     Modernity was not diffused from Europe outward; it emerged from
     global interactions and parallel developments across connected regions
     Indian reformers, Chinese literati, Islamic modernists, American
     progressives all facing similar challenges simultaneously

  2. ARCHAIC GLOBALIZATION:
     Pre-1800 globalization (archaic): driven by trade, religion, empire
     18th-century commercial expansion: connected Asian, African, American
     markets before the industrial revolution

  3. NATIONALISM AS GLOBAL:
     Nationalism emerged simultaneously in many regions; not a European export
     It took different forms in different places; but the timing is connected
     (similar structural pressures: colonial state formation, print capitalism)

  4. METHODOLOGICAL:
     No single model predicts global modernity
     But: global context essential for explaining ANY national or regional history
     "The history of the world is not the sum of the histories of nations"
```

## Decolonizing History: Curriculum and Practice

```
DECOLONIZING THE CURRICULUM

  CONTEXT (2010s-2020s):
    Rhodes Must Fall (Cape Town, 2015): student movement demanding removal
    of Cecil Rhodes statue + curriculum changes
    Extended to Oxford (Oriel College Rhodes), Cambridge, US campuses
    1619 Project (New York Times, 2019): placed slavery at center of American
    history rather than as aberration from founding ideals

  WHAT "DECOLONIZING HISTORY" MEANS (contested):
    1. DIVERSIFYING SOURCES: read more non-Western, non-elite sources
    2. DECENTERING EUROPE: study non-Western history as primary, not supplement
    3. QUESTIONING CATEGORIES: which analytical frameworks carry colonial assumptions?
    4. INSTITUTIONAL CRITIQUE: who gets hired, what gets funded, what counts as
       significant history?
    5. EPISTEMIC: challenge what counts as historical knowledge

  CRITICISMS:
    "Decolonizing" as metaphor: conflates curriculum change with material
    decolonization (land return, sovereignty) -- Tuck and Yang 2012
    Some versions: present-presentism; impose current values on past
    Without theoretical rigor: becomes just "add more diverse content"

  COUNTERARGUMENTS:
    Genuine historical distortions have resulted from colonial frameworks:
    African history as pre-historical; non-Western political thought as
    not theoretical; indigenous knowledge as folklore not science
    These are not matters of perspective but of demonstrable error

  1619 PROJECT CONTROVERSY:
    Nikole Hannah-Jones: American history reframed around 1619 arrival of
    enslaved Africans, not 1776 Declaration of Independence
    Critics: "The 1776 Project" (Trump) vs. historical critique
    Historians (Gordon Wood, Sean Wilentz): specific factual corrections
    requested on some claims; broader reframing accepted as legitimate
```

## Big History (David Christian)

```
BIG HISTORY

  DAVID CHRISTIAN: "Maps of Time" (2004); founder of Big History Project
  Time frame: 13.8 billion years (from Big Bang to present)
  Integrates: cosmology, physics, chemistry, biology, human history

  METHOD:
    "Thresholds of increasing complexity": Big Bang, stars, galaxies,
    Earth, life, human consciousness, civilization
    Uses energy flows and information content as universal measures
    Interdisciplinary: no single methodology

  PEDAGOGICAL IMPACT:
    Big History Project (with Bill Gates): curriculum in 1,200+ high schools
    Teaches: common framework across science and humanities
    Appeal: overcomes disciplinary fragmentation

  CRITIQUE:
    Human history becomes episode in cosmic story: risks losing specificity
    "Energy flows" as universal measure: reductive
    Enormous scope: everything is overview; nothing is analyzed
    Historians skeptical: scientific framework risks erasing historical
    contingency and human agency entirely
    Philosophy: teleological dimension (toward greater complexity)?
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Approach | Scale | Key Question | Core Method | Limitation |
|----------|-------|--------------|-------------|------------|
| World systems (Wallerstein) | Global 16th c. to present | How does capitalist world-economy structure history? | Structural analysis | No agency; over-determined |
| Connected histories | Regional/global | What actual connections shaped parallel developments? | Trace links (trade, ideas, people) | Scale: hard to maintain at large scale |
| Provincializing Europe | Conceptual | How do European categories distort non-Western history? | Conceptual critique | More diagnostic than prescriptive |
| Comparative history | Cross-national | What explains variation across similar cases? | Control variables | Risks treating cases as isolates |
| Atlantic history | Regional | How did Atlantic basin form as connected unit? | Regional integration | Ignores internal Asia, Pacific |
| Big History | Cosmic | What is humanity's place in cosmic time? | Interdisciplinary synthesis | Loses historical specificity |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Global history" is not "all history put together"** -- Adding a chapter on China to a European history textbook is not global history. Global history requires analytical frameworks that treat the globe as a unit with meaningful connections, circulations, and comparative patterns. The method changes, not just the subject matter.

**World systems theory ≠ dependency theory** -- Dependency theory (Prebisch, Frank) focused on periphery underdevelopment as result of core extraction. World systems theory (Wallerstein) is more structural: the three-zone structure is a system property, not just an extraction relationship. The analytical unit is the whole system, not the periphery's disadvantage.

**Provincializing Europe does not mean abandoning universal categories** -- Chakrabarty explicitly does not advocate for complete particularism or abandoning Enlightenment values. He is problematizing the particular-as-universal move: when European experience is taken as the norm against which all other histories are measured. The goal is not to replace European universalism with multiple particularisms but to develop genuinely universal frameworks from multiple starting points.

**The 1619 Project is not primarily a work of historiography** -- It is a journalistic and public history intervention, aimed at changing popular understanding of American history. Professional historians engaged with it on different terms from how they would engage a monograph. The debate about specific factual claims is different from the debate about the project's broader reframing purpose, which is legitimate historiographical territory.
