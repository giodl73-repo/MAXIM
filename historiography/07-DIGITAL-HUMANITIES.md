# Digital Humanities: Text Mining, Network Analysis, GIS in History

## The Big Picture

Digital humanities (DH) applies computational methods to humanistic research. For historians, this means three main capabilities: reading large corpora that no single person could read (text mining); analyzing relational structures across thousands of documents (network analysis); and situating historical data in geographic space (GIS). The fundamental methodological tension is between distant reading (patterns in large datasets) and close reading (detailed interpretation of specific texts) -- a tension that maps onto the longstanding historiographical debate between structural and interpretive approaches.

## Text Mining and Computational Reading

### The N-Gram Viewer and Distant Reading

Google Books Ngram Viewer (Michel et al., Science, 2011): popularized "culturomics" -- the study of cultural trends through word frequency in digitized books:

```
NGRAM VIEWER BASICS

  Data: ~5 million books digitized (2012 dataset), covering 1500-2008
  Method: count n-grams (1 to 5 consecutive words) in each year
  Output: frequency curve showing how often a term appeared over time

  USES:
    Track rise/fall of concepts: "democracy," "slavery," "gender"
    Identify periodization: when does a term enter common use?
    Compare corpora: English vs. French vs. German patterns

  LIMITATIONS:
    OCR errors: especially pre-1800 typography (long s = f problem)
    Sampling bias: which books were digitized? Library holdings ≠ all texts
    Genre mix changes over time: scientific literature grew; poetry shrank
    Raw frequency ≠ significance: a term appearing more often may just
    reflect publication of one prolific author

  FRANCO MORETTI'S "DISTANT READING" (2000, "Conjectures on World Literature"):
    Literary history currently studies 0.1% of published literature (the canon)
    To study "world literature" need to study books you haven't read
    Statistical patterns across thousands of texts reveal structural features
    invisible in close reading
    Genres rise and fall; formal features spread and decline
    "Graphs, Maps, Trees" (2005): three models for literary history
```

### Topic Modeling (LDA)

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (Blei, Ng, Jordan 2003): a generative probabilistic model for discovering thematic structure in large text corpora:

```
LDA TOPIC MODELING

  INPUT: Corpus of documents (articles, letters, newspapers)
  OUTPUT: K topics (each topic = probability distribution over vocabulary)
          Each document = mixture of K topics with weights

  INTUITION:
    Assume: documents were generated by choosing a mix of topics,
    then choosing words from those topic distributions
    Goal: work backwards from observed words to infer topic distributions

  EXAMPLE (historical newspaper corpus):
    Topic 7: {cotton: 0.08, crop: 0.06, harvest: 0.05, acre: 0.04...}
    Topic 12: {cavalry: 0.09, regiment: 0.07, battle: 0.06, general: 0.05...}
    Article = 70% Topic 7, 20% Topic 12, 10% other topics
    -> Article about cotton agriculture with some military context

  HISTORICAL APPLICATIONS:
    Cameron Blevins: "Space, Nation, and the Triumph of Region" (2015)
      Analyzed 100,000 newspaper articles from 19th century
      Topic modeling revealed geographic structure of news coverage
    Robert Nelson: "Mining the Dispatch" -- Richmond Civil War newspapers
      LDA revealed how news topics shifted through the war years
    Cameron Blevins, Lincoln Mullen: many iterations

  PROBLEMS:
    Number of topics K is a hyperparameter: no "correct" choice
    Topics need human interpretation: algorithm gives word lists, not meaning
    Coherence metrics help but don't solve interpretation problem
    Stop words, preprocessing choices significantly affect results
    Optimal for English; worse for languages with rich morphology
```

### Word Embeddings and Semantic Change

word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) and its descendants map words to vector spaces where geometric proximity encodes semantic similarity:

```
WORD EMBEDDINGS FOR HISTORICAL SEMANTICS

  CONCEPT:
    Train neural network on large corpus: predict word from context
    Words appearing in similar contexts -> similar vectors
    Vector arithmetic: king - man + woman ~ queen

  HISTORICAL APPLICATION:
    Train separate word2vec models on corpora from different time periods
    Track how a word's semantic neighborhood changes over time
    Example: "computer" 1950 -> central to vacuum tube, calculation, mathematician
              "computer" 1980 -> central to IBM, software, memory, program
              "computer" 2000 -> central to internet, mouse, monitor, keyboard

  TOOLS:
    histwords (Hamilton et al., 2016): pre-trained historical English embeddings
    (Google Books 1800-1990, 10-year intervals)
    BERT: contextual embeddings; better for disambiguating polysemy

  LIMITATIONS:
    Large data requirement: need millions of words per period
    Training instability: different runs produce different embeddings
    Alignment problem: vectors from different periods not directly comparable
      without careful alignment (orthogonal Procrustes or similar)
    Meaning change vs. distribution shift: hard to distinguish
```

## Network Analysis in History

Historical network analysis (HNA) applies social network analysis tools to historical datasets, primarily correspondence corpora and relational records:

### Republic of Letters

```
REPUBLIC OF LETTERS PROJECTS

  Early Modern Republic of Letters: 1500-1800
  Extensive correspondence networks among scholars, natural philosophers,
  theologians across Europe (and globally)
  Physical letters survive in archives; mostly digitized

  PROJECTS:
    EMLO (Early Modern Letters Online, Oxford): metadata for 125,000+ letters
    Six Degrees of Francis Bacon: collaborative network visualization (CMU)
    Mapping the Republic of Letters (Stanford): visualization tool
    Cultures of Knowledge (Oxford): EMLO database + analysis

  NETWORK METRICS APPLIED:
    Degree centrality: who received/sent most letters?
    Betweenness centrality: who connected otherwise disconnected communities?
      (Brokers: Mersenne in Paris; Oldenburg at Royal Society)
    Clustering coefficient: how tightly connected is each sub-community?
    Community detection: Louvain algorithm identifies sub-networks

  FINDINGS:
    Some expected: Erasmus, Newton, Mersenne highly central
    Some surprising: female correspondents (often ignored in narrative)
      appear as significant nodes in some subnetworks
    Geographic structure: Leiden and London most central (printing + trade)
    Temporal: network density increased through 17th century

  LIMITATIONS:
    Survivorship bias: wealthy, institutional correspondence survives better
    Letter content not usually analyzed (just metadata: who to whom, when)
    Network centrality does not equal historical significance
```

## GIS in History

Historical GIS (HGIS) combines geographic information systems with historical data:

```
HISTORICAL GIS APPLICATIONS

  BASIC APPROACH:
    Create historical map layers in GIS software
    Overlay modern and historical geographic data
    Query: which events occurred within X km of Y feature?
    Spatial analysis: clustering, distance, proximity, flow

  PROJECT EXAMPLES:

    CHINA HISTORICAL GIS (CHGIS):
      Harvard/Fudan: digitized boundaries of Chinese administrative units
      from 221 BCE to 1911 CE (2,200 years)
      Query: which county did person X live in during dynasty Y?

    HESTIA (UK): Herodotus geospatial
      Mapped all place names in the Histories
      Revealed: spatial structure of Herodotus's narrative
      Eastern Mediterranean core; declining detail toward periphery

    ENVIRONMENTAL HISTORY MAPPING:
      Map historical deforestation, agricultural expansion
      Overlay census data with geographic features
      Environmental constraints on historical settlement patterns

    CENSUS AND DEMOGRAPHIC GIS:
      NHGIS (National Historical GIS): US census data 1790-present
      Map demographic change: migration, urbanization, ethnic distribution
      Boundary files for historical census units

  TOOLS:
    QGIS (open source): standard for academic historical GIS
    ArcGIS (Esri commercial): industry standard
    Leaflet / Mapbox: web-based interactive historical maps
    StoryMapJS: narrative + map integration (Knight Lab)

  CHALLENGES:
    Boundary change: county X in 1850 is different from county X in 1900
    Coordinate systems: historical maps often lack precise coordinates
    Geocoding historical addresses: imprecise, time-consuming
    Historical basemaps: rasterizing old maps introduces errors
```

## Digitization Infrastructure

```
MAJOR DIGITIZATION PROJECTS

  BOOKS AND MANUSCRIPTS:
    HathiTrust Digital Library: 17 million+ volumes (university libraries)
    Google Books: 40 million+ volumes (mostly metadata searchable; OCR varies)
    Gallica (BnF): French national library; emphasis on French history
    British Library Digitised Manuscripts
    Internet Archive: text.archive.org; many out-of-copyright books

  NEWSPAPERS:
    Chronicling America (Library of Congress): US 1770-1963
    British Newspaper Archive: 66+ million pages UK papers
    Europeana Newspapers: multiple European countries

  HISTORICAL DATABASES:
    ESTC (English Short Title Catalogue): all English-language printing 1473-1800
    EMLO (Early Modern Letters Online): correspondence metadata
    HHARP (Hutchins's History of the Americas Repository Program)
    Old Bailey Online: all London criminal trials 1674-1913 (190,000+ proceedings)

  OCR QUALITY ISSUES:
    18th century and earlier: long s (looks like f), varied spelling -> errors
    Gothic / Fraktur typography (German historical documents): worse accuracy
    Standard OCR: ~99% character accuracy on good quality modern print
    Historical documents: 90-95% common; means 1 error every 10-20 words
    Post-correction tools: ABBYY, Tesseract, deep learning models
    "OCR quality determines what is findable"
```

## The Distant vs. Close Reading Debate

```
METHODOLOGICAL TENSIONS

  MORETTI'S POSITION:
    "One cannot seriously propose that literary history should be based on,
    say, 200 texts, when the actual archive includes perhaps 30,000"
    Statistical patterns require full corpora
    Close reading: epistemologically legitimate but limited in scope

  CRITICS:
    N. Katherine Hayles: "How We Read" (2012)
    Reading is not uniform: close, hyper, machine reading are different modes
    each appropriate for different questions
    Machine reading cannot substitute for interpretive engagement with text

    Ted Underwood: acknowledges limits; "distant reading answers different
    questions than close reading; neither subsumes the other"

  SAMPLING VS. FULL CORPUS:
    Reading 100 randomly sampled texts from a corpus of 30,000 may give
    better insight than topic modeling the whole corpus
    because random sample allows close engagement with each text
    Full corpus methods: miss meaning that requires close reading to recover

  ALGORITHMIC HERMENEUTICS:
    The algorithm does not "read" in any meaningful sense
    LDA produces word distributions; interpretation is entirely the historian's
    The computational step does not remove interpretive responsibility
    It shifts it: from reading texts to choosing methods and interpreting output
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question Type | Method | Tool | Caution |
|---------------|--------|------|---------|
| How did word meaning change over time? | Word embeddings | histwords, word2vec | Alignment required across periods |
| What topics appear in a large corpus? | LDA topic modeling | MALLET, Python gensim | K selection; human interpretation required |
| Who was most connected in a network? | Network centrality | Gephi, R igraph | Survivorship bias in sources |
| How did X distribute geographically? | Historical GIS | QGIS | Boundary changes over time |
| What patterns appear across many texts? | Statistical analysis, N-grams | Google Ngrams, custom scripts | Sampling bias; OCR errors |
| What specific text means | Close reading | Human attention | Scale limitation |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Digital humanities" does not mean digitizing sources** -- Digitizing a collection of letters (scanning, OCR, metadata) is digital archive infrastructure. Digital humanities analysis means computational methods applied to that digitized material: network analysis, topic modeling, spatial analysis. The terms are related but not synonymous.

**Topic models need human interpretation** -- LDA does not "find topics" in the sense of discovering pre-existing thematic categories. It finds word co-occurrence patterns and assigns them to K latent variables. Those variables become "topics" only when a human looks at the top words and says "this looks like it's about military affairs." The algorithm provides a lens; the historian provides the interpretation.

**High OCR accuracy does not mean findability** -- 99% character accuracy on a 1,000-word document means ~10 errors. Keyword searches fail if the keyword is one of those 10 errors. Pre-modern orthographic variation (thee/the, hath/has) and spelling inconsistency compound this. Full-text search on historical corpora: treat results as samples, not exhaustive finds.

**Network centrality is not historical significance** -- The most betweenness-central node in a correspondence network is the person whose removal would most fragment the network. This may or may not correlate with historical importance as conventionally understood. Henry Oldenburg (Royal Society secretary) is extremely central in early modern science networks -- and is indeed important historically. But many central network nodes are obscure individuals whose historical significance is precisely in their connective role, not any intellectual contribution.
