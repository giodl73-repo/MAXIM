# Social History and Cultural Turn: Class, Microhistory, Gender

## The Big Picture

Two overlapping transformations reshaped history from the 1960s through the 1990s. New Social History (1960s-70s) expanded the subject matter to ordinary people, quantified social patterns, and brought Marxist and sociological frameworks to bear. The Cultural Turn (1980s-90s) then shifted attention from social structures to cultural meanings, symbols, representations, and identity -- drawing on anthropology, literary theory, and semiotics. The result was an enormous expansion of what historians study and how.

```
THE EXPANSIONS

  TRADITIONAL HISTORY (pre-1960):       SOCIAL + CULTURAL HISTORY:
  Political events                  ->  Social structures and everyday life
  Great men                         ->  Women, workers, enslaved, colonized
  Western Europe / North America    ->  Subaltern / global
  Narrative of politics             ->  Quantification / interpretation
  Documents of state                ->  All traces (material, oral, visual)
  One explanatory framework         ->  Multiple: class, gender, race, culture

  KEY CONCEPT PROGRESSION:
  Class (Marxist framework) -> Experience (Thompson's synthesis) ->
  Culture (Geertz/Darnton) -> Gender (Scott) -> Subalternity (Spivak)
```

## E.P. Thompson and the Making of the English Working Class

E.P. Thompson's The Making of the English Working Class (1963) is perhaps the most influential single work of 20th-century social history:

```
THOMPSON'S ARGUMENT AND METHOD

  THESIS:
    "The working class made itself as much as it was made"
    Class is not a structure (a position in relations of production)
    but a process -- a "happening" that occurs when people, sharing experiences,
    identify their interests as conflicting with others
    -> Agency: class is not imposed but developed through experience and struggle

    Famous opening:
    "I am seeking to rescue the poor stockinger, the Luddite cropper, the
    'obsolete' hand-loom weaver, the 'utopian' artisan, and even the deluded
    follower of Joanna Southcott, from the enormous condescension of posterity"

  METHODOLOGY:
    History from below: not elites but craftsmen, weavers, miners, radical clubs
    Sources: pamphlets, radical newspapers, trade union records, Methodist registers,
             Home Office spy reports, parliamentary testimony
    Reading against the grain: government sources used to recover the
    lives government agents were surveilling

  MORAL ECONOMY:
    Thompson's earlier essay (1971): "The Moral Economy of the English Crowd"
    18th-century food riots: not irrational hunger response but principled
    appeal to traditional norms of fair price and community obligation
    -> Popular protest has its own rationality, its own ethical framework
    This concept became hugely influential in agrarian history worldwide

  CLASS EXPERIENCE:
    Workers don't start with a class identity and then experience the world
    Workers experience industrial capitalism and through that experience
    develop class consciousness
    -> Experience is the mediating concept between structure and agency

  HISTORIOGRAPHICAL POSITION:
    Thompson was a Marxist but attacked Althusserian "structuralism" sharply
    "The Poverty of Theory" (1978): polemic against theory without evidence
    History must be empirically grounded; structures must be qualified by
    the messiness of historical experience
```

## Microhistory: The Cheese and the Worms

Microhistory (Italian: microstoria) emerged in the 1970s, partly from within the Italian Annales tradition and partly as a reaction against its structural scale:

```
MICROHISTORY METHOD AND ARGUMENTS

  CARLO GINZBURG: "The Cheese and the Worms" (1976)
    Subject: Menocchio, a 16th-century Italian miller tried by the Inquisition twice
    for heterodox cosmological views (the world was made like cheese, angels
    like worms from fermented matter)
    Sources: Inquisition trial records (extraordinarily detailed)

  SCALE AS METHOD:
    Why one person?
    1. Individual as window: Menocchio's beliefs cannot be reduced to
       official culture OR to a pure "popular culture" -- he is mixing,
       synthesizing, and transforming
    2. Exceptional normal: the exceptional case that slips through normal
       social controls reveals the structure of those controls
    3. "Normal exceptions": marginality reveals rules
    GINZBURG: "clues" model (like Sherlock Holmes; Morelli's art attribution;
    Freud's slips): minor, often overlooked details as diagnostic indicators
    of deeper structures

  GIOVANNI LEVI: Thesis
    "Inheriting Power" (1988): notary's family in Piedmont
    Even in stable social situations: people exercise small forms of agency,
    negotiate within structures, use marginal room
    Microhistory: reveals the indeterminacy that structural history masks

  SCALE EFFECTS:
    What is visible at micro scale? Individual variation, negotiation, uncertainty
    What is invisible? Aggregate patterns, structural forces (longue duree)
    Neither scale is more "true": they reveal different things
    Ideal: oscillate between scales (Ginzburg's "zoom")
```

## The Cultural Turn: Geertz, Darnton, Chartier

The "cultural turn" brought anthropological methods -- especially Clifford Geertz's thick description -- into history:

```
CLIFFORD GEERTZ AND THICK DESCRIPTION

  From "The Interpretation of Cultures" (1973):
  "Thick description": ethnographic practice of not just describing behavior
  (thin) but the "stratified hierarchy of meaningful structures in terms of
  which [people] produce, perceive, and interpret" behavior

  EXAMPLE (Geertz): The wink vs. the twitch
    Thin description: "man rapidly contracted his right eyelid"
    Thick description: context, intent, convention, role in a social performance
    The same physical movement can mean: involuntary twitch / conspiratorial
    signal / parody of another's wink / rehearsal for the above
    Only thick description captures the meaning

  APPLICATION TO HISTORY:
    Treat historical events as texts to be interpreted
    Not what happened, but what it meant to those who experienced it
    How did they make sense of their world?

ROBERT DARNTON: "The Great Cat Massacre" (1984)

  Seven chapters analyzing moments in pre-revolutionary France
  "The Great Cat Massacre": journeyman printers' ritual killing of cats
  why was this funny? Why did contemporary accounts describe it as hilarious?
  -> Understanding the joke unlocks the symbolic world of artisan labor culture

  METHOD: "cultural history in the anthropological vein"
  Read cultural expressions (folktales, rituals, police archives, Rousseau)
  as texts to be decoded
  Historical anthropology: what a society's own rituals and stories tell
  about its internal logic

ROGER CHARTIER:
  "Cultural History: Between Practices and Representations" (1988)
  Distinction: representations (cultural meanings, symbolic systems) vs.
  practices (how people actually use texts and objects)
  Books: not neutral transmitters of meaning; how they are formatted,
  who reads them, in what contexts, shapes their meaning
  -> Reading as historical practice, not just texts as containers of ideas
```

## Women's History and Gender History

```
FROM WOMEN'S HISTORY TO GENDER HISTORY

  WAVE 1: COMPENSATORY HISTORY (1970s)
    Add women who were left out: great women, female rulers, women reformers
    Criticism: adds women to existing frameworks without questioning them
    Why were women left out in the first place?

  WAVE 2: SOCIAL HISTORY OF WOMEN (mid-1970s)
    Women's experience as subject: work, family, reproduction, sexuality
    Women not individuals but as a group with shared experiences
    Sources: court records, demographic data, wills, medical records

  WAVE 3: GENDER HISTORY (1980s-1990s)
    JOAN SCOTT: "Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis" (1986)
    Landmark article in American Historical Review
    Argument: "gender" is not just a synonym for women; it is a primary way
    of signifying relationships of power
    Gender is a:
      1. Constitutive element of social relationships (based on perceived
         sex differences)
      2. Primary way of signifying power relationships

    Examples:
      Military metaphors use gender (strong = masculine; retreat = feminine)
      Political authority is gendered (paternal authority, virile leadership)
      Even where women are absent, gender logic structures relationships

    This shifted the field: not just recovering women's experience but
    analyzing how gender as a category structures all of history

FEMINIST CRITIQUES:
    Rankean archive: biased toward male-authored documents
    Social science history: women statistically invisible (most economic
    activity uncounted if not market-wage labor)
    Annales: structuralist; women appear only in demographic statistics
    Microhistory (Ginzburg): better -- individual records include women,
    especially inquisition records
```

## Postcolonial and Subaltern Studies

```
SUBALTERN STUDIES GROUP (1982)

  Founded: Ranajit Guha (with Partha Chatterjee, Dipesh Chakrabarty, others)
  Context: South Asian history; critique of both colonial historiography
  AND nationalist historiography as seen "from above"

  SUBALTERN: from Gramsci: groups subordinated in hegemonic structures
    In South Asian context: peasants, agricultural laborers, tribal peoples,
    women -- those whose historical agency was erased by both
    colonial and nationalist historians

  RANAJIT GUHA: "Elementary Aspects of Peasant Insurgency in Colonial India" (1983)
    Read colonial records against the grain: find peasant consciousness
    behind the violence the colonial state recorded and tried to suppress
    The colonial record does not give access to peasant experience
    but it gives traces of the structures of peasant consciousness

  SPIVAK'S CHALLENGE: "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988)
    Even Subaltern Studies historians speak FOR subalterns, not with them
    The very categories of historical analysis are Western
    The subaltern woman is doubly subordinated: colonialism + patriarchy
    And when she speaks, she speaks through frameworks that were not hers
    The widow immolating herself: colonial account (barbarism); nationalist
    account (heroism); both obliterate her own perspective
    -> The subaltern cannot speak in the frameworks available

  SIGNIFICANCE:
    Challenged the idea that history can simply "recover" marginalized voices
    All historical representation is also a construction
    This fed directly into the postmodern challenge
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| School | Key Question | Key Method | Key Limitation |
|--------|-------------|------------|----------------|
| New Social History | What were ordinary people's lives like? | Quantification, aggregate data | Individual variation lost |
| E.P. Thompson | How did class consciousness develop? | Experience as mediator | Class-centric; other identities? |
| Microhistory | What was marginal people's experience? | Single case, thick documentation | Not generalizable; source-dependent |
| Cultural history | What did events mean to participants? | Anthropological thick description | Over-reliance on elite texts |
| Gender history | How does gender structure power? | Category analysis (Scott) | Risk of losing material conditions |
| Subaltern studies | Can marginalized people's history be recovered? | Read against the grain | Self-critique: still speaking for |

## Common Confusion Points

**"History from below" and microhistory are not the same** -- History from below focuses on ordinary/subaltern people as a class or group (Thompson, Hobsbawm). Microhistory focuses on a single individual or small community to reveal structural features through the exceptional case. Both expand historical subjects beyond elites, but with different scales and methods.

**Gender history is not the same as women's history** -- Women's history focuses on women as subjects and recovering their experiences. Gender history (following Scott) analyzes how gender as a relational category structures all historical processes, including those where women are absent. Analyzing masculine ideals in military history is gender history.

**Thick description is not the same as detailed description** -- Geertz's "thickness" is about the layers of meaning attached to a cultural practice, not just more detail about what happened. A very long description of every physical detail of the cat massacre would not be thick description. What makes it thick is the interpretive framework that explains what the behavior meant within its cultural context.

**The Subaltern Studies critique does not mean history of marginalized people is impossible** -- Spivak's challenge is methodological: we should be self-conscious about the frameworks we impose when we claim to speak for others. It is a caution against naive recovery narratives, not a claim that such history should not be attempted. Subsequent subaltern studies historians continued the project while incorporating the critique.
