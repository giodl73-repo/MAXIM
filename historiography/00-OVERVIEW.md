# Historiography — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Historiography has two related but distinct meanings: (1) the history of historical writing — who wrote what, when, and why; (2) the theory and methodology of how history should be written — what standards of evidence, explanation, and narrative are legitimate. The field is simultaneously a catalog of past historians and a live debate about the epistemological foundations of the discipline.

```
THE DUAL STRUCTURE OF HISTORIOGRAPHY

  DESCRIPTIVE HISTORIOGRAPHY:          METHODOLOGICAL HISTORIOGRAPHY:
  "What have historians written?"      "How should history be written?"
  |                                    |
  +-- History of History as a          +-- What counts as evidence?
  |   discipline (this is part            (document, material, oral,
  |   of intellectual history)            statistical, archaeological)

  |                                    |
  +-- Individual historians:           +-- Can history be objective?
      Thucydides to Hayden White           (positivism vs. relativism
                                           debate -- still live)
```

## The Central Methodological Debates

Three debates structure the field and recur in every period:

```
DEBATE 1: POSITIVISM vs. INTERPRETATION

  POSITIVIST POLE:                    INTERPRETIVE POLE:
  History approaches science          History is always from a perspective
  Primary sources give access to      Sources are constructed; all reading
  "how it actually was"               is interpretation
  Standards of evidence can be        There is no view from nowhere
  objective
  Ranke -> Buckle -> social science   Droysen -> Dilthey -> hermeneutics
  history (cliometrics)               -> Gadamer -> linguistic turn

  CURRENT CONSENSUS: Neither pure pole. "Best-justified inference
  from evidence" (Appleby, Hunt, Jacob). Evidence constrains
  interpretation; interpretation cannot be eliminated.

DEBATE 2: STRUCTURE vs. AGENCY

  STRUCTURE:                          AGENCY:
  Large forces shape events           Individuals and groups make choices
  (capitalism, climate, geography,    within structures that could have
  population dynamics, technology)    gone differently
  Braudel's longue duree              Ranke's narrative of great actors
  World systems theory                Microhistory (individual as window)
  Annales school                      Biography, political history

  No resolution: entangled at every scale. Structure constrains; agency
  acts within constraints; outcomes are underdetermined.

DEBATE 3: NARRATIVE vs. ANALYSIS

  NARRATIVE:                          ANALYSIS:
  History as story: causally          History as systematic investigation:
  connected sequence of events        patterns, structures, statistics
  with meaningful beginning,          "Social science history"
  middle, end                         Quantitative history (cliometrics)
  Gibbon, Macaulay, Schama            Fogel, Annales second generation
  Accessible, shapes memory           Rigorous, often unreadable
  Stone: "revival of narrative"
  (1979) as reaction against
  social science model
```

## The Historian's Basic Toolkit

```
SOURCE TYPES AND THEIR PROBLEMS

  WRITTEN DOCUMENTS (the Rankean core)
    Primary: contemporary with event (letters, administrative records,
             chronicles, legal documents, newspapers)
    Secondary: later analysis and interpretation
    PROBLEMS: Survival bias (what was kept?), literacy bias (who wrote?),
              intentional distortion (official records lie), languages,
              archival access (some archives still closed)

  MATERIAL CULTURE
    Archaeology: physical remains when documents are absent or silent
    Objects: coins, ceramics, tools, buildings, art
    ADVANTAGE: Often biases toward different social groups than documents
    PROBLEMS: Interpretation requires frameworks; "silent" material

  ORAL SOURCES
    Interview: living witnesses (limits to 100-year window)
    Oral tradition: transmitted across generations
    PROBLEMS: Memory distortion, transmission errors, contemporary
              interpretation of past events, power dynamics in collection

  STATISTICAL / QUANTITATIVE
    Census records, vital registers, economic data, price series
    ADVANTAGE: systematic, comparable over time
    PROBLEMS: Categories change (what "household" means in different centuries);
              collection biases; missing populations

  IMAGES AND AUDIOVISUAL
    Maps, paintings, photographs (from 1839), film (1890s), audio
    PROBLEMS: All are interpretive artifacts, not neutral records
```

## The Meta-Question: Can History Be Objective?

```
POSITIONS ON HISTORICAL OBJECTIVITY

NAIVE REALISM (Rankean ideal):
  Past events happened; documents record them; historian recovers the facts.
  "Wie es eigentlich gewesen" = "as it essentially/actually was."
  PROBLEM: Selection is interpretation; archival survival is not random;
           every narrative choice involves values.

HISTORICAL RELATIVISM (Beard, 1934):
  "Written History as an Act of Faith": historian cannot transcend their
  own time, culture, class, gender, nationality.
  Every history is an ideology.
  PROBLEM: Taken to extreme, makes all historical claims equally valid.
           But some claims are better supported than others.

POSTMODERN POSITION (Hayden White, Derrida):
  Historical texts are linguistic constructions. The "past" is only
  accessible through texts. Texts don't transparently represent reality.
  PROBLEM: Cannot explain why some historical accounts are better
           than others, or why archives matter.

PRACTICAL REALISM (current dominant position):
  Acknowledge: selection is inevitable; perspective is unavoidable.
  BUT: Evidence constrains interpretation. Some claims are better
  justified than others. Progress in historical knowledge is real.
  Nagel's "view from nowhere" is impossible, but objectivity as
  intersubjective agreement on standards is achievable.

  Key formulation: "Objectivity in historical study is an aspiration,
  a regulative ideal, not a description of achieved practice."
  (Megill 2007)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Historiography is not history** -- History is the study of past events; historiography is the study of how history has been written and should be written. A historiographical essay asks: "How have historians approached this question, and what assumptions and methods distinguish their approaches?"

**Primary sources do not "speak for themselves"** -- The Rankean ideal that the historian becomes transparent in the face of primary evidence is untenable. The archivist chose what to preserve; the bureaucrat chose what to record; the historian chooses which archives to visit, which documents to read, which passages to cite. These are all interpretation.

**The linguistic turn did not destroy history** -- Hayden White's claim that history is narrative fiction was widely read as making historical claims as arbitrary as novels. Most historians rejected this extreme conclusion while accepting the more modest point that form shapes content. The result was greater awareness of narrative choices, not abandonment of evidence standards.

**"Revisionist" is neutral, not pejorative** -- All good history is revisionist: it revises previous accounts based on new evidence or new frameworks. The term becomes pejorative only in political discourse (Holocaust denial, historical falsification). In professional usage, revisionism is a compliment: it means the historian found something previous accounts missed.
