# Postmodernism and After

## The Big Picture

Postmodernism is not one thing. It is a reaction against modernism's universalism —
and that reaction took at least four distinct forms. What they share is a rejection
of the idea that there is one correct architecture for the modern world.

```
FOUR STRANDS OF POSTMODERNISM
===============================

STRAND 1: HISTORICIST PM               STRAND 2: DECONSTRUCTIVISM
(Venturi, Moore, Graves, Johnson)       (Gehry, Hadid, Koolhaas,
                                         Libeskind, Eisenman, Tschumi)
Bring history back.                    Fragmentation, not history.
Reintroduce ornament, reference,        Geometry broken, displaced,
irony, symbolism.                       non-orthogonal. Derrida.
Building = communication.               Building = critique of certainty.
Decorated shed (Venturi).               CATIA enables complex curves.

STRAND 3: CRITICAL REGIONALISM         STRAND 4: HIGH-TECH
(Frampton, Siza, Murcutt, Ando)        (Foster, Rogers, Piano)
Resist placeless modernism.             Celebrate structure + services.
Use local material, topography,         Machine aesthetic. Visible
climate, culture as design generators.  ducts, columns, bracing.
Not style imitation — thoughtful local  Pompidou Centre, Lloyd's.
response.

TIMELINE:
1966  Venturi "Complexity and Contradiction" — the shot fired
1972  Learning from Las Vegas (Venturi/Scott Brown/Izenour)
1972  Pruitt-Igoe demolished (Jencks's "death of Modern Architecture")
1977  Jencks "The Language of Post-Modern Architecture"
1978  Portland Building competition won by Michael Graves
1982  Portland Building completed — historicist PM's flagship
1984  AT&T Building (Chippendale pediment skyscraper), NYC
1988  MoMA "Deconstructivist Architecture" exhibition
1997  Guggenheim Bilbao (Gehry) — "Bilbao effect" coined
2003  Pritzker Prize: Jorn Utzon (Sydney Opera) — 30 years late
```

## Venturi: Complexity and Contradiction (1966)

Robert Venturi's "Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture" (1966) is the founding
text of architectural postmodernism — the book that gave critics and architects
permission to challenge modernism's orthodoxy.

### The Core Argument

```
VENTURI'S ARGUMENT (1966)
==========================

THESIS: "I like complexity and contradiction in architecture."
  — This is a manifesto sentence in opposition to Mies's "less is more"
  — Both/and instead of either/or.
  — Good architecture has multiple meanings, not singular clarity.

EXAMPLES FROM HISTORY:
  Venturi demonstrates that the great buildings of history are
  COMPLEX and CONTRADICTORY — not simple and unified.
  The Parthenon's subtle refinements (entasis, stylobate curve):
    simultaneously regular AND irregular.
  Borromini's facades: simultaneously convex AND concave.
  Michelangelo's Laurentian Library vestibule: columns that
    don't support anything (bracketed out from the wall),
    staircases that won't let you walk normally.

MODERNISM'S PROBLEM (per Venturi):
  Modernism's exclusions (no ornament, no history, pure function)
  are impoverishments.
  "Less is a bore."
  Complexity is not failure — it is richness.
  Buildings communicate. They communicate culturally, symbolically,
  through reference and memory.
  Modernism's willful amnesia about history cuts buildings off
  from the communication system buildings have always used.

VERSUS FUNCTIONALISM:
  "A building can be more than a diagram of its function"
  Function is necessary but not sufficient for architecture.
  The hospital is functional. The church is functional. But they
  must also mean something — and meaning requires reference.
```

### Learning from Las Vegas (1972)

With Denise Scott Brown and Steven Izenour, Venturi studied the Las Vegas Strip as
a model of popular communication in architecture.

```
LEARNING FROM LAS VEGAS: KEY CONCEPTS
=======================================

THE STRIP (Las Vegas, 1966 research):
  Buildings set back from the road behind parking lots
  The building is invisible from the car at highway speed
  What communicates: the SIGN
  Giant signs, neon, billboards — the symbolic front
  The building behind is a "shed" (cheap, functional box)
  The communication is applied to the shed

DUCK vs. DECORATED SHED:
  DUCK: The building's form IS its symbol.
    The building shaped like a duck selling duck food.
    Form = symbol. The symbol IS the architecture.
    Gordon Wu Hall (Brown University) — building shaped like a
    traditional building = duck architecture.
    Falling Water, Ronchamp = duck (form is the message)

  DECORATED SHED: A conventional building + applied symbol.
    The shed is functional. The decoration communicates.
    Medieval cathedral: the architecture is a shed + symbols
    (rose window, tympanum sculpture, statuary program) applied.
    The Strip casinos: box + giant sign = decorated shed.
    MOST ARCHITECTURE IS DECORATED SHED.

  Venturi's argument: decorated shed is NOT inferior.
  It is the honest acknowledgment that communication
  and structure are different problems with different solutions.
  Modernism tried to make the duck (every building should be
  its own symbol through form) — and failed most of the time.

MAIN STREET IS ALMOST ALL RIGHT:
  Commercial vernacular (strip malls, signs, parking lots)
  was despised by modernist planners (=visual pollution).
  Venturi: this IS the built environment of American democracy.
  Don't plan it away. Understand it. Learn from it.
  This was provocative in 1972. It still is.
```

## Historicist Postmodernism

### Portland Building (1982, Michael Graves)

```
PORTLAND BUILDING: HISTORICIST PM IN CONCRETE
===============================================

Competition program: Portland, Oregon city office building
Graves won over two modernist competitors.
Building: 15-story office block, completed 1982

VOCABULARY:
  Rusticated base (painted blue): references classical base
  Keystone ornament: giant abstracted keystone over entry
  Garland swags: applied decorative motifs (abstract, not literal)
  Color: terracotta, blue, turquoise — not the gray/white of modernism
  Portlandia statue: hammered copper female figure, 8m tall, above entry

CRITICAL RECEPTION:
  Modernist critics: garish, nostalgic, postcard architecture
  Postmodern critics: brave, communicative, breaks the monotony
  Public: divided, mostly liked the color and ornament
  Philip Johnson: "a major breakthrough"
  Ada Louise Huxtable (NYT): "an ambitious failure"

STRUCTURAL REALITY:
  Steel frame with thin cladding (standard office building construction)
  The ornamental elements are applied to the cladding
  This IS the decorated shed Venturi described — and Graves was
  explicitly demonstrating it. The ornament is not structural.
  This is precisely the "honesty" modernism would reject.
  For Graves: decorating a shed is fine. Sheds need decorating.

2020 RENOVATION:
  The building was in serious disrepair (glass panels deteriorating,
  mechanical systems failing). Major renovation completed 2020.
  The renovation preserved and restored the ornamental vocabulary.
  The building turned out to need $195M in repairs 38 years after
  opening — the thin cladding system was not durable.
  Irony: modernist curtain walls also had durability problems.
```

### AT&T Building / 550 Madison Ave (1984, Philip Johnson)

The AT&T Building brought postmodern historicism to the most visible possible context:
a Midtown Manhattan skyscraper.

```
AT&T BUILDING: THE CHIPPENDALE SKYSCRAPER
==========================================

DESIGN ELEMENTS:
  Base: triumphal arch entry (Romanesque/Bramante reference)
  Shaft: standard glass and granite curtain wall (modernist)
  Crown: broken pediment — a "Chippendale" double-scroll top
         (referring to 18th-century furniture pediment profile)

WHY IT WAS SIGNIFICANT (and scandalous):
  Skyscrapers since Mies had flat tops (or setbacks or antennas).
  A broken pediment on a 197m granite skyscraper is legible.
  It communicates from blocks away: "I have a top."
  This was Johnson's point: the skyscraper skyline had become
  monotonous because no building had a distinctive crown.
  Historical skylines (pre-1940): Chrysler (Art Deco crown),
    Empire State (Art Deco mast), Woolworth (Gothic crown) —
    every building had identity.
  Modernist skylines: all flat boxes. No identity.
  Johnson restored the crown. Whether the borrowed Chippendale
  vocabulary was appropriate or ironic was left to interpretation.

PHILIP JOHNSON'S HISTORY:
  Johnson had been the champion of modernism (Seagram, MoMA 1932
  International Style exhibition, curated Deconstructivist Architecture 1988)
  — and now he designed the most famous postmodern building.
  Johnson was architecture's greatest opportunist (or pluralist).
  He understood every movement well enough to lead it.
```

## Deconstructivism

### 1988 MoMA Exhibition: Deconstructivist Architecture

Philip Johnson curated the 1988 MoMA exhibition "Deconstructivist Architecture"
featuring seven architects: Frank Gehry, Zaha Hadid, Rem Koolhaas / OMA, Daniel
Libeskind, Peter Eisenman, Bernard Tschumi, and Coop Himmelb(l)au.

The theoretical basis was Derrida's philosophical deconstruction — the idea that
architectural space and form could be made to destabilize its own authority, reveal
contradictions, and resist singular readings.

```
DECONSTRUCTIVISM: WHAT IT IS (AND ISN'T)
=========================================

NOT historicist (unlike Graves/Johnson/Moore).
NOT minimalist (unlike Mies).
NOT "just weird shapes."

CORE STRATEGIES:
  1. Non-orthogonal geometry: walls, floors, roofs at unexpected angles.
     Nothing is horizontal. Nothing is perpendicular. Reference to
     Russian Constructivism (1920s), which Derrida referenced.

  2. Fragmentation: the building appears broken apart, displaced,
     cut through, or incomplete. Parts that should align don't.

  3. Collision: multiple incompatible geometries occupy the same site.
     The building is the record of a geometric conflict.

  4. Destabilization: the building denies visual stability.
     Leaning walls. Floors that tilt. Unclear structure/enclosure.

WHAT IT MEANS:
  If the International Style's grid expressed rational certainty
  (the machine age, scientific progress, modernity), then
  deconstructivism expresses uncertainty (postmodern condition:
  no grand narrative, no singular truth, multiple readings).
  The fragmented building IS the philosophical statement.
```

### Frank Gehry: Titanium + CATIA

Gehry (b. 1929) is the figure whose work most changed public perception of what
architecture could look like.

```
GEHRY'S TECHNOLOGICAL BREAKTHROUGH
=====================================

EARLY GEHRY (1970s–1980s):
  Gehry Residence, Santa Monica (1977–1978):
  Gehry wrapped his ordinary house in industrial materials
  (chain link, corrugated metal, raw plywood) — deconstructed it.
  Low-budget materials + deliberate roughness = new aesthetic.

THE PROBLEM WITH COMPLEX CURVED SURFACES (pre-CATIA):
  A design with doubly-curved, non-repetitive panels is impossible
  to describe with traditional architectural drawings.
  A curved titanium panel: every panel is a different size and shape.
  You cannot draw 3,000 different panels in 2D.
  Contractors cannot price 3,000 unique panels.
  Fabricators cannot cut 3,000 unique panels without CNC machines.

CATIA (Computer Aided Three-dimensional Interactive Application):
  Originally developed by Dassault Systèmes for aerospace (F-16 design)
  Gehry's project manager Jim Glymph saw it and realized:
  CATIA can describe any 3D curved surface exactly.
  You can unfold any panel into flat geometry for fabrication.
  CNC cutting produces each unique panel from digital description.
  The contractor's price comes from the digital model, not from
  traditional working drawings.

  CATIA = the enabling technology for complex parametric form.
  Without it, Bilbao is impossible.
  With it, any surface describable as a continuous geometry is buildable.
```

**Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (1997)**

```
BILBAO: STRUCTURE AND SURFACE
================================

STRUCTURE:
  Conventional steel frame inside (no structural magic)
  The curving titanium skin is CLADDING — attached to the frame
  The frame provides the actual structural support

TITANIUM CLADDING:
  ~33,000 individual titanium panels
  Each panel is unique (different size, curvature, geometry)
  0.38mm thick — about as thin as a human fingernail
  Will last 100+ years without maintenance (titanium doesn't corrode)
  Cost: $174/sqm installed (cheaper than copper because thinner)
  Color: silver-warm in cloudy light, golden in sun

THE BILBAO EFFECT:
  Bilbao (1997): A declining industrial city in Basque Country.
  The Guggenheim was part of a waterfront regeneration strategy.
  Within 3 years: 4 million visitors (city expected 400,000)
  Revenue generated covered construction cost in 3 years
  82,000 new jobs attributed to the museum's economic impact
  Scores of cities attempted to replicate with "starchitect" museums.
  CRITIQUE: The "Bilbao effect" is not reliably reproducible.
  Most flagship cultural buildings don't produce this impact.
  Bilbao was the right building in the right city at the right moment
  with the right content (a genuine Guggenheim collection).
  The effect was not just the architecture.

SPATIAL QUALITY:
  The titanium fragments into facets at building scale → scale ambiguity
  The river-facing atrium: 50m tall, irregular volumes, natural light
  The building has exceptional gallery quality (flat-floored, lit properly)
  It is not just a sculpture — it works as a museum.
```

### Zaha Hadid: Fluid Forms

Hadid (1950–2016) developed a more continuous and fluid version of deconstructivism —
not fragmented but flowing, as if poured.

```
ZAHA HADID ARCHITECTS: GEOMETRIC APPROACH
==========================================

EARLY HADID (paintings + unrealized projects):
  The Peak, Hong Kong (1982, competition): geological strata as building layers
  Vitra Fire Station, Weil am Rhein (1993): sharp wedge-like planes
  Cardiff Bay Opera House (1994): won competition, not built (controversy)
  The early work established the visual language before the technology
  existed to build it easily.

ZHA SIGNATURE LANGUAGE:
  Continuous curved surfaces (vs. Gehry's faceted panels)
  No right angles, no clear floor-to-floor distinction
  Surfaces flow from floor to wall to ceiling
  Multiple focal points, no single organizing axis
  Forms suggest movement, flow, speed

KEY BUILDINGS:
  MAXXI Museum, Rome (2009): concrete and glass
    Multiple overlapping circulation paths (not one route through)
    Structural walls ALSO define circulation geometry
    The structural system IS the spatial system
    Long concrete cantilevers require significant engineering

  Heydar Aliyev Center, Baku (2012): white fiberglass composite
    Single continuous surface folds from ground to building envelope
    The plaza, the facade, and the roof are ONE surface
    Technology: fiberglass panels over steel substructure + composite deck

  Guangzhou Opera House (2010): black granite and glass
    The brief: two "pebbles" beside the river
    The theater and the smaller public building as two geological forms
    Internal spaces: complex non-orthogonal geometry
    Acoustic engineering in non-orthogonal spaces is extremely difficult
    (sound reflections don't follow simple rectangular geometry)
```

### Rem Koolhaas / OMA: The Ironic Program

Koolhaas (b. 1944) is simultaneously an architect, theorist, and provocateur. His
work uses analysis and irony rather than formal expression as primary tools.

```
KOOLHAAS / OMA: KEY CONCEPTS
==============================

"DELIRIOUS NEW YORK" (1978):
  Book about Manhattan's architectural logic.
  The grid + the skyscraper produce a "culture of congestion."
  Unlike modernism (which saw congestion as a problem to solve),
  Koolhaas celebrates it as the generator of metropolitan life.
  New York is not a failure of planning — it is a SUCCESS of
  programmatic density: every possible function, every possible
  person, stacked and next to each other.

"JUNKSPACE" (2001):
  The built product of modernization is "junkspace" —
  the malls, airports, hotel lobbies, generic interiors.
  These are not failures of design — they are what the real
  economy of building produces. Architects design the
  exceptions; junkspace is the rule.
  This is simultaneously critique and acceptance.

S,M,L,XL (1995, book):
  Architecture at four scales (small, medium, large, extra-large)
  produces different problems and different approaches.
  At XL (the airport, the theme park, the new Chinese city),
  traditional architectural thinking doesn't apply.
  The "Generic City" essay: cities that have shed their identities
  because growth has overwhelmed their historical core.

SEATTLE CENTRAL LIBRARY (2004):
  Program: books spiral (by Dewey Decimal) on a continuous
  ramp → reading rooms → meeting spaces → public functions
  STRUCTURE: The library's functional program generates an
  irregular stacking of floor plates at different heights and
  orientations → the exterior is the projection of this stacking.
  It is not arbitrary form — it is the functional logic made visible.
  Diamond-pattern glass and steel skin wraps the complex stacking.
  Anti-duck: the building is its own decorated shed —
  the function makes the form, and the skin makes it legible.
```

## Critical Regionalism

Kenneth Frampton's essay "Towards a Critical Regionalism" (1983) proposed a third way
between universalist modernism and historicist postmodernism.

```
CRITICAL REGIONALISM: FRAMPTON'S FRAMEWORK
============================================

THE PROBLEM WITH UNIVERSALISM:
  International Style glass boxes are the same in Osaka, Lagos,
  Houston, and Oslo. They are "placeless."
  They ignore local climate, topography, materials, culture.
  They are optimized for visual image (the photographic facade)
  but not for tactile, olfactory, or climatic experience.

THE PROBLEM WITH HISTORICISM:
  Using historical styles (colonial porches in Houston, "Tudor" in
  British suburbs) is nostalgia — scenography without content.
  It fakes memory rather than creating genuine local identity.

CRITICAL REGIONALISM'S APPROACH:
  Use modernism's structural and spatial techniques (skeleton frame,
  open plan, etc.) — don't reject them.
  BUT: mediate universal modernism with local specifics:
    - Topography: how the building meets the ground
    - Climate: light quality, thermal envelope, air movement
    - Materials: local quarry stone, locally made brick, timber
    - Tectonic culture: the tradition of making in the region
  NOT visual imitation of historical forms —
  genuine engagement with local conditions.

KEY PRACTITIONERS:

Álvaro Siza (Portugal, b. 1933):
  Leça Swimming Pools (1966): concrete platforms on Atlantic rocks
  Buildings that read the landscape and respond to it
  Modernist vocabulary + Portuguese granite + Atlantic light
  Pritzker Prize 1992

Glenn Murcutt (Australia, b. 1936):
  Tin-roofed pavilions on the Australian landscape
  Corrugated iron (the vernacular material), steel frame,
  deep verandahs (climate), orientation for cross-ventilation
  Never built outside Australia
  Solo practitioner — no large firm
  Pritzker Prize 2002

Tadao Ando (Japan, b. 1941):
  Board-formed concrete + water + light
  Church of the Light, Osaka (1989): cross of light cut through
    concrete wall — minimal geometry, maximum spiritual effect
  Water Temple, Awaji Island (1991): approach path under water
    (descend through lily pond to enter the lotus-shaped hall)
  The materials are universal (smooth concrete) but the
  spatial experiences are specific to Japanese approaches
  to nature, threshold, and sacred space.
  Pritzker Prize 1995
```

## High-Tech Architecture

High-Tech is not postmodern but contemporaneous. It expresses the building's
structure and services as the primary ornament — machines celebrating themselves.

```
HIGH-TECH: KEY BUILDINGS
==========================

POMPIDOU CENTRE (1977, Renzo Piano + Richard Rogers)
  The building inside-out: all structure and services on the exterior.
  Steel exoskeleton: cast "gerberettes" (structural brackets) visible
    at each floor level — the force geometry expressed in steel castings.
  Color-coded services: blue = air, yellow = electrical, green = water,
    red = circulation (escalators + elevators in exterior tubes).
  The interior: one large uninterrupted floor plate per story.
  No structural columns inside (all loads to perimeter).
  The building is infinitely flexible for exhibitions.
  Cultural impact: the piazza in front became the most visited public
    space in Paris — Pompidou created the public plaza as gift to city.

LLOYD'S OF LONDON (1986, Richard Rogers)
  Same inside-out logic: lifts and services in steel towers on exterior.
  But different program: insurance market with the Room (trading floor)
    at center, surrounded by galleries looking down.
  Central atrium: 12-story glass-roofed trading space.
  The concrete/steel/glass machine at the heart of the City of London
  provoked exactly the controversy Pompidou had in Paris.
  Now Grade I listed (the UK's highest protection level).

HONG KONG + SHANGHAI BANK (1985, Norman Foster)
  Steel skeleton expressed externally in 5 "coat hanger" masts.
  Load is hung from the masts at 5 levels → floors suspend from above.
  This transfers ALL structural loads to 8 perimeter masts:
    no interior columns anywhere in the building.
  Atrium at center: mirrors on sun scoops on roof track sun
    and redirect it to a mirror array that sends light down the
    full height of the atrium. (This was pre-LED: saving air
    conditioning load in Hong Kong was worth the engineering.)
  Construction: each mast can be built independently, simultaneously,
    → faster overall construction than sequential floor-by-floor.
  Delivered 3 months early.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Deconstructivism = postmodernism"**
Deconstructivism is contemporaneous with postmodernism but philosophically opposed
to historicist PM. Graves uses history and wants buildings to communicate through
familiar reference. Gehry and Hadid have no interest in historical reference — the
fragmented geometry expresses philosophical uncertainty, not nostalgia.

**"The Bilbao effect proves starchitecture works"**
The Bilbao Guggenheim was exceptional, not typical. The formula (commission famous
architect + unusual building = tourist revenue) has not reliably worked elsewhere.
The Guggenheim Las Vegas (2001, Gehry) and Guggenheim Las Vegas (2001, Koolhaas) both
closed. The "starchitect as regeneration" strategy usually underperforms Bilbao's
results.

**"Critical Regionalism is just regionalism"**
No — traditional regionalism means imitating local historical styles (colonial revival
in the American South, Tudor revival in England). Frampton explicitly rejects that.
Critical Regionalism uses modernist structural techniques but responds to local
climate, topography, and materials. Murcutt's corrugated iron pavilions are not
imitating Australian vernacular buildings — they are modern structures that respond
to the Australian landscape, climate, and light.

**"Philip Johnson was an opportunist"**
He was, and he was also serious. Johnson was the first American to understand and
promote each major architectural movement: International Style (1932 MoMA), brutalism
(he built himself a glass house in 1949), postmodernism (AT&T Building), and
deconstructivism (1988 MoMA exhibition). He was the movement-maker who understood
every movement. Whether that is opportunism or genuine pluralism is a debate that
continues.
