# Modernism

## The Big Picture

Modernism in architecture is a program, not just a style. The program: reject all
historical ornament, embrace industrial production methods, design rationally for
function, and produce a universal architecture for the modern age. The structural
innovation that enables it (reinforced concrete + steel skeleton) was already in place
from the Chicago School. Modernism is a philosophical decision about what to do with
that structural freedom.

The structural shift from load-bearing masonry to reinforced concrete frame is a platform shift: the new substrate (column + slab) removes constraints that had shaped architecture for millennia. Load-bearing walls no longer define the plan; the facade no longer carries load. This is analogous to a new ISA or runtime enabling programming models that were previously impossible — remove the old constraints and a new design space opens up. Le Corbusier's Five Points (free plan, free facade, pilotis, roof garden, ribbon windows) are the new design freedoms the platform shift enables — not arbitrary aesthetic preferences but logical consequences of what the new material permits.

```

======================================
```

## Loos: "Ornament and Crime" (1908/1913)

Adolf Loos' essay is the founding manifesto of architectural modernism.

**The argument:**
1. Primitive people tattoo themselves and decorate everything because they have
   not yet developed higher culture.
2. The modern craftsman who uses ornament wastes his labor and others' money.
3. Cultural evolution = withdrawal from ornament.
4. "The evolution of culture is synonymous with the removal of ornament from
   utilitarian objects."
5. Therefore, ornament IS crime — it steals value and wastes labor.

**The buildings Loos designed** (Goldman & Salatsch, Vienna; Villa Müller, Prague)
have entirely unornamented exteriors and richly materialled interiors (marble, wood,
brass) — but the richness comes from material quality, not applied decoration.

**The legacy:** Loos was not widely influential in his own time but became a founding
text for modernism in retrospect. His argument set the ideological program that
Gropius and Le Corbusier would implement at scale.

## Bauhaus (1919–1933)

### The Institution

The Staatliches Bauhaus was founded in Weimar by Walter Gropius in 1919 by merging
the Weimar Academy of Fine Art with the School of Arts and Crafts. The synthesis:
fine art + craft + technology.

```
BAUHAUS STRUCTURE
==================

GOAL: "Unite all creative art forms under the primacy of architecture"
  (Gropius's founding manifesto)

PEDAGOGY:
  Year 1: Vorkurs (preliminary course)
    — Itten (1919–1923): intuitive, expressive, based on materials
    — Albers + Moholy-Nagy (1923+): analytical, visual perception
    — Students make fundamental studies of material, form, color

  Years 2–3: Workshop training
    — Each student works in TWO parallel tracks:
      FORMMEISTER (artist/designer): teaches aesthetics, form
      WERKMEISTER (craftsman): teaches the actual technique
    — Workshops: metalwork, weaving, ceramics, glass painting,
      woodwork, wall painting, typography, photography, theater,
      architecture (added 1927, Hannes Meyer)

FACULTY ACROSS YEARS:
  Paul Klee: form theory, color
  Wassily Kandinsky: color theory, analytical drawing
  Marcel Breuer: furniture (Wassily Chair, tubular steel, 1925)
  Herbert Bayer: typography (universal font, lowercase only)
  László Moholy-Nagy: light/shadow studies, photography
  Mies van der Rohe: last director, architecture

MOVES:
  Weimar (1919–1925): original location, political pressure
  Dessau (1925–1932): new building, peak productivity
  Berlin (1932–1933): closed by Nazis (4 months)
  DIASPORA: Bauhaus faculty dispersed globally
    Gropius → Harvard GSD
    Mies → IIT Chicago
    Moholy-Nagy → New School of Design (Chicago)
    Albers → Black Mountain College, then Yale
    → The Bauhaus dispersed into American design education
```

### The Dessau Bauhaus Building (1925–1926)

Gropius designed the school's own building as a demonstration of the program:

```
DESSAU BAUHAUS: THE MANIFESTO IN BUILDING FORM
================================================

PLAN: Three wings connected by bridges
  Workshop wing: north (double-height studios + glass curtain wall)
  Dormitory wing: east (student rooms)
  Prelim course wing: south (smaller studios)
  Bridge connecting workshop + admin + school

EXTERIOR:
  Glass curtain wall on workshop wing: full-height, floor-to-ceiling
  The glass wraps AROUND the corner (structural corner column hidden inside)
  No corner pilaster, no decorative element — the glass IS the wall
  Flat roofs throughout
  White rendered surfaces
  No historical ornament anywhere

STRUCTURAL CONCEPT:
  Reinforced concrete frame
  Frame is INSIDE — walls and glass are attached to frame
  The frame is NOT expressed on the exterior
  (Compare to Mies who expresses the frame)

SIGNIFICANCE AS MANIFESTO:
  Every design decision encodes the program:
  Glass curtain wall = transparency (modern, open, honest)
  Flat roof = rejection of historical pitched roof convention
  White surfaces = absence of ornament (Loos)
  Corner glass = denial of traditional corner emphasis
  Workshop wing visible from road = pride in making
  The building teaches what the Bauhaus teaches, by being itself
```

## Le Corbusier: The Five Points

### The Theoretical Program

Le Corbusier (born Charles-Édouard Jeanneret, 1887–1965) is the most influential
theoretical architect of the 20th century. His Five Points (1926) are a manifesto
about what the reinforced concrete frame makes possible.

```
LE CORBUSIER'S FIVE POINTS OF A NEW ARCHITECTURE (1926)
=========================================================

The five points derive from the technical capabilities of the
reinforced concrete (domino) frame, where slab is supported by
columns and not by walls.

+--+--+--+--+--+--+
|  1. PILOTIS      |   Building raised on columns.
|                  |   Ground floor freed (pass through, park).
|  • • • • • • •   |   Columns → ground open for pedestrians/nature.
+--+--+--+--+--+--+

+--+--+--+--+--+--+
|  2. FREE PLAN    |   No loadbearing walls.
|                  |   Columns carry structure.
| |  | | | | |    |   Partitions can be placed anywhere.
| __ __ _____     |   Each floor can be completely different.
+--+--+--+--+--+--+

+--+--+--+--+--+--+
|  3. FREE FAÇADE  |   Exterior wall carries no load.
|                  |   Windows can be placed anywhere.
|  ====  ====      |   Wall can be entirely glass if desired.
|  ====  ====      |   Façade is a design decision, not a
+--+--+--+--+--+--+   structural necessity.

+--+--+--+--+--+--+
|  4. HORIZONTAL   |   Windows full width of facade.
|     WINDOWS      |   Strip of glass from column to column.
|                  |   Maximum light, panoramic view.
| [===============]|   More light per unit of wall than
+--+--+--+--+--+--+   traditional punched windows.

+--+--+--+--+--+--+
|  5. ROOF GARDEN  |   Flat roof → usable roof terrace.
|                  |   Replaces the garden area lost by
| ~~~~~ ~~~~~ ~~~~ |   building on the ground.
| (grass/terrace)  |   Insulation for the floor below.
+--+--+--+--+--+--+   Health benefits (sun, air).

These five points only work because concrete slabs can cantilever —
no need for load path at the edge of the building.
```

### Villa Savoye (1929–1931)

Villa Savoye is the canonical demonstration of all five points in a single building:

```
VILLA SAVOYE: FIVE POINTS IN ACTION
=====================================

SECTION:

         ROOF GARDEN (pt. 5) + solarium
         ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
         +-----------------+
         |  Piano nobile   |  ← main living floor
         |  FREE PLAN (2)  |     ribbon windows (4)
         |  FREE FACADE(3) |     horizontal glazing
         +-+---+---+---+-+-+
           |   |   |   |     ← PILOTIS (1)
           |   |   |   |       columns let ground pass through
           •   •   •   •       entry and garage under building

PLAN of piano nobile:
  No structural walls (columns only)
  Partitions placed as needed for:
    Living room, dining, kitchen, bedrooms (3), bath
  The free plan allows the ramp to pass through the building
  (promenade architecturale — Corbusier's concept of moving
  through the building as a choreographed experience)

SITE:
  Flat farmland at Poissy, 30km from Paris
  Building seems to hover over the grass
  Pilotis create shadow/ground-plane separation
  The building is an object in the landscape
  (vs. Palladio: building is part of the landscape)
```

### Unité d'Habitation, Marseille (1947–1952)

Le Corbusier's answer to postwar housing: a complete vertical city.

```
UNITÉ D'HABITATION: THE MACHINE FOR LIVING IN
===============================================

PROGRAM:
  337 apartments + hotel + shops + gym + nursery school + roof terrace
  in a single 18-story block on pilotis
  No ground-floor activities — all community space is inside or on roof

APARTMENT SECTION (duplex "bottle rack" system):
  Each apartment spans the FULL WIDTH of the block
  with a balcony on BOTH facades (east + west).
  Apartments interlock vertically (cross-section of a wine bottle rack):
  Even-floor apartment: enters at main level, one floor goes UP
  Odd-floor apartment: enters at main level, one floor goes DOWN
  Internal corridor every OTHER floor → no corridor noise for half apartments.
  Every apartment has cross-ventilation (east balcony → west balcony)

CONSTRUCTION SYSTEM:
  Rough (brut) concrete — béton brut (the term that gives "Brutalism")
  The formwork marks are visible — not smoothed or painted
  The texture of the making is the finish

ROOF:
  Gymnasium, running track, play areas, nursery school
  Views of Mediterranean and Alps
  The roof is the communal garden (Five Point 5 at building scale)

SOCIAL VISION:
  Vertical village (village vertical)
  Frees the ground for parkland
  Internal "street" (the corridor) is the rue intérieure
  Self-contained community — never need to leave the building
  THE CRITIQUE (Team X, Jane Jacobs, etc.):
  By lifting residents from the street, destroyed street life.
  No sidewalks, no shops at grade, no spontaneous encounter.
  The Unité was successful. The housing estates that COPIED it
  without Le Corbusier's quality (Pruitt-Igoe, Park Hill) were not.
```

## Mies van der Rohe: "Less Is More"

Mies (1886–1969) is the other pole of architectural modernism — where Corbusier was
expansive, theoretical, and rhetorical, Mies was reductive, precise, and silent.

### Barcelona Pavilion (1929, rebuilt 1986)

```
BARCELONA PAVILION: STRUCTURAL AND SPATIAL ANALYSIS
=====================================================

PROGRAM: German national pavilion for 1929 International Exposition
  No permanent function — just architecture. Pure experience.
  Destroyed at exposition's end; rebuilt 1986 in identical location.

PLAN:
  Two floating roofs (not one) over an asymmetric composition of walls
  Walls are: onyx from Atlas, green Tinos marble, golden Cipollino,
    gray-tinted glass, clear glass
  No room. No enclosure. Space flows around and through the walls.
  The walls suggest spaces without defining them.

  +---+  +----+   No walls form a box.
  |   |  |    |   Each wall is a plane suspended in space.
  |   |  +----+   You can see past and through every element.
      |       The floor continues outside under the roof.
  +---+       The roof continues past the walls.
              There is no inside vs outside — just spatial layers.

STRUCTURE:
  Eight cruciform chrome-plated steel columns carry both roof slabs.
  The columns are where the structure is. Period.
  None of the walls are structural — they are hung or just standing.
  The marble walls have steel frames to hold them up.
  The chrome cruciform shape: Mies chose it because a standard
  rolled I-section has flanges that imply direction (this way or that).
  A cruciform is non-directional — pure presence without bias.

BARCELONA CHAIR:
  Designed for this pavilion. King and Queen of Spain sat in it.
  Chrome X-legs, leather cushions.
  Still in production, still expensive.
```

### Seagram Building, NYC (1954–1958, with Philip Johnson)

```
SEAGRAM BUILDING: THE URBAN GLASS TOWER
=========================================

INNOVATION:
  First major glass tower set back from the street with a plaza.
  Previous NYC skyscrapers built to lot line (required by zoning
  to provide street enclosure — the 1916 setback rules required
  stepped profiles, not open plazas).
  Seagram chose voluntarily to give up 40% of buildable area.
  The plaza was unprecedented generosity to the city.
  NYC rezoning (1961) subsequently incentivized plazas
  (gave floor area bonuses for plaza setbacks).
  → Dozens of New York plazas followed, many of them lifeless
  (because zoning only required the plaza, not its design quality).

FACADE:
  Bronze mullions (not structural — steel frame is inside)
  The bronze I-beams applied to the glass surface reveal the
  structural GRID even though the I-beams are decorative.
  Mies: "Structural honesty" — but the applied I-beams are
  not the structure. They represent it.
  The difference matters: Mies values the appearance of
  structural clarity, not structural nakedness.

FLOOR-TO-FLOOR HEIGHT:
  3.96m — generous. The proportional height was essential.
  "God is in the details": window proportions, mullion profiles,
  the decision to use bronze rather than aluminum.
  Each detail is the result of extreme care about proportion.
```

## The International Style (1932 MoMA Exhibition)

Henry-Russell Hitchcock and Philip Johnson's 1932 exhibition "The International Style"
at MoMA named, defined, and disseminated modernism globally.

```
THE INTERNATIONAL STYLE: DEFINING CHARACTERISTICS
===================================================

Hitchcock + Johnson's three formal principles:

1. VOLUME NOT MASS
   Building as a thin-walled enclosure of space,
   not as solid mass.
   The wall is a surface, not a thick solid element.

2. REGULARITY NOT SYMMETRY
   Not bilateral symmetry (Beaux-Arts axiality).
   Instead: regular structural grid expressing
   the repetitive frame.
   The grid is the organizing principle, not the axis.

3. AVOIDANCE OF APPLIED ORNAMENT
   Not "no ornament" but deliberate absence.
   The building is its own ornament.
   Proportion and material quality replace decoration.

VISUAL GRAMMAR THAT FOLLOWS:
  Flat roof (no historicist pitched roof)
  White or light-colored surfaces (or glass)
  Horizontal strip windows (Corbusier's fourth point)
  No projecting cornices
  Steel or reinforced concrete frame

GLOBAL SPREAD:
  MoMA's endorsement gave the style institutional authority in USA.
  European modernists fled fascism → came to USA (Gropius, Mies,
  Breuer, Sert, Moholy-Nagy, Albers) → entered academia → trained
  a generation that built the postwar urban landscape.
  The International Style became the default language for:
    Corporate office buildings
    Government buildings
    Universities
    Hospitals
    Public housing
  By 1965, the glass box dominated new construction globally.
```

## Brutalism (1950s–1970s)

Brutalism derives from Le Corbusier's béton brut (raw/rough concrete) — not from
the English adjective. It is characterized by the deliberate exposure of structural
material (concrete) without surface finish.

### Why Brutalism?

```
BRUTALISM'S RATIONALE (1950s)
===============================

AFTER WW2:
  Massive rebuilding demand (bombed cities, returning soldiers)
  Modernism is the progressive, anti-fascist style
  Welfare state building programs (UK: housing, schools, universities)
  Functionalism is honest: building shows what it is made of

SMITHSONS' "AS FOUND" AESTHETIC (UK, 1953):
  Alison + Peter Smithson: use materials as found/given by industry.
  No coating, no cladding, no hiding.
  Raw concrete: the formwork marks ARE the finish.
  This is not poverty of aspiration — it is a positive statement.

LE CORBUSIER'S LATE WORK:
  Chandigarh (1950–1965): massive concrete forms
  Ronchamp (1954): curved concrete walls, sculptural
  The late Corbusier abandoned the smoothness of Villa Savoye
  for something raw, heavy, and direct.

TEAM X CRITIQUE (1956):
  The generation after Gropius/Corbusier/Mies:
  van Eyck, Smithsons, Bakema, de Carlo
  Criticism: CIAM's (International Congress of Modern Architecture)
  modernism is too abstract, too rational, ignores human patterns
  of association (identity, community, street).
  Brutalism in some hands tries to respond: make the building's
  community structure legible in its form.
```

### Key Brutalist Buildings

**Boston City Hall (Kallmann McKinnell & Wood, 1968):**
- Winner of a design competition; considered a masterpiece by critics
- Consistently voted one of the ugliest buildings in America by public
- Heavy concrete overhangs, monumental exterior steps, harsh surfaces
- The gap between critical praise and public reception defines Brutalism's problem

**Paul Rudolph, Art and Architecture Building, Yale (1963):**
- Corduroy concrete (vertical ribs created by bush-hammered aggregate exposure)
- Complex sectional organization: 37 floor levels in 7 stories
- Set on fire by students in 1969 (at the height of anti-Vietnam protests)
- Rebuilt preserving the structure and character

## Failure Modes of Modernist Urbanism

```
PRUITT-IGOE (St. Louis, 1954–1972)
====================================

Architect: Minoru Yamasaki (also designed World Trade Center)
Program: 33 towers × 11 stories = 2870 apartment units
Completed: 1956
Demolished: Beginning 1972 (implosion of first tower, July 15, 1972)

Charles Jencks famously called this demolition
"the death of Modern Architecture."

WHAT WENT WRONG:
1. Racial segregation: Pruitt (Black) + Igoe (white) were nominally
   separate developments. Pruitt was for Black residents.
   White tenants left quickly. Pruitt became entirely Black.
   By late 1950s: vacancy, poverty concentration.

2. Maintenance failure: Congress cut funding for public housing
   maintenance. Elevators broke. Lobbies became dangerous.
   The wide gallery corridors between elevator stops (Corbusier's
   rue intérieure concept applied here) became spaces of crime.

3. Social isolation: The design lifted residents from the street.
   No shops at grade. No street life. No casual encounter.
   The social network of the street neighborhood was destroyed.

4. Management failure: The Housing Authority was overwhelmed.

WHAT THIS REVEALS:
  The design was influenced by Le Corbusier's Radiant City concept
  but lacked his quality of construction and management.
  The design was not the ONLY cause — economic, racial, management
  factors were at least as important.
  But the design contributed by removing the street as social space.

  The demolition became symbolic of modernist urbanism's failure.
  In 1972, Minoru Yamasaki's other famous work — the World Trade
  Center — had just opened. Architecture's tragedy and ambition
  in one architect's biography.
```

|----------|--------|
| What are Le Corbusier's Five Points? | Pilotis, free plan, free façade, horizontal windows, roof garden — all enabled by the concrete frame |

| What is the International Style? | Flat roof, glass curtain wall, regular grid, no ornament — named in 1932 MoMA exhibition |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Brutalism is just ugly"**
Brutalism was a coherent ethical position: show what the building is made of, nothing
hidden. The failures came when rough concrete was applied without Le Corbusier's
precision of proportion and spatial quality. Many Brutalist buildings (Barbican, London;
Smithsons' Economist Building) are genuinely elegant. The failures are failures of
execution or of scaling the concept beyond its means.

**"Le Corbusier's Five Points = minimalism"**
Corbusier's buildings are not minimal in the Miesian sense. Villa Savoye has curves,
ramps, irregular wall placements, a solarium with sculptural cutouts. The Five Points
are a structural/compositional system, not a style. Minimalism is Mies. Corbusier
is exuberant even when he's being rational.

**"Modernism = the glass box"**
The glass box is specifically Mies and the International Style. Le Corbusier never
built a glass box. Alvar Aalto (Finland) and Hans Scharoun (Berlin Philharmonic) are
modernist but emphatically not glass boxes. Modernism is a broad tent.

**"The Bauhaus was closed by the Nazis because they hated modern design"**
Not exactly. The Bauhaus was politically suspect — its faculty included Communists
and internationalists, and its design aesthetic was seen as "Jewish" or "Bolshevik"
by the right. The Nazis closed it for political reasons, not just aesthetic ones.
The Nazis had their own modernism (Albert Speer's neoclassicism was not gothic revival
— it was rationalized classical stripped of detail, which shares much with the
International Style's abstraction).
