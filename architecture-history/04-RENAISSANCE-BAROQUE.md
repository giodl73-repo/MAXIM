# Renaissance and Baroque Architecture

## The Big Picture

The Renaissance is first a structural problem solved (Florence Cathedral dome, 1436)
and then a theoretical project (Vitruvius rediscovered, proportion systematized). The
Baroque is what happens when the theory is fully internalized and the goal shifts from
rational order to emotional effect. Both treat the building as a composed object — the
result of a conscious design process — rather than an accumulated construction.

```
RENAISSANCE AND BAROQUE: KEY FIGURES AND WORKS
================================================

EARLY RENAISSANCE (Florence, 1400–1500)
  Brunelleschi (1377–1446)
    — Florence Cathedral dome (structural problem)
    — Mathematical perspective (visual representation)
    — Ospedale degli Innocenti (rational arcade)
  Alberti (1404–1472)
    — De Re Aedificatoria (architectural theory treatise)
    — Santa Maria Novella facade (triumphal arch overlay on Gothic church)

HIGH RENAISSANCE (Rome, 1500–1527)
  Bramante (1444–1514)
    — Tempietto (1502, first fully Classical Renaissance building)
    — St. Peter's original plan (Greek cross, centralized dome)
  Michelangelo (1475–1564)
    — St. Peter's dome redesign (double shell, compressed)

LATE RENAISSANCE / MANNERISM (1520–1600)
  Palladio (1508–1580)
    — Villa Rotonda, Villa Barbaro
    — I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura (most influential book in arch. history)
  Michelangelo (Mannerist architecture at Laurentian Library)

BAROQUE (Rome + Europe, 1600–1750)
  Bernini (1598–1680) — St. Peter's colonnade, Baldachin
  Borromini (1599–1667) — S. Carlo alle Quattro Fontane, S. Ivo
  Wren (1632–1723)  — St. Paul's Cathedral (triple dome)
  Fischer von Erlach — Karlskirche Vienna
  Guarini (1624–1683) — complex intersecting arches, Turin
  Le Vau + Le Nôtre — Versailles
  Guarini, de Cotte, Hardouin-Mansart — French variants
```

## The Context: Vitruvius and the Classical Revival

In 1414, the humanist Poggio Bracciolini found a complete manuscript of Vitruvius's
"De Architectura" (c. 15 BCE) in a monastery in Switzerland. This was the only
surviving ancient architectural treatise — and it became the theoretical foundation
for Renaissance architecture.

```
VITRUVIUS: THE CORE FRAMEWORK
===============================

De Architectura, Ten Books (c. 15 BCE)

Three principles (in Latin): FIRMITAS / UTILITAS / VENUSTAS
  Firmitas:  Structural soundness / durability
  Utilitas:  Functional appropriateness
  Venustas:  Beauty / grace / aesthetic delight

THE THREE ORDERS (Vitruvius describes their origins):
  Doric:    Male — its proportions derived from the male body
  Ionic:    Female — slender, with the volute as a curl of hair
  Corinthian: Maiden — most slender, acanthus leaf capital
             (story of Callimachus seeing acanthus on a basket)

PROPORTIONAL SYSTEM:
  Everything relates to the column diameter as module.
  Column height = k × diameter (different for each order)
  Spacing (intercolumniation) = ratio of diameter
  Entablature height = fraction of column height
  Everything is rationally related — the building has internal
  proportional consistency like a musical scale.

WHY THIS WAS REVOLUTIONARY FOR THE RENAISSANCE:
  Medieval building proceeded by accumulated practice and local
  tradition — no underlying theory.
  Vitruvius provided a THEORETICAL BASIS: proportional reasoning,
  historical precedent, written authority.
  Renaissance architects could now argue for design decisions
  using rational argument, not just custom.
```

## Brunelleschi: The Dome of Florence Cathedral

### The Problem

The Florence Cathedral (Santa Maria del Fiore) presented an unsolved engineering
problem in 1418 when Brunelleschi entered the competition for the dome:

- The existing drum was 42m in internal diameter
- The drum had already been built (construction started 1296)
- The drum walls were too thin to support centering (temporary formwork)
- No suitable timber existed in Florence for the formwork spans required
- Political context: Florence could not import materials from enemies
- The dome needed to span 42m — larger than any dome built since the Pantheon
  (43.3m), which used concrete. Medieval builders had no concrete.

```
BRUNELLESCHI'S DOME: THE PROBLEM SPACE
========================================

Medieval dome construction:
  Build timber centering → place stone voussoirs → remove centering
  For 42m dome: centering would need to span 42m
  Largest available timber: perhaps 15–18m
  Braced trusses possible but require enormous material + very tall
  scaffolding to support the centering

BRUNELLESCHI'S INSIGHT:
  Build the dome WITHOUT centering.
  Each ring of bricks must be self-supporting during construction.
  How? Use the herringbone (spina pesce) brick pattern.
```

### The Herringbone Brick Solution

```
HERRINGBONE (SPINA PESCE) BRICK PATTERN
=========================================

Normal horizontal brick coursing in a dome:
  Each brick leans inward slightly (dome profile).
  Before the mortar sets, the brick wants to slide inward.
  A temporary wooden frame (centering) holds it in place.

Herringbone pattern:
  Most bricks laid in horizontal courses (normal).
  Every ~10 bricks, a brick is placed VERTICALLY (the spine brick).
  The vertical brick leans against the horizontal bricks on each side.
  This creates interlocking wedge-action.

  NORMAL COURSES:    HERRINGBONE PATTERN:
  === === ===        === === ||| === ===
  === === ===        === ||| === ||| ===
  === === ===        ||| === === === |||
                     (| = vertical spine brick)

  The vertical spine bricks lock each ring as it is placed.
  Each ring is self-supporting before mortar sets.
  No centering needed.

  STRUCTURAL LOGIC:
  The horizontal bricks in a partially-built ring form an arch.
  Add the next brick: the arch is incomplete (open at top).
  The brick would fall before mortar sets.
  The vertical spine bricks GRIP the in-place bricks enough
  to hold the arch open while mortar sets.
  Once mortar sets, the ring is a complete compression ring.
  Build the next ring on top.
```

### Double Shell Construction

Brunelleschi's dome is not one dome — it is two:

```
DOUBLE SHELL CROSS-SECTION
============================

       LANTERN
         ||
         \/
   +-----------+          ← OUTER SHELL (lighter, protective, visible)
   |  /     \  |            Brick + sandstone, ~50–80cm thick
   | / void  \ |            Supported by ribs that connect both shells
   |/         \|          ← VOID SPACE (man can walk in it)
   +-----------+
   |/    S    \|          ← INNER SHELL (structural, thicker ~1.2m)
   |     T    |             Brick in herringbone pattern
   |     R    |             Carries dome weight
   +     U    +
   |     C    |           ← DRUM (existing, 1.6m thick)
   |     T    |
   +-----+----+

EIGHT MAIN RIBS (marble, visible as white ribs on exterior):
  Run from drum to lantern, visible outside
  Two smaller ribs between each pair of main ribs (hidden)
  Ribs tie inner and outer shells together
  Ribs carry most of the compressive loads

HOOP TENSION PROBLEM:
  Any large dome has tendency to "crack open" at the base
  (like an orange segment separating)
  The base course of the dome tries to expand outward
  = HOOP TENSION (circumferential tension at dome base)
  Stone and brick can't take this tension.
  Brunelleschi's solution: timber and iron chains at the base
  of the dome act as tension hoops (like barrel hoops).
  Later domes (including St. Peter's) used iron chains for this.

STRUCTURAL ACHIEVEMENT:
  42m clear span — first done since the Pantheon (43.3m, 125 CE)
  Built 1418–1436 — completed in Brunelleschi's lifetime
  No centering used for the main dome
  Still standing, essentially unrepaired, after 590 years
```

## Bramante and the Classical Ideal: The Tempietto

The Tempietto (1502) in the courtyard of San Pietro in Montorio, Rome is the first
building of the Renaissance that can be called fully Classical — not just borrowing
classical elements but recomposing them in a purely classical spirit.

```
TEMPIETTO COMPOSITION
======================

PLAN:                          ELEVATION:
                                  +-+
                                  | | ← lantern
    [CYLINDER CELLA]           +--+-+--+
    [PERISTYLE]                | DRUM  |
                               +--+ +--+
                               |  | |  |  ← DOME
                               |  |P|  |
   Circular cella              |  |E|  |  ← Drum with
   surrounded by               |  |R|  |    entablature
   Doric peristyle             |  |I|  |
   (16 columns)                |  |S|  |
   Set in circular             |  |T|  |  ← PERISTYLE
   courtyard                   |  |Y|  |    (Doric order)
   (the courtyard was          |  |L|  |
   never finished)             |  |E|  |
                               +--+ +--+

   Diameter: only 4.5m
   Bramante intended it to sit in a circular courtyard,
   which would have made the relationship of cylinder to
   circle explicit.
   At this small scale, the design is proportionally perfect.
   It could be scaled up to any size while maintaining ratios.
   This is what Bramante proposed for St. Peter's.
```

## St. Peter's Basilica: The Dome Competition

St. Peter's dome is one of the most studied structures in architectural history — it
was designed by multiple architects over 120 years and had a structural failure during
construction that required major remediation.

```
ST. PETER'S DOME: DESIGN HISTORY
==================================

BRAMANTE (1506): Greek cross plan, dome on pendentives
  → Died 1514, little built

RAPHAEL (1514): Changed to Latin cross plan
  → Died 1520

ANTONIO DA SANGALLO (1520): Elongated Latin cross
  → Died 1546

MICHELANGELO (1546): Returned to Greek cross, redesigned dome
  → Built much of the drum before his death (1564)
  → His model shows hemispherical dome

GIACOMO DELLA PORTA (1588–1590):
  → Changed dome profile from hemispherical to MORE POINTED
  → Reduces horizontal thrust at base (same argument as Gothic)
  → Built the actual dome based on modified Michelangelo design

CARLO MADERNO (1607–1612):
  → Added long nave (returned to Latin cross plan)
  → Destroyed the centralized effect Michelangelo intended
  → The facade he added is now criticized for hiding the dome
    from street level

CURRENT CONFIGURATION:
  Inner dome diameter: ~41.5m (just under Pantheon's 43.3m)
  Top of cross to floor: 136.6m (interior), 132.5m (interior nave)
  Dome shell thickness: ~2.4m (double shell with void between)
  Iron chains at dome base: 3 iron rings added 1743 by Vanvitelli
    after cracks appeared in the drum
    (drum was cracking from hoop tension — same problem as Florence)
```

## Palladio: The Most Influential Architect in History

Andrea Palladio (1508–1580) may be the most influential architect ever to live — not
for his buildings (most in the Veneto, Italy) but for his book.

### I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura (1570)

"The Four Books of Architecture" established the template for classical design that
dominated Western architecture for 300 years. It was translated into every European
language and read by every architect and educated patron.

The book's structure:
- Book I: General principles, materials, orders
- Book II: Private buildings (villas and palaces) — with Palladio's own projects
- Book III: Roads, bridges, public buildings
- Book IV: Roman temples (measured drawings)

The critical feature: **measured drawings of Palladio's own buildings alongside his
reconstructions of Roman buildings** — this established Palladio's work as equivalent
in authority to ancient Rome.

### The Villa Rotonda (1566–1591)

```
VILLA ROTONDA: COMPOSITIONAL LOGIC
=====================================

PLAN:
  +---------+---------+
  |  /      |      \  |
  | / PORCH |PORCH  \ |
  |/        |        \|
  +---------+---------+
  | PORCH   |   PORCH |  ← Four identical porticos
  |         |         |    on all four facades
  +---------+---------+
  |  \      |      /  |
  |   \     |     /   |
  +----+----+----+----+
           |
        CENTRAL
        CIRCULAR
        HALL (domed)

KEY FEATURES:
  Central circular hall under low dome
  Four identical Ionic porticos (one on each side)
  Building sited on a hill → porticos frame views in all four
  directions (piano nobile = piano for views, not ground floor)
  Absolute bilateral symmetry on TWO axes
  Piano nobile raised one story above ground

PALLADIO'S INNOVATION:
  The temple front (pedimented porch) applied to a HOUSE
  (not a temple or church)
  This was scandalous to strict classicists
  It became the model for the "Palladian villa" that spread
  through England (Inigo Jones), Ireland, Virginia (Jefferson's
  Monticello uses this template), and colonial America.

PROPORTIONAL SYSTEM:
  Palladio used musical ratios (from harmonic theory):
  Octave (1:2), Fifth (2:3), Fourth (3:4), Major Third (4:5)
  These same ratios govern room proportions, window heights,
  intercolumniation — the building is a frozen musical score.
```

## Baroque Architecture

### The Shift from Rational to Emotional

Baroque (c. 1600–1750) does not abandon classical grammar. It knows the rules so well
it can bend them deliberately for effect. Where Renaissance architecture seeks
rational clarity and serene proportion, Baroque seeks emotional engagement — drama,
movement, surprise, the manipulation of light as theatrical medium.

**Triggers for Baroque:**
- Counter-Reformation (Council of Trent, 1545–1563): The Catholic Church needed
  architecture that was emotionally compelling, not intellectually dry. Art and
  architecture should move the soul toward faith.
- Availability of Roman wealth for construction (papal patronage)
- The completion of St. Peter's provides the proving ground

### Bernini: Master of Theatrical Space

**St. Peter's Baldachin (1623–1634)**

```
BALDACHIN STRUCTURAL AND SYMBOLIC LOGIC
=========================================

The Baldachin is the enormous bronze canopy over the tomb of
St. Peter, directly under the crossing of St. Peter's dome.

Dimensions: 28.7m tall — as tall as the Palazzo Farnese
Material: bronze (stripped from the Pantheon porch ceiling —
  "quod non fecerunt Barbari, fecerunt Barberini"
  = what the barbarians did not do, the Barberini did)

TWISTED COLUMNS:
  Eight 20m-tall twisted (barley-sugar) bronze columns
  Four main columns carry the baldachin canopy
  Model: the twisted columns of the old St. Peter's ciborium
  Tradition: these "Solomonic" columns were believed to come
  from the Temple of Solomon in Jerusalem
  Visual effect: the twist implies rotation, movement, energy
  The columns "move" without going anywhere

SYMBOLIC FUNCTION:
  Marks the tomb of St. Peter (Christianity's foundation)
  Defines the sacred center of the largest church in Christendom
  Visible under the dome — connects earthly tomb to heavenly dome
  Scale: deliberately monumental at human eye level
  (the dome is so high it becomes abstract; the baldachin fills the
  middle zone, bridging human and divine scales)
```

**St. Peter's Colonnade (1656–1667)**

Bernini designed the enormous elliptical piazza in front of St. Peter's as "the
arms of the church welcoming the faithful." The geometry is precise:

```
ST. PETER'S COLONNADE GEOMETRY
================================

Two curved colonnades (each 196m long) embrace an elliptical piazza
Center of colonnade curves: exactly one focal point of the ellipse
  → From the two focal-point markers on the ground, all four
    rows of columns align optically into a single row

Why 4 rows of columns?
  Walking into the piazza, you see an impenetrable forest.
  As you pass the focal marker, the four rows align:
  suddenly there is just one transparent colonnade.
  The revelation is the experience.
  Bernini makes geometry produce a moment of perception.

Meaning: "Welcoming embrace" metaphor was Bernini's own description.
  The colonnade also frames the view of the façade and dome
  (controlling the approach sequence).
```

### Borromini: Curves in Plan

Francesco Borromini (1599–1667) took Baroque further — not just dramatic surfaces but
actually curving the plan itself. His buildings have no straight walls.

```
SAN CARLO ALLE QUATTRO FONTANE (1638–1646)
============================================

PLAN (extremely simplified):
  The main body is defined by a continuous curving wall
  oscillating between convex and concave:

  +====+
 (      )   ← Nave: oval plan (not rectangle)
  +====+
      |
  [ENTRANCE]

  The wall surface alternates:
    CONVEX → CONCAVE → CONVEX → CONCAVE
  As you move around the perimeter.
  Every surface is curved. Every column is engaged in a
  curved wall. Nothing is flat.

EFFECT:
  The space appears to breathe — expanding and contracting
  as you move. You cannot see the whole building at once.
  Multiple focal points pull attention in different directions.
  Light enters from unexpected directions.

SCALE: The entire church fits in the plan of one of the
  four piers of St. Peter's crossing. Borromini achieved
  maximum spatial complexity at minimum scale.

CONTRAST WITH BRUNELLESCHI:
  Brunelleschi: clarity, rational geometry, legible structure
  Borromini: complexity, hidden structure, spatial drama
  Both equally sophisticated — opposite programs.
```

### Wren's St. Paul's: The Three-Dome Solution

St. Paul's Cathedral (1675–1711) solves the same problem Brunelleschi faced — and
takes an entirely different approach.

```
ST. PAUL'S THREE-DOME SYSTEM
==============================

DOME 1: INNER DOME (brick shell, plastered)
  What the congregation sees when they look up
  Scale: appropriate to human interior — not overwhelming
  Diameter: ~30m (visible from below)
  Decorated with James Thornhill's illusionistic paintings
  Not structural — it is a ceiling

DOME 2: MIDDLE BRICK CONE (structural)
  Invisible (hidden between the two visible domes)
  Circular brick funnel ~1.0m thick rising steeply
  Carries the weight of the stone lantern at the top
  (Lantern weighs approximately 850 tonnes)
  Structural logic: straight-sided cone = pure compression
  (no bending in a thin shell if the geometry is right)

DOME 3: OUTER TIMBER DOME (external appearance)
  What you see from the street — the iconic London silhouette
  Timber framework + lead sheathing
  Not carrying any structural load
  Designed for external visual impact — height, silhouette
  The profile is more pointed than the inner dome → taller
  visible from a distance

SUMMARY:
  Inner dome: what congregation sees (scale appropriate)
  Cone: what carries the lantern (pure structure)
  Outer dome: what London sees (visual composition)
  Three separate design problems → three separate solutions
  Each optimized for its purpose independently

  This is fundamentally different from Brunelleschi (who
  made ONE double shell do ALL three functions).
  Wren separated the problems.
```

## English Palladianism: Inigo Jones to Thomas Jefferson

Palladio's influence spread to England through Inigo Jones (1573–1652), who traveled
to Italy and returned to introduce Italian classicism to England:

```
PALLADIAN TRANSMISSION PATH
=============================

Palladio (Veneto, 1560s)
    |
    v
I Quattro Libri (1570) — published, widely distributed
    |
    v
Inigo Jones (London, 1620s)
  — Banqueting House, Whitehall (1622)
  — Queen's House, Greenwich (1616–35)
  — First Palladian buildings in England
    |
    v
William Kent, Colen Campbell, Lord Burlington (1720s)
  — Chiswick House (1729): Villa Rotonda for London
  — Stourhead (1721): Palladian villa + landscape garden
  — Burlington = impresario of English Palladianism
    |
    v
Thomas Jefferson (Virginia, 1780s–1820s)
  — Monticello: Rotonda + Italian villa references
  — University of Virginia: Rotonda (adapted Pantheon)
  — Jefferson read Palladio in Virginia before going to Europe
  — Made Palladio's model the architectural language of
    American republican government
    |
    v
US Federal architecture (18th–19th c.)
  — Capitol building: dome + classical portico
  — Countless government buildings = borrowed legitimacy
    from Roman/Palladian classical grammar
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet: Renaissance and Baroque

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why did Brunelleschi use herringbone brickwork? | Self-supporting rings during construction — no centering required |
| Why is St. Peter's dome double-shelled? | Inner shell for interior scale; outer for external profile; iron chains for hoop tension |
| What is a pendentive dome? | Circular dome on a square room via spherical-triangle transitions |
| What is entasis? | Subtle column bulge — optical correction or structural expression |
| What is the most influential architectural book ever? | Palladio's I Quattro Libri (1570) — established classical proportional system for 300 years |
| What is Baroque's defining characteristic? | Emotional engagement over rational clarity; curved surfaces, dramatic light, movement |
| What are Bernini's twisted columns? | "Solomonic" columns evoking the Temple of Solomon — spiral implies movement/energy |
| Why does Borromini use curves in plan? | Spatial drama — space breathes, multiple focal points, no single readable geometry |
| What is Wren's three-dome solution at St. Paul's? | Inner (visible, plastered), structural brick cone (hidden, carries lantern), outer timber (external silhouette) |
| What is Palladianism? | Application of Palladio's proportional system to country houses and public buildings (England, USA) |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Brunelleschi invented the dome"**
He didn't invent the dome — he solved a specific engineering problem (dome without
centering over an existing 42m drum). The Pantheon (43.3m) preceded him by 1300 years.
What Brunelleschi invented was the construction METHOD for Florence's specific problem,
and the approach was unique to that problem's constraints.

**"Baroque is just ornate Renaissance"**
Not quite. Renaissance seeks rational serenity and proportional clarity. Baroque
deliberately creates tension, movement, and emotional effect. Borromini's curved walls
are not ornate Renaissance — they are a fundamentally different spatial strategy.
Bernini's colonnade is not decorated Bramante — it is a space-making gesture that
requires the visitor's movement to reveal its geometry.

**"The classical orders are structural"**
In ancient Greece and Rome, yes. In Renaissance and Baroque, no — they are
applied ornament. The Colosseum's three-story order sequence is engaged columns on an
arched concrete and stone structure. The arch carries the load; the columns decorate.
Renaissance use of orders is always primarily ornamental; the actual structure is often
behind them.

**"Michelangelo designed St. Peter's dome"**
He designed the dome's form and built the drum. But he died in 1564 before the dome
was built, and Giacomo della Porta changed the profile from hemispherical (Michelangelo's
model) to more pointed (della Porta's version, 1588–1590). The dome you see is della
Porta's, based on Michelangelo's structural concept with a different profile.
