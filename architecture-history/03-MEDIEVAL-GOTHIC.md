# Medieval and Gothic Architecture

## The Big Picture

The Gothic cathedral is one of the most audacious structural achievements in human
history. A system of stone, lime mortar, and gravity creates a self-supporting
machine for light — walls that are mostly glass, supported by external stone armatures.
To understand how, you must first understand what came before and why it didn't work.

```
THE STRUCTURAL REVOLUTION: ROMANESQUE TO GOTHIC
=================================================

                ROMANESQUE                          GOTHIC
                (800–1150 CE)                      (1140–1500 CE)
                ===========                         ============

Wall thickness:    1–2 meters                     ~0.5–0.8 m (at triforium)
Window size:       Small (wall needs mass)         Maximum (wall = glass screen)
Light level:       Dark, cave-like                 Flooded with colored light
Vault type:        Barrel / groin                  Ribbed (pointed)
Arch form:         Semicircular                    Pointed
External profile:  Massive, fortress-like          Delicate, pinnacled, vertical
Structural logic:  MASS resistance                 MECHANISM (thrust redirection)
                   (wall thick enough that          (thrust exported outside
                   compression stays OK            by flying buttress)
                   at every cross-section)

Key insight: These are OPPOSITE strategies, not evolution of the same idea.
Romanesque uses brute force. Gothic uses precision engineering.
```

## Romanesque Architecture (800–1150 CE)

### Structural Logic: Mass as Solution

Romanesque architects faced the same tension problem as everyone before them: stone
cannot take tension, so bending must be avoided. Their solution: make every element
so thick that the compressive stress is never close to failure, even when bending
introduces tension on one face.

```
ROMANESQUE WALL STRUCTURAL LOGIC
==================================

WHY THICK WALLS WORK:
  Stone has ~100 MPa compressive strength.
  If a wall is 1.5m thick, the center of gravity (of the load coming
  down from the vault above) must shift very far from center before
  the tension face stress exceeds ~2 MPa tensile limit.

  In engineering terms: the kern of the cross-section is large.
  The resultant stays inside the kern → no tension anywhere.

  WALL CROSS-SECTION:
  +-+   +-+    ←    THIN wall: small kern
  | |   | |         small lateral load → tension
  +-+   +-+

  +------+          THICK wall: large kern
  |      |          larger lateral load needed to cause tension
  +------+          vault thrust can be tolerated

WINDOW SIZE CONSTRAINT:
  A window opening removes wall area.
  Remaining wall must carry the same arch/vault thrust.
  The wall cross-section (t × h) decreases → kern shrinks.
  The resultant of combined gravity + thrust is more likely
  to go outside the kern → tension → crack → failure.

  Therefore: more windows = thinner wall = more dangerous.
  Romanesque windows are small because the wall needs its area.
```

### Key Romanesque Buildings

**Durham Cathedral (1093–1133):** Often cited as the first building to use pointed rib
vaults over a large nave (a proto-Gothic element in a Romanesque building). But the
walls are still Romanesque thickness — the structural logic remains mass-based.

**Speyer Cathedral (1030–1106):** Largest Romanesque church. Four-tower west facade,
groin-vaulted nave. Scale as statement of imperial authority (Salian dynasty).

**Cluny III (1088–1130):** Largest building in Western Europe until St. Peter's Rome.
Cluniac monastic order at its peak. Five-aisled nave, barrel vault with pointed profile
(reduces thrust). Mostly destroyed during French Revolution.

**Abbey of St-Denis (~1140):** The hinge building between Romanesque and Gothic.
Abbot Suger's renovation of the choir (1140–44) is credited as the first Gothic work.
The pointed arch, ribbed vault, and large stained-glass windows appear together here
for the first time as a unified structural and theological system.

## The Gothic Revolution: Three Interlocking Innovations

The Gothic system is not one innovation — it is three innovations that only work
together. Remove any one and the system fails.

```
THE THREE PILLARS OF GOTHIC STRUCTURAL LOGIC
=============================================

   INNOVATION 1         INNOVATION 2         INNOVATION 3
   =============        =============        =============
   POINTED ARCH         RIBBED VAULT         FLYING BUTTRESS
   (lower thrust)       (load to ribs        (carries thrust
                        → ribs to piers)     outside building)

   Each enables the others:
   - Ribbed vault needs pointed arch (to equalize height)
   - Flying buttress works because ribs concentrate load to piers
     (no distributed thrust along wall — thrust is at rib points)
   - Pointed arch reduces thrust that flying buttress must carry

   Without flying buttress: even Gothic vault would push walls out
   Without ribbed vault: flying buttress doesn't know where to push
   Without pointed arch: vault webs don't work efficiently

   ALL THREE TOGETHER: the wall can become a non-structural screen.
   Fill it with stained glass. The building glows.
```

### Innovation 1: The Pointed Arch

```
SEMICIRCULAR VS POINTED ARCH: THRUST COMPARISON
=================================================

SEMICIRCULAR ARCH (Romanesque):
   For span S, rise = S/2 (fixed by geometry)

         ___
        /   \
       /     \           Rise = S/2
      |       |
   ---+-------+---
      |<--S-->|

Horizontal thrust H = W × S / (8 × rise) = W × S / (4S) = W/4
  (approx., uniform load W)

POINTED ARCH (Gothic):
   Rise can be varied independently of span.
   Taller arch → smaller horizontal thrust.

         |
        / \
       /   \              Rise = r (greater than S/2)
      /     \
     /       \
   --+---------+--
     |<---S--->|

H = W × S / (8 × r)
  If r = S (1:1 ratio): H = W/8 — half the thrust of semicircular

ADDITIONAL BENEFIT: Can vary span while keeping same height.
  If nave bay and aisle bay are different widths, semicircular arches
  would be different heights (ugly, discontinuous).
  Pointed arches can equalize heights by adjusting the point angle.
  This is essential for ribbed vaults with unequal bay geometry.
```

### Innovation 2: The Ribbed Vault

```
RIBBED VAULT STRUCTURAL LOGIC
================================

GROIN VAULT (Romanesque):
  Two barrel vaults intersect. Load spreads across entire surface.
  The groin lines are where the two surfaces meet — stress
  concentrates here but there's no structure to manage it.
  The web surface must be thick to handle the concentrated stress.

RIBBED VAULT (Gothic):
  Ribs are placed along the groin lines (and other directions).
  The ribs ARE structural — they carry concentrated loads.
  The web panels between ribs only carry their OWN weight.
  Web panels can be THIN (50–80mm at Chartres).

  +----------+
  |  \  /  /|      Ribs: thick, carry loads
  | r  \/  r|      Web: thin, self-supporting only
  | i  /\  i|      Ribs deliver loads to PIER CAPITALS
  | b /__\ b|      Ribs act as concentrated load paths
  |  /    \ |
  +----------+

Load path:
Vault self-weight + snow + maintenance
  → Web panels carry to ribs (short spans, manageable bending)
  → Ribs carry to capitals (ribs in compression along their curve)
  → Capitals deliver to piers
  → Piers carry to foundations

The wall between piers carries NO vault load. None. Zero.
The wall is now structurally redundant — a screen only.
Fill it with glass.
```

### Innovation 3: The Flying Buttress

The flying buttress is the key to understanding why Gothic cathedrals look the way
they do from outside. The external stone armature is not decoration — it is a machine
for redirecting vault thrust to the ground.

```
FLYING BUTTRESS FORCE PATH DIAGRAM
====================================

        VAULT THRUST (horizontal)
              →→→→
NAVE              SIDE AISLE      EXTERIOR

              [FLYING BUTTRESS]
              (arch-shaped strut)
  VAULT         →→→→→→→→→→→→→     OUTER PIER
    |           FLYING BUTTR.         |
    |           carries H thrust      |
  CLERESTORY    over aisle roof      PINNACLE
  WINDOW        to outer pier         (adds
  (large)                            vertical
    |                                load →
  TRIFORIUM                         resultant
  GALLERY                           stays in
    |                                pier)
  AISLE ROOF
  (flyng bttr
   passes over)

INNER FLYING BUTTRESS: carries vault thrust from high vault
OUTER FLYING BUTTRESS: carries clerestory wind load
  (Notre-Dame Paris has TWO flying buttresses per bay)

PINNACLE FUNCTION:
  Flying buttress delivers horizontal thrust to outer pier.
  Horizontal + vertical gravity → resultant force = diagonal.
  For the resultant to stay INSIDE the pier cross-section,
  add more vertical load: heavy pinnacle on top of pier.
  Pinnacle = structural element, not just decoration.
  Gothic pinnacles that look ornamental are doing real work.

  +-+                  +-+
  |P|  ← pinnacle      |P|
  |I|     weight       |I|  F = resultant
  |N|                  |N| /
  |N|                  |N|/
  |A| ← thrust →       |A+--
  |C|                  |C|
  |L|                  |L|
  |E|                  |E|
  +-+                  +-+
  Without pinnacle: thrust goes outside kern → failure
  With pinnacle: resultant stays inside kern → safe
```

## Chartres Cathedral: Structural Analysis

Chartres (1194–1220) is the paradigmatic Gothic cathedral — rebuilding after fire,
incorporating all three Gothic innovations at mature scale.

```
CHARTRES STRUCTURAL DIAGRAM
=============================

CROSS-SECTION (nave bay):
                    |  |
                    |  |  ← Flying buttress (inner, to vault)
                   / /  \ \
    CLERESTORY    / /    \ \
    WINDOWS      | |      | |
    [large glass]| |      | |
                  | |    | |
    TRIFORIUM     +========+   ← Passage in wall thickness
    GALLERY       | |    | |
    [blind arcade]| |      | |
                  | |      | |
    AISLE         | |      | |
    ARCADE        | |      | |
    [arches to    | |      | |
     side aisle]  | |      | |
    MAIN PIER     |  ||||  |  ← Bundled columns (responds to vault ribs)
                  |  ||||  |

DIMENSIONS:
  Nave width:      16.4m clear
  Nave vault height: 36.6m
  Wall thickness at triforium: ~0.5m (remarkably thin)
  Flying buttress length: approximately 15m from pier to pier

THREE ELEVATION ZONES:
  Arcade (bottom): arches to side aisle, tall piers, bundled colonnettes
  Triforium (middle): blind gallery or passage in wall thickness
  Clerestory (top): large stained-glass windows, maximum light
```

**The bundled pier:** Chartres' main piers are circular columns with four colonnettes
attached (one for each rib direction). This is not merely aesthetic — each colonnette
responds to a specific vault rib above. The load path is legible: rib → colonnette →
pier → foundation. The column announces its structural logic in its form.

## Notre-Dame de Paris: Engineering at the Frontier

Notre-Dame (1163–1345) pushed the Gothic system to its limits, requiring double flying
buttresses — a solution invented here.

```
NOTRE-DAME FLYING BUTTRESS SYSTEM
===================================

PROBLEM: Notre-Dame's nave vault is high (33m) AND the clerestory
is large AND the side aisles are wide. The outer pier is too far
from the vault. A single flying buttress would be too long.

SOLUTION: Double flying buttress
  OUTER PIER
     ||||
     ||||
     ||||   ← upper flying buttress: carries clerestory wind load
     ||||\____________________
     ||||                    \
     ||||   ← UPPER AISLE ROOF\
     ||||   ← lower flying    |___ VAULT
     ||||\___________________/     THRUST
     ||||                          ← horizontal
     ||||   ← LOWER AISLE ROOF
     ||||
  FOUNDATION

The inner/lower buttress: carries vault thrust (from the main vault)
The outer/upper buttress: carries wind load on the tall clerestory wall

This was the structural innovation invented at Notre-Dame around 1180.
```

## Gothic Structural Limits: Beauvais Choir Failure

The Gothic height race was not safe. Beauvais Cathedral choir (1225–1272) pushed the
system beyond its stable range.

```
BEAUVAIS CHOIR: THE FAILURE THAT DEFINES GOTHIC LIMITS
========================================================

Target vault height: 48.5m — highest Gothic nave vault ever attempted
 (Amiens: 42.3m, Chartres: 36.6m)

Failure (1284): The choir vaults collapsed. Investigation suggests:
  - Piers too slender for the height (likely under-designed for
    combined vertical + horizontal loads)
  - Possibly foundation settlement
  - Possibly resonant loading (wind, bells)
  - Timber scaffolding that had provided lateral support was removed

Repair: Doubled the number of flying buttresses (5 per bay instead
  of the planned 2–3), added intermediate piers.

1573: Tower over crossing collapsed.

The nave was never built. Beauvais remains a choir + transept only,
  a monument to Gothic ambition exceeding Gothic means.

THE LESSON: Gothic structural intuition was empirical, not calculated.
  Master builders learned from observation and precedent, passed skills
  through workshop apprenticeship. They were not solving equations.
  The failures were how the system self-corrected.
  (This is exactly how software evolved before formal testing — and had
  the same characteristic: works until it suddenly doesn't.)
```

## Tracery Evolution: Gothic Ornament as Structural Logic Made Visible

Gothic tracery is the stone pattern in windows — the support structure for the glass.
Its evolution tracks the progression from structurally-motivated form to pure ornament.

```
TRACERY TYPOLOGY
=================

1. PLATE TRACERY (early, ~1200):
   Solid stone wall with holes cut through it.
   The "tracery" is the residual stone between openings.
   +--+--+
   |  O  |    O = circular openings
   +--+--+
   |  |  |    | = pointed arch openings
   +--+--+
   Structurally conservative — stone plate still doing work.

2. BAR TRACERY (~1230, Reims):
   Slender stone bars assembled to create patterns.
   The openings are as large as possible.
   The bars are just enough to hold the glass in place.
   Much lighter and allows more complex geometry.
     _O_      O = trefoil, quatrefoil (3- or 4-lobed)
   /|   |\    The bars define the ornamental pattern
   | | | | |

3. CURVILINEAR TRACERY (Decorated Gothic, ~1290–1350):
   S-curves (ogees), flowing lines, no straight bars.
   Maximum ornamental complexity.
   Ely Cathedral Lady Chapel, Southwell Minster chapter house.
   Structurally same as bar tracery but geometrically exuberant.

4. PERPENDICULAR TRACERY (English Gothic, ~1350–1540):
   Vertical bars continue from sill to head of window,
   crossed by horizontal transoms (divisions).
   Grid-like, rational, typically English.
   King's College Chapel Cambridge, Gloucester choir.
   Fan vaults go with perpendicular tracery.
```

## Fan Vaults: Gothic Geometry's Culmination

Fan vaults (uniquely English, ~1350–1540) are the most complex purely geometric vault
form in Gothic architecture.

```
FAN VAULT GEOMETRY
===================

Normal Gothic ribbed vault: ribs diverge at different angles from pier capital
  → different ribs have different curvatures, different lengths

Fan vault: ALL ribs from a single conoid (truncated cone) surface.
  Every rib has the SAME curvature. The vault surface is a half-cone.
  The pattern is produced by the mathematical regularity of the cone.

   [       FAN VAULT TOP VIEW        ]
   [  +---------------------------+  ]
   [  |   \    |    |    /   |   |  ]
   [  |    \   |    |   /    |   |  ]
   [  |     \  |    |  /     |   |  ]
   [  |      \ |    | /      |   |  ]
   [  |       \|    |/       |   |  ]
   [  +---------------------------+  ]
   [  Each fan = half-cone surface    ]
   [  Fans from opposite piers meet   ]
   [  at center pendant boss          ]

King's College Chapel (Cambridge, 1508–1515):
  Fan vaults over 88m × 12m nave
  Each fan: 10 conoid cells
  Pendant boss (hanging stone) at center of nave bay
  John Wastell, master mason — the mathematical perfection
  of the geometry means the pattern is self-consistent: no
  fudging, no adjustment — every line meets exactly.

  The vault looks impossible. It is one of the most technically
  demanding masonry structures ever built.
```

## Construction Timeline and Process

Gothic cathedrals were not built quickly. They accumulated over generations:

| Cathedral | Start | Consecrated | Completion | Years |
|-----------|-------|-------------|------------|-------|
| Chartres | 1194 | 1220 | 1220 | 26 |
| Amiens | 1220 | 1269 | ~1400 | 180+ |
| Cologne | 1248 | 1322 | 1880 | 632 |
| Reims | 1211 | 1275 | ~1480 | ~270 |
| Salisbury | 1220 | 1258 | 1320 | 100 |
| Notre-Dame | 1163 | 1345 | ~1345 | ~180 |

**Cologne Cathedral** holds the record: construction paused in 1473 with the south tower
only 59m completed. The original 13th-century drawings were rediscovered in Paris in
1814. The cathedral was completed using these 560-year-old drawings from 1842–1880
under the German nationalist project of completing a symbol of German unity.

### Construction Technique

```
HOW GOTHIC VAULTS WERE BUILT
==============================

1. CENTERING: Temporary timber framework (formwork) erected on
   scaffolding to support vault stones until mortar sets.

2. RIBS FIRST: Stone ribs are placed along the centering.
   Ribs are assembled voussoir by voussoir.
   The keystone goes in last — locks the rib in compression.

3. WEB INFILL: Between the set ribs, thin web stones are placed.
   Web stones lean against adjacent ribs — ribs support web
   during construction as well as in service.

4. MORTAR SETS: Lime mortar (not Portland cement).
   Lime mortar is FLEXIBLE — allows micro-cracking and healing.
   Portland cement is BRITTLE — cracks propagate.
   This is why Gothic vaults can accommodate slow settlement
   that would crack a Portland cement structure catastrophically.

5. CENTERING REMOVED: Once vault is self-supporting, centering
   strikes. If the vault is properly designed, it stands.
   If not, it collapses during striking (immediate feedback).
```

## Lime Mortar vs Portland Cement: A Critical Distinction

```
LIME MORTAR              vs    PORTLAND CEMENT
============                   ===============
Carbonates slowly (years)      Sets quickly (days)
Slightly flexible              Brittle
Micro-cracks heal by           Cracks propagate
  recarbonation                  (no self-healing)
Low compressive strength       High compressive strength
  (~5–10 MPa)                    (~25–50 MPa)
Allows differential settlement Resists then fractures
Historically available         Industrial process (1820s)
  (burn limestone, slake)
Gothic mortars still working   Modern concrete cracks
  after 800 years                after decades

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR CONSERVATION:
  Pointing Gothic churches with Portland cement causes damage.
  Cement is stronger than the stone — stress concentrates in
  the stone, not the joint, cracking the stone itself.
  Historic repair requires matching lime mortar properties.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet: Medieval and Gothic

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why are Romanesque interiors dark? | Thick walls needed for stability → small windows only |
| What is a flying buttress doing structurally? | Carrying vault thrust over the side aisle roof to the outer pier |
| Why are pinnacles on Gothic piers? | They add vertical load to keep the thrust resultant inside the pier cross-section |
| Why are Gothic arches pointed? | Lower horizontal thrust than semicircular for same span; can equalize heights across different spans |
| What is a rib vault vs a groin vault? | Rib vault has structural ribs concentrating loads to piers; groin vault distributes load across the surface |
| Why did Beauvais choir collapse? | Pushed vault height beyond what the structural system could handle — empirical limits |
| What is a fan vault? | Vault where all ribs have identical curvature (conoid surface) — purely mathematical geometry |
| Why use lime mortar? | Allows micro-movement without catastrophic cracking; self-healing through recarbonation |
| What three innovations define Gothic? | Pointed arch + ribbed vault + flying buttress — must work together |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Gothic is a style; Romanesque is just an earlier version"**
Different structural strategies, not different versions of the same thing. Romanesque
relies on mass resistance — thick walls provide a large enough cross-section that
thrust never exceeds tensile strength. Gothic relies on mechanism — thrust is actively
redirected outside the building. You cannot gradually evolve one into the other: the
conceptual jump at Saint-Denis (1140) was a discontinuity.

**"Flying buttresses are structural but pinnacles are decorative"**
Both are structural. The pinnacle adds vertical load to the outer pier to keep the
resultant of gravity + thrust inside the pier cross-section. Remove the pinnacle and
the thrust line goes outside the kern → tension → crack → failure. Many Gothic
pinnacles that look like decoration are doing real structural work.

**"Fan vaults are just decorative"**
Fan vaults are among the most structurally sophisticated masonry forms ever created.
The uniform curvature means the conoid surface is self-consistent mathematically —
no fudging. And the sheer thinness of fan vault construction (much of it equivalent
to 50–100mm stone shell) requires extreme precision in design and execution.

**"Gothic cathedrals were built by faith, not engineering"**
They were built by master masons (workshop system, guild knowledge) who understood
structural behavior through empirical observation and transmitted knowledge.
No calculations — but a deeply sophisticated body of structural intuition. The
failures (Beauvais) were how the system learned its limits. The analytical
understanding came centuries later (19th–20th c. structural analysis of Gothic buildings).
