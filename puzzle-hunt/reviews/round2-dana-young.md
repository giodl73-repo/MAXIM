# Round 2 Review — Puzzle Pool Ranking

**Reviewer**: Dana Young
**Perspective**: Craft, presentation, accessibility
**Date**: 2026-02-26
**Scope**: 89 puzzle briefs across 14 categories. Rank for Red Joker inclusion.

---

## Framing

The Red Joker is a book. Not a kit, not a website, not an event. A book you hold, write in, and come back to. Every puzzle must answer one question before any other: does this belong on a printed page?

A puzzle that requires scissors belongs in a different object. A puzzle that requires sunlight belongs outside. These may be fine puzzles. They are not book puzzles.

I am also watching for the on-ramp. The Red Joker is the friendly volume. A newcomer who has never solved a puzzle hunt picks this up and tries the first one. If that first experience is hostile, unclear, or visually chaotic, you have lost them. You do not get a second chance at a first puzzle.

## Section-by-Section: Top 2 Per Category

### K — Computing & Software

**Top pick: K1 — Cipher Decryption**

Visual integration: A decryption grid is clean tabular layout. Ciphertext in monospace, key row, plaintext row. It looks like a worksheet from a cryptanalysis textbook. The page will be orderly and satisfying.

Accessibility: High. Everyone has seen a substitution cipher. The encyclopedia teaches you the specific one. No prior knowledge gatekeeps.

Encyclopedia honor: The solver must read `cryptography/` to identify the cipher and `computing/25-SECURITY.md` to find the key. Two real sections, used for their real purpose.

Durability: Ciphers do not go out of date. This works in 50 years.

**Runner-up: K3 — State Machine Traversal**

The state diagram is the kind of visual that belongs in a CS reference. Clean circles, labeled arrows, a path to trace with a pencil. It reads as natural content. K2 (execute pseudocode) is mechanically sound but visually dull — a table of variable states is not something you want to look at. K3 gives you a diagram you trace with your finger. That is engagement.

K4 and K5 are both weaker on accessibility. Call stacks require genuine CS background. Binary decoding is tedious rather than illuminating.

### Q — Arts & Culture

**Top pick: Q5 — Anamorphic Drawing**

Visual integration: Extraordinary. The page contains what looks like abstract streaks — beautiful in their own right. The puzzle IS the visual. No worksheet, no grid, no extraction table. You tilt the book and the word appears. This is the single most visually striking puzzle in the entire pool.

Accessibility: You tilt a book. A child can do this. No domain knowledge required to execute the mechanism, though recognizing that you should try it requires either a hint or familiarity with Holbein.

Encyclopedia honor: Directly teaches anamorphic perspective from `art-history/`. The puzzle is a working example of the technique it references.

Durability: Anamorphic art has worked since 1533. It will work in 2036.

Physical note: This requires tilting, not cutting. The book stays intact. This is a book puzzle.

**Runner-up: Q2 — Visual Rebus**

A page of spatial word puzzles is visually engaging and immediately inviting. Rebuses are one of the oldest puzzle forms — zero gatekeeping. The arts connection is natural (visual representation of concepts). Q1 (crossword) is functional but generic. Q3 (musical staff) gatekeeps anyone who cannot read notation. Q6 and Q7 require rulers and counting — they are math puzzles wearing arts costumes.

Q4 (color overlay) requires holding two pages to light. It is a beautiful idea but it is not a book puzzle. It is a lightbox puzzle.

### J — Mathematics & Physics

**Top pick: J1 — Proof Completion**

Visual integration: A proof chain with blanks is the most natural-looking page in all of mathematics. It looks like a textbook exercise. Solvers fill in the missing terms. The page is clean, structured, and unmistakably mathematical.

Accessibility: Moderate. The blanks are contextual — you can often narrow candidates from the surrounding logic even without deep background. The encyclopedia provides the specific terms. This is harder than some sections' top picks but appropriate for the Mathematics card.

Encyclopedia honor: This is literally how mathematicians work. Complete the proof. The mechanism IS the discipline.

Durability: Proofs do not expire.

**Runner-up: J2 — Symmetry Operations**

Grid transformations are visual and tactile (trace the transformed positions). The connection to group theory is genuine, not decorative. J3 (compass constructions) requires drawing tools. J4 (matrix multiplication) is arithmetic, not insight. J5 (equation balancing) is chemistry in disguise. J6 (topology) maps genus to A1Z26, which is a generic encoding bolted onto a topology concept.

### 10 — Language & Communication

**Top pick: 10-1 — Multi-Cipher Decoder**

Visual integration: Ten small encoding puzzles on one page, each visually distinct — dots and dashes, raised-dot patterns, flag positions, a grid cipher. This page will be the most visually varied in the book. Every encoding looks different from every other. The eye moves around the page.

Accessibility: High. Each encoding is self-contained and small. A solver who gets stuck on semaphore can skip to Morse and come back. Ten small wins instead of one large one.

Encyclopedia honor: The `codes/` section exists to teach these systems. The solver uses every one. This puzzle is the section's final exam and it is a fair one.

Durability: Morse code is not going anywhere.

**Runner-up: 10-6 — Semaphore / Flag Reading**

Stick-figure flag positions are charming on a page. They are visual, they are decodable, they print well. 10-2 (Rosetta Stone) is interesting but requires three scripts on one page, which is a typesetting challenge. 10-3 (phonetic riddle) requires speaking aloud — that is not a book interaction. 10-4 (etymological chain) is a research exercise, not a puzzle. 10-5 (cipher wheel) requires scissors.

Note: 10-1 already subsumes 10-6 (semaphore is one of its ten encodings). If 10-1 is selected, 10-6 is redundant. In that case, promote 10-2.

### 9 — Social Sciences

**Top pick: 9-1 — Logic Grid**

Visual integration: A logic grid is one of the most recognizable puzzle formats in existence. The grid of checkmarks and X's is immediately legible. Solvers know what to do the moment they see it.

Accessibility: Maximum. Logic puzzles appear in every airline magazine. The social science content (governance types, economic models) provides the flavor without gatekeeping the mechanism.

Encyclopedia honor: The scenario requires understanding the concepts to apply the clues. You cannot solve "Nation C has a mixed economy" without knowing what that means. The encyclopedia earns its place.

Durability: Einstein's riddle has been working for over a century.

**Runner-up: 9-3 — Voting Paradox**

Three elections, three systems, three different winners from the same ballots. This is genuinely surprising and teaches something real about voting theory. It is also compact — three small tables fit cleanly on a page. 9-2 (prisoner's dilemma) requires sequential game-theory decisions that are hard to present clearly. 9-4 (market equilibrium) is graph-reading. 9-5 (treaty network) is too abstract for the page.

### 8 — Technology

**Top pick: 8-2 — Logic Gate Circuit**

Visual integration: A circuit diagram with AND/OR/NOT gates is the iconic image of digital technology. It prints beautifully. The solver traces inputs through gates — pencil on page, following wires. This is what the Technology section looks like.

Accessibility: Logic gates are binary. Given inputs, apply rules, get outputs. The rules are simple (AND: both must be 1). A newcomer can learn this in two minutes from the legend.

Encyclopedia honor: The circuit diagram is native to `semiconductor-manufacturing/` and `computer-architecture/`. This is not a metaphor. It is the thing itself.

Durability: Logic gates predate transistors and will outlast them.

**Runner-up: 8-5 — PCB Trace Following**

Following copper traces on a board is visual, spatial, and satisfying. The diagram is intricate but the task is simple: follow the line, collect the letters. 8-1 (signal tracing) is similar but more abstract — "determine each component's effect" is harder to present on a page than "follow this wire." 8-3 (frequency spectrum) is a graph-reading exercise. 8-4 (bit pattern) is a weaker version of K5.

### 7 — Mechanics

**Top pick: 7-1 — Engineering Calculation**

Visual integration: Five mechanical diagrams (lever, pulley, gear train, inclined plane, hydraulic press) — each one a classic technical illustration. This page belongs in an engineering reference. The calculations are beside the diagrams. The layout is textbook-clean.

Accessibility: Simple machines are taught in middle school. The calculations are arithmetic, not calculus. The difficulty is appropriate and the subject matter is universally familiar.

Encyclopedia honor: These are the foundational machines. The section exists to explain them. The puzzle asks you to use them.

Durability: Archimedes would recognize these diagrams.

**Runner-up: 7-3 — Rube Goldberg Chain**

A Rube Goldberg machine drawn across a full page is delightful. It is visually rich, narratively engaging, and rewards careful reading. The brief says "very high fun factor" and it is right. 7-2 (gear train) is a subset of 7-1. 7-4 (bridge analysis) is interesting but tension/compression as binary encoding is a stretch. 7-5 (fluid flow) requires calculations that are harder to present clearly on a page.

### 6 — History & Ideas

**Top pick: 6-1 — Primary Source Detective**

Visual integration: Eight real quotes from real documents, unattributed. The page looks like a curated anthology — elegant and text-forward. No diagrams needed, no grids. Just words on a page, and the challenge of recognizing them.

Accessibility: Everyone has encountered famous historical quotes. "We hold these truths..." is not gatekept. The difficulty gradient comes from mixing well-known sources with obscure ones. A solver can get partial credit.

Encyclopedia honor: This puzzle asks you to know the History section well enough to identify primary sources. That is exactly what historical literacy means.

Durability: Primary sources are permanent.

**Runner-up: 6-3 — Philosophical Argument Chain**

A multi-step argument with valid and invalid steps is a logic exercise wrapped in philosophical content. It prints cleanly (numbered premises, conclusion). The solver must know enough logic and philosophy to identify fallacies — that is genuine engagement with the section. 6-2 (map overlay) requires physical manipulation. 6-4 (cause-and-effect web) is too open-ended. 6-5 (fairy tale logic) is charming but the mythology connection is decorative rather than structural.

### 5 — Life Sciences

**Top pick: 5-1 — Codon Decoding**

Visual integration: DNA → mRNA → codon → amino acid → single-letter code. The page is a cascade of translations, each row feeding the next. A codon table on the page (or referenced in the encyclopedia) is a standard biological figure. This is the cleanest visual pipeline in the pool.

Accessibility: The mechanism is pure lookup. Find the codon in the table, write the amino acid code. No biology degree required — the table does the work. But you learn how the genetic code works by using it.

Encyclopedia honor: This is the genetic code. The solver is doing what a ribosome does. The mechanism is not an analogy. It is the real process, simplified.

Durability: The genetic code is 3.8 billion years old.

**Runner-up: 5-2 — Phylogenetic Tree Traversal**

A tree of life diagram is visually beautiful and immediately readable — trace from leaf to leaf, note the branch points. The tree is one of biology's most iconic images. 5-3 (metabolic pathway) is a strong concept but the pathway names are jargon-heavy on the page. 5-4 (Punnett squares) is clean but the ratio-to-letter mapping is arbitrary. 5-5 (cell labeling) is a classroom exercise. 5-6 (epidemic network) is timely but may age poorly.

### 4 — Material Culture

**Top pick: 4-1 — Element Identification**

Visual integration: Seven riddle-clues, each a short paragraph describing an element's properties. The page reads like a quiz from a materials handbook. Clean, text-forward, inviting.

Accessibility: Elements are universally known. "Soft yellow metal, does not tarnish" — you do not need a chemistry degree. The riddle format is inherently accessible.

Encyclopedia honor: Material Culture is about what things are made of. Identifying the materials by their properties is the core skill of the section.

Durability: Gold is still gold.

**Runner-up: 4-3 — Textile Pattern Binary**

A weaving draft grid is a real textile notation system. Over/under as 1/0 is not an imposed metaphor — it is how weavers actually encode patterns. The grid prints well and the decoding is systematic. 4-2 (craft process ordering) is functional but generic. 4-4 (glaze chemistry) is too specialized. 4-5 (periodic table spelling) has a known constraint problem (many letters have no element symbol). 4-6 (kiln temperature) is graph-reading.

### 3 — Earth & Space

**Top pick: 3-1 — Connect-the-Dots Star Chart**

Visual integration: A coordinate grid with poetic descriptions. The solver plots points and connects them. The finished page is a star chart with letter-shaped constellations drawn by the solver's own hand. This is the "Chicago Fire" moment — the page transforms through interaction.

Accessibility: Plot points on a grid. Connect the dots. This is the most intuitive physical interaction in the entire pool.

Encyclopedia honor: Astronomers identify celestial objects and plot their positions. The solver does the same thing. The puzzle teaches observational astronomy by being observational astronomy.

Durability: The stars are not moving appreciably.

Physical note: Drawing on the page is writing in a book. This is a book interaction. The book changes because you used it. That is different from cutting it apart.

**Runner-up: 3-2 — Geological Cross-Section**

A geological cross-section is a beautiful diagram — layered strata with labels. Hidden words in strata names is a word-search variant that is immediately accessible. 3-3 (river confluence) and 3-5 (tectonic plates) require drawing or cutting. 3-4 (mountain silhouette) is clever but fragile — the letter-shapes must be unambiguous in print, and mountain silhouettes are subtle. 3-6 (sundial) requires sunlight and assembly.

### 2 — Natural World

**Top pick: 2-3 — Leaf / Mushroom Identification Key**

Visual integration: A dichotomous key with specimen illustrations is the most beautiful page format in natural history. Branching paths, detailed drawings, Latin names. It looks like a field guide. A solver traces the key for each specimen, making binary choices at each fork.

Accessibility: "Is the cap convex or flat?" "Are the gills attached or free?" — these are yes/no questions with pictures. No background required. The key teaches you how to look.

Encyclopedia honor: Dichotomous keys are how naturalists actually identify organisms. This is not a puzzle metaphor. It is the real tool.

Durability: Linnaeus published his system in 1735. Still works.

**Runner-up: 2-1 — Organism Identification + Diagonal**

Five organism descriptions with a diagonal read is simple and clean. The page is five short paragraphs with a name grid. It works, though it is less visually distinctive than 2-3. 2-2 (food web) is good but network diagrams can become cluttered at puzzle scale. 2-4 (birdsong as Morse) is creative but spectrograms are hard to read in print — they need color or very high resolution. 2-5 (spice route) requires drawing on a map.

Accessibility: Identifying famous figures from their work is a knowledge challenge, but the encyclopedia provides the answers. The chain structure gives partial confirmation — if you know three of five, you can infer the others.

Encyclopedia honor: The People section is about intellectual lineage. This puzzle is literally a map of who influenced whom. Mechanism and content are the same thing.

Durability: Intellectual history does not change.

**Runner-up: A-4 — Letter Exchange**

Real excerpts from real historical letters. The page is primary source material — fascinating to read regardless of the puzzle. A-2 (portrait gallery) is a simpler version of A-1. A-3 (overlapping lifetimes) is a date-arithmetic exercise. A-5 (invention dependency) is too similar to A-1 in structure (trace a chain).

### X — Cross-Section, Visual, and Physical

This category requires a different lens. Most X-puzzles are physical constructions — they are Black Joker candidates or standalone experiences, not Red Joker book puzzles. I will sort them into three bins.

**Belong in a book:**
- X3 — Fold the Page. The book stays intact. The fold is reversible. This is a book interaction.
- X9 — Mirror Puzzle. You need a mirror, not scissors. The page is intact. Leonardo did it.
- X5 — Fibonacci Structural Sequence. This is a meta-structural principle, not a standalone puzzle. If it shapes the Red Joker's architecture, it is invisible and elegant.

**Do not belong in a book:**
- X1 — Paper + Light. Scissors, flashlight, 3D assembly. This is a kit puzzle.
- X7 — Cipher Wheel. Scissors and a pin. This is a kit puzzle.
- X8 — Punch Card Overlay. Punching holes in the book. No.
- X10 — Tangram Assembly. Scissors. No.
- X11 — Origami Fold. Destroys the page layout.
- X15 — Phenakistoscope. Scissors, spinning, viewing slits. Spectacular but not a book.
- X16 — Sundial. Scissors, sunlight, latitude calibration. Not a book.
- X5 duplicate: X4 (Golden Ratio Spiral) requires a ruler and measurement — borderline.

**Edge cases:**
- X2 — Color Overlay. Requires backlighting. Could work if the pages are thin enough, but you are now dependent on paper stock. Risky.
- X6 — Musical Score. Same as Q3. Gatekeeps non-musicians.
- X13 — River Letters. Same as 3-3. Drawing on a map.
- X17 — Stereo Pair. Requires the solver to cross their eyes. Excludes anyone who cannot do this.

**My top 2 from X:**
1. X3 — Fold the Page. Simple, reversible, surprising.
2. X9 — Mirror Puzzle. Requires one common object. The da Vinci connection is historically grounded.

## Mechanism Diversity Check

A good set of 13 should not repeat mechanism families. Here are the families represented by my top picks:

| Family | Puzzles | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Lookup/decode | K1, 10-1, 5-1 | Use a reference to translate encoded data |
| Spatial/trace | K3, 8-2, 3-1, 5-2 | Follow paths or plot points on a diagram |
| Identify/classify | 6-1, 4-1, 2-3, A-1 | Recognize things from descriptions |
| Logical deduction | 9-1, J1 | Apply reasoning to constraints |
| Visual/spatial trick | Q5 | Physical interaction with the page |
| Calculation | 7-1 | Arithmetic yielding letters |

Six families across 13 puzzles. Three families have 3-4 members each, which is acceptable because the content domains make them feel different (decoding Morse feels nothing like decoding codons, even though both are lookups). No family has more than four. This is a balanced set.

### What this lineup gets right

**Visual range.** A grid of ciphertext. An anamorphic streak drawing. A proof chain. Ten tiny encoding puzzles. A logic grid. A circuit diagram. Five mechanical illustrations. Eight historical quotes. A codon translation cascade. Seven element riddles. A star chart you draw on. A field-guide key with specimen illustrations. Biographical prose. No two pages look the same.

**Accessibility gradient.** A newcomer starts with K (cipher — everyone knows substitution ciphers) or 9 (logic grid — everyone has seen one). They build confidence. By the time they reach J (proof completion) or 10 (multi-cipher), they are ready. No puzzle requires external tools beyond a pencil. No puzzle requires specialized training. Every puzzle gives you what you need or tells you where to find it.

**Encyclopedia integration.** Nine of thirteen puzzles use a mechanism native to their discipline — the genetic code, a dichotomous key, a proof chain, a circuit diagram, a decryption algorithm, a logic grid, simple machines, primary sources, and intellectual lineage. The other four (anamorphic drawing, multi-cipher, star chart, element riddles) use mechanisms closely adjacent to their sections. Nothing feels imported from elsewhere.

**Durability.** Nothing in this lineup depends on current technology, cultural references, or formatting that might change. Ciphers, proofs, codons, elements, stars, logic, circuits, machines, quotes, keys, chains — these are permanent fixtures.

### What I traded away

**7-3 Rube Goldberg** is more fun than 7-1. But fun is a bonus; clarity is a requirement. Five clean mechanical diagrams beat one elaborate chain for page layout.

**Q2 Visual Rebus** is more accessible than Q5. But Q5 is the single best visual moment in the book. One puzzle should make you catch your breath. This is that puzzle.

**2-1 Organism ID** is simpler than 2-3. But the identification key is the most elegant page design in natural history. Simplicity is not always the right optimization.

**6-3 Philosophical Argument** is a strong alternate for 6. But 6-1's real quotes give the page historical weight that a constructed argument cannot match.

### One substitution I would accept

If the answer word NICHE proves too short for the meta mechanism, swap 2-3 for 2-1 (Organism ID + Diagonal, answer NICHE is the same — or adjust to SYMBIOSIS with a different extraction). The identification key is the more beautiful page, but both work.
