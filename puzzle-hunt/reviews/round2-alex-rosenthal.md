# Round 2 — Alex Rosenthal Review
## Ranking the Puzzle Pool: Accessibility, Wonder, and "Wait, Really?"

*Simulated review from the perspective of Alex Rosenthal — Editorial Director of TED-Ed Animations, Head of TED Games, creator of Pandora's Legacy.*

---

## Preamble: What I'm Looking For

I've spent a decade trying to answer one question: **how do you make four billion people care about a puzzle?**

Not four thousand. Not the MIT Mystery Hunt crowd (love you, but you're covered). Four billion — the YouTube audience, the Kickstarter backers, the people who watched a TED-Ed riddle at 2 AM and thought "wait, I can DO that?"

So when I look at 89 puzzle briefs, I'm asking:

1. **The One-Sentence Test** — Can I pitch this in one sentence that makes a non-puzzler say "wait, really?" If I can't, it doesn't matter how clever it is.
2. **The Share Test** — Would someone text this to a friend? Would it work as a TikTok? As a TED-Ed video?
3. **The On-Ramp Test** — Does this expand the community or preach to the choir?
4. **The Wonder Test** — Does solving this create a moment of genuine awe, or just relief?
5. **The Physical Test** (for physical puzzles) — Does this feel like magic or arts and crafts?

Wonder scoring: 1 = technically fine but no one gasps. 5 = this is the thing people tell other people about.

### X — Cross-Section, Visual, and Physical Puzzles

This is where the magic lives — or dies. Physical puzzles in a book are either transcendent or embarrassing. There's almost no middle ground.

**The Magic Test for Physical Puzzles:**
- Magic = "the book just DID something." The solver barely had to think about the physical mechanism; it just happened and was stunning.
- Arts & Crafts = "okay, now I need scissors, tape, a flashlight, and twenty minutes of cutting." The solver spends more time building than discovering.

| ID | Puzzle | One-Sentence Pitch | Wonder | Magic or A&C? | Notes |
|----|--------|-------------------|--------|---------------|-------|
| X1 | Paper + Light (Raiders) | "Build a 3D shape from the book, shine a flashlight through it — shadows spell the answer." | **5** | **MAGIC** (if executed right) / **A&C** (if not) | This is either the single greatest moment in puzzle-book history or an embarrassing craft project. The Raiders of the Lost Ark reference is perfect — the staff, the headpiece, the beam of light revealing the location of the Ark. If the construction is simple (3-4 folds, no glue) and the shadow reveal is clean, this is a 5. If it requires 15 minutes of cutting and a protractor, it's a 2. Execution is EVERYTHING. Put this in the hands of a paper engineer. Budget the money. This is your closer. |

## Final Notes to the Design Team

**What this pool gets right:** The range is extraordinary. 89 briefs across 13+ sections with genuine mechanism variety. The best puzzles (5-1, 2-4, 3-1, 10-1) don't just USE the encyclopedia content — they reveal that the content was ALREADY a puzzle. DNA is a code. Stars form patterns. Weaving is binary. Nature is full of puzzles. That philosophy — "puzzles are everywhere" — is the beating heart of this project.

**What needs work:**
1. **Kill the homework.** Any puzzle whose one-sentence pitch sounds like a class assignment needs to either be radically reframed (wrap it in narrative a la 6-5 Fairy Tale Logic) or cut. The pool has enough A-tier ideas that you don't need to keep the C-tier ones.
2. **Physical puzzles need a paper engineer.** X1 (Paper + Light) and X15 (Phenakistoscope) are either transcendent or terrible depending entirely on construction simplicity. Budget for a professional paper engineer to prototype these. Do not ship them based on concept alone.
3. **Deduplicate draw-on-a-map.** You have 3-1 (star chart), 3-3 (river confluence), 2-5 (spice route), and X13 (river letters) all using "plot points on a map, connect, see letters." Pick one. It's 3-1.
4. **The Multi-Cipher (10-1) is the thesis puzzle.** It's the one that says "this encyclopedia teaches you TEN different encoding systems." It should be either the first puzzle or the most prominent. It's your billboard.
5. **Narrative framing matters more than mechanism.** Compare 6-3 (Philosophical Argument Chain — spot the fallacies) with 6-5 (Fairy Tale Logic — cross three bridges). Same difficulty. Same domain. But 6-5 is a STORY and 6-3 is an EXERCISE. Always choose the story.

The library is building something remarkable. These aren't puzzles bolted onto an encyclopedia — the encyclopedia IS the puzzle. That's the insight. That's the TED Talk.

*"The most powerful puzzles aren't the ones that test what you know. They're the ones that change what you notice."*
