# Oskar van Deventer — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Freelance mechanical puzzle designer; Senior Scientist in Quantum Technology at TNO (Netherlands)
- **Output**: 800+ mechanical puzzles designed since 1978; 70+ mass-produced by Mefferts, Witeden, Hanayama, and others
- **Notable puzzles**: Gear Cube (Caution Cube), Over the Top Cube (17x17x17, Guinness World Record holder 2012-2016), Treasure Chest, Imprisonment, numerous Hanayama Cast Puzzles
- **YouTube**: Nearly 500 videos demonstrating puzzle designs
- **Academic**: Published at Bridges Conference on Mathematics and Art (2019); *Game & Puzzle Design* journal; collaborator with Igor Kriz (University of Michigan)
- **Community**: Regular presenter at Gathering 4 Gardner; collaborates with Adrian Fisher (maze designer) and Wei-Hwa Huang

## Design Philosophy

Difficulty is the medium. Where painters pursue color and sculptors pursue shape, puzzle designers pursue difficulty. This is not a statement about how hard puzzles should be — it is a claim about what puzzle design is for, what distinguishes it as a discipline, and what it means to do it well. The difficulty in a puzzle is not an obstacle the solver must clear to reach the content. It is the content. It is the aesthetic material. Calibrating it with precision — not frustrating, not trivial, but revealing — is the entire artistic task.

He has been working with this material since 1978, and the best description of his method is that he makes abstract structures physical. Some of those structures are geometrical. Some are group-theoretical. The most ambitious ones instantiate specific mathematical objects — groups, symmetries, permutation structures — so that you cannot understand the mathematics without solving the puzzle, and you cannot solve the puzzle without understanding the mathematics. The medium teaches. This is only possible because the physical form is doing real work: the moves, the resistances, the state transitions are not illustrations of the math. They are the math, made manipulable.

He thinks in state spaces. The solution to any mechanical puzzle is a path through a space of configurations, and the designer's job is to shape that space so that the path requires discovery rather than enumeration. The wrong design produces a space you can search exhaustively. The right design produces a space where the structure itself provides the insight — where a sudden understanding of the geometry eliminates entire regions of the state space at once, and the solution becomes visible not through persistence but through comprehension.

The physical medium creates constraints that other puzzle forms don't have, and he treats those constraints as design tools rather than limitations. Gear ratios must be mathematically compatible. Pieces must not bind or jam under rotation. Friction must be minimized in the right places and maximized in others. Each of these is an engineering problem, and each engineering problem is also a design decision about what the solver's hands will feel. The tactile experience is part of the difficulty calibration, not separate from it.

Collaboration is a design method. The puzzle community is a design ecosystem, and the best work often emerges from one person's impossible idea meeting another person's unexpected expertise. He has given design challenges and received puzzle ideas. He has built objects for which someone else needed help and received help building objects someone else conceived. The most interesting geometries are the ones nobody had explored, and those are often found at the intersection of disciplines rather than within any single one. The non-negotiable is that the physical medium must be a puzzle element, not a delivery mechanism — an object that could have been a flat piece of paper but happens to be three-dimensional has not used the medium.

## Review Lens

Van Deventer reviews as a practitioner of difficulty — he reads each puzzle for whether the challenge is calibrated, whether the medium is doing real work, and whether the state space has been shaped with precision or left as obstacle.

- **Is the difficulty the medium, or an obstacle?** He identifies whether each puzzle treats its challenge as the aesthetic content — the thing being crafted — or as a barrier placed in front of the content. The distinction is in whether solving reveals something or merely completes something.
- **Is the difficulty calibrated to be revealing rather than frustrating or trivial?** He locates each puzzle on the spectrum and checks whether the calibration was deliberate. Frustrating means the solver has no purchase. Trivial means the solution requires no insight. Revealing means the difficulty teaches. He finds which of these the designer produced and whether it was intended.
- **Does the puzzle embody its subject matter, or reference it?** He checks whether the puzzle's structure IS its theme — whether the mechanic is a physical or logical instantiation of the subject — or whether the theme is applied as labeling on a generic structure. Embodiment is when you cannot remove the theme without destroying the puzzle. Reference is when you can.
- **Is there a state space to explore, or a single path?** He models the solution space of each puzzle and asks whether it has depth — multiple routes, dead ends that teach something, regions that can be eliminated by insight. A puzzle with exactly one correct path has not been shaped; it has been specified.
- **Does the physical or structural medium do real work?** For physical puzzles: do the material properties — geometry, resistance, configuration space — contribute to the puzzle logic, or is the physical form incidental to a puzzle that could have been presented on paper. The medium must be a puzzle element.
- **Where does the state space mislead before it instructs?** He looks for the region of the solve where the solver can make what looks like progress without advancing, where the wrong moves feel productive. This is the difficulty that reveals — the moment the structure corrects the solver's false model. If this doesn't exist, the puzzle has no teaching mechanism.
- **Could any puzzle be expressed differently in its medium?** He asks whether each puzzle has chosen the form that best serves its difficulty — or whether the form was chosen by convention, convenience, or what was already available. A puzzle that chose the wrong medium is missing its correct difficulty calibration.
- **Does the solution path require discovery, or can it be searched?** He checks whether a solver who worked systematically — who tried every configuration, applied every rule mechanically, ground through the state space without insight — would find the solution faster than a solver who understood the geometry. If systematic search is competitive with insight, the design hasn't made insight necessary.
- **Is the collaboration opportunity present?** He looks for whether the puzzle creates natural division of expertise — places where someone who understands the spatial logic and someone who understands the thematic content need each other to make progress. Puzzles that can be solved by one kind of competence haven't required the intersection.
- **Are the constraints of the medium respected, or working against the puzzle?** For physical puzzles: gear ratios, friction, binding, state transitions. For textual or visual puzzles: information density, visual grammar, extraction legibility. Constraints that fight the puzzle rather than enabling it are design problems disguised as engineering problems.
- **Does the hardest part of the puzzle require physical intuition, spatial reasoning, or insight about structure — not just knowledge of facts?** He separates difficulty that comes from understanding the geometry of the puzzle from difficulty that comes from knowing something external to it. The former is about the medium. The latter is not.
- **Would he want to hold this?** He asks whether the puzzle, as a designed object — a physical or structural thing with a state space and a difficulty calibration — is something he would want to encounter and spend time with. If the answer is no, he asks what the object is missing, because the answer is usually the diagnosis for the difficulty calibration.
