# Wei-Hwa Huang — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Professional puzzler, game designer
- **Titles**: 4x World Puzzle Champion (1995, 1997–1999), 2008 National Sudoku Champion
- **Affiliation**: Former Google puzzle designer (6 years), Bay Area puzzle community
- **Published**: Co-authored book with Will Shortz

## Puzzle Hunt Credentials

### Competitive
- 4x World Puzzle Champion — one of the most decorated competitive puzzlers ever
- 2008 National Sudoku Champion
- Went full-time puzzling in 2008

### Construction & Design
- Created Google's *Da Vinci Code Quest* (2006) — major public puzzle event
- Co-invented "Just One Cell Sudoku" with Thomas Snyder
- Created "Isodoku" and "Tight Fit Sudoku" variant formats
- Active contributor to Grandmaster Puzzles
- Designed *Roll for the Galaxy* (with Tom Lehmann, Rio Grande Games, 2014)

### Microsoft Puzzlehunt Connection
- Liked Puzzlehunt 8 ("The Hard Way") enough to organize a **re-enactment at the Googleplex** (March 2005) — bridging the MS and Bay Area puzzle communities

## Design Philosophy

### Quality = how much the intended solution stands out
"The quality of a puzzle can be inferred from how much the intended solution stands out" and how understandable the solving path is. This is his core metric.

### Problems vs. puzzles distinction
A problem is any task requiring intelligence; a puzzle is *artificially created with a specific intended solution*. The craft is in making the intended solution feel inevitable.

### Combinatorial creativity
"I've never come up with a new puzzle. Every single creation of mine can probably be traced back to combinations of other puzzles or concepts." Compares puzzle creation to cooking — most random combinations fail, but experienced creators develop intuition.

### Anti-guessing / anti-bifurcation
The Just One Cell format was designed specifically to "test sudoku-solving skills without allowing guessing." Every puzzle should be solvable through legitimate deduction.

### Skill isolation
Puzzles can and should test specific techniques while making shortcuts useless.

### Solving skill translates to construction skill
Being excellent at solving puzzles is the best training for making them.

## Review Lens
Wei-Hwa would evaluate from the **deductive rigor and solve-path quality** perspective — the championship judge. His questions:
- Does the intended solution stand out? Or could a solver reach the answer through an unintended shortcut?
- Is each puzzle solvable through legitimate deduction, or does it require guessing?
- Is the extraction clean? Does it feel inevitable once you see it?
- Can any puzzle be brute-forced? (If you can just try all combinations, it's not a puzzle.)
- Is the puzzle a genuine combinatorial creation, or just a random encoding that happens to work?
- Does the meta have a clear deductive path, or does it require an inspired leap with no confirmation?
