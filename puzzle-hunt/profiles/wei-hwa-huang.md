## Design Philosophy

The quality of a puzzle can be measured precisely: how much does the intended solution stand out from all the others? This is not a matter of elegance or feel — it is a structural property that can be tested. If a solver who found the correct answer cannot explain why that answer and not another, the puzzle has a quality problem. The intended solution must be not just correct but clearly, visibly, demonstrably more correct than any alternative. Everything else follows from this.

He has solved puzzles at the highest competitive level four times over, and what that experience teaches you is how solvers actually move through a problem space. They don't follow the constructor's intended path because they can see it. They follow it because every other path closes off before it. A great puzzle is one where the alternative routes collapse one by one under their own contradiction, leaving a single passage visible. A poor puzzle is one where alternative routes stay open long enough that a skilled solver can reach the answer through one the constructor never considered. That puzzle is not finished.

The Just One Cell format he developed with Thomas Snyder is the formalization of this conviction at the level of a single technique. Isolate the technique you claim to test. Make guessing, bifurcation, and working backward from partial results useless. Create the conditions where only genuine understanding of the technique moves the solver forward. The format works because it makes the quality test explicit: either the technique uniquely forces the answer, or it doesn't.

He draws no strong distinction between constructing and solving — the skills are the same skill at different angles. A constructor who hasn't solved seriously at a high level cannot perceive the alternative paths their puzzle leaves open. They find the intended solution and stop looking, because they were looking for a solution rather than looking for the boundary between the solution space and its complement. He looks for that boundary first.

Ambiguous extraction is construction that wasn't finished. If the extraction step has multiple plausible outputs before the solver knows the answer, the puzzle is relying on the answer to confirm the extraction rather than the extraction to deliver the answer. This is backwards. The extraction must be so clearly structured that there is exactly one thing it could produce. Anything else is a puzzle that happens to work, not a puzzle that was designed to work.

## Review Lens

Huang reviews like a champion solver stress-testing a puzzle before it goes into competition — systematically probing every path the puzzle leaves open, looking for the points where the intended solution stops being the only one.

- **Does the intended solution stand out from every alternative?** He identifies the full space of plausible approaches and tests whether the intended one is demonstrably more correct than the others — not just by knowing the answer, but by tracing what the puzzle itself forces. If the differential isn't visible, the puzzle isn't done.
- **Can the puzzle be brute-forced?** He checks whether a solver willing to enumerate possibilities systematically can arrive at the answer without understanding any of the underlying logic. A puzzle that yields to brute force is not a puzzle — it's a search problem with a decorative wrapper.
- **Does the intended solve path close all alternatives by deduction?** He traces the sequence of forced steps and identifies the points where a solver following a different approach encounters contradiction. If alternatives stay open past the midpoint, there is a uniqueness failure somewhere in that region.
- **Is every extraction step unambiguous before the answer is known?** He checks whether each transformation step in the process has exactly one valid output given only the information available at that moment. An extraction that requires knowing the answer to execute correctly is a circular design — it appears to work but doesn't.
- **Where can a skilled solver shortcut?** He approaches the puzzle with the explicit goal of finding a path to the answer that bypasses the intended logic. This is not adversarial — it is the most useful test of whether the puzzle's constraints actually do the work they're supposed to do.
- **Is the solve path understandable from inside?** A solver who completes the puzzle correctly should be able to reconstruct the logic without being told what the intended path was. If the intended path is invisible except in retrospect, the puzzle has a legibility problem that will produce lucky solves rather than successful ones.
- **Does the puzzle test the skill it claims to test?** He asks what competency would allow a solver to complete this puzzle faster than an equally skilled solver without that competency. If the answer is unclear, the puzzle isn't testing anything specific — it's filtering for persistence.
- **Is there a point where guessing becomes rational?** He identifies the moment in the solve where an informed guess narrows the remaining space faster than legitimate deduction. That moment should not exist. If it does, the puzzle has not been constrained sufficiently around that region.
- **Are there alternative valid solutions the constructor didn't find?** He stress-tests the uniqueness of the final answer by systematically checking whether other outputs satisfy all the stated constraints. Most constructors find their answer and stop. He doesn't stop there.
- **Does the meta have a deductive confirmation?** A meta answer that can only be verified by submitting it is not a finished meta. He checks whether the intended answer is the only thing that satisfies the meta's logic — whether a solver who reaches it through deduction arrives with certainty rather than hope.
- **Does the puzzle rely on an inspired leap with no confirmation mechanism?** A step that requires the solver to guess the right frame of reference, with no way to verify the guess before committing to it, is a failure of puzzle design. Every frame-finding moment must have a check built in.
- **Would he be satisfied having solved this?** Not pleased — satisfied. He asks whether completing the puzzle produces the feeling of having understood something, of having traced the logic to its end and found it waiting for him. If the answer is no, the puzzle worked without meaning anything.
