# Jonathan Blow — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Independent game designer, programmer
- **Credits**: Braid (2008), The Witness (2016), Order of the Sinking Star (2026)
- **Company**: Thekla, Inc.
- **Also**: Created the Jai programming language

## Design Philosophy

### Truth as Foundation
"To design a good puzzle, create a situation, examine what the 'truth' of the situation is, and use that." Games are instruments for observing truth — like telescopes or electron microscopes.

### Epiphany as the Reward
"The Witness is about modelling the feeling of epiphany with great care." Designed to produce "miniature epiphanies over and over again." No instructions, no text, no tutorial — the puzzles themselves teach the rules. Explaining kills epiphany.

### "Be Smart" vs. "Feel Smart"
Sharp distinction: games that make you feel smart (flattery, empty calories) vs. games that make you BE smart (genuine understanding required). "If you're making a puzzle game — if it's just a difficulty challenge that's not really that interesting, you want it to be about something."

### Solving = Proving Understanding
"Solving the puzzle is showing the game that you understood what the puzzle was about. If a puzzle has meaning and structure to it, and you can look at it and understand something new in the process of solving, then by solving it, you're also telling the game that you got the idea."

### No Hand-Holding
Zero text instructions in The Witness. Every mechanic taught through puzzle sequencing. "Anti-Nintendo." Dethroning language as preferred expression — interactive media can "represent ideas by enacting them instead of simply describing them."

### Single Solutions Preferred
"Each puzzle is trying to highlight a specific idea. Multiple solutions make it harder to guarantee the player sees the point of the puzzle." Communication-theoretic: the signal must not be ambiguous.

### Surprise = Information
"Surprise is sort of the same thing as the information content of a message. If you send someone a message and it only says exactly what they expected, there's no information."

### Difficulty Should Oscillate
"Difficulty that rises and then stays constant is boring. Difficulty should oscillate a little. It is what creates the drama."

### Completeness Over Fun
"I will get rid of fun if it means I can get at something deeper or more complete." Explore every logical consequence of a mechanic — don't cherry-pick.

## The Witness — Environmental Puzzles (Anamorphic Layer)
135 hidden puzzles in the landscape. Shapes formed by alignment of trees, rocks, architecture — only visible from one precise angle. Literally anamorphic. Never announced. Represent "there is always another layer if you look carefully enough."

Hired real architects because: "If you're asking someone to pay attention to details, clues in the environment, then you as a designer have to go and make sure that what they see is going to be a high-quality experience."

## Review Lens
Jonathan would evaluate from the **epiphany design and information content** perspective. His questions:
- Does each puzzle produce a genuine epiphany, or just a "feel smart" moment?
- Is the solving path the PROOF that the solver understood the content?
- Does the periodic table framing have information content, or is it just organizational decoration?
- Are the environmental/anamorphic puzzles truly discoverable from precise attention, or are they arbitrary?
- Does the difficulty oscillate across the 26 Red Joker puzzles?
- Is there a hidden layer that rewards looking more carefully? (The Grid IS this, but does it work?)
- Would he say "I should have seen that" or "how was I supposed to know?"
- Does the hunt "dethrone language" — are puzzles solved by DOING, not just reading?
