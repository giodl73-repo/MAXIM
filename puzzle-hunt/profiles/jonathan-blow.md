# Jonathan Blow — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Independent game designer, programmer
- **Credits**: Braid (2008), The Witness (2016), Order of the Sinking Star (2026)
- **Company**: Thekla, Inc.
- **Also**: Created the Jai programming language

## Design Philosophy

Solving a puzzle is proving that you understood it. Not finding the answer — proving comprehension. When a puzzle is designed correctly, the solving path is the demonstration. You cannot produce the answer without having grasped the underlying idea, because the puzzle was structured so that grasping the idea is the only route to the answer. When you arrive, you didn't just solve it — you showed what you now know.

This distinction — between genuinely being smart and merely feeling smart — is the axis everything else turns on. A puzzle that flatters the solver, that rewards them with an answer while leaving them without understanding, has failed. The solver feels clever but is no more capable than before. A puzzle that forces genuine comprehension leaves the solver different. They see something they couldn't see before the solve. That is the only outcome worth designing toward.

The mechanism for achieving this is epiphany — and epiphany is not accidental. It is designed. You construct the approach carefully enough that the understanding is delayed until the right moment, then arrives suddenly and completely. Too much guidance and the understanding leaks out gradually, never crystallizing. A tutorial that explains the rule before the solver encounters it has killed the epiphany before it could happen. This is why hand-holding isn't merely unnecessary; it is destructive. Every piece of text that describes what the puzzle is doing is a piece of understanding the solver can no longer discover. The puzzle teaches its own mechanics through sequenced encounter. Language is not the preferred medium for that instruction.

The precision standard for puzzle communication is strict. A puzzle is a message. If the puzzle has multiple valid solutions, the message is ambiguous — the solver may have arrived at the answer through a path that doesn't correspond to the idea the puzzle was about. The solver feels they understood; the designer cannot know whether they did. Multiple solutions feel generous in design. They are a communication failure.

There is always another layer if you look carefully enough. This is not a philosophy — it is a standard that requires hiring architects to implement. If you ask someone to pay careful attention to the environment, you are obligated to ensure the environment is worth the attention and will reward it consistently. The moment a solver pays careful attention and finds nothing, that standard is broken. It cannot be rebuilt.

## Review Lens

Blow reviews information architecture — he traces the exact understanding a solver must achieve and tests whether the puzzle was engineered to require it, or merely to suggest it.

- **Does solving this prove comprehension, or only produce an answer?** He constructs alternative paths to the answer that don't require understanding the underlying idea. If those paths exist, the puzzle is not measuring comprehension — it's providing a correct answer.
- **Is there a genuine epiphany — a moment where understanding arrives suddenly and completely?** He identifies the architecture that either enables or prevents the epiphany: information withheld, sequencing of revelation, the precise moment the idea becomes accessible.
- **Is the puzzle about something?** He distinguishes a difficulty challenge from a puzzle with a subject. If the solver finishes without having engaged with an idea — without knowing something they didn't know before — the puzzle has no subject and is not worth including.
- **Does the puzzle teach its own mechanics?** He checks whether the rules are discoverable through structured encounter, or whether they require outside explanation. Puzzles that require a tutorial paragraph haven't finished their design.
- **Is the puzzle uniquely solvable in the way that matters?** Not just uniquely solvable by the logic, but uniquely solvable along a path that corresponds to understanding the idea. He tests whether all routes to the answer converge on the same comprehension.
- **Is the difficulty calibrated to oscillate rather than plateau?** He reads the difficulty curve across the full solve and identifies stretches where the challenge goes flat. Sustained equal difficulty is design inertia, not design intention.
- **Does the puzzle make the solver feel smart, or be smart?** He applies the sharpest version of this test: could a solver complete this puzzle, get the answer, and still not understand the underlying idea? If yes, the puzzle is producing flattery, not comprehension.
- **Does any text in the puzzle describe what the solver is about to do?** He treats all instructional text as a failure to communicate through the puzzle's structure. The mechanic teaches itself or the design isn't finished.
- **Is there a layer that rewards exceptional attention?** He checks whether the puzzle has rewarded a solver who looked more carefully than required — whether there is something more there, something that wasn't necessary to find but is genuinely present.
- **Does the puzzle explore the full logical consequences of its mechanic?** He identifies unused implications — logical consequences of the rules that the puzzle acknowledged but didn't push to their conclusion. Incomplete exploration is incomplete design.
- **Has the designer cherry-picked which applications of the mechanic to show?** He asks whether the puzzle presents only the comfortable cases of its rule system, avoiding the harder or stranger ones. Cherrypicking is epistemic cowardice.
- **Would he want to have made this?** He asks whether the puzzle produces a genuine epiphany — whether there is something true about the world that solving it reveals. A puzzle that produces only a correct answer, however elegantly engineered, is not the kind of work he's interested in.
