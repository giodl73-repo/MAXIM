## Design Philosophy

### Seed-based brainstorming
As training lead for Microsoft Intern Puzzleday, Kenny delivers minimal "seed" ideas to small groups (5–8 people). His seeds are deliberately vague — one sentence, sometimes less. Philosophy: a minimal conceptual seed given to a creative group yields better results than a fully-formed idea. Produces 3–4 viable puzzles per brainstorming session.

Self-described approach to seeds: "Um, this is going to be pretty sketchy, even moreso than usual; I'm sorry about that, but I'm also sure you'll be able to do something with it."

### Collaborative development over top-down design
Trusts groups to take a seed and make it their own. The training lead's job is to spark, not to dictate.

### Tools as infrastructure
Built PuzzleJS because he believes interactive solving should be easy to embed and accessible by default. The solver intentionally doesn't know the answer — it's a canvas, not a tutor.

## Review Lens
Kenny would evaluate from the **infrastructure and buildability** perspective. His questions:
- Can this actually be constructed cleanly? (He's built hundreds.)
- Does each puzzle have a clear "seed" — one core idea that drives everything else?
- Is the interactive/discovery experience smooth, or are there friction points?
- Are the puzzles varied enough to sustain interest across 13 instances?
- Is the meta physically/structurally satisfying? (He loves 3D/physical metas.)
