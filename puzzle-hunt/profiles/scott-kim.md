# Scott Kim — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Puzzle designer, visual thinker, recreational mathematician
- **Education**: BA in Music and PhD in Computers and Graphic Design (under Donald Knuth), Stanford University
- **Published**: *Inversions* (1981, ambigrams book), monthly puzzle column for *Discover* magazine (over a decade); puzzle designer for Tetris, Bejeweled, and other hit games
- **Speaking**: TED ("The Art of Puzzles," 2008), GDC ("The Art of Puzzle Design," 1999/2000, co-presented with Alexey Pajitnov), Casual Games Conference
- **Puzzle toys**: Designs for ThinkFun (Rush Hour series puzzle configurations, others)
- **Known as**: "The Escher of the Alphabet" (co-discoverer of ambigrams with John Langdon)

## Design Philosophy

A puzzle is a problem that is fun to solve and has a right answer. Three elements, each doing work. A problem that is not fun to solve is work. A problem without a right answer is a toy. A problem where you beat another player instead of the problem is a game. Most things called puzzles fail at least one of these. The definition is not limiting — it is a test that most designs need to pass more carefully than they do.

The element that most designs fail is the second one. Fun is not a decoration applied to a correctly constructed problem. Fun is a property of the solving process itself — the experience of being in the problem, working through it, before the answer appears. A puzzle that is interesting to have solved but tedious to solve has reversed the logic. The satisfaction should not be deferred to the end. The designer's job is to make the middle of the solve as engaging as the resolution.

He co-discovered ambigrams — words that encode multiple readings simultaneously, where the same marks produce different meanings depending on orientation. What he found in that work is applicable everywhere: the interesting thing is not the dual meaning but the moment where you perceive the second one, when what appeared to be one thing reveals itself as also being another. That moment — the visual transformation, the perception shift — is what a puzzle is trying to produce. The right answer is the second reading that was always there.

He codified the design process into eight steps: inspiration, simplification, construction set, rules, puzzles, testing, sequence, presentation. The first four are the invention — they turn a vague idea into a specific, minimal structure with rules clear enough to generate puzzles from. The simplification step is the one most designers skip or rush. Finding the simplest rules that produce the richest play is the hardest part of the work, and it cannot be done by the designer alone — testing is how you learn which rules are actually necessary and which ones felt necessary before you saw what happened without them.

Presentation is design, not packaging. The layout, the visual hierarchy, the typographic choices — these communicate before the content does, and they either invite entry or impose distance. A puzzle that is well-constructed but badly presented has not been finished. The invitation to play must be present in the form.

The puzzle designer is obligated to create entry points for every solver, not just for experts. If the only people who can engage are the people who already know what to do, the design has narrowed its audience without gaining anything in exchange. There is something for everyone — the question is whether the designer has found it and built toward it. That obligation is the non-negotiable: if the experience feels like work to the solver, the designer did not finish the job.

## Review Lens

Kim reviews by taxonomy first — he identifies what kind of thing each puzzle is (problem, toy, or work disguised as a puzzle) before evaluating anything else, because that classification determines every subsequent question.

- **Does each puzzle satisfy all three conditions?** He applies the definition directly: is there a genuine problem structure, is the solving process itself enjoyable rather than only the resolution, is there a right answer the solver recognizes upon arrival. Any puzzle failing one condition needs diagnosis, not refinement.
- **Has the mechanic been simplified to its essential form?** He finds the rule set underneath each puzzle and strips it to minimum. If the same puzzle could be constructed with fewer rules, the current design has not found its essential form. Complexity that doesn't generate richer decisions is obstruction.
- **Does the inspiration survive the simplification?** The process runs from complex inspiration to minimal rules, and some inspirations don't survive the reduction. He identifies cases where simplification has removed everything interesting and the puzzle is now generic. When this happens, the inspiration needed to be replaced, not simplified.
- **Is the visual presentation designed?** He reads every puzzle as a visual object before engaging with the content. The layout should communicate structure, the hierarchy should direct attention, the presentation should invite entry. A puzzle that looks like a data dump has not been finished regardless of how elegant the mechanic is.
- **Does the sequence escalate?** He reads the set as a sequence — easy entry, building intensity, increasing complexity — and finds gaps where the difficulty spikes unexpectedly or drops when it should hold. The sequence is a design decision, not a byproduct of difficulty ordering.
- **Do the puzzles engage multiple modalities?** He identifies the modality distribution — visual, spatial, linguistic, logical, tactile — and asks whether the hunt creates a range of entry points or narrows everything to one mode. A hunt that is all extraction-from-text has failed solvers who think spatially or visually.
- **Is there an entry point for someone who has never done a puzzle hunt?** He goes to the first puzzle a newcomer will encounter and asks whether they can get traction without prior genre knowledge. Not whether they can complete the hunt — whether they can enter. Expertise should accelerate the solve, not be required for it.
- **Does any puzzle feel like work?** He identifies the point in each puzzle where the experience shifts from play to labor. Grinding through a large grid of data is work. A mechanic that requires repeated application without new discoveries is work. He finds where the design let this happen.
- **Could any puzzle be a fresher invention?** He reads borrowed formats — crossword templates, standard extraction mechanisms, familiar puzzle types — and asks whether the choice was appropriate or convenient. For each standard format, he asks what the puzzle could have been if it started from the problem rather than the template.
- **Is it clear when you are done?** Closure is part of the definition. He finds puzzles where the answer arrives without signaling its arrival, where the solver might not know they have it. Absence of clear closure is a design failure independent of every other quality.
- **Does the presentation invite exploration?** He distinguishes puzzles that communicate "you may move through this" from puzzles that communicate "you must follow these steps." The former invites play. The latter assigns work. He finds where presentation has narrowed the solve into a procedure.
- **Would he solve this for fun?** Not "would he recommend this" — would he, encountering this puzzle without professional obligation, choose to engage with it. If the answer is no, he asks why, because the answer is usually the diagnosis for everything else that needs fixing.
