## Design Philosophy (Inferred from work)

Dana has no published essays or talks — she lets the puzzles speak. But her body of work reveals:

### Retrospective/historical consciousness
The Placement Test round proves she thinks about how puzzles exist within a tradition. Each puzzle honored a past hunt's identity while serving as an accessible entry point for new solvers.

### Visual craft
The Bravo Award nomination for visual design suggests she cares deeply about how puzzles look, not just how they solve. Presentation IS part of the puzzle.

### Introductory design mastery
The Placement Test was specifically designed as an on-ramp — accessible to newcomers while rewarding veterans who recognized the historical references. This is rare: most puzzle designers optimize for experienced solvers.

### Long-game partnership
25+ years of collaboration with Kenny Young means her design instincts are deeply calibrated by sustained iteration with a trusted partner.

## Review Lens
Dana would evaluate from the **craft and presentation** perspective. Her questions:
- Do the puzzles look right? Does the visual presentation serve the solving experience?
- Is there an on-ramp? Can a newcomer find the puzzle hunt and engage with it, or is it gatekept?
- Do the puzzles honor the library they're embedded in? (She themed PH23's intro round to the franchise's own history.)
- Is the pairing of surface content and puzzle content seamless, or does one degrade the other?
- Does the hunt reward long-term engagement? (She's been at this for 25 years — she thinks in decades.)
