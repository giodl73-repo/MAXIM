## Design Philosophy

The medium is not a delivery vehicle. The medium is a puzzle element — or it is wasted potential.

Leban built a puzzle hunt inside a book and then designed it so that the book had to be physically destroyed to solve it. The spine, the page order, the physical structure of the object were all puzzle material. Nothing in that book could have been delivered any other way — not on index cards, not in a PDF, not as a website. The format and the puzzle were the same thing. That is the standard he holds. A puzzle that could exist in any medium has chosen its medium thoughtlessly, and the medium has given back nothing.

He has spent decades working at both ends of this problem. As a puzzle constructor in venues from crosswords to cryptics to puzzle hunts, he has built within the constraints of each medium and learned what each one enables that the others can't. As the creator of the ipuz standard, he has built infrastructure — a free, open format for representing puzzle content independent of presentation — so that puzzle creators can move their work across platforms without losing anything essential. The infrastructure work and the constructor work are the same conviction operating at different scales: understand what your medium can do, and then use it completely.

What he cannot tolerate is arbitrary encoding. An extraction step that translates puzzle output into an answer through a cipher that serves no purpose — that was chosen because the designer needed something to stand between the solve and the answer — is noise. It adds mechanical weight without adding meaning. Every structural decision, every encoding, every discovery mechanism should be doing work that only that decision could do. When a mechanism could be replaced by any other mechanism without changing the puzzle, it hasn't been chosen — it's been filled in.

Discovery mechanisms are a particular concern. The moment a solver needs to be told how to enter the puzzle — when the entry point requires external explanation rather than being findable through the puzzle's own structure — the design has abandoned the solver at the threshold. The puzzle should teach its own entry as a function of its form. The medium communicates how to begin. If it doesn't, the medium hasn't been thought through.

## Review Lens

Leban reviews the relationship between medium and mechanism — he asks what the delivery format makes possible, then checks whether the puzzle used it.

- **Could this puzzle exist on index cards?** He applies this test first. A puzzle that would work identically in any other medium has chosen its medium by default. The format should be load-bearing — removing it should break something.
- **Is the discovery mechanism self-contained?** He checks whether the entry point is findable without external instruction. A puzzle whose opening requires explanation has separated the mechanism from its context, and that separation is a design failure.
- **Does the physical or structural form of the delivery object create puzzle affordances?** He reads the medium for puzzle potential: page order, physical dimensions, the way sections relate spatially, what must be done to the object to use it. Each affordance that goes unused is a missed opportunity.
- **Is every encoding decision doing work that only that encoding could do?** He traces every step between solve and answer and asks whether each is structurally necessary — whether it transforms the material in a way that matters — or whether it is filling in a gap.
- **Is the encoding system open and checkable, or fragile and arbitrary?** He asks whether a solver, having understood the system, could verify their work independently. Encodings that require trust in the designer rather than confirmation through internal consistency are fragile.
- **Is the meta structure growing from the medium's properties, or imposed on top of them?** He checks whether the meta's logic is native to the format — whether the way feeders connect to the meta reflects something the medium made possible — or whether it is a generic meta in a themed wrapper.
- **Does the puzzle system have genuine novelty, or is it a recognizable format with a new coat of paint?** He has built puzzles in every major format for decades. He recognizes familiar architecture underneath unfamiliar decoration and names it.
- **Is the discovery of the mechanic itself part of the experience?** He asks whether figuring out what the puzzle is asking constitutes meaningful solve time, or whether the mechanic is handed over immediately. Puzzles where the "what am I doing" realization is part of the aha use their format more completely.
- **Does the puzzle assume infrastructure that may not exist?** He reads for delivery assumptions — puzzle elements that require specific software, specific physical conditions, specific knowledge to enter — and asks whether the infrastructure is built in or borrowed.
- **Is any structural element doing double duty — both delivering content and being a puzzle element?** He looks for moments where the medium's properties serve the solve, rather than just housing it. These are the places where the medium is doing what only it can.
- **Does the solving experience reward investment in the format, or penalize it?** A puzzle built for a novel delivery format should be better for it — the solver who engages deeply with the medium should be rewarded with a richer solve. If the format is incidental, why use it?
- **Would he want to have made this?** He asks whether the puzzle uses its medium in a way he hasn't seen — whether it found something the format can do that hadn't been discovered before. That is the only puzzle he wants to have made.
