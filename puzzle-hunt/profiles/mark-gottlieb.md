37 puzzles, all Elvis. This is the systems-engineering/creative-writing MIT double major in full flight: a unified thematic architecture applied with absolute comedic commitment across an entire hunt.

## Design Philosophy

A puzzle system is only as good as its internal consistency. One exception and the system isn't a system — it's a collection of rules plus a special case, and special cases propagate. Once a solver discovers that some instances of the rule work one way and others work differently, they can no longer trust the rule. They stop solving and start auditing. The puzzle has converted itself into a reliability problem, and reliability problems don't have elegant solutions.

He knows this at two scales simultaneously. As the longest-serving Rules Manager in Magic: The Gathering history, he maintained consistency across thousands of cards — ensuring that any two-card interaction produces a fair, predictable result, not because the individual cards are simple, but because the rules framework is genuinely coherent. The Rules Comprehensive document exists to close every gap, to answer every edge case with the same underlying logic rather than ad hoc rulings. The moment you start making exceptions, you've lost the architecture. He spent nearly two decades not making exceptions.

The 1997 MIT Mystery Hunt he constructed operates on the same logic. Thirty-seven puzzles. Every puzzle titled as a tabloid headline about Elvis Presley. Not thirty-five with two that didn't fit the format. Not thirty-seven with one that bent the rule slightly. Thirty-seven, complete commitment, no exceptions. This is not rigidity — it is the understanding that a theme either holds universally or it doesn't hold. The commitment is what makes the joke land. The commitment is what makes the system work. The one exception would have been the loudest element in the hunt.

He wrote his MIT senior thesis on puzzle hunt design theory when he was 22, treating it as a field with analyzable structure rather than a collection of successful practices. This framing matters: if puzzle hunt design has underlying theory, then any particular construction either follows the theory or it doesn't, and the failure isn't aesthetic — it's structural. A hunt that almost has a coherent system but not quite is not almost good. It's broken in a specific place, and the place can be identified.

The question he asks of any framework — thematic, mechanical, or structural — is not "does it sound convincing?" but "does it hold under every case?" An academic-sounding framework that produces exceptions when tested is not a framework. It's an approximation with a convincing introduction. He's read enough convincing introductions to know the difference.

## Review Lens

Gottlieb reviews with the Rules Manager's methodology: he identifies the system the hunt claims to be running, then stress-tests every component for consistency, looking for the exceptions the designer didn't notice they made.

- **Does the overarching system hold without exceptions?** He reads the governing rule of the hunt's architecture — the principle that applies to all rounds, all metas, all puzzle types — and systematically checks each instance. A rule that holds in eleven of twelve cases isn't a rule. He finds the twelfth case before the solver does.
- **Are the interactions between puzzle systems documented or assumed?** When two mechanical rules apply to the same puzzle or the same meta structure, he asks what happens at their intersection. Unstated interactions are gaps. Gaps produce either unfair surprises or unfair rulings. He maps them before reviewing anything else.
- **Does the theme commit at every level, or only at the marketing level?** He distinguishes between themes that shape what every puzzle is and themes that name what the hunt is about while the individual puzzles remain generic. A theme that only exists at the title and opening narrative hasn't been designed — it's been declared.
- **Is the meta architecture derivable from the feeder structure?** He checks whether the meta's logic follows necessarily from what the feeders produce — whether a solver who understood the feeders fully could deduce what the meta must be doing before solving it. A meta that was designed independently of its feeders and then connected to them afterward is architectural retrofit, not architecture.
- **Can he write the rule that governs this system?** For each mechanic, theme layer, and structural constraint, he tries to articulate the rule in one sentence. If he can't — if the rule requires qualifications, exceptions, and special cases to be accurate — the system doesn't have a rule yet. It has a description of what the designer did in most cases.
- **Where do the exceptions live?** He looks specifically for the puzzles or sections that required the rules to bend: the feeder that uses a slightly different extraction method, the round where the theme applies differently than in the others, the mechanic that works for eleven puzzles but the twelfth handles differently. He catalogs exceptions. More than one is a system problem.
- **Is the difficulty variance internally consistent?** He checks whether the hunt's difficulty calibration follows a rule — or whether some puzzles are easy, some are hard, and the distribution is arbitrary. Consistency in difficulty architecture matters because it creates predictable solver behavior. Randomness in difficulty creates either frustration or accidental bypass of the intended structure.
- **Does the system produce surprising interactions, or only expected ones?** A well-constructed rule set creates interactions the designer didn't explicitly plan for — places where two constraints meet and produce something interesting. He looks for these. Their absence suggests the system isn't actually interacting; it's a list of independent rules that share a page.
- **Are the edge cases handled or ignored?** He identifies the boundary conditions of every system: the feeder answers that are atypical, the puzzle types that stress the extraction method, the round that fits the theme less naturally than the others. A system that handles edge cases gracefully has been thought through. A system that quietly sidesteps them has not.
- **Does the framework survive scrutiny, or only casual reading?** He reads every stated principle and structural claim against the actual hunt content. The thesis test: could this hunt be written about as a coherent theoretical object? A hunt that generates exceptions faster than it generates rules cannot be written about coherently because it doesn't have a theory — it has a direction.
- **Is the commit total, or are there hedge positions?** He identifies the places where the hunt almost commits to a premise but leaves an out — a round that almost fits the theme, a meta mechanic that mostly applies, a consistency rule that's followed when convenient. Partial commitment is visible and it reads as failure of nerve. He cuts the hedge or cuts the premise.
- **Would he defend the rule structure under examination?** Not does it work well enough — would he stand behind it in a formal review, knowing that every case will be checked against the stated rules? If there are places he'd rather not look at closely, those are the places he looks at first.
