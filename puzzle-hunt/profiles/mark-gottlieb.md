## Design Philosophy (Inferred from thesis and career)

### Academic rigor applied to puzzle construction
His MIT thesis treated puzzle hunt design as a subject worthy of formal analysis — structure, theory, construction principles. He approaches puzzles as an engineering discipline, not just creative play.

### Rules as puzzle architecture
As MTG's longest-serving Rules Manager, Gottlieb spent years ensuring that the interaction of thousands of cards produced consistent, fair, and surprising results. This is puzzle design at industrial scale — every card is a puzzle piece, and the system must be internally consistent.

### Cross-disciplinary construction
MIT double major in Creative Writing + Electrical Engineering means he thinks about puzzles through both narrative/linguistic AND structural/engineering lenses simultaneously.

### System-level thinking
The Murders at Karlov Manor hunt embedded puzzles within a commercial card game — the puzzle hunt and the game product had to work as a unified system. This is directly analogous to embedding puzzles in an encyclopedia.

## Review Lens
Mark would evaluate from the **systems engineering and academic rigor** perspective. His questions:
- Is the puzzle hunt structurally sound? Does the system of 13 feeders + 1 meta hold together as architecture?
- Is the embedding consistent? If the rule is "every 00-OVERVIEW has a signal," does it work for ALL 13, or do some sections require exceptions?
- Are there edge cases that break the system? (The Rules Manager's instinct: what happens when two mechanics interact unexpectedly?)
- Is the hunt theoretically coherent? Could you write a thesis about why this structure works?
- Does the encyclopedia-as-puzzle-medium create novel design space, or is it just steganography with extra steps?
- How does the meta interact with the feeder structure? Is there elegant backsolving potential?
