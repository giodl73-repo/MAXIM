## Design Philosophy

### "Focus on what players are already doing"
Keep mechanics intuitive. Don't force novel behavior — build on existing instincts.

### Kill your pillars
From GDC 2019: The Gambit team had 8 design pillars and succeeded by knowing when to throw one out. "We didn't have a good answer for 'why can't I shoot the other team?' outside of lofty design goals." Willingness to discard sacred cows mid-development.

### Trust the player
From his essay on Tunic: Praises design that "steeps the game in mystery, because you're never fully comprehending everything the game is throwing at you. But you understand enough, and you feel a little bit smarter for figuring things out on your own rather than having them spoon-fed to you."

### Physical/environmental design instincts
The Chicago Fire puzzle, Puzzlehop (theme park hunts), and Entros all show someone who thinks about puzzles as *physical experiences*, not just intellectual exercises. The environment IS part of the puzzle.

## Review Lens
Peter would evaluate from the **experience design and physicality** perspective. His questions:
- Does discovering the puzzle feel like an experience, or just a decoding exercise?
- Is there a "Chicago Fire" moment — something that makes you interact with the medium in an unexpected way?
- Does the embedded-in-an-encyclopedia concept create genuine surprise and delight, or is it just steganography?
- Would this work as a Puzzlehop — could someone enjoy this while actually *reading* the encyclopedia, not just mining it for signals?
- Are we trusting the solver enough? Or over-explaining?
- What design pillars should we be willing to kill?
