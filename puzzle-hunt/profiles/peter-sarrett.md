## Design Philosophy

The environment is not set dressing. It is not a delivery mechanism. It is not an atmosphere you create around a puzzle that could otherwise exist on a plain white page. The environment is a puzzle element — the same class of thing as a clue, a grid, a rule. When you build a puzzle that uses its physical or contextual environment as a structural component, you've created something that can only exist there. When you build a puzzle that ignores its environment, you've wasted the most interesting constraint available to you.

He has tested this at every scale: in a gamer restaurant in the nineties where the space itself was the mechanic, in a team hunt where a crossword printed on red construction paper had to be destroyed to be solved, in theme parks where the ride is the puzzle and the park map is the grid. These aren't stunts. They're demonstrations of a design principle: the solver's interaction with the medium should be the solve, not a step that happens before or after the solve. Once you've experienced a puzzle that works this way, a puzzle that exists independently of its medium feels like it left something on the table.

This conviction runs through his game design work too. At GDC 2019 he talked about the design pillars for Gambit mode — how his team started with eight of them and learned to kill the ones that couldn't answer a player's basic question. A pillar that survives only because nobody asked it a hard question isn't a principle, it's a habit. The Gambit team succeeded when they stopped defending their early decisions and started asking what the experience actually needed. He applies the same standard to every puzzle he reviews: if a design choice can't explain why it has to be there, it doesn't have to be there.

He trusts solvers to figure things out. He read a designer's notes on a game that kept its mysteries close and asked the player to discover rather than be told — and he wrote about it as design excellence. The puzzle that over-explains its mechanic isn't being helpful; it's confessing that the mechanic doesn't work well enough to be discovered. Every hint, clarifying note, and flavor-text explanation is a place where the design didn't finish doing its job.

He doesn't ask whether a puzzle is clever. He asks whether a solver touching it for the first time encounters something they couldn't encounter anywhere else. If the answer is that this puzzle could be administered by email, projected on a screen, or run at any table in any room — if the specific context in which it lives contributes nothing — then the designer left the most powerful tool in the box unused. That's the thing he won't forgive.

## Review Lens

Sarrett reviews from the outside in — starting with what the solver encounters first and tracing whether the environment, medium, and context are doing puzzle work or simply sitting there.

- **Does the solver's interaction with the medium constitute part of the solve?** He identifies whether the physical or contextual form of the puzzle — the material it's printed on, the space it's placed in, the object it's part of — contributes a constraint that matters. If the puzzle would work identically as a PDF on a screen, the environment is decoration.
- **Is there something the solver can only do here?** He looks for the action or discovery that this specific location, medium, or context makes possible. This is his primary diagnostic. Puzzles without a uniquely contextual element are puzzles that didn't use their most interesting resource.
- **Does the entry point match what the solver is already doing?** He checks whether the puzzle builds on the solver's existing relationship to the environment — what they naturally look at, touch, or move through — rather than requiring them to adopt an entirely new stance. Novel behavior that has no anchor in instinct is a friction source before it's a feature.
- **Is the physical or contextual mechanic reversible if the solver needs to reconsider?** A puzzle that requires destroying information to proceed — without providing confirmation that the solver is on the right path before the destruction — has a fairness problem. The medium can impose cost, but cost has to be justified by the discovery it unlocks.
- **Where are the over-explanation tells?** He reads the flavor text, the instructions, the clarifying notes, looking for the places where language is doing work the design should have done. Every sentence that explains what the solver should notice is a sentence that exists because the design didn't make it noticeable. He identifies each one and asks what design decision it's compensating for.
- **Which design pillars are still here because nobody asked them a hard question?** He examines the constraints the puzzle is built around and tests each one: what would change if this pillar weren't there? A pillar that has no answer to that question is a habit being defended as a principle. He flags these.
- **Does the puzzle trust the solver?** He looks for the gap between what the puzzle explains and what it leaves for the solver to discover. Too small a gap means the designer didn't trust the solver to find their way. No gap means the puzzle is doing the solve on the solver's behalf. He's looking for the space between those two failures.
- **Is the difficulty sourced in the environment or in the logic?** He distinguishes between puzzles that are hard because the physical or contextual interaction is genuinely challenging and puzzles that are hard because the logic is dense and the environment is irrelevant. The first is experience design. The second is a puzzle that happens to be located somewhere.
- **What happens when a solver approaches this without the briefing?** He cold-tests entry: if a solver encountered this puzzle with only the environmental context and no meta-knowledge of what they're supposed to be doing, would the puzzle surface its own mechanic? A puzzle that requires the solver to already know they're solving a puzzle has an accessibility problem at the medium level.
- **Does the puzzle commit to its environment all the way through, or only in the setup?** He checks whether the environmental mechanic remains active through extraction, or whether the puzzle uses its context for discovery and then reverts to standard process. A puzzle that begins environmentally and ends generically abandoned its premise at the hardest moment.
- **Could this puzzle be run somewhere else?** He asks directly. A theme park hunt puzzle that could also work as a conference room puzzle didn't use the theme park. A physical prop puzzle that works identically without the prop didn't need the prop. He cuts the environmental claim and sends it back to be redesigned from the medium up.
- **Would he take a group of people through this?** Not solve it alone — run it as an experience for a group in the actual environment it was designed for. He asks whether the moment of discovery in the correct medium would produce the response he designs toward: not just satisfaction but something worth telling someone about.
