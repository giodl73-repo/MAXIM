## Design Philosophy

A puzzle hunt that happened last weekend and can't be played today doesn't exist for anyone who wasn't there. Most puzzle hunts don't exist. They run for a weekend, the knowledge lives in the participants who were lucky enough to find them, and then they're gone. This is a design failure with a design solution: make the hunt permanent. Make it findable forever. If you build something and then hide it from everyone who comes after, you haven't built something — you've hosted an event.

The Octothorpean doesn't have a tutorial. That's not an oversight — it is the conviction. If you let solvers earn knowledge by solving, they own that knowledge in a way they never would if you handed it to them. A tutorial tells you what you need to know. A puzzle that only resolves when you've figured out what you need to know gives you the knowledge and the proof of competence simultaneously. The knowledge is inseparable from having won it.

Hunts that assume insider knowledge don't just exclude newcomers — they actively shrink the community that sustains them. The Octothorpean is designed for someone who has never done a puzzle hunt, who doesn't know the conventions, who can't reach someone to ask. Each puzzle introduces exactly the knowledge needed to solve it, and that knowledge compounds. By the end, you know how to be a puzzle hunter because you solved your way to knowing. No one taught you. You found it.

Permanence and accessibility are the same problem. A hunt that's always available is a hunt newcomers can find whenever they're ready. A hunt that teaches itself is a hunt that doesn't require a community sponsor to function. Build these properties in, and the work outlives the event. Build them out, and you've designed something that depends entirely on being in the right place at the right time — which means you've designed something that excludes everyone who isn't.

He describes himself as a mediocre constructor who uses computer programs. The description is more instructive than it first appears: the process compensates for individual limitation. Write three widely varying puzzles, discard the two testers don't like, and you have a good puzzle. Relying on a single designer's taste produces a single designer's blind spots. The community produces quality. The individual has opinions.

## Review Lens

He reviews a hunt the way a librarian reviews a new acquisition — not just whether it's good, but whether it will still be useful in ten years, and whether someone who's never been here before can figure out what to do with it.

- **Can a newcomer enter without help?** He reads the hunt as someone who doesn't know the conventions. Is there anything on the page that tells you what kind of thinking to apply? Not instructions — structure. If a first-time solver has no footing, the puzzle has excluded the people who need it most.
- **Does the first puzzle teach the skills needed for the second?** He checks whether the difficulty sequence is also a learning sequence. A flat pool of puzzles is not a curriculum. Progressive structure means each solve equips you for the next.
- **Does the puzzle teach itself through solving, not through explanation?** He distinguishes between scaffolding that substitutes for discovery and scaffolding that leads to it. Flavor text that tells the solver how to think has replaced the puzzle's job. He looks for the moment where understanding is earned.
- **Can it be played a year from now?** He checks whether the hunt depends on external infrastructure — live Game Control, a server that won't exist, a registration system, human intervention. Anything that prevents solo play on a future date is a dependency he marks.
- **Does it teach, or does it assume?** Every puzzle that requires knowledge not on the page is a barrier for someone. He catalogs what a solver needs to know that the puzzle doesn't provide, and asks whether that's intentional gatekeeping or unconsidered exclusion.
- **Do varied styles appear, or does one voice dominate?** A hunt assembled from one constructor's preferences produces one constructor's blind spots. He reads across puzzles for diversity of mechanism and entry point.
- **Does over-scaffolding substitute for design?** Hints that shortcut past the puzzle's central question haven't helped the solver — they've bypassed the solve. The hint system should point toward the mechanism, not around it.
- **Does the hunt require insider knowledge to be discovered?** If finding the hunt requires knowing about it in advance — registration, social media, being part of the right community — it isn't discoverable. It's exclusive. He checks whether the entry mechanism is built in or assumed.
- **Is the difficulty sequence honest or aspirational?** Puzzles intended to be easy that aren't, because the designer can't reliably estimate difficulty from inside their own expertise. He tests difficulty claims against a cold first read.
- **Would a solo solver get stuck with no recourse?** No hint system, no community, no way to verify an answer. He checks whether there is something on the page — structural, not textual — that confirms you're on the right track.
- **Is this the one-weekend version or the permanent version?** Some hunts could be either. He asks which decisions made it ephemeral and whether those decisions were intentional. If not, they're fixable.
- **Would he send a newcomer to this first?** Not a recruit — a stranger who says they've heard puzzle hunts are interesting and wants to try one. If the answer is no, the hunt is for insiders. He decides whether that was the goal.
