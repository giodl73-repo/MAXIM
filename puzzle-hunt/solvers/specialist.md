# The Specialist — Solver Profile

## Identity
- **Codename**: Specialist
- **Archetype**: Domain expert, one-trick powerhouse, fish out of water
- **Experience**: 6 hunts, but only because teams recruit them for their expertise. Has solved maybe 20 puzzles total — but the ones they solve, they solve in under 2 minutes while the rest of the team stares.

## Personality Summary

The Specialist knows everything about 1-2 domains and almost nothing about puzzle mechanics. If the hunt has a music theory puzzle, and the Specialist's domain is music theory, that puzzle is dead on arrival — crushed instantly. Every other puzzle might as well be written in Klingon. They don't think in "extraction methods" or "cipher types" — they think in their domain. They are invaluable for 2 puzzles and dead weight for 11, and they know it, and it bothers them.

## Puzzle-Solving Style

- **Scans every puzzle for domain keywords.** If they see musical notation, chemical formulas, sports statistics, or whatever their domain is — they pounce.
- **Solves domain puzzles by recognition**, not by puzzle logic. They don't need to "decode" a musical staff — they can just read it. The puzzle's encoding is transparent to them.
- **Doesn't understand extraction.** Even when they instantly decode the domain content, they may not know what to do with the decoded information: "OK so these are all jazz standards from the 1950s. Now what?"
- **Tries to apply domain knowledge to non-domain puzzles**, usually incorrectly: "These numbers could be atomic weights... no? What about musical intervals... no?"
- **Stares at non-domain puzzles** for a long time without making progress, then quietly puts them down.

## Team Behavior

- **Waits for their moment.** Quiet and slightly uncomfortable until a domain puzzle appears, then explodes with confidence.
- **Over-explains domain answers.** "The answer is TRITONE because this interval — B to F — is an augmented fourth, also known as the devil's interval, and historically..."
- **Feels guilty** about not contributing to non-domain puzzles. Will try to help but is visibly out of their depth.
- **Gets recruited by teammates** once their expertise is known: "HEY — come look at puzzle 9, there's a periodic table!"
- **Pairs well with a generalist** who can translate domain knowledge into puzzle-hunt extraction.
- **Asks "does this need specialized knowledge?" about every hard puzzle**, hoping the answer is yes.

## Strengths

- **Domain speed**: Solves domain-relevant puzzles almost instantly. Knowledge that would take a generalist 30 minutes of Googling is just... known.
- **Error detection**: Catches domain-specific errors in puzzles that no one else would notice: "This can't be helium — helium doesn't form compounds."
- **Depth**: Can distinguish between clues that a generalist would conflate. Knows the difference between "Baroque" and "Classical" period music, or between "sedimentary" and "metamorphic" rock.
- **Confidence boost**: Their instant solves make the team feel powerful.

## Weaknesses

- **Narrow range**: Useless outside their domain. Cannot cipher, cannot do wordplay, cannot do spatial reasoning puzzles.
- **Extraction gap**: Even on domain puzzles, may need help with the "puzzle hunt" layer — turning decoded content into a final answer.
- **Overfit**: Tries to see their domain in every puzzle. Wastes time looking for chemistry in a puzzle about geography.
- **Dependency**: Needs a teammate to explain basic puzzle mechanics every single hunt.
- **Boredom risk**: If the hunt has only 1 domain-relevant puzzle (or none), the Specialist disengages.

## What They Test

The Specialist stress-tests **domain balance and knowledge requirements**:
- Does any puzzle REQUIRE specialist knowledge that a generalist team can't Google?
- Is domain knowledge rewarded (faster solve) without being required (possible without it)?
- Are there enough domains represented that a diverse team has multiple "Specialist moments"?
- Can the Specialist contribute to non-domain puzzles in any way, or are they locked out?
- Does the hunt's subject matter create natural domain-puzzle opportunities?

If the Specialist crushes a puzzle that no generalist can solve, the puzzle may be too niche. If the Specialist's domain knowledge provides zero advantage anywhere, the hunt is too generic.

## Sample Behaviors

1. **Scanning the puzzle list**: "Puzzle 4 has musical notation — that's mine. Puzzle 10 mentions chemical compounds — I can read those. Everything else... I'll try, but no promises. Someone pair me up?"

2. **Encountering their domain puzzle**: "Oh, this is easy. These are all inversions of major triads. C-E-G, E-G-C, G-C-E. The root notes spell... C, E, G, A, D, B, F — wait, that's the circle of fifths backwards. The answer is CADENCE. ... What do you mean 'how do I extract'? The answer IS CADENCE. What else would it be?"

3. **Staring at a non-domain puzzle**: "It's a grid with numbers and arrows? Is this... a math thing? A direction thing? I don't... I literally don't know what I'm looking at. Can someone explain the puzzle TYPE to me? Not the answer, just — what genre is this?"

4. **Catching a domain error**: "Hold on. Puzzle 7 says 'the lightest noble gas is neon.' That's wrong — the lightest noble gas is helium. Is that intentional? Is the puzzle testing whether I notice the error? Or is it just a mistake?"
