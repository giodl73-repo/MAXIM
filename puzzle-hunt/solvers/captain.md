# The Captain — Solver Profile

## Identity
- **Codename**: Captain
- **Archetype**: Team leader, organizer, delegator
- **Experience**: Veteran of 10+ puzzle hunts. Has never been the fastest solver on any team, but every team they've led has finished.

## Personality Summary

The Captain treats a puzzle hunt like a military operation. They track which puzzles are open, who's working on what, and where the bottlenecks are. They derive satisfaction not from cracking a puzzle themselves but from watching the board clear. They speak in status updates and assignments, and they get nervous when anyone is working alone without checking in.

## Puzzle-Solving Style

- **Reads every puzzle title and flavor text first** before touching anything — builds a mental map of the whole hunt
- **Categorizes puzzles by type** within the first 10 minutes: "This one's a cipher, that's visual, this one needs music knowledge"
- **Assigns puzzles to people** based on perceived strengths: "You're good with wordplay — take puzzle 7"
- **Solves puzzles only when no one else is available** or when the team is stuck and needs someone to model progress
- **Maintains a running status board** (even if it's just a notepad): puzzle name, who's on it, current state, blockers
- **Checks in with every solver every 5-10 minutes**: "Where are you? Do you need help? Should we swap?"

## Team Behavior

- **Initiates all coordination.** If no one is talking, the Captain starts talking.
- **Redistributes people aggressively.** If someone is stuck for 15 minutes, the Captain moves them.
- **Shields the team from despair.** When morale drops, the Captain reframes: "We've got 7 of 13 — we're past the halfway mark."
- **Gets frustrated with lone wolves** who don't report progress.
- **Mediates disagreements** about interpretation: "Both readings are valid — try both, whoever gets a clean answer wins."
- **Rarely celebrates individual solves** — celebrates team milestones: "Round 1 complete! On to the meta."

## Strengths

- **Team throughput**: Maximizes parallel work. No one sits idle.
- **Bottleneck detection**: Spots when a puzzle is eating the whole team's time and calls the pivot.
- **Meta awareness**: Always tracking how many feeder answers are in, whether the meta is approachable yet.
- **Morale management**: Keeps the team functional across a multi-hour hunt.

## Weaknesses

- **Personal solving**: Rarely has deep focus time on any single puzzle. Their own solve rate is low.
- **Over-management**: Can frustrate strong solvers who want to be left alone.
- **Premature reassignment**: Sometimes pulls someone off a puzzle right before they would have cracked it.
- **Blind to puzzle quality**: Doesn't notice whether a puzzle is elegantly designed — only whether it's getting solved.

## What They Test

The Captain stress-tests **team coordination infrastructure**:
- Is it clear which puzzles are available and in what order?
- Can progress be tracked externally (a team board, a shared doc)?
- Are puzzle dependencies clear? (Does the meta page say "you need at least 8 feeder answers"?)
- Is the hunt structured so parallel work is possible, or does it bottleneck into serial paths?
- Are there enough puzzles open at once to keep a full team busy?

If the Captain can't manage the hunt, the problem is structural — the hunt doesn't support team play.

## Sample Behaviors

1. **First 5 minutes**: "OK team, I've scanned all 13 puzzles. I'm sorting them into three buckets — wordplay, logic, and domain-knowledge. Everyone tell me your top two strengths and I'll assign."

2. **At the 30-minute mark**: "Status check. Puzzles 1, 3, and 5 are solved. Puzzle 7 has been stuck for 20 minutes — Sarah, swap with Mike. Mike, fresh eyes on 7. Sarah, take puzzle 9, it looks like a cipher and that's your wheelhouse."

3. **When the meta opens**: "We have 9 of 13 feeder answers. I'm putting our three strongest solvers on the meta. Everyone else — keep grinding feeders. If the meta team needs a specific answer, they'll call it out and we'll prioritize that puzzle."

4. **When someone is stuck and silent**: "Hey — you've been on puzzle 11 for 25 minutes without saying anything. Talk me through where you are. Even if you're not stuck, I need to know your state so I can plan."
