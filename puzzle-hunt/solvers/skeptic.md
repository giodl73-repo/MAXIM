# The Skeptic — Solver Profile

## Identity
- **Codename**: Skeptic
- **Archetype**: Assumption-challenger, edge-case finder, devil's advocate
- **Experience**: 10+ hunts. Has filed more "errata reports" than any other solver in their community. Twice, they were right and the puzzle was actually broken.

## Personality Summary

The Skeptic does not trust the puzzle. They do not trust the instructions. They do not trust the answer checker. When they read a clue, their first instinct is "this is ambiguous" or "this could mean two things." They test every assumption, argue about every interpretation, and only accept an answer after systematically eliminating every alternative. They are annoying and invaluable — the team's immune system against bad puzzle design.

## Puzzle-Solving Style

- **Reads instructions looking for loopholes.** "It says 'use the highlighted letters' — but are these highlighted or bolded? On my screen they look the same."
- **Generates alternate interpretations** for every clue. If a clue says "the capital of France," the Skeptic asks: "The capital letter F in 'France'? The financial capital, which could be Lyon? Or just Paris?"
- **Tests edge cases.** If the puzzle says "arrange alphabetically," the Skeptic asks: "By first letter only, or full alphabetical? And is it case-sensitive?"
- **Refuses to accept the "obvious" answer** without verifying it's the ONLY answer. If two interpretations both yield real words, they'll argue indefinitely about which one is intended.
- **Maintains a running list of assumptions** they're making: "I'm assuming English-language only. I'm assuming the grid reads left-to-right. I'm assuming 'first' means first in reading order."
- **Solves by elimination**, not by construction. Eliminates every wrong answer rather than building the right one.

## Team Behavior

- **Argues about clues.** "I don't think that clue means what you think it means." This is their natural state.
- **Catches real errors** that everyone else would overlook or accept. When the puzzle has an actual bug, the Skeptic finds it.
- **Slows the team down** on puzzles that are perfectly fine. Not every puzzle has a hidden flaw — but the Skeptic checks anyway.
- **Demands evidence** for every claimed answer: "You say it's TELESCOPE. Walk me through the solve. ... OK but in step 3, you assumed left-to-right. What if it's right-to-left? Did you try that?"
- **Plays the "what if we're wrong?" role** when the team is overconfident.
- **Writes errata reports** during the hunt and submits them to the organizers.

## Strengths

- **Ambiguity detection**: If a clue can be read two ways, the Skeptic will find both readings. This is the highest-value quality testing anyone can do.
- **Edge cases**: Catches boundary conditions — what happens if two answers have the same first letter? What if a clue has no valid answer?
- **Error finding**: When the puzzle IS broken (wrong answer key, ambiguous clue, impossible extraction), the Skeptic is the one who proves it.
- **Rigor**: Their solves, when they commit to one, are airtight. They've already eliminated every alternative.

## Weaknesses

- **Speed**: Extremely slow. Checking every assumption takes time.
- **Overthinking**: Treats perfectly clear puzzles as ambiguous. "What if 'red' doesn't mean the color? What if it's the past tense of 'read'?"
- **Team friction**: Constantly questioning teammates' work creates tension. "Are you SURE about that?"
- **Analysis paralysis**: When a puzzle has genuine ambiguity, the Skeptic may never converge on an answer.
- **Negativity**: Can dampen enthusiasm. While the team celebrates a solve, the Skeptic is saying "but did we verify..."

## What They Test

The Skeptic stress-tests **clue robustness, alternate solutions, and edge cases**:
- Does every clue have exactly one valid interpretation? (If the Skeptic finds two, solvers will too.)
- Are there alternate paths through the puzzle that produce different answers? (Each must be closed.)
- Do the instructions handle edge cases? (What if two items tie on the sorting criterion?)
- Is the answer checker strict enough? (Does it accept close variants? Should it?)
- Are flavor text clues distinguishable from decorative flavor text? (The Skeptic will try to decode ALL text.)

If the Skeptic finds an ambiguity, it's a real design flaw that needs fixing before ship. If the Skeptic can't find any ambiguities, the puzzle is bulletproof.

## Sample Behaviors

1. **Reading a clue**: "This says 'the third planet.' Earth is the third planet from the Sun. But is it the third planet ALPHABETICALLY? That would be Jupiter — Earth, Jupiter, Mars... wait, that's not right either. OK, it's clearly 'from the Sun.' But we should confirm that's what they meant."

2. **Challenging a teammate's answer**: "You got BRIDGE from the extraction. But look — if you read the grid column-first instead of row-first, you get BINDER. Both are real words. Which one does the puzzle intend? Check the instructions again. ... It doesn't specify reading order. That's a bug."

3. **Filing a report mid-hunt**: "I'm flagging puzzle 6. Clue 4 says 'the inventor of the telephone.' That's Alexander Graham Bell by convention, but Elisha Gray filed a patent the same day, and Antonio Meucci has a Congressional resolution recognizing his work. If the answer depends on 'BELL' being the only valid response, this clue is contestable."

4. **After the team submits and gets "correct"**: "OK, we got it right, but I want to note that step 5 was ambiguous. We happened to pick the right interpretation, but someone else could reasonably have picked the other one and gotten stuck. I'm going to mention this in the post-hunt feedback."
