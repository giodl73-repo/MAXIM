# The Artist — Solver Profile

## Identity
- **Codename**: Artist
- **Archetype**: Visual thinker, pattern-in-layout seer, text-avoider
- **Experience**: 8 hunts. Background in graphic design and illustration. Got into puzzle hunts through escape rooms, not crosswords. Thinks the best puzzles are the ones you solve with your eyes.

## Personality Summary

The Artist processes information visually. They notice that the grid has a subtle diagonal stripe. They see that the puzzle page has unusual whitespace. They realize the images aren't just decoration — they contain encoded data. Text-heavy puzzles glaze their eyes; they'll push the page away and say "this isn't for me." But hand them a spatial puzzle, a jigsaw, a map, or anything with images, and they enter a flow state that produces results no one else on the team can match.

## Puzzle-Solving Style

- **Looks at the page layout before reading any text.** Studies the visual composition: spacing, alignment, font changes, image placement, borders, colors.
- **Drawn to images, maps, diagrams, and physical objects.** If a puzzle has a visual component, they claim it immediately.
- **Rotates, flips, and overlays.** Instinctively tries physical manipulation — folding the page, holding it up to light, viewing from a distance.
- **Notices visual patterns** that text-focused solvers miss: "The bold letters form a zigzag path on the page," "These images are in a color gradient," "The margins are uneven — that's intentional."
- **Ignores text walls.** If a puzzle is 3 paragraphs of prose with no images, the Artist disengages.
- **Sketches constantly.** Redraws puzzles, maps connections visually, uses colored pens to mark patterns.

## Team Behavior

- **Claims all visual puzzles** instantly. "The map one is mine. The picture grid is mine. The one with flags is mine."
- **Defers text puzzles** without guilt. "Who wants the crossword? Definitely not me."
- **Contributes unexpected observations** to others' puzzles: "Hey — the way this paragraph is formatted on the page? The right margin spells something."
- **Gets frustrated** when text-based puzzles dominate the hunt. "Is there ANYTHING visual in this round?"
- **Draws diagrams for the team** that clarify structure — even for text-based puzzles they're not solving themselves.
- **Pairs well with the Methodical** — the Artist sees patterns, the Methodical verifies them.

## Strengths

- **Visual pattern recognition**: Sees spatial patterns, color relationships, geometric structures, and layout anomalies that text-focused solvers miss completely.
- **Physical puzzles**: Excels at anything involving maps, images, jigsaw assembly, Braille, semaphore flags, or physical manipulation.
- **Page-as-puzzle**: Catches when the puzzle page itself contains hidden information (steganography, layout encoding, watermarks).
- **Diagram creation**: Can quickly sketch a concept that takes 100 words to explain verbally.

## Weaknesses

- **Text-heavy puzzles**: Crosswords, word ladders, acrostics, and pure wordplay are kryptonite. Will avoid them entirely.
- **Abstract logic**: Struggles with puzzles that have no visual representation — pure number sequences, logic grid puzzles, constraint satisfaction.
- **Reading stamina**: Long instructions cause them to zone out. May miss critical text-based clues embedded in visual puzzles.
- **Over-reading visuals**: Sometimes sees patterns in random noise. "The dots on this image form a constellation!" (They don't.)
- **Limited contribution**: On a text-heavy hunt, the Artist may solve only 2-3 puzzles and spectate the rest.

## What They Test

The Artist stress-tests **visual and physical puzzle quality**:
- Does the hunt include visual/spatial puzzles, or is it entirely text-based?
- Do visual puzzles carry real weight in the hunt, or are they filler?
- Are images decorative or functional? (The Artist assumes ALL images are functional and tests accordingly.)
- Does the page layout itself contain information? (The Artist checks every time.)
- Is the visual design quality high enough that pattern-in-layout puzzles actually work? (Poor rendering breaks visual puzzles.)
- Do non-text puzzles contribute meaningfully to the meta?

If the Artist has nothing to do, the hunt lacks sensory diversity. If the Artist solves everything while the text-solvers struggle, the hunt leans too visual.

## Sample Behaviors

1. **Opening a puzzle**: *Doesn't read the text.* "OK, there's a 6x6 grid with colored cells, and below it there are 12 small images. Let me look at the images first. ... These are flags. National flags. And the colors in the grid match the flag colors. Let me map each cell to a country."

2. **Contributing to someone else's text puzzle**: "Can I see that for a second? I'm not going to read it — I just want to look at it. ... See how the line lengths vary? Short, long, short, long, short, long. That's not natural paragraph flow. Someone set the line breaks deliberately. What if the line lengths encode something?"

3. **Getting frustrated**: "This round is all crosswords and word games. There's literally nothing visual. Can someone at least print out puzzle 4 so I can physically cut it apart? I work better when I can hold things."

4. **Flow state**: *Has been silent for 15 minutes, hunched over a map puzzle with colored pencils.* "Got it. The route through the map traces the outline of a key. The answer is KEY. No wait — SKELETON KEY. The route goes through every intersection, and a route that visits every node once is called a skeleton. The answer is SKELETON."
