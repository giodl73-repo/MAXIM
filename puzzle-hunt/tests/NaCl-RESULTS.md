## Issues

1. **Classifications are too explicitly signaled.** Each word's description contains a near-verbatim match to the table definition: "The meaning weakened" -> Bleaching ("meaning weakens"), "The meaning degraded" -> Pejoration ("meaning degrades"), "The meaning expanded" -> Broadening ("meaning becomes more inclusive"). The solver doesn't need to understand semantic change -- they just need to match synonyms. The puzzle TELLS you the classification rather than making you DEDUCE it from the etymology. A Witness-style design would present the etymology and let the solver classify without the hint phrases.

2. **The extraction is the weakest step.** Counting to the Nth letter of a type name is mechanical. It's the one part of the puzzle that requires no understanding. The classification is the real intellectual work; the extraction is just encoding. Ideally the extraction mechanism would itself require understanding (e.g., "extract the letter that corresponds to the position of this type in the table's ordering").

3. **Parenthetical ambiguity (minor).** "Spell each type exactly as it appears in bold" -- the bold text includes "(generalization)" and "(specialization)." The solver must decide whether to count into the parenthetical. It doesn't affect the answer here, but it's imprecise.

## Suggested Fixes

1. **Remove the explicit signal phrases.** Instead of "The meaning weakened," let the etymology speak for itself. The solver should read "sal ammoniac, sal soda -- a bare classifier, emptied of force" and deduce Bleaching from understanding what bleaching IS. This transforms the puzzle from synonym-matching to genuine semantic analysis. It makes the solver BE smart, not feel smart.

2. **If signal phrases must stay, make them subtler.** Instead of "The meaning degraded" (which is the literal definition of Pejoration), try something like "The word descended from appetite to accusation" -- still pointing toward degradation, but requiring the solver to recognize the direction of change.

3. **Clarify counting convention.** Add: "Count letters within the first word of the type name (before any parenthetical)."

## Issues

1. **The classification step is trivially easy.** Every word's description contains a phrase that is nearly identical to the table definition. "The meaning weakened" = Bleaching ("meaning weakens"). "The scope contracted" = Narrowing ("meaning becomes more restricted"). "The meaning degraded" = Pejoration ("meaning degrades"). "The meaning expanded" = Broadening ("meaning becomes more inclusive"). "The meaning improved" = Amelioration ("meaning improves"). This is lookup, not deduction. An experienced solver doesn't even need the table -- the signal phrases are self-classifying.

2. **The Social Sciences paragraphs are decorative.** Comparative advantage, signaling theory, Nash equilibrium, Weber's monopoly on violence -- these are interesting but irrelevant to the solve. They don't constrain the classification. They don't provide clues. They're educational filler. A tighter puzzle would make these paragraphs DO something -- perhaps the classification requires understanding the economic concept, not just matching a signal phrase.

3. **No difficulty oscillation.** Every word is equally easy to classify. There's no hard word that forces the solver to think. Blow would call this a flat difficulty curve. The SAUCE/Metaphorical extension ambiguity is the closest thing to a challenge, and the puzzle resolves it immediately.

4. **Extraction positions feel arbitrary.** Why position 5 of Bleaching? Why position 8 of Broadening? The positions exist to spell CAPITAL, but they have no relationship to the content. A more elegant design would derive the positions from the puzzle content itself (e.g., "extract the letter at the position corresponding to the century when this semantic change is first attested").

## Suggested Fixes

1. **Remove the signal phrases.** Present only the etymology and let the solver classify from understanding. For SAL: "Latin *sal* persists in chemical nomenclature: *sal ammoniac*, *sal soda*. The word still means 'salt' -- but stripped of every connotation." Don't say "the meaning weakened." Make the solver recognize Bleaching from the description alone. This transforms a 5-minute synonym match into a genuine semantic-analysis puzzle.

2. **Make the Social Sciences paragraphs load-bearing.** For at least two words, require the solver to use information from the Social Sciences paragraph to determine the classification. For example, if SAUCE's description didn't explicitly say "expanded," the solver might need the trade-theory paragraph to recognize that the word's scope widened alongside the globalization of the spice trade.

3. **Introduce one genuinely ambiguous word.** SOLDIER is the best candidate: its etymology involves concrete-to-abstract (mineral -> coin -> wage -> person, which could be Metaphorical extension) AND positive shift (each stage gains stature, which is Amelioration). Remove the signal phrase "The meaning improved" and let the solver use the verification table to resolve the ambiguity. This creates the puzzle's "aha" moment.

4. **Consider reducing to 5 or 6 words.** Seven words at equal difficulty is "doing the same thing seven times." Five words (one per type used) would be tighter. Or six words (with one repeated type as the surprise). Seven words at zero difficulty each = tedium risk.

1. **Classification is synonym-matching, not deduction (all three).** Every tester independently identified this as the puzzle's central weakness. The signal phrases ("weakened," "degraded," "expanded," "improved," "contracted") map directly to the table definitions. The solver doesn't need to understand semantic change -- they just need to match words. Blow: "The puzzle TELLS you the classification rather than making you DEDUCE it." Stripping it would not change the puzzle's solvability. In a puzzle embedded in an encyclopedia, these paragraphs should DO something.

3. **Extraction is mechanical (Blow and Katz).** Counting to the Nth letter of a type name requires no understanding. It's the weakest step in an otherwise concept-driven puzzle.

4. **Parenthetical ambiguity is minor but present (all three).** "Broadening" vs. "Broadening (generalization)" -- doesn't affect the answer but creates a moment of unnecessary uncertainty.

## Consensus Strengths

1. **The core concept is excellent (all three).** Seven words from one PIE root, each demonstrating a different semantic change type, with the extracted letters spelling the thematic synthesis. This is a puzzle that teaches by doing. The concept earns universal praise.

2. **The answer CAPITAL is a perfect thematic payoff (all three).** Salt was the original capital. The answer encodes the thesis of the puzzle. Blow: "The answer is the abstraction that all seven words orbit around." Selinker: "The form mirrors the content." Katz: "Salt was capital before capital was capital."

5. **Strong narrative arc (Selinker especially).** The seven words trace a dramatic arc from loss (Bleaching, Narrowing, Pejoration) to growth (Broadening, Amelioration). Salt's legacy is net positive. This gives the puzzle emotional shape.

## Verdict: PASS

**Score: 27.0/30** -- well above the passing threshold.

The puzzle's concept, thematic payoff, and confirmation design are outstanding. Its weakness is execution difficulty: the classification step is too easy because the signal phrases give away each answer. This is a high-concept puzzle with low-friction solving -- which may be appropriate for its position in the hunt, but limits its ceiling for experienced solvers.

### Recommended Revisions (not blocking, but would elevate)

1. **Remove or soften the signal phrases** in at least 3 of the 7 word descriptions. Let the etymology do the work. Keep the explicit signals for the 2 hardest classifications (SAL/Bleaching and SALACIOUS/Pejoration are the least obvious) and remove them from the 5 clearest (SALAD, SAUCE, SALARY, SALTS, SOLDIER). This creates difficulty oscillation and makes the verification table essential rather than confirmatory.

2. **Make at least one Social Sciences paragraph load-bearing.** If the solver needs the comparative-advantage paragraph to understand WHY salad's scope contracted (specialization in trade mirrors specialization in meaning), the encyclopedia connection becomes structural, not decorative.

3. **Add a counting-convention note** to the extraction instructions: "Count letters within the first word of the type name (before any parenthetical). Spaces are not counted."

4. **Optional: reduce to 6 words** to tighten the puzzle and eliminate redundancy. One word per singleton type (Bleaching, Narrowing, Pejoration) and one word per doubled type (one Broadening, one Amelioration) plus one "surprise" repeat = 6. This removes one classification step without losing the distribution constraint.
