### VII. The Plasterer

Before oil, before canvas, this was how walls were painted. Pigment
ground in water was applied directly to freshly laid lime plaster --
the *intonaco* -- while it was still wet. As the plaster dried and
carbonated, the pigment bonded permanently into the wall surface.
The artist worked in sections called *giornate* (a day's work per
section), because once the plaster dried, no more paint could bind.
Michelangelo used this technique on the Sistine ceiling. Leonardo,
characteristically, did NOT use it for the Last Supper -- he wanted
to revise, so he used the dry-wall variant, and it began to
deteriorate within decades.

## Issues

1. **No structural interlock between clues.** All eleven clues are independently solvable. This means the puzzle is eleven mini-quizzes plus extraction. A well-crafted puzzle should have internal dependency -- solving one part should either enable or constrain another. Currently, you could solve the clues in any order with zero effect on difficulty.

2. **The extraction is purely mechanical.** Extract the Nth letter. No insight required beyond correct identification. The extraction does not reward deeper understanding of the content. Compare to a puzzle where the extracted letters are themselves positioned to form a secondary pattern (acrostic, crossword constraint, etc.).

3. **Etymology imprecision in Clue II.** "Derived from the Italian for 'dark'" -- tenebrism is from Latin *tenebra*, not the standard Italian word for dark (*scuro*, *buio*). The Latin word *tenebra* does exist as a cognate in Italian, but calling it "the Italian for dark" is misleading. A careful solver might second-guess themselves wondering if there is a different technique derived from an Italian word.

4. **Clue VII: FRESCO vs BUON FRESCO ambiguity.** The clue describes specifically the wet-plaster method (buon fresco), distinguished from secco (dry-wall variant Leonardo used). The technically precise answer is "buon fresco." However, the 6-character blank forces FRESCO and the common usage supports it. A pedantic art historian might hesitate.

## Suggested Fixes

1. **Add structural interlock.** Make some clues depend on others. For example: "The technique described in Clue VIII was perfected by the painter whose name you used in Clue V's century" -- forcing solvers to cross-reference answers. Or: "The 3rd letter of your answer to Clue IV names the debate that Clue IX's artist championed the other side of" -- building thematic connections into the mechanism.

2. **Fix the etymology in Clue II.** Change "derived from the Italian for 'dark'" to "derived from the Latin for 'darkness'" or "from the Latin tenebra, darkness." This is more accurate and removes ambiguity.

3. **Add a meta-constraint.** Currently the only confirmation is that PERSPECTIVE is a real word. Consider adding: "Each clue title (The Analyst, The Dramatist...) describes not just the answer but a way of seeing. The extracted word names what all eleven share." This adds thematic depth to the extraction.

## Issues

1. **High knowledge barrier with no ramp.** All eleven clues require art history knowledge. There is no clue that a generally educated person could solve from general knowledge (even "The painter of the Mona Lisa" would at least give one entry point). The solver either knows art history or must use the encyclopedia for every clue. There is no gentle on-ramp.

2. **The solving act is research, not deduction.** Each clue is: "here is a description of X -- what is X?" The solver who knows the answer fills it in. The solver who does not knows looks it up. Neither is doing puzzle-logic. There is no moment where knowing answer A helps you figure out answer B. Compare to a crossword where crossing letters constrain both Across and Down -- here there are no crosses.

3. **The anamorphic concept is the wonder, but it is inaccessible.** The best version of this puzzle -- tilt the physical page and see PERSPECTIVE resolve from stretched streaks -- is unavailable in digital form. The identification exercise is explicitly a "fallback." For a TED-Ed video, you could demonstrate the anamorphic page. For a digital solver, you are solving the backup plan.

4. **Narrow cultural scope.** All eleven answers come from European Renaissance art. A solver from a non-Western art tradition would find no entry points from their own expertise. The puzzle assumes familiarity with the Western canon -- appropriate for the encyclopedia's art-history section, but limiting for broad accessibility.

## Suggested Fixes

1. **Add one "gimme" clue solvable from general knowledge.** Even "He painted the Mona Lisa and the Last Supper" for Leonardo (though Leonardo is not directly an answer here -- sfumato is). Or frame the Mona Lisa clue (VIII) more accessibly: "The most famous painting in the world was made using this technique -- the Italian word for smoke." A non-expert entry point.

2. **Create cross-referencing between clues.** If solving Clue IV (disegno) reveals context that helps solve Clue IX (Titian), the puzzle has internal logic beyond pure identification. Even a simple note like "The artist in Clue IX championed the opposing position to the term in Clue IV" would create a thread through the puzzle.

3. **Consider a physical artifact.** For a TED-Ed presentation or a puzzle-hunt event: print the anamorphic page and hand it to solvers alongside the identification puzzle. Let them confirm each other. The physical artifact IS the wonder. Without it, you need to manufacture wonder through puzzle structure alone.

4. **Add a narrative wrapper.** Instead of eleven standalone clues, frame them as a tour: "The Surveyor leads you through eleven rooms of a gallery. In each room, one work hangs. She describes it from an angle you have never tried." This creates the guided-exploration feeling that makes non-experts feel like participants, not students taking a quiz.
