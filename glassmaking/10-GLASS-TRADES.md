# 10 — Glass Trades

## Glassblower, Gaffer, Glazier, Optical Fabricator — The Trade Hierarchy

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Gaffer**: the master glassblower who leads the "shop" or team; term from British glassmaking (possibly from "grandfather" or "godfather" — a term of respect for the master); in traditional glassblowing, complex pieces require a team — the gaffer leads and does primary forming while assistants (servitors, bit gatherers) deliver hot gathers, add handles, and assist; gaffer selects the design and makes key forming decisions; traditional Venetian glassblowing = the gaffer system at its most developed; skill level: 10-15+ years to reach gaffer level; the gaffer's assistant positions: servitor (second in command, delivers gathers, assists with handles), bit gatherer (takes smaller gathers for additions), footmaker (makes foot pieces)
- **Glassblower**: furnace glassblower working with hot molten glass; physical demands: heat exposure (furnace mouth ~1,200°C), heavy glass on pipe, continuous rotation to prevent drooping; traditional working schedule in British trade: 3-hour "move" at the furnace (continuous work), then break; modern studio glass practice more flexible; training: formal apprenticeship in industrial glass (UK, Germany still have Glass and Ceramics Technology apprenticeship frameworks) or art school (BFA/MFA in glass); most studio glass practitioners are largely self-taught supplemented by workshops
- **Flameworker (lampworker)**: works glass in a bench-mounted torch (oxy-propane or oxy-natural gas); distinct trade from furnace glassblower; primarily borosilicate glass (Pyrex — requires hotter flame); used for: scientific glassware (bench-blown flasks, tubing connections, specialty apparatus); glass beads (bead maker = perlenmacher in German tradition — major bead production industry in Czech Republic/Bohemia); glass eyes (prosthetics — still made by lampwork); art glass (small-scale sculpture, sea life figures, flamework pendants); also neon and other gas-discharge tube bending (sign tubes bent with gas flame)
- **Scientific glassblower**: specialized trade within glass; makes custom laboratory glassware; typically works in research laboratory or custom glass shop; must understand vacuum systems, chemistry, the purpose of each piece to design appropriate geometry; thick quartz work (fused silica — requires oxygen-hydrogen torch, very different from borosilicate); some research groups cannot function without their scientific glassblower; increasingly rare trade (commercial lab glassware and plasticware has reduced demand)
- **Glazier**: installs and works with flat glass (architectural); cuts flat glass to size (scoring with tungsten carbide wheel, breaking along score line); installs windows (bedding, caulking, glazing bars); leads stained glass (leading came); double-glazing installation; structural glazing (structural silicone, spider fittings for frameless glass); safety glazing selection and specification; glass cutting: scoring depth and direction, break mechanics; specialized: leaded light glazier (stained glass restoration), mirror installation, shower enclosure installation; UK NVQ in Fenestration Installation; US glazier apprenticeship under SMWIA (Sheet Metal Workers' International Association)
- **Glass cutter (decorative)**: wheel-cutting and engraving on blown or cast glass; traditional Bohemian/German copper wheel engraving (small copper wheels charged with abrasive slurry, rotate against glass surface, create intaglio cuts — extremely detailed portrait and landscape engraving); stone wheel cutting (larger cuts for lead crystal facets — the faceting that makes Waterford and Baccarat cut crystal); diamond-point engraving (scratching with diamond tip — historical Dutch technique, stipple engraving); sandblasting (controlled abrasion through stencil for modern frosted/cut effects); water jet cutting (cold process for architectural glass shapes without thermal stress)
- **Optical fabricator (optician)**: precision grinding and polishing of optical glass for lenses, prisms, windows; grinding sequence: coarse grinding (abrasive slurry on cast iron lap) → fine grinding (closer to final radius) → polishing (pitch lap with cerium oxide or rouge — nanometer-scale removal for optical quality surface); interferometric testing (HeNe laser interferometer checks surface figure to fraction of wavelength); coating (see optical coatings — AR coatings, HR coatings, beamsplitters); industries: camera lenses, microscope objectives, telescope mirrors, laser optics, semiconductor lithography; precision: telescope primary mirrors ground to λ/8 surface accuracy (~70nm); EUV lithography optics to <1nm
- **Float glass plant operator**: highly automated but requires skilled operators; furnace operators monitor and adjust melt chemistry and temperatures; tin bath operators control ribbon width, thickness, atmosphere (N₂ + H₂ to prevent tin oxidation); lehr operators control cooling curve; cutting room operators set cutting patterns to optimize yield from master sheet; total quality management critical (one defect in 1,000 sq m); day-to-day plant work combines process chemistry, mechanical engineering, and materials quality
