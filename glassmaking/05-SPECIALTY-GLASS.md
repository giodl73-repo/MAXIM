# Specialty Glass — Borosilicate, Lead Crystal, Optical, Glass-Ceramics

## The Big Picture

Standard soda-lime glass handles ~90% of all applications. For the other 10%, specialized compositions enable performance impossible from a commodity material: the thermal shock resistance of Pyrex, the near-zero expansion of Zerodur, the extreme scratch resistance of Gorilla Glass, or the transparency of fused silica at 193nm wavelengths used in chip fabrication.

## Borosilicate Glass

```
BOROSILICATE — COMPOSITION AND PROPERTIES
==========================================

COMPOSITION:
 SiO₂:  ~80%  (network former)
 B₂O₃:  ~13%  (network former; triangular BO₃ groups + some BO₄)
 Al₂O₃: ~2%   (network former; improves chemical durability)
 Na₂O:  ~4%   (modifier; maintains workability)

INVENTED: Otto Schott (Carl Zeiss Glass Laboratory, Jena, 1887)
 Schott → founder of Schott AG, still major specialty glass company

PYREX (Corning, 1915):
 Eugene Sullivan and William Taylor at Corning Glass Works
 Originally: railroad lantern lenses shattering from thermal shock
  (hot lamp + rain splash → rapid cooling → fracture → train signal failure)
 Pyrex formula: similar to Schott's borosilicate; CTE ~3.3×10⁻⁶/°C
 Pyrex brand: now generic; license expired; many brands use "Pyrex" name
  Quality: US Pyrex (Corning) = borosilicate; EU "Pyrex" = sometimes soda-lime
  (Different product under same name in different markets — source of confusion)

PROPERTIES vs SODA-LIME:
 CTE: 3.3×10⁻⁶ vs 9×10⁻⁶ → 3× lower expansion → dramatically better thermal shock
 Chemical durability: higher silica + alumina → better resistance to acids
 Working temperature: higher than soda-lime (~1,250°C working point)
 Softening point: ~820°C vs ~720°C soda-lime
 UV transmission: slightly better than soda-lime; not as good as fused silica
 Cost: 2-4× soda-lime

APPLICATIONS:
 Laboratory glassware (flasks, beakers, condensers, round-bottom flasks):
  Withstands direct flame heating + cooling; acid/base exposure; autoclaving
 Bakeware (Pyrex dishes): oven-safe, dishwasher-safe
 Pharmaceutical packaging (Type I glass):
  Ampules, vials, syringes for injectable drugs
  Must pass ISO 4802 and USP standards for drug compatibility
 Lighting: halogen lamp envelopes (hot); HID (high-intensity discharge) envelopes
 Nuclear waste storage: borosilicate glass matrix immobilizes radioactive waste
  (Hanford Site in US; La Hague in France — vitrification)
 Telescope mirror blanks (large grinding blanks, not Zerodur-class)

TYPE I PHARMACEUTICAL GLASS:
 Strictest chemical durability class (ISO 4802, USP Type I)
 Required for all injectable drug formulations (IV, IM, SC)
 Standard: borosilicate (Schott FIOLAX, Corning Valor)
 Critical: drug stability; no leaching of glass ions into formulation
 Tungsten contamination: glass-to-metal seals in prefilled syringes
  → trace tungsten can denature protein biologics; major QA issue
```

## Fused Silica and Quartz Glass

```
FUSED SILICA (VITREOUS SILICA)
================================

COMPOSITION: Pure SiO₂; no modifiers
Produced by: fusing high-purity quartz crystal or synthetic SiO₂

TYPES:
 Type I: natural quartz crystallite (OH content ~5 ppm or less)
 Type II: electrically fused natural quartz (higher OH, ~30-200 ppm)
 Type III: synthetic (flame hydrolysis of SiCl₄; high OH ~1,000 ppm)
 Type IV: synthetic (plasma-fused; very low OH; high purity)
 OH content matters: high OH absorbs at 2.7μm and overtones → limits IR use
  but OH-free glass absorbs more strongly in certain UV bands

EXTREME PROPERTIES:
 Tg: ~1,200°C (vs 520°C soda-lime)
 CTE: 0.55×10⁻⁶/°C (vs 9 for soda-lime) — 16× lower
 Working point: >2,000°C — requires special equipment
 Chemical resistance: extreme (dissolves only in HF and hot concentrated NaOH)
 Transmission: 180nm (deep UV) to ~4μm (near-IR) — broad range
 UV radiation resistance: does not solarize (UV darkening) unlike soda-lime

APPLICATIONS:
 Semiconductor wafer fabrication:
  Quartz diffusion tubes (1,050°C+ processes; must not contaminate Si)
  Quartz wafer carriers, boats, paddles for furnace processing
  Quartz bell jars for epitaxial growth, LPCVD
  Any high-temperature process near silicon wafers = quartz glass

 UV optics:
  Excimer laser (193nm ArF; 248nm KrF) photolithography optics
  Soda-lime absorbs UV; fused silica transparent to 180nm
  DUV (deep UV) stepper lenses: fused silica blanks, ground to nanometer
  accuracy; all semiconductor fab lithography optics use fused silica

 Astronomical mirrors:
  Large telescope primary/secondary mirrors: ULE glass (Corning)
  or Zerodur (Schott) preferred for near-zero CTE
  Fused silica: backup for smaller optics

 Specialty lab: UV-transparent cuvettes; UV windows for spectrometers
```

## Lead Crystal and Lead-Free "Crystal"

```
LEAD CRYSTAL
=============

COMPOSITION:
 Full lead crystal (UK/EU standard): PbO ≥24%; SiO₂ ~55%; K₂O ~10%
 Lead "cristal": PbO 10-23%
 Crystallin: PbO <10%; technically not "crystal"

PROPERTIES:
 Refractive index: ~1.56 (vs 1.52 soda-lime)
 Dispersion (Abbe): lower than soda-lime → more "fire" in cut facets
 Density: ~3.1 g/cm³ (vs 2.5 soda-lime) → heavier feel
 Hardness: Mohs ~5.5 (softer than soda-lime ~6) → easier hand cutting
 Ringing sound: resonant when tapped — acoustic quality from density
 Working temperature: ~900-1,000°C (lower than soda-lime → easier forming)

WHY LEAD:
 PbO is both flux (lowers working temperature) AND high-RI network modifier
 The combination of lowered hardness (cuttable by wheel) + high RI + low
 working point was the basis for English lead crystal tradition (Ravenscroft 1676)

HEALTH CONCERNS:
 Lead leaches from glass into acidic liquids stored for extended periods
 Most dangerous: lead crystal decanters filled with wine, port, spirits
  Lead levels in wine stored 1+ month in lead decanter: up to 1,000+ μg/L
  FDA and EU limits for lead in beverages: 10-100 μg/L (varies by category)
 Food-contact lead crystal: EU restricted commercial use; still legal for
  occasional use tableware; most manufacturers switched to lead-free
 Modern "crystal": BaO, K₂O, ZnO, or Ti/Zr additions to achieve
  high RI + optical performance without lead

MAJOR BRANDS:
 Waterford (Ireland): iconic cut crystal; major UK/EU wedding gift tradition
 Riedel (Austria): wine glasses; focus on form over decoration
 Baccarat (France): oldest major crystal house (1764); prestige market
 Swarovski: fine lead crystal for jewelry/decoration, not tableware
 Schott Zwiesel: Tritan crystal (non-lead; BaO/ZnO/TiO₂) — bar industry
```

## Optical Glass

```
OPTICAL GLASS — PRECISION COMPOSITION
=======================================

PURPOSE: precisely controlled RI and Abbe number for lens design

SCHOTT CATALOG: 90+ glass types; each identified by type code
 N-BK7: Standard borosilicate crown; RI 1.5168, Abbe 64.2; most common
 N-SF11: Dense flint; RI 1.7847, Abbe 25.8; high dispersion
 N-LAK22: Lanthanum dense crown; RI 1.6510, Abbe 56.2
 P-LASF50: Heavy flint; RI 1.8070, Abbe 40.9
 LOHSM (heavy metals): Nb₂O₅, Ta₂O₅, La₂O₃ for very high RI up to ~2.0

MANUFACTURERS:
 Schott (Germany, Mainz): oldest; most comprehensive catalog
 Ohara (Japan): major catalog, slightly different type designations
 Hoya (Japan): primarily for photographic optics
 CDGM (China): growing market share; broader price range

PRODUCTION REQUIREMENTS:
 Platinum-lined crucibles: no contamination from refractory
 Very slow controlled cooling (weeks for large blanks): minimize stress
 Homogeneity testing: interferometry checks RI variation within blank
  → must be <5×10⁻⁶ for typical optics; <1×10⁻⁶ for precision
 Bubble/inclusion free: critical for high-end optics
  (seed density measured in mm³; specified per volume)
 Transmission: specified at multiple wavelengths (Fraunhofer lines)

ACHROMATIC OPTICS:
 Crown glass (high Abbe, moderate RI) paired with flint glass (low Abbe, high RI)
 Achromatic doublet: cemented pair cancels longitudinal chromatic aberration
  (two wavelengths brought to same focus)
 Apochromat: three glasses or fluorite; brings three wavelengths to same focus
 Used in: camera objectives, microscope objectives, telescope refractors
```

## Glass-Ceramics

```
GLASS-CERAMICS — CONTROLLED DEVITRIFICATION
============================================

DEFINITION:
 Start as glass → heat treatment causes controlled partial crystallization
 Crystalline phase(s) embedded in residual glassy matrix
 Properties: intermediate between ceramic and glass; often better than either alone

DISCOVERY:
 S. Donald Stookey (Corning), 1957
 Accidentally: furnace malfunction overheated photosensitive glass
 Expected: molten glass would run out of furnace; expected disaster
 Actual: glass became white and opaque — and did NOT break
 Investigation: controlled crystallization; the material was strong
 This led to: Pyroceram (first commercial glass-ceramic)

PROCESS:
 1. Melt glass with nucleating agents (TiO₂, ZrO₂, P₂O₅)
 2. Form glass object by standard methods
 3. Nucleation heat treatment: hold at 650-800°C
    → nucleating agents create crystal nuclei throughout material
 4. Crystal growth heat treatment: hold at 800-1,000°C
    → crystals grow from nuclei; volume fraction/size controlled
 5. Result: 40-80% crystalline by volume; properties change dramatically

KEY GLASS-CERAMICS:

 ZERODUR (Schott, 1968):
  LAS (lithium aluminosilicate) glass-ceramic
  CTE: ~0.0±0.1×10⁻⁶/°C over 0-50°C range — effectively zero expansion
  Crystal phase: β-quartz solid solution (negative CTE) + residual glass (positive CTE)
  → balanced to achieve near-zero net expansion
  Application: telescope mirror blanks (VLT, ELT primary mirrors);
               ring laser gyroscope blocks; precision instruments
  Why critical for telescopes: thermal expansion of mirror → focus changes;
   Zerodur mirrors → thermally stable even as temperature changes overnight

 MACOR (Corning, 1970s):
  Machinable glass-ceramic (fluorophlogopite mica crystals)
  The mica crystals have low cleavage strength → crack deflects along mica
  → can be machined with ordinary carbide tooling to precise dimensions
  Application: high vacuum components, electrical insulators, precision forms
   where grinding to shape is impractical

 SCHOTT CERAN / EUROKERA (stovetop glass-ceramics):
  LAS glass-ceramic; very low CTE (~0.5×10⁻⁶/°C) — thermally stable
  Smooth glass surface (easy cleaning) over ceramic body (thermal stability)
  Transmits IR (radiant element visible/effective through panel)
  Black glass-ceramic (iron oxide colorant + crystalline opacifier)
  "Ceramic cooktop" is actually glass-ceramic; not crystalline ceramic

 CORELLE (Corning, 1970):
  Laminated glass-ceramic dinnerware
  Three-layer structure:
   Outer layers: compressive stress from CTE mismatch
   Core: LAS glass-ceramic
  Result: very high strength in bending; chip resistant
  Thermal shock resistant; lightweight vs earthenware
  Failure mode: when force exceeds threshold → shatters suddenly
   (stored energy releases; characteristic "Corelle explosion")

GLASS-CERAMIC vs CERAMIC:
 Glass-ceramic: starts as glass; crystallizes partially; glassy matrix remains
 Technical ceramic: formed from powder; no glassy matrix
 Glass-ceramic can retain glass's advantages (transparency possible if
 crystal size < wavelength; forming by glass methods) + gain ceramic properties
```

## Aluminosilicate Glass — Gorilla Glass

```
GORILLA GLASS AND ION EXCHANGE STRENGTHENING
=============================================

COMPOSITION:
 Base: alkali aluminosilicate glass (high Al₂O₃ + alkali)
 More Al₂O₃ than standard glass; Na₂O + some K₂O
 Higher viscosity at working temperature than soda-lime
 Tg ~550-600°C

ION EXCHANGE STRENGTHENING:
 Immerse glass in molten KNO₃ at ~400-450°C (below Tg)
 K⁺ ions from salt replace smaller Na⁺ ions in glass surface
 K⁺ radius (1.38Å) > Na⁺ radius (1.02Å)
 K⁺ packs more tightly in existing Na⁺ sites → surface in compression
 Compressive stress layer: 30-80 μm deep (DoL = depth of layer)
 Surface compression: 600-900 MPa (glass surface normally ~70 MPa)
 Central tension: must balance compression (stored elastic energy)

PERFORMANCE:
 Compressive surface = shield: surface crack must grow against compression
 → dramatically higher force to initiate fracture
 Fragmentation: when fracture does occur, all stored energy releases →
  usually complete fracture; not tempered "dice" but sudden break
 Gorilla Glass 7 (latest): DoL ~50μm; compressive stress ~800 MPa
 Drop test: 2m drop onto rough surface → withstand (spec varies by version)

CORNING GORILLA GLASS HISTORY:
 Chemcor (1962): original ion-exchange glass; never commercialized
  (plastic windshields chosen for automotive; original application)
 2007: Steve Jobs calls Wendell Weeks (Corning CEO) 6 weeks before iPhone launch
  "I want this glass; I need 1 million sq ft of it in 6 months"
  Corning restarted Chemcor production (now with 45 years of improvement)
  → iPhone 1 launched with Gorilla Glass
 Gorilla Glass 1 (2008) → ongoing development; annual generations

COMPETITORS:
 Schott Xensation: German competitor; similar ion exchange performance
 AGC Dragontrail: Japanese; primarily for non-Apple devices
 All use same fundamental chemistry; differentiate on DoL/CS specs

AUTOMOTIVE APPLICATION (growing):
 Gorilla Glass windshields: ~30% thinner/lighter than standard laminated
 Weight saving: 50-60% of standard glass windshield (glass is heavy)
 Hybrid laminate: Gorilla Glass exterior + thin float glass inner ply
 Aerodynamic + fuel economy benefit; growing adoption
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Borosilicate = thermal shock resistant; not the same as tempered**:
Borosilicate (Pyrex) handles thermal shock because of low CTE — small temperature changes create small stress. Tempered glass handles mechanical impact because of compressive surface stress. They're different solutions to different failure modes. Borosilicate is less resistant to impact than tempered soda-lime.

**"Crystal" glass is not crystalline**:
Lead crystal and crystal glass are amorphous — they have no crystal structure. The name comes from their resemblance to rock crystal (quartz). Gorilla Glass (amorphous) is unrelated to "crystal" despite having "glass-ceramic" processing options.

**Glass-ceramics are not fully ceramic**:
A glass-ceramic has a residual glassy phase (typically 20-60% glass). It is not a traditional ceramic (sintered powder compact). The glass phase contributes processability; the crystalline phase contributes properties.

**Zerodur ≠ fused silica**:
Both have very low CTE but via different mechanisms. Fused silica: pure SiO₂, very low CTE from molecular structure. Zerodur: glass-ceramic where crystal and glass phases have opposing CTEs that cancel. Fused silica is much harder to process; Zerodur is engineered to near-net shape and polished.
