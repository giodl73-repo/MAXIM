# 05 — Specialty Glass

## Borosilicate, Lead Crystal, Optical Glass, Glass-Ceramics

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Borosilicate glass**: SiO₂ (~80%) + B₂O₃ (~13%) + Al₂O₃ (~2%) + Na₂O (~4%); boron acts as network former (triangular BO₃ groups) + some tetrahedral BO₄ — results in very low thermal expansion coefficient (~3.3×10⁻⁶/°C vs ~9×10⁻⁶ for soda-lime); invented by Otto Schott (Carl Zeiss glass laboratory, Jena, 1887); Corning Glass Works developed "Pyrex" (1915, patented by Eugene Sullivan and William Taylor) specifically for railroad lantern lenses that were fracturing from rain splash while hot; Pyrex brand now generically used; applications: lab glassware (flasks, beakers, test tubes — withstands sudden temperature changes and chemical attack); bakeware (oven-safe); lighting (high-intensity discharge lamps, halogen bulbs); pharmaceutical packaging (Type I borosilicate ampules, vials — highest chemical resistance, non-reactive with drugs); telescope mirror blanks; nuclear waste storage glass
- **Fused silica / quartz glass**: pure SiO₂, no modifiers; Tg ~1,200°C; CTE ~0.55×10⁻⁶/°C (extremely low); optically transparent from deep UV to near-IR; chemically inert; expensive (requires temperatures >2,000°C to melt and form); applications: semiconductor manufacturing equipment (diffusion tubes, quartz wafer boats — must withstand 1,000°C+ process temperatures without contaminating silicon); UV optics (lens systems that transmit UV — soda-lime absorbs UV); astronomical telescope mirrors; extreme thermal applications; VLSI circuit photolithography (excimer laser optics require fused silica transparency at 193nm)
- **Lead crystal**: traditional "crystal" = lead oxide glass (24% PbO = "full lead crystal" by UK/EU standard; EC standard = 24% PbO); lead glass properties: high RI (~1.56 vs 1.52 soda-lime), high dispersion → sparkle and brilliance; softer than soda-lime (Mohs ~5.5 vs 6) → can be hand-cut (wheel cutting) to sharp facets; density ~3.0 g/cm³ (heavier "feel"); ringing tone when tapped; health concerns: lead leaches into acidic liquids stored in lead crystal decanters; EU restricted "lead crystal" designation in 2005; modern "crystallin" = BaO or K₂O or ZnO substituting for PbO to achieve optical properties without lead; Waterford, Riedel, Baccarat = major crystal brands
- **Optical glass**: precise, controlled composition for specific RI and Abbe number combinations; Schott, Ohara, Hoya as primary manufacturers; Schott catalog = 90+ glass types spanning RI 1.45-2.0 and Abbe 20-95; critical requirements: homogeneity (RI uniform within a melt), bubble and inclusion free, specified transmission; manufacturing: platinum-lined electric melting furnaces (no contamination from refractories), very slow cooling; glass ceramics sometimes used for extreme precision optics (see below)
- **Glass-ceramics**: engineered materials that start as glass but are heat-treated to partially crystallize in a controlled way; crystalline phase embedded in remaining glassy matrix; first commercial glass-ceramic: Pyroceram (Corning, S. Donald Stookey, 1957 — discovered accidentally when a furnace malfunction overheated a photosensitive glass); properties can be dramatically different from parent glass:
  - **Corelle** (dinnerware): LAS (lithium aluminosilicate) glass-ceramic; extremely strong and chip-resistant; thermal shock resistant; the laminated sheet structure (compressive outer layers from ion exchange + LE glass-ceramic core)
  - **Zerodur** (Schott): near-zero thermal expansion (CTE ~0.02×10⁻⁶/°C) over -40 to +300°C; used for telescope mirror blanks (VLT, ELT mirrors) and laser gyroscope ring laser blocks; precise dimensional stability → no thermal distortion
  - **Macor** (Corning): machinable glass-ceramic (fluorophlogopite mica crystals interrupt crack propagation); can be machined with carbide tools to precise dimensions without grinding; used in electrical insulators, high-vacuum components
  - **Stovetop/cooktop glass-ceramics** (Schott Ceran, Eurokera): LAS glass-ceramic; low thermal expansion survives rapid thermal cycling; smooth surface; defines modern ceramic cooktops; transmits infrared from radiant heating elements below
- **Aluminosilicate glass / Gorilla Glass**: ion-exchanged alkali aluminosilicate glass; high alumina + some Na₂O or K₂O; chemical strengthening by ion exchange (immersion in molten KNO₃ → K⁺ ions replace smaller Na⁺ in glass surface → compressive stress layer → very high surface strength); Corning Gorilla Glass (first commercial in 2007, iPhone 1 — Steve Jobs called Corning CEO one month before iPhone launch demanding cover glass); depth of layer, compressive stress, and central tension as quality metrics; Gorilla Glass 7i + Victus series = current products; competitors: Schott Xensation, AGC Dragontrail; also used in automotive (Gorilla Glass windshields — lighter than standard laminated glass)
