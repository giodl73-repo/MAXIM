# Art Glass — Stained Glass, Tiffany, Studio Glass Movement (Littleton 1962)

## The Big Picture

Art glass spans from the 13th-century Gothic cathedral window to the individual studio artist working today. Three distinct traditions define the field: the monumental craft tradition of stained glass (architecture-scale, workshop production), the decorative object tradition (Gallé, Tiffany, Lalique — art applied to glass objects), and the studio glass movement (individual artists working with hot glass since 1962).

## Medieval Stained Glass

```
STAINED GLASS — TECHNIQUE AND TRADITION
=========================================

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:
 Gothic cathedrals (12th-15th c.) = defining synthesis of architecture + glass
 Before Gothic: Romanesque churches had small, thick windows; little glass
 Gothic structural innovation (pointed arch, flying buttress, ribbed vault):
  → load-bearing function moved to ribs and buttresses
  → walls could be dissolved into glass
  → stained glass became the primary surface of the interior
 Light through colored glass: theological program (divine light), narrative
  (Bible stories for illiterate congregation), donor commemoration

GLASS COLORING:
 Color in the melt (pot metal glass):
  Cobalt oxide → blue (the "Chartres blue" — intensely saturated)
  Copper → green; Mn → purple/brown; Fe → amber
  Red (ruby): the problem — copper ruby or gold ruby glass
   required "flashing" (thin layer of red on clear glass)
   → most medieval red glass = flashed ruby (red surface layer on clear)
   → abrading removes red → exposes clear → two-color in one piece

GRISAILLE:
 Iron oxide + glass fit (ground glass) + gum arabic binder
 Painted on glass surface for: outlines, facial features, shading, drapery
 Fired in muffle kiln ~600-700°C → fuses to glass surface permanently
 Also: vitreous silver stain (silver nitrate) → on firing → yellow-amber
  (silver ions exchange into glass surface → absorb blue → transmit yellow)
  Allows two colors from one piece of glass (clear + silver yellow)
 Yellow stain: painted on one side of glass → fire → yellow penetrates
  (actually: Ag⁺ ion exchange into glass surface; reduction to Ag⁰ nanoparticles)

LEADING:
 H-section lead came: glass pieces held in channel
 Solder at intersections
 Putty/cement: forced under lead → waterproofs + stiffens
 Saddle bars: horizontal iron bars across window → support against wind
 Armature: heavier iron framework for large windows

GREATEST MEDIEVAL WINDOWS:
 Chartres Cathedral (12th-13th c.):
  Rose windows + 176 lancet windows; remarkable survival
  "Chartres blue": cobalt + copper formula producing iconic intense blue
  ~180 windows still in original lead (extraordinary preservation)
 Sainte-Chapelle, Paris (1248):
  Louis IX's private chapel; almost entirely glass walls
  15 windows (mostly original glass); 6,458 individual panels
 Canterbury Cathedral (12th-15th c.): multiple phases; Trinity Chapel windows
 York Minster: "Great East Window" (1405-08, John Thornton); world's largest
  medieval stained glass (72 narrative panels)

19th CENTURY REVIVAL:
 Gothic Revival architecture → demand for medieval-style glass
 Problem: the medieval techniques were largely lost
 Reconstruction research: Charles Winston (1847 treatise); William Morris
  and Edward Burne-Jones: Pre-Raphaelite figures + William Morris
  medieval designs but 19th-c. production methods
 Sowers (glass studios) formed: Hardman, Clayton & Bell, Lavers & Barraud (UK)
 Morris & Co. (1861): Arts & Crafts windows; Burne-Jones cartoons
```

## Tiffany Glass and Studios

```
LOUIS COMFORT TIFFANY (1848-1933)
===================================

BACKGROUND:
 Son of Charles Lewis Tiffany (Tiffany & Co. founder)
 Trained as painter; traveled to North Africa and Europe
 Interests: Islamic architecture, Byzantine mosaics, Japanese decoration
 Transitioned from painting to decorative arts ~1875

FAVRILE GLASS:
 Patented 1894; name from Old English "fabrile" (handmade)
 Iridescent art glass: metallic luster from metal oxide vapors
  introduced into furnace during blowing → surface metallic film
  (same physics as luster glass — thin-film interference)
 Colors: peacock blue, gold, amber, green; iridescent surface shift
 Distinctive: each piece unique; blown by Tiffany Studios gaffers
  from Tiffany's designs; signed "L.C.T. Favrile"

TIFFANY STUDIOS (1893-1933):
 New York; employed hundreds of craftspeople
 Corona/Queens, NY furnace operations + design/construction studios
 Primary products:
  Leaded glass lamps: the most famous product
  Flat windows (ecclesiastical, residential, mausoleum)
  Mosaic panels and architectural elements

LEADED LAMP SHADES:
 Hundreds of designs in continuous production (~10,000 designs documented)
 Famous designs: Wisteria, Peony, Dragonfly, Laburnum, Daffodil
 Technique: Tiffany's copper foil method (not traditional H-came)
  Copper tape wrapped around each glass piece edge → pieces assembled →
  soldered together (foil method allows curved 3D surface vs flat came)
 Glass used: Favrile blown glass, opalescent glass, textured glass
 Value (2024):
  Common designs: $5,000-$50,000
  Wisteria shade: $1-2.5 million+ (major auction records)
  Largest authentic record: Wisteria table lamp ~$2.8M (Christie's)
 Forgeries: extensive; enormous market for reproduction and fake Tiffany
  Authentication: Tiffany's signed pieces; studios marks; provenance documentation

TIFFANY WINDOWS:
 Opalescent glass (white glass containing calcium phosphate or tin):
  Tiffany preferred for windows vs traditional clear pot metal glass
  → richer, more varied light transmission; more painterly effect
 Traditional glass painters objected (European tradition preferred clear glass)
 American vs European "stained glass": this debate defined the split

BANKRUPTCY:
 Depression (1929) + Tiffany's death (1933) → Studios bankrupt
 Louis C. Tiffany Foundation continued (Laurelton Hall estate, Oyster Bay)
 Legacy: enormous influence on contemporary studio glass and decorative arts
```

## Emile Gallé and the Nancy School

```
EMILE GALLÉ (1846-1904) — NANCY, FRANCE
=========================================

CONTEXT:
 Nancy (Lorraine) = art capital; Japonisme influence; Art Nouveau
 Gallé: son of glass manufacturer; trained in botany + art history
 His glass work: synthesis of science (botany), art (Japonisme, symbolism),
  and craft (technical mastery of glass as medium)

CAMEO GLASS (overlay glass):
 Multiple layers of different colored glass cased over each other
 Typically: 2-5 layers; white over dark, or dark over light, or multi-color
 Decoration: acid etching removes outer layers selectively
  → reveals inner layers of different color
  Create depth: different etching reveals different layers
 Wheel cutting refines the image after acid etching
 Marqueterie de verre: unusual — inlaying pieces of hot colored glass
  into each other while hot → extraordinary but rarely attempted
 Final effects: botanical subjects (water lilies, thistles, dragonflies),
  landscapes, insects with extraordinary naturalistic detail

THREE QUALITIES OF GALLÉ PRODUCTION:
 Industrial (marked "Gallé" after his death): fire-polished two-layer cameo
  made in large quantities; the "budget" Gallé
 Standard studio: signed pieces in his lifetime; higher quality etching
 Exhibition ("verreries parlantes" — "speaking glass"):
  Unique pieces with literary quotations engraved + complex cameo
  Philosophical/symbolist themes; the finest and rarest works
  "Clair de lune" pieces: moonlit landscape in cool tones
  Estimated value: $50,000-$500,000+ for finest exhibition pieces

DAUM FRÈRES (DAUM BROTHERS):
 Nancy school contemporaries; still operating today
 Auguste and Antonin Daum; adopted Gallé techniques after 1891
 Signature technique: powdered glass fused into matte surface (pâte de verre)
 Intercalaire technique: trapping colored glass between layers
 Current production: crystal sculpture (Daum still in business making
  pâte de verre art objects); not antiques — contemporary production
```

## René Lalique

```
RENÉ LALIQUE (1860-1945)
=========================

TWO CAREERS IN ONE LIFETIME:

PHASE 1: JEWELRY (1880s-1910):
 Art Nouveau jewelry designer; transformed the field
 Materials: enamel (plique-à-jour), horn, ivory, glass (insets)
  along with gold and precious stones
 The snake, dragonfly, female figure, insects as dominant motifs
 Museum quality: Gulbenkian Museum (Lisbon) = world's finest Lalique jewelry
 Collected by: Sarah Bernhardt; international elite
 Key shift 1905: purchased glassworks; began transitioning to glass

PHASE 2: GLASS (1910-1945):
 Press-molded glass with satin/frosted surfaces
 Technique: precision molds → glass pressed → surface finish (acid, sandblast)
 Contrast: deeply frosted opaque passages + polished clear areas
 Scale: from perfume bottle to architectural installation

PERFUME BOTTLES:
 Major clients: François Coty (sent rough designs 1907 → Lalique transformed them)
  Worth, Guerlain, Lubin: all commissioned Lalique bottles
 The perfume bottle as art object: Lalique established this concept
 Some bottles: designed so the bottle outlasts the perfume in value
 Collectors: rare Lalique perfume bottles auction: $5,000-$50,000+

CAR MASCOTS (HOOD ORNAMENTS):
 29 designs (1925-1931); mounted on Lalique-designed radiator cap
 Most famous: Victoire ("Spirit of the Wind") — woman's profile; 1928
  Chrysis: female torso; Naïads; Sirens; animals
 Illuminated by car headlights → dramatic effect
 Today: among the most collected Lalique objects ($10,000-$100,000+ for rarest)

ARCHITECTURE:
 Fontaine des Sirènes and other large installations
 Normandie (1935 ocean liner): 40 light columns; major architectural project
 Church windows and panels (different aesthetic from traditional stained glass)
 Contemporary Lalique company: still producing crystal objects, jewelry
```

## Studio Glass Movement (Harvey Littleton, 1962)

```
THE 1962 REVOLUTION
====================

PROBLEM BEFORE 1962:
 Hot glass = industrial process
 Requires: large furnace (300-500+ lbs molten glass)
            team of workers (gaffer + 3-4 assistants)
            industrial facility (not an artist's studio)
 Artists = excluded from working with hot glass independently
 The fine art world could not engage with hot glass as a medium

HARVEY LITTLETON (1922-2013):
 Professor of ceramics, University of Wisconsin-Madison
 Son of Jesse T. Littleton (Corning Glass VP — glass was in the family)
 Idea: build small-scale glass furnaces accessible to individual artists

TOLEDO WORKSHOPS (1962):
 Two workshops organized at Toledo Museum of Art, Ohio
 Spring 1962: First workshop; Littleton + Dominick Labino (Johns-Manville)
  Labino: glass technologist; designed low-melting glass formula
  (marbles — small glass pieces that melt at lower temperature; practical
   for small furnaces)
  Success: individual artists could blow glass with small furnace
 Summer 1962: Second workshop; technique refined
 Significance: proof of concept → sparked the movement

SPREAD THROUGH UNIVERSITIES:
 Littleton returned to Wisconsin; established first university glass program
 His students became faculty elsewhere: chain reaction
 Marvin Lipofsky (Berkeley, then CCAC, Oakland)
 Dale Chihuly (RISD, then Pilchuck) — most famous student
 Fritz Dreisbach, Erwin Eisch, others
 By 1980: studio glass programs at 50+ US universities
 By 2000: international presence; Europe (Venice as crossover), Japan, Australia

DALE CHIHULY (b. 1941):
 Most commercially successful studio glass artist
 Trained: Littleton at Wisconsin → Fulbright to Venice → Murano
 Founded Pilchuck Glass School (1971, near Seattle) — most important
  studio glass school in the world
 Lost sight in left eye (1976 car accident) → lost depth perception
 → delegated forming to assistants → became "director" of his own studio
 Scale: room-sized installations (Bellagio Hotel ceiling, 1998;
        Kew Gardens; V&A retrospective 2001; Chihuly Garden & Glass Seattle)
 Economic model: essentially industrial production under artistic direction
 Major works sell for $50,000-$500,000+; installations: millions

LINO TAGLIAPIETRA (b. 1934, Murano):
 The cross-fertilization figure: Murano maestro → American studio glass
 Murano career: master glassblower; became a maestro; worked in traditional forms
 1979: first visit to US; worked with Seattle studio community
 Brought Venetian techniques (retortoli, filigrana) to American studio glass
 Continues to teach; workshops globally; bridge between industrial Venice
  and studio art movement

CZECH/BOHEMIAN GLASS ART:
 Parallel development to American studio movement; not derived from it
 Industrial artistic tradition from Bohemian/Czech glass industry
 Stanislav Libenský (1921-2002) and Jaroslava Brychtová (1924-2020):
  Cast glass sculpture (kiln-cast, not blown)
  Large-scale architectural works; monumental geometric forms
  Color and light interaction = their primary medium
  International recognition; Prague's Czech Museum of Applied Arts
 Post-1989 Czech glass internationally collected
```

## Contemporary Glass Art Market

```
CONTEMPORARY GLASS ART
========================

MAJOR INSTITUTIONS:
 Corning Museum of Glass (Corning, NY):
  World's largest glass art collection; 50,000+ objects, 3,500+ years
  GlassLab residency program; annual collect contemporary
  Hot glass demonstration studio; a pilgrimage site for the field
 Pilchuck Glass School (Stanwood, WA): premier studio glass school
  Established by Chihuly + Anne Gould Hauberg; summer residencies
  International faculty; most important professional development in the field
 GAS (Glass Art Society): international membership organization
  Annual conference (moves between cities); journal

MARKET SEGMENTS:
 Blue-chip historical: Gallé, Daum (antique), Lalique (antique)
  → $10,000 to $500,000+
 Post-war/studio market: Chihuly, Libenský/Brychtová, Tagliapietra
  → $5,000 to $1,000,000+ for major works
 Emerging studio: MFA graduates, younger artists
  → $500 to $10,000 for gallery-represented work
 Craft/functional: hand-blown vessels, studio bowls
  → $50 to $2,000

GALLERY REPRESENTATION:
 Habatat Galleries (Royal Oak, MI): longest-running studio glass gallery (1971+)
 Marx-Saunders Gallery (Chicago)
 Claire Oliver Gallery (NY)
 Gallery Seomi (Seoul): growing Asian market for glass

CRAFT VS FINE ART TENSION:
 Studio glass field struggles with museum categorization
 Glass often placed in decorative arts departments, not fine art
 The "craft" tag limits auction prices and critical attention
 Some artists (Chihuly) have successfully crossed into fine art pricing
 The medium's tactility and functionality associations are persistent
  (compare: ceramics has the same struggle; Voulkos partially solved it)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Stained glass ≠ always painted**:
Much of the color in medieval stained glass is in the glass itself (pot metal glass — colored in the furnace melt). Grisaille (iron oxide paint) is applied for detail only. The major colors — blue, red, green — come from cobalt, copper, iron in the glass itself.

**Tiffany lamps vs reproductions**:
Enormous quantities of reproduction Tiffany lamps (some quite good quality) exist. Authentic Tiffany Studios pieces are signed, have documented provenance, and show specific glass characteristics. "Tiffany-style" is not "Tiffany" — the price difference is 1,000-fold.

**The studio glass movement is American (mostly)**:
The 1962 Toledo workshops created the studio glass movement as an art education tradition. Czech/Bohemian glass art developed separately as an industrial art tradition, not from Littleton's workshops. The two traditions eventually cross-fertilized through international exhibitions.

**Gallé died in 1904; "Gallé" pieces were still made after**:
After Emile Gallé's death, the factory continued production under his name (and continues today as a fine art company). Post-1904 pieces are marked with a star after the "Gallé" signature. Valuable but not the same as Gallé's personal work.
