# 09 — Art Glass

## Stained Glass, Tiffany, Studio Glass Movement (Littleton 1962)

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Medieval stained glass**: the greatest artistic glass tradition; stained glass windows in European Gothic cathedrals = the defining artistic medium of the High Middle Ages; glass colored in melt (cobalt blue, copper green, manganese purple, iron green/amber) or with flashed glass (thin layer of colored glass on clear glass — ruby red from gold or copper flashed glass, then abraded to reveal both colors); leading (came) = H-section lead strips holding glass pieces together; painting with vitreous enamels (grisaille = gray-black iron oxide glass paint for details and shading, fired onto glass); greatest examples: Chartres Cathedral (12th-13th c., rose windows and lancets — the finest blues in the world, "Chartres blue" from cobalt); Sainte-Chapelle, Paris (1248 — extraordinary survival, 15 windows); Canterbury Cathedral; York Minster; techniques largely lost and reinvented in 19th century Gothic Revival
- **Leaded glass technique**: cut glass pieces to pattern (using glass cutter, grozing pliers, grinder); place in came channels; solder came joints; putty/cement to seal and reinforce; lead came sizes (3mm to 12mm face width); H-came (joining), U-came (perimeter); repair vs replacement of historic windows = major conservation debate; authenticity of re-leading original glass vs structural necessity
- **Tiffany glass and lamps**: Louis Comfort Tiffany (1848-1933); son of Tiffany & Co. founder; revolutionary glass art; Favrile glass = his iridescent art glass (patented, blown with metallic luster from metallic oxides — extraordinary color range); Tiffany Studios (1893-1933) → the leaded glass lamp shades; thousands of designs (Wisteria, Peony, Dragonfly most famous and most valuable); authentic vs reproduction; Wisteria shade auction records $1M+; Tiffany's flat windows (mausoleum windows, church installations); copper foil technique (thin copper tape wrapped around each glass piece, soldered together — Tiffany improved on traditional came for curved, 3D lampshades)
- **Emile Gallé and Nancy School**: Emile Gallé (1846-1904), Nancy, France; cameo glass (acid-etched carved glass with up to 5 layers of different colored overlay glass carved back to reveal layers — complex botanical/insect/landscape subjects); wheel-engraved cameo; marqueterie de verre (inlaying pieces of hot glass into each other); Gallé invented a "suicide" glass (opalescent glass that goes wrong at times — possibly apocryphal); Daum Brothers (still operating in Nancy today — crystal sculpture and vases); Art Nouveau naturalistic subjects (dragonflies, water lilies, mistletoe, Oriental influence from Japonism)
- **René Lalique** (1860-1945): started as jewelry designer (Art Nouveau jewelry, 1890s-1900s); transitioned to glass after 1910; press-molded glass with frosted/satin surfaces, then polished or stained highlights; car hood ornaments (mascots) — most famous (Victoire "Spirit of the Wind", Chrysis); perfume bottles (Coty, Worth, Guerlain commissioned Lalique bottles); architecture (Lalique panels, screens, fountains); tableware; still operating as company today (modern Lalique crystal); Lalique glass recognizable by his technical mastery of pressed glass surface texture and relief
- **Studio glass movement (Harvey Littleton, 1962)**: before 1962, hot glass artmaking was an industrial process requiring large teams and furnaces; not accessible to individual artists; Harvey Littleton (University of Wisconsin ceramics professor, son of Corning Glass VP) → organized two workshops at Toledo Museum of Art, 1962, with glass technologist Dominick Labino (Johns-Manville) → developed small-scale furnace and lower-melting glass formulas accessible to individual artists; this created the "studio glass movement" — artists could work independently with hot glass; Littleton's students spread across US universities establishing programs; the movement internationalized; now thousands of studio glass artists; major figures: Dale Chihuly (Seattle — enormous installations, his studio is essentially industrial but artistically driven; his personal injuries → team-based production; blown glass installations at Bellagio, Kew Gardens); William Morris; Richard Marquis; Lino Tagliapietra (Venetian maestro who crossfertilized Murano technique with American studio movement)
- **Czech/Bohemian art glass**: separate from American studio movement; long industrial tradition; Czech art glass studios; Stanislav Libenský and Jaroslava Brychtová (cast glass sculpture — monumental architectural glass casts); Bohumil Eliáš; post-1989 Czech glass internationally recognized; UMĚLECKOPRŮMYSLOVÉ MUSEUM (Prague museum of decorative arts)
- **Contemporary glass art market**: major galleries (Habatat, Marx-Saunders in Chicago); Corning Museum of Glass (largest glass collection in world); GAS (Glass Art Society); auction records; tension between craft vs fine art status; glass in public collections (V&A, Smithsonian, LACMA); blown vs kiln-formed vs lampwork as different subfields with different communities
