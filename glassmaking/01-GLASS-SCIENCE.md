# 01 — Glass Science

## Silicate Networks, Viscosity, Annealing, Tg — Structure and Properties

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Silicate network structure**: SiO₄ tetrahedra are the building blocks; in pure silica glass (fused silica/vitreous silica), every oxygen is shared between two silicon atoms → 3D continuous random network; no crystalline order; random bond angles prevent crystal nucleation at normal cooling rates; this disordered structure = the defining feature of glass; William Zachariasen (1932) formulated the random network theory of glass structure
- **Network formers vs modifiers vs intermediates** (Zachariasen/Warren classification):
  - **Network formers**: can form glass alone; SiO₂ (silicon), B₂O₃ (boron), P₂O₅ (phosphorus), GeO₂ (germanium); each forms an oxide with small, highly charged cation in tetrahedral or triangular coordination with oxygen
  - **Network modifiers**: cannot form glass alone; break up the network by providing non-bridging oxygens; Na₂O, K₂O (alkali oxides), CaO, MgO, BaO (alkaline earth oxides); each modifier oxide provides two non-bridging oxygens; result = interrupted network = lower melting point, lower viscosity, lower chemical durability; necessary to make glass workable at practical temperatures
  - **Intermediates**: can act as either former or modifier depending on conditions; Al₂O₃ (usually substitutes for Si), ZnO, TiO₂, PbO
- **Viscosity and the working range**: glass is shaped by viscosity control; the key viscosity reference points (Littleton scale for soda-lime glass):
  - Melting (~1,500°C, ~10 Pa·s): fully fluid, homogeneous melt
  - Working point (~1,100°C, ~10³ Pa·s): fluid enough to blow, press, draw
  - Softening point (~720°C, ~10⁷·⁶ Pa·s): glass deforms under own weight
  - Annealing point (~550°C, ~10¹²·⁴ Pa·s): stresses relax in minutes
  - Strain point (~515°C, ~10¹³·⁸ Pa·s): below here, glass too rigid to relax
  - Borosilicate glass Tg (~565°C); fused silica (~1,200°C) — enormous working range, requires special equipment
- **Annealing**: glass that cools rapidly develops internal stress (outer surface cools and stiffens first; interior contracts later, putting surface in compression and interior in tension — or vice versa depending on geometry); stressed glass can spontaneously fracture; annealing = controlled slow cooling through the annealing/strain range to allow stresses to relax; lehr = long annealing oven with temperature gradient (industrial); float glass cooled through lehr at controlled rate; unannealed glass (poorly cooled studio glass) breaks unpredictably
- **Thermal expansion**: critical material property; coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) must be matched between glass components that are fused (e.g., glass to glass seals, glass to metal seals, optical coatings); soda-lime glass CTE ~9×10⁻⁶/°C; borosilicate ~3.3×10⁻⁶/°C (much lower = better thermal shock resistance); fused silica ~0.55×10⁻⁶/°C; thermal shock resistance ∝ 1/CTE (lower expansion = better resistance to sudden temperature changes)
- **Optical properties**: refractive index (RI) of glass controlled by composition; soda-lime glass RI ~1.52; heavy lead glass ~1.9; high-RI is important for optics, camera lenses; Abbe number (V) = dispersion measure (how much RI varies with wavelength); crown glass (low dispersion, lower RI) vs flint glass (high dispersion, higher RI); achromatic doublet lens combines crown + flint to cancel chromatic aberration; germanate and chalcogenide glasses for infrared optics
- **Devitrification**: the enemy of glassmakers; crystallization during cooling or reheating converts glass to opaque crystalline ceramic; nucleation and growth of crystals at specific temperatures; controlled devitrification = glass-ceramics (Corning NeoCeram, Zerodur, Pyroceram, Corelle); uncontrolled = ruined piece or product; glassmakers add nucleation inhibitors (Al₂O₃, La₂O₃) to prevent or control; temperature-time profiles designed to avoid devitrification range
- **Chemical durability**: glass in water undergoes ion exchange (Na⁺ leaches out, H⁺ or H₃O⁺ replaces it) → silica-rich surface layer → "weathering film"; soda-lime glass in rain/humidity: weakens over decades; chemical durability improved by: higher silica content, addition of alumina (Al₂O₃), reducing alkali; pharmaceutical glass (Type I borosilicate) must pass strict durability standards to prevent drug contamination; "glass corrosion" in museum collections (antique glass turning iridescent, crizzled, or powdery from leaching)
