# Float Glass — Pilkington Process, Flat Glass Revolution, Coatings

## The Big Picture

The float glass process is one of the great manufacturing innovations of the 20th century. Before 1959, flat glass required grinding and polishing to achieve flatness — expensive, slow, and wasteful. After float, a continuous ribbon of optically flat glass emerged from a bath of molten tin and needed no further surface treatment. Within 20 years it replaced every previous flat glass manufacturing method globally.

```
FLOAT GLASS PROCESS
=====================

FURNACE → TIN BATH → ANNEALING LEHR → CUTTING

   Batch mix + cullet
          |
   MELTING FURNACE
   (~1,550°C; continuous)
          |
   REFINING ZONE
   (~1,450°C; bubbles removed)
          |
   FOREHEARTH/CONDITIONER
   (~1,100°C; cool to working temperature)
          |
          ↓ Molten glass flows onto tin bath surface
   ——————————————————————————————————————————————
   TIN BATH (50-60m long)
   ——————————————————————————————————————————————
   Entry: ~1,050°C, glass ~3m wide
   Glass floats on molten tin (ρ_glass ~2.5; ρ_tin ~7.3 g/cm³)
   Width controlled by graphite/ceramic edge restrainers
   Thickness controlled by draw speed (faster = thinner)
   Atmosphere: N₂ + 5-10% H₂ (prevents tin oxidation)
   Exit: ~600°C (below tin solidification but above strain point)
   ——————————————————————————————————————————————
          |
   ANNEALING LEHR (60-100m)
   Controlled slow cooling to ~60°C
          |
   CUTTING ROOM
   Laser or diamond scribe → break
   Standard jumbo: 3.21m × 6.0m
          |
   WAREHOUSE + SECONDARY PROCESSING
```

## The Pilkington Discovery

```
HISTORY AND DEVELOPMENT
========================

INVENTOR: Alastair Pilkington (1920-1995)
 Note: he was an employee of Pilkington Brothers (eventually knighted);
 the company was named after a different branch of the family;
 Alastair was a distant relation — hired 1947, developed float 1952-1959

DEVELOPMENT TIMELINE:
 1952: Alastair Pilkington has the key insight (reportedly watching
  washing up — scum floating on water surface = flat surface idea)
 1952-1955: small-scale laboratory experiments; prove concept viable
 1955-1959: pilot plant trials at Cowley Hill, St Helens;
            multiple failures; continuous refinement
 1959: commercial production begins; process announced publicly
 ~£7 million development cost (enormous for the era)
 The company almost ran out of money before success

COMPETITORS:
 Pilkington licensed the float process to all major flat glass makers
 Rather than attempt secrecy, Pilkington chose licensing
 First license: PPG (Pittsburgh Plate Glass, USA), 1962
 Eventually 40+ licensees globally
 License income: >£120 million over the life of the patents
 This licensing strategy = one of the great industrial licensing decisions

PRE-FLOAT COMPARISON:
 Polished plate glass:
  Rolled glass → anneal → grind both surfaces with abrasive → polish
  Machine continuous polisher: 400m of glass at a time
  Cost: ~3× float glass; wastage: ~25-30% of glass ground away
  Quality: optically flat but expensive, wasteful
 Drawn sheet glass:
  Fourcault/PPG processes → vertical draw from tank
  Cost: lower than plate
  Quality: draw lines, slight wavy distortion (visible in old windows)
 Float: optically flat (gravity and surface tension, no grinding);
        fire-polished (both surfaces smooth from glass physics);
        no draw lines; continuous high-volume production
```

## Float Process Details

```
THE TIN BATH — PHYSICS
========================

WHY TIN:
 Liquid at glass working temperatures (Sn melts at 232°C;
  glass enters at ~1,050°C; exits at ~600°C)
 Higher density than glass (Sn ~7.3 g/cm³ vs glass ~2.5 g/cm³)
  → glass floats; completely supported
 Surface tension of glass slightly less than tin → glass spreads to ~7mm
  "equilibrium thickness" (if allowed to spread freely)
 Tin doesn't react significantly with glass at working conditions
  (small amount of SnO₂ migration to glass bottom surface — detectable)

THICKNESS CONTROL:
 Equilibrium: unconstrained glass ribbon → 7mm thick naturally
 Thicker (>7mm): use "spigots" (graphite paddles that push glass inward
               from sides) → glass piles up → thicker
 Thinner (<7mm): stretch ribbon using "top rolls" (graphite-capped wheels
               grip glass edges; rotating outward → stretches glass thin)
 Standard products: 2mm, 3mm, 4mm, 5mm, 6mm, 8mm, 10mm, 12mm
 Commercial minimum: ~1.6mm (very thin flat glass for some applications)
 Commercial maximum: ~25mm (thick cast/processed float)

ATMOSPHERE:
 N₂ + 5-10% H₂ in enclosed tin bath enclosure
 Why reducing: prevent tin oxidation (SnO₂ would contaminate glass surface)
 H₂: reductant; also prevents any trace O₂ from reacting
 Atmosphere maintained at slight positive pressure: keep out ambient air
 Temperature gradient: hottest at entry end; cooling toward exit

TIN SIDE vs AIR SIDE:
 Bottom surface (tin-side): ~30 ppm SnO₂ migrates into glass
  Detectable: UV fluorescence at 365nm → tin side fluoresces white
  Practical importance: some coatings adhere differently to tin vs air side
  The SnO₂ is a few microns deep; affects ion exchange depth if used
 Top surface (air-side): no contamination; slight inherent flatness advantage
 Visual: both appear identical to naked eye

FLOAT LINE ECONOMICS:
 Continuous operation: line runs non-stop for 10-15 years (one "campaign")
 Startup: weeks to bring furnace to operating temperature; expensive
 A cold restart = months of time + millions of dollars
 Unplanned shutdown: potentially catastrophic loss
 Energy: glass furnace = major energy consumer; 5,000-8,000 kWh per tonne
 Scale: one float line produces 500,000-1,000,000 tonnes glass/year
        equivalent to enough glass to cover 20+ square miles per year
```

## Coatings on Float Glass

```
GLASS COATINGS — THE VALUE-ADD LAYER
=======================================

COATING METHODS:
 ONLINE (pyrolytic / "hard coat"):
  Applied during manufacture; glass still hot (500-700°C in lehr)
  CVD (chemical vapor deposition): precursor vapors react on glass surface
    → thin crystalline coating bonded to glass network
  Properties: very durable (hard); can withstand subsequent tempering
              and laminating; but optical performance usually less than offline
  Examples: SnO₂:F (fluorine-doped tin oxide) = online low-E;
            TiO₂ = online self-cleaning

 OFFLINE (sputter / "soft coat"):
  Applied to cold glass after manufacture and cutting
  Magnetron sputtering: metal targets (Ag, Si, Nb, etc.) sputtered in vacuum
    → atoms deposit on glass surface in thin controlled layers
  Build up multi-layer stack of nanometer-thin layers
  Properties: much better optical performance than online
              but cannot be tempered after coating (heat damages layers)
              Must be processed into IGU (sealed unit) promptly
              Cannot be exposed to harsh environments (hence "soft")
  Standard residential Low-E = sputtered triple-silver "soft coat"

LOW-E GLASS:
 Purpose: reduce long-wave infrared (heat radiation) transfer through glass
 Physics: window glass transmits solar radiation (short-wave, 0.3-3μm)
          but glass itself absorbs and re-emits long-wave IR (~10μm)
          Low-E coating: thin metallic layer reflects long-wave IR back
          → reduces heat transfer between inside and outside
 Silver layers: primary functional element; 1-3 silver layers
 Dielectric layers between silver: antireflection (maintains visible transmission)
  also protect silver (Ag oxidizes easily without protection)
 Standard triple-silver low-E stack:
  Glass | ZnO | Ag | NiCrOx | ZnO | Ag | NiCrOx | ZnO | Ag | ZnO | Glass
  ~100 layers total; total thickness ~100-200nm; invisible to naked eye
 Performance metrics:
  U-value (heat loss coefficient): target <1.0 W/m²K for triple glazing
  SHGC (solar heat gain coefficient): balance between solar heat gain
   and visible transmission; climate-dependent optimization

SOLAR CONTROL COATINGS:
 Reflect or absorb solar near-infrared (0.78-3μm)
 Reduces cooling load; important in hot climates with large glass facades
 SHGC <0.25 possible; may be colored (reflective blue/silver facades)
 Commercial buildings: combination of low-E + solar control

SELF-CLEANING GLASS (Pilkington Activ):
 TiO₂ (titanium dioxide) online hard coat; ~15nm thick
 Mechanism 1 — PHOTOCATALYTIC:
  UV light (from sun) activates TiO₂ (semiconductor; bandgap 3.2 eV)
  → generates reactive O species → oxidize and break down organic dirt
  → organic compounds decomposed to CO₂ + H₂O
 Mechanism 2 — HYDROPHILIC:
  TiO₂ surface is highly hydrophilic when UV-activated
  → water spreads as sheet rather than beads
  → sheets off, taking loose inorganic dirt with it
 Requires: UV exposure; doesn't work in dark/low-UV conditions
 Best application: skylights, conservatory roofs, hard-to-clean glazing
 Limitation: doesn't remove all types of soil; needs rain to rinse

ANTI-REFLECTIVE COATINGS:
 Reduce glass surface reflectance (standard glass: ~8% total at normal incidence)
 Single-layer MgF₂: ~1.5% reflectance (quarter-wave at design wavelength)
 Multi-layer: <0.5% broadband (sputtered; expensive)
 Applications: display glass (tablets, monitors), museum glass, solar cells
               (higher transmission → more power)
 Museum glass: both AR and non-reflective + optically clear;
               conserved artworks need true optical quality AR
```

## Insulating Glass Units (IGU / Double Glazing)

```
IGU CONSTRUCTION
=================

Standard residential IGU:
 Pane 1 (exterior) + sealed spacer + Pane 2 (interior)
 Standard dimensions: 4mm glass + 16mm spacer + 4mm glass = 24mm total

SPACER BAR:
 Traditional: hollow aluminum profile with desiccant beads
  (silica gel or molecular sieve absorb internal moisture)
  Thermal bridging: aluminum has high thermal conductivity → cold spot at edge
 "Warm edge" spacer: stainless steel (lower conductivity), composite spacer
  (Swisspacer, TGI spacer, etc.) → reduces edge heat loss
  Edge condensation problem: aluminum spacer → cold at edge →
  first point of condensation on inside surface (unsightly)

EDGE SEALS:
 Primary seal: PIB (polyisobutylene) butyl rubber; applied to spacer
  → seals against moisture vapor transmission
 Secondary seal: polysulfide or silicone; structural bond of unit
 Structural silicone only for structural glazing (no frame retention)

CAVITY GAS:
 Air: standard; free; some thermal benefit vs solid glass
 Argon (Ar): most common upgrade; thermal conductivity 0.016 W/mK
  vs air 0.026 W/mK → better thermal performance; ~10% cost premium
 Krypton (Kr): better than argon; expensive; used in thin triple glazing
 SF₆ (sulfur hexafluoride): acoustic performance; excellent for airports,
  studios; major greenhouse gas (25,200 GWP); being phased out

SURFACE NUMBERING:
 Counting from outside to inside:
 Surface 1: exterior face of outer pane
 Surface 2: interior face of outer pane (cavity side)
 Surface 3: exterior face of inner pane (cavity side)
 Surface 4: interior face of inner pane
 Low-E coating position:
  #2 (heating climates): reflects interior heat back in; warm climates: reduces heat from outside
  #3: common for triple glazing center lite
  Rule: coating faces the cavity (protected); never on #1 or #4

TRIPLE GLAZING:
 Three panes; two cavities; higher performance
 U-value <0.8 W/m²K possible
 Standard in Scandinavian construction; growing in UK/Germany
 Weight: 30-50% heavier than double; hardware must accommodate

PERFORMANCE STANDARDS:
 UK: Building Regulations Part L; Windows Energy Rating (WER) A-G
 EU: EN 14351-1; Energy Performance of Buildings Directive
 US: ENERGY STAR criteria; NFRC U-factor + SHGC label
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Float glass ≠ tempered or laminated**:
Float is the process by which flat glass is made. The resulting "annealed float glass" is the standard building block. Tempering and laminating are subsequent processes applied to float glass for specific applications. A float glass pane by itself is the weakest, most dangerous form of flat glass in a hazardous location.

**Low-E "hard coat" vs "soft coat"**:
Hard coat (online pyrolytic): applied during manufacture; very durable; can be tempered after; lower performance. Soft coat (offline sputtered): applied cold; much better performance; cannot be tempered after application; must be in sealed IGU. The coating types aren't interchangeable — installers must know which type they're handling.

**Tin side matters for some applications**:
Tin side vs air side can affect: ion exchange strengthening depth (Gorilla Glass type processes), specific coating adhesion, some acid etching effects. For most standard window applications, it's irrelevant. Detect with 365nm UV light — tin side fluoresces white.

**Float lines don't stop**:
A float line is a continuous operation running for 10-15 years. There is no "batch production" — the furnace runs continuously and the ribbon runs continuously. This is why supply chain reliability matters enormously to float glass producers.
