# 04 — Float Glass

## Pilkington Process, Flat Glass Revolution, Low-E Coatings

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **The Pilkington process (float glass)**: invented by Sir Alastair Pilkington (no family relation to Pilkington Brothers glass company, despite the name — or rather, distant; the point is it was invented by an employee, not the founding family); process developed 1952-1959 at Pilkington Brothers, St Helens, Lancashire; patent filed 1959; announced publicly 1959; seven years of development and ~£7 million spent before commercial success; the process that replaced all previous flat glass manufacturing methods within 20 years
- **How float works**: molten glass flows continuously from tank furnace → ribbon onto a bath of molten tin (SnO₂ is solid, but Sn is liquid at glass working temperatures — melting point 232°C; glass bath temperature ~1,050°C at entry, ~600°C at exit); glass floats on the tin (glass density ~2.5 g/cm³, tin ~7.3 g/cm³ → glass floats); gravity and surface tension create a perfectly flat, fire-polished surface on both sides (no grinding/polishing needed); width controlled by mechanical edge restrainers; thickness controlled by speed of ribbon draw (faster = thinner); glass exits the tin bath, enters the annealing lehr, cooled over ~60-100m; emerge as continuous ribbon cut to standard sizes (typically 3.21m × 6m jumbo sheets); standard thicknesses: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12mm
- **Economic and quality revolution**: pre-float flat glass required grinding and polishing to achieve flat surfaces (plate glass process — expensive, wasteful); drawn glass (Fourcault/Colburn) was cheaper but had wavy distortion (visible when looking through old windows); float glass = near-optical quality, low cost, high yield; one float line produces 500,000-1,000,000 tonnes/year of glass; ~300 float lines operate globally; continuous process (lines run non-stop for 10-15 years before rebuild)
- **Tin side vs air side**: one surface of float glass contacts molten tin (bottom) = "tin side"; has ~30 ppm tin migration into glass surface (detectable by UV fluorescence — fluoresces white under 365nm UV; important for coating adhesion and some processing decisions); the other surface = "air side"; both surfaces appear identical to naked eye
- **Glass coatings on float glass**: the major value-add in architectural glass; applied either "online" (during manufacture by CVD in the tin bath or in the lehr — "hard coat") or "offline" (sputtered onto cold glass after manufacture — "soft coat"):
  - **Low-E (low-emissivity) glass**: the most important coating; reduces heat radiation transfer through glass; invisible to visible light; thin metallic or metal oxide layer (silver is the primary functional layer; 1-3 silver layers with dielectric antireflection layers between) reflects long-wave infrared (heat radiation) back; this dramatically reduces U-value (heat loss) of windows; standard in energy-efficient windows; soft-coat (sputtered triple-silver low-E) outperforms hard-coat (pyrolytic tin oxide)
  - **Solar control coatings**: reflect/absorb solar near-infrared; reduces cooling load; SHGC (Solar Heat Gain Coefficient) below 0.25 possible; colored glass or reflective coatings; relevant for commercial buildings with large glass areas
  - **Self-cleaning glass (Pilkington Activ)**: titanium dioxide (TiO₂) coating; two mechanisms: (1) photocatalytic — UV light activates TiO₂ to break down organic dirt; (2) hydrophilic — water sheets off rather than beading, washing dirt away; requires UV exposure (doesn't work in northern winters); primarily for skylights and hard-to-clean windows
- **Insulating glass units (IGU / double glazing)**: two or three glass panes separated by sealed air/gas space; edge seal (primary seal = polyisobutylene butyl; secondary seal = silicone or polysulfide); spacer bar (aluminum, thermally broken "warm edge" spacer, or stainless steel); cavity gas: air, argon (most common — low thermal conductivity), krypton (better performance but expensive), SF₆ (in some acoustic units — toxic greenhouse gas, being phased out); standard residential double-glazed IGU = 4mm glass + 16mm argon + 4mm glass with low-E coating on #2 or #3 surface (surface numbering: 1=exterior face, 2=interior face of outer pane, 3=exterior face of inner pane, 4=interior face)
- **Curved/bent glass**: reheating flat glass and sagging/slumping it over or into a mold (gravity bending); cylindrical or complex curves for automotive, architectural, interior design; cold bending (forcing flat glass into a curved frame — elastic deformation, permanent stress, limits of curvature); automotive windshields (complex double-curvature): gravity bending in pairs (matched pairs to ensure identical curvature for laminating)
