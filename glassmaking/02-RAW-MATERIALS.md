# 02 — Raw Materials

## Silica Sand, Soda Ash, Cullet, Colorants, Decolorizers

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Silica (SiO₂)**: 70-75% of soda-lime glass batch; source = silica sand (quartz sand, rounded grains, low impurity); key specification: iron content (<0.02% Fe₂O₃ for colorless container glass; <0.015% for flat glass; <0.01% for optical/flat display glass — iron gives green tint); silica sand from specific deposits: Fontainebleau (France), Lochaline (Scotland), U.S. Appalachian deposits; beach sand too fine, too round, and often contaminated with shell (CaCO₃ introduces calcium but variable); crushed quartzite (coarser, high purity) also used; silica melts at 1,713°C alone → modifiers/fluxes lower this to practical range (~1,500°C)
- **Soda ash (Na₂CO₃)**: primary flux; provides Na₂O; decomposes above 850°C releasing CO₂, leaving Na₂O in melt; produced by Solvay process (synthetic) or from natural deposits (trona from Wyoming — Green River Basin is primary US source); important historically: pre-Solvay, natural soda from burning coastal plants (barilla — Spain, used for Venetian glass) or natron (Egyptian mineral) or wood ash (contains K₂CO₃/potash — potassium flux used in Bohemian/forest glass); the shift from wood ash to soda ash in 18th-19th century changed glass properties (potash glass = more fusible, suitable for cut glass)
- **Limestone and dolomite (CaO, MgO)**: stabilizers; calcium provides durability (without Ca, Na silicate glass dissolves in water easily); dolomite (CaMg(CO₃)₂) provides both CaO and MgO simultaneously; 10-12% CaO in standard soda-lime glass; MgO slightly improves durability and processability
- **Feldspar (K,Na)(AlSi₃O₈)**: provides both alkali (K or Na) and alumina; natural mineral eliminates need for separate soda ash + alumina additions for some compositions; used in container glass to reduce batch cost
- **Cullet (broken glass)**: pre-consumer (factory cullet from broken product, trimmed edges — very clean) and post-consumer (recycled glass from collection); cullet melts at lower temperature than raw batch → energy savings (~2.5% energy saved per 10% cullet added); improves glass quality (fewer bubbles since CO₂ already driven off from carbonate batch); color sorting critical — green cullet cannot be used in clear or amber glass; color-sorted post-consumer cullet more valuable; flat glass cullet typically cannot be recycled into container glass and vice versa (different composition); cullet percentage in container glass furnaces: 50-90% in modern European plants (EU recycling targets)
- **Colorants**:
  - Iron (Fe): unavoidable impurity in most raw materials; Fe²⁺ → blue-green; Fe³⁺ → yellow-brown; the standard "green" wine bottle = controlled iron + sulfur combination; amber beer bottle = Fe-S chromophore (iron-sulfide color center, requires reducing conditions, good UV protection for beer)
  - Cobalt oxide (Co₃O₄): deep blue; very powerful (0.001-0.1% can produce strong blue); blue glass, cobalt blue decorative objects
  - Copper oxide (CuO/Cu₂O): CuO = blue-green; Cu₂O (reduced) = red; "copper ruby" glass (striking = reheating special batch at specific temp allows Cu nanoparticles to nucleate → deep red); Murano copper ruby glass tradition
  - Manganese dioxide (MnO₂): purple; also used as decolorizer (see below)
  - Chromium oxide (Cr₂O₃): green; chromium-bearing green bottle glass; stable colorant
  - Selenium (Se) + cadmium sulfide (CdS): orange to red glasses; traffic light glass; cadmium restricted (toxic) in modern use
  - Gold (Au): at 0.001-0.01%, gold nanoparticles produce ruby red (Cranberry/gold ruby glass); same mechanism as copper ruby — nanoscale particle light scattering; expensive; medieval red glass in stained windows used gold ruby
  - Uranium oxide: pre-1940s "vaseline glass" / "uranium glass" = greenish-yellow, fluorescent under UV; radioactive but low dose; collectors' item; ceased production (uranium became strategic material)
- **Decolorizers**: used to counteract iron tint in soda-lime glass for colorless products:
  - **Chemical decolorization**: convert Fe²⁺ (blue-green absorber) to Fe³⁺ (yellow absorber, weaker); use oxidizing agents (sodium nitrate, sodium sulfate/saltcake); also sulfur chemistry in amber glass
  - **Physical/complementary decolorization**: add a complementary colorant that cancels remaining tint visually; manganese dioxide (purple) + iron green → neutral gray by mixing complementary colors; selenium (red) similarly used; result = slightly gray or neutral colored but not truly colorless; energy-intensive alternative = ultra-pure raw materials + electric melting
- **Refining agents**: bubbles (seeds) in finished glass are a defect; CO₂ and SO₂ from carbonate decomposition form bubbles during melting; refining agents release gas at high temperature to promote bubble coalescence and rise: sodium sulfate (saltcake — releases SO₂/SO₃ above 1,400°C); arsenic and antimony oxides (As₂O₃ — historically, now restricted in food-contact glass); modern refining: Contamination with platinum boat fragments from melting equipment catalyzes; vacuum refining for optical glass
