# Safety Glass — Tempered, Laminated (PVB), Automotive, Standards

## The Big Picture

Annealed glass breaks into large, sharp shards — lethal in high-energy impact situations. Two technologies address this with fundamentally different strategies: tempering (break into harmless granules by pre-stressing) and laminating (hold together by bonding to plastic interlayer). The choice depends on whether you need the glass to disappear safely or to remain structurally intact after breakage.

```
SAFETY GLASS DECISION MATRIX
==============================

                    TEMPERED                LAMINATED
                    ————————————————————    ——————————————————————
Breakage pattern    Small granules ("dice")  Fractures; interlayer holds
                    — no sharp shards        — maintains integrity
Structural after    None: complete failure   Yes: reduced but usable
breakage
Penetration resist. None: glass gone         High: interlayer resists
Strength (intact)   ~4× annealed             ~2× annealed (depends on)
Can be cut after    NO (shatters)             YES (before tempering)
Weight vs annealed  Same                      Heavier (interlayer + 2+ plies)
Applications        Doors, showers, auto      Windshields, overhead, structural
                    side/rear windows         glazing, security, balustrades

ANNEALED (baseline):
 Standard float glass as produced
 Breaks: long, sharp, dagger-like fragments → most dangerous
 Required safety glazing standard: must NOT use annealed in hazardous locations
```

## Tempered (Toughened) Glass

```
THERMAL TEMPERING PROCESS
==========================

PHYSICS:
 Same mechanism as Gorilla Glass but by thermal quench, not ion exchange
 Goal: create compressive surface stress by differential cooling

PROCESS:
 1. Cut annealed float glass to final size and shape FIRST
    (cannot cut after tempering)
 2. Grind/polish any edges (edge quality affects strength)
 3. Drill any holes required (same reason — before tempering)
 4. Wash and inspect
 5. Load on horizontal roller conveyor
 6. HEAT: pass through roller furnace to ~640-680°C
    (just above Tg; glass softens slightly but doesn't flow)
 7. QUENCH: exit furnace → high-velocity air jets blast both surfaces
    simultaneously → surface cools rapidly → surface stiffens
    → interior still hot/viscous → interior cools later → contracts
    → interior contraction → surface pulled into compression
    → interior in residual tension

RESULTING STRESS PROFILE:
 Surface: ~100-200 MPa compression (high quality tempering)
 Interior: ~50-100 MPa tension (must balance; Cauchy principle)
 Stress distribution: parabolic through thickness

PROPERTIES:
 Bending strength: ~4× annealed (ASTM C1048)
 Impact resistance: dramatically higher than annealed
 Failure mode: stored elastic energy releases catastrophically
  → thousands of small "dice" granules when fracture threshold exceeded
  → size regulated (EN 12150: 40+ granules per 50cm² area minimum)
 The "dice" are not sharp (more like gravel) → dramatically reduced injury
 Thermal resistance: tolerates larger ΔT than annealed (~200°C vs ~40°C)

CANNOT BE:
 Cut (immediately shatters)
 Drilled after tempering
 Re-annealed and re-tempered (impractical)
 Marked with glass cutter (will shatter from scoring)

HEAT-STRENGTHENED GLASS (partial tempering):
 Same process; slower quench → lower surface stress (~40-70 MPa)
 Stronger than annealed; does NOT dice
 Larger fragments on failure → used where complete dice is unacceptable:
  Structural glass (fins, overhead panels where fragments must remain)
  Glass floors and balustrades (dice pattern unacceptable aesthetically + risk)

EDGE QUALITY MATTERS:
 Most tempering failures initiate at edges (stress concentration)
 Polished seamed edge (PSE): basic; ground and polished → reduces edge flaws
 Flat polished edge: 90° grinding → better uniformity
 Clear bevelled: angled polish for aesthetics + best edge quality
 "Nickel sulfide inclusions" — rare spontaneous failure of tempered glass:
  NiS particles in glass can expand slightly with time → crack initiation
  → entire pane explodes spontaneously (rare; 1:1000-1:3000 panes)
  Prevention: "heat soak testing" (hold tempered at 290°C for 8 hrs →
  any NiS inclusion causes failure in oven, not in building)
```

## Chemical Strengthening

```
CHEMICAL STRENGTHENING (ION EXCHANGE)
=======================================

PROCESS: same as Gorilla Glass (see 05-SPECIALTY-GLASS)
 KNO₃ salt bath at ~400°C (below Tg)
 K⁺ replaces Na⁺ at glass surface → compressive stress

ADVANTAGES OVER THERMAL TEMPERING:
 Applicable to thin glass (<2mm; too thin for thermal quench)
 Any shape (curved glass, complex geometry)
 No surface roller marks
 Better edge quality (thermal tempering heats to Tg; can cause edge distortion)
 Higher surface stress possible with extended treatment

APPLICATIONS:
 Smartphone screens (Gorilla Glass — most visible application)
 Pharmaceutical vials (strengthened for handling during filling/capping)
 Aircraft windows (thin; complex curve; must be chemically strengthened)
 Automotive glass (specialty applications)
 Medical devices (optics, diagnostic instrument windows)
```

## Laminated Glass

```
PVB LAMINATED GLASS
=====================

INVENTION: Edouard Benedictus (France, 1903)
 Dropped flask coated internally with celluloid nitrate solution
 Flask fell; shattered but held together
 Developed for automotive windshields; commercial production 1920s

PVB (POLYVINYL BUTYRAL) INTERLAYER:
 Production: reacted from polyvinyl alcohol + n-butyraldehyde
 Properties: clear; flexible; tough; strongly adhesive to glass
 Melting onset: ~60-70°C (bonds to glass under heat and pressure in autoclave)
 Impact energy absorption: PVB deforms and tears rather than letting glass cut
 Thickness: 0.38mm (standard), 0.76mm (structural, acoustic), 1.52mm (thick acoustic)

AUTOCLAVE PROCESS:
 1. Two clean, dry glass plies
 2. PVB sheet placed between plies (in clean room; avoid dust, moisture)
 3. Nip roll: squeeze out air bubbles; temporary bond from PVB tack
 4. AUTOCLAVE: 120-140°C, 10-12 bar (10-12 atm) for 30-45 minutes
    → PVB fully bonds to both glass surfaces under pressure/heat
 5. Remove: cooled; permanent laminate
 Windshield version: two glass plies + PVB; formed as matched pairs
  (both plies bent simultaneously → identical curvature for laminating)

SGP (SENTRYGLAS PLUS):
 Ionoplast interlayer (Kuraray, formerly DuPont)
 ~5× stiffer than PVB → post-fracture deformation much lower
 Higher tear resistance → better penetration resistance
 Applications: structural glazing (balustrades, canopies, glass floors)
  → continues to carry load after breakage; glass laminate remains intact

EVA (ETHYLENE VINYL ACETATE):
 Alternative interlayer; processed without autoclave (cast/cured)
 Used in: solar panels (between glass and silicon cells), some laminates
 Optical quality slightly lower than PVB in some applications

ACOUSTIC LAMINATED GLASS:
 Thicker PVB or specialty acoustic PVB (viscoelastic mid-layer)
 Coincidence frequency shifted; sound transmission loss improved
 STC 35-40 vs STC 28-32 for single annealed glass
 Common: hotel rooms on busy streets, recording studios, airports
```

## Automotive Glass

```
AUTOMOTIVE GLASS — SYSTEM OVERVIEW
=====================================

WINDSHIELD (front):
 MUST be laminated by law (all major markets)
 Reasons: holds together in crash (no glass intrusion into cabin)
          airbag deploys against windshield → must not shatter
          "safety" = vision maintained even if cracked
 Typical construction: 2.1mm glass + 0.76mm PVB + 2.1mm glass
  or 3mm + 0.38mm + 3mm (older standard)
 Modern trend: thinner outer glass + Gorilla Glass inner
  (Corning Gorilla Glass in Autoglass hybrid = weight saving)
 Compound curve: double curvature; formed by gravity bending
  matched glass pairs (two plies bent together for precise matching)
 Defrost: resistance heating via conductive film or printed silver paste
  lines (fine pitch; almost invisible at distance)
 UV/IR filter: PVB can contain UV absorbers; glass can have IR filter coating
 HUD-ready: special wedge PVB or inner glass with slight wedge angle
  → corrects double reflection of HUD projection onto windshield

SIDE AND REAR WINDOWS:
 MUST be tempered (most markets)
 Reason: immediate egress; fire, drowning scenarios → must be breakable
          by emergency responders (emergency hammer → one strike)
 Single glass (not laminated): can be broken by rescue team
 Side tempered glass: "dices" on impact → reduced injury; occupant can
  break with rescue hammer or center punch in emergencies

ACOUSTIC WINDSHIELDS:
 Thick PVB (typically 0.76mm or acoustic PVB grade)
 Significant NVH (noise, vibration, harshness) reduction
 Premium vehicles: ~50% thinner windshields than standard

PANORAMIC GLASS ROOFS:
 Laminated + solar control coating (reduces thermal load)
 Large format: complex double-curvature pressing
 Safety concern: overhead laminated glass must retain fragments absolutely
  → SGP interlayer preferred over PVB for structural stability

SPECIALIST:
 Embedded antenna: printed silver antenna lines for radio/GPS/4G
 Heads-up display (HUD): special optical requirements
  Standard flat glass: two reflections (inner and outer surface)
  → ghost image in HUD projection
  Solution: wedge-shaped PVB or slight glass wedge → converges
  the two reflections so they overlap → single sharp HUD image
```

## Bulletproof and Blast-Resistant Glass

```
SECURITY GLAZING
=================

BULLETPROOF GLASS:
 Multiple laminated layers; typically glass-polycarbonate-glass-polycarbonate
 Or: multiple laminated glass plies with PVB
 Polycarbonate inner layer (spall shield): catches glass fragments from last
  glass layer as bullet penetrates layers → spall containment
 Thickness: ~25mm for handgun resistance; 50-75mm+ for rifle
 Weight: very heavy (25-75+ kg/m²); structural support required
 Not "bulletproof" — "bullet-resistant" to specified threat level:
  UL 752 (US): levels 1-8 (9mm to 7.62mm high-power)
  EN 1063 (EU): classes BR1-BR7
  The glass passes if it stops the specified projectile after 3 shots

BLAST-RESISTANT GLASS:
 Fundamentally different threat: impulse load (shock wave + duration)
 vs bullet (small area, very high velocity)
 Window panels become missiles in explosive events → principal injury mechanism
 Design goal: window remains in frame; absorbs blast energy; fragments don't fly
 SGP interlayer: high stiffness → resists sagging post-fracture
 Frame design critical: the laminate must be retained by frame under blast pressure
 Standards: GSA (US government) TS01-2003; ISO 16933 blast testing

BALLISTIC GLASS AT SCALE:
 Armored vehicle glass: glass-ceramic or multi-laminate
 Bank teller windows: bullet-resistant; typically 35-40mm thick
 Presidential limousines: extensive glazing package; weight concerns
```

## Standards and Codes

```
SAFETY GLAZING STANDARDS
==========================

US STANDARDS:
 ANSI Z97.1: Safety Glazing Material Used in Buildings — test standard
  Impact test: ball drop or pendulum impact
  CPSC 16 CFR Part 1201: mandatory for hazardous locations
  Locations requiring safety glazing: doors/sidelights, shower enclosures,
  pool fencing, low-level glazing (<18" above floor in traffic areas)

EU STANDARDS:
 EN 12150-1: Tempered glass specification (composition, dimensions, flatness)
 EN 12600: Glass in building — impact pendulum test
  EN 14179: Thermally toughened and heat-soaked borosilicate
 EN 14449: Laminated safety glass

UK:
 BS 6206: Impact performance requirements for flat safety glass
 Building Regulations Part N (now Part K): glazing safety in buildings

BUILDING CODE REFERENCES:
 IBC (International Building Code) Section 2406: glazing in hazardous locations
 NFPA 80: Fire door assemblies (includes rated glass requirements)
 Rated fire glass: must resist fire AND thermal shock; different product
  (wired glass, intumescent laminated fire glass) from safety glass
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Tempered glass is NOT the same as laminated glass**:
Completely different products for different safety purposes. Tempered = fragments into granules (emergency egress; shower doors); laminated = holds together (windshields; overhead glazing). Confusing them in specification is a serious design error.

**"Toughened" = "tempered"**:
British terminology uses "toughened glass"; North American uses "tempered glass." Same product, same process. UK standard: BS EN 12150.

**Safety glass ≠ fire-rated glass**:
Safety glass (tempered, laminated) is not automatically fire-rated. Fire-rated glass must meet fire resistance criteria (integrity/insulation) AND be impact-safe. Fire-rated glass products (intumescent laminates, borosilicate) are a separate product category from standard safety glass.

**Heat-strengthened ≠ tempered**:
Heat-strengthened glass has lower surface stress (~40-70 MPa vs 100-200 MPa) and does NOT dice. It's stronger than annealed but is NOT a safety glass product by most standards. Do not substitute for tempered in safety-critical applications.

**NiS inclusions: rare but real**:
Nickel sulfide inclusions cause spontaneous failure of tempered glass after installation — without any external impact. Most visible in dramatic façade glass failures. Heat-soak testing eliminates most problematic units. The failure rate without heat soak: ~1 in 1,000-3,000 panes.
