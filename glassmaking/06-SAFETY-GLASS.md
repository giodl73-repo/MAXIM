# 06 — Safety Glass

## Tempered, Laminated (PVB), Automotive, Standards

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Why glass needs to be "safe"**: annealed flat glass breaks into large, sharp shards — dangerous in human impacts; the two approaches produce opposite fracture patterns for different safety purposes:
  - **Tempered**: shatters into small, relatively harmless granules ("pebbles") → no shards but complete structural failure; cannot be repaired → immediate loss of integrity
  - **Laminated**: holds together when broken (plastic interlayer retains glass fragments) → maintains structural integrity even after breakage; can impede penetration; heavier than tempered
- **Tempered (toughened) glass**: heat-strengthening or thermal tempering; float glass heated uniformly to above softening point (~640°C) → quenched rapidly with air jets; surface cools and stiffens first → surface put in compression; interior cools later and contracts → puts interior in tension; result: permanent surface compression stress (~100-200 MPa); fracture in tempered glass requires breaking through the compressive surface layer before propagating → much higher force required to initiate fracture; but once the fracture initiates, the stored elastic energy releases catastrophically → "dice" into thousands of small granules; tempered glass is ~4× stronger than annealed in bending; applications: shower doors, building entrance doors, oven doors, automobile side and rear windows, furniture glass; CANNOT be cut after tempering (immediately shatters)
  - Partially tempered (heat-strengthened): same process but slower quench; surface compression ~40-70 MPa; stronger than annealed but does NOT dice → larger fragments; used where complete dice failure is unacceptable (structural glass, glass floors)
- **Chemically strengthened glass**: same strengthening principle (compressive surface stress) by different method (ion exchange rather than thermal); KNO₃ bath at ~400°C → K⁺ replaces Na⁺ at surface → larger ions pack more tightly → compression; advantages: can be applied to thin glass (<2mm too thin for thermal tempering), any shape including curved glass; high compression depths possible with extended treatment; Gorilla Glass (see 05-SPECIALTY-GLASS); used for smartphone screens, aircraft windows, thin glass applications
- **Laminated glass**: two or more glass panes bonded with interlayer under heat and pressure; interlayer holds fragments together on breakage; primary applications: automotive windshields (legally required in most countries since 1930s in US; required in all EU vehicles); building safety glazing (balustrade panels, overhead glazing, hurricane glazing)
  - **PVB (polyvinyl butyral)**: most common interlayer; sheet of PVB between glass panes → autoclave processing (120-140°C, 10-12 bar) → complete bond; PVB is clear, tough, flexible, strongly adhesive to glass; standard windshield = 3mm glass + 0.38mm PVB + 3mm glass (or 2.1 + 0.76 + 2.1 configurations); acoustic laminated glass = thicker PVB (typically 0.76mm or 1.52mm) or specialty PVB with acoustic damping
  - **SGP (SentryGlas Plus, DuPont / now Kuraray)**: ionoplast interlayer; ~5× stiffer than PVB; much higher tear strength; post-breakage structural performance; used in structural glazing (glass balustrades, canopies) where continued load-bearing after breakage is required; minimal post-fracture deformation vs PVB which allows significant sagging
  - **EVA (ethylene vinyl acetate)**: alternative interlayer; processed without autoclave (cast/cure); used in solar panels (between glass and silicon cells), some specialty laminates; less optical clarity than PVB
- **Automotive glass**: windshield = laminated (required — holds together in crash, doesn't intrude into cabin, airbag deploys against it); side/rear windows = tempered (required in most markets — dices on impact, allows emergency egress by breaking); acoustic windshields = thick PVB; heated windshields (resistance heating wires); UV/IR filtering layers; heads-up display (HUD) windshields (specially wedge-shaped PVB or inner glass wedge for sharp HUD image projection — two reflections on standard flat windshield would give ghost image; wedge corrects this); embedded antennas
- **Bulletproof / blast-resistant glass**: multiple laminated layers; glass-clad polycarbonate (polycarbonate spall shield on room side — catches glass fragments from last glass layer); thicknesses from 25mm (handgun) to 75mm+ (rifle); very heavy; NSF bulletproof standards (UL 752 in US, EN 1063 in EU); blast glazing for buildings (much larger impulse load than bullet; designed to remain in frame and not become projectile)
- **Standards**: ANSI Z97.1 (US safety glazing); EN 12150 (EU tempered); EN 14449 (EU laminated); EN 12600 (impact test — 50kg bag pendulum); CPSC 16 CFR Part 1201 (US consumer product safety); BS 6206 (UK safety glazing); glazing in hazardous locations (NFPA, IBC building codes specify when safety glazing required)
