# Forming Techniques — Blowing, Casting, Drawing, Pressing

## The Big Picture

Glass can be shaped by any technique that exploits its viscous behavior in the working range. Every major forming technique maps to a specific viscosity window — too hot and the glass won't hold form, too cool and it won't move. The revolution of glass blowing (~50 BCE) was that it made glass forming fast and accessible; the IS machine (1925) made it industrial.

```
GLASS FORMING TECHNIQUES
=========================

TECHNIQUE         VISCOSITY RANGE       SCALE        TYPICAL PRODUCT
——————————        ———————————           —————        ———————————————
Free blowing      10³-10⁵ Pa·s          Studio        Art glass, vessels
Mold blowing      10³-10⁵              Craft/indust.  Bottles, shaped vessels
IS machine        10³-10⁴              Industrial     100M+ bottles/day globally
Float             10³-10⁵              Industrial     Flat glass ribbon
Drawing           10⁴-10⁶              Industrial     Sheets, tubing, fiber
Pressing          10³-10⁵              Industrial     Tableware, lenses, thick items
Casting           10²-10⁴              Studio/indust. Sculptural, optical blanks
Fusing/slumping   Below softening       Studio/indust. Bowls, plates, panels
Lampworking       10³-10⁵ (local)      Studio         Beads, scientific ware
Pâte de verre     ~Sintering temp      Studio         Small cast objects
```

## Glass Blowing — Offhand and Mold

```
OFFHAND GLASSBLOWING (FREE BLOWN)
==================================

INVENTION: ~50-30 BCE, Levantine coast (Sidon/Syria region)
 Prior to blowing: all glass formed by casting or core-forming
  → expensive; slow; limited to small vessels
 Blowing: revolutionary — one person can make a vessel in minutes
  → glass democratized in Roman Empire (~1st century CE)
  Why not earlier? (glass worked 1,500 years before blowing)
  Blowing requires: hollow iron tube; recognition that hot glass can be
  inflated; probably developed from tube-drawing technique

TOOL SET:
 Blowpipe: hollow iron tube, 1.2-1.8m; 25-30mm diameter
           refractory tip survives molten glass contact
 Bench: glassblower sits astride; blowpipe rests on arms for rotation
 Marver: flat iron/steel surface for gathering and initial shaping
 Jacks: spring steel tweezers; primary forming tool; jacks in while rotating
 Block: wooden bowl; shapes gather by rotation in concave surface
 Pucellas: tong-like tool for constriction, opening, shaping rim
 Tweezers/yokes: pulling, shaping feet
 Bit: small secondary gather added for handles, foot rings, applied decoration
 Pontil/punty: solid iron rod; attached to base of piece after blowing
               to free the mouth for finishing; snapped off → pontil mark

PROCESS — BASIC GATHER AND BLOW:
 1. Heat blowpipe end in furnace gather port
 2. Gather: rotate blowpipe into molten glass; pick up gather on end
 3. Marver: roll on marver to center and cool slightly
 4. Block: shape in wooden block, rotating continuously
 5. Blow: short puffs inflate glass bubble; shape with jacks
 6. Reheat (glory hole): reheat as needed; glass must stay in working range
 7. Develop form: combination of blowing, rotation, gravity, tools
 8. Transfer: attach pontil to base → crack off blowpipe at neck
 9. Finish mouth: reheat mouth; open, flare, or form rim with jacks
 10. Crack off pontil: tap → glass breaks at pontil attachment
 11. Anneal: immediately to annealing oven (lehr)

VENETIAN TECHNIQUE — EXTREME FORM:
 Crystal (cristallo): near-colorless glass; blown to walls <1mm thick
 Vetro a filigrana: white glass canes (lattimo) pulled into glass gather
  → twisted forms (latticino, retortoli, reticello — increasingly complex)
 Zanfirico: complex spiral cane within glass
 Murano gaffer system: gaffer + 3-4 assistants; complex forms required team
 Secret: kept on Murano 300+ years; still center of artistic glass today

MOLD BLOWING:
 Gather blown into a mold (dip mold, typically 2-3 part hinged)
 Mold imposes exterior shape and relief pattern
 Interior remains free-blown (mold just controls outside)
 Roman mold-blown vessels: detailed figural reliefs, patterns
 Industrial: mold blowing for patterned containers, drinking glasses
```

## Industrial Bottle Making — The IS Machine

```
IS MACHINE (INDIVIDUAL SECTION MACHINE)
=========================================

History:
 1925: Hartford Empire Corporation; developed by Henry Ingle
 Prior: Owens suction machine (Michael Owens, 1903) — first automated
  bottle machine; large, complex, expensive
 IS machine: modular sections, each making complete bottles; simpler

Modern IS machine:
 4-24 sections in a single machine
 Each section: independent; makes 1-3 gobs simultaneously (single/double/triple gob)
 Production rate: 250-600 bottles/minute per section
 Container sizes: 50ml to 5L

GOB FEEDER:
 Bottom of furnace: forehearth maintains working temperature
 Shears cut precise glass gobs (by weight, not volume)
 Gob weight matches target bottle weight
 Gob temperature uniformity: critical for consistent forming

TWO FORMING METHODS:
 BLOW-BLOW:
  1. Gob falls into blank (parison) mold
  2. "Settle blow" (air from top) seats gob
  3. "Counterblow" (air from bottom) forms parison (inverted primary shape)
  4. Transfer to blow mold
  5. Final blow → full bottle shape
  Used for: narrow-mouth bottles (wine, beer, spirits)

 PRESS-BLOW:
  1. Gob falls into blank mold
  2. Metal plunger presses up into gob from below
  3. Parison formed by mechanical pressing
  4. Transfer to blow mold
  5. Final blow → full shape
  Used for: wide-mouth containers (jars, food containers)
  Advantage: thinner, more uniform walls than blow-blow

POST-FORMING:
 Transfer mechanism → annealing lehr (immediate; critical)
 Hot-end coating: tin oxide spray at high temperature
  → improves scratch resistance; applied before annealing
 Cold-end coating: wax/silicone spray after annealing
  → lubricity for conveyor handling
 Inspection: every bottle checked by automated vision systems
  (checks: dimensions, wall thickness, seeds, chips, foreign inclusions)
```

## Casting

```
GLASS CASTING TECHNIQUES
==========================

CORE-FORMING (ancient, ~1550-50 BCE):
 Core: sand-dung-clay mixture shaped around mandrel
 Glass rods/canes wound onto hot core while rotating
 Additional glass rods fused together; marver to smooth surface
 Decoration: trail contrasting color threads; comb while hot
 Remove core: after cooling, scrape out; leaves rough interior
 Result: small unguentaria, amphoriskoi; richly decorated
 Technique predates blowing by 1,500 years

LOST WAX CASTING (à la cire perdue):
 Same principle as jewelry lost wax:
  Wax model → investment mold → wax burnout → glass fill → demolding
 Refractory investment: silica + plaster or ceramic fiber blend
 Glass: poured as molten or loaded as cullet/frit (melts in mold during firing)
 Kiln casting: mold + glass loaded in kiln → heat to ~830°C → glass melts
  and flows into mold → slow controlled cooling (critical)
 Complex shapes possible; glass retains mold texture or fire polishes
 Major studio artists: Libenský and Brychtová (Czech; monumental cast glass)

PÂTE DE VERRE ("glass paste"):
 Ground glass (frit) + colorants + water (or gum) → paste
 Pressed into refractory mold
 Kiln-fired to sintering/partial fusion (~700-800°C)
 Result: translucent, grainy-textured, colored solid
 Not fully melted — sintered particles; characteristic matte texture
 Art Nouveau technique: Daum frères; Amalric Walter; Gabriel Argy-Rousseau
 Contemporary revival: studio glass artists

OPEN CASTING / TABLE CASTING:
 Molten glass ladled or poured onto flat steel table
 Spread to desired thickness (rollers or spreader bar)
 Used for: architectural glass panels, thick cast elements, kiln blanks
 Texture: one side fire-polished; other side textured by table or roller
```

## Drawing

```
FLAT GLASS DRAWING (PRE-FLOAT)
================================

CYLINDER METHOD (oldest drawn flat glass):
 Blow large cylinder (~60cm long, 15cm diameter)
 Slit lengthwise while hot
 Reheat in flattening oven → unfolds flat
 Limited size; non-uniform thickness (natural variation in blown cylinder)
 Used through early 20th century

FOURCAULT PROCESS (1904, Emile Fourcault, Belgium):
 Molten glass drawn upward through slotted "debiteuse" slot at tank bottom
 Vertical draw: sheet rises ~6m, cooled as it moves up
 Non-uniform due to natural convection; slight ripple ("draw lines")
 Replaced by Colburn in US, Pittsburgh in UK

COLBURN/LIBBEY-OWENS PROCESS (1910s):
 Sheet drawn up then deflected horizontal over roller
 Easier handling; longer sheets; but roller marks possible
 Standard US flat glass method before float

PITTSBURGH PROCESS:
 Vertical draw from surface of melt (not through slot)
 Better quality than Fourcault; some ripple remains
 Used by Pittsburgh Plate Glass (PPG) until float displaced

ALL REPLACED BY FLOAT (1959+):
 Float glass: superior quality; no draw lines; optically flat
 Float displaced all drawing methods for mainstream flat glass within 20 years
 Drawing survives for specialty products:
  Schott down-draw: produces very thin specialty glass (display, pharma)
  Corning Fusion draw: precision thin glass for display (0.1-0.7mm thick)

TUBING AND RODS:
 Danner process: molten glass wound onto rotating mandrel (hollow → tube;
                 solid → rod); continuous drawing
 Vello process: glass drawn over pointed mandrel vertically
 Used for: fluorescent tube blanks, pharmaceutical vials (tubing conversion),
            thermometer tubes, neon sign tube stock
```

## Pressing

```
PRESSING — MECHANICAL FORMING
================================

PROCESS:
 Molten gob dropped into female mold
 Male plunger presses down into gob
 Glass fills space between plunger and mold → forms shape
 Rapid forming; no blowing required

ADVANTAGES:
 Consistent wall thickness (controlled by mold + plunger geometry)
 Complex cross-sections possible (rib patterns, handles)
 Economical for tableware, insulators, lens blanks
 No hollow interior possible (pressing = solid or near-solid forms)
 Wide-mouth containers (same as press-blow in containers)

PRESSED GLASS TABLEWARE:
 American pressed glass (19th c.): New England Glass, Sandwich Glass
  (Deming Jarves, Sandwich, MA, 1825+)
  → "Lacy glass" (stippled background; pressed geometric patterns)
  Pattern pressed glass: imitates cut crystal at fraction of cost
  "Brilliant period" (1880s-1900s): pressed patterns imitating
   cut crystal (hobstars, fans, crosshatching)

TV FUNNEL GLASS:
 CRT television: funnel (back) + face plate (front) pressed separately
 Funnel glass: lead-containing (radiation shielding for electron beam)
 Face plate: precision flat-pressed
 Now: flat panel displays replaced CRTs → demand collapsed

OPTICAL PRESSING:
 Precision pressing of optical blanks (near-net-shape)
 Aspheric lens pressing: pressing glass into precise mold shapes
  → near-final form requiring minimal grinding
 Precision glass molding (PGM): mold at working temperature;
  no grinding needed; used for camera lenses, LED optics
 Mold material: tungsten carbide, silicon carbide
  (must survive repeated thermal cycling with glass)
```

## Kiln Forming — Studio Techniques

```
KILN FORMING
=============

Working below blowing temperatures; glass shaped by gravity + heat.
Does not require a furnace (just a kiln).

FUSING:
 Layered flat glass pieces in kiln
 Heat to ~780-820°C (below working point; surface tension active)
 Glass pieces merge together; colors blend at interfaces
 Tack fuse (~750°C): pieces bond but retain raised texture/edges
 Full fuse (~820°C): completely smooth; pieces flat and merged
 Full fuse → very flat; colors mix slightly at boundaries
 Compatible glass required (CTE must match within ~0.5×10⁻⁶)
 Bullseye and Spectrum (now merged into Bullseye) glass: specifically
  manufactured for kiln work; guaranteed compatibility within brand

SLUMPING:
 Fused or single sheet heated to ~650-700°C
 Glass softens → slumps by gravity over or into a mold
 "Slump into": piece drapes into concave mold → bowl shape
 "Slump over": piece drapes over convex mold → dome shape
 Rate of slumping depends on temperature; fast = gravity wins before
  surface tension can smooth; requires experimentation
 Release agent (kiln wash = alumina/kaolin mixture) on mold prevents sticking

LAMPWORKING (FLAMEWORKING):
 Glass rod or tube worked in torch flame (not furnace)
 Oxygen-propane or oxygen-natural gas torch
 Borosilicate (hard glass): 1,650°C+ torch required; COE ~33
 Soft glass (soda-lime ~COE 90-104): propane + air torch sufficient
 (COE = coefficient of expansion, glass industry shorthand for CTE scaled)

Applications:
 Scientific glassware: custom flask shapes, vacuum connections
 Beads: wind molten glass onto mandrel; decorate with color trails
 Art glass: small sculptures, sea creatures, flowers
 Eyes: prosthetic eye fabrication (glass eyes still sometimes made by
       lampwork by specialized ocularists — though now mostly acrylic)
 Neon signs: tube bending (low-heat flame, not hot enough to melt, just soften)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Mold-blown vs pressed glass**:
Mold blowing: glass inflated by blowing inside a mold (interior free-blown, exterior mold-shaped). Pressed: mechanical plunger pushes glass into mold (completely mechanically formed). Pressed glass looks similar to mold-blown but is typically more uniform in wall thickness.

**Casting vs kiln casting**:
"Glass casting" can mean pouring molten glass at furnace temperature into a mold (traditional casting, immediate). "Kiln casting" means loading glass frit/cullet into a mold, then firing the whole assembly in a kiln to melt and fill — a slower, lower-tech alternative.

**Flotation = shaping, not the same as float glass**:
"Float" in glassmaking specifically means the Pilkington process of floating a glass ribbon on molten tin. "Flotation" in other contexts means density separation in mineral processing. Don't confuse these.

**Lampwork ≠ blown glass**:
Glassblowing (offhand) uses a furnace and a blowpipe; lampworking uses a bench torch. They are distinct skills, use different glass (often different CTE/composition), require different tools, and serve different applications. A skilled glassblower is not automatically a skilled lampworker.
