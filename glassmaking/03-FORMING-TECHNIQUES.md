# 03 — Forming Techniques

## Blowing, Casting, Drawing, Pressing — Hot Glass to Form

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Blowing (offhand / free-blown)**: the dominant hot glass forming technique since Roman invention (~50 BCE); molten glass gathered on the end of a hollow iron blowpipe (1.2-1.8m long); air blown through pipe → glass inflates; shaped by tools, gravity, and rotation while hot; remarkable that this technique was not invented earlier (glass worked for 1,500 years before blowing); prior to blowing, glass worked by casting (laborious, expensive) → blowing democratized glass vessels; basic vocabulary: gather (pick up molten glass), blow, marver (roll on iron table to shape/cool), jack (tool to manipulate form), jacks line (constriction marking neck), pucellas, tweezers, block (wooden cup to shape base), bit (small gather added as handle, foot), pontil/punty (solid iron rod to hold piece inverted while finishing mouth)
  - **Free blown**: no mold; form determined entirely by glassblower's skill; bubbles of glass shaped by blowing, rotation, tools; Venetian tradition of extremely thin, elaborate forms
  - **Mold blown**: gather blown into mold (dip mold = open-top, typically 3-part hinged mold); the mold defines exterior shape; interior remains free-blown; allows repeatable shapes and patterns pressed into mold; Roman mold-blown vessels; modern production mold blowing in glassblowing studios
  - **Gather-blown on mandrel**: rare technique for beads (wind molten glass onto mandrel)
- **Industrial bottle making (IS machine)**: Hartford Empire/Owens-Illinois IS (Individual Section) machine (1925) replaced hand-blowing for container glass; modern IS machines produce 250-600 bottles/minute per section; two methods: blow-blow (parison blown, transferred, blown again in final mold) and press-blow (parison formed by pressing plunger, then blown in final mold — used for wide-mouth jars); complete automation; gob feeder delivers precise gobs of molten glass to each section; annealing lehr follows immediately
- **Casting**: pouring molten glass into molds; ancient technique (core-forming predates blowing for vessels; sand or clay core, glass wound or pressed around, then core removed); open casting into flat molds; solid casting (pâte de verre — "glass paste" — ground glass + colorant + binder pressed into mold, fired to fuse → colored, opaque to translucent solid forms; Art Nouveau technique, Daum frères); lost wax glass casting (same principle as jewelry — wax model → investment mold → wax out → molten glass in); kiln casting vs hot casting (furnace pour vs kiln melt in mold)
- **Drawing**: pulling glass to make flat sheets (before float process); cylinder method (blow large cylinder → cut lengthwise → reheat and flatten); Fourcault process (1904 — draw flat glass sheet directly from molten bath using slotted debiteuse nozzle); Colburn/Libbey-Owens process (sheet drawn horizontally from surface of tank); Pittsburg process; all replaced by float process for most applications; Schott and others still draw specialty thin glass using down-draw
- **Rolling**: pouring molten glass onto rolling table and passing through rolls to create flat glass; used for textured/figured glass (ribbed, hammered, wired glass patterns pressed by textured rolls); wired glass (wire mesh embedded during rolling → holds together when broken → historical safety glass, now largely replaced by tempered)
- **Pressing**: molten glass gob pressed in mold by plunger; fast, economical for thick or complex cross-section parts; tableware (pressed glass dishes, lenses), insulators, TV funnel glass; "brilliant cut glass" pattern — pressed glass imitates hand-cut crystal at fraction of cost; American pressed glass tradition (New England Glass, Sandwich Glass, 19th c.)
- **Casting on a table / "casting"**: flat casting for large pieces; molten glass ladled onto steel table, spread, then annealed; cast glass objects (architectural panels, kiln-formed panels)
- **Kiln forming** (studio technique): using kiln to shape glass by heat (without furnace):
  - **Fusing**: layering flat glass pieces in kiln → heat to ~800°C → glass pieces fuse together; colors combine, surface levels; tack fuse (pieces remain distinct with raised texture) vs full fuse (smooth, completely merged)
  - **Slumping**: fused or single glass sheet heated over/into mold → slumps to shape by gravity; used for bowls, plates, architectural elements
  - **Pâte de verre**: see casting above
  - **Lampworking (flameworking)**: working glass in flame (propane + oxygen torch); borosilicate or soft glass; technique for scientific glassware, beads, sculptures, small vessels; different tradition from furnace glassblowing (can be done alone, no team required); borosilicate lampworking requires hotter flame than soft glass
