# Historical Glassmaking — Egyptian Core-Formed, Roman, Venice/Murano, Bohemia

## The Big Picture

Glass has a 3,500-year history as a manufactured material. Three moments transformed it: the invention of glass blowing (~50 BCE) made glass affordable; the Venetian monopoly (12th-17th c.) made it a geopolitical asset; and the industrial revolution made it structural and then ubiquitous. Each era used fundamentally different technology and served fundamentally different markets.

```
GLASS HISTORY TIMELINE
========================

~3,500 BCE   Egyptian faience (silica+alkali — first glassy material; not true glass)
~1,600 BCE   First true glass vessels — Egypt + Mesopotamia simultaneously
             Core-formed technique; rare luxury (= precious stone value)
~50 BCE      Glass blowing invented — Levantine coast (Sidon area)
             → glass vessels affordable; Roman Empire fills with glass
1st c. CE    Roman glass: mold blown, mosaic, cameo; extraordinary variety
             Pompeii (79 CE) preserves extraordinary glass collection
7th-13th c.  Islamic glass: wheel-engraved, enameled, lustered traditions
12th c.      Venetian glass industry established (Byzantine craftsmen)
1291         Murano: Venice moves glassmakers to island (fire safety + monopoly)
~15th c.     Venetian cristallo: near-colorless glass revolution
~17th c.     Bohemian potash-lime glass + wheel engraving dominates cutting
1676         Ravenscroft: English lead crystal (high RI + softness → cuttable)
1827         Chance Brothers continuous sheet glass (Crystal Palace, 1851)
1959         Pilkington float glass → flat glass revolution
```

## Origins — Egyptian and Near Eastern Glass

```
FAIENCE ≠ TRUE GLASS (~3,500 BCE):
 Egyptian faience: SiO₂ + alkali + copper colorant → turquoise glaze
 Core: sand/quartz, not clay
 Not amorphous glass (partially crystalline)
 See ceramics/07-EARTHENWARE-TRADITIONS for full faience treatment
 Faience = gateway material → same chemistry → eventually true glass

FIRST TRUE GLASS (~1,600-1,550 BCE):
 Simultaneously in Egypt (Thutmose III, 18th Dynasty) and Mesopotamia
 Probable origin: Syrian/Mesopotamian craftsmen brought to Egypt as war captives
  after Thutmose III's campaigns (~1450 BCE)
 First form: solid glass ingots → glass beads
 First vessels: core-formed (see below)

Early glass properties:
 Deep cobalt blue (copper + cobalt from ore deposits)
 Turquoise-blue (copper-bearing)
 Dark purple-black (manganese)
 Opaque/semi-opaque; not clear
 Value: comparable to semi-precious stones (lapis lazuli, turquoise)
 Distribution: royal gifts, diplomatic exchange, palace inventories
```

## Core-Formed Glass (~1,550 BCE – 100 BCE, then revival)

```
CORE-FORMING TECHNIQUE
=======================

PROCESS:
 1. Core: sand + clay + organic binder (dung) packed around metal mandrel
    → shaped to interior vessel form
 2. Heat: core heated; glass rods wound around core while hot
    and/or: coils of softened glass wound onto core
 3. Additional material: remelted glass rods trailed in patterns
    over core surface
 4. Decoration: while hot, drag metal comb/tool across surface
    → feather or chevron pattern in contrasting color trails
 5. Cool: glass body cools; core still inside
 6. Remove: after full cooling, scrape out core material
    (results in rough interior surface)
 7. Add handles/feet: separately formed glass additions

FORMS PRODUCED:
 Amphoriskoi (small amphora shapes): perfume/ointment containers
 Alabastra: elongated flasks; narrow neck; ointment containers
 Aryballi: globular small flasks (oil/perfume)
 Oinochoai: small jugs
 All small-scale: no large vessels possible (technique limits size)

DECORATION:
 Characteristic: yellow, white, or turquoise threads on dark background
 Or: dark threads on light ground
 Comb-dragged patterns: feather, zigzag, scale patterns
 Handles: applied separately after body formed; often pulled-glass loop

DISTRIBUTION:
 Primary: eastern Mediterranean; Rhodes, Cyprus, Egypt, Syria, Anatolia
 Duration: ~1,550-50 BCE with a gap (period of decline ~1,000 BCE);
  Hellenistic revival (4th-1st c. BCE) with refined technique
 Replacement: glass blowing (~50 BCE) — faster; allows larger forms;
  immediately eclipses core-forming

MAJOR COLLECTIONS:
 British Museum: extensive ancient glass collection including core-formed
 Metropolitan Museum (NY): significant Hellenistic and Egyptian examples
 Corning Museum of Glass: comprehensive world glass collection
```

## Roman Glass (~50 BCE – 400 CE)

```
THE BLOWING REVOLUTION
=======================

INVENTION: ~50-30 BCE, Levantine coast (Sidon/Syria region)
 No single credited inventor
 Technique: hollow iron blowpipe + breath → glass inflated
 Why so late? (Glass worked 1,500 years before blowing)
  Hollow iron tube + recognition that glass can be inflated
  Probably evolved from tube-drawing technique
 Effect: single worker can make a vessel in minutes vs hours of core-forming
         Large vessels now possible; forms unlimited by mandrel size

ROMAN GLASS TYPES:

Mosaic glass (millefiori — "thousand flowers"):
 Bundles of colored glass canes arranged in patterns
 Cross-sections of bundled canes → mosaic pattern
 Sliced and fused together → bowl/plate cast over mold
 OR: cross-section canes fused flat → sheets
 Sophisticated color patterns; bowls, plates
 Tradition continues: Venetian millefiori (venices its own tradition)

Cage cups / diatreta:
 A carved glass masterwork: a thick glass blank carved until an outer
 "cage" (elaborate openwork decoration) is freed from the inner cup
 The cage is connected to the cup only at the rim
 Technically extraordinary: cutting glass from the outside without
 breaking the thin connecting points or the inner cup
 Cannot be fully replicated by modern glassworkers
 Surviving examples: ~50 worldwide; Trivulzio Cup (Milan, 4th c.)

Cameo glass:
 Two-layer: opaque white glass over cobalt blue (or other combination)
 Outer white layer carved away → relief design remains on colored ground
 Technique: same as gem carving; uses copper wheels and abrasives
 Portland Vase (1st c. BCE/CE, British Museum): the masterpiece
  Blue glass + white overlay; mythological scene; 25cm tall
  Smashed 1845 by a disturbed museum visitor; painstakingly reassembled
  Fragments and reconstruction debates still ongoing
 Roman cameo glass = the most technically demanding ancient glass object

Blown glass varieties:
 Mold-blown vessels: inflated into molds with relief patterns
 Free-blown forms: jugs, flasks, unguentaria (ointment bottles)
 Windowpane glass: cast flat or blown cylinder then flattened;
  first architectural glass; found in Pompeii excavations

ROMAN GLASS SIGNATURE:
 Characteristic blue-green tint (controlled low iron level)
 Very clear compared to earlier glass
 Wide variety from luxury (cameo, cage cups) to mass commodity
  (blown unguentaria found across entire empire)

GEOGRAPHIC EXTENT:
 Glass factories found from Cologne (Germany) to Alexandria (Egypt)
 Standard product transported by ship; Rome = center of luxury production
```

## Islamic Glass (7th–13th c. CE)

```
ISLAMIC GLASSMAKING TRADITIONS
================================

CONTINUATION:
 Islamic expansion absorbed existing Roman/Sasanian glass technology
 Syria, Iraq, Egypt = centers of expertise → became Islamic workshops
 7th-9th c.: primarily continuation of Roman techniques
  Wheel engraving (cutting glass with rotating abrasive wheels)

HEDWIG BEAKERS (11th-12th c.):
 12 known examples (named after Saint Hedwig of Silesia, legendary ownership)
 Deep relief cutting; stylized eagle, griffin, lion; geometric patterns
 Origin disputed: most likely Syria or Egypt; possibly Fatimid or Crusader context
 Technically extraordinary; the finest Islamic relief-cut glass

ENAMELED GLASS (Syrian, 12th-14th c.):
 Painted with vitreous enamel and gold on clear blown glass
 Fired in muffle kiln → enamel fused to surface
 Subjects: hunting scenes, floral, calligraphy, heraldic
 Syrian/Mamluk (Egypt) mosque lamps: large glass lanterns
  painted with Quranic inscriptions + heraldic emblems
  Commissioned for the Sultan Hassan Mosque (Cairo, 1356-63)
  Major example: V&A, Louvre, Islamic Museum Cairo collections
 Quality: the finest polychrome glass painting before Venice

LUSTREWARE GLASS:
 Same metal-reduction technique as luster ceramics (see ceramics/05-DECORATION)
 Metallic sheen on glass surface; gold/copper/silver luster
 Fatimid Egypt: major center; 10th-12th c.

TRADE AND TRANSMISSION:
 Islamic glass exported along Silk Road to China, India, Europe
 Venetian glass industry directly descended from Byzantine → Islamic tradition
  (craftsmen from Constantinople after 1204 Crusader sack)
```

## Venice and Murano (12th–17th c.)

```
THE VENETIAN MONOPOLY
======================

ESTABLISHMENT:
 12th century: Venetian glass industry growing; craftsmen from
  Byzantine (Constantinople) tradition; skilled workers valued
 Muranese glass: actually refers to glass made on Murano island

MURANO EXILE (1291):
 Republic of Venice orders all glassmaking furnaces moved to Murano island
 STATED reason: fire safety (glass furnaces frequently caused city fires)
 ACTUAL reason: concentrate the industry → control the monopoly
  → prevent workers from escaping with trade secrets
  Venice = trading empire; glass = major export product; secrecy = wealth

SOCIAL STATUS OF GLASSMAKERS:
 Privileges: could carry a sword (aristocratic right); daughters could
  marry into Venetian nobility (extremely unusual for craftsmen)
 Penalty for leaving: Republic of Venice would send assassins after
  any glassmaker who emigrated with knowledge
  Several documented cases of execution of escaped glassmakers
 The island = a gilded prison: high status, good pay, cannot leave

TECHNICAL INNOVATIONS:

Cristallo (~1450, Angelo Barovier attributed):
 Near-colorless glass — a revolution
 Prior to this: all glass greenish or tinted from iron
 Technique: manganese dioxide decolorizer + ultra-clean raw materials
  (barilla from Spain — clean soda source; pure quartz)
 Effect: Venetian crystal traded for gemstone prices in northern Europe
  A nobleman wanting colorless glass tableware had no alternative to Murano

Lattimo (opaque white glass):
 Arsenic or tin in melt → opaque white glass
 Imitates Chinese porcelain (which Europeans couldn't make)
 Lattimo vessels: tableware, pharmacy bottles

Vetro a filigrana ("filigree glass"):
 White glass canes (lattimo) or colored canes embedded in clear glass
 Types:
  Vetro a retortoli: single canes twisted into spiral rods
  Vetro a reticello: two intersecting sets of twisted canes; net pattern
   with a tiny air bubble in each "mesh" cell (controlled; extraordinary)
  Latticino: complex lattice patterns from pulled canes
 These techniques require: building bundles of canes →
  fusing together → reheating → pulling and blowing
  Multiple reheating cycles; requires entire team

Aventurine glass:
 Copper crystals in glass → glittering gold effect
 "Goldstone" or "aventurine" mineral was the original namesake
 Technique: reducing conditions + dissolved copper → Cu crystallites
  on careful reheating; size and distribution control the appearance

DECLINE:
 17th century: secret leaked; Bohemian competitors developed better glass
 The monopoly lasted ~400 years (extraordinary by any standard)
 How it leaked: one theory is via defecting workers despite penalties
  The Bohemian potash-lime glass is harder → better for wheel cutting
  Venetian soda glass was superior for elaborate blown forms but inferior
  for decorative cutting (the 18th-century luxury preference)
```

## Bohemian and German Glass (17th–18th c.)

```
WALDGLAS AND THE BOHEMIAN TRADITION
=====================================

WALDGLAS ("FOREST GLASS"):
 German/Bohemian tradition: glass made in forests (fuel abundant)
 Flux: potash (K₂CO₃) from wood ash
  (vs Venetian glass: soda/barilla from coastal plants)
 Composition: potash-lime-silica (vs soda-lime-silica for Venetian)
 Color: naturally greenish-yellow ("forest green") from iron in wood ash
 Properties vs Venetian:
  Harder (potash glass is harder)
  More brittle (less forgiving of thick-walled forms)
  Better suited for: wheel engraving and cutting (harder → holds edge better)
  Less suited for: thin elaborate free-blowing (too stiff; Venetian specialty)

CASPAR LEHMANN (~1600):
 Prague court lapidary and glassworker
 Adapted gemstone wheel engraving to glass
 First artist to engrave glass with copper wheel + abrasive
 Foundation of Bohemian engraved glass tradition

BOHEMIAN CENTERS:
 Jablonec nad Nisou (Gablonz): glass bead capital of the world
  19th-20th c.: millions of glass beads, buttons, imitation gems exported
  globally; Czech national industry
 Nový Bor (Haida): engraved glass; major export center
 Harrachov: fine crystal and colored glass

ENGLISH LEAD CRYSTAL (George Ravenscroft, 1676):
 Ravenscroft: glass merchant; experimented with lead oxide additions
 Lead glass patent 1676 (Glass Sellers Company of London)
 PbO advantages: high RI → sparkle; soft → easy wheel cutting;
                 clear → colorless; lower working temp → workable
 "Flint glass": called this because Ravenscroft originally used flint
  (calcined flint = pure SiO₂) instead of sand → cleaner starting material
 English lead crystal: 18th-19th c. luxury; Waterford Wedgwood group tradition

COMPETITION WITH VENICE:
 By 1700: Bohemian glass and English lead crystal both available
 Bohemian glass: harder; better cut; cheaper to produce
 Venetian monopoly: ended; Murano makers now competing in open market
 The 18th-century luxury preference: cutting and engraving → Bohemian + English win
 The fine blown vessel tradition: Venetian technique never fully replaced
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Core-forming ≠ glass casting**:
Core-forming wraps glass threads around a shaped core (not poured). Casting pours molten glass into or around a form. Different processes, different periods, different results.

**Roman cameo glass is NOT painted**:
The white layer on Portland Vase-type cameo glass is a separate layer of glass, carved away to reveal the blue glass beneath. It is not paint or enamel. The carving is done with copper wheels and abrasives — same technique as carving an agate.

**Venice glass workers were not free**:
The combination of high status + execution for leaving is not a paradox — it was a deliberate strategy to keep monopoly knowledge on the island. The privilege (sword, noble marriage) was compensation for the restriction.

**Bohemian glass ≠ crystal**:
Bohemian glass (early period) is potash-lime glass, not lead crystal. English lead crystal (Ravenscroft, 1676) is the origin of the "lead crystal" tradition. Bohemian glass became clearer with potash-lime formulations and excelled at wheel engraving, not for the same reasons as lead crystal.
