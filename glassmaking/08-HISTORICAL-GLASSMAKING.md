# 08 — Historical Glassmaking

## Egyptian Core-Formed, Roman, Venice/Murano, Bohemia — 3,500 Years

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Origins (~3500-1550 BCE)**: earliest glass objects = glazed stone beads and faience (Egyptian faience ~3500 BCE — not true glass); first true glass objects (non-glaze): Egyptian and Mesopotamian, ~1600-1550 BCE; first glass vessels in Egypt and Mesopotamia simultaneously (New Kingdom Egypt, 18th Dynasty, reign of Thutmose III — possibly from Syrian craftsmen brought as war captives); glass was initially rare luxury — comparable in value to semi-precious stones; early Egyptian glass was typically cobalt blue, turquoise, or dark green (copper and cobalt colorants)
- **Core-formed glass** (~1550-1000 BCE, then Hellenistic revival): vessels formed by winding molten glass rods around a core of sand and dung mixed to shape; after forming, core scraped out; technique produces small unguentaria (ointment/perfume containers), amphoriskoi; characteristic combed or feathered decoration (contrasting color thread trailed over surface and combed with metal tool while hot); used in Mediterranean from Egypt to Rhodes; the technique predates blowing by 1,500+ years
- **Roman revolution — blown glass (~50 BCE onward)**: glass blowing invented on the Syrian/Levantine coast (~50-30 BCE, Sidon region); transforms glass from luxury to everyday material; by 1st century CE, glass vessels common throughout Roman Empire; Pompeii and Herculaneum preserve extensive Roman glass collections; mosaic glass (millefiori — "thousand flowers" — cross-sections of bundled color rods fused together → mosaic pattern); caged glass (diatreta — intricate openwork cage cut away from solid glass block, Roman 3rd-4th c. — technically extraordinary); cameo glass (Portland Vase, 1st century BCE/CE — opaque white over cobalt blue, carved like gemstone cameo; the masterpiece of ancient glass); cage cups/diatreta still cannot be fully replicated; Roman glass often has distinctive blue-green tint (controlled iron content)
- **Islamic glass** (7th-13th c. CE): continuation and innovation of Roman/Hellenistic tradition; wheel-engraved and relief-cut glass (Hedwig beakers — finest examples, origin debated but possibly Syrian); gilt and enameled glass (finest from Syria — Syria/Egypt Mamluk period 13th-14th c.; large mosque lamps, drinking vessels with polychrome enamel and gilding); lustre glass (metallic sheen from metallic oxide firing in reducing atmosphere); Fatimid cut glass; traded along Silk Road → influenced Chinese and European glass
- **Venice and Murano** (12th-17th c.): Venetian glass industry established by immigration of Byzantine/Middle Eastern craftsmen after fall of Constantinople; glass furnaces in Venice moved to Murano island by order of Venetian government (1291) — both fire safety concern and to concentrate the monopoly; Murano glassblowers had special social status (could carry sword, could marry into Venetian nobility) but were effectively imprisoned (forbidden to leave on pain of death — Republic of Venice executed escaped glassmakers); techniques developed: cristallo (near-colorless glass — revolutionary for the time), lattimo (opaque white glass containing arsenic or tin), vetro a filigrana (canes of white or colored glass twisted into the clear glass — latticino, reticello, retortoli), millefiori, aventurine (copper glittering from tiny copper crystals); Murano glass = luxury export throughout Europe; trade secret eventually broken when Angelo Barovier or other craftsmen carried knowledge to Bohemia (~17th c.) and the monopoly collapsed
- **Bohemian glass** (17th-18th c.): German/Bohemian glassmakers (Waldglas = forest glass, potash-lime composition from wood ash) developed high-quality potash-lime glass (harder than Venetian soda glass → suited to wheel engraving and cutting); Caspar Lehmann in Prague developed wheel engraving for glass (~1600); Bohemian cut and engraved glass became European luxury standard 17th-18th c.; centers: Jablonec, Haida (Nový Bor), Harrachov; glass beads (Jablonec = major bead export town); rock crystal imitation (rock crystal = natural quartz — Bohemian glass imitated its clarity and allowed cutting); lead crystal developed in England by George Ravenscroft (1676) as Venetian-quality alternative → English lead crystal tradition
- **Industrial revolution — glass mass production**: cast plate glass (earlier, expensive, used for mirrors); cylinder and crown glass for windows; Chances Brothers, St Helens (Pilkington precursor) as major English producers; plate glass rolling mills; railroad expansion → increased glass demand for trains, buildings; by 1900, pressed glass, machine-blown glass democratize glass containers (then float glass — see 04-FLOAT-GLASS)
- **Art glass movements**: Arts and Crafts reaction → Emile Gallé (Nancy, France) — naturalistic acid-etched cameo glass; Daum Brothers; Art Nouveau; Louis Comfort Tiffany (New York) — leaded glass lamps and windows, Favrile glass; René Lalique — press-molded decorative glass; see 09-ART-GLASS
