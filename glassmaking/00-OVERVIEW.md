# 00 — Overview

## Supercooled Liquid: Glass Science and Industry

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **What glass is physically**: an amorphous (non-crystalline) solid; sometimes described as a "supercooled liquid" — this is a historical misnomer (cathedral glass is not thicker at the bottom due to flow; it was simply made that way); more accurately: glass is a solid material with liquid-like disordered structure; atoms arranged randomly rather than in crystal lattice; technically a "rigid amorphous solid"; no long-range order (unlike ceramics or metals), which is why glass is optically isotropic (no birefringence in unstressed soda-lime glass)
- **The fundamental glass transition**: glass has a glass transition temperature (Tg) below which it behaves as a solid and above which it becomes increasingly viscous liquid; soda-lime glass Tg ≈ 520-570°C; this is NOT a sharp melting point (unlike crystalline materials) but a range over which viscosity changes by many orders of magnitude; log(viscosity in Pa·s): working point ~10³ (fluid enough to shape), softening point ~10⁷.⁶, annealing point ~10¹²·⁴ (where internal stresses relieve over hours), strain point ~10¹³·⁸ (below this, stresses are essentially permanent)
- **Three main glass families**:
  1. **Soda-lime-silica glass**: ~72% SiO₂ + 14% Na₂O + 10% CaO + minor; 90%+ of all glass made; windows, bottles, containers, tableware
  2. **Borosilicate glass**: ~80% SiO₂ + 13% B₂O₃ + minor; low thermal expansion (1/3 of soda-lime); Pyrex, lab glassware, lighting
  3. **Lead glass**: SiO₂ + PbO (up to 30%); high refractive index, high dispersion; "crystal" tableware, optical glass (historically); now mostly replaced by lead-free "crystal" formulas
- **Supply chain overview**: silica sand mining → raw material batching (sand + soda ash + limestone + cullet) → melting furnace → forming (float, blowing, casting, pressing, drawing) → annealing lehr → cutting/finishing → secondary processing (tempering, laminating, coating)
- **Scale of production**: global glass market ~$250B annually; flat glass (windows, solar) largest segment; container glass (bottles, jars); fiberglass (insulation + composites); specialty glass (optical, display, pharmaceutical); float glass process (1959) transformed flat glass manufacturing completely; solar panel glass now a massive growth segment (silicon solar cells on glass substrates, glass-glass modules)
- **Trade roles overview** (detailed in 10-GLASS-TRADES): glassblower (hot glass forming), gaffer (master glassblower supervising team), bit gatherer (assistant), glazier (installs and cuts flat glass), glass cutter (decorative cutting and engraving), optical fabricator (precision optical components), glass scientist/engineer (materials development)
- **Historical significance**: glass = one of oldest synthetic materials; Egyptian glass vessels ~1550 BCE; Roman blown glass revolution ~50 BCE (suddenly glass vessels affordable, not just luxury); Venetian/Murano glass monopoly (12th-17th c.) — traded at gemstone prices; industrial float glass (Pilkington, 1959) = one of the great manufacturing innovations of the 20th century; optical fiber (Corning, 1970) = enabler of the internet; modern smartphones use specialty aluminosilicate glass (Corning Gorilla Glass)
