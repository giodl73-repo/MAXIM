# Urban Planning — The Landscape

## The Big Picture

Urban planning is the discipline of managing complex adaptive systems where thousands of independent decisions aggregate into city-scale outcomes. It sits at the intersection of law, economics, engineering, politics, and design.

## Historical Arc — How Planning Evolved

### Pre-Modern: Organic Growth (Antiquity — 1800s)

Cities grew without formal planning. Streets followed desire paths, markets formed at natural crossroads, walls defined edge conditions. Roman grids, Islamic courtyard housing, medieval European organic patterns — each reflects the logic of its constraints (defensibility, water access, trade routes).

```
ORGANIC CITY LOGIC:
  Water source --> settlement --> market --> path --> road --> neighborhood

  No master plan. Emergent order from individual optimization.
  Result: legible to inhabitants, confusing to outsiders.
  Resilient (no single point of failure).
  Fragile to rapid population growth.
```

### 19th-Century Crisis: Cholera, Overcrowding, Reform

Industrialization drove massive rural-to-urban migration. Cities grew faster than their organic systems could handle.

### Garden City Movement (1898 — 1920s)

Ebenezer Howard's "To-morrow: A Peaceful Path to Real Reform" (1898, republished as "Garden Cities of To-morrow") proposed a third option between the overcrowded city and the underdeveloped countryside.

```
HOWARD'S THREE MAGNETS:

    TOWN MAGNET              COUNTRY MAGNET
  (High wages,             (Beauty, low rent,
   social life,             fresh air, nature,
   high rent,               long hours,
   overcrowding,            low wages,
   pollution)               lack of society)
        \                        /
         \                      /
          v                    v
       TOWN-COUNTRY MAGNET
       (Best of both worlds:
        low rent + high wages +
        beauty + social opportunity +
        fresh air + no smoke)

IMPLEMENTATION:
  Letchworth (1903) -- First Garden City, UK
  Welwyn Garden City (1920) -- Second

  Design principles:
  - 32,000 population limit
  - Green belt prevents sprawl
  - Industrial zone separate from residential
  - Municipally owned land (captures value increase)
  - Mix of housing types

INFLUENCE:
  UK New Towns Act 1946 (28 new towns built)
  Singapore satellite towns
  Canberra layout
  Radburn NJ, Greenbelt MD (New Deal)
```

Howard's cooperative land ownership model was routinely dropped in practice. What survived was the visual vocabulary (single-family houses, greenery, curvilinear streets) without the economics. This mutation drove Howard's intended urban-rural synthesis into its opposite: car-dependent sprawl.

### City Beautiful Movement (1893 — 1920s)

Daniel Burnham's answer to the industrial city was not dispersal but monumental civic order. The 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago demonstrated that planned, unified design could transform urban experience.

```
CITY BEAUTIFUL PHILOSOPHY:
  "Make no little plans; they have no magic to stir men's blood."
  -- attributed to Burnham

  Core premise: Beautiful, ordered civic space creates civic virtue.
  Monumental public buildings, grand boulevards, classical
  architecture --> social uplift.

  Burnham's 1909 Plan of Chicago:
  - Grant Park lakefront (public, not private)
  - Diagonal boulevards (speed + grandeur)
  - Uniform building setbacks on Michigan Ave.
  - Regional system of parks/forest preserves
  - Civic center (never built)

  Legacy: DC Mall, San Francisco Civic Center,
          Manila plan, Cleveland Group Plan.

  Critique: Beautiful for whom?
  Displaced existing residents.
  Prioritized aesthetics over equity.
  Civic monuments serve those who can visit them.
```

### Radiant City: Towers in the Park (1920s — 1960s)

Le Corbusier proposed the most radical solution: demolish the existing city and rebuild it as towers in a park. His 1925 "Plan Voisin" proposed demolishing central Paris north of the Seine and replacing it with 18 identical cruciform skyscrapers.

```
LE CORBUSIER'S LOGIC:

  Problem: Dense, low-rise city is unhealthy
           (no light, no air, no greenery)

  Solution: TOWERS IN PARK

  +-------+    +-------+    +-------+
  |  ||   |    |  ||   |    |  ||   |
  | tower |    | tower |    | tower |
  |  ||   |    |  ||   |    |  ||   |
  +-------+    +-------+    +-------+

  [   green park / car roads between towers   ]

  Reasoning: Towers = same density as slums
             but with 95% green space between.

  MASSIVE influence on post-war housing:
  - Pruitt-Igoe, St. Louis (1954) -- demolished 1972-76
  - Cabrini-Green, Chicago (1942-1962)
  - UK council estate high-rises
  - Soviet "microrayon" residential districts

  What went wrong:
  - Eliminated "eyes on the street" (natural surveillance)
  - Destroyed existing community networks
  - Concentrated poverty in isolated towers
  - Maintenance costs not budgeted for
  - Cars between towers = no pedestrian life
  - Vertical separation of uses killed street activity
```

### The Jacobs Critique (1961 — Present)

Jane Jacobs' "The Death and Life of Great American Cities" (1961) was the most consequential critique of modernist planning. Jacobs was not an academic — she was a journalist and activist who observed cities as they actually functioned.

```
JACOBS' FOUR CONDITIONS FOR URBAN VITALITY:

1. MIXED PRIMARY USES
   +-----------------------------------------------+
   | Office + housing + retail + culture =         |
   | activity at different times of day.            |
   | Single-use zones = dead streets                |
   | after 5pm (or 9am on weekends).                |
   +-----------------------------------------------+

2. SHORT BLOCKS
   +-----------------------------------------------+
   | Short blocks = more intersections =           |
   | more routes = more foot traffic =             |
   | more viable retail = more safety.             |
   | Portland: 200ft. Typical US: 400-600ft.       |
   +-----------------------------------------------+

3. BUILDINGS OF DIFFERENT AGES
   +-----------------------------------------------+
   | New buildings = high rents = only chains.     |
   | Old buildings = cheap rents = small risky     |
   | uses (galleries, restaurants, startups).      |
   | Both needed for a living neighborhood.        |
   +-----------------------------------------------+

4. CONCENTRATED DENSITY
   +-----------------------------------------------+
   | Enough people per acre to support diverse     |
   | businesses and active streets.                |
   | Below threshold: not enough customers for     |
   | specialized retail to survive.                |
   +-----------------------------------------------+

"The sidewalk ballet" -- the daily choreography
of a living neighborhood: morning delivery,
school kids, lunch crowds, evening shoppers.
Destroyed by separation of functions.
```

Jacobs fought Robert Moses (NYC's master planner) on multiple fronts, most famously blocking the Lower Manhattan Expressway that would have demolished SoHo and Greenwich Village. She won. Moses lost. The conflict defines the central tension in 20th-century American urbanism: infrastructure-scale top-down planning vs. neighborhood-scale bottom-up life.

## Planning Theory — How Planners Think

### Rational Comprehensive Planning (1950s — 1970s)

The dominant post-war paradigm: planners as objective technical experts applying scientific methods to determine optimal outcomes.

```
RATIONAL COMPREHENSIVE MODEL:

  1. Define goals
       |
       v
  2. Collect data
       |
       v
  3. Generate alternatives
       |
       v
  4. Evaluate alternatives
       |
       v
  5. Select optimal solution
       |
       v
  6. Implement
       |
       v
  7. Evaluate results --> feedback to step 1

  ASSUMPTIONS:
  - Planner is a neutral expert
  - Values can be separated from facts
  - Comprehensive analysis is feasible

  CRITIQUE (Lindblom 1959, "The Science of Muddling Through"):
  Nobody plans comprehensively. All real planning
  is "disjointed incrementalism" -- small adjustments
  from the status quo. The rational model is
  a normative ideal, not a description of practice.
```

### Communicative/Collaborative Planning (1980s — Present)

John Forester (1989) and Patsy Healey (1997) reframed planning as an inherently political communicative act. Planners do not discover optimal solutions; they facilitate negotiations between actors with different interests.

```
COMMUNICATIVE PLANNING:

  "Planning is not optimization. It is
   structured argumentation and negotiation."
   -- Forester, "Planning in the Face of Power"

  Key moves:
  - Acknowledge that "facts" are contested
  - Design deliberative processes (not just
    informational public hearings)
  - Treat local knowledge as legitimate
  - Habermas's ideal speech situation as
    normative goal (undistorted communication)

  Weakness: Can be captured by organized interests.
  "Collaborative planning" that gives veto to
  existing homeowners is not neutral --
  it privileges incumbents over future residents.
```

### Advocacy Planning (1965 — Present)

Paul Davidoff (1965, "Advocacy and Pluralism in Planning") argued planners should not pretend to neutrality.

```
ADVOCACY PLANNING LOGIC:

  Rational planning claimed: "We serve the public interest."

  Davidoff's response: "There is no single public interest.
  There are competing interests. Planners should be advocates
  for specific groups -- especially those without resources
  to hire their own technical experts."

  Legacy:
  - Environmental justice movement
  - Community-based planning organizations
  - Participatory design
  - Equity planning (Norman Krumholz in Cleveland)
```

### New Urbanism (1993 — Present)

Congress for the New Urbanism (CNU, founded by Andres Duany, Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk, Peter Calthorpe) as a design-based response to sprawl.

```
NEW URBANISM PRINCIPLES:

  Traditional Neighborhood Design (TND):
  - Walkable 5-minute radius from center
  - Mixed uses (housing above retail)
  - Variety of housing types and prices
  - Civic buildings at prominent sites
  - Grid or connected streets
  - Parking hidden (rear alleys, garages)

  Transit-Oriented Development (TOD):
  - Dense, mixed-use within 1/4-mile of transit
  - Tapering density outward from station
  - Parking maximums, not minimums
  - Calthorpe's "Pedestrian Pocket" (1989)

  SmartCode: form-based code template
  implementing NU principles.

  Built examples: Seaside FL, Kentlands MD, Stapleton CO.

  Critique: Can be nostalgic/exclusionary.
  Often greenfield (suburban) not infill.
  "Neotraditional" aesthetics mandate.
```

## The Implementation Gap

Plans are not buildings. The distance between an adopted plan and what gets built is vast, contested, and politically determined.

```
PLAN --> IMPLEMENTATION GAP:

  General Plan / Comprehensive Plan
  (aspirational, not legally binding)
         |
         | GAP 1: Zoning may contradict plan
         v
  Zoning Code + Map
  (legally binding land use regulation)
         |
         | GAP 2: Developer proposes something
         |        different --> discretionary review
         v
  Development Application
  (specific project, specific site)
         |
         | GAP 3: Entitlement process
         |        Public hearings, EIR/CEQA,
         |        neighbor opposition, political
         |        pressure, conditions of approval
         v
  Approved Entitlement
         |
         | GAP 4: Finance, market timing,
         |        construction costs
         v
  Permitted Project
         |
         | GAP 5: Change orders, inspections,
         |        certificate of occupancy
         v
  BUILT BUILDING
  (5-10 years after first plan, often
   radically different from original vision)

At every gap, organized interests can
intervene to block, modify, or redirect.
```

## The Software Architecture Analogy

Three TCS bridges into urban planning: (1) Transit network design is **graph optimization** — Wardrop's first principle (users minimize individual travel time) is a Nash equilibrium on the network graph; the system-optimal solution requires tolling (pricing externalities). (2) Urban growth models are **agent-based simulation** — cellular automata and ABMs model land-use change through local rules producing emergent spatial patterns. (3) Zoning is **constraint satisfaction** — permitted uses = feasible set, FAR = capacity bound, building permit = feasibility check.

Urban planning and software architecture share the same fundamental problem: designing systems that will be built incrementally by many independent actors over decades, where early decisions constrain later options.

```
URBAN PLANNING            SOFTWARE ARCHITECTURE
--------------            ---------------------
Zoning code           ~   API contract / interface spec
General plan          ~   System architecture diagram
Building permit       ~   Pull request review
Variance              ~   Technical exception / RFC carve-out
NIMBY opposition      ~   Stakeholder resistance to refactoring
Legacy building       ~   Legacy codebase (cannot demolish easily)
Easement              ~   Shared dependency
Right-of-way          ~   Platform layer owned by no one
Infrastructure        ~   Platform layer others build on
Land rent             ~   Compute/storage cost as binding constraint

PATH DEPENDENCY (in both):
  Early decisions (1811 Manhattan grid, 1956 Interstate Highway
  System) constrain ALL future decisions at enormous reversal cost.
  Once a freeway is built through a neighborhood, or a database
  schema is in production, the cost of change is extraordinary.

CONWAY'S LAW ANALOG:
  "The form of a city mirrors the governance structure
   that produced it."
  Monocentric government --> monocentric city structure.
  Fragmented metro governance --> fragmented polycentric
  city with no coordination on regional housing or transit.

COMPLEX ADAPTIVE SYSTEMS (both):
  Individual actors optimize locally.
  Global behavior emerges -- not designed, often surprising.
  In cities: each developer builds what the market pays for.
  City-level outcomes (gentrification, traffic, affordability)
  emerge from those decisions and routinely surprise planners.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"The comprehensive plan is legally binding"**
It is not (in the US). It is aspirational policy. Zoning is the legally binding instrument. They are supposed to be consistent, but often are not. Oregon requires consistency; most states do not strictly enforce it.

**"Planners decide what gets built"**
Planners write rules and review applications. Developers propose. Politicians decide. The market finances. Planners are one node in a complex decision network, not the central authority.

**"New Urbanism and Smart Growth are the same thing"**
New Urbanism is a design philosophy (architecture, street design, block structure, aesthetics). Smart Growth is a policy framework (where to grow, compact development, infill over greenfield). They are complementary but distinct. NU can be applied on greenfield suburban sites that are not smart in the growth-management sense.

**"Urban planning is the same as urban design"**
Urban planning: land use policy, zoning, economics, governance. Urban design: the physical form of the built environment — how buildings relate to streets, public space, massing, building envelopes. Architects do urban design. Planners do urban planning. The fields overlap significantly in form-based codes and corridor plans.

**"The garden city was the origin of modern suburbia"**
Howard intended cooperative land ownership, population limits, and economic integration. What was built as "garden city-inspired" was the visual vocabulary (houses, greenery, curvilinear streets) without the cooperative economics. The mutation produced the opposite of Howard's intent.

**"Haussmann's Paris was purely about aesthetics"**
The boulevards had multiple functions: military (troop movement, cannot be barricaded as in 1848), economic (slum clearance, land speculation), sanitary (sewers, water), and aesthetic. Napoleon III and Haussmann were operating at the intersection of all four.
