Urban economics explains why cities exist, why they grow, why some thrive and others decline, and how economic activity distributes across space. The central puzzle: why are people and firms so much more productive when they are near each other?

## Why Cities Exist: Agglomeration Economies

The fundamental question of urban economics: why do firms and workers cluster in cities when they could spread out and pay less for land?

The answer is agglomeration economies — the productivity benefits of proximity.

### Marshall's Trinity (1890)

Alfred Marshall identified three mechanisms through which geographic concentration of industry creates productivity advantages:

```
MARSHALL'S THREE MECHANISMS:

1. LABOR MARKET POOLING (Thick Markets)
   +-----------------------------------------+
   |                                         |
   |  ISOLATED LOCATION:                     |
   |  Firm A needs 50 engineers.             |
   |  If engineer quits: hard to replace.    |
   |  Engineer: If firm closes: hard to      |
   |  find next job nearby.                  |
   |  Both face risk of mismatch.            |
   |                                         |
   |  CLUSTER:                               |
   |  100 firms, 5,000 engineers.            |
   |  Firm A can poach from Firms B-C.       |
   |  Engineer can move to 99 other firms.   |
   |  Matching quality improves.             |
   |  Both sides reduce risk.                |
   |  Wages higher (competition for labor).  |
   |  Productivity higher (better matches).  |
   +-----------------------------------------+

2. INPUT SHARING (Specialized Suppliers)
   +-----------------------------------------+
   |                                         |
   |  ISOLATED LOCATION:                     |
   |  Firm A needs specialized legal advice  |
   |  on semiconductor patents.              |
   |  Law firm: too small a market to        |
   |  specialize in just that.               |
   |                                         |
   |  CLUSTER:                               |
   |  100 semiconductor firms.               |
   |  Enough demand for a specialized law    |
   |  firm, specialized equipment repair,    |
   |  specialized recruiting firm.           |
   |  Division of labor at the cluster level.|
   |  Each firm gets better specialized      |
   |  inputs at lower cost.                  |
   +-----------------------------------------+

Agglomeration economies are **platform network effects** applied to physical space: value scales superlinearly with density (Metcalfe's law), creating self-reinforcing feedback loops. Zipf's law for city sizes (rank x size = constant) also appears in network degree distributions and word frequency in information theory.

3. KNOWLEDGE SPILLOVERS (Face-to-Face)
   +-----------------------------------------+
   |                                         |
   |  Tacit knowledge: cannot be fully       |
   |  codified in a document or patent.      |
   |  Transmitted by: observation, informal  |
   |  conversation, job-hopping, shared      |
   |  technical communities.                 |
   |                                         |
   |  Silicon Valley: engineers job-hop      |
   |  between firms carrying ideas with      |
   |  them. AnnaLee Saxenian's "Regional     |
   |  Advantage": contrast with Boston's     |
   |  Route 128 (proprietary culture,        |
   |  lower mobility, less innovation).      |
   |                                         |
   |  Glaeser: Cities are "idea machines."   |
   |  Face-to-face interaction produces      |
   |  innovation that remote interaction     |
   |  does not replicate.                    |
   |                                         |
   |  Zucker/Darby (1996): Star scientists   |
   |  (highly cited) co-located with biotech |
   |  startups drove those firms' success.   |
   |  Geographic proximity to specific       |
   |  individuals mattered.                  |
   +-----------------------------------------+
```

### Localization vs. Urbanization Economies

```
TWO TYPES OF AGGLOMERATION:

  LOCALIZATION ECONOMIES (MAR):
  Benefits from co-location of firms
  in the SAME industry.
  Named: Marshall-Arrow-Romer (MAR model).
  Example: Detroit auto industry, Silicon
  Valley semiconductors, Hollywood film,
  Wall Street finance.
  Mechanism: labor pooling + specialized
  suppliers most important for same-industry.

  URBANIZATION ECONOMIES (Jacobs externalities):
  Benefits from the SIZE and DIVERSITY of
  the city -- regardless of your industry.
  Named after Jane Jacobs.
  Jacobs (1969): "The Economy of Cities"
  -- diversity of industries cross-fertilizes
  ideas; new sectors emerge from combination
  of existing ones (brassiere industry in NYC:
  emerged from women entering
  garment industry + need for support).
  Example: NYC's diverse economy.

  MEASUREMENT:
  Localization LQ (location quotient):
  LQ = (industry share in city) /
       (industry share nationally)
  LQ > 1 = location advantage in that industry.

  Urbanization: city size effect on
  productivity controlling for industry.

  EMPIRICAL FINDING:
  Both matter. In early stages: urbanization.
  In mature clusters: localization.
  For innovation: urbanization dominates.
  For manufacturing efficiency: localization.
```

### Quantifying Agglomeration

```
AGGLOMERATION ELASTICITY:
  How much does productivity increase
  when city size doubles?

  Empirical estimate (Combes/Duranton/Gobillon):
  5-8% wage premium per doubling of city employment.
  (i.e., going from city of 1M to city of 2M:
   wages ~5-8% higher, controlling for individual
   and industry effects.)

  SORTING PROBLEM:
  Productive people sort into cities.
  Productive firms sort into cities.
  Is the wage premium from agglomeration,
  or from the kinds of people who live there?

  Combes/Duranton/Gobillon (2008):
  Tracked workers who moved from smaller
  to larger cities. Their wages rose.
  Controlled for individual fixed effects.
  Result: genuine agglomeration effect,
  not just sorting.
  Estimate: ~3-5% productivity boost per
  doubling of city employment density
  (after removing sorting).

  SKILL-BIASED AGGLOMERATION:
  Moretti "The New Geography of Jobs" (2012):
  College graduates benefit ~8% per
  doubling of city size.
  Non-college graduates benefit ~3%.
  Cities increasingly pay off most for
  the highly educated.
  This drives educational sorting and
  income inequality across cities.
```

## Urban Scaling Laws

Geoffrey West, Luis Bettencourt, Jose Lobo (Santa Fe Institute) discovered that cities scale systematically with population.

```
URBAN SCALING RELATIONSHIPS:

  SUPERLINEAR SCALING (N^1.15):
  Wages, GDP, patents, innovation,
  crime, disease all scale faster
  than population.

  Double city size --> 15% more per capita
  of these quantities.

  SUBLINEAR SCALING (N^0.85):
  Infrastructure: road lane-miles,
  electrical cable length, number of
  gas stations, water pipes.

  Double city size --> 15% LESS infrastructure
  per capita needed.

  EXPLAINS: WHY CITIES ARE EFFICIENT
  +-------------------------------+
  |  2x population                |
  |                               |
  |  Economic output: 2.3x       |  (superlinear)
  |  Infrastructure cost: 1.8x   |  (sublinear)
  |  NET: 2.3/1.8 = cities are    |
  |  economically efficient       |
  +-------------------------------+

  THE 15% RULE:
  Every time you double city size:
  +15% per capita economic output
  -15% per capita infrastructure cost

  EXCEPTIONS (also superlinear):
  Crime: also scales as N^1.15
  Infectious disease: also superlinear
  CO2 emissions from driving: sublinear
  (larger cities = more transit + walking)
  Traffic congestion: mixed (more severe
  in large cities but not consistently scaling)

  TEMPORAL SCALING:
  Cities grow, their metabolism speeds up.
  Firms eventually plateau. Cities (so far)
  have not found a bound.
  West: "The city is the greatest social
  invention in history."
```

## Spatial Structure: Central Place Theory

Walter Christaller (1933) developed central place theory to explain why settlements of different sizes are distributed the way they are.

```
CHRISTALLER'S CENTRAL PLACE THEORY (1933):

  ASSUMPTIONS:
  - Homogeneous plain
  - Uniform population density
  - People buy from nearest supplier
  - Transport cost proportional to distance

  MARKET AREA:
  Each seller has a market area.
  Size determined by: range (max travel
  distance for a purchase) and threshold
  (minimum customers to be viable).

  HIERARCHY:
  High-order goods: infrequent purchase
  (car dealer, specialist doctor, university).
  Wide range. Large market area.
  Few centers. Widely spaced.

  Low-order goods: frequent purchase
  (grocery, barbershop, gas station).
  Short range. Small market area.
  Many centers. Closely spaced.

  RESULT: Nested hexagonal market areas.
  K=3 marketing principle (each higher
  center serves 3x as many people).
  K=4 transport principle (4x).
  K=7 administrative principle (7x).

RANK-SIZE RULE / ZIPF'S LAW:
  Empirical regularity discovered by
  George Zipf (1949):
  Rank × Size = Constant
  OR: Size(city rank r) = Size(rank 1) / r

  EXAMPLE (US metro areas ~2020):
  Rank 1 (NYC): 20M
  Rank 2 (LA): ~10M (expected: 10M, actual 13M)
  Rank 3 (Chicago): 6.7M (expected: 6.7M)
  Rank 5: ~4M (expected: 4M)
  Rank 10: ~2M (expected: 2M)

  US: Fits well (except LA slightly large).
  Most developed countries: fits.

  DEVIATIONS:
  PRIMATE CITIES: One city dominates far
  beyond Zipf prediction.
  Paris: 12M vs France's next city Lyon 1.7M.
  Bangkok: 10M vs Thailand's next city Chiang Mai 1.6M.
  Buenos Aires: 15M vs Cordoba 1.5M.
  Causes: Colonial legacy, single economic
  center, political centralization.

  SUB-ZIPF: No dominant city.
  Germany, Netherlands, US (relative):
  federated history, multiple capitals,
  distributed economic activity.
```

## New Economic Geography (Krugman 1991)

Paul Krugman won the 2008 Nobel Prize partly for his "New Economic Geography" models.

```
KRUGMAN'S CORE-PERIPHERY MODEL:

  Two regions. Two sectors:
  - Agriculture (dispersed, immobile)
  - Manufacturing (footloose)

  CENTRIPETAL FORCES (pull to core):
  - Forward linkages: workers want to buy
    manufactured goods --> go to where goods
    are produced (lower transport costs)
  - Backward linkages: firms want to sell
    to large markets --> go to where population is

  CENTRIFUGAL FORCES (push to periphery):
  - Land rent: higher in core
  - Agricultural goods: fixed dispersal
    (food must be near farmers)
  - Congestion

  BIFURCATION:
  Below a critical transport cost:
  Agglomeration wins. Core emerges.
  Above a critical transport cost:
  Dispersal wins. Even distribution.

  POLICY IMPLICATION:
  Trade liberalization (lower transport costs)
  --> more agglomeration.
  EU integration --> more geographic concentration.
  This is what happened: German manufacturing
  became more concentrated post-single market.

  CUMULATIVE CAUSATION (Myrdal):
  Once a region gets ahead, it pulls ahead.
  Capital and labor flow to growing regions.
  Backwash effects dominate spread effects.
  Regional divergence is the natural equilibrium.
  Intervention required to prevent it.
```

## Creative Class Theory and Critiques

Richard Florida's "The Rise of the Creative Class" (2002) was massively influential on urban economic development policy in the 2000s-2010s.

```
FLORIDA'S CREATIVE CLASS THEORY:

  PREMISE:
  The economy is shifting from industrial
  to creative production.
  "Creative class" = workers who create
  new ideas, designs, content, technology,
  entertainment.
  ~38M US workers (30% of workforce).

  3 T's FRAMEWORK:
  +------------------------------------------+
  |  TECHNOLOGY: High-tech industry presence |
  |  TALENT: % of workforce with college degree|
  |  TOLERANCE: Gay Index, Bohemian Index    |
  |  (open, diverse, tolerant culture)       |
  +------------------------------------------+
  Cities high on all 3T = attract creative
  class = economic growth.

  INDICATORS:
  Gay Index: (gay couples in city) /
  (gay couples nationally) -- as proxy
  for openness to diversity.
  Bohemian Index: artists, musicians,
  writers, actors as % of workforce.

  POLICY PRESCRIPTION:
  Cities should invest in:
  - Edgy arts districts
  - Coffee shops, bike lanes
  - LGBTQ+ friendly policies
  - University research
  to attract creative class.

  CRITIQUES:

  PECK (2005), "Struggling with the
  Creative Class":
  Florida's policy prescription ("build
  it and they will come" for creatives)
  led cities to invest in entertainment
  districts and arts venues while neglecting:
  - Low-wage service workers
  - Affordable housing
  - Basic services
  Creative class strategy = gentrification
  strategy that benefits existing professionals
  while displacing low-income residents.
  "A good idea hijacked by yuppies."

  CAUSATION/CORRELATION:
  Cities with high creative class already
  had tech industries and universities.
  Tolerance may attract talent, but talent
  and agglomeration may have come first.

  INEQUALITY:
  Florida himself (2017, "The New Urban Crisis"):
  acknowledged that creative class strategy
  produced extreme inequality and housing
  unaffordability. NYC, SF, DC: world-class
  creative economies, world-class housing crises.
  "Winner-take-all urbanism."

  WHAT SURVIVES:
  The data that diverse, tolerant cities
  attract high-skill workers is real.
  The causal story and the policy prescription
  are more contested.
  Tolerance matters (especially for LGBTQ+
  workers). Universities matter.
  But housing, transit, and services for
  non-creative workers matter too.
```

## The Great Divergence: Superstar Cities

```
CITY DIVERGENCE SINCE 1980:

  SUPERSTAR METROS:
  NYC, SF, Boston, Seattle, DC.
  Winner-take-all agglomeration.
  Premium wages for college graduates.
  Wage growth: 60-80th percentile up strongly.

  LAGGING METROS:
  Detroit, Cleveland, St. Louis, Milwaukee.
  Manufacturing collapse.
  Population decline.
  Property value decline.
  Social service demand increase.

  MORETTI'S "NEW GEOGRAPHY OF JOBS" (2012):
  "Innovation hub" vs. "brain drain" metros.
  Every high-skilled job in innovation hub
  creates 5 additional local service jobs
  (the local multiplier).
  High-skill agglomeration feeds itself.

  THE HOUSING COST PROBLEM:
  Wage premium in superstar cities:
  30-50% above national average.
  Housing cost premium:
  200-500% above national average.
  Real wage (after housing): not much
  better than lower-cost metros.
  People cannot migrate to high-wage cities
  because housing restricts entry.
  This is the misallocation of labor thesis
  (Chang-Tai Hsieh / Enrico Moretti 2019):
  If SF/NY had Houston-style housing supply:
  US GDP would be 2-9% higher (labor
  could flow to most productive uses).

  THE REMOTE WORK DISRUPTION (post-2020):
  COVID revealed: many knowledge workers
  can work from anywhere.
  Geographic premium for living in expensive
  city may decline.
  BUT: Bloom et al.: Hybrid work (not fully remote)
  dominates. Face-to-face still matters for
  certain activities (onboarding, collaboration,
  serendipitous encounters).
  Prediction: some deconcentration, but
  superstar cities will not lose dominance.
  They will evolve (more hybrid, more
  distributed within metro, less
  daily commute to downtown core).
```

## Economic Development Tools

### Enterprise Zones / Opportunity Zones

```
ENTERPRISE ZONES:
  Tax incentives to locate businesses in
  distressed areas. Federal designation
  + state incentives + local incentives.
  Origin: UK (Thatcher, 1980).
  US: various state programs since 1980s.
  Federal Empowerment Zones (Clinton era).

  EVIDENCE:
  Mixed at best. Zone benefits often
  capture activity that would have
  occurred anyway ("deadweight loss").
  Businesses at zone edge relocate across
  the line to capture incentive.
  Little evidence of sustained job creation
  for pre-existing residents.

OPPORTUNITY ZONES (2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act):
  Federal program: census tracts
  designated by governors as Opportunity Zones.
  Investors who reinvest capital gains in
  Qualified Opportunity Funds (QOF):
  - Defer capital gains until 2026
  - Reduce gain by 10-15% if held 5-7 years
  - Exclude new gains in OZ investments
    if held 10+ years

  SCALE: 8,762 Opportunity Zone tracts.
  Investment estimates: $75-100B as of 2022.

  EVIDENCE:
  Sage/Zwick (2022): OZ investments concentrated
  in already-improving areas, not distressed.
  Treasury Office of Tax Analysis (2020):
  minimal impact on employment or poverty
  in designated areas.
  Primary beneficiary: wealthy investors
  with capital gains.
  Classic case: tax subsidy delivered to
  investors, not to communities.
```

### Business Improvement Districts (BIDs)

```
BID STRUCTURE:
  Defined geographic area.
  Property owners assessed a fee
  (mandatory, collected with property taxes).
  BID organization provides:
  - Supplemental cleaning and sanitation
  - Security (ambassadors, cameras)
  - Marketing and events
  - Streetscaping (planters, lighting)
  - Business advocacy

  SCALE: ~1,000 BIDs in US.
  NYC: 76 BIDs, $250M+ annual spending.
  Bryant Park BID: transformed from
  dangerous drug market to successful
  public space. One of the best case
  studies in urban management.

  EVIDENCE ON EFFECTIVENESS:
  Ellen et al. (2007): BIDs reduce crime
  in their areas and in adjacent areas.
  Hoyt (2005): BIDs increase commercial
  property values.
  Brooks (2008): small BIDs disproportionately
  reduce violent crime.

  EQUITY CONCERNS:
  BIDs serve commercial property owners.
  In residential neighborhoods: no BID
  mechanism (homeowner associations fill
  gap in private communities, but not
  in renter neighborhoods).
  BID services create "clean zones" where
  homeless individuals are moved out of
  BID territory -- not housed or helped.
  The question: supplemental services or
  displacement of public priorities?
```

### Stadium Economics: The Big Lie

```
STADIUM ECONOMICS:

  THE PROMISE (team owners / developers):
  New stadium will:
  - Create X,000 jobs
  - Generate $Y million in economic activity
  - Revitalize surrounding neighborhood

  THE REALITY (economists, including skeptics
  of all political stripes):
  Noll and Zimbalist (1997): "Sports, Jobs
  and Taxes" -- comprehensive study.
  Coates and Humphreys (1999): per capita
  income actually declines in cities
  that build new sports facilities.

  WHY STADIUMS FAIL AS ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT:
  1. SUBSTITUTION: Sports spending substitutes
     for other entertainment spending.
     People have fixed entertainment budgets.
     Money spent at Seahawks game is not
     spent at restaurants or theaters.
     Net new spending: near zero.

  2. IMPORT SUBSTITUTION: Player salaries
     go to players who live elsewhere
     (or in gated communities). Not
     recirculated in local economy.

  3. CANNIBALIZATION: Stadium districts
     often displace existing businesses.

  4. EVENT ECONOMY: 8-16 events/year at
     an NFL stadium. Vast infrastructure
     (100,000+ seat venue) serving sporadic
     events. Low utilization rate.

  5. THE PUBLIC SUBSIDY:
     Average NFL stadium: $500M-2B.
     Typical public subsidy: 40-60%
     ($200M-$1B of public money).
     Return on investment: near zero
     for public.

  EXCEPTION: Planned sports-anchored districts
  with mixed use (housing, office, retail)
  and high utilization (arena with basketball
  + hockey + concerts + events):
  Evidence slightly better. But still
  rarely justifies the subsidy level.

  POLITICAL ECONOMY:
  Team owners have enormous leverage:
  "Move the team" threat.
  Fans (voters) want to keep the team.
  Politicians capitulate.
  Economists consistently oppose.
  Politicians consistently fund stadiums.
  The gap between economic evidence and
  political outcome is near-perfect.
```

## Microsoft Context: Why Urban Economics Matters

```
MICROSOFT'S URBAN ECONOMICS:

  REDMOND HQ HISTORY:
  Microsoft moved from Albuquerque to
  Bellevue WA (1979), then Redmond campus (1986).
  Low land cost, proximity to University of
  Washington engineering program, founders'
  origins (Gates/Allen from Seattle area).
  Classic "thin market" labor pool growth:
  Microsoft attracted engineers who attracted
  more engineers. Localization economy.

  TALENT AGGLOMERATION:
  Microsoft is a localization force --
  it makes the Seattle tech cluster more
  productive for all tech firms.
  Amazon (2010s Seattle growth), Zillow,
  Expedia, Tableau all benefit from the
  labor pool Microsoft helped build.

  THE HOUSING COST CONSEQUENCE:
  Microsoft + Amazon tech compensation
  bids up Seattle housing prices.
  Entry-level engineer at $150K
  cannot afford to buy in Redmond.
  Microsoft's 2019 commitment:
  $750M over 3 years to increase
  affordable housing supply in Puget Sound.
  This is Microsoft explicitly recognizing
  that housing markets are a talent strategy
  constraint, not just an abstract policy issue.

  DATA CENTER AGGLOMERATION:
  Microsoft's hyperscale data center decisions
  are urban economics in action:
  - Power cost (electricity price differential)
  - Water availability (cooling)
  - Fiber connectivity (network topology)
  - Tax incentives (government competition)
  - Labor pool (technical operators)
  - Land cost
  DC-area Northern Virginia: data center
  "cluster" -- Ashburn, the "Data Center
  Capital of the World" (~70% of world's
  internet traffic transits through it).
  Classic localization economy:
  specialized labor, dense fiber, power
  infrastructure, proximity to DC demand.

  CONWAY'S LAW OF CITIES:
  Microsoft builds its campuses in the
  form of its organizational structure:
  Redmond (core engineering) + distributed
  sales/engineering offices in every major
  metro (talent access) + data center
  regions (infrastructure requirements).
  The geographic footprint mirrors the
  organizational architecture.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Agglomeration is just about being in a big city"**
Agglomeration is about density and specialization, not just size. A dense cluster of related firms in a small metro can have stronger agglomeration effects than isolated firms in a large metro. Silicon Valley agglomeration benefits existed when the area was much smaller than LA. It's not city size but cluster density and relatedness.

**"Creative class strategy failed everywhere"**
It worked in cities that already had strong technical clusters and universities. It failed in cities that tried to attract creatives as a substitute for economic fundamentals. A coffee shop district does not create jobs; a research university does.

**"Opportunity zones help distressed areas"**
The evidence is that OZ investment has concentrated in areas that were already gentrifying or that offered attractive real estate plays (luxury apartments in "technically OZ" census tracts adjacent to thriving areas). The capital gains deferral primarily benefits high-income investors. Community benefit: limited.

**"Stadiums create economic development"**
The economic consensus against stadium subsidies is unusually strong for urban economics (where most things are contested). Noll, Zimbalist, Coates, Humphreys, Baade — economists across the ideological spectrum agree that stadiums do not generate net economic benefit proportional to their public subsidy. The political economy (voters want teams) overrides the economic evidence.

**"City productivity comes from the people, not the place"**
The sorting vs. agglomeration debate. The evidence (Combes/Duranton/Gobillon, tracking workers who move) shows genuine agglomeration effects after controlling for worker quality. The place itself — the density, the connections, the knowledge flows — generates productivity above and beyond the quality of the workers in it.
