# 01 — Ancient Maps

## What Maps Were For Before They Had to Be Accurate

The history of ancient cartography is a history of maps that worked perfectly for their intended purpose while being dramatically wrong by modern geographic standards. This is not a failure story. A T-O map that puts Jerusalem at the center is not a bad map — it is a precise answer to a different question than "where is Jerusalem relative to London?" Understanding what question each map was answering is the entire analytic task.

```
ANCIENT MAP GENEALOGY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  600 BCE   Babylonian World Map
            Clay tablet, Sippar, Iraq
            Ocean ring encircling flat disk
            Purpose: cosmological representation
                │
                │  (parallel tradition — Greek geography)
                │
  ~500 BCE  Greek theoretical geography
            Hecataeus, Anaximander — disk surrounded by ocean
            First attempt at systematic coastal description
                │
  ~150 CE   Ptolemy's Geographia
            Coordinates + projection + 8,000 place names
            Purpose: comprehensive geographic reference
                │
                │  (knowledge lost in Western Europe ~600 CE)
                │
  ~400 CE   Peutinger Table (Roman itinerary)
            Scroll format — roads and distances
            Purpose: military/administrative routing
                │
  ~700–     T-O Maps (mappa mundi)
  ~1400     Jerusalem centered, East at top
            Purpose: theological cosmography
                │

            Most complete surviving mappa mundi
            ~500 named locations, biblical and classical

               (empirical coastal surveys — see 02-PORTOLAN)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## The Babylonian World Map (c. 600 BCE)

The Babylonian World Map (Imago Mundi) is a clay tablet discovered at Sippar, now in the British Museum. It is approximately 12 cm × 8 cm. Most of what makes it significant has nothing to do with geographic accuracy.

```
BABYLONIAN WORLD MAP — STRUCTURE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ╔═══════════════════════════════════════╗
  ║      OUTER OCEAN (ring/moat)          ║
  ║  ┌──────────────────────────────┐     ║
  ║  │  Babylon (at center)         │     ║
  ║  │      ●                       │     ║
  ║  │  Assyria  ●    ●  Urartu     │     ║
  ║  │                              │     ║
  ║  │  Swamp  ●        ● Cities   │     ║
  ║  │                              │     ║
  ║  │  Bit Yakin ●                 │     ║
  ║  │   (marshes of S. Iraq)       │     ║
  ║  └──────────────────────────────┘     ║
  ║  ╱ ╲  Triangular "outer regions"      ║
  ║  labeled with legendary distances     ║
  ╚═══════════════════════════════════════╝

  Key features:
  - Flat disk of known world
  - Babylon at geometric center
  - Ocean ring = absolute boundary
  - Outer triangles = mythological regions
    beyond the ocean, named by distance
    ("4 beru" = ~28km past the ocean)

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**What it was for**: This is a schematic diagram of the cosmos, not a navigation tool. The text accompanying the map describes mythological regions and their legendary inhabitants. The Babylonians knew perfectly well that the world extended beyond the map's edge — they traded with distant regions. The map encodes cosmological structure: Babylon is the center because Babylon is the center of the cosmos, not because someone measured the distances.

**The key insight**: The map is correct for its purpose. If your question is "what is the structure of the cosmos?" then Babylon-at-center with ocean surrounding is a defensible answer given Babylonian theology. If your question is "how do I sail from Babylon to Egypt?" you would use a different document (text-based itinerary, local pilots, accumulated route knowledge).

No one was confused about this distinction at the time. The error would be to evaluate the Babylonian World Map as a failed attempt at geographic accuracy rather than a successful attempt at cosmological representation.

## Ptolemy's Geographia (~150 CE)

Claudius Ptolemy (Alexandria, ~100–170 CE) wrote the Geographia in eight volumes. It is arguably the most consequential work in the history of cartography — not because it was accurate, but because it introduced the framework that all subsequent cartography would use.

```
PTOLEMY'S INNOVATIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  COORDINATE SYSTEM
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Latitude:  0° at equator, 90° at pole                     │
  │             measured from equator northward                │
  │  Longitude: 0° at "Fortunate Isles" (Canary Islands)       │
  │             measured eastward to end of known world        │
  │                                                            │
  │  Every place name has (latitude, longitude) coordinates    │
  │  ~8,000 named locations with coordinates in Geographia     │
  │                                                            │
  │  This IS the modern system. We just moved the prime        │
  │  meridian to Greenwich in 1884.                            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PROJECTION (first systematic use)
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Ptolemy used conic projection for his world maps          │
  │  and a modified conic for his regional maps                │
  │                                                            │
  │  Meridians converge toward north pole (correctly)          │
  │  Parallels as arcs of circles                              │
  │                                                            │
  │  First cartographer to acknowledge the problem of          │
  │  projecting a sphere onto a flat surface and offer         │
  │  a systematic solution                                     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  STRUCTURE OF THE GEOGRAPHIA
  Book 1:    Theory — how to construct a world map,
             projection methods, coordinate system
  Books 2–7: Gazetteer — place names with coordinates
             organized by region
  Book 8:    Regional map descriptions (27 regions)

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### The Errors and Why They Mattered

Ptolemy's Geographia contained systematic errors that shaped world history:

**The Mediterranean problem**: Ptolemy's Mediterranean is roughly 25% too long east-west. He relied on Marinus of Tyre's measurements, which accumulated small errors in the dead-reckoning distances that sailors reported. The systematic bias was that sailors overestimated distances traveled — they were counting strokes, not measuring displacement.

**The Earth-size problem**: Ptolemy used Posidonius's estimate of Earth's circumference (~28,000 km vs the actual ~40,000 km). His Earth was too small.

**The Asia-is-close problem**: Combined — a Mediterranean that's too long and an Earth that's too small meant that the distance from Europe westward to Asia appeared far shorter than it actually is. Columbus used Ptolemy. When Columbus found land 37 days after leaving the Canary Islands — roughly where Ptolemy said Japan should be — he concluded (incorrectly) that he had found Asia. He held this belief until his death.

**The Indian Ocean problem**: Ptolemy drew the Indian Ocean as an enclosed sea, with Africa connecting to a "southern land" that enclosed it from the south. This persisted on European maps until Bartolomeu Dias rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1488, proving that Africa had a southern tip and the Indian Ocean was open.

These errors were not random noise — they were systematic biases with consequences. Ptolemy's framework was correct; his data was wrong.

### Ptolemy's Legacy

The Geographia was lost to Western Europe after the fall of Rome. Arab geographers preserved and extended it (al-Idrisi, 12th century, produced a world map far more accurate than Ptolemy's for the Middle East and Mediterranean). When a Byzantine manuscript of the Geographia arrived in Florence in 1406, translated into Latin, it detonated the European geographic imagination. See 03-AGE-OF-EXPLORATION for the consequences.

## The Peutinger Table (Tabula Peutingeriana)

The Peutinger Table is a medieval copy (c. 1200) of a Roman map originally created around the 4th century CE. It depicts the road network of the Roman Empire — roughly 200,000 km of roads from Britain to India.

```
PEUTINGER TABLE — WHAT IT IS AND ISN'T
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FORMAT:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Scroll: approximately 6.82m long × 0.34m tall              │
  │  Horizontal stretch: entire Roman world from Britain to India│
  │  Vertical compression: massive (north-south squashed)       │
  │                                                             │
  │  Britain  → Rome → Constantinople → Antioch → Persia → India│
  │  ←————————————————— 6.82 meters ————————————————————————→   │
  │  ↕ 34 cm (north-south — Mediterranean is a thin line)       │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHAT IT SHOWS:
  ✓  Roads with distance markings (in Roman miles)
  ✓  Cities with pictographic symbols (size = importance)
  ✓  Way stations (mansiones) for overnight stops
  ✓  Rivers, mountains as rough landmarks
  ✓  Spas and bathing facilities (marked specially)

  WHAT IT DOESN'T SHOW:
  ✗  Geographic accuracy (Mediterranean is ~30cm wide)
  ✗  Relative distances to scale
  ✗  Orientation (barely geographic)
  ✗  Most of what we'd call a map

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**The key insight**: The Peutinger Table is a topological schematic, not a geographic map. It resembles the London Underground map far more than it resembles a road atlas. Harry Beck's 1931 Tube map (which distorted geographic relationships to maximize clarity of connections) was using an approach the Romans had already discovered by pragmatic necessity — when the goal is "how do I get from A to B via the road network," topological relationships (connectivity and sequence) matter more than geographic accuracy.

The Roman road user knew that traveling from Rome to Londinium required crossing the Alps and the Channel. They didn't need the Alps accurately positioned — they needed to know the route, the waypoints, and the distances between them. The Peutinger Table delivers exactly that.

## T-O Maps and the Mappa Mundi Tradition

The T-O map (also called T-in-O) was the dominant world map form in medieval Western Europe from roughly 600 CE to 1400 CE.

```
T-O MAP STRUCTURE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  The T-O form:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    East (top)                             │
  │              ┌─────────────────┐                          │
  │              │   ASIA          │                          │
  │              │  (Noah's sons:  │                          │
  │              │  Shem's land)   │                          │
  │  North ──────┼─────────────────┼────── South             │
  │              │EUROPE │ AFRICA  │                         │
  │              │(Japhet)│(Ham)   │                         │
  │              └────────┴────────┘                         │
  │                    West (bottom)                           │
  │                                                            │
  │  The "T":  Don/Nile rivers (vertical bar)                 │
  │            Mediterranean Sea (horizontal bar)             │
  │  The "O":  Ocean surrounding the known world              │
  │                                                            │
  │  Jerusalem: geometric center of the inhabited world       │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Theological basis:
  - Three continents = three sons of Noah
  - Jerusalem as spiritual and geometric center (Ezekiel 5:5)
  - East at top = orientation toward Paradise (Garden of Eden)
    [This is where "orientation" — east-ing — comes from]

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

Note the etymology embedded in the word "orientation" — it derives from *orient* (east). Medieval European maps put east at the top; you oriented yourself by facing east toward the rising sun and Paradise. The modern convention (north at top) is a choice, not an inherent feature of maps. Chinese maps historically used south at top. There is no physically meaningful "up" for a map of Earth's surface.

### The Hereford Mappa Mundi (~1300)

The most elaborate surviving mappa mundi is the Hereford Map (~1.59m × 1.34m, vellum) now in Hereford Cathedral. It is simultaneously a geographic reference, a religious document, a bestiary, and a historical narrative.

```
HEREFORD MAPPA MUNDI — CONTENTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Center: Jerusalem
  Top: Christ in Majesty (Last Judgment scene)
  East (top): Garden of Eden with Adam and Eve
  Outer ring: Ocean with monsters

  Content includes (~500 named locations):
  ├── Biblical geography (Exodus route, Christ's life)
  ├── Classical history (Troy, Alexander's conquests)
  ├── Marvels of the East (Cynocephali — dog-headed people,
  │   Sciapods — people who use their feet as shade,
  │   Blemmyae — people whose faces are in their chests)
  ├── Actual geographic features (Rhine, Nile, Britain)
  └── Pilgrimage routes (indirectly encoded)

  PURPOSE: encyclopedic representation of the known world
  in its religious, historical, and classical dimensions

  NOT PURPOSE: navigation, distance estimation,
  coordinate lookup

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**What the mappa mundi was actually for**: Partly religious education (theological geography made visual), partly classical reference (where Troy was, where Alexander campaigned), partly entertainment (the monsters and marvels at the edges), and partly legitimation of Christian cosmological claims. Placing Jerusalem at the center was not geographic ignorance — it was a theological assertion encoded in cartographic form.

The monks who made these maps knew that Jerusalem was not the geometric center of the Earth's land area. They had access to Ptolemy's work via Arabic transmission in some cases. The choice to center Jerusalem was a statement of values, not a measurement error.

## The Radical Shift: From Sacred Space to Spherical Space

```
TWO WAYS OF ANSWERING "WHERE AM I?"
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  RELATIONAL / SACRED GEOGRAPHY:
  "I am 3 days' journey from Rome, which is 12 days from
   Jerusalem, which is the center of the sacred world."

  Location = narrative + relationship to sacred centers
  Distance = time, effort, social/religious significance
  Map = cosmological diagram

  COORDINATE GEOGRAPHY (Ptolemy):
  "I am at 51°N, 0°W."

  Location = mathematical tuple (φ, λ)
  Distance = calculable from coordinates
  Map = mathematical projection of sphere onto plane

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  The shift between these frameworks is the intellectual
  revolution that enabled the Age of Exploration.

  Without the Ptolemaic framework, you cannot:
  - Plan a circumnavigation
  - Calculate where unexplored land should be
  - Accumulate systematic geographic knowledge across voyages
  - Predict where new landmasses might exist

  The Babylonian/medieval framework is not inferior for its
  own purposes. It is a different ontology of space.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

This is the deep story of ancient cartography: not a progression from bad maps to good maps, but from maps embedded in sacred/relational ontologies to maps based on mathematical coordinates. The latter framework is not "more correct" in some absolute sense — it is more useful for navigation, resource exploitation, colonial administration, and scientific geography. Those are not neutral goods; they are the purposes that European expansion required.

## Accuracy Comparison — Ancient Maps vs Modern

| Map | Date | Purpose | Geographic Accuracy | Effective for Purpose? |
|-----|------|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Babylonian World Map | ~600 BCE | Cosmological | Very low | Yes |
| Ptolemy's Geographia | ~150 CE | Geographic reference | Medium (Mediterranean ~25% long) | Yes, for reference |
| Peutinger Table | ~4th c. CE | Road routing | Very low (intentionally) | Yes |
| Hereford Mappa Mundi | ~1300 CE | Religious/encyclopedic | Low | Yes |
| Catalan Atlas | 1375 CE | Navigation/reference | High (Mediterranean) | Yes |

The accuracy column being "low" for non-navigational maps is a category error — like saying a subway map has "low accuracy" because it doesn't show that two stations 1km apart on the map are actually 500m apart.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Medieval Europeans thought the Earth was flat."** No. Educated Europeans had known the Earth was spherical since at least Aristotle (~340 BCE). Eratosthenes measured its circumference in ~240 BCE (to within 1–2%). The spherical Earth is assumed by every significant medieval cosmologist. The T-O maps are not flat-Earth diagrams — they are schematic representations of a known sphere, simplified to a disk for the purpose of representing the three continents in their relative relationships.

**"Ptolemy was useless since his measurements were wrong."** Ptolemy's *framework* (coordinate system + projection theory) was the essential tool. His *data* was wrong. The framework was rediscovered in 1406 and immediately enabled systematic cartographic improvement. The concept of (latitude, longitude) is Ptolemy's; the accurate measurement of positions came later.

**"The Peutinger Table is not a real map."** Depends on your definition of "map." If a map is "a spatial representation that enables route-finding in real geographic space," the Peutinger Table is an excellent map. If a map is "a geographically accurate representation of terrain," it fails. The subway map problem again.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Right tool | Wrong tool |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Where is X relative to sacred/biblical events? | Mappa mundi | Ptolemy's Geographia |
| How do I travel from Rome to Constantinople? | Peutinger Table | Mappa mundi |
| Where on a coordinate grid is Alexandria? | Ptolemy's Geographia | Peutinger Table |
| What is the cosmological structure of the world? | Babylonian World Map | Any modern map |
| Can I sail accurately across the Mediterranean? | Portolan chart (13th c.+) | Any of the above |
