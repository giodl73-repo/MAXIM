# Religion and Secularization

## The Big Picture

Religion is simultaneously the most universal human institution and the one whose future trajectory is most contested. Sociologists since Durkheim have analyzed it not as a question of theological truth but as a **social phenomenon**: what functions it serves, how it changes with modernization, and why the "death of religion" predicted by 19th-century theorists has not occurred uniformly. The secularization debate is now the central empirical controversy in the sociology of religion.

```
SOCIOLOGICAL APPROACHES TO RELIGION:

  FUNCTIONALIST:
    Durkheim — religion as social glue; collective conscience; boundary maintenance
    Parsons — religion integrates normative order; provides ultimate values
    Civil religion (Bellah) — secular societies generate sacred symbols and rituals

  CONFLICT / CRITICAL:
    Marx — religion as ideology; "opium of the people"; legitimates inequality
    Weber — religion shapes economic action; Protestant work ethic
    Bourdieu — religious field; capital; clergy as symbolic power holders

  INTERPRETIVE / MICROSOCIOLOGY:
    Goffman — religious ritual as face-work; interaction ritual chains (Collins)
    Meaning-making: religion provides theodicy (answers to suffering/death/injustice)
    Berger — religion as "sacred canopy"; plausibility structures

  SECULARIZATION / RELIGIOUS CHANGE:
    Wilson, Bruce — differentiation thesis: modern institutions replace religious functions
    Stark, Finke — supply-side model; religious competition; market of faiths
    Casanova — deprivatization; public religion persists; different from decline
    Norris, Inglehart — existential security hypothesis
```

## Durkheim: Religion as Social Solidarity

```
DURKHEIM (The Elementary Forms of Religious Life, 1912):

METHODOLOGICAL APPROACH:
  Study the "simplest" religion to find universal elements
  → Australian Aranda tribe: totemism (clan animal/plant as sacred symbol)
  → Argument: if we understand what religion does in its simplest form,
               we understand what it does universally

SACRED vs PROFANE:
  The fundamental religious distinction is not God vs no God
  → SACRED: things set apart, protected by prohibition, approached with ritual
  → PROFANE: ordinary, everyday, available for use
  → Religion is not about the supernatural; it is about the sacred/profane division
  → The same object can be sacred in one context, profane in another
    (bread = profane; communion wafer = sacred)

TOTEM = CLAN = GOD:
  The totem (eagle, kangaroo, etc.) is the clan's sacred symbol
  Clan members feel awe and reverence for the totem
  But: the totem represents the clan itself — it is the clan's collective identity made concrete
  → Therefore: when worshippers feel the power of God, they are actually feeling
               the power of their social group acting through them
  → "God" is society hypostatized — experienced as external, powerful, demanding

FUNCTIONS OF RELIGION:
  1. Social solidarity: collective rituals → effervescence → shared feelings → cohesion
     "Collective effervescence": the emotional intensification in shared ritual
     → Rock concert, sports stadium, religious service: same sociological structure
  2. Norm clarification: sacred designates what society values most
  3. Meaning provision: theodicy (explains suffering, death, injustice)
  4. Social identity: we/them; community boundary definition

COLLECTIVE EFFERVESCENCE:
  In shared ritual: individual self dissolves momentarily into group
  Participants feel energized, unified, lifted above ordinary life
  → Sacred energy is literally the social energy of group assembly
  → After ritual: sacred objects carry the charge; remind members between gatherings
  Collins (2004) extends: interaction ritual chains → micro-sociology of emotion
    All sacred objects = emotion symbols from previous high-intensity interactions
```

## Weber: Protestant Ethic

```
WEBER (The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism, 1904–05):

THE PUZZLE:
  Weber's starting point: capitalism (rational accumulation; reinvestment) concentrated
  disproportionately in Protestant regions of Europe in the 16th–18th centuries
  → Was this coincidence, or did Protestantism cause/enable capitalism?

  NB: Weber is not claiming religion was the sole cause of capitalism
      He is tracing one causal strand via "elective affinity" — spiritual ideas
      that happened to have unintended economic consequences

CALVINIST THEOLOGY:
  Predestination: God has already decided who is saved; humans cannot change this
  → Psychological problem: am I among the elect? Cannot know. Severe anxiety.
  → Relief strategy: look for signs of election in worldly success
  → If God has blessed me with prosperity → sign I may be elect → anxiety relieved

  → WORK BECAME A RELIGIOUS VOCATION (Beruf = vocation + profession):
    Hard work → more wealth → sign of election
    Leisure, luxury, enjoyment: sin (wealth = sign of grace only if not spent on pleasure)
    → Accumulate wealth; reinvest; don't consume → CAPITAL ACCUMULATION
    This is precisely the behavior required for capitalist investment

SPIRIT OF CAPITALISM:
  The resulting ethic: "time is money" (Benjamin Franklin); work as duty
  Originally religious in motivation; became secular over time
  → "Iron cage" (stahlhartes Gehäuse): capitalism no longer needs religious justification
    but the rationalized, bureaucratized system traps us regardless

ELECTIVE AFFINITY (Wahlverwandtschaft):
  Not simple causation; not that Protestantism mechanically produced capitalism
  Rather: Protestant ideas and capitalist practices had an elective affinity —
    they "chose each other" because each reinforced the other
  → Similar to: how Marxist thought and Russian conditions had elective affinity
  → Weber's method: ideal types + historical comparison; not law-like causation

CRITIQUE AND REVISION:
  Tawney (Religion and the Rise of Capitalism): reversed causation is also plausible
    → Capitalism rose; Protestantism adapted to accommodate it
  Samuelsson: empirical data shows Catholic areas also had early capitalism
  Recent quantitative: Protestant counties had faster 19th-c. growth — but via
    literacy (Bible reading → schools) not via work ethic per se (Becker/Woessmann)
  → Weber's mechanism may be wrong even if correlation is real
```

## Secularization Theory

### The Classic Thesis

```
SECULARIZATION THESIS (Wilson 1966; Bruce 2002):

DIFFERENTIATION:
  Modernization → functional differentiation of social institutions
  → Education separated from church; law secular; medicine scientific
  → Church loses functions → loses social significance
  → Religion privatized: personal choice, not public necessity

RATIONALIZATION (Weber):
  Science + technology explain natural events (thunder, disease, harvest)
  → "Disenchantment of the world" (Entzauberung)
  → Supernatural explanations recede as scientific ones advance

URBANIZATION + INDIVIDUALIZATION:
  Rural community → face-to-face; church at center of social life
  Urban → anonymous; church optional; multiple plausibility structures available
  → Pluralism (exposure to different religions) → undermines certainty of each

EVIDENCE FOR:
  Western Europe: precipitous decline in church attendance (UK, Germany, France)
  UK: weekly church attendance fell from ~40% (1950) to ~5% (2020)
  Netherlands: from majority Christian → majority non-religious in 1 generation
  Scandinavian exceptionalism: highest secularization; also highest wellbeing
```

### Critics and Complications

```
AMERICAN EXCEPTIONALISM:
  US: highly modern + highly religious (anomaly for classic thesis)
  Church attendance: ~25–30% weekly attendance (despite declining slowly)
  Stark + Finke: "supply-side model" explains this
    → Established church (state religion) → monopoly → complacency → decline
    → Free market of religions (US: no establishment) → competition → innovation
    → Religious "firms" compete for adherents → more vibrant religious market

RATIONAL CHOICE MODEL OF RELIGION (Stark, Finke, Iannaccone):
  Religion provides: certainty, community, meaning, afterlife insurance
  People "shop" for religion like any product
  → More competition → higher quality supply → higher consumption
  → Strict churches have more committed members (costly commitment = signal of seriousness)
  → Sects outcompete mainline denominations: strict requirements = high commitment

CRITICISM OF SUPPLY-SIDE:
  Bruce: American "exceptionalism" is overstated; religiosity is declining in US too
  Hadaway et al.: self-reported church attendance ~2× actual (Americans over-report)
    → US actual attendance: ~~15%, not 40% → less exceptional
  Supply-side doesn't explain: why supply of churches doesn't increase attendance
    everywhere it increases (plenty of churches in secular Europe)

GLOBAL SOUTH GROWTH:
  Africa, Latin America, Asia: Christianity growing rapidly
  Sub-Saharan Africa: most secular estimate 14% + Pentecostalism explosive growth
  China: ~100 million Christians (unofficial) despite state atheism
  Islam: projected to match Christianity in total adherents by 2070 (Pew)
  → Global pattern: secularization is a Western European (+ some East Asian) phenomenon
  → World is becoming more, not less, religious in absolute terms

NORRIS AND INGLEHART (Sacred and Secular, 2004):
  Existential security hypothesis:
  → Societies with HIGH existential security (low poverty, good health, safety)
    → less need for religion (insurance against suffering)
  → Societies with LOW existential security (inequality, precarity, violence)
    → high need for religion
  → US: high inequality/insecurity for a wealthy nation → explains persistence
  → Fits: most secular = Scandinavia (highest security); most religious = sub-Saharan Africa
  → Better explanatory frame than supply-side for global variation
```

## Fundamentalism as Modernity Response

```
FUNDAMENTALISM AS MODERNITY'S CHILD:

  NOT a survival from pre-modern tradition
  IS a modern reaction against modernity
  → Fundamentalism arises as a RESPONSE to modernization pressure
  → "Fundamentalism" coined 1910: American Protestant movement against higher criticism
    + Darwinism + liberal theology; in reaction to modernity, not preceding it

COMMON FEATURES ACROSS TRADITIONS:
  Almond, Appleby, Sivan (Strong Religion, 2003):
  1. Reactive: emerges in reaction to perceived threats from modernity
  2. Selective: selects elements from tradition for emphasis (≠ pure traditionalism)
  3. Absolutism: claims sole possession of truth; condemns compromise
  4. Inerrancy: some privileged text is literally true
  5. Manichaean: sharp we/them; sacred/profane; good/evil division
  6. Chosenness: in-group as special, elect, or divinely favored

  Examples:
    US Protestant fundamentalism (1910–) — anti-Darwinism, Bible inerrancy
    Islamic fundamentalism (Wahhabi 1740s; Muslim Brotherhood 1928; Iranian revolution 1979)
    Jewish ultra-Orthodoxy (Haredi: reaction to Haskalah — Jewish Enlightenment)
    Hindu nationalism (RSS, BJP — reaction to colonialism + partition)
    → All share: "we are being threatened by modernizing forces; we must defend the sacred tradition"

MODERNIZATION THREAT MECHANISMS:
  Higher criticism: academic analysis of Bible/Quran as historical documents → undermines inerrancy
  Evolution: challenges special creation → human dignity narrative
  Women's rights: challenges patriarchal gender theology
  Pluralism: exposure to other faiths → undermines "only true religion" claim
  State formation: secular states claim authority formerly held by religious institutions

GLOBALIZATION PARADOX:
  Globalization → more contact, more pluralism → should produce secularization
  Instead often produces: religious intensification as reaction to perceived cultural threat
  → "Globalization produces both McDonaldization and Jihad" (Barber)
  → Religious identity used as resistance to cultural homogenization
```

## Civil Religion

```
ROBERT BELLAH (Civil Religion in America, 1967):

ARGUMENT:
  Even in formally secular states, a "civil religion" emerges —
  a set of sacred symbols, rituals, and beliefs about the nation's transcendent meaning

AMERICAN CIVIL RELIGION:
  Not Christianity; not any denomination; but shares religious structure:
    Sacred: founding documents (Declaration, Constitution), Founding Fathers
    Rituals: July 4th, Thanksgiving, presidential inauguration
    Shrines: Washington DC monuments, battlefields
    Martyrs: Lincoln, Kennedy → sacrificial deaths for national redemption
    Transcendent mission: America as "city on a hill"; providential destiny
    Holy texts: founding documents read as scripture (originalism as hermeneutics)

  SACRED LANGUAGE IN PUBLIC DISCOURSE:
    "God Bless America"; "one nation under God" (added 1954)
    Presidential oath on Bible; inaugural references to divine guidance
    → Not denominational Christianity; but national transcendence

FUNCTIONS:
  Integrates diverse population around shared sacred narrative
  Provides moral framework for collective action (Vietnam: civil religion used both
    by protesters AND supporters — different interpretations of same sacred symbols)
  → Lincoln's Second Inaugural: explicit civil religious language about national sin/redemption

  "BROKEN" CIVIL RELIGION:
  Vietnam, Watergate → crisis of legitimacy → sacred symbols contested
  → Civil religion requires moral credibility to function as integrating force
  → Culture wars: competing civil religions (Christian nationalism vs secular progressive)

CRITIQUE:
  Bellah overstated consensus: American civil religion was always contested
  Power dimension: whose sacred symbols? Whose founding narrative?
  → Whose ancestors were enslaved; whose were not counted in founding
  → Civil religion can be ideology: legitimates existing power structure
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Durkheim's theory doesn't require God to be real**
Durkheim is analyzing the social functions of religion, not evaluating its truth claims. His argument works whether or not there is a god. If religion's function is to represent and reinforce collective solidarity, then it performs that function regardless of supernatural reality. This is why Durkheim's approach is often described as "reductive" by believers — and correctly so: it explains religion without religion.

**Weber's Protestant Ethic is not a claim that capitalism is good**
Weber is describing a causal historical process, not endorsing it. The "iron cage" metaphor is explicitly pessimistic: capitalism has outlasted its religious justification and now operates as a self-sustaining system of rational calculation with no spiritual foundation. The work ethic that was once a calling is now obligation. Weber sees this as a kind of spiritual impoverishment.

**Secularization thesis and resurgence of religion are both empirically supported**
They're not contradictory: they apply to different populations. Western Europe is secularizing. The US is secularizing slowly. Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and South/Southeast Asia are becoming more religious. The world average (in absolute terms) is moving toward more religiosity because the fastest-growing populations are in high-religiosity regions. The secularization thesis describes a pattern of modernization; it's not a universal law.

**American exceptionalism in religion is partially a measurement artifact**
Survey-reported church attendance in the US is approximately double actual counted attendance. This has been documented since the 1990s (Hadaway, Marler, Chaves). Americans systematically over-report religious practice. When corrected, the US looks less like an outlier — still more religious than Western Europe, but not dramatically so. The supply-side explanation partially survives, but the anomaly it was designed to explain is smaller than initially assumed.

**Civil religion is neither propaganda nor sincere religion — it's both**
Bellah's concept captures a genuine sociological phenomenon: the ritualization of national identity with sacred symbolism. It functions as real religion does — integrating, motivating, providing transcendent meaning. That it can also be cynically deployed as ideology (my side = God's side) doesn't make it not-religion; religion throughout history has served both functions simultaneously.
