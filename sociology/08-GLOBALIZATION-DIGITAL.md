# Globalization and the Digital

## The Big Picture

Globalization is the **compression of time and space** through intensification of cross-border flows of capital, goods, information, people, and culture. The digital revolution has accelerated every dimension of globalization while creating new asymmetries — a "network society" (Castells) in which power flows through informational networks, and a "surveillance capitalism" (Zuboff) in which human behavioral data becomes the raw material of a new economic logic.

```
GLOBALIZATION — DIMENSIONS AND DEBATES:

  ECONOMIC:
    Trade liberalization (WTO, NAFTA/USMCA, EU single market)
    Global finance: currency flows, derivatives, sovereign debt
    Production chains: global value chains (GVC) → Apple designed in US;
                       components from 40 countries; assembled in China
    Labor arbitrage: production migrates to low-wage countries

  CULTURAL:
    Media flows: Hollywood, K-pop, telenovelas → unequal circulation
    "Cultural imperialism" thesis (Schiller): American cultural dominance
    Counter: "Glocalization" (Robertson): global products adapted locally
             (McDonalds menu in India ≠ McDonalds in US)
    Cultural hybridity (Bhabha, Appadurai): new forms from mixture

  POLITICAL:
    Nation-state power: weakened by global capital mobility?
    Trans-national governance: WTO, IMF, IPCC, ICC
    Nationalism resurgence as reaction to globalization

  DIGITAL:
    Network society (Castells): flows of information restructure power
    Platform economies: Facebook, Amazon, Alibaba — transnational private power
    Surveillance capitalism (Zuboff): behavioral data as commodity
    Digital divides: differential access by country, class, gender

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS:
  World-systems theory (Wallerstein): structural economic hierarchy
  Global cities (Sassen): spatial command centers of global capital
  Migration sociology: transnationalism; diaspora; push-pull
  Network society (Castells): space of flows vs. space of places
```

## World-Systems Theory

```
WALLERSTEIN (The Modern World-System, 4 vols., 1974–2011):

THEORETICAL ROOTS:
  Dependency theory (Prebisch, Frank): underdevelopment of LDCs is not lag
    but structural product of their incorporation into global capitalism
  Annales school (Braudel): long historical time spans; civilization analysis

CORE ARGUMENT:
  The modern world-system (emerged 16th century) is a SINGLE economic system
  Organized by a hierarchical division of labor:
    → Not just between states but between zones of production

THREE ZONES:
  CORE:
    Processes high value-added production + financial services
    Capital-intensive, high technology, high wages
    Strong states: can enforce advantageous exchange terms
    Historical: Western Europe → US → now contested
    Extract surplus from periphery through unequal exchange

  PERIPHERY:
    Extracts raw materials, labor-intensive manufacturing
    Low wages, weak states
    "Unequal exchange": labor and resources undervalued globally
    Historical: colonies → now: Global South in some sectors

  SEMI-PERIPHERY:
    In between: some core functions, some peripheral
    Politically: buffer zone; neither core nor periphery aligned
    Examples: Brazil, India, China (partially), Mexico, Turkey
    China's rise: semi-periphery → core in some sectors, still peripheral in others

  UPWARD/DOWNWARD MOBILITY:
    States can move between zones but system structure persists
    Japan: periphery → core (20th century)
    Spain, Portugal: core → semi-periphery decline
    → Structure is the point: individual mobility ≠ system change

UNEQUAL EXCHANGE (Emmanuel):
  Trade between high-wage and low-wage countries:
  Low-wage goods: embody more labor hours per unit price
  High-wage goods: embody fewer labor hours per unit price
  → Trade transfers value from periphery to core even in "equal" exchange
  → This is structural, not about exploitation in individual transactions

CRITIQUE:
  Unit of analysis: world-system or states? (Debate with mainstream IR)
  Agency: too structural; insufficient room for political contestation
  China: rise challenges simple core/periphery narrative
  Services: financial and informational services don't fit commodity exchange model
  → But: as descriptive framework for global inequality, remarkably durable
```

## Global Cities

```
SASKIA SASSEN (The Global City, 1991; updated 2001):

PREMISE:
  As production dispersed globally → management and coordination of production
  became more complex → required specialized service clusters → agglomerate in
  a small number of "global cities"

COMMAND AND CONTROL FUNCTIONS:
  Global cities specialize in: finance, legal services, accounting, consulting,
    advertising, media, design → the "producer services" that coordinate global production
  Not just financial centers: centers of specialized expertise + networks
  Primary: New York, London, Tokyo (original trinity)
  Secondary: Frankfurt, Singapore, Hong Kong, Paris, Sydney, Dubai

GLOBAL CITY FEATURES:
  1. Concentration of HQs of global corporations
  2. Global financial centers (stock exchanges, major banks, derivatives markets)
  3. Specialized producer services cluster (law firms, consulting, design)
  4. Point of entry for global immigration → dual labor market:
     → High-skill financial workers + low-skill service workers (restaurant, cleaning)
  5. Infrastructure: airports, fiber optic, financial infrastructure
  6. Disconnected from national hinterland: New York more connected to London
     than to rural Ohio in terms of capital flows

DUAL LABOR MARKET:
  Finance and professional services: very high wages → demand for services
  Service workers (cleaning, food, childcare): low wages; informal often
  → Global city creates extreme within-city inequality:
    gentrification; displacement of working class; rising costs of living
  → Immigration: fills bottom of labor market; global elite fills top
  → Paradox: most globalized cities are most unequal cities

ALPHA / BETA / GAMMA CLASSIFICATION (GaWC — Globalization and World Cities Network):
  Alpha++: London, New York (most integrated)
  Alpha+: Singapore, Hong Kong, Paris, Tokyo, Dubai
  Alpha: Chicago, LA, Beijing, Shanghai, Sydney, Zurich
  → Measured by: interlocking firm connections (how many global firm offices in each city)
```

## Migration Sociology

```
PUSH-PULL THEORY (Lee 1966 — neoclassical):
  Migration driven by:
    PUSH factors: poverty, violence, discrimination, environmental degradation at origin
    PULL factors: economic opportunity, safety, services at destination
    Intervening obstacles: distance, legal barriers, social networks
  Limitation: treats migrants as isolated rational actors; ignores structure + network

TRANSNATIONALISM (Glick Schiller, Basch, Szanton-Blanc — 1990s):
  Traditional immigration studies: assumed migrants assimilate and break with origin
  Transnationalism: migrants maintain simultaneous social relationships across borders
    → Live "transnationally": work in US; send remittances to Mexico;
      participate in Mexican politics; children raised in both cultures
    → Homeland politics: diaspora vote on home country politics; fund parties
    → "Dual frame of reference": compare host country to homeland
  REMITTANCES: annual flows > official development aid globally
    Mexico: ~$60B/yr from US migrants (2022)
    El Salvador: remittances = 26% of GDP
    → Remittances are major macro-economic force; more stable than FDI

DIASPORA:
  Dispersed population maintaining cultural identity and homeland connection
  Beyond transnationalism: may lack living memory of homeland but maintain identity
  Armenian, Jewish, Irish, Chinese diasporas: centuries of diaspora identity
  Functions: cultural preservation, political lobbying, economic network, investment
  "Imagined homeland": Safran → diaspora maintains myth of return even without real return

MIGRATION SYSTEMS THEORY (Massey, Arango, Taylor — 1993):
  Networks: once migration starts → networks reduce cost and risk of subsequent migration
    → Chain migration: existing migrants help new migrants → path dependence
    → Network effects: migration streams become self-sustaining independent of original cause
  Cumulative causation: migration changes origin communities → more likely future migration
    (cultural change; capital absence; social network abroad)
  State and policy: control attempts → often redirect rather than reduce flows

REFUGEE / FORCED MIGRATION:
  2023: 110+ million forcibly displaced globally (UNHCR record)
  Refugee definition (1951 Refugee Convention): outside home country; fear of persecution
    on grounds of race, religion, nationality, political opinion, social group membership
  IDPs (Internally Displaced Persons): largest category; inside home country → different
    legal framework (not protected by 1951 Convention)
  Climate displacement: not currently covered by 1951 definition → policy gap
```

## Network Society (Castells)

```
MANUEL CASTELLS (The Information Age: Economy, Society and Culture, 3 vols., 1996–1998):

INFORMATION CAPITALISM:
  New economic paradigm: informationalism (like industrialism, but based on knowledge)
  PRODUCTIVITY: now primarily driven by knowledge generation and information processing
    → Competitive advantage = capacity to innovate (informational capability)
    → Not cheap labor or raw materials (old industrial advantage)

NETWORKS AS DOMINANT SOCIAL FORM:
  Information technology → networks can be flexible, scalable, and open-ended
  Networks outcompete hierarchies in processing information and adapting to change
  → All major institutions restructuring as networks:
    Corporations: flat; outsourced; networked; just-in-time
    States: governance networks; multi-level (EU, UN, national, regional)
    Social movements: networked (Occupy, Arab Spring) — leaderless, horizontal

SPACE OF FLOWS vs SPACE OF PLACES:
  SPACE OF FLOWS: electronic networks; capital markets; information highways
    → Flows of capital, information, technology, organizational interaction
    → Timeless time: 24-hour markets; simultaneous global events
    → Connected to all places that matter (other nodes) but to no specific place

  SPACE OF PLACES: locally-bounded experience; specific geography
    → Most people live here: their neighborhood, city, body
    → Increasingly disconnected from the space of flows that shapes their life

  DOMINANT LOGIC: space of flows shapes and restructures space of places
    → Finance capital flows: Detroit deindustrializes; Seoul industrializes
    → Cities compete to become nodes in space of flows (tax breaks for HQs)
    → But: places matter for reproduction, politics, culture

THE FOURTH WORLD:
  Not South; not North; but socially excluded from informational networks
  Pockets everywhere: Black inner-city US, rural Africa, declining rustbelt Europe
  → Excluded from flows of capital and information
  → Asymmetric globalization: some places hyperconnected; others bypassed

POWER IN THE NETWORK SOCIETY:
  Power = ability to control network architecture + what flows through it
  Programmers (who set the rules) > switchers (who connect networks)
  → 21st c. network power: platform companies (Google, Facebook, Amazon)
    control the architecture of major information networks
  → Governments: can threaten exclusion from national market; but struggle with network power
```

## Surveillance Capitalism

```
SHOSHANA ZUBOFF (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism, 2019):

BEHAVIORAL SURPLUS:
  Search companies (Google 2001 crisis) discovered:
    → Data generated by user behavior is MORE than needed to improve search
    → Behavioral surplus: residual behavioral data → can be sold to advertisers
    → Key insight: users provide data; data is used to MODIFY user behavior
    → Not just "personalization" — behavior prediction and modification as product

THE LOGIC OF SURVEILLANCE CAPITALISM:
  1. EXTRACT: harvest behavioral data (searches, clicks, location, purchases, conversations)
     at scale across all activities
  2. PROCESS: analyze with ML → create behavioral prediction products
     "Behavioral futures market": contracts on user future behavior
  3. SELL: sell predictions of behavior to advertisers, insurers, employers
     Advertisers want: guarantee of attention + influence on purchase
  4. ACCUMULATE: more data → better models → more accurate predictions → more valuable

  KEY CLAIM: the commodity is not the attention (Smythe's audience commodity) —
    it is the PREDICTION of future behavior (even attention)

UBIQUITOUS SURVEILLANCE:
  "Surveillance capitalism requires more data" → expand surveillance
    → Move beyond online behavior into real-world:
      Pokémon Go (Niantic — spun out from Google's internal lab, later independent):
        gathered large-scale outdoor spatial mapping data via AR gameplay
      Google Street View: harvested WiFi data while photographing
      Smart home devices (Echo, Nest, Ring): in-home surveillance
      Fitness trackers: physiological data → health/insurance predictions
      Smartphone: always-on location, communication, purchase, movement
  "The prediction imperative" → every behavior potentially predictable → all data relevant

BEHAVIORAL MODIFICATION (Instrumentarian Power):
  Beyond prediction: the product improves if behavior can be nudged toward prediction
  → Offers, prompts, notifications, environmental cues designed to herd behavior
  → Facebook: "emotional contagion" study (2014) — manipulated news feed
    → Users shown more negative content → posted more negative content
    → Emotional states of 700,000 people manipulated without consent
  → "Economies of action": platforms get more valuable data when they change behavior
  → COMPARISON TO BEHAVIORISM: Skinner's operant conditioning industrialized at scale

CRITIQUE OF ZUBOFF:
  Historical uniqueness: is surveillance capitalism novel or continuous with capitalism?
    → Advertising has always targeted behavior modification; data makes it more precise
  Agency: users do have choices (limited); Zuboff somewhat paternalistic about user understanding
  Non-US context: Alibaba, Tencent use same logic; not uniquely American
  Regulation: GDPR (EU) provides framework Zuboff endorses; question is enforcement
  Alternatives: public data commons? Government oversight? Platform cooperatives?
```

## Platform Power and Algorithmic Governance

```
PLATFORM ECONOMICS:

  PLATFORM AS INFRASTRUCTURE:
    Platform: technological infrastructure that enables interactions between groups
    → Amazon: buyers + sellers; Uber: drivers + riders; Airbnb: hosts + guests
    → Two-sided (or multi-sided) markets: platform intermediates between groups
    → Network effects: platform value increases with participants → winner-take-all

  MARKET CONCENTRATION:
    Digital markets tend toward monopoly/oligopoly:
    → Zero marginal cost of replication → first-mover scale advantage
    → Data advantage: more data → better algorithm → better service → more users
    → Platform lock-in: switching costs (social graph, data, habits)
    → GAFAM (Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft): combined market cap > many GDPs

  DATA AS COMPETITIVE MOAT:
    Alphabet: Google Search + Maps + YouTube + Android = comprehensive behavioral profile
    Facebook/Meta: social graph + WhatsApp + Instagram = relationship + communication data
    Amazon: purchase history + Alexa + AWS (infrastructure) = commerce + personal
    → Not replicable by competitors: data accumulation is path-dependent; years of behavior

ALGORITHMIC GOVERNANCE:

  CONTENT MODERATION:
    Platforms decide what speech is allowed/amplified/removed at scale
    → Effectively: govern the global public sphere
    → Section 230 (US): platforms not liable for user content → enables moderation + scale
    → EU DSA (Digital Services Act): transparency, risk assessment, systemic oversight
    → China: comprehensive censorship + positive propaganda

  ALGORITHMIC AMPLIFICATION:
    Content recommendation: engagement-optimized algorithms
    → Maximizing engagement → extremifying content (outrage, fear drive engagement)
    → "Rabbit holes": recommendation leads toward more extreme content
    → Empirical evidence: mixed (Huszár et al. 2022 Twitter political bias study)
    → Algorithmic discrimination: systems trained on historical data → replicate historical bias
      COMPAS recidivism: Black defendants scored higher risk → disparate impact
      Resume screening: word2vec trained on historical text → reproduces gender bias

  PLATFORM LABOR:
    Gig economy (Uber, DoorDash, Amazon Flex): algorithmic management
    → Workers managed by algorithm: task assignment, performance monitoring, deactivation
    → No employment relationship → no benefits, no organizing rights
    → Labor sociology: Kessler's "Gigged"; van Doorn "platform labor"
    → Classification fights: California AB5; EU directive → platforms pushed back hard

  REGULATORY RESPONSES:
    GDPR (EU, 2018): data protection rights; consent; portability; right to erasure
    DSA/DMA (EU, 2022–23): platform transparency; interoperability (DMA);
      systemic risk assessment; ban on targeted advertising to children (DSA)
    Antitrust: US DOJ vs Google (August 2024 ruling: found Google violated antitrust law
      by maintaining monopoly in general search through exclusive default agreements;
      remedy phase ongoing as of 2025 — structural remedies including potential Chrome
      or Android divestiture under consideration; outcome not yet determined)
    China: data sovereignty; DiDi banned; Alibaba fined $2.8B (2021)
```

## From Internet Architecture to Sociological Theory

This module is a sociological description of the systems you have built and operated. The frameworks name and formalize patterns that engineers encounter as technical or business problems but which have structural social-scientific explanations.

**Castells' "space of flows" is the sociological description of internet architecture.** The infrastructure you work with — BGP routing, CDN edge nodes, cloud availability zones, API gateways — is the physical instantiation of Castells' claim: power concentrates in the nodes that control what flows through networks, not in any particular place. The "space of places" (geographically rooted experience of workers, communities, regulatory jurisdictions) is continuously subordinated to the "space of flows" (capital markets, data centers, platform routing decisions). When Azure decides to close a region, or AWS shifts pricing, or a platform changes its ranking algorithm, places experience consequences from flows they did not control and cannot easily influence. This is not metaphor — Castells was describing, in 1996, the social architecture that hyperscale cloud and platform companies later constructed.

**Zuboff's surveillance capitalism is a theoretical account of the ad-tech stack.** The behavioral surplus argument — that data collected to improve a service generates more than is needed for that purpose, and the excess is the raw material of a new commodity (behavioral prediction) — is a description of what Google discovered in 2001 when it found that behavioral targeting made ads far more valuable than contextual targeting. The "behavioral futures market" is programmatic advertising: RTB auctions where the commodity being sold is a prediction of a specific user's probability of taking a specified action. The "prediction imperative" (more behavioral data → better models → more valuable predictions → expand data collection) is the structural logic that drove the expansion of Google into Android, Maps, Nest, and Fitbit, and Facebook into WhatsApp and Instagram. Engineers who built these systems participated in constructing Zuboff's theoretical object; reading her account gives the structural logic that individual engineering decisions served.

**Platform network effects, data moats, and switching costs are the engineering-strategy face of world-systems dynamics.** Wallerstein's core/periphery structure in the digital economy is platforms (core) versus the businesses and individuals that depend on them (periphery). The "unequal exchange" operates through platform fees, API pricing, data asymmetries, and algorithmic ranking. Sellers on Amazon, developers on the App Store, advertisers on Google — they gain access to the platform's scale while the platform extracts information rents and shapes their business terms. The structural inimitability of these positions is exactly the VRIN analysis of the resource-based view: the data accumulated over time (path dependency), the ambiguity about which features matter (causal ambiguity), and the social complexity of the developer/user ecosystem are the sources of sustained competitive advantage.

**Algorithmic governance is the sociological problem of code as law.** When a platform's recommendation algorithm determines which speech is amplified, which businesses are visible, and which workers receive rides, the platform is performing governance functions that were previously distributed across democratic institutions, markets, and civil society. The Section 230 framework (platforms not liable for content) was designed for a world in which platforms were neutral conduits; the surveillance capitalism model in which platforms actively curate, rank, and nudge means the neutral-conduit premise is false. Regulatory frameworks (GDPR, DSA) are attempts to re-impose accountability norms on private governance systems — which is the political-opportunity-structure opening that privacy-by-design and federated architectures are positioned to exploit.

## Common Confusion Points

**World-systems theory is about structure, not agency**
Wallerstein argues that individual states or firms making rational choices produces and reproduces the core/periphery structure — it doesn't require anyone to intend it. China's manufacturing growth moved it from periphery toward semi-periphery, but it didn't change the global structure of unequal exchange. The theory's critics note it leaves little room for understanding how states successfully "move up," but its defenders say the structure is the point.

**Sassen's "global city" is not the same as "large city" or "world city"**
A global city is defined by its function in global capital flows, not its size. Los Angeles is larger than Zürich but Zürich is arguably more of a global financial city by Sassen's criteria. The category is about the density of producer service functions and financial operations, not population. Many very large cities (Dhaka, Lagos, Kinshasa) are not global cities in Sassen's sense.

**Surveillance capitalism is not just "they know a lot about you"**
Zuboff's argument is not about privacy violation (though that is a component). The core claim is about economic logic: the commodity being traded is not your data but a prediction of your future behavior, and the accumulation dynamic drives expansion of surveillance into all areas of life. The unique feature is that behavior modification becomes the means to improve the prediction product — making you more predictable is what creates value, not just knowing what you've done.

**Network effects favor consolidation but not always monopoly**
Strong network effects (Facebook: your value = connections) can produce monopoly. But multi-homing is possible (TikTok and Instagram coexist; Uber and Lyft coexist) when switching is cheap. The question is whether the data moat or social graph creates genuine lock-in. Amazon and Google have more durable advantages from data accumulation than Uber does from ride-hailing data, because the data types differ in stickiness.

**Transnationalism describes lived reality, not an immigrant failure to assimilate**
Early 20th century assimilationist models assumed and expected immigrants to abandon homeland connections. Transnationalism describes empirically that contemporary migrants maintain meaningful dual connections — not as a pathology but as a rational, technology-enabled adaptation. The question of "assimilation vs. transnationalism" is somewhat false: migrants can be highly integrated into host societies while maintaining transnational connections.
