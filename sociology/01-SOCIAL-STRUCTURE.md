# Social Structure

## The Big Picture

Social structure is the **patterned organization of social relationships and institutions** that exists independently of particular individuals. Structures persist when individuals come and go because they are institutionalized — built into roles, norms, physical environments, and organizational routines.

```
LEVELS OF SOCIAL STRUCTURE

MACRO: Societies / Global systems
  ├── Nation-state institutions: legal system, military, bureaucracy
  ├── Cultural systems: shared language, values, religion, media
  └── Stratification systems: class, race, gender hierarchies

MESO: Organizations / Communities / Fields
  ├── Formal organizations: corporations, universities, hospitals
  ├── Informal organizations: social movements, networks, clubs
  └── Communities: neighborhoods, occupational communities

MICRO: Interaction / Situated practice
  ├── Face-to-face encounters and their ordering
  ├── Roles and scripts in recurring situations
  └── Identity management in specific contexts

KEY INSIGHT: Each level constrains and enables the levels below it.
  Your organization's structure constrains how you interact with colleagues.
  Society's stratification system shapes what organizations you can access.
  Yet individuals also reproduce and occasionally transform these structures.
```

## Status and Role

### Status: Social Positions

```
STATUS = recognized social position with associated rights and obligations

Ascribed status: conferred at birth, without individual choice
  Age, sex assigned at birth, race/ethnicity (in most societies), birth order,
  national origin, family caste position
  → Primary basis of traditional/pre-modern stratification

Achieved status: acquired through individual effort or market processes
  Education credentials, occupation, income, marital status, awards
  → Dominant rhetoric of meritocratic modern societies
  → CRITICAL: achievement is never fully de-linked from ascription
               parental status shapes educational opportunity
               race/gender affect career returns to same credentials

Master status: one status that dominates all others in how others perceive you
  Convicted felon, racial identity in racially stratified society,
  celebrity, disability
  → Goffman's "spoiled identity" — master status overrides other statuses

Status set: the full collection of statuses an individual occupies simultaneously
  One person might be: parent, employee, neighbor, citizen, veteran, patient
```

### Role: Behavioral Expectations

```
ROLE = set of behavioral expectations associated with a status

Role expectations are:
  • Shared: others know what to expect from you in a role
  • Normative: violation produces social sanctions (disapproval, exclusion, legal penalty)
  • Differentiated: vary by relationship (doctor-patient vs doctor-colleague)

ROLE CONFLICT: incompatible expectations from TWO DIFFERENT ROLES held simultaneously
  Example: doctor who is also patient's friend
           parent who is also employee during school crisis
  → Resolution strategies: compartmentalization, hierarchy (prioritize one), role exit

ROLE STRAIN: incompatible demands from within a SINGLE ROLE
  Example: manager expected to be both tough disciplinarian and supportive mentor
           teacher expected to maintain standards and ensure all students succeed
  → Resolution: Merton's "insulation" — keep role obligations somewhat ambiguous

ROLE EXIT (Helen Rose Ebaugh): process of disengagement from a role central to identity
  Stages: first doubts → seeking alternatives → turning point → creating ex-role identity
  Examples: leaving religious orders, divorce, retirement, recovery from addiction
  → Residual identity: "ex-" always partially defines new role
```

## Goffman's Dramaturgical Model

Erving Goffman's core insight: **social life is theatrical performance**. We are not playing ourselves — we are performing versions of ourselves appropriate to the audience and setting.

```
DRAMATURGICAL ELEMENTS:

PERFORMANCE: any activity that influences observers

FRONT STAGE: settings where impression management is active
  Personal front: appearance (costume, props) + manner (behavior)
  Setting: the physical environment that serves as stage backdrop

BACK STAGE: areas where performers relax from front-stage performance
  Relaxation of role performance
  Rehearsal for front stage
  Contradiction of front-stage impression allowed
  Examples: kitchen vs dining room; employee break room vs customer floor;
            text message to friend vs public social media post

IMPRESSION MANAGEMENT: active work to project a desired self-image
  Idealization: present best version; conceal contradictions
  Mystification: maintain social distance to prevent scrutiny
  Protective practices: team members cover each other's mistakes

FACE-WORK (interaction ritual):
  Face = positive social value claimed in an encounter
  Maintaining face = basic social obligation
  Face threat → embarrassment (social emotion that enforces norms)
  Saving face = remedial work after potential face loss

INTERACTION RITUAL CHAINS (Randall Collins, extending Goffman):
  Rituals generate emotional energy (EE) and cultural symbols
  High EE = focus, enthusiasm, confidence → motivates action
  People are drawn to interactions that generate more EE
  → Explains variation in motivation and social participation
```

### Stigma

Goffman (1963) on stigma — deeply discrediting attributes that reduce someone from a whole person to a tainted, discounted one.

```
TYPES OF STIGMA:

1. Abominations of the body: physical disabilities, visible differences
2. Blemishes of individual character: mental illness, imprisonment, addiction,
                                      unemployment, political radicalism
3. Tribal stigma: race, ethnicity, nationality, religion (transmitted through lineage)

MANAGEMENT:

Discredited: stigma is visible, known to others already
  → manage tension in interaction: control information about self, redirect attention

Discreditable: stigma not yet known to current audience
  → manage information: passing (concealing), covering, selective disclosure

Passing strategies:
  → Omit information; redirect; avoid settings where stigma would be exposed

Covering: stigma known but person works to reduce obtrusiveness
  → minimizing: not drawing attention to disability
  → does not involve deception, just impression management

In-group vs out-group dynamics:
  Stigmatized people often form identity communities (disability pride,
  mental health advocacy) that reframe stigma as difference, not deficiency
```

## Bourdieu's Theory of Social Fields and Capital

Pierre Bourdieu provides the most sophisticated framework for understanding how social inequality is reproduced through structure and practice.

### Capital: Multiple Forms of Social Resource

```
ECONOMIC CAPITAL
  Money, property, financial assets; convertible to other forms
  Direct exchange medium

SOCIAL CAPITAL
  Networks of relationships; connections that can be mobilized
  "It's not what you know, it's who you know"
  Access to resources through relationships (jobs, information, favors)
  Coleman: social capital as trust and norms enabling collective action
  Putnam: bonding (within group) vs bridging (across groups) social capital

CULTURAL CAPITAL
  Three states:
  1. Embodied: dispositions of mind and body — taste, accent, manners, bearing
              acquired through socialization; cannot be bought quickly
  2. Objectified: cultural goods — books, art, instruments, wine collection
  3. Institutionalized: academic credentials, degrees, certifications
                        formal recognition of embodied cultural capital

  Key: dominant cultural capital is defined by the class that has it
       Schools valorize middle-class cultural capital → middle-class kids
       have "natural" advantage that appears to be merit

SYMBOLIC CAPITAL
  Legitimized form of other capitals: prestige, honor, reputation, authority
  The form capital takes when its arbitrary social basis is not recognized
  → When difference in accumulated capital appears as natural distinction

CONVERSION BETWEEN CAPITALS:
  Economic → cultural: pay for private education, tutors, instruments
  Cultural → economic: degrees → credentials → higher income
  Social → economic: network → job referral → career advancement
  All conversions involve friction, transaction costs, time
```

### Habitus: Internalized Social Structure

```
HABITUS = system of durable, transposable dispositions
         generated by conditions of existence (class position)
         that structure perception, thought, and action

Not rules: habitus is not a rulebook you consciously consult
Not deliberate: most habituated practice is pre-reflective
Transposable: dispositions developed in one field tend to transfer to others

Origin: childhood experience in family position
  → embodied dispositions (taste in food, music, art, decor, body use)
  → linguistic practices (vocabulary, intonation, code-switching ability)
  → relationship to authority, time horizon, risk tolerance

"Feel for the game":
  Skilled social practice = habitus attuned to field structure
  Expert practitioners don't calculate strategy — they sense the right move
  (Same as how skilled athletes don't think through each movement)

Class habitus reproduces class:
  Working-class habitus: practical, immediate, present-oriented, suspicious of abstraction
  Middle-class habitus: abstract, future-oriented, comfortable with ambiguity
  Upper-class habitus: effortless distinction, comfort with high-cultural forms

  Each habitus positions its carrier in the educational field in ways that
  appear to be natural talent but reflect socialized disposition
```

### Field: Structured Arena of Competition

```
FIELD = structured space of positions and position-takings
        with its own logic (illusio), stakes, and capital

Properties of every field:
  1. Has specific capital valued within it (intellectual in academic field,
     sales in commercial field, physical in sports field)
  2. Has a nomos (its own fundamental law/logic)
  3. Relatively autonomous from other fields but never fully independent
  4. Site of struggle: agents compete to improve or defend position
  5. Has a history: current positions are products of past struggles

Examples of fields:
  Academic field: capital = intellectual recognition (publications, citations)
  Artistic field: capital = artistic legitimacy (peer recognition)
  Political field: capital = political power (votes, party position)
  Economic field: capital = profit and market share

Doxa: unquestioned assumptions within a field
  What goes without saying because it comes without saying
  → Naturalized arbitrary aspects of field that serve dominant positions
  → Critique: making doxa explicit is always politically charged

Symbolic violence: imposition of meaning that legitimizes power
  → Dominated groups often complicit in their own domination
  → Because the categories through which they perceive their position
    are the very categories imposed by the dominant group
```

## Social Capital: Networks and Trust

Distinct from Bourdieu's capital, network-focused approaches:

```
GRANOVETTER: STRENGTH OF WEAK TIES (1973)

  STRONG TIES (family, close friends):
    High emotional intensity, time investment, mutual confiding, reciprocity
    → Information redundant: your close friends know the same people you do
    → Job search through strong ties: often leads to similar positions

  WEAK TIES (acquaintances, professional contacts):
    Lower intensity, infrequent contact, may not know your other contacts
    → Information non-redundant: bridges to different social worlds
    → Job search: most job leads come through WEAK TIES
    → Structural holes (Burt): positions that bridge different clusters
       → strategic informational advantage; brokerage

  Counter-intuitive result: weak ties more useful for job mobility than strong ties

BURT: STRUCTURAL HOLES
  Positions that bridge separate networks are information brokers
  → Advantage accrues to those who span structural holes
  → Network closure (dense mutual connections) → trust but redundant info
  → Network brokerage → information advantage but less trust/norms enforcement

  For careers: brokerage networks advantage in early career (entrepreneurial)
               closure networks advantage for executing collective action
```

## From Engineering Organizations to Social Structure

The concepts in this module are not abstract — they describe the operating dynamics of any large engineering organization. Three direct mappings:

**Goffman's front-stage/back-stage is the architecture of professional performance.** An engineering VP presenting to the board versus running a staff meeting versus talking to a junior engineer in a hallway — these are different performances with different audiences, costumes (language register, technical depth), and stage sets. Impression management is not cynicism; it is the normal medium of organizational life. The dramaturgical insight is that all three performances are partially constructed, which is why the "authentic leader" literature is philosophically incoherent: there is no backstage self that precedes all staging.

**Role conflict is the structural condition of middle management.** A director simultaneously reporting to a VP and managing engineers faces role strain by design: the VP role requires enforcing resource constraints; the manager role requires advocating for the team. This is not a personal failure — it is the structural consequence of occupying a status at a hierarchical boundary. Merton's "insulation" strategy (keeping role obligations ambiguous) is exactly what middle managers do when they say "I'll take it up the chain."

**Bourdieu's field theory explains why technical credibility and organizational power diverge.** In a large engineering organization, the economic field (budget authority) and the technical field (engineering credibility) are relatively autonomous. A principal engineer may have high technical capital but limited organizational capital; a product manager may have high organizational capital but limited technical capital. Conversions between these are costly and friction-laden — which is why "getting engineers promoted into management" and "getting managers to earn engineering respect" are both hard problems, not just cultural ones.

**Granovetter's weak ties explain why external hiring beats internal promotion for certain roles.** Strong internal networks are information-redundant — everyone connected to a candidate already knows the same things about them. External candidates bring weak-tie information from different organizational contexts, which is more novel. The implication: if you want genuinely new capability in an organization, referral networks (strong ties) systematically underproduce it.

## Common Confusion Points

**Ascribed vs achieved is not a binary — it's a spectrum that varies by society and historical period**
Race is legally "achieved" in Brazil (skin color self-report for affirmative action) but biologically ascribed in DNA testing. Caste is ascribed in India but reformers argue it should be achievable. These categories are social constructs with real institutional embeddedness.

**Habitus is not destiny**
Bourdieu is sometimes read as deterministic — habitus simply reproduces position. But he explicitly allows for habitus transformation through: education and credential acquisition, social mobility (with associated habitus-field mismatch — the "fish out of water" feeling), and reflexive sociology (being able to analyze one's own habitus is the beginning of transcending it). The reproduction of class positions is powerful but not absolute.

**Social capital is not always positive for society**
Bonding social capital within tight communities produces trust and reciprocity for insiders but can enforce conformity, exclude outsiders, and concentrate resources. Organized crime syndicates have very high social capital. The question is always: capital for whom, to do what?

**Goffman is not saying social interaction is fake or cynical**
The dramaturgical metaphor is analytic, not evaluative. We are not being hypocritical by managing impressions — impression management is the normal, necessary medium of social interaction. We could not have stable interaction without shared expectations about how roles should be performed.

**Bourdieu's "fields" are not all equivalent in power**
Fields are relatively autonomous but not fully independent. The economic field tends to dominate others in capitalist societies — artistic fields feel the pressure of commercial logic; academic fields struggle with market relevance; political fields are shaped by economic power. The degree of autonomy varies historically and is itself a site of struggle.
