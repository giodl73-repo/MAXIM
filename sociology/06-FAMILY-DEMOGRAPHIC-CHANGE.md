# Family and Demographic Change

## The Big Picture

Family is the primary institution of reproduction, socialization, and care — but its form is neither universal nor static. Demography provides the quantitative framework: birth rates, death rates, marriage rates, and their long-run trends tell us about structural change in societies. Sociology asks why those numbers shift and what they mean for inequality, care, and the organization of daily life.

```
DEMOGRAPHIC TRANSITIONS — TWO WAVES:

  FIRST DEMOGRAPHIC TRANSITION (FDT) — 19th–20th century:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Stage 1: High mortality + high fertility → stable (pre-industrial)
  Stage 2: Mortality falls (sanitation, medicine) → population boom
  Stage 3: Fertility falls (education, urbanization) → stable again
  Stage 4: Low mortality + low fertility → below-replacement fertility

  SECOND DEMOGRAPHIC TRANSITION (SDT) — late 20th century–present:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Non-marriage, cohabitation (not leading to marriage)
  Delayed first marriage + delayed childbearing
  Fertility below replacement (TFR < 2.1) — "lowest low" fertility in S. Europe/Japan
  Rise of voluntary childlessness + lone parenthood
  LGBTQ+ family formation

  CAUSE (SDT — Van de Kaa / Lesthaeghe):
    Not poverty: happens in wealthy postindustrial societies
    → Value shift: self-actualization, autonomy, gender egalitarianism
    → Contraceptive revolution (1960s pill): decouples sex from reproduction
    → Women's labor force participation → opportunity cost of motherhood rises
    → "Individualization thesis": intimate life becomes a reflexive project

FAMILY FORMS SPECTRUM:
  Nuclear family    Two parents + children (biological or adopted)
  Extended family   Three+ generations; lateral kin sharing household
  Stem family       One child inherits; others move out (European peasant)
  Polyandry         One woman, multiple husbands (rare — Tibetan fraternal)
  Polygyny          One man, multiple wives (sub-Saharan Africa, parts of ME)
  Single parent     One adult (divorce/death/never-married)
  Cohabiting        Unmarried couple; with or without children
  Same-sex family   All above structures, same-sex partners
  Fictive kin       Non-biological social family networks (common in US Black communities)
```

## Family Forms Cross-Culturally

### Nuclear vs. Extended

```
MURDOCK'S CROSS-CULTURAL SURVEY (1949):
  Studied 250 societies → nuclear family universal (always recognizable)
  → BUT: embeddedness in larger structures varies enormously

NUCLEAR FAMILY (husband-wife-children):
  Idealized in 20th-century US as "normal" (breadwinner + housewife)
  Historical context: became prevalent with industrialization
    → Wage labor → adults move away from extended kin → nuclear household
    → Pre-industrial: nuclear household embedded in larger household economy
  "Standard North American Family" (Smith 1993) = ideological construct
    → Most families always deviated: boarders, servants, grandparents, kin labor

EXTENDED FAMILY:
  Predominant in agrarian societies, pastoralists, high-mortality contexts
  Advantages: labor pool (subsistence agriculture), risk pooling, elder care
  South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia: extended household norms persist
  China: filial piety (xiào) → obligation to live with/support aging parents
  South Asia: joint family system → brothers share property under one roof

HOUSEHOLD vs FAMILY:
  Household: people sharing a dwelling (co-residence)
  Family: people connected by kinship (may not co-reside)
  → These diverge increasingly in modern societies (nuclear family scattered)
  → Census counts households; family is sociological construct
```

### Marriage Systems

```
MARRIAGE SYSTEMS:

  MONOGAMY: one spouse (legal norm in all Western societies)
    Serial monogamy: successive marriages after divorce (statistically common in US)

  POLYGYNY: one husband, multiple wives
    Found in ~75% of known human societies (Murdock's survey)
    → But only a minority of men in those societies are polygynous
    (requires resources to support multiple wives)
    Banned or severely restricted in all Western legal systems
    Bride price (bridewealth): groom's family pays bride's family
    → Common in sub-Saharan Africa; compensates family for loss of labor + kin

  POLYANDRY: one wife, multiple husbands (rare — <1% of societies)
    Fraternal polyandry (Tibet): brothers share one wife
    → Prevents land fragmentation; maintains household as economic unit

  SAME-SEX MARRIAGE:
    Legally recognized in 35+ countries (2024)
    Cross-cultural: various forms of recognized same-sex partnership historically
    → Not a modern invention; institutionalization is new in legal form

KINSHIP SYSTEMS:
  Patrilineal: descent through father's line (most common)
  Matrilineal: descent through mother's line (~15% of societies)
  Bilateral: descent through both (US norm)
  Residence norms:
    Patrilocal: wife moves to husband's family (most common)
    Matrilocal: husband moves to wife's family
    Neolocal: couple establishes new household (Western norm)
```

## The Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

```
DTM — FOUR STAGES:

  Births/Deaths
  per 1,000
        │
    50 ─│  ████████████████           Stage 1: High stationary
        │  ████████████████  ──────── Birth rate
    30 ─│         ████████████████ ── Death rate
        │                 ████████
    10 ─│                         ████████
        │                                 ████────────────────
         ────────────────────────────────────────────────────► time
         Stage 1    Stage 2    Stage 3    Stage 4    Stage 5(?)

  STAGE 1 (Pre-industrial):
    High birth rate + high death rate → low growth
    Poor nutrition, disease, no medical care → life expectancy 35–40
    High infant/child mortality → compensated by high birth rate
    Still: some isolated populations + historical baseline

  STAGE 2 (Mortality transition):
    Death rate drops rapidly (sanitation, vaccines, medicine, better food)
    Birth rate stays high (cultural lag; births are still "rational" hedge)
    → POPULATION EXPLOSION
    Most of LDC development in 20th century: this stage
    England/Wales: 1740–1880; Sub-Saharan Africa: ongoing

  STAGE 3 (Fertility transition):
    Birth rate begins falling as:
    → Urbanization: children cost more; provide less labor value
    → Female education + labor force participation: opportunity cost rises
    → Access to contraception
    → Child mortality falling → fewer "replacement" births needed
    → Cultural shift: child quality vs. quantity tradeoff
    Population growth slows

  STAGE 4 (Low stationary):
    Both rates low → near-zero growth
    Most Western Europe, Japan, US (approaching): TFR ~1.5–2.1
    Life expectancy: 75–85+
    Aging population → dependency ratio reversal

  STAGE 5 (Proposed):
    TFR < 2.1 (below replacement) for sustained period
    Population declining without immigration
    Japan, S. Korea, Germany, Italy: in this stage
    → Structural aging crisis: pension systems, elder care, GDP growth

TOTAL FERTILITY RATE (TFR):
  Average number of children per woman (given current age-specific fertility)
  Replacement rate: 2.1 (accounts for female-male ratio + infant mortality)
  2024 examples:
    Niger: 6.8 | Sub-Saharan Africa avg: ~4.5
    India: 2.0 (just crossed replacement) | US: ~1.7
    Germany: 1.53 | South Korea: 0.78 (record low, 2023)
    Japan: 1.20 | China: 1.09 (officially, may be lower)
```

## Second Demographic Transition

```
SDT — RON LESTHAEGHE AND DIRK VAN DE KAA (1986):

Core argument: in postindustrial societies, family change is not due to
  economic hardship but to value shifts toward self-actualization and autonomy

SDT TRENDS (especially Northwestern Europe → spreading):
  1. Rise of cohabitation (not as prelude to marriage, but as alternative)
  2. Delay of first marriage → average age of first marriage rising
     US 2023: women 28.6; men 30.5 (vs 1960: women 20, men 23)
  3. Delay of first birth → women in 30s, not 20s
  4. TFR below replacement → voluntary childlessness rising
  5. Rising extra-marital births (≠ teen births — mostly cohabiting couples)
     Sweden: ~55% of births outside marriage; US: ~40%; France: ~60%
  6. Rising divorce rate (see below)
  7. Increasing same-sex family formation

INGLEHART'S MATERIALIST → POST-MATERIALIST VALUES:
  Materialist values: economic security, order, traditional institutions
  Post-materialist: self-expression, autonomy, tolerance of diversity
  → Wealth + security frees younger generations from economic constraints
  → Family form becomes a choice rather than a necessity
  → SDT concentrated in post-materialist societies (Northern Europe, NE US, Japan)

COHABITATION:
  Competing interpretations:
    Substitution thesis: cohabitation replaces marriage (especially in Nordic countries)
    Transition thesis: cohabitation is a precursor to marriage (US pattern historically)
    → Evidence: US shifting toward substitution; Nordic already there
  Cohabitation stability: lower than marriage in US; comparable in Sweden
  → Institutional context matters: cohabitation law, rights, benefits
  "Living apart together" (LAT): committed couple; separate households by choice
    → Rising especially in older adults (divorced/widowed; don't want full integration)
```

## Divorce Trends and No-Fault Laws

```
DIVORCE RATES:

  US divorce peak: ~1979–1981 (~50% of marriages predicted to end in divorce)
  US divorce 2020s: declining → ~39–40% (but also: fewer people marrying)
  → Rate per 1000 population: peaked ~5.3/1000 in 1979; now ~2.5/1000

  Decline explained by:
    Delayed marriage (older at marriage → more stable)
    Selection: those who marry now are more committed (cohabitation filtered)
    Educational gradient: college-educated women → lower divorce (reversed)
    Economic conditions: COVID → mixed effects

EDUCATIONAL GRADIENT REVERSAL:
  Pre-1970: educated women had higher divorce rate (economic independence → exit)
  Post-1980: educated women have LOWER divorce rate
  → "Capstone" model: marriage as reward after education + career
  → Lower rates also: more compatible careers, partners with shared values
  → Working-class marriage rates dropped faster than divorce rates changed

NO-FAULT DIVORCE:
  Pre-1970 US: required grounds for divorce (adultery, cruelty, desertion)
  1969: California enacts first no-fault divorce law (Reagan signed)
  1985: All 50 states have some no-fault provision

  Effects:
    Short-term: divorce rates rose ~20% after adoption (estimates: Wolfers 2006)
    Long-term: domestic violence rates fell ~30% in no-fault states (Stevenson/Wolfers)
    Female suicide rates fell ~15%
    → No-fault = exit option → reduces trap → reduces violence
    Counter-intuitive: option to leave without penalty → some stay (less abuse)

DIVORCE AND CHILDREN:
  Amato meta-analysis: divorce associated with:
    Lower academic achievement, behavioral problems, reduced wellbeing
    Effects: modest (d ≈ 0.1–0.2) but statistically robust
  Confound: pre-divorce conflict is the actual driver in many cases
    → High-conflict marriages that end in divorce: children's wellbeing often improves
    → Low-conflict divorces: children's wellbeing often decreases
  Policy implication: "stay together for the kids" depends entirely on conflict level
```

## Care Work and Gender

### Hochschild's Second Shift

```
ARLIE HOCHSCHILD (The Second Shift, 1989):

  Empirical finding: employed mothers work roughly an extra month per year
  compared to employed fathers, in unpaid domestic + care work

  THE SECOND SHIFT:
    First shift: paid employment (both partners)
    Second shift: domestic labor + childcare (women carry disproportionate load)
    → Women effectively work 15 more hours/week than men
    → Over a year: an extra month of 24-hour days

  GENDER STRATEGIES:
    Traditional: husband = breadwinner; wife = homemaker ideology
    Transitional: wife works but still primarily responsible for home
    Egalitarian: both share market + domestic work equally
    → Hochschild found most couples: egalitarian in ideology; traditional in practice

  WHY THE GAP PERSISTS:
    Gender ideology: both partners believe women should do more (partially)
    Time availability: who works more paid hours does less domestic
    Bargaining model: power in relationship = who can refuse unpleasant tasks
    Habit + skill: women often develop domestic skills; creates efficiency argument
    "The economy of gratitude": men credited for doing "extra" domestic; women not

INTERNATIONAL COMPARISON (OECD time use data):
  Hours of unpaid work per day (F / M):
    Denmark:  3.7 / 2.4 hrs → 1.3 hr gap (most equal)
    Sweden:   3.5 / 2.4 hrs
    USA:      3.9 / 2.6 hrs
    Italy:    5.0 / 1.8 hrs → 3.2 hr gap
    Japan:    4.5 / 1.1 hrs → 3.4 hr gap
    Korea:    4.2 / 0.8 hrs → 3.4 hr gap
  → Absolute gap correlates with traditional gender attitudes + limited public childcare
```

### Care Work as Economic Problem

```
CARE WORK ECONOMICS:

  TYPES:
    Childcare: intensive parenting, physical care, transportation
    Elder care: rapidly growing as population ages
    Disability care: ongoing personal assistance
    Domestic labor: cooking, cleaning, household management

  ECONOMIC INVISIBILITY:
    GDP does not count unpaid care work
    → US: estimated $700 billion–$2 trillion annually in unpaid care (RAND, 2019)
    → Care work by women subsidizes formal economy by enabling market work
    → If all mothers worked and hired childcare: formal GDP rises + childcare GDP rises
       but actual production of care = same; just reclassified

  CARE DEFICIT:
    Both parents in paid labor → who does care?
    Solutions: market care (daycare, nanny), kin care (grandparents), state care
    US: minimal state support (no paid parental leave; limited public childcare)
    Nordic: extensive subsidized childcare + generous parental leave → gender gap smaller

  MATERNAL WAGE PENALTY:
    Mothers: earn ~5% less per child than childless women (US)
    Fathers: earn more than childless men ("daddy bonus")
    → Penalty: employer discrimination + actual time out of labor force + reduced hours
    → Claudia Goldin (2023 Nobel): "greedy jobs" — winner-take-all hours premium
       → careers punish those who need schedule flexibility → falls on mothers

  GLOBAL SOUTH:
    Women's unpaid care work: 3–6× men's in most developing countries
    Water and fuel collection (African women): 2–10 hrs/day
    → Massive opportunity cost: education, market work, political participation
    → UNICEF "Time Poverty" → first constraint on women's economic empowerment
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Phenomenon | Relevant concept |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Why population exploded in 20th century | DTM Stage 2: mortality fell; fertility lagged |
| Why South Korea has TFR 0.78 | SDT + extreme "greedy jobs" culture + housing costs + post-materialist values |
| Why divorce rates fell after the 1980s peak | Delayed marriage + educational selection + cohabitation filtering |
| Why no-fault divorce reduced domestic violence | Exit option changes power dynamics; trapped partners face higher abuse risk |
| Why US women still do more housework despite working | Hochschild second shift: gender ideology + bargaining + "economy of gratitude" |
| Why Nordic countries have smaller gender gap in care | Universal subsidized childcare + paid parental leave + culture |
| Why care work doesn't count in GDP | GDP counts market transactions only; unpaid care is invisible in national accounts |
| Why extended family predominates in agrarian societies | Labor pooling + risk sharing + elder care without state pensions |

## Common Confusion Points

**The DTM is a model, not a guaranteed trajectory**
Some countries experience stalled fertility transitions (sub-Saharan Africa: mortality fell but fertility decline slower than expected). The model describes a historical pattern, not a law. Development is not automatic — high child mortality remains, reducing incentive to lower fertility. Female education is the strongest predictor of fertility decline, not income alone.

**Cohabitation rates rising ≠ marriage declining for everyone**
The pattern is highly stratified by education and class. College-educated Americans still marry at high rates (typically after cohabitation). It's non-college Americans whose marriage rates have dropped sharply — often replaced not by cohabitation but by single parenthood. The "marriage gap" by education has widened dramatically since the 1980s.

**"50% of marriages end in divorce" is now outdated and always misleading**
The statistic compared a single-year divorce count to a single-year marriage count — a methodologically flawed comparison. Current cohort tracking suggests ~40% of marriages divorce eventually, and it's falling. Higher for those who marry young, lower socioeconomic status, and those who married pre-1980.

**Hochschild's "second shift" is not just about housework time**
The deeper argument is about the "economy of gratitude" and gender ideology: men are praised for "helping" with housework; women are expected to manage it. The asymmetry isn't simply hours — it's the mental load (tracking schedules, anticipating needs, managing logistics) that falls to women even in partnerships where both do equal physical tasks.

**Population decline is not a catastrophe, but it is a transition problem**
Aging populations do create structural challenges for pay-as-you-go pension systems and elder care capacity. But absolute population size is not a welfare metric. The problem is the transition speed — the ratio of workers to retirees shifts faster than institutions adapt. Immigration can buffer but is politically contested everywhere it's needed.
