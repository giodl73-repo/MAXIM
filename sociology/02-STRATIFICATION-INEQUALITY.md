# Stratification and Inequality

## The Big Picture

Social stratification is **the structural ranking of social groups in a hierarchy of unequal access to valued resources** — income, wealth, power, status, education, health. Unlike individual differences in resources, stratification is systematic, institutionalized, and transmitted across generations.

```
DIMENSIONS OF STRATIFICATION

  ECONOMIC                STATUS              POWER
  ─────────              ────────            ───────
  Income                 Prestige             Decision-making authority
  Wealth (stock)         Honor                Political influence
  Occupation             Social deference     State coercive capacity
  Market position        Lifestyle            Organizational control
  Credit access          Community standing

  Weber's key insight: these three are correlated but SEPARABLE
    A religious leader may have high status + power but low income
    A "new money" entrepreneur may have high income but low status
    An academic may have high status but low income and power
    A gangster may have high income + power but low legitimate status

FORMS OF STRATIFICATION SYSTEM:
  SLAVERY: persons owned as property; extreme; legally abolished globally (though persists)
  CASTE: birth-ascribed, endogamous, ritual-based hierarchy; limited mobility
  ESTATE (feudal): land-based nobility + clergy + commoners; legally differentiated
  CLASS (modern capitalist): property and market position; theoretically open; actual mobility limited
```

## Class: Three Traditions

### Marx on Class

```
MARXIAN CLASS THEORY:
  Classes defined by OWNERSHIP OF MEANS OF PRODUCTION

  Bourgeoisie (capitalists):
    Own means of production (factories, land, capital)
    Purchase labor-power; appropriate surplus value
    Interest: maintain and expand capital; suppress labor costs

  Proletariat (workers):
    Possess only their labor-power
    Must sell it to survive; no property to fall back on
    Interest: improve wages, working conditions; ultimately overthrow capitalism

  Petty bourgeoisie:
    Small business owners who work alongside employees
    Own some means of production but also labor
    Intermediate position → contradictory class interests
    Unstable: tend to be ruined into proletariat or succeed into bourgeoisie

SURPLUS VALUE:
  Workers produce more value in a day than their wages represent
  Difference = surplus value → appropriated by capitalist as profit
  This is exploitation in Marx's technical sense (not moral judgment)

  Working day = necessary labor time (to reproduce labor-power) + surplus labor time
  Rate of exploitation = surplus value / variable capital (wage bill)
  → Capitalists increase profit by: lengthening working day (absolute surplus value)
     OR intensifying/rationalizing production (relative surplus value)

CLASS CONSCIOUSNESS:
  Class-in-itself: objective class position
  Class-for-itself: class that recognizes its position and acts collectively
  Ideology prevents class-for-itself: workers adopt ruling-class ideas
  → "False consciousness" explains why proletariat doesn't consistently revolt
```

### Weber on Class, Status, and Party

```
WEBER'S THREE-DIMENSIONAL STRATIFICATION:

CLASS (economic order):
  Property classes: those who can live from returns on property (rents, dividends)
  Commercial classes: those who use economic goods in markets (varies by skill, service)
  Social classes: aggregates of class positions between which mobility is typical
  → Weber identifies "social class" more flexibly: middle classes are not simply
     petty bourgeoisie — they include educated service workers, technicians

  KEY: Property is important, but so is MARKETABLE SKILL
  A highly trained surgeon with no property has different life chances than
  an unskilled worker with no property — both "propertyless"

STATUS GROUPS (prestige order):
  Communal groups sharing a style of life and positive or negative social estimation
  → Honor, prestige, social respect — often based on birth, lifestyle, education
  → Exclusive social circles; endogamy tendencies
  Status and class can diverge: "genteel poor" (high status, low income)
                                "nouveau riche" (high income, not-yet accepted)

PARTIES (power order):
  Organizations oriented toward acquiring power, regardless of class or status basis
  → Political parties may represent class interests, but not always or exclusively
  → Power as analytically independent of economic class

CLOSURE:
  Social closure = strategies groups use to monopolize resources and exclude others
  Exclusionary: dominant group bars others (racial discrimination, credential requirements)
  Usurpationary: subordinate group uses collective action to appropriate from above
  → Credentialing as a closure strategy: require formal credentials to exclude
    (Randall Collins: "credential inflation" — diplomas for jobs that don't require them)
```

### Contemporary Class Schemes

```
WRIGHT (Neo-Marxist):
  Retains ownership of means of production as primary dimension
  Adds: authority (command over others' labor)
       credentials (control of scarce knowledge)
  → Middle classes occupy "contradictory class locations":
     simultaneously in multiple classes

ERIKSON-GOLDTHORPE-PORTOCARERO (EGP) scheme:
  Empirically-driven; widely used in mobility research
  Based on employment relationship (labor contract vs service contract):

  Class I: Higher service class (professionals, managers, administrators)
  Class II: Lower service class (technicians, semi-professional)
  Class III: Routine non-manual (clerical, admin)
  Class IVa-c: Small employers, petty bourgeoisie, farmers
  Class V-VI: Skilled manual workers, foremen
  Class VIIa-b: Unskilled, semi-skilled manual; farm laborers

BOURDIEU (field theory):
  Capital volume + capital composition (economic vs cultural)
  Two-dimensional space: overall volume + relative weight of each
  → Working class: low economic + low cultural
  → Professionals: high cultural, moderate economic
  → Business class: high economic, moderate cultural
  → Dominant class: high on both
  → Cultural capital determines position within class as much as economic
```

## Caste

```
CASTE (Louis Dumont, Homo Hierarchicus, 1966):

Pure Hindu caste system:
  Hierarchy based on RITUAL PURITY — not wealth, not power primarily

  VARNA (4 theoretical categories, Brahminic text):
    Brahmins: priestly; purity/learning
    Kshatriya: warrior/ruler
    Vaishya: merchant/farmer
    Shudra: servant/artisan
    + Dalits (formerly "untouchables"): outside varna; ritual pollution

  JATI (actual social reality): ~2,000–3,000 endogamous groups
    Each with specific occupation, dietary rules, ritual function, and region
    Endogamy (marriage within jati) is the key boundary-maintenance mechanism
    Mobility: individual virtually impossible; collective jati repositioning possible

CASTE AS ANALYSIS vs RACE:
  Dumont: caste is a holistic ideology (the whole = more than parts)
          each jati meaningful only in relation to others
  African American scholars (Wilkerson, Casteists): American racial hierarchy
  has caste-like features: birth-based, endogamous (anti-miscegenation laws),
  hierarchy of ritual pollution, occupational segregation
  → Contested: conceptual travel of "caste" to American context has both
     illuminating and distorting effects

Indian caste today:
  Constitutionally abolished (1950); reservations for SCs/STs (scheduled castes/tribes)
  Persistent in marriage (>90% endogamous), housing, occupation, rural violence
  Jati identity intensified (paradoxically) by affirmative action politics
  (groups mobilize jati identity to access quotas)
```

## Race and Ethnicity as Social Construction

```
BIOLOGICAL REALITY vs SOCIAL CONSTRUCT:

Genetic evidence:
  Human genetic variation: ~0.1% difference between any two individuals
  Variation WITHIN conventionally defined racial groups:
    > Variation BETWEEN those groups
  → More genetic diversity within "Africa" than between any two continents
  → "Race" as defined socially (Black, White, Asian, etc.) does not capture
     natural genetic clusters in any clean way

  Exception: geographic ancestry clusters (admixture analysis): there ARE
  geographically correlated genetic clusters, but they don't map to
  socially defined racial categories, and are not meaningfully determinative
  for complex traits

SOCIAL CONSTRUCTION:
  Race = socially meaningful category whose meaning is created, maintained, and
  changed through social processes (law, practice, politics)

  Historical contingency of racial categories:
  • Irish immigrants were not "white" in 1850 Boston; became white by 1900
  • Jews were classified as separate race in Nazi Germany; are white in US
  • "Hispanic/Latino" is an ethnic category in US census (not racial)
  • "Asian" collapses enormous genetic and cultural diversity (Bengali ≠ Korean)
  • One-drop rule (hypodescent) in US: historical legal definition of "Black"

RACIALIZATION:
  Process by which groups get assigned racial meaning and differential treatment
  → Immigration law (Chinese Exclusion Act, national origin quotas)
  → Housing (redlining: systematic denial of mortgages by neighborhood race)
  → Criminal justice (disparate sentencing, stop-and-frisk)
  → Medicine (different treatment for same symptoms by patient race)
```

## Gender Stratification

```
SEX vs GENDER:
  Sex: biological categories (chromosomal, gonadal, hormonal, anatomical)
       not binary in biology: intersex (~1.7% of births by broader definitions)
  Gender: social roles, expectations, identities assigned to sexes
          historical and cross-cultural variation → demonstrates social construction
          Western binary (man/woman) is one form; others have ternary+ systems
          (India hijra, Samoa fa'afafine, indigenous two-spirit)

GENDER STRATIFICATION GLOBALLY:
  Women everywhere: lower average earnings than men (~20–40% gap globally)
  Women: disproportionate unpaid care and domestic labor
  Women: less political power (global average women in parliament ~26%)
  Violence: women are disproportionate targets of intimate partner violence, rape
  → Cross-cultural variation in degree; not in direction

EXPLANATIONS:
  Human capital: women's skills/experience → income gap
    → But gap persists after controlling for education, experience, occupation
  Occupational segregation: "female" occupations paid less
    → Causal direction unclear: nursing paid less because women do it,
       or women entered because it was available to them?
  Discrimination: audit studies (identical resumes, different names) → women
                  with children penalized; men with children not penalized
  Care penalty: "motherhood penalty" + "fatherhood bonus"
  → Claudia Goldin: last residual of gender pay gap is within-occupation,
    driven by flexibility premium (jobs that reward continuous presence)

GENDER AND INSTITUTIONS:
  "Doing gender" (West and Zimmerman, 1987): gender is accomplished in interaction
    not just ascribed but actively performed and policed
  Hegemonic masculinity (Connell): culturally dominant form of masculinity
    that subordinates other masculinities and femininity
    → Multiple masculinities with differential status
  → Structural: jobs have gender (mining = masculine; nursing = feminine)
     not people → gendered institutions precede gendered individuals
```

## Intersectionality

Kimberlé Crenshaw (1989) developed intersectionality analyzing Black women's experience in US employment discrimination law.

```
THE FOUNDATIONAL CASE:

DeGraffenreid v. General Motors (1976):
  Black women were discriminated against in hiring
  Court ruled: not race discrimination (GM hired Black men)
               not sex discrimination (GM hired white women)
  → Black women's specific experience fell through the legal cracks
  → Their discrimination = race × sex together, not either alone

INTERSECTIONALITY AS FRAMEWORK:

Race × Gender × Class × Sexuality × Disability × Age × Immigration status
  ↓
Not additive: 1 + 1 + 1 = 3 disadvantages
Actually multiplicative: create qualitatively new positions

  Black woman ≠ Black person + woman
  → Different labor market experience, different body politics,
    different relationship to criminal justice, different healthcare experience

MATRIX OF DOMINATION (Patricia Hill Collins):
  Social systems of oppression are interconnected at:
  Structural domain: law, policy, institutions, economy
  Disciplinary domain: bureaucracy, surveillance, management practices
  Hegemonic domain: ideology, culture, media, religion
  Interpersonal domain: face-to-face interactions

  → Oppression is not just external coercion; it's embedded in structures
    that produce seemingly neutral outcomes that systematically disadvantage
    certain intersectional positions

INTERSECTIONALITY AND PRIVILEGE:
  "Privilege" (McIntosh, 1989): unearned advantages from occupying dominant
  categories — invisible from within, visible from outside
  → Not about individual guilt; about structural positioning
  → Intersectionality: everyone occupies multiple positions, some dominant,
    some subordinate — not a single hierarchy
```

## Social Mobility

```
INTERGENERATIONAL MOBILITY:
  The extent to which children's class outcomes differ from parents'

ABSOLUTE MOBILITY: has the average person improved in absolute terms?
  → Strong in post-WWII high-growth economies; weakening in many contexts
  → "Economic growth lifts all boats" — true when growth reaches lower rungs

RELATIVE MOBILITY (status attainment):
  The extent to which children's rank ordering changes from parents'
  → This is the measure of "meritocracy" and "equal opportunity"
  → Relatively stable across most developed economies (40–50% correlation
     between parent and child earnings in US; lower in Scandinavia)

Featherman-Jones-Hauser (FJH) hypothesis: relative mobility rates are
  constant across industrial societies (same total fluidity)
  → empirically challenged: Nordic countries have substantially higher
    relative mobility than US, UK, despite all being capitalist

INTERNATIONAL COMPARISON:
  "Great Gatsby Curve" (Miles Corak/Alan Krueger):
    Higher inequality → lower intergenerational mobility
    Countries with high Gini coefficient have lower mobility
    US: high inequality + relatively low mobility (vs European peers)
    Denmark: lower inequality + higher mobility

MECHANISMS:
  Inheritance (direct): wealth transfer, gifts, inheritance
  Indirect: neighborhood effects → school quality → college → occupation
  Cultural: Bourdieu's habitus → fits or doesn't fit educational institutions
  Network: parental connections → job referrals and internships
  Assortative mating: educated/affluent people marry each other → concentration
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Marx's "class" is a structural category, not a cultural identity**
In Marx, class is defined by your position in production relations — not your income level, lifestyle, or self-identification. A highly paid knowledge worker who owns no means of production is technically proletarian in Marx's framework. Most people conflate "working class" with blue-collar work or low income — which is a Weberian or cultural definition.

**Race being "socially constructed" doesn't mean racial outcomes are imaginary**
The social construction claim is about the arbitrary, historically variable classification system — not about whether being classified by race has real effects. Being Black in America shapes life expectancy, wealth, incarceration rates, and treatment in medicine. The social fact is powerful even if the biological basis is weak.

**Intersectionality is an analytical framework, not a list of identity categories**
Intersectionality is a method for understanding how systems of oppression interact at specific structural locations. It's not about ranking oppressions or accumulating identities. The theoretical claim: some experiences cannot be captured by single-axis analysis (race alone OR gender alone) — only by analysis that examines their intersection.

**Gender pay gap and "controlled" gender pay gap are different claims**
Unadjusted gap (~20%): women earn ~80 cents per dollar earned by men. Controlled for occupation, hours, experience: gap narrows to ~5–8%. The controlled gap measures discrimination within comparable jobs. The remaining gap after controls — occupational segregation, career interruption, flexibility premium — explains most of the uncontrolled gap. Both are real and important; they measure different things.

**Social mobility data can show high mobility AND persistent inequality simultaneously**
High absolute mobility (everyone does better than their parents in absolute terms) does not imply low inequality or high relative mobility. The US from 1950–1980 had high absolute mobility because the economy grew; relative mobility (reshuffling of rank order) was not higher than Europe.
