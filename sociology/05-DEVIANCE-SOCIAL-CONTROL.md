# Deviance and Social Control

## The Big Picture

Deviance is **behavior that violates social norms and is subject to negative social sanctions**. It is not inherent to the act but to the social reaction — the same act can be deviant in one context and normal in another, by one social group and criminal by another. Social control includes all mechanisms — formal and informal — through which societies enforce conformity to norms.

```
DEVIANCE AND CONTROL — THE SPECTRUM:

  INFORMAL CONTROL ◄───────────────────────────────► FORMAL CONTROL
  Family pressure                                    Criminal law
  Peer ridicule                                      Police
  Community gossip                                   Courts
  Religious disapproval                              Incarceration
  Ostracism                                          Execution

  Primary deviance: first violation; individual doesn't define self as deviant
  Secondary deviance: after labeling; deviance becomes part of self-concept
  → The label can be more consequential than the original act

WHAT VARIES ACROSS THEORIES:
  1. What causes deviance? (strain, labeling, social disorganization, rational choice)
  2. Who defines deviance? (moral entrepreneurs, power holders)
  3. What is the function of deviance? (Durkheim: necessary for social cohesion)
  4. What should be done about it? (deter, treat, transform, abolish categories)
```

## Durkheim: Crime as Functional

Durkheim's counterintuitive argument: deviance (including crime) is normal and has positive social functions.

```
DURKHEIM ON CRIME (Rules of Sociological Method, 1895):

Crime is NORMAL because:
  1. It exists in all known societies at all historical periods
  2. It is integral to social organization, not a failure of it

If crime magically disappeared:
  → Society would tighten norms to catch new marginal behaviors
  → What was previously tolerated becomes deviant
  → New deviance emerges to fill the regulatory space
  → Conclusion: crime rate reflects social sensitivity to deviance, not absolute behavior

FUNCTIONS OF CRIME:
  1. Norm clarification:
     When a crime is punished publicly, the norm is made explicit
     → Community reaffirms shared values through collective response to violation
     → Durkheim: punishment is a moral ritual that regenerates social solidarity
     → "The punishment is for the living, not the dead criminal"

  2. Social innovation:
     Yesterday's deviance is sometimes tomorrow's norm
     → Suffragettes (criminal): criminal behavior that changed norms
     → Civil rights activists (criminal under Jim Crow): generated norm change
     → Heresy produced scientific revolution
     → Innovation requires deviation from established rules

  3. Strengthening group boundaries:
     Common enemy coheres the group; identifying deviance clarifies "us vs them"
     → Moral panics unify communities, briefly

COLLECTIVE CONSCIENCE:
  The totality of shared beliefs, values, moral codes of a society
  Stronger in mechanical solidarity (traditional societies): intense repression of deviance
  Weaker in organic solidarity (modern): more tolerance, more individualism
  → Modernization = loosening of collective conscience → more deviance tolerated

ANOMIE:
  State of normlessness when collective moral constraints break down
  → From Durkheim: rapid social change destroys previously effective norms
    before new ones are established
  → Individuals lack clear guides → anxiety, disorientation, self-destructive behavior
  → Explains: suicide rates rise during economic booms (not just downturns)
     → disruption of stable reference points, not just deprivation, causes anomie
```

## Merton's Strain Theory

Robert Merton (Social Structure and Anomie, 1938) adapted Durkheim's anomie concept to American capitalism.

```
MERTON'S STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT:

American society emphasizes two things simultaneously:
  1. CULTURAL GOALS: financial success, wealth, material achievement
                     ("The American Dream" — available to all who work hard)
  2. INSTITUTIONAL MEANS: legitimate pathways — education, hard work, deferred gratification

STRUCTURAL STRAIN:
  Lower-class individuals internalize the same cultural goals as everyone else
  BUT have differential access to legitimate means (unequal education, discrimination)
  → Strain between goals (aspiration) and available means

MERTON'S 5 ADAPTATION MODES:
         Goals    Means    Response
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Conformity   +       +    Accept both; most people
  Innovation   +       -    Accept goals; reject means → crime (the key deviance route)
                             "Find another way to get rich"
  Ritualism    -       +    Reject goals; follow means anyway → bureaucratic personality
                             "Going through the motions"
  Retreatism   -       -    Reject both → withdrawal, escapism, drug use, mental illness
  Rebellion    ±       ±    Replace both with different values/means → revolutionary, cult

INNOVATION AS THE DEVIANCE EXPLANATION:
  Those who most want to succeed but have fewest legitimate opportunities
  face the greatest strain → highest motivation to "innovate" (crime, fraud, gangs)
  → Explains: street crime concentrated in lower class (strain is highest)
               white-collar crime also "innovation" (accepting goals; bypassing legal means)
               → "Mertonian" explanation of corporate fraud

CRITIQUE:
  Assumes everyone shares the same success goals → not empirically demonstrated
  Doesn't explain: crime by those with legitimate means already
                   crime that isn't about material gain (violence, hate crimes)
                   why strain → crime for some but not others
                   subcultural transmission of crime (Sutherland: learned, not strained into)
```

## Labeling Theory

Howard Becker (Outsiders, 1963): deviance is a social attribution, not an intrinsic property of acts.

```
BECKER'S CORE CLAIM:
  "Deviance is not a quality of the act the person commits, but rather a
  consequence of the application by others of rules and sanctions to an 'offender'"

  → The deviant is one to whom a label has been successfully applied
  → Deviance requires: an act + someone with the power to apply a label

MORAL ENTREPRENEURS:
  Individuals and groups who create and enforce moral rules
  Rule creators: people who crusade to have something defined as deviant
    → Temperance movement → Prohibition
    → Anti-marijuana campaign (Harry Anslinger, Bureau of Narcotics, 1930s)
    → Psychiatrists defining new disorders
    Motivated by: sincere moral belief, economic interest, status politics
  Rule enforcers: police, courts, psychiatrists — apply labels to specific individuals

DEVIANCE AS CAREER:
  Primary deviance: initial violation; individual doesn't identify as deviant
                    → Most people commit many acts that could be labeled deviant
                       without being caught or labeled

  Labeling event: caught, processed, labeled publicly
                  → "Deviant" identity attached; public record

  Secondary deviance (Lemert, 1951):
    Person incorporates the deviant label into self-concept
    → Behavior changes to conform to the label
    → "Master status" as deviant overrides other identities
    → Social exclusion → reduced access to legitimate alternatives
    → Further deviance as career

STIGMA AND LABELING:
  Official record: criminal record → employment discrimination, housing denial
  Social stigma: informal discrimination continues after legal punishment
  Recidivism as labeling effect: not moral failure but structural exclusion
    → After prison, legitimate opportunities are fewer → crime is relatively
      more accessible than before incarceration

CRITIQUE:
  Romanticizes deviant subcultures; ignores real harm done by deviants
  Doesn't explain initial deviance (before labeling)
  Not all labeled deviant become secondary deviants (labeling is not deterministic)
  Power relations in labeling need more explicit theorization
```

## Foucault: Discipline and Punish

Michel Foucault (Surveiller et Punir, 1975): the history of punishment is not a story of progress but of the transformation of power techniques.

```
FOUCAULT'S GENEALOGICAL ARGUMENT:

Historical shift (18th → 19th century):
  SPECTACLE OF PUNISHMENT (pre-modern):
    Public torture, execution, drawing-and-quartering
    Power displayed on the body; public witnessing
    → King's power made visible through suffering
    Damiens the regicide case (1757): opening scene of the book
    → 3 hours of public torture before death

  DISCIPLINE (modern):
    Regularized, timetabled institutions (prisons, schools, hospitals, factories, armies)
    Not punishment of the body but training/normalization of the soul
    → Prison reforms: cells, inspection, time schedules, work, rehabilitation discourse

DISCIPLINARY MECHANISMS:
  1. HIERARCHICAL OBSERVATION: constant visibility of subjects
     → Architecture of surveillance: barracks where officers can see all soldiers
                                     school where teacher can see all students
                                     hospital where nurse can survey all beds

  2. NORMALIZING JUDGMENT:
     Everything measured against a norm (the average, the ideal)
     → Not punishment for specific crimes but constant correction of deviations
     → "Normal" becomes a weapon: you deviate from normal → disciplined back
     → Comparison, differentiation, ranking — the examination

  3. THE EXAMINATION:
     Combines hierarchical observation + normalizing judgment
     → Makes individuals visible, comparable, documentable
     → Creates the individual as a documented case: case file, medical record,
       school record, performance review
     → Power operates through knowledge about individuals

THE PANOPTICON (Jeremy Bentham's 1791 design):
  Central tower surrounded by ring of cells
  Inspector in tower can see all cells at all times
  Inmates can never know if they are being observed → must behave as if always watched
  → Inmate internalizes surveillance → self-policing → power without direct force
  → "A machine to carry out experiments" on behavior modification

  Panopticism as social principle:
  Not just prisons — schools, factories, hospitals, armies all operate on this logic
  The consciousness of being watched produces compliance
  → SELF-SURVEILLANCE: Foucault's central insight
    Modern power operates through subjects regulating themselves
    Not: king commands → subjects obey
    But: subjects internalize norms → regulate themselves → power needs no enforcement

POWER/KNOWLEDGE:
  Disciplines (criminology, psychiatry, medicine, pedagogy):
    Claim to be neutral scientific knowledge about deviants
    Actually produce the categories they claim to discover
    → The criminal is not a natural type; criminology produces the criminal
    → The mentally ill is not a natural type; psychiatry produces the patient
  Power and knowledge are inseparable → "power/knowledge" (pouvoir/savoir)

NORMALIZATION:
  Modern power doesn't primarily exclude or kill — it normalizes
  → Creates the norm; identifies deviations; corrects them
  → "Abnormality" (mental illness, learning disability, sexual deviance) as product of
    normalizing gaze, not discovery of natural variation
```

## Mass Incarceration as Social Fact

The US imprisonment rate is a sociological outlier requiring explanation beyond individual criminology.

```
US INCARCERATION DATA:
  US incarceration rate: ~620 per 100,000 population (2020, declining from peak ~760)
  International comparison:
    Cuba: ~510 (next highest) | Russia: ~350 | UK: ~130 | Germany: ~80
    France: ~100 | Japan: ~38 | Netherlands: ~60
  US incarcerates ~5× the rate of any comparable wealthy democracy

  Total US incarcerated: ~2.2 million (2020); ~7 million under correctional supervision
  (probation, parole, jail, prison)
  Peak: ~2.3 million in 2008 — largest absolute incarcerated population in history

RACIAL DISPARITY:
  Black Americans: ~38% of prison population; ~12% of US population
  Incarceration rate: Black men ~5× white men at comparable ages
  1 in 3 Black men born in US today estimated to be incarcerated at some point
  → Not explained by criminal behavior differences; racial disparities in:
      arrest rates for same behavior
      prosecution rates
      plea bargaining pressure
      sentencing outcomes

HISTORICAL CAUSES:
  "The New Jim Crow" thesis (Michelle Alexander, 2010):
    Mass incarceration arose specifically as a racial caste system after civil rights
    → War on Drugs (Nixon 1971; Reagan escalation): drug enforcement concentrated
      in Black communities despite similar use rates across races
    → Mandatory minimums, truth in sentencing, three-strikes laws (1980s–90s)
    → "Felon disenfranchisement": criminal record → loss of voting rights,
      public housing, SNAP, student loans, employment → permanent second-class status
    → "Collateral consequences" reproduce caste outcomes

  Political economy:
    Prison-industrial complex: for-profit prisons, prison labor, correctional industry
    → Structural interests in maintaining incarceration rates
    Bipartisan crime politics: "tough on crime" rhetoric
    → 1994 Crime Bill (Biden); three strikes; "superpredators" (Clintons)

SOCIOLOGICAL EFFECTS:
  Social epidemiology: incarceration → destroys family ties, community social capital,
    employment prospects → increases crime long-term even as it reduces it short-term
  Spillover: incarceration of parents → adverse outcomes for children
  Community destabilization: high-incarceration neighborhoods lose social organization
  → Robert Sampson: neighborhood collective efficacy → crime control
    → Mass removal of men destroys that efficacy → counter-productive

DECARCERATION MOVEMENT:
  Evidence: crime rates not strongly correlated with incarceration rates across states
            1990s crime drop predated major incarceration increases
  Reform: reduce mandatory minimums; end cash bail; front-end diversion
          drug courts; mental health courts; restorative justice
  Abolition: prison abolition movement argues prisons cannot be reformed
             → focus on transformative justice, community accountability
```

## Social Control Theory (Travis Hirschi)

```
HIRSCHI'S CONTROL THEORY (Causes of Delinquency, 1969):

Reverses the usual question: not "why do people commit crime?"
  but "why do most people NOT commit crime?"

Social bond as inhibitor of deviance — four elements:
  1. ATTACHMENT: caring about others' opinions; affective ties to family, school, peers
     → Delinquency is lower when: attached to parents, teachers, peers
     → Not because they are watching but because offender cares about disappointing them

  2. COMMITMENT: investment in conventional activities (education, career, reputation)
     → "Stake in conformity": what do you have to lose?
     → Those with more to lose commit less crime
     → Why middle-class crime is lower (more commitment to conventional goals)

  3. INVOLVEMENT: time spent in conventional activities (school, sports, work)
     → "Idle hands" — simply less time for deviance
     → After-school programs: involvement theory basis

  4. BELIEF: belief that the law and moral order are legitimate
     → People who believe rules are fair are more likely to follow them
     → Legitimacy crisis → reduced belief → increased deviance

General Theory of Crime (Hirschi + Gottfredson, 1990):
  Self-control is the key variable
  → Low self-control (impulsivity, insensitivity, risk-seeking): explains crime
  → Established by age 8; stable across life
  → Criticism: circular (crime = low self-control; low self-control = tendency to crime)
                ignores structural factors
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Phenomenon | Relevant theory |
|-----------|----------------|
| Why deviance isn't inherent to acts but to social reactions | Labeling theory (Becker) |
| Why crime concentrates in lower class despite shared values | Strain theory (Merton) |
| Why punishment is a moral ritual, not just deterrence | Durkheim: norm clarification function |
| Why prisons create the types they claim to treat | Foucault: discipline produces subjects |
| Why mass incarceration doesn't reduce crime proportionally | Social control theory; collateral consequences |
| Why ex-offenders have high recidivism | Secondary deviance + labeling/structural exclusion |
| Why some societies tolerate behaviors others criminalize | Moral entrepreneur variation; collective conscience strength |
| Why drug enforcement is racially disparate despite similar use rates | Racial caste hypothesis (Alexander); political economy |

## Common Confusion Points

**Durkheim saying crime is "normal" is not endorsing crime**
Durkheim's claim is analytical: deviance is a universal feature of social life because it is structurally generated by the gap between absolute conformity (impossible) and perfect consensus (impossible). Any society will have some deviance; its form changes but its presence doesn't. This is a claim about social structure, not about whether harm matters.

**Labeling theory doesn't say labels cause initial deviance**
The theory is about the consequences of labeling — how being labeled deviant changes the trajectory of behavior, self-concept, and social opportunities. What causes initial deviance is a separate question (structural, motivational, situational). The insight is that the label can be more consequential than the behavior.

**Foucault's panopticon is a theoretical tool, not a literal blueprint**
Foucault uses Bentham's physical design as a crystallized form of a broader principle: how surveillance-based power operates. The claim is not that all modern institutions are literal panopticons but that they embody the logic of internalized self-surveillance. Digital surveillance (social media monitoring, credit scoring) is the 21st-century analog.

**Mass incarceration in the US is bipartisan, not solely Republican**
The 1994 Crime Bill (signed by Clinton, supported by Biden) added mandatory minimums, three-strikes provisions, and billions for prison construction. The "tough on crime" consensus of the 1980s–90s was bipartisan. The racial disparities were not the stated intent but the documented outcome of policies that both parties supported.

**Self-control theory's prediction of stability is empirically contested**
Hirschi and Gottfredson claim self-control is established by age 8 and stable thereafter. But longitudinal studies show that social bonds in adulthood (marriage, stable employment) do change criminal behavior — even for those with low self-control in childhood. Sampson and Laub's life-course theory demonstrates this turning-point effect, which self-control theory cannot easily accommodate.
