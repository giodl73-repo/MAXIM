# Collective Behavior and Social Movements

## The Big Picture

Collective behavior is **group action outside of institutionalized, routine channels** — from spontaneous crowd behavior to organized social movements. Understanding the conditions under which people act collectively, how movements sustain themselves, and what makes them succeed or fail is central to understanding social change.

## Crowd Behavior Theories

### Le Bon: Contagion Theory (1895)

```
GUSTAVE LE BON (La Psychologie des Foules, 1895):

Core claim: crowds operate by contagion — individuals in crowds lose
  their individuality and become subject to a collective "crowd mind"

Mechanisms:
  Anonymity: in a crowd, individual cannot be held responsible → inhibitions lower
  Suggestibility: contagion spreads ideas/emotions like a disease
  Mental unity: crowd acquires collective intelligence, sentiment, behavior
                that overrides individual reason

Crowd characteristics (Le Bon):
  • Impulsive, mobile, easily excitable
  • Incapable of perseverance or reasoning
  • Extreme in emotion; primitive
  • Easily led by strong "master" (implication: demagogic leaders)

INFLUENCE: Le Bon influenced propaganda theories; fascist mass psychology;
           Freud's Group Psychology and Analysis of the Ego (1921)

CRITIQUE:
  Empirical: crowds do NOT uniformly lose rationality — people remain themselves
  Normative: Le Bon's description systematically pathologizes collective action
             → crowds of workers/colonized peoples = irrational danger
             → crowds of bourgeoisie = legitimate assembly
             The theory is ideological as much as empirical
  Empirical studies of crowd behavior (Quarantelli, Dynes):
    Disasters: crowds typically orderly, cooperative, helpful to strangers
    "Panic": rarely occurs; media accounts systematically exaggerate disorder
```

### Turner and Killian: Emergent Norm Theory (1957)

```
RALPH TURNER + LEWIS KILLIAN (Collective Behavior, 1957):

Rejects: contagion model — crowds are not mindless
Core claim: crowds develop emergent norms that define appropriate behavior
             in ambiguous situations

Process:
  1. Ambiguous situation (fire, protest, festivity): no established script
  2. Milling: people look to each other; exchange glances and words
  3. Keynoting: certain individuals define the situation and suggest behavior
  4. Emergent norm: consensus on what's appropriate forms rapidly
  5. Crowd acts in accordance with emergent norm
     → Differential expression: individuals conform because norm seems unanimous
        even if privately uncertain → creates appearance of uniformity

IMPLICATIONS:
  Crowds are not mindless: they are making and following social norms
  Crowd behavior is interpretive: people read the situation and define appropriate action
  Different crowd behaviors emerge from different norm-defining processes
  → Same physical gathering might become cooperative or violent
     depending on the norm that emerges

EVIDENCE:
  Better fits disaster data: orderly behavior emerges from ambiguity
  Protest crowds: explicit norms about violence, property (enforced internally)
  Riot contexts: norms about targets (not random; specific property or persons)
```

## Moral Panics

Stanley Cohen (Folk Devils and Moral Panics, 1972) on how societies construct episodes of disproportionate alarm.

```
MORAL PANIC: episode in which a person, condition, or group is defined as a threat
  to societal values and interests; characterized by stylized and stereotypical
  representation; the diagnosis is presented by moral entrepreneurs and experts

COMPONENTS:
  Folk devils: the labeled threat (Mods and Rockers in 1960s Britain; satanic cults;
               video games; immigrants; social media; specific drugs)
  Moral entrepreneurs: those who define the threat and push for response
               (journalists, politicians, police, religious leaders)
  Volatility: panics arise quickly, are often disproportionate, and eventually recede
  Disproportionality: response greatly exceeds the actual threat
  Consensus: widespread agreement that the threat is real and serious

MORAL PANIC SEQUENCE:
  1. Identification of threatening phenomenon
  2. Media coverage → amplification → moral entrepreneurs mobilize
  3. Expert panels, committees, hearings → institutional response
  4. Control culture responds: laws, policing, treatment
  5. Often: phenomenon recedes or was never as severe as claimed
             but institutional changes (laws, agencies) persist
             sometimes the "cure" is worse than the "disease"

EXAMPLES:
  1950s–60s: juvenile delinquency + rock and roll
  1970s–80s: mugging panic (UK, Hall et al. — explicitly racialized)
  1980s: satanic ritual abuse (McMartin preschool case — fabricated)
  1990s: "crack babies" and crack cocaine (disproportionate)
  2000s–: various internet/video game moral panics
  Recurring: immigration as crime threat (empirically reversed)

COHEN'S CONTRIBUTION:
  Framework for analyzing how societies construct deviance
  → "Deviance amplification spiral": media coverage → public alarm →
     police response → more arrests → confirms alarm → more coverage
  Asymmetry: easily created, hard to debunk
  → Once folk devil label applied, evidence doesn't easily dislodge it
```

## Social Movement Theory

### Resource Mobilization Theory

John McCarthy and Mayer Zald (1977): social movements are organizations that must mobilize resources to survive and pursue goals.

```
RESOURCE MOBILIZATION (RM) THEORY:

Core shift: from grievance-based explanations (everyone has grievances;
  what matters is capacity to organize) to resource/organizational focus

KEY INSIGHT: grievances alone are insufficient for social movement emergence
  → Grievances exist constantly; movements require resources to act on them

Resources:
  Material: money, labor, office space, equipment
  Human: time, skills, members, leaders
  Social organizational: networks, coalitions, legitimacy
  Cultural: frames, public sympathy, shared identity

Social Movement Organizations (SMOs):
  Formal organizations pursuing social movement goals
  Compete for adherents, resources, influence
  Industry of SMOs: multiple organizations competing in same cause space
  Professionalization: full-time staff; fundraising apparatus; bureaucratization

IMPLICATIONS:
  Movements are more likely when resources are available
  → Middle-class movements common (resource access)
  → Explaining 1960s civil rights: not more grievances than before;
    but black community organizations, HBCU networks, church infrastructure → resources

CRITIQUE:
  Underplays: role of emotions, identity, ideology
              why people join movements when free-riding would benefit them
              (selective incentives argument: private goods to members)
  Doesn't explain: why some movements win with fewer resources than others
                   timing — why this grievance activates now
```

### Political Process / Political Opportunity Theory

Doug McAdam (Political Process and the Development of Black Insurgency, 1982).

```
POLITICAL OPPORTUNITY STRUCTURE:
  The political context that shapes movement emergence and success

Key variables:
  Openness/closure of institutionalized political system
    Open: multiple access points, federalism → more movement opportunities
    Closed: authoritarian state → movements go underground or are crushed

  Stability/instability of elite alignments
    Elite division → openings for challengers
    United elite → closed system; challengers can be isolated

  Presence/absence of elite allies
    Movements more likely to succeed when some elite faction supports them

  State capacity and propensity for repression
    Low repression risk + grievances → movement emergence
    High repression → movements suppressed unless international visibility

McADAM'S THREE COMPONENTS FOR INSURGENCY:
  1. Organization strength: network, leadership, resource base
  2. Cognitive liberation: belief that collective action is both necessary and feasible
  3. Political opportunity: openness in political system

CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT APPLICATION:
  Pre-WWII: few political opportunities; organization building quietly continued
  Post-WWII:
    Cold War → US racial image internationally → federal government interest in reform
    Black veteran demands → not satisfied → cognitive liberation
    NAACP + HBCU + church networks → organization strength
    → Combination produced insurgency of the 1950s-60s
```

### Framing Theory

David Snow and Robert Benford (1988): how social movements construct meaning to mobilize participants.

```
FRAMING:
  Process by which movements define problems, diagnose causes, suggest solutions,
  and motivate action — within a particular frame

FRAME COMPONENTS:
  Diagnostic framing: identifying and attributing blame for a problem
    "Economic inequality is caused by corporate capture of government"
    "Crime is caused by moral decline"
    → Different diagnoses suggest different solutions

  Prognostic framing: articulating solutions and strategies
    "We need campaign finance reform and corporate tax reform"
    "We need stronger law enforcement and traditional values"

  Motivational framing: calling people to action
    "You can make a difference"; "now is the time"; urgency + agency

FRAME RESONANCE: how well a frame connects with audience beliefs/values
  Empirical credibility: does the frame match observable events?
  Narrative fidelity: does it resonate with cultural narratives already held?
  Centrality: does it address core values and concerns?

MASTER FRAMES:
  Broad interpretive frameworks that multiple movements draw on
  "Rights" master frame: civil rights → women's rights → disability rights → LGBTQ rights
  Each movement borrows and extends the rights frame to their constituency

FRAMING CONTESTS:
  Movements and counter-movements compete over how an issue is defined
  → Whoever defines the frame wins the political debate
  "Estate tax" → "death tax" (reframing by opponents)
  "Pro-life" vs "pro-choice" (both movements chose names that emphasize positive value)
  Climate change: "global warming" (specific, measurable) vs "crisis" vs "hoax"

COUNTER-MOVEMENT FRAMES:
  Opposing movements arise to contest successful movement frames
  Tea Party vs Obama-era liberalism; anti-abortion vs reproductive rights
  Counter-movements often more resourced than movements they oppose
```

## Movement Success and Failure

```
OUTCOMES OF SOCIAL MOVEMENTS:

POLICY CHANGE:
  Easier to measure; but reform without enforcement = symbolic only
  Movement success: disability rights (ADA) → enforcement + culture change
  Movement partial success: environmental laws → industry influence → weak enforcement

CULTURAL CHANGE:
  Harder to measure; long-term; often outlasts specific policy wins
  Civil rights movement: legal segregation ended; but cultural change slower
  Women's movement: profound cultural change in gender roles despite continued inequality

ORGANIZATIONAL SURVIVAL:
  Many movements succeed in institutionalizing as NGOs, think tanks, political parties
  → But institutionalization can domesticate radical goals → "goal displacement"

CONDITIONS FOR SUCCESS:
  Political opportunity (McAdam): elite division, access points
  Resources (McCarthy/Zald): organizational capacity
  Framing success (Snow/Benford): resonant message
  Tactical disruption: making normal life impossible focuses attention
  Non-violence (Sharp): paradox — suffering of non-violent protesters generates sympathy;
                          violence by protesters allows state violence and legitimation

MOVEMENT DECLINE:
  Repression: state uses force to end movement
  Success: goals achieved; movement disbands or routinizes
  Co-optation: movement leaders integrated into institutions; radicalism diffused
  Factional conflict: internal divisions destroy cohesion
  Tactical freezing: movement relies on tactics that stop working

CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT PERIODIZATION:
  1955–1965: high-point; Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965)
  Post-1965: strategic uncertainty; Black Power vs integration; coalition fractures
  → Classic pattern: initial disruption → concession → internal division → decline
```

## Diffusion of Innovation

Everett Rogers (Diffusion of Innovations, 1962, 5th ed. 2003): how new ideas, practices, and technologies spread through social systems.

```
ROGERS' DIFFUSION FRAMEWORK:

INNOVATION: idea, practice, or object perceived as new by an individual/group

  % of population
  adopting        Innovators  Early     Early    Late     Laggards
  over time        (2.5%)    Adopters  Majority  Majority  (16%)
                             (13.5%)   (34%)    (34%)

                  ██
                 ████
                ████████
               ████████████
              ████████████████████
  ─────────────────────────────────────────── time

  S-CURVE: cumulative adoption follows sigmoid (S-shaped) curve
    Slow start (only innovators) → acceleration (early majority joins)
    → deceleration → saturation

ADOPTER CATEGORIES:
  Innovators (2.5%): risk-tolerant; access to information; often outside community center
  Early Adopters (13.5%): respected; social centrality; opinion leaders; influence others
  Early Majority (34%): deliberate adoption; follow opinion leaders; above-average social contact
  Late Majority (34%): skeptical; adopt after majority; below-average resources
  Laggards (16%): tradition-oriented; last to adopt; often adopt as innovation obsoletes

OPINION LEADERS:
  Central to diffusion: Rogers found ~13.5% of adopters are early adopters who are
  heavily networked and respected → their adoption signals safety to majority
  → Targeting opinion leaders accelerates diffusion (viral marketing)
  → Mismatch: if innovators are too different from potential adopters, they can't
    effectively model innovation for the majority (the "chasm" in technology adoption)

DIFFUSION VARIABLES:
  Relative advantage: perceived superiority over prior practice
  Compatibility: consistency with existing values, past experiences, needs
  Complexity: how difficult to understand and use
  Trialability: can it be tried on limited basis?
  Observability: are results visible to others?
  → All five affect adoption rate; relative advantage and compatibility most important

CRITICAL MASS:
  For interactive technologies: adoption accelerates once critical mass reached
  → Below critical mass: not worth adopting (nobody else uses it)
  → Above: network effect → rapid spread
  → Explains: fax machine adoption; email; social media platforms
```

## From Technology Adoption to Movement Theory

The frameworks in this module are not sociological curiosities — several have direct analogs in technology strategy and product development.

**Rogers' diffusion model is the foundation of technology adoption theory.** Moore's "Crossing the Chasm" (1991) is an applied derivative of Rogers (1962): the "chasm" between early adopters and early majority is Rogers' observation that innovators and early adopters are structurally different from the pragmatist majority. The S-curve of cumulative adoption is the sigmoid growth curve that venture investors model when sizing markets. Critical mass for interactive technologies (Rogers) is the network effects threshold that platform companies race to reach. When engineers debate "tipping points" in technology markets, they are reasoning with Rogers' framework without naming it.

**Framing theory is the sociological model of what product positioning and marketing do.** A product's positioning statement is a diagnostic frame (this problem exists; it is caused by this gap) plus a prognostic frame (our product is the solution) plus a motivational frame (here's why you should act now). The "jobs to be done" framework in product development is diagnostic framing: identify the problem the customer is trying to solve before proposing a solution. Frame resonance — whether the message connects with the audience's existing values and experiences — is exactly what A/B testing on product messaging is trying to measure.

**Resource mobilization maps directly to startup constraints.** A startup's fundamental problem is not ideas — it is resources. A movement's fundamental problem is not grievances — it is organizational capacity. McCarthy and Zald's insight (grievances are always present; what varies is capacity to act) parallels the startup observation that most problems worth solving are already known; what separates successful startups is execution capability, not insight uniqueness. The Social Movement Organization (SMO) competing for adherents and resources in an "industry" of SMOs is the organizational analog of startups competing in a market segment.

**Political opportunity theory models platform-era regulatory dynamics.** When regulatory regimes shift (GDPR, DSA, AI Act), the "political opportunity structure" for tech challengers changes: entry barriers imposed on incumbents create openings for new entrants; elite divisions (US vs EU regulators, antitrust uncertainty) create space for challengers to maneuver. The framework that McAdam used to explain the civil rights movement is structurally applicable to analyzing when regulatory windows open for platform disruption.

| Phenomenon | Relevant theory |
|-----------|----------------|
| Why massive crowds remain orderly | Emergent norm theory (Turner/Killian) |

| Why civil rights mobilized in 1950s, not 1930s | Political opportunity + organizational infrastructure (McAdam) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Collective behavior ≠ irrational behavior**
The Le Bon tradition embedded an assumption that crowds are primitive and irrational. Decades of empirical research (disaster sociology, protest studies) find the opposite: crowds typically maintain norms, facilitate cooperation, and behave in contextually appropriate ways. The irrationality narrative serves to delegitimize collective action.

**Resource mobilization and political opportunity are complementary, not competing**
Resources without opportunity: organization can't win even if strong.
Opportunity without resources: opening will pass without capacity to exploit.
Political opportunity tells you when; resource mobilization tells you whether.
Most contemporary movement scholarship uses both.

**Diffusion curves describe population aggregates, not individual behavior**
The S-curve is a statistical aggregate — it doesn't say anything about what individual adopters experience. The adopter categories (innovators, early majority, etc.) are analytical constructs imposed retrospectively on adoption data. In real time, you don't know where on the curve you are.

**Framing and deliberate propaganda overlap but differ analytically**
Framing is a normal, unavoidable feature of all communication — any description of an event involves selection and emphasis. Propaganda is strategic frame construction for manipulation. The difference is largely one of intentionality and transparency. Movements frame their cause; so does the state; so does the media. The analytical question is whose frame wins and why.

**Social movements and interest groups are different kinds of organizations**
Interest groups (lobbying organizations, professional associations) work within the political system — they are institutionalized challengers. Social movements operate at least partly outside institutionalized channels — disruption, protest, civil disobedience. The distinction blurs as movements succeed and institutionalize (NAACP, NOW, Sierra Club all started as movements; they function now more like interest groups).
