# Organizations and Institutions

## The Big Picture

Organizations are **goal-directed social systems** with formal structures, boundaries, and members. Institutions are **persistent, taken-for-granted rules, norms, and cognitive frameworks** that constrain and enable organizational behavior. Understanding their relationship is central to explaining why organizations behave as they do — which is often not primarily about efficiency.

```
ORGANIZATION vs INSTITUTION

ORGANIZATION: specific, bounded entity
  Microsoft Corporation, MIT, the US Army, the Catholic Church
  Has members, rules, resource allocation, hierarchy
  Can be created, changed, dissolved

INSTITUTION: pattern of rules + norms + cognitive scripts
  Private property, marriage, academic credentials, the corporation as legal person,
  money, scientific peer review
  Transcends any particular organization
  "Institution" in common usage often conflated with "organization"
  → "Hospital as institution" (the organization) vs
     "medicine as institution" (the rules and norms governing healthcare)

RELATIONSHIP:
  Organizations operate within institutional environments
  Institutional pressures shape how organizations look, what they do, and
  what they say they do (legitimacy vs efficiency)
  → New institutional theory: organizations adopt practices because they are
     legitimate in their field, not because they are efficient
```

## Weber's Ideal-Type Bureaucracy

Max Weber's bureaucracy is the most influential organizational form theory in sociology.

```
THE BUREAUCRATIC IDEAL TYPE (Weber):

1. HIERARCHY OF OFFICES
   Each position supervised by the one above
   → Clear chain of command; accountability upward

2. WRITTEN RULES AND PROCEDURES
   All activities governed by impersonal rules
   Not personal discretion but rule application
   → Predictability; consistency; protection from arbitrariness

3. TRAINED SPECIALISTS
   Each office requires specific technical expertise
   Selection by qualification, not birth or personal loyalty
   → Meritocratic principle; rationality of method

4. IMPERSONALITY
   Rule-bound, not person-bound
   Same treatment regardless of who the client is
   → Formal equality before bureaucratic procedure
   Emotions, personal relationships excluded from formal process

5. FULL-TIME SALARIED OFFICIALS
   Officials hold position in an organization, not as personal property
   Salary based on rank; career trajectory within organization
   → Separation of office from person; no venality

6. WRITTEN DOCUMENTATION (FILES)
   All administrative acts, decisions, and rules maintained in files
   → Institutional memory; accountability; continuity across personnel

WEBER'S ASSESSMENT:
  Bureaucracy is the most efficient form of large-scale organization
  → "Precision, speed, unambiguity, knowledge of the files, continuity,
     discretion, unity, strict subordination, reduction of friction and
     of material and personal costs" (Weber, Economy and Society)
  BUT: the "iron cage" — rationalization imprisons social life in its own logic
       → Formal rationality at expense of substantive rationality
       → Bureaucracy destroys charisma, tradition, individual judgment
```

### Dysfunctions of Bureaucracy (Merton, Gouldner)

```
MERTON'S BUREAUCRATIC DYSFUNCTION:

Goal displacement: rules become ends in themselves, not means to organizational goals
  → Following procedure correctly is more important than achieving the goal
  → Common example: government agencies that process forms correctly but
    accomplish nothing useful

Trained incapacity: expertise in the current methods makes adaptation harder
  → Those best at the old way are worst at learning the new way
  → Dysfunctional in rapidly changing environments

Bureaucratic personality: rules internalized as absolute values
  → Rigidity; inability to handle exceptional cases outside rules

GOULDNER'S PATTERNS OF BUREAUCRACY:
  Mock bureaucracy: rules exist but neither management nor workers enforce them
    → Visible form with no behavioral substance
  Representative bureaucracy: both sides agree rules serve everyone's interests
    → Cooperative; functional
  Punishment-centered bureaucracy: one side imposes rules the other resists
    → Subversion, resentment, conflict
  → "Bureaucracy" is not one thing — actual organizational practices vary
     from formal rules enormously
```

## Scientific Management (Taylorism)

Frederick Winslow Taylor's system for rationalizing manual work, published 1911.

```
SCIENTIFIC MANAGEMENT PRINCIPLES (Taylor):

1. Replace rule-of-thumb methods with scientific study of each task
   → Time and motion studies: decompose every movement; find the "one best way"
   → Remove worker discretion over how to do the work

2. Scientifically select and train workers
   → Not any worker for any job; match person to task scientifically

3. Separate planning from execution
   → Management plans; workers execute
   → "The workman who is best suited to actually doing the work is incapable of
      fully understanding it" (Taylor)

4. Cooperation between management and workers via financial incentives
   → Piece-rate pay or differential rates for exceeding standard
   → "Fair day's pay for a fair day's work" defined scientifically

RESULTS AND SPREAD:
  Applied in: steel mills (Bethlehem Steel), Ford assembly line
  Ford's moving assembly line (1913) + Taylorist job design = mass production
  → Productivity increased dramatically; wages increased (Ford paid $5/day)
  → Deskilling: work decomposed into simple repetitive tasks → lower skill needed
  → Loss of craft knowledge from workers to management

CRITIQUE:
  Dehumanizing: treats workers as machines; removes creativity, discretion
  Often inefficient in practice: soldiering (work-to-rule) as resistance
  Assumes tasks can be fully specified — fails for complex, variable work
  Taylor's own studies often fabricated or exaggerated

LEGACY:
  Mass production paradigm dominated manufacturing to 1970s
  Toyota Production System (lean manufacturing) as reaction: reintegrate skill,
  delegate problem-solving to workers → higher quality + flexibility
  Taylorism lives on: call center scripts, Amazon warehouse packing rates,
  algorithmic management (software specifies exact behavior, monitors compliance)
```

## The Hawthorne Effect and Informal Organization

The Hawthorne studies (1924–1933) at Western Electric's Hawthorne Works in Chicago fundamentally changed management theory.

```
HAWTHORNE STUDIES — HISTORY AND FINDINGS:

Original premise (1924): engineers tested whether lighting affected productivity
  Result: productivity went up regardless of whether lighting increased or decreased
  → Concluded: workers responded to observation, not lighting change

Relay Assembly Test Room (1927–1932, Elton Mayo/Roethlisberger):
  6 women isolated; tested effects of rest breaks, hours, lighting, temperature
  Productivity increased through almost all changes including reversals
  Interpretation (Mayo): women responded to:
  • Special attention from researchers
  • Group cohesion (they talked, developed friendships)
  • Sense of participation and autonomy
  → "Human relations" factors matter as much or more than physical conditions

Bank Wiring Observation Room:
  Male workers wiring telephone equipment — NO manipulation of conditions
  Finding: group enforced an informal output norm
    → Workers deliberately limited production to "a day's work" (below what they could)
    → To prevent management from raising rate / dismissing workers
  → INFORMAL ORGANIZATION with its own norms, sanctions, and leadership
    → Not captured by formal organizational chart

HAWTHORNE EFFECT (as commonly understood):
  Subjects change behavior because they are being observed
  → Research artifact; but also genuine organizational phenomenon
  → Management attention itself → productivity change

CRITIQUES:
  The original findings were poorly documented and probably exaggerated
  Subsequent reanalysis: weather effects, production incentives, personal changes
    among workers explain results without invoking "human relations" effect
  Mayo was paternalistic and anti-union
  → Historical importance remains despite methodological problems:
    it redirected management theory from pure Taylorism toward social factors
```

## New Institutionalism: Why Organizations Look Alike

Paul DiMaggio and Walter Powell (1983): "The Iron Cage Revisited: Institutional Isomorphism and Collective Rationality in Organizational Fields."

```
ISOMORPHISM: organizations in the same field come to resemble each other
             NOT because it's efficient, but because it's legitimate

ORGANIZATIONAL FIELD: set of organizations that constitute a recognized area
  of institutional life — similar organizations, suppliers, consumers, regulators
  Examples: US hospitals, US research universities, US semiconductor fabs

THREE MECHANISMS OF ISOMORPHIC PRESSURE:

1. COERCIVE ISOMORPHISM
   Formal and informal pressure from organizations on which they are dependent
   + cultural expectations from the broader society

   Examples:
   • Regulatory requirements: hospitals must have IRBs; schools must have accreditation
   • Contractual requirements: suppliers must adopt quality certification (ISO 9001)
   • Legal mandates: environmental reporting, accessibility requirements
   • Government funding requirements: conditions attached to grants

   Result: organizations adopt practices because they must, not because it improves
            their core work

2. MIMETIC ISOMORPHISM
   Imitation under uncertainty: when goals are ambiguous or environment is uncertain,
   organizations copy successful (legitimate) others

   Examples:
   • Consulting firms spread "best practices" across client organizations
   • Business schools teach the same organizational models globally
   • Technology startups all adopt similar org structures (flat hierarchy, OKRs)
   • After Harvard Business School case studies, strategies spread
   → "Nobody ever got fired for buying IBM"

   Result: practices spread because they're legitimate templates, not because they work

3. NORMATIVE ISOMORPHISM
   Professional networks spread standardized practices and norms

   Two bases:
   • Education: universities socialize professionals into common frameworks
     (MBAs learn same org theory; MDs learn same clinical protocols)
   • Professional associations: define standards, license practitioners, define ethics
     (ABA, AMA, CPA, engineering boards)

   Examples:
   • Hiring from same elite schools → similar cognitive frameworks and norms
   • Professional standards (accounting GAAP, medical protocols)
   • Shared professional ideology (MBA → shareholder value maximization)

PREDICTION: isomorphism increases as:
  Fields become more institutionalized
  Organizations have more dependence on external resources/legitimacy
  Organizations have more contact with other organizations in field

WHY THIS MATTERS:
  Organizations in same field should look alike and adopt similar practices
  REGARDLESS of whether those practices improve performance
  → Isomorphism is about institutional conformity, not competitive efficiency
  → Explains: why university departments all have similar structures;
               why hospitals all look similar; why tech companies all have
               similar culture/org despite radically different products
```

### Decoupling

```
DECOUPLING (Meyer and Rowan, 1977):
  Organizations often formally adopt practices that they don't actually use
  → Surface compliance with institutional expectations
  → Actual operations follow different logic

Why decoupling occurs:
  Institutional requirements (what legitimacy demands) ≠ technical requirements
    (what actually produces results)
  Tight coupling would reveal discrepancies → undermine legitimacy
  Loose coupling (decoupling) allows both to coexist

Examples:
  Schools adopt formal curriculum standards but teachers teach as they always have
  Companies adopt "DEI initiatives" for legitimacy without changing hiring practices
  Hospitals create patient experience committees while actual care patterns unchanged
  Manufacturing firms get ISO 9001 certification without fully implementing QMS

CONSEQUENCES:
  Isomorphism + decoupling → organizations look identical on paper but vary widely
    in actual practice
  This is why: simply mandating adoption of best practices often doesn't work;
               enforcement and accountability mechanisms needed
```

## Organizational Ecology

Population ecology of organizations (Hannan and Freeman, 1977): treat organizations like species in niches.

```
ORGANIZATIONAL ECOLOGY FRAMEWORK:

NICHE: the set of resource conditions that support organizational survival
  General niche: wide range of environments (generalists)
  Specialist niche: narrow, specific environment (specialists)

DENSITY DEPENDENCE:
  At low density: new organizations face high failure rates (legitimacy gap)
                   → founding rates low; mortality high
  As density rises: legitimacy increases → founding rates rise; mortality falls
  At high density: competition increases → resources contested → mortality rises again
  → Inverted-U shape for founding rates; U-shape for mortality

STRUCTURAL INERTIA:
  Organizations have difficulty changing rapidly because:
  • Internal: sunk costs, vested interests, coordination costs
  • External: stakeholders value reliability and accountability
  → Selection pressure acts on existing organizational forms
  → Change by selection (new forms replace old) more than by adaptation

LIABILITY OF NEWNESS:
  New organizations fail at higher rates than old ones
  → Establishing trust, routines, inter-organizational relationships takes time
  → "Liability of newness" — early period of maximum vulnerability

LIABILITY OF AGING:
  Some evidence: very old organizations accumulate rigidity, "dead wood"
  → Optimal form is moderate age (past initial vulnerability; not yet sclerotic)

APPLICATIONS:
  Explains: why many new organizations fail quickly (newspaper industry collapse)
             why incumbent large organizations survive market shocks
             why certain organizational forms proliferate in specific contexts
             why isomorphism (from institutional theory) has ecological analogs
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Weber's bureaucracy is an ideal type, not a description of any real organization**
Weber abstracted the defining features of bureaucratic authority for analytical purposes. No actual organization perfectly instantiates all features. The value is comparative — how much does this organization deviate from the ideal type, in what direction, with what consequences?

**Scientific management was more ideological than scientific**
Taylor's "scientific" claims rested on timed experiments that he often staged or misrepresented. The "one best way" was almost never actually derived scientifically; it was often imposed by management authority. The system worked in the sense that it enabled control and productivity extraction from deskilled workers — but the "science" label was legitimating rhetoric.

**Institutional isomorphism predicts similarity, not efficiency**
The key claim of DiMaggio/Powell is that fields tend toward homogeneity NOT because the dominant form is most efficient but because it is most legitimate. This is empirically testable: if efficiency drove similarity, we'd expect the most similar organizations to be highest-performing. In many fields, the variance in performance doesn't track variance in form at all.

**Decoupling is a stability mechanism, not just hypocrisy**
Organizations decouple for adaptive reasons: institutional requirements and technical requirements genuinely conflict. A school that perfectly implemented state testing standards might alienate teachers and harm actual learning. The gap between formal structure and actual operation is functional in many cases, not simply corrupt.

**The Hawthorne effect in popular use is overstated and poorly defined**
The original studies were poorly designed and the "effect" may not exist as typically described. What does exist: (1) observer effects (behavior changes when watched — this is real), (2) group dynamics and informal norms affect output (real), (3) worker social needs affect motivation (real). The precise mechanism claimed by Mayo and Roethlisberger was largely their interpretation, not a clear experimental finding.
