# Sociology — Overview

## The Big Picture

Sociology is the systematic study of **social structure and collective behavior** — patterns, institutions, and forces that shape human action at the group level. The fundamental claim: individual behavior cannot be fully explained without the social context that produces it.

```
LEVELS OF SOCIOLOGICAL ANALYSIS
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  MACRO-SOCIOLOGY: societies, global systems, long historical change  │
│  Units: nation-states, world-systems, civilizations, class formations│
│  Questions: How do societies industrialize? Why does inequality      │
│  reproduce across generations? How does globalization reshape        │
│  labor? What explains variation in democracy across societies?       │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  MESO-SOCIOLOGY: organizations, communities, social movements        │
│  Units: firms, schools, churches, neighborhoods, political parties   │
│  Questions: Why do organizations resist change? How do norms spread  │
│  through institutions? What makes social movements succeed or fail?  │
│  How do neighborhoods shape life outcomes?                           │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  MICRO-SOCIOLOGY: face-to-face interaction, everyday life            │
│  Units: dyads, small groups, encounters, conversations               │
│  Questions: How do people manage identity in encounters? How is      │
│  meaning constructed in talk? What are the micro-foundations of      │
│  social order and trust? How does stigma operate in interaction?     │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Sociology vs. Psychology:
  Psychology: Why does THIS person behave this way?
  Sociology: Why do people in THIS STRUCTURAL POSITION systematically
             behave this way? — unit of analysis is structural position, not
             individual psychology
```

## The Founding Triumvirate

Three 19th-century thinkers established the core theoretical tensions that organize the discipline today.

### Émile Durkheim (1858–1917)

```
Core claim: Social facts are real, external, coercive — they exist outside
           individual minds and constrain individual behavior

"Social fact": suicide rates, marriage rates, religious observance, crime rates,
  division of labor — patterns that persist regardless of individual variation

Key methodological move: treat social phenomena as THINGS to be explained
  empirically, like natural scientists explain natural phenomena

Suicide (1897) — the paradigm case of sociological method:
  Suicide seems maximally individual → yet varies systematically by:
  Protestant vs Catholic, married vs single, peacetime vs war
  → explained by social integration and moral regulation, not individual pathology

  ┌────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    │  LOW REGULATION     HIGH REGULATION    │
  ├────────────────────┼────────────────────┬───────────────────┤
  │  HIGH INTEGRATION  │  Altruistic         │  Fatalistic       │
  │  LOW INTEGRATION   │  Egoistic           │  Anomic           │
  └────────────────────┴────────────────────┴───────────────────┘

Division of Labor: pre-industrial = mechanical solidarity (shared beliefs)
                   modern = organic solidarity (interdependence of specialists)
Anomie: normlessness during rapid social change → disorientation, deviance

Legacy: functionalism, social statistics, quantitative sociology
```

### Max Weber (1864–1920)

```
Core claim: Social action is meaningful; understanding requires interpreting
           the subjective meanings actors attach, not just measuring outcomes

Verstehen (interpretive understanding): causally adequate + meaningfully adequate
  → must explain what an action means to the actor, not just that it occurred

Types of social action:
  Zweckrational: instrumental-rational (means to calculated ends) — dominates modernity
  Wertrational: value-rational (action from principle regardless of consequences)
  Affective: emotionally driven action
  Traditional: habitual, customary action

Rationalization: the historical sweep replacing tradition/magic/emotion with
  formal, calculable, rule-bound logic
  → Bureaucracy: the ideal-typical rational-legal organization
     - Impersonality  - Hierarchy  - Written rules  - Trained specialists
     - Full-time salaried officials  - File-based administration
  → The "iron cage" (stahlhartes Gehäuse): rationalization imprisons us in
    its own logic — efficient but meaning-destroying

Types of legitimate authority:
  Traditional: "we've always done it" — kings, elders, custom
  Charismatic: "follow this person's exceptional qualities" — prophets, demagogues
  Rational-legal: "follow the office, not the person" — bureaucracies, modern states

Protestant Ethic (1904): Calvinist predestination → anxiety about salvation →
  worldly calling as sign of grace → hard work + frugality → reinvestment of
  surplus → capital accumulation → capitalism's distinctive spirit
  (Not causation claim; "elective affinity" between doctrine and economic behavior)

Legacy: interpretive sociology, organizational theory, comparative-historical method
```

### Karl Marx (1818–1883)

```
Core claim: Social structure is determined by economic relations of production;
           ideas and institutions serve to maintain the dominant class

Historical materialism:
  BASE (mode of production) ─────────────────► SUPERSTRUCTURE
  Forces of production:                         Law, politics, religion,
    technology, skills, labor,                  culture, ideology, education
    natural resources                           → these appear autonomous but
  Relations of production:                        reflect and reproduce the
    who owns the means of production              class interests of those
    → defines class structure                     who control the base

Classes defined by relationship to means of production:
  Bourgeoisie: own factories, land, capital
  Proletariat: own only labor-power; must sell it or starve

Class conflict: "History of all hitherto existing society is the history of
  class struggles" — slavery, feudalism, capitalism, (communism)

Alienation: workers separated from product (don't own what they make),
  process (controlled, not creative), species-being (human potential thwarted),
  other workers (competition) → commodity fetishism (social relations appear
  as relations between things)

Ideology: "ruling ideas of every age are the ideas of the ruling class"
  → naturalization of historically contingent arrangements as inevitable,
    eternal, universal

Legacy: conflict theory, critical theory, political economy, labor studies
```

## Major Theoretical Traditions

```
STRUCTURAL FUNCTIONALISM (Durkheim → Parsons → Merton)
  ├── Society as organism: institutions exist because they serve functions
  ├── Parsons' AGIL: Adaptation / Goal-attainment / Integration / Latency
  ├── Merton: manifest functions (intended) vs latent functions (unintended)
  │           e.g., rain dance → latent function: community solidarity
  └── Critique: conservative bias; existing institutions treated as functional
                by definition; change is "dysfunction"

CONFLICT THEORY (Marx → C. Wright Mills → Dahrendorf → Collins)
  ├── Society as arena of competing interests across unequal power
  ├── Institutions serve dominant groups, not "society as whole"
  ├── C. Wright Mills (1956): power elite — military-industrial-political complex
  │   → top positions in military, corporate, government are interlocking
  └── Critique: overpredicts conflict; explains social order less well

SYMBOLIC INTERACTIONISM (Mead → Blumer → Goffman → ethnomethodology)
  ├── Society constituted through face-to-face interaction and shared symbols
  ├── Mead: self emerges through social interaction — "I" (acting self) + "me"
  │          (reflected self seen through eyes of others)
  ├── Blumer: meaning arises in interaction, not pre-given; people act on meanings
  ├── Goffman: dramaturgical model — social life as theatrical performance
  │   front stage: behavior for audience  |  back stage: relaxed, authentic
  │   impression management: controlling others' perception
  └── Critique: ignores macro structures; too micro-focused; can't explain inequality

BOURDIEU'S FIELD THEORY (synthesis of Marx + Weber + Durkheim)
  ├── Capital (multiple forms):
  │   Economic: money, property, financial assets
  │   Social: networks, connections, obligations
  │   Cultural: credentials, taste, knowledge (institutionalized, embodied, objectified)
  │   Symbolic: legitimated form of other capitals — recognized authority
  ├── Habitus: internalized, durable dispositions generated by position in field
  │   → "feel for the game" — unreflective, embodied — reproduces class position
  ├── Field: structured arena of competition with its own rules and stakes
  │   each field (academic, artistic, economic, political) has its own logic
  └── Reproduction: habitus × field × capital → social class reproduces itself

POST-STRUCTURALISM / FOUCAULT
  ├── Power/knowledge nexus: power and knowledge are inseparable
  │   disciplines produce subjects (criminals, madmen, students, patients)
  ├── Discourse: determines what can be thought/said/known in a domain
  ├── Genealogy: history of the present — show contingency of what seems natural
  ├── Panopticon: internalized surveillance → self-discipline → power without
  │   constant application of force
  └── Critique: unfalsifiability; difficult to operationalize; political embedding

FEMINIST AND INTERSECTIONAL SOCIOLOGY
  ├── Gender as social construction layered on biological sex
  ├── Standpoint epistemology (Hartsock, Collins): knowledge is situated
  │   — marginalized standpoints reveal what dominant views naturalize
  └── Intersectionality (Kimberlé Crenshaw, 1989): race × class × gender ×
      sexuality interact multiplicatively, not additively
      → Black women's discrimination ≠ race discrimination + sex discrimination
         it has a unique form not captured by either alone
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Sociology is not social work**
Sociology is an empirical academic discipline — it describes and explains social patterns. Social work is a professional practice. The boundary between explanation and critique has always been contested within sociology (value-free vs critical sociology), but the discipline is defined by systematic inquiry, not advocacy.

**Structure vs. agency tension is genuinely unresolved**
The deepest theoretical problem: are individuals shaped by structures (class, institutions, culture) or do they make meaningful choices that constitute structures? The sophisticated answer is both, interactively. Bourdieu's habitus-field framework and Giddens' structuration theory both attempt this synthesis — but every theory tends to overweight one side.

**Durkheim's suicide study is methodologically problematic**
His category boundaries were retrospective constructs applied to inconsistent historical records. His causal attributions required many unjustified steps. The study is important as a methodological manifesto (treat social facts as things, use statistics comparatively) — not as a reliable causal finding about suicide.

**"Social construction" does not mean "not real"**
Race is socially constructed — the categories are human-made, shift historically, and have no clean biological basis. And yet race is powerfully real in its consequences: life expectancy, wealth, incarceration, healthcare quality. The Thomas theorem is precise: consequences are real whether or not the definition is biologically grounded.

**Weber's bureaucracy is an ideal type, not a real organization**
Weber's bureaucracy is an analytical construct — a pure form against which real organizations can be compared. No real organization is a perfect bureaucracy. Weber used ideal types as heuristic tools for comparison, not descriptions of actual institutions. Real bureaucracies always have informal networks, personality-based authority, and rule-bending that the ideal type excludes.
