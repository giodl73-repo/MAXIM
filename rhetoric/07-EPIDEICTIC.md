# Epideictic Rhetoric

## The Big Picture

Epideictic rhetoric is the genre of praise and blame — the rhetoric of ceremonies, memorials, commemorations, and celebrations. It was the least studied of the three classical genres (Aristotle devoted less space to it than to deliberative or forensic) but it is the most frequent genre in professional and civic life. Every eulogy, commencement address, award presentation, team celebration, and organizational All-Hands operates in the epideictic mode.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|          EPIDEICTIC RHETORIC — LANDSCAPE                         |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  THE CLASSICAL DEFINITION                                        |
|  Genre: praise and blame                                         |
|  Time orientation: PRESENT (what this person/thing IS NOW)      |
|  But: draws past for evidence, points to future as implication  |
|  Audience: spectators/witnesses, not decision-makers            |

|  - External conference keynotes                                  |

```

## Layer 1: The Theory of Epideictic

### What Epideictic Actually Does

Aristotle's treatment of epideictic was brief and has been misread as merely ceremonial. Modern rhetoricians (especially Chaïm Perelman in *The New Rhetoric*, 1958) rehabilitated epideictic as the most fundamentally rhetorical genre:

```
PERELMAN ON EPIDEICTIC — WHAT IT REALLY DOES
----------------------------------------------
Epideictic is not merely decorative or ceremonial.
Its function: INTENSIFYING ADHERENCE TO VALUES.

Epideictic does not argue for new values. It:
  - Makes shared values vivid and present
  - Connects those values to specific persons or events
  - Strengthens the community's commitment to values
    it already nominally holds

WHY THIS MATTERS:
  A eulogy does not argue that courage is admirable.
  The audience already believes courage is admirable.
  The eulogy connects the deceased to courage; by doing so,
  it makes courage real, present, and vivid in this
  community at this moment.

  A company all-hands does not argue that quality matters.
  It connects quality to specific team achievements,
  making the abstract value concrete and reinforcing
  the community's commitment to it.

EPIDEICTIC AND COMMUNITY:
  Epideictic rhetoric creates and maintains communities.
  The ceremony is the occasion; the epideictic speech
  is the mechanism by which shared values are performed
  and renewed.
  Without epideictic — without funerals, celebrations,
  commemorations — communities lose their sense of
  what they stand for and why.
  This is not trivial; it is constitutive.
```

### The Temporal Structure of Epideictic

```
EPIDEICTIC'S THREE-TIME STRUCTURE
------------------------------------
Classical epideictic operates in all three times:

PAST: The source of evidence and ethos
  Establish the subject's virtues through specific deeds.
  The past is not mere background — it is the proof
  that the values being celebrated are real.
  "Twenty years ago, she made the call that saved this team."
  Specificity of the past creates credibility for the present.

PRESENT: The primary locus of the genre
  The praise is of what the subject IS, not was.
  The present includes both the current state and the
  current ceremony — epideictic draws attention to itself
  as a speech, to this community gathered here now.
  "We are here today because of what she built."

FUTURE: The hinge to action
  Epideictic often ends with a turn to the future.
  This is where epideictic's hidden deliberative function
  shows: by praising certain values in the present,
  the speaker orients the community toward those values
  in the future.
  "Her work is unfinished. Let us carry it forward."
  The funeral sermon's "Go and do likewise" is this move.

TEMPLATE:
  1. Occasion-setting (why are we here? what is at stake?)
  2. Past evidence (specific deeds that prove the virtues)
  3. Present amplification (the person/thing's full meaning now)
  4. Future orientation (what this means going forward)
  5. Peroration (the final emotional and valuative statement)
```

## Layer 2: Specific Epideictic Forms

### The Eulogy

```
EULOGY STRUCTURE AND CRAFT
----------------------------
A eulogy is the most demanding epideictic form:
  - High emotional stakes (grief, loss, grief)
  - The audience is in a heightened emotional state
  - The speaker must manage their own emotion AND
    the audience's emotion
  - The speech must be memorable but not ostentatious

THE FUNDAMENTAL TASK:
  Not "praise this person." That is too abstract.
  The task: make this specific person PRESENT to this audience
  in a way that captures what was most true about them.
  The best eulogies make the deceased feel momentarily
  alive in the room.

STRUCTURAL TEMPLATE:
  1. ACKNOWLEDGMENT (30-60 seconds)
     Name the loss. Don't euphemize ("passed away,"
     "no longer with us"). The word "died" or "death"
     is honest; euphemism creates emotional distance.
     "Jane died on Tuesday. That fact is still impossible."

  2. ORIENTING DETAIL (60-90 seconds)
     One specific, vivid detail that captures the person.
     Not "she was kind" but "she kept a list of birthdays
     in a notebook and never missed one."
     The specific stands in for the universal.

  3. THE NARRATIVE (2-4 minutes)
     Two or three stories — not a chronological biography,
     but stories that embody the person's virtues.
     Each story should be complete: setup, action, meaning.
     Stories are more memorable than attributes.

  4. THE RELATIONAL TURN (60-90 seconds)
     How the audience will carry this person forward.
     Not grief but connection: "Every time you [X], you
     are continuing what she started."

  5. THE CLOSE (30-60 seconds)
     The final image or statement.
     It should be the most emotionally concentrated moment.
     Often a repetition of an image from the opening,
     now transformed by the speech.

CRAFT NOTES:
  HUMOR: Appropriate if it captures the person.
    Not nervous laughter — genuine representation.
    A laugh that is immediately followed by tears
    is the most emotionally true epideictic moment.

  BREVITY: A eulogy over 8 minutes is a burden on
    the audience. Under 5 minutes is often ideal.
    The Gettysburg Address was 272 words, 2 minutes.

  DELIVERY: Prepare so thoroughly that you can
    look at the audience, not the page.
    Your composure gives the audience permission
    to express their emotion.
    Pause at the emotional peaks — don't rush through them.
```

### The Toast

```
TOAST STRUCTURE — WEDDING/RETIREMENT/PROMOTION
------------------------------------------------
The toast is the intimate epideictic form.
Scale: one speaker, one subject, one occasion, short.
Length: 2-5 minutes maximum.

THE FUNDAMENTAL MISTAKE:
  Most toasts are lists of attributes.
  "She is smart, kind, hardworking, and a great friend."
  This is epideictic vocabulary without epideictic power.
  The audience already knows she has these attributes.

THE CORRECT APPROACH:
  One story that embodies the person's defining quality.
  Then: connect that story to the occasion being toasted.
  Then: the toast itself (raising of glasses, final line).

STRUCTURAL TEMPLATE:
  1. ESTABLISH YOUR RELATIONSHIP (15 seconds)
     Not biography — just your standing to speak.
     "I've worked with David for eight years."

  2. THE ONE STORY (60-90 seconds)
     A specific incident that captures the person.
     Beginning, middle, end. Present tense for immediacy.
     The story should make the audience nod ("that's exactly him").

  3. THE CONNECTION TO THE OCCASION (30 seconds)
     Bridge from the story to the ceremony.
     Wedding: "That's the person Emma is marrying."
     Retirement: "That's what we lose on Friday."

  4. THE TOAST (15-30 seconds)
     Direct address to the subject (shift from "he/she" to "you").
     A wish or aspiration.
     The final line that people will quote.
     Raise glass.

CRAFT NOTES:
  Avoid inside jokes that exclude most of the room.
  Avoid embarrassing the subject (unless agreed in advance
    for a roast — a different genre).
  The shift to direct address in the toast creates
  intimacy and emotional force.
  End on an up note — even retirement toasts should
  not end in melancholy.
```

### The Commencement Address

```
COMMENCEMENT ADDRESS — THE PUBLIC EPIDEICTIC FORM
---------------------------------------------------
The commencement address is epideictic at scale.
Audience: graduates, families, institution.
Occasion: transition (not just celebration but threshold).

THE PROBLEM:
  The commencement is a genre with conventions
  so thoroughly known that the audience anticipates
  every move. Breaking the convention risks confusion;
  following it risks cliché.

  The standard form:
  1. Congratulate the graduates.
  2. A story about the speaker's career.
  3. Three pieces of advice, each with a story.
  4. "The world needs you" peroration.

  This is the template that speakers must decide
  whether to follow, modify, or subvert.

DAVID FOSTER WALLACE — "THIS IS WATER" (2005):
  Kenyon College commencement. Perhaps the most
  influential recent commencement address.
  STRUCTURE: Inverts the genre.
    Opens with a joke ("water" fish parable) that is
    actually the thesis.
    Argues that education's purpose is not career
    success but conscious attention to the world.
    "It is unimaginably hard to do this, to stay
    conscious and alive in the adult world day in
    and day out."
  WHY IT WORKS: Honors the occasion while refusing
    its conventions. The audience gets something
    they didn't expect from the genre.

STEVE JOBS — STANFORD 2005:
  Three stories structure.
  Biographical, explicit.
  The third story (death and the cancer diagnosis)
  reframes the entire occasion.
  "Your time is limited, so don't waste it living
  someone else's life."
  EPIDEICTIC FUNCTION: The address performs Jobs's
    own values (follow your passion, death as teacher)
    and asks the graduates to adopt them.

WHAT MAKES A COMMENCEMENT ADDRESS WORK:
  Specificity over generality.
  A single strong idea over three weak ones.
  Honest acknowledgment of difficulty over
    empty encouragement.
  One memorable line (the only thing graduates
    will remember in 10 years).
```

### The Award Presentation and Acceptance

```
AWARD SPEECHES — PRESENTATION AND ACCEPTANCE
----------------------------------------------
Award speeches are the most common epideictic form
in professional settings.

PRESENTATION SPEECH STRUCTURE:
  1. What the award means (the values it represents)
  2. The criteria for this year's selection
  3. The recipient's specific accomplishments
     (the specific, not the general)
  4. The name of the recipient (held until last —
     this is both standard and effective; the name
     is the reveal)
  5. If presenting: welcome the recipient to the podium

COMMON ERROR: Leading with the name.
  "I'm pleased to present this award to [NAME]..."
  This sacrifices the one moment of dramatic structure
  the form allows.

ACCEPTANCE SPEECH:
  The most watched award speeches (Oscars, Nobel)
  follow recognizable patterns:
  1. Gratitude to those who made the work possible
     (establishes humility; names specific people)
  2. What the work means / why it matters
     (elevates the occasion beyond personal success)
  3. A forward-looking statement
     (the acceptance becomes a declaration of intent)

THE EPIDEICTIC FUNCTION:
  The award speech is not just recognition.
  The winner's acceptance speech defines what winning
  this award means. If a winner accepts with humor and
  grace, the award is associated with those qualities.
  If they accept with political speech, the award is
  redefined. The speech IS the value statement.

VP APPLICATION — TEAM RECOGNITION:
  WRONG: "I want to acknowledge John for his work on Q3."
  RIGHT:
    "What makes Platform work is not just technical
    skill — it's the judgment to know when to escalate
    and when to absorb. John showed that this quarter
    when [specific incident]. That's what 'Platform
    excellence' actually means. John, thank you."
  The specific story + the explicit value connection
  = epideictic that works.
```

## Layer 3: The Epideictic Occasion

### All-Hands and Organizational Addresses

```
ALL-HANDS AS EPIDEICTIC
------------------------
The organizational All-Hands is epideictic at scale.
Occasion: quarterly/annual review + community renewal.
Audience: the full organization; families; sometimes public.

THE HYBRID FORM:
  All-Hands is not purely epideictic — it contains
  deliberative elements (strategy, goals) and informational
  content (metrics, updates).
  But the epideictic frame is what gives the other
  content its significance.

STRUCTURE FOR A HIGH-IMPACT ALL-HANDS:
  1. ACKNOWLEDGMENT OF WHERE WE ARE (2-3 minutes)
     What actually happened. Not spin, not hedging.
     The audience can read dashboards; they know the numbers.
     Acknowledge the reality before moving to interpretation.

  2. INTERPRETATION — MEANING (3-5 minutes)
     Not "here's the spin" but "here's what this means."
     What do the results tell us about our strategy?
     What did we learn?
     This is the leader's primary epideictic function:
     making meaning out of shared experience.

  3. RECOGNITION — THE EPIDEICTIC CORE (3-5 minutes)
     Specific people, specific acts, specific values demonstrated.
     Not "the team did great work" (valueless generality).
     Not a list of names (laundry list, not epideictic).
     Two or three stories that embody what the organization
     stands for, illustrated by actual events.

  4. DIRECTION — THE DELIBERATIVE TURN (5-8 minutes)
     What are we doing next? Why?
     This section benefits from Toulmin structure:
     Claim + Data + Warrant, with qualifier and rebuttal.

  5. PERORATION — THE VALUES CLOSE (1-2 minutes)
     Return to the epideictic frame.
     What do we stand for? Why does this work matter?
     The close should be the most emotionally concentrated
     moment of the speech.

THE FREQUENCY PROBLEM:
  Quarterly All-Hands + monthly team meetings + weekly
  standups = epideictic fatigue.
  "I want to recognize the team's great work" said weekly
  is empty. The epideictic function works only when
  the recognition is specific, timely, and proportionate.
```

### Inaugural Addresses

```
INAUGURAL ADDRESSES — THE GENRE
---------------------------------
The inaugural address is the most studied epideictic form
in political rhetoric: it marks a threshold and
simultaneously defines the values of a new administration.

RHETORICAL SITUATION:
  Audience: mixed (supporters, opponents, world watching).
  Constraint: cannot be partisan (epideictic, not deliberative).
  Function: frame the incoming administration's values
    AND acknowledge the transition as a democratic ritual.

STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS:
  1. Acknowledge the predecessor (ethos of transition)
  2. Invoke shared national values (epideictic core)
  3. Describe the national situation honestly
  4. Articulate the administration's guiding principles
  5. Address the world / adversaries
  6. Peroration (the final memorable statement)

JFK INAUGURAL (1961):
  "The torch has been passed to a new generation."
  — Epideictic amplification: youth, energy, America's future.
  "Ask not what your country can do for you..."
  — Chiasmus as the peroration's final line.
  Notable: no specific policy proposals. Pure epideictic.
  The values-statement IS the substance.

LINCOLN SECOND INAUGURAL (1865):
  The most praised inaugural in American history.
  "With malice toward none, with charity for all..."
  — Written during the Civil War; the values-statement
    is also a policy directive (reconstruction through
    reconciliation, not retribution).
  The epideictic and deliberative are fused.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Occasion | Structure | Key Moves |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Eulogy | Acknowledgment → specific stories → relational turn → close | One vivid detail; shift from "she" to "you" at the end |
| Toast (wedding/retirement) | Relationship → one story → connection to occasion → direct address toast | Hold the person's defining quality to the story; no lists of attributes |
| Award presentation | Award's meaning → criteria → accomplishments → name (held to last) | Don't lead with the name; use the reveal |
| Award acceptance | Gratitude (specific names) → meaning of the work → forward declaration | Elevate beyond personal thanks |
| All-Hands address | Acknowledge reality → interpret meaning → specific recognition → direction → values close | Specific stories beat name-lists; acknowledge before pivoting |
| Commencement | Subvert or execute the convention with full awareness | One strong idea; one memorable line; specific over general |
| Inaugural | Transition acknowledgment → shared values → situation → principles → peroration | No policy; pure epideictic; the values statement IS the content |

## Common Confusion Points

**Epideictic is not "just ceremonial"**
Perelman's rehabilitation of epideictic is correct: it is the genre that creates and maintains communities by making values vivid and present. A eulogy that does this well does not just honor the dead — it reminds the living what they stand for. A team recognition ceremony that does this well is not HR box-checking — it is constitutive of organizational culture. The mistake is treating epideictic as less important than deliberative.

**Specificity is the key virtue**
Every epideictic occasion fails or succeeds on specificity. "She was a great colleague" is epideictic vocabulary without epideictic substance. "She stayed until 2am to help me prep for my first board presentation" is epideictic. The specific deed carries the universal virtue. Lists of attributes are easy to produce and impossible to remember; one specific story is hard to produce and impossible to forget.

**The future turn is not a cliche**
The turn from praise to future direction at the end of a eulogy or commencement address is not a rhetorical cliche — it is the hidden deliberative function of epideictic. By praising certain values in the present, the speaker creates an implicit obligation in the audience toward those values in the future. "Her work is unfinished. Let us carry it forward" is not a feel-good ending — it is a call to action dressed in epideictic form.

**Epideictic also includes blame**
Aristotle's definition is "praise and blame." Blame epideictic (vituperatio) includes the roast (humor as controlled blame), the critique, and the denunciation. The roast operates by the same structural logic as the eulogy — specific stories, the subject's defining qualities — but in the blame register, with humor as the modal marker that distinguishes friendly blame from hostile attack. The distinction: the roast affirms the relationship; the attack severs it.
