# The Five Canons of Rhetoric

## The Big Picture

The five canons are the organizational framework of the complete rhetorical task: from finding what to say (inventio) through delivering it effectively (actio). They originated in Roman rhetoric (Cicero's De Inventione, the Rhetorica ad Herennium) and remained the curriculum of rhetoric for 1,500 years. Each canon is a distinct dimension of the communication challenge.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              FIVE CANONS — REFERENCE MAP                         |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  1. INVENTIO       Finding arguments, evidence, and content     |
|     (Invention)    The topoi; Aristotle's pisteis; stasis        |
|                                                                  |
|  2. DISPOSITIO     Arranging content for maximum effect         |
|     (Arrangement)  Classical arrangement; modern structures      |
|                                                                  |
|  3. ELOCUTIO       Expressing content in words and style        |
|     (Style)        Three levels; virtues of style; figures      |
|                                                                  |
|  4. MEMORIA        Remembering what to say                      |
|     (Memory)       Method of loci (memory palace); recall tools |
|                                                                  |
|  5. ACTIO /        Delivering the speech                        |
|     PRONUNTIATIO   Voice, gesture, pacing, presence             |
|     (Delivery)                                                   |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Canon 1: Inventio — Finding Arguments

Inventio is the discovery of what there is to say. Not invention from nothing but the discovery of available arguments for this case and audience.

### The Core Tools of Inventio

```
INVENTIO TOOLBOX
-----------------
1. STASIS THEORY (finding the point at issue)
   See 02-ROMAN-RHETORIC.md.
   Before arguing, identify WHAT is actually in dispute:
   conjectural / definitional / qualitative / translative

2. THE THREE APPEALS
   Logos: what logical arguments are available?
   Ethos: what establishes my credibility for this audience?
   Pathos: what emotional stakes does this audience have?

3. THE TOPOI (places/topics)
   General lines of argument applicable across cases.
   Common topoi: opposites, degree, definition, cause/effect,
   comparison, consequence, analogy, precedent.

4. THE SPECIAL TOPOI
   Arguments specific to the type of speech:
   Deliberative: what is advantageous? what is feasible?
                 what is expedient now vs. later?
   Forensic: what are the facts? who had motive, means, opportunity?
             what is the legal definition of the act?
   Epideictic: what virtues does the person embody?
               what are their achievements? what will be their legacy?
```

### Inventio for Professional Communication

```
INVENTIO IN PRACTICE (VP-level application)
--------------------------------------------
SCENARIO: You must recommend a major infrastructure platform change
to senior leadership.

STASIS: What is the actual point of issue?
  Is it factual? (Will this new platform actually perform better?)
  Is it definitional? (What does "better" mean?)
  Is it qualitative? (Even if it performs better, is the
    migration cost worth it?)
  Is it translative? (Should this decision be made here,
    or escalated to the CTO?)
  → Identify the stasis before building the argument.

TOPOI for this case:
  Degree: "Even if current platform isn't broken, this
    improvement is significant enough to justify migration."
  Consequence: "If we don't change now, the consequences
    will be X (cost, performance, technical debt)."
  Comparison: "Competitor Y made this change and achieved Z."
  Precedent: "We successfully migrated from A to B in 2019
    using a similar approach."
  Analogy: "This is analogous to the database consolidation
    project — similar risk profile, similar reward."

ETHOS considerations:
  What establishes your credibility for THIS decision?
  Your track record with similar decisions.
  The rigor of your analysis.
  Whether you have skin in the game.

PATHOS considerations:
  What does this AUDIENCE care about?
  Cost? Risk? Competitive position? Engineering retention?
  Meet them where their concerns are.
```

## Canon 2: Dispositio — Arrangement

Dispositio is the art of ordering what you have to say. The order in which arguments are presented significantly affects their persuasive force.

### Classical Arrangement

```
CLASSICAL ARRANGEMENT (Cicero, Quintilian)
------------------------------------------
1. EXORDIUM (introduction)
   Goals: establish ethos; engage the audience; introduce
   the subject; make the audience receptive (benevolus),
   attentive (attentus), and willing to follow (docilis).

   Techniques:
   - Direct (principium): state your case immediately.
     For simple cases where audience is already sympathetic.
   - Indirect (insinuatio): approach the subject obliquely
     when the audience is hostile or the case is difficult.
     Win them over before stating your position.

2. NARRATIO (statement of facts)
   Lay out the facts of the case clearly and favorably.
   Three virtues: brief, clear, plausible.
   The narratio is NOT the argument — it is the
   presentation of the situation that the argument will
   be about.

3. PARTITIO (partition)
   Optional but helpful: tell the audience what you will
   be arguing. "I will show three things: first... second...
   third..."
   Sets expectations; makes complex arguments followable.

4. CONFIRMATIO (arguments/proof)
   The substantive arguments supporting your case.
   Strongest argument: usually last (most memorable) or
   first (most impact), rarely in the middle.
   The "anticlimactic order" error: saving your best
   point for last only works if the audience is still
   listening.

5. REFUTATIO (refutation)
   Addressing the opposing arguments.
   Two approaches:
   - Anticipate objections (pre-empt them before your
     opponent raises them — shows confidence and fairness)
   - Respond to stated objections (after hearing them)
   The refutatio demonstrates thoroughness.

6. PERORATIO (conclusion)
   1. Summary: briefly restate the main arguments.
   2. Amplification (amplificatio): make the stakes vivid.
   3. Emotional appeal: the final emotional move.
      If the speech is for conviction → indignation at wrongdoing.
      If the speech is defensive → pity and sympathy.
      The conclusion's job is to leave the audience in the
      right emotional state to take the right action.
```

### Modern Arrangement Alternatives

```
MODERN ARRANGEMENT OPTIONS
----------------------------
BOTTOM-LINE UP FRONT (BLUF):
  State the conclusion first; then support.
  Start: "We should migrate to Platform X."
  Then: evidence, reasoning, objections.
  Used in: executive summaries, military/government docs,
           consulting deliverables.
  When to use: audience is time-limited; they need
               the answer first, then the reasoning.

PROBLEM-SOLUTION-BENEFIT:
  State the problem, then the solution, then the benefits.
  Classic structure for proposals and pitches.
  When to use: audience doesn't yet know there's a problem.

STORY ARC (narrative structure):
  Set up the situation → introduce the complication →
  build to a turning point → resolve.
  When to use: when emotional engagement matters;
               when the audience needs to feel the problem
               before they'll accept the solution.

INVERTED PYRAMID (journalism):
  Most important facts first; details follow.
  Each paragraph less important than the preceding.
  The reader can stop at any point and has the key info.
  When to use: written documents where readers skim.

5-MINUTE, 1-HOUR, ALL-DAY VERSION:
  Any major argument should be expressible at three granularities.
  The 5-minute version: headline + 3 key points.
  The 1-hour version: full classical arrangement.
  The all-day version: appendices, Q&A, workshop.
  A common error: giving the all-day version when
  the audience only has 5 minutes.
```

## Canon 3: Elocutio — Style

Elocutio is how to say it: word choice, sentence structure, and figures of speech. Full treatment of figures in 04-FIGURES-OF-SPEECH.md.

```
THE VIRTUES OF STYLE (Theophrastus, adopted by Cicero)
--------------------------------------------------------
1. GRAMMATICALITY (latinitas/hellenismos)
   The words must be correctly formed and used.
   The foundation; without this, nothing else matters.

2. CLARITY (perspicuitas)
   The audience must understand what you mean.
   Not just technically correct but intelligible.
   Enemy: unnecessary abstraction, jargon (unless appropriate),
   ambiguity (unless deliberate).

3. ORNAMENTATION (ornatus)
   The figures: metaphor, anaphora, chiasmus, etc.
   Not decoration but enhancement of force and memorability.
   See 04-FIGURES-OF-SPEECH.md.

4. PROPRIETY (aptum/decorum)
   Fitness to the occasion, audience, and purpose.
   The style must be appropriate (decorum):
     Grand occasion → grand style
     Intimate occasion → plain style
     Mixed → middle style
   The most technically accomplished speech can fail
   through inappropriate style (too grand for a
   casual team meeting; too casual for a board presentation).

THE THREE STYLES:
  PLAIN (genus tenue):   Simple, clear, direct.
  MIDDLE (genus medium): Moderate ornament; varied; pleasant.
  GRAND (genus grande):  Full periodic sentences; figures; emotion.
```

## Canon 4: Memoria — Memory

The memory canon is less prominent in modern practice (we have notes) but the techniques developed in this tradition remain the most effective memory systems known.

### The Method of Loci (Memory Palace)

```
METHOD OF LOCI (MEMORY PALACE)
--------------------------------
Origin: Attributed to Simonides of Ceos (c. 556-468 BCE).
The story: Simonides was called out of a banquet;
  the roof collapsed, killing everyone inside and
  crushing the bodies beyond recognition.
  Simonides recalled where each person had been sitting —
  the spatial memory was perfect when the visual/face
  memory failed.
  Insight: spatial memory is strong; anchor content to it.

THE TECHNIQUE:
  1. Select a familiar location with a clear route
     (your home; a building you know well; a walk route).
  2. Place vivid, memorable images at specific loci
     (locations along the route) representing the items
     to remember.
  3. To recall: mentally walk the route; "see" the images
     at each locus; decode them back into content.

REQUIREMENTS FOR THE IMAGES:
  Vivid, unusual, emotionally striking (not neutral).
  Concrete and visual (not abstract concepts directly).
  Active: doing something (not just present).
  Absurd or surprising: the more unexpected, the better recalled.

EXAMPLE:
  To remember: budget, team, platform, deadline, risk
  Route: front door → hallway → kitchen → living room → bedroom
  Images:
    Front door: a huge COIN is wedged in the door (budget)
    Hallway: your TEAM is having a soccer game in the hall
    Kitchen: a PLATFORM (like a stage) in the middle of the floor
    Living room: a CLOCK melting off the wall (deadline)
    Bedroom: a LANDMINE on the pillow (risk)
  Walk through mentally → see each image → decode.

EFFECTIVENESS:
  World memory champions use this technique.
  The "24 decks of cards in sequence" record uses method of loci.
  It works because spatial-visual memory is evolutionarily
  ancient and robust; declarative memory is newer and weaker.

RHETORICAL MEMORIA:
  In classical rhetoric, memoria meant memorizing speeches.
  The memory palace allowed a speaker to deliver a long speech
  without notes while appearing natural and authoritative.
  Notes were considered a weakness (the speaker doesn't know
  their own material).
  Modern application: the ability to speak without slides/notes
  is still regarded as higher authority than reading from a screen.
```

## Canon 5: Actio/Pronuntiatio — Delivery

Delivery is the physical presentation of the speech: voice, gesture, movement, pacing. Demosthenes, when asked what the most important thing in rhetoric was, allegedly replied: "Delivery, delivery, delivery."

```
DELIVERY — COMPONENTS
----------------------
VOICE (vox):
  Volume: audible to the entire audience; varied for emphasis.
  Pitch: varies with emotion; monotone loses audience.
  Rate: varied; pauses create emphasis; too fast = incomprehensible;
    too slow = loses audience.
  Articulation: clear; every syllable formed.
  Resonance: the quality of the voice (resonant voices command
    more authority than thin or nasal voices).

  The PAUSE is the most powerful voice tool:
    Before an important point: creates anticipation.
    After an important point: allows it to land.
    A speaker who never pauses signals nervousness.
    A speaker who pauses deliberately signals confidence.

GESTURE (gestus):
  Cicero (De Oratore): the hand is the second voice.
  Appropriate gesture: reinforces the word; does not
    distract from it.
  Inappropriate gesture: contradicts the word (says calm,
    looks frantic); or repetitive (the same gesture for
    everything; meaningless).
  Modern: hand gestures when visible on video or stage
    significantly increase perceived credibility and clarity.

FACIAL EXPRESSION:
  Must match the content.
  Smiling while describing disaster = disconnection.
  Blank face = authority in some contexts; coldness in others.

EYE CONTACT:
  Direct eye contact = engagement, confidence, sincerity.
  Reading from notes → loses eye contact → loses audience.
  Modern extension: on video calls, the camera IS the audience.
    Looking at the camera = looking at the audience.
    Looking at the screen (to see them) = looking away.
    The most common "distant" behavior on video calls is
    looking at the faces rather than the camera.

POSTURE AND PHYSICAL PRESENCE:
  Erect posture = confidence and authority.
  Slumped = disengagement.
  The physical occupation of space:
    Standing still (not pacing randomly) = groundedness.
    Moving with purpose (to a new position to address
    a different part of the audience) = engagement.

THE ANXIETY PROBLEM:
  Most speakers are anxious; anxiety affects delivery.
  Physical manifestations: tight voice, fast rate, shaking hands.
  Classical and modern advice converges:
    Deep breath before starting (slows rate, opens voice).
    Stand still before speaking (demonstrates control).
    Speak slowly (anxiety speeds up rate; deliberate slowing
    sounds authoritative not slow).
    Pause deliberately (pauses feel longer to the speaker
    than to the audience; what feels like an eternity
    is a natural beat to the audience).
```

### Delivery in the Digital Age

```
DELIVERY ON VIDEO AND SLIDES (modern extensions)
--------------------------------------------------
VIDEO CONFERENCE:
  Camera position: eye level; slightly above is authoritative;
    looking up at camera = diminished authority.
  Lighting: face should be lit; back wall should not
    be brighter than the face.
  Background: controlled; meaningful or neutral.
  The camera IS the audience: look at it when speaking
    to the group, not at the gallery view.

SLIDES AND PRESENTATIONS:
  Slides are a prop, not a script.
  The speaker is the rhetor; slides are visual aids.
  Reading slides to the audience = treating them as
    the speaker rather than as support.
  "Lessig style" (minimal text; one image per slide):
    forces the speaker to speak, not read.
  "Takahashi method" (very large text, few words):
    the text reinforces a single point per slide.

  THE COGNITIVE LOAD PROBLEM:
    If slides contain complex diagrams, the audience
    reads them rather than listening to you.
    Slide complexity and speaker authority trade off.
    Keep slides simple if you want attention on you.
    Share detailed documentation separately.
```

## The Five Canons in Professional Practice

```
MAPPING THE CANONS TO PROFESSIONAL COMMUNICATION
--------------------------------------------------
EXECUTIVE PRESENTATION:
  Inventio: what arguments will move THIS exec? what do they
    care about? what objections will they raise?
  Dispositio: BLUF + 3 key points + evidence + what you need
    from them
  Elocutio: clear, plain, concrete; no jargon they don't know
  Memoria: know your material; don't read from slides
  Actio: confident pacing; eye contact; pause for emphasis

WRITTEN DOCUMENT (design doc, RFC, memo):
  Inventio: what is the claim? what supports it?
    What are the objections?
  Dispositio: executive summary first; logical flow;
    appendices for detail
  Elocutio: precise technical language; clear structure;
    appropriate figures (but not too much "color")
  Memoria: n/a for written (document itself is the memory)
  Actio: n/a for written (but formatting is the typography
    of delivery — bold, headers, whitespace as pacing)

DIFFICULT CONVERSATION (firing, performance review, conflict):
  Inventio: what is the real issue? (stasis) what is the
    most important thing to say?
  Dispositio: insinuatio if the person is defensive;
    begin with acknowledgment before critique
  Elocutio: plain, direct; avoid euphemism and hedging
  Memoria: prepare the key points; don't improvise structure
  Actio: unhurried; maintained eye contact; pause after
    delivering difficult information (let it land)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want to apply... | Canon and key tool |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Find the real argument for my specific audience | Inventio: stasis + topoi + audience analysis |
| Structure my presentation/document | Dispositio: classical arrangement or BLUF |
| Choose words and style | Elocutio: virtues of style, three levels |
| Remember a speech without notes | Memoria: method of loci (memory palace) |
| Improve how I come across in person or on video | Actio: pause, eye contact, pacing, posture |

## Common Confusion Points

**Inventio is not brainstorming**
Inventio is the systematic discovery of available arguments using tools (stasis theory, topoi, the three appeals). It is more disciplined than brainstorming and more audience-specific. The topoi are heuristics for finding arguments, not a free-association exercise.

**BLUF (bottom-line up front) is not always correct**
BLUF is the right dispositio for executive communication, written documents, and time-limited audiences. But sometimes the narrative structure (problem → complication → resolution) is more effective for building buy-in — the audience needs to feel the problem before they'll accept the solution. Classical insinuatio (indirect approach) is the right structure when the audience is hostile.

**The memory palace is not a metaphor**
"Memory palace" (method of loci) is a real, testable technique with demonstrable results. Memory competitors who can remember the order of 24 shuffled decks of cards use this technique. It is not a mystical practice — it exploits the well-documented strength of human spatial-visual memory relative to declarative memory.

**Delivery is the most undervalued canon**
In academic rhetoric, inventio and elocutio receive the most attention. In professional communication, actio is often what differentiates. Two equally well-prepared speakers: the one with better delivery will be more persuasive. Cicero and Quintilian both emphasize this; modern research on communication confirms it (the voice and body affect perceived credibility significantly).
