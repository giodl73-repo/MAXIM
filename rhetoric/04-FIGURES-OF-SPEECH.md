# Figures of Speech: Schemes and Tropes

## The Big Picture

Figures of speech are departures from ordinary language use — ways of arranging words (schemes) or substituting meanings (tropes) that produce emphasis, beauty, emotional force, or memorable expression. Classical rhetoric catalogued hundreds of figures; this guide covers the most important ones with definitions, examples, and practical effects.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|         FIGURES OF SPEECH — TAXONOMY                            |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  SCHEMES (figures of arrangement/sound)                         |
|  Deviation from ordinary arrangement of words                  |
|  The FORM is changed; the basic meaning remains                 |
|  Examples: anaphora, chiasmus, asyndeton, antithesis            |
|                                                                  |
|  TROPES (figures of meaning/thought)                            |
|  Deviation from ordinary meaning of words                      |
|  The MEANING is transferred or substituted                      |
|  Examples: metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, irony                |
|                                                                  |
|  THE PRACTICAL VALUE:                                            |
|  Figures create: emphasis, memorability, emotional force        |
|  pattern recognition, rhythm, surprise                          |
|  Every memorable sentence uses at least one figure.             |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Schemes (Figures of Arrangement)

### Repetition Schemes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Anaphora | Repetition of a word/phrase at the start of successive clauses | "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields..." (Churchill) | Builds intensity; creates rhythm; signals determination |
| Epistrophe (Antistrophe) | Repetition at the END of successive clauses | "Government of the people, by the people, for the people." (Lincoln) | Creates emphasis through reinforcement; final word most stressed |
| Symploce | Anaphora + epistrophe combined | "When you call it a tax, they say no. When you explain that it's a fee, they say no." | Maximum structural emphasis |
| Anadiplosis | End of one clause = start of next | "Fear leads to anger. Anger leads to hate. Hate leads to suffering." | Creates chain of causation; momentum |
| Epanalepsis | Clause begins and ends with the same word | "The king is dead, long live the king." | Creates circular closure; the word "frames" the content |
| Diacope | Repetition of a word/phrase with a few words between | "Bond. James Bond." | Emphasis through separation and return |

### Sound and Pattern Schemes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Alliteration | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers" | Euphony; memorability; playful or portentous depending on context |
| Assonance | Repetition of vowel sounds | "The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain" | Musicality; soft connection between words |
| Homoioteleuton | Similar endings in successive phrases | "Good morning, good afternoon, good evening" | Rhythm; creates parallel structure audibly |

### Structural Schemes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Chiasmus | ABBA reversal of grammatical elements | "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country." (JFK) | Creates perfect balance; memorable; the reversal forces reflection |
| Antithesis | Contrasting ideas in parallel structure | "To err is human, to forgive divine." (Pope) | Clarifies contrast; creates balanced judgment |
| Parallelism | Same grammatical structure for parallel ideas | "I came, I saw, I conquered." (Caesar: veni, vidi, vici) | Order, clarity, momentum |
| Asyndeton | Omitting conjunctions between items | "I came, I saw, I conquered." | Speed, urgency, forward momentum; the list arrives fast |
| Polysyndeton | Repeating conjunctions between items | "And the rain descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew..." (Matthew 7) | Slowing, accumulation, biblical weight |
| Isocolon | Parallel clauses of equal length | "Veni, vidi, vici" | Compact, rhythmic, equal weight; easy to remember |

### Omission and Interruption Schemes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Ellipsis | Omission of words understood from context | "Some have greatness thrust upon them." (not "some [people] have greatness thrust [upon them]") | Compression; elegance; assumes shared understanding |
| Parenthesis | Insertion of a qualifying thought mid-sentence | "The general (who had not slept in 36 hours) ordered the advance." | Adds information while maintaining main flow |
| Aposiopesis | Sudden stopping in mid-sentence | "If you do that again, I will—" | Creates threat by omission; the imagination supplies the ending |
| Apophasis | Mentioning something by saying you won't mention it | "I won't even bring up your voting record." | Introduces the very thing denied; ironic emphasis |

## Tropes (Figures of Meaning)

### Substitution Tropes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Metaphor | Implicit comparison: A is B | "Life is a journey." "The meeting was a minefield." | Creates new understanding by mapping one domain to another; cognitive efficiency |
| Simile | Explicit comparison: A is like B | "He fought like a lion." "My love is like a red, red rose." | Softer than metaphor; keeps the comparison visible |
| Metonymy | Replace a thing with an attribute/container | "The White House announced..." (= the President) "The pen is mightier than the sword." (= writing/power) | Economy; the substitution focuses attention on the relevant attribute |
| Synecdoche | Part for whole (or whole for part) | "All hands on deck." (hands = sailors) "Nice wheels." (wheels = car) | Compression; the part selected is the relevant part |
| Antonomasia | Proper name used as a common noun (or vice versa) | "He's a real Einstein." "The Napoleon of crime." | Quick characterization through famous example |
| Catachresis | Deliberately strained or "wrong" metaphor | "The arm of the chair." "The head of the corporation." | Some are so established they're no longer felt as metaphors; conscious catachresis creates surprise |

### Relationship and Degree Tropes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Hyperbole | Deliberate exaggeration for effect | "I've told you a million times." "He's the greatest engineer in history." | Emphasis through excess; the exaggeration signals emotional intensity |
| Litotes | Understatement, usually by negating the opposite | "Not bad." (= very good) "He's not the sharpest tool in the shed." (= he's dull) | Ironic understatement; leaves gap for the audience to fill; British understatement |
| Irony | Saying the opposite of what is meant | "Oh yes, that's a brilliant plan." (= terrible plan) "He was hardly a saint." | Forces the listener to decode; creates sophistication; can be cutting |
| Oxymoron | Apparent contradiction in two adjacent terms | "Deafening silence." "Living death." "Bittersweet." | Creates tension; forces reflection; signals complexity |

### Personification and Substitution

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Personification | Attributing human qualities to non-human things | "The market fears inflation." "The code is lying to you." | Makes abstract forces graspable; evokes emotional response |
| Apostrophe | Direct address to an absent or abstract entity | "Death, be not proud." (Donne) "O Liberty, what crimes are committed in thy name!" | Elevates the addressee; creates dramatic presence; emotional intensity |
| Allegory | Extended metaphor where a whole narrative means something else | Orwell's Animal Farm (= Soviet communism) | Allows indirect communication of a message; creates protective distance |

### Question Tropes

| Figure | Definition | Example | Effect |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------|
| Erotema (Rhetorical Question) | Question posed for effect, not answer | "Is the Pope Catholic?" "Was this the face that launched a thousand ships?" | Forces the audience to supply the obvious answer; creates engagement |
| Hypophora | Speaker asks a question and answers it | "Why should we change? Because..." | Controls the objection by raising and answering it preemptively |
| Anthypophora | Imagining and responding to possible objections | "You might say... But I would answer..." | Creates dialogue; demonstrates thorough thinking |

## Power Figures — The Most Useful

For a VP-level writer and speaker, these are the highest-return figures:

### Chiasmus and Antithesis

```
CHIASMUS — THE MOST MEMORABLE STRUCTURE
-----------------------------------------
Pattern: A-B :: B-A (reversal of grammatical elements)

"Ask not what your country can do for you
— ask what you can do for your country."
A = your country    B = you
→
B' = you             A' = your country

The reversal forces the listener to hold both
versions simultaneously; the second gains power
from its relationship to the first.

ANTITHESIS — THE CLEAREST CONTRAST
  "To err is human, to forgive divine."
  "Better to be roughly right than precisely wrong."
  "We observe today not a victory of party, but a
  celebration of freedom."

  The parallel structure makes the contrast visible.
  Antithesis clarifies judgment by presenting both sides
  in a form that emphasizes their difference.

LEADERSHIP APPLICATION:
  Chiasmus and antithesis are the two most effective
  structures for making a memorable strategic statement.
  "The question is not can we do this; it is
  should we do this" — chiasmus applied to a decision.
  "Fast is slow; slow is fast" — antithesis applied
  to speed vs. quality in software development.
```

### Anaphora

```
ANAPHORA — BUILDING THROUGH REPETITION
---------------------------------------
The repetition of the opening word/phrase across
successive units builds cumulative force.

Churchill at his most famous:
"We shall fight on the beaches,
we shall fight on the landing grounds,
we shall fight in the fields and in the streets,
we shall fight in the hills;
we shall never surrender."

Each unit is independent; the anaphora holds them together.
The first repetition: emphasis.
The second: accumulation.
The third: the audience is now predicting the next unit.
The final break ("never surrender"): the anaphora ends;
the climax arrives.

MARTIN LUTHER KING JR. — "I Have a Dream":
Eight consecutive "I have a dream" clauses.
The repetition does not tire — it intensifies.
Each clause is a new instance of the dream.
The accumulation is the argument.

APPLICATION:
  Anaphora works best for three or more units.
  Two units = antithesis, not anaphora.
  Four to eight units = maximum impact.
  The final unit should break the pattern or
  complete it with special force.
```

### Metaphor

```
METAPHOR — THE ENGINE OF THOUGHT
----------------------------------
Metaphor is not decoration. Cognitive linguists
(Lakoff and Johnson, "Metaphors We Live By," 1980)
argue that conceptual metaphors structure thought itself.

CONCEPTUAL METAPHOR:
  ARGUMENT IS WAR:
  "He attacked my position."
  "She defended her thesis."
  "His argument was demolished."
  "I've never won an argument with her."

  We don't just talk about arguments as war;
  we THINK about them that way. The metaphor
  shapes what aspects of argument we notice.

  ARGUMENT IS CONSTRUCTION (alternative):
  "The foundations of his argument are weak."
  "He built a strong case."
  "The structure of the argument is sound."

  Different metaphors highlight different aspects.
  The metaphor you choose is a rhetorical choice.

FRAMING IMPLICATIONS:
  "Technical debt" = accumulated obligation (financial metaphor).
  The metaphor highlights: it must be paid back;
  it accrues interest; it is a liability.
  Alternative: "Technical artifacts of prior decisions."
  Completely different framing; different implications.
  See 06-POLITICAL-RHETORIC.md for framing as a political tool.
```

## Figures in Technical and Professional Prose

Classical figures are not ornamental in technical prose — several map directly onto professional writing patterns that engineers and executives use without naming them.

```
FIGURES THAT WORK IN TECHNICAL WRITING

ANTITHESIS (juxtaposition of contrasting ideas in parallel structure):
  Technical use: tradeoff framing in design docs and exec memos.
  "Fast or correct — this architecture chooses correct."
  "We could optimize for throughput or for tail latency.
   This proposal optimizes for tail latency."
  The parallel structure makes the tradeoff explicit and the
  decision legible. Most design docs that bury tradeoffs in prose
  would be clearer as antithesis.

LITOTES (understatement via negation of the contrary):
  Technical use: hedged claims in technical communication.
  "This approach is not without risk." = (the risk exists but is bounded)
  "The performance improvement is not negligible." = (substantial gain)
  In engineering communication, litotes signals appropriate epistemic
  humility while still asserting a positive claim. It is the register
  of "we've done the analysis but acknowledge uncertainty."
  Overuse → weaseling. Calibrated use → credibility.

HYPOPHORA (raising a question then immediately answering it):
  Technical use: FAQ-style sections; preemptive objection handling.
  "Why not use gRPC instead of REST?
   gRPC requires binary compatibility between client and server.
   For public APIs, REST's negotiability is more important."
  The hypophora format puts the objection explicitly on the page
  rather than hoping the reader won't think of it.
  Standard in RFC-style documents for good reason.

ANAPHORA (repetition of a word/phrase at the start of successive clauses):
  Technical use: enumerated architectural principles, strategic pillars.
  "Simplicity first. Simplicity in the API contract.
   Simplicity in the deployment model. Simplicity in the error messages."
  Creates emphasis through repetition. Works in executive presentations
  and strategy memos where the repeated term is the organizing principle.

ASYNDETON (omission of conjunctions for compactness and speed):
  Technical use: high-density summaries, bullet points, log messages.
  "Read the spec. Write the test. Fix the code. Ship."
  Engineering culture favors asyndeton: shorter, faster, imperative.
  The pace mirrors the operational rhythm.

RHETORICAL QUESTION (hypophora without an immediate answer):
  Technical use: structuring an argument doc around a question.
  "What should our consistency model be?"
  If used as a section header, it invites the reader into the analysis.
  If used at the start of a document, it frames the entire argument.
```

## Common Confusions Among Figures

```
MOST-CONFUSED PAIRS
--------------------
METAPHOR vs. SIMILE:
  Metaphor: "Life is a journey." (IS — no comparison word)
  Simile: "Life is like a journey." (LIKE — explicit comparison)
  Simile keeps the comparison visible; metaphor asserts identity.

METONYMY vs. SYNECDOCHE:
  Metonymy: replace with an attribute or container.
    "The Crown declared war." (the Crown = the king/state)
    "Hollywood hasn't released anything good." (= film industry)
  Synecdoche: part for whole (or whole for part).
    "All hands on deck." (hands = sailors)
    "Nice wheels." (wheels = car)
  The distinction: metonymy is associated-attribute; synecdoche
  is part-of. Both are substitution tropes. The distinction
  matters in linguistic analysis but not always in practice.

SCHEME vs. TROPE:
  Scheme: the FORM of language is altered (arrangement, repetition,
    omission); the meaning is not fundamentally changed.
  Trope: the MEANING of language is altered (substitution,
    comparison, extension); the form may be normal.
  Chiasmus = scheme (reversal of structure, same meaning).
  Metaphor = trope (substitution of meaning, normal structure).

IRONY vs. SARCASM:
  Irony: saying the opposite of what you mean, without necessarily
    a hostile intent. "That went well." (after a disaster, to
    a friend who understands) — gentle, shared.
  Sarcasm: irony with hostile, cutting intent. "Oh, great job."
    (to someone who failed, to mock them).
  Irony is a broad category; sarcasm is aggressive irony.

HYPERBOLE vs. LITOTES:
  Hyperbole: exaggeration. "I've told you a thousand times."
  Litotes: understatement by negating the opposite.
    "It's not rocket science." "She's not unattractive."
  Litotes underemphasizes; hyperbole overemphasizes.
  Both distort from the literal; in opposite directions.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Figures are not decoration**
The classical tradition was clear: figures are functional, not ornamental. Anaphora doesn't just make a speech sound nice — it builds cumulative force that flat syntax cannot achieve. Metaphor doesn't just enliven; it creates conceptual structure (Lakoff and Johnson). Using figures is not "dressing up" your language; it is choosing the right tool for the communication task.

**You already use figures constantly**
"That meeting was a disaster." (metaphor) "We need all hands on this." (synecdoche) "She's not bad at debugging." (litotes) "This is the third time I've said this!" (hyperbole). The question is not whether to use figures but whether to use them intentionally and effectively.

**Overuse is as bad as non-use**
A speech with an anaphora every sentence is exhausting. A metaphor mixed with another metaphor becomes confused ("We need to run the playbook on this minefield" — athletic + warfare metaphors clashing). The figures are most effective when they arrive at the moments of maximum purpose, against a background of plainer expression.
