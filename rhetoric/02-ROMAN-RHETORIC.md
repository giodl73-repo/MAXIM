# Roman Rhetoric

## The Big Picture

Roman rhetoric is Greek rhetoric institutionalized, expanded, and systematized for a society in which public speaking was the path to political power. Cicero and Quintilian are not just important figures in rhetorical history — they are the defining voices of Western humanist education for 1,500 years.

## Layer 1: Cicero — The Ideal Orator

Cicero (106–43 BCE) is the most important figure in Latin literature and the greatest Roman orator. His rhetorical theory is developed across multiple works; the mature synthesis is *De Oratore* (55 BCE).

### De Oratore — The Ideal Orator

```
DE ORATORE (On the Orator, 55 BCE)
------------------------------------
A dialogue set in 91 BCE at Crassus's villa.
Participants: Crassus (Cicero's model), Antonius, others.
Format: the dialogue form allows Cicero to present
  multiple positions without committing to one — very
  Platonic in structure; un-Platonic in content.

THE CENTRAL QUESTION:
  What is the ideal orator?
  The dialogue is a negotiation between:
    Crassus: the ideal orator must have BROAD PHILOSOPHICAL KNOWLEDGE.
    Antonius: practical skill and experience are sufficient.

  Cicero (via Crassus) wins: the ideal orator combines
  philosophical wisdom (to know the truth) with rhetorical
  skill (to present it effectively).
  Neither the philosopher who cannot speak persuasively
  nor the clever debater without genuine knowledge is ideal.

THE IDEAL ORATOR'S QUALIFICATIONS:
  1. Knowledge of the subject (you cannot speak well about
     what you don't understand)
  2. Philosophical training (ethics, politics, psychology)
  3. Command of all three styles (plain/middle/grand)
  4. Mastery of all five canons
  5. Natural talent + training + practice

CICERO ON ELOQUENCE:
  "Sed quid ego de utilitate loquor? Etiam si summo studio
  colitur [eloquentia], tamen...pauci hunc cursum conficere
  potuerunt."
  "Eloquence is the most powerful force in human affairs.
  Its mastery is rare; it changes nations."
  This is not boasting — it is what Cicero observed in
  the Roman Republic: the best speeches changed votes,
  freed innocent men, condemned the guilty, and moved
  the Senate.
```

### De Inventione — The Early Theory

```
DE INVENTIONE (On Invention, c. 87 BCE; early work)
----------------------------------------------------
Cicero's earliest rhetorical treatise.
Systematic treatment of inventio (finding arguments).
Later Cicero was embarrassed by it (too schematic, too
schoolbook) but it was the most-read rhetorical text
in the medieval period.

STASIS THEORY (from Hermagoras):
  The core analytical tool for forensic rhetoric.
  Before you can argue a case, identify the STASIS —
  the point at issue, the exact question in dispute.

  FOUR TYPES OF STASIS:
  1. Conjectural stasis (an sit): did the act happen?
     "Did he kill her?" — a question of fact.
  2. Definitional stasis (quid sit): what kind of act is it?
     "Was it murder or self-defense?" — a question of definition.
  3. Qualitative stasis (quale sit): what is its moral character?
     "Even if he did it — was it justified?" — a question of quality.
  4. Translative stasis (translation): should this court
     hear this case? — a jurisdictional question.

  WHY STASIS THEORY MATTERS:
  Most arguments fail to persuade because the parties are
  arguing at DIFFERENT stasis levels.
  "Did he do it?" (conjectural)
  "Even if he did it, it was appropriate in context!" (qualitative)
  If you answer a qualitative question with conjectural evidence,
  you haven't addressed the actual dispute.

  BUSINESS APPLICATION:
  Design review arguments often have a stasis problem:
    Person A: "Does this design work?" (conjectural — does it function?)
    Person B: "Even if it works, is it the right approach?" (qualitative)
    Person C: "Who should decide this?" (translative)
  Identifying the actual stasis saves enormous meeting time.
```

## Layer 2: Cicero's Political Career and Speeches

Cicero's speeches are the complement to his theory — demonstrating the practice:

```
CICERO'S MAJOR SPEECHES
------------------------
PRO ROSCIO AMERINO (80 BCE):
  Cicero's breakthrough case. Defended Sex. Roscius on a
  murder charge. The real issue: the charge was orchestrated
  by a Sullan freedman (Chrysogonus) who had bought Roscius's
  confiscated estate cheaply. Cicero attacked Chrysogonus
  while technically not attacking the dictator Sulla.
  Brilliant navigation of political danger.
  Acquittal. Career made.

IN CATILINAM I-IV (63 BCE):
  As consul, Cicero discovered Catiline's conspiracy to
  overthrow the Republic. The four speeches to the Senate
  and people are models of deliberative and forensic rhetoric.
  "Quo usque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra?"
  ("How long, Catiline, will you abuse our patience?")
  — The anaphoric opening that has been quoted and imitated
  for 2,000 years.

  The speeches demonstrate: pathos (fear of the conspiracy),
  ethos (Cicero as consul protecting Rome), logos (evidence
  of the plot). The emotional and logical appeals are integrated.

THE PHILIPPICS (44-43 BCE):
  Fourteen speeches against Mark Antony after Caesar's
  assassination. Named after Demosthenes' speeches against
  Philip of Macedon. Cicero knew the risk:
  "This is a declaration of war against Antony."
  He made the speeches anyway. Antony won; Cicero was
  executed. His head and right hand were displayed in
  the Forum.

  THE IRONY: his death was partly caused by his best
  rhetoric. The Philippics were so effective that Antony
  could not forgive them.
```

## Layer 3: Quintilian's Institutio Oratoria

Quintilian (c. 35–100 CE) wrote the most comprehensive work on rhetorical education in antiquity — and one of the most comprehensive in any era:

```
INSTITUTIO ORATORIA (Foundations of the Orator)
-------------------------------------------------
12 books; c. 95 CE.
Complete from infancy to the mature orator.

SCOPE:
  Book I:   Infancy and early education; the nurse's language;
            elementary grammar; teaching children to read and write.
  Books II: The teacher of rhetoric; what rhetoric is.
  Books III-VII: Inventio (books III-V), Dispositio (VII)
  Books VIII-XI: Elocutio (VIII-IX), Memory (XI.1-2), Delivery (XI.3)
  Book XII: The perfect orator.

THE CENTRAL THESIS: VIR BONUS DICENDI PERITUS
  "The good man skilled in speaking."
  The ideal orator is, first of all, a good man.
  You cannot be a great orator if you are not a good person.
  This ethical prerequisite is not just a moral position —
  it is a rhetorical argument: genuine ethos requires genuine
  virtue; the simulation of virtue will eventually fail.

  Quintilian's argument:
  "I do not merely say that the orator should be a good man,
  but I say that no man can be an orator unless he is a good man."
  The reasoning: rhetoric's highest end is justice; a man who
  uses rhetoric for unjust ends perverts its nature.
  The best rhetoric cannot be separated from the best character.

BOOK XII — THE MATURE ORATOR:
  The orator must be broadly educated:
    Philosophy (ethics, logic, natural philosophy)
    History (examples for argument and illustration)
    Law (for forensic practice)
    Mathematics, music, geometry (for mental training)
  Quintilian describes a curriculum that sounds remarkably
  like a modern liberal arts + professional program.
```

### Quintilian's Style Advice

```
QUINTILIAN ON STYLE (Books VIII-X)
------------------------------------
Quintilian is one of the most practical stylists in the tradition.
Key advice:

ON CLARITY:
  "Let our aim be above all to say clearly what we wish to say."
  Not at the expense of richness — but clarity first.
  The reader/listener must be able to follow.

ON OBSCURITY:
  "I should prefer an intelligent pupil not to understand me
  than to understand badly." — obscurity that produces
  wrong understanding is worse than non-understanding.

ON COPIA (abundance of expression):
  The well-trained orator should have a rich vocabulary
  and command of many ways to say the same thing.
  Not to use all of them at once — to have them available
  and select appropriately.

THE THREE STYLES (following Cicero):
  PLAIN (genus tenue/subtile):
    Simple diction, short sentences, factual.
    For teaching; for intimate conversation.
    Risk: dryness and boredom.
    Model: Attic Greek; Lysias.

  MIDDLE (genus medium/mixtum):
    Moderate ornamentation; varied; the style of
    pleasure, not grandeur.
    For most occasions; praise and blame.
    Risk: lack of force.
    Model: Cicero's letters; Horace in verse.

  GRAND (genus grande/sublime):
    Full periodic sentences; elaborate figures;
    emotional intensity; designed to move.
    For great occasions; calls to action.
    Risk: bombast; can collapse into noise.
    Model: Demosthenes; Cicero at his best.

  MATCHING STYLE TO OCCASION:
  Grand style for a case involving capital punishment.
  Plain style for explaining the details of a contract.
  Middle style for complimenting a colleague.
  Using the wrong style is as bad as using wrong content.
```

## Layer 4: Roman Forensic Practice

### The Roman Court System

```
ROMAN FORENSIC CONTEXT
-----------------------
The Roman court system was where careers were made:
  Cicero made his reputation defending Roscius.
  Caesar was a capable advocate.
  The courts were public; large audiences watched.

ADVOCATUS (advocate): not a professional lawyer in
  the modern sense but a patron/friend who spoke
  on behalf of the accused.
  Advocacy was a form of social obligation (officium).
  You spoke for those who were in your social network.
  Financial payment was technically illegal (though
  gifts after the fact were normal).

THE COURT LAYOUT:
  Centumviral court (the most important civil court):
  Up to 180 judges sitting in four sections.
  Standing audience of hundreds.
  The speech was a public performance, not a closed proceeding.
  Applause, jeers, and tears were part of the experience.

THE ADVOCATE'S STRATEGY:
  Ethos: establish your authority and good faith immediately.
  Stasis: identify and address the actual point at issue.
  Narratio: tell the facts as a compelling story.
  Argue: use logos to establish your version of the facts.
  Refutatio: preemptively address the opposing argument.
  Peroratio: emotional climax; make the judges feel the
             rightness of your conclusion.

TIME LIMITS:
  Clepsydra (water clock) measured speaking time.
  Prosecutors and defenders had allotted time.
  You could request more time (and often got it).
  Cicero's speeches as delivered were longer than any
  surviving text — court speeches were condensed/edited
  for publication afterward.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Cicero's theory and Cicero's practice are not always the same**
De Oratore's ideal orator combines philosophical wisdom with rhetorical skill. Cicero's actual speeches are sometimes more manipulative than his theory allows — he was a political survivor. The theory represents an ideal; the practice shows what survival requires.

**The vir bonus principle is an argument, not a platitude**
Quintilian's "good man skilled in speaking" is not a moral lecture. It is a claim about what makes rhetoric work at the highest level: genuine virtue produces genuine ethos that is more durable than feigned virtue. The corrupt speaker may win cases but will eventually lose the audience's trust. This is a theory of the long run.

**Stasis theory is practically useful right now**
The four types of stasis (conjectural, definitional, qualitative, translative) map directly onto modern professional disagreements. Most engineering debates have an implicit stasis problem: people argue at different levels without noticing. Asking "What is the actual stasis here?" often resolves arguments faster than more argumentation.

**Roman rhetoric was not just academic**
Cicero was executed because his speeches worked. Political rhetoric in Rome was not a decorative art — it was a survival skill with life-or-death consequences. The power that Gorgias's Encomium of Helen attributed to logos was, in Rome, real and demonstrable.
