# Political Rhetoric

## The Big Picture

Political rhetoric is the deliberative genre in practice at scale — the use of language to shape public belief, mobilize constituencies, and exercise or contest power. It encompasses speechwriting craft, framing theory, propaganda analysis, and the mechanics of coded language. The most important modern theoretical contribution is George Lakoff's cognitive framing theory, which explains why political argument often operates through metaphor and association rather than explicit logic.

## Layer 1: Framing Theory — The Key Framework

George Lakoff's work — particularly *Don't Think of an Elephant* (2004) and *Metaphors We Live By* (with Johnson, 1980) — is the most practically applicable theory for understanding political language.

### Frames and Conceptual Metaphors

```
LAKOFF'S FRAMING THEORY
------------------------
A FRAME is a mental structure that shapes how we understand
situations. Frames are activated by language; the words we
use evoke the frames they fit.

EXAMPLE: TAX RELIEF
  "Tax relief" frames taxes as a burden from which citizens
  need relief. Accepting this framing means:
  - Taxes are inherently burdensome
  - Those who offer cuts are heroes
  - Those who oppose cuts are blocking relief
  Opponents who argue "tax relief is a bad idea" have already
  accepted the frame. The word "relief" does the work.

  Alternative frame: "tax investment"
  - Taxes are collective investment in infrastructure,
    security, education, health
  - Cutting taxes = reducing investment = getting less
  Different frame → different policy implications

THE CRUCIAL RULE:
  "Negating a frame evokes the frame."
  "Don't think of an elephant" makes you think of an elephant.
  In politics: "I am not a crook" (Nixon) evokes "crook."
  Counter-move: don't negate the opposing frame; activate
  your own frame instead.

FRAMING IS NOT SPIN:
  Framing is not dishonest. Every description of reality
  uses a frame. The question is which aspects of reality
  a frame highlights and which it obscures.
  "Collateral damage" (obscures: civilians are being killed)
  "Killed civilians" (highlights: the agency and result)
  Both are descriptions; neither is neutral.
```

### The Strict Father vs. Nurturant Parent Frames

```
LAKOFF'S POLITICAL FAMILY FRAMES
----------------------------------
Lakoff argues that American political divisions map onto
two different family-metaphor frames for government:

STRICT FATHER MODEL:
  The world is dangerous; children need discipline.
  The father's role: set rules, enforce them, punish violations.
  Children learn self-reliance through discipline.
  Government equivalent: law and order, personal responsibility,
    punishment as deterrent, minimal intervention.
  Policy implications: punishment-based crime policy,
    anti-welfare ("dependency"), pro-military, anti-regulation.

NURTURANT PARENT MODEL:
  Children need support, empathy, and guidance to flourish.
  The parent's role: provide safety, foster development.
  Government equivalent: social safety nets, education,
    healthcare, environmental protection.
  Policy implications: rehabilitation over punishment,
    support programs, environmental regulation.

THE ANALYTICAL VALUE:
  This is not a claim that one model is correct.
  It is an explanation of why political debates feel
  incommensurable — the two sides are using different
  underlying frames that lead to different policy conclusions
  even from similar values (e.g., "freedom").

PROFESSIONAL APPLICATION:
  In corporate settings, similar frame conflicts operate:
  STRICT FATHER: "Hold teams accountable; no excuses;
    high standards through consequences."
  NURTURANT PARENT: "Create psychological safety; support
    teams in developing; focus on systemic causes."
  Leadership communication is more effective when you
  identify which frame your audience uses, not just
  which arguments they'll find logically compelling.
```

### Lakoff's Frame-Setting Rules for Communicators

```
PRACTICAL RULES FROM LAKOFF
------------------------------
1. ACTIVATE YOUR FRAME, DON'T NEGATE THEIRS.
   Wrong: "We don't think of healthcare as a burden."
   Right: "Healthcare is a right. It protects families."

2. REPEAT YOUR LANGUAGE CONSISTENTLY.
   Repetition activates frames in neural circuits.
   The repeated frame becomes the default lens.
   Strategic repetition is not propaganda — it is
   how conceptual change works cognitively.

3. USE CONCRETE IMAGES, NOT ABSTRACT ARGUMENTS.
   Frames are activated by concrete, vivid language.
   "A child who can't see the blackboard" is more
   frame-activating than "educational access."

4. REFRAME, DON'T JUST REBUT.
   When someone uses a frame you reject, reframe:
   "The question isn't whether we can afford this;
    it's what kind of company we want to be."

ENGINEERING APPLICATION:
  "Technical debt" — STRICT FATHER frame: past teams
    were undisciplined; now we're paying the price.
  "Foundation investment" — NURTURANT PARENT frame:
    we need the infrastructure to reach our goals.
  Both are true descriptions; the frame shapes what
  budget holders hear and how they respond.
  A VP who understands framing chooses the frame
  that fits the decision-making environment.
```

## Layer 2: Speechwriting Craft

### The Architecture of a Political Speech

```
POLITICAL SPEECH STRUCTURE
----------------------------
A well-structured political speech has these components:

1. OPENING HOOK (30-60 seconds)
   - The opening line is the most-remembered and most-quoted.
   - Classic moves: a story, a direct address, a provocative claim.
   - Must establish ETHOS immediately (why you have standing to speak).
   - JFK: "Ask not what your country can do for you..."
     — Chiasmus as the opening line. Permanent memorability.

2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
   - Define what is at stake; the audience must feel the problem.
   - Kairos: why THIS problem, why NOW?
   - Use the emotions catalog (Aristotle): fear, anger, indignation
     are the motivating emotions for deliberative rhetoric.
   - The problem statement must match the audience's existing frame.

3. VISION STATEMENT
   - What does the world look like if the audience acts?
   - This is pathos (positive): hope, pride, belonging.
   - MLK's "I Have a Dream" anaphora: the vision, repeated and
     escalated, is the speech's architecture.

4. ARGUMENT / EVIDENCE
   - The logos section: why this solution is the right one.
   - In political speeches, this is often the weakest section —
     the audience came for inspiration, not data.
   - Best practice: one strong empirical anchor; avoid lists.
   - The Toulmin warrant must be shared with the audience.

5. CALL TO ACTION
   - The peroration's job: leave the audience in the right
     emotional state to take the right action.
   - Must be specific enough to act on; must be emotionally charged.
   - The final sentence is as important as the first.
```

### Great Speeches as Case Studies

```
CASE STUDIES: PRESIDENTIAL RHETORIC
--------------------------------------
LINCOLN — GETTYSBURG ADDRESS (1863):
  272 words. One of the most analyzed political speeches.
  STRUCTURE:
    Past: "Fourscore and seven years ago" — invokes the founding.
    Present: the battle and its dead; the unfinished work.
    Future: "this nation, under God, shall have a new birth
      of freedom" — the transformation.
  EPIDEICTIC + DELIBERATIVE FUSION:
    Ostensibly a eulogy (epideictic: honor the dead).
    Actually a redefinition of the war's purpose (deliberative:
    this is now a war for equality, not just union).
  FRAMING MOVE:
    "Four score and seven years ago" (1776, not 1789) makes
    the Declaration of Independence, not the Constitution,
    the founding document — which frames slavery as
    incompatible with the nation's founding principle.
  STYLE: Plain + elevated. Short sentences. No ornament except
    anaphora ("we cannot consecrate—we cannot hallow—we
    cannot dedicate") and chiasmus in the peroration.

MLK — "I HAVE A DREAM" (1963):
  A masterwork of anaphora (8× "I have a dream"),
  allusion (Declaration, Constitution, spirituals),
  and escalating emotional architecture.
  FRAMING: The Declaration's promise as a "bad check" —
    a financial metaphor that reframes injustice as
    a legal debt owed, not a favor asked.
  REGISTER MODULATION: shifts between legal/constitutional
    argument and prophetic-biblical register —
    appeals to both logos and pathos simultaneously.
  NOTE: The "I Have a Dream" section was largely improvised.
    Mahalia Jackson called out "Tell them about the dream!"
    This is kairos: recognizing the moment and responding.

OBAMA — 2004 DNC KEYNOTE:
  Introduced the "There is not a liberal America and a
  conservative America — there is the United States of America"
  frame. Antithesis used to collapse a false dilemma.
  EPIDEICTIC AS LAUNCH: a convention speech (epideictic:
  praise the party) used as a platform for a national
  frame-setting exercise.
```

## Layer 3: Propaganda Techniques

Propaganda is political rhetoric conducted systematically, at scale, often with institutional resources. The techniques are neutral as analytical categories — they describe what works, regardless of the political valence of the message.

### Bernays and the Engineering of Consent

```
EDWARD BERNAYS (1891-1995) — "PROPAGANDA" (1928)
--------------------------------------------------
Bernays is the founder of modern public relations.
He was Sigmund Freud's nephew; he applied psychoanalytic
theory to mass persuasion.

KEY INSIGHT: "The conscious and intelligent manipulation
of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is
an important element in democratic society."
This is the blunt version; PR is the polite version.

TECHNIQUES:
  BANDWAGON: "Everyone is switching to X." Social proof.
  The desire to be on the winning side.
  In business: "Every major tech company has adopted X."

  TESTIMONIAL: Endorsement by a trusted authority or celebrity.
  The authority transfer: their credibility → your product.
  Effective when the authority is perceived as domain-relevant.

  PLAIN FOLKS: The speaker is "just like you."
  Establishes in-group membership; reduces social distance.
  Counter to: the expert who is seen as out of touch.

  CARD STACKING: Select only the evidence favorable to
  your position; ignore contrary evidence.
  The straw man's sibling: not misrepresenting the opponent
  but misrepresenting the totality of evidence.

  GLITTERING GENERALITIES: Abstract positive terms
  ("freedom," "democracy," "innovation," "excellence")
  that no one can oppose but that mean nothing specific.
  Effective because they activate positive frames
  without committing to specific claims.

  TRANSFER: Associate your cause with a respected symbol
  (flag, religious image) or prestigious person.
  Lakoff's frame-by-association in concentrated form.

  FEAR APPEAL: Legitimate when the fear is real and the
  proposed action addresses it. Manipulative when:
  (a) the fear is exaggerated, (b) the causation is false,
  (c) the proposed action doesn't address the actual risk.
  Aristotle: fear requires the object to seem real and near.
```

### Dog Whistles and Coded Language

```
DOG WHISTLES — CODED POLITICAL LANGUAGE
-----------------------------------------
A "dog whistle" is language that carries a different
meaning for an in-group than for the general audience.
Origin: ultrasonic whistles audible to dogs but not humans.

FUNCTION:
  The surface message is deniable to outsiders.
  The in-group message activates specific frames.
  The speaker can deny the in-group meaning if challenged.

EXAMPLES IN U.S. POLITICS:
  "States' rights" (surface: federalism) (in-group: opposition
    to federal civil rights enforcement)
  "Law and order" (surface: crime policy) (in-group: control
    of minority communities in specific historical contexts)
  "Inner city" (surface: urban geography) (in-group: racial
    coding in specific periods)
  "Globalist" (surface: pro-international trade) (in-group:
    antisemitic trope in some usage contexts)

THE ANALYTICAL PROBLEM:
  Not all coded language is intentionally deceptive.
  Some "codes" are in the ear of the listener.
  Some speakers use language without knowing its history.
  The distinction: does the speaker know the in-group
  activation? Is the deniability intentional?
  Ian Haney López's "Dog Whistle Politics" (2014)
  argues the strategic use is documented and intentional
  in post-Civil Rights era American conservatism.

CORPORATE EQUIVALENTS:
  "Culture fit" can function as coded language for
  demographic homogeneity.
  "Meritocracy" can function as a legitimating frame
  for existing hierarchies.
  Not necessarily intentional; the rhetorical effect
  operates regardless of intent.
  Recognition is the first step to course-correction.
```

## Layer 3b: Frank Luntz — Competitive Frame-Setting

Lakoff's counterpart on the conservative side. While Lakoff works as an academic theorist, Luntz is a practitioner — a pollster and messaging consultant who has shaped the vocabulary of American conservatism since the 1990s.

```
FRANK LUNTZ — "WORDS THAT WORK" (2007)

CORE METHODOLOGY:
  Luntz uses focus groups and polling to find the formulation
  of a position that produces the strongest response.
  He tests multiple phrasings of the same policy and measures
  emotional resonance, not just agreement.
  The result: a vocabulary guide for a political position
  that maximizes persuasion with target audiences.

KEY REFRAMINGS (Luntz's most-cited examples):

"DEATH TAX" vs. "ESTATE TAX":
  Both describe the same policy (federal tax on inherited estates).
  "Estate tax": procedural, institutional framing.
  "Death tax": two loaded elements:
    "Death": makes the tax apply at the worst moment.
    "Tax": always negatively valenced in conservative framing.
  Effect: "death tax" framing increased opposition to estate tax
  repeal by significant margins in polling, even among people
  who would not pay it (estates below the exemption threshold).
  This is Lakoff's framing theory in pure practice form.

"CLIMATE CHANGE" vs. "GLOBAL WARMING":
  Luntz advised (2002 memo) using "climate change" rather than
  "global warming": "While 'global warming' has catastrophic
  connotations attached to it, 'climate change' sounds a more
  controllable and less emotional challenge."
  The memo was eventually leaked; the deliberate substitution
  became itself a rhetorical issue.
  Note: the substitution has since been inverted — "global warming"
  is now sometimes preferred by activists for precisely the
  emotional force Luntz wanted to eliminate.

"ENERGY EXPLORATION" vs. "DRILLING":
  The active agent framing ("exploration") vs. the mechanical
  operation framing ("drilling") — same activity, different connotation.

FRAME COMPETITION IN PRACTICE:
  The estate tax example shows that framing is competitive.
  "Estate tax" advocates tried "Paris Hilton tax" as a counter-frame
  (reframe: only the very wealthy pay this).
  Both sides are doing the same thing: selecting the frame that
  activates the desired inference chain in the target audience.

LUNTZ'S OWN PRINCIPLE:
  "It's not what you say, it's what people hear."
  This is Grice's cooperative principle's complement:
  the speaker's intention is irrelevant; the listener's
  interpretation is the rhetorical event.

RELATION TO LAKOFF:
  Lakoff (Moral Politics, Don't Think of an Elephant!):
    Academic; descriptive; left-leaning application.
    Argues that conservatives have been better at framing.
  Luntz (Words That Work): practitioner; prescriptive;
    conservative application.
  They are using the same underlying model of how language
  works in political persuasion. The model is correct;
  the applications differ.
```

## Layer 4: Chomsky/Herman — Manufacturing Consent

```
MANUFACTURING CONSENT (Herman and Chomsky, 1988)
-------------------------------------------------
The "propaganda model" of mass media.
Not about individual speeches but about structural
constraints on what gets said in mass media.

THE FIVE FILTERS (what shapes media content):
1. OWNERSHIP: Media owned by large corporations have
   structural incentives not to challenge corporate interests.
2. ADVERTISING: Media funded by advertising cannot alienate
   major advertisers without financial consequences.
3. SOURCING: Journalists must maintain access to official
   sources. This creates structural deference to official
   positions.
4. FLAK: Organized pressure on media (from governments,
   corporations, organized pressure groups) shapes what
   gets published.
5. ANTI-COMMUNISM (originally) / FEAR: The ideological
   framework that defines acceptable debate boundaries.
   In later formulations: terrorism, rogue states.

RELEVANCE TO POLITICAL RHETORIC:
  The propaganda model explains not what is said but
  what is not said — what is excluded from the agenda.
  Rhetoric operates within these structural constraints.
  The most effective political rhetoric is that which
  can navigate these filters or circumvent them
  (which is why social media disrupted established politics:
  it bypassed filters 1-3).

CRITIQUE OF CHOMSKY/HERMAN:
  The model is structural, not conspiratorial — but
  critics argue it underweights journalistic autonomy
  and treats all media as equivalent.
  The internet has partially refuted filter #3 (sourcing)
  by creating alternative information ecosystems.
```

## Layer 5: Kairos — The Rhetoric of the Moment

```
KAIROS IN POLITICAL RHETORIC
------------------------------
Kairos: the right moment; the opportune time.
Not just what to say or how, but WHEN.

KAIROS AS A STRATEGIC RESOURCE:
  Political windows open briefly:
    A crisis creates an opportunity to reframe an issue.
    A speech at a specific historical moment can anchor
    a frame permanently.
    A question in a debate asked at the right moment
    can define a candidacy.

RHETORICAL KAIROS EXAMPLES:
  Reagan in 1980 debate: "There you go again."
    Not a substantive argument. A tone-setter.
    Activated the "Reagan as relaxed, Carter as anxious" frame.
    The line was prepared; the kairos was recognizing
    when to deploy it.

  MLK at the March on Washington: the improvised "dream" section.
    The prepared speech was moving; the improvised section
    was transcendent. Kairos + mastery = the right departure
    from the script at the right moment.

  The hostile question: asked when you're off-balance,
    when the answer would commit you to a frame you reject.
    Rhetorical kairos = recognizing that sometimes
    not answering is the right answer.

VP-LEVEL APPLICATION:
  The all-hands meeting after a difficult quarter:
    Kairos matters — the sequence of acknowledgment before
    pivot determines whether the pivot lands.
  The investor call after a miss:
    Don't pivot to the future before the present is fully
    acknowledged. Kairos: the emotional readiness
    of the audience before you move to your message.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Framing is not dishonest**
Every description of reality uses a frame. The question is which frames are accurate, which aspects they highlight, and whether the speaker is aware of the frame they are using. Lakoff's contribution is making the implicit explicit — understanding that "tax relief" is a rhetorical choice, not a neutral description, allows you to make conscious choices about your own language.

**Dog whistles are not always intentional**
The rhetorical effect of coded language operates regardless of intent. A speaker can use language with a coded history without knowing its history. The effect on the audience (activation of specific frames in the in-group) is the same. Rhetorical analysis is about effect, not intent.

**Propaganda techniques are neutral as analytical categories**
Bandwagon, testimonial, fear appeal — these describe what works, not just what is deceptive. A legitimate public health campaign uses fear appeals. A respected expert giving a testimonial for their genuine area of expertise is legitimate. The manipulative versions are when the technique is used to exploit rather than to inform.

**Terms with contested pragmatic force in organizational contexts**
Certain terms carry different pragmatic force for different audiences depending on their context, history, and the speaker's position. "Culture fit" activates the in-group frame for some listeners (shared values, team cohesion) and the exclusion frame for others (rejection of difference, rationalizing bias). "Meritocracy" similarly activates either the just-rewards frame or the legitimation-of-existing-hierarchy frame depending on the listener's position and the organization's track record. Neither term is inherently coded; both have become contested in contemporary organizations. The rhetorical analysis (not the political analysis): using either term in contexts where its contested pragmatic force is likely to activate requires either explicit disambiguation or deliberate avoidance. This is not a question of intent — it is a question of effect on diverse audiences.

**Kairos is a skill, not luck**
The ability to recognize and seize the rhetorical moment is a trainable skill, not mere improvisation. Lincoln had been refining the Gettysburg Address's ideas for years; the 272-word version was a distillation. MLK had preached the "dream" imagery before; what he recognized at the March on Washington was the moment when the audience was ready for it. Preparation enables kairos; it does not precede it.
