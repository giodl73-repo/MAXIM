# Rhetoric — Overview and Landscape

## The Big Picture

Rhetoric is the art and science of persuasion through language. It is distinct from logic (which studies validity regardless of audience), linguistics (which studies language structure), and literature (which studies aesthetic uses of language). Rhetoric is practical — it asks: how do you move a specific audience to a specific belief or action using language?

## The Three Genres of Rhetoric

Aristotle distinguished three genres by time, audience, and purpose:

```
THREE GENRES (SPECIES) OF RHETORIC
-------------------------------------
DELIBERATIVE (symbouleutikon)
  Time: future
  Audience: a legislative body or decision-maker
  Purpose: advise on a course of action
  Judgment: expedient/inexpedient; advantageous/harmful
  Examples: legislative speeches, policy recommendations,
    business strategy presentations, executive briefings
  Your context: any presentation to leadership about
    what to do next quarter/year

FORENSIC (dikanikon)
  Time: past
  Audience: a judge or jury
  Purpose: accuse or defend a person/organization
  Judgment: just/unjust; guilty/not guilty
  Examples: trial arguments, post-mortem reviews,
    incident retrospectives, performance reviews
  Your context: any review of what happened and who/what
    is responsible

EPIDEICTIC (epideiktikon)
  Time: present
  Audience: spectators/witnesses
  Purpose: praise or blame; express shared values
  Judgment: noble/base; honorable/dishonorable
  Examples: eulogies, award speeches, toasts, commencement,
    team recognition, product launch speeches
  Your context: any speech that celebrates, honors,
    or marks a communal moment
```

## The Three Appeals

```
THREE APPEALS (PISTEIS / MEANS OF PERSUASION)
----------------------------------------------
LOGOS (reason/argument)
  The logical substance of the argument.
  Evidence, inference, structure.
  Deductive: from premises to conclusion (syllogism, enthymeme)
  Inductive: from examples to general conclusion (paradigm)
  NOT just about being logical — about being perceived
  as offering a well-reasoned case.

ETHOS (character/credibility)
  The speaker's credibility as perceived by the audience.
  Three components (Aristotle):
    Phronesis (practical wisdom): you know what you're
      talking about; you have experience and judgment
    Arete (virtue): you are honest; you don't have
      a hidden agenda; you're acting in good faith
    Eunoia (goodwill): you care about the audience's
      interests, not just your own

  CRITICAL POINT: Ethos is constructed IN the speech,
  not brought to it from outside.
  Your reputation helps, but what you say and how you
  say it in this speech is what matters for ethos.

PATHOS (emotion/audience engagement)
  The emotional state of the audience.
  Aristotle: you must put the audience in a specific
  emotional state to receive your argument.
  Fear, hope, anger, compassion, indignation — each
  disposes an audience differently.
  NOT manipulation in a pejorative sense — APPROPRIATE
  emotional engagement is part of rational persuasion.
  A jury that feels nothing will not convict; they must
  feel the appropriate indignation, sympathy, etc.

THE INTERPLAY:
  Effective rhetoric integrates all three.
  A logos-only speech is cold and may be dismissed.
  A pathos-only speech is emotional manipulation.
  An ethos-only speech depends entirely on reputation.
  The classic Aristotelian speech: established ethos
  (you should trust my judgment because...) + logos
  (here is the argument) + pathos (here is why this
  matters to you personally).
```

## The Five Canons

```
FIVE CANONS OF RHETORIC
------------------------
Developed primarily by Cicero; the framework for
understanding the complete rhetorical task.

1. INVENTIO (invention/discovery of arguments)
   Finding what there is to say.
   Not making up arguments from nothing but discovering
   what arguments are available for this case and audience.
   The "topics" (topoi/loci communes): general lines of
   argument applicable to many cases.
   See 03-FIVE-CANONS.md for full treatment.

2. DISPOSITIO (arrangement/organization)
   Ordering the material for maximum effect.
   Classical arrangement: exordium (introduction) + narratio
   (statement of facts) + confirmatio (arguments) + refutatio
   (handling objections) + peroratio (conclusion).
   Modern: what goes first, middle, last? Why?
   See 03-FIVE-CANONS.md.

3. ELOCUTIO (style/expression)
   How to say it: word choice, sentence structure, figures.
   Four virtues of style (Theophrastus):
     Grammaticality, clarity, ornamentation, propriety.
   The "plain/middle/grand" style levels.
   See 04-FIGURES-OF-SPEECH.md for figures.

4. MEMORIA (memory)
   How to remember what to say.
   Method of Loci (memory palace): a spatial mnemonic.
   See 03-FIVE-CANONS.md for the memory palace.

5. ACTIO / PRONUNTIATIO (delivery)
   Voice, gesture, pacing, presence.
   Demosthenes: asked what was most important in rhetoric.
   "Delivery. Delivery. Delivery."
   See 03-FIVE-CANONS.md.
```

## Rhetoric vs. Logic vs. Poetics

```
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| LOGIC               | RHETORIC         | POETICS            |
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Studies: validity   | Studies:         | Studies: how       |
| of inference        | persuasion of    | language creates   |
| regardless of       | specific         | aesthetic effects  |
| audience            | audiences        |                    |
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Audience: none      | Audience: central| Audience: reader   |
| (purely formal)     | — defines the    | — may not be       |
|                     | rhetorical task  | considered at all  |
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Goal: truth/        | Goal: action or  | Goal: pleasure,    |
| validity            | belief (in the   | insight, aesthetic |
|                     | audience)        | experience         |
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Method: deduction,  | Method: ethos,   | Method: imagery,   |
| induction,          | logos, pathos;   | narrative,         |
| formal proof        | topoi, figures   | metaphor, form     |
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Tools: syllogism,   | Tools: enthymeme,| Tools: prosody,    |
| truth tables,       | example, topics, | tropes, devices,   |
| proof systems       | figures, canon   | narrative structure|
+---------------------+------------------+--------------------+

KEY: Aristotle wrote the Organon (logic), the Rhetoric,
     and the Poetics as three distinct disciplines.
     The three overlap (logical arguments in rhetoric;
     rhetorical figures in poetry; emotional appeals
     in logic about what is worthy of belief) but
     are analytically distinct.
```

## Direct Professional Applications

For a VP of Engineering at Microsoft, rhetoric is not ancient history — it is applied every day:

```
PROFESSIONAL RHETORICAL OCCASIONS
-----------------------------------
DELIBERATIVE:
  - Strategy presentations to senior leadership
  - Budget justification documents
  - Hiring decisions presented to the staffing committee
  - Roadmap presentations (what should we build next?)
  - Technical decision documents ("should we adopt X?")
  → Inventio: what are the arguments? What does this
    audience care about? What objections will arise?
  → Dispositio: bottom-line up front, or story arc first?
  → Ethos: why should they trust your recommendation?

FORENSIC:
  - Incident retrospectives (postmortems)
  - Performance reviews
  - Security breach explanations
  - "What went wrong and who was responsible?"
  → This genre is about establishing what happened and
    attributing responsibility fairly.

EPIDEICTIC:
  - Team all-hands celebrating a launch
  - Award speech for an exceptional engineer
  - Eulogy for a colleague
  - Commencement-style talk at an onboarding event
  - Toast at a team dinner
  → Templates in 07-EPIDEICTIC.md

TOULMIN IN PRACTICE:
  Every design review document is a Toulmin argument.
  Every pull request description is a Toulmin argument.
  Every RFC (Request for Comments) is a Toulmin argument.
  → See 05-ARGUMENTATION.md for the full model.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Rhetoric is not "empty speech"**
The popular meaning of "rhetoric" is inflated or empty language ("empty rhetoric," "mere rhetoric"). This is a centuries-old anti-rhetorical tradition (Plato condemned it). In the technical/academic sense, rhetoric is the systematic study of persuasion — which is anything but empty. You cannot communicate without rhetoric; you can only use it better or worse.

**"Rhetorical question" means something specific**
A rhetorical question is a question posed for effect, not for an answer ("Is the sky blue?"). It is a figure of speech (anthypophora or erotema) used to make a point. It is NOT a vague synonym for "interesting question" as it is often used in casual speech.

**Ethos is not "ethics"**
Ethos (character/credibility as perceived in the speech) is related to ethics (moral correctness) but not the same thing. An unethical speaker can construct effective ethos in a speech (politicians do this routinely). The relationship between ethical behavior and rhetorical ethos is a live question, but they are analytically distinct.

**The five canons are not a sequence**
They can be treated as a temporal sequence (invent first, arrange second, etc.) but in practice they are interdependent. How you arrange material affects what arguments you discover (dispositio influencing inventio). How you phrase something affects what argument it makes (elocutio influencing logos). The five canons are five dimensions of the rhetorical task, not five chronological steps.
