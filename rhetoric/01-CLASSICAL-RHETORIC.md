# Classical Rhetoric

## The Big Picture

Classical Greek rhetoric developed in the 5th century BCE in Athens and Sicily as a response to democratic institutions that required citizens to speak in courts, assemblies, and public occasions. Aristotle systematized it in the 4th century; it became the dominant intellectual discipline of the Hellenistic and Roman world.

## Layer 1: The Sophists and the Problem of Rhetoric

The Sophists were the first professional teachers of rhetoric in Athens (c. 480–380 BCE). They are the contested origin point: reviled by Plato, defended by modern scholars.

### What the Sophists Claimed

```
SOPHIST CENTRAL CLAIMS
-----------------------
PROTAGORAS (c. 490-420 BCE):
  "Man is the measure of all things: of things that are,
  that they are; of things that are not, that they are not."
  → Truth is relative to the perceiver/community.
  → There is no absolute truth, only what is persuasive
    to a specific audience at a specific time.

  The antilogiai: arguments on both sides of any question.
  Protagoras claimed to be able to argue EITHER SIDE
  of any case. He taught students to do the same.
  The charge: this is mere sophistry (later meaning),
  teaching people to make the worse argument appear the better.

GORGIAS (c. 483-375 BCE):
  The Encomium of Helen: a defense of Helen of Troy
  (usually blamed for causing the Trojan War).
  Arguments:
    1. If she went to Troy by fate/divine force — not responsible
    2. If she was taken by force — not responsible
    3. If she was persuaded by speech — speech is irresistible,
       like physical force; the listener is compelled by logos
    4. If she went out of love — love is itself a divine force
  Therefore: Helen is innocent under any scenario.

  Gorgias's point: logos (speech) is the greatest power.
  It can create belief, emotion, and action in its hearers.
  The speaker who controls logos controls people.
  This is both an advertisement for his teaching and
  a serious philosophical argument about language's power.

THRASYMACHUS (in Plato's Republic):
  Justice is the advantage of the stronger.
  The ruling class defines "justice" to mean whatever
  benefits them; the weaker class is persuaded to accept it.
  Rhetoric is the tool by which the powerful maintain power.
```

### Plato's Critique

```
PLATO'S ANTI-RHETORIC (primarily in Gorgias and Phaedrus)
-----------------------------------------------------------
GORGIAS:
  Socrates argues that rhetoric is not a techne (art/skill)
  but a knack (empeiria) — like cookery.
  A real art has knowledge of its subject matter:
    Medicine knows the body; it aims at health.
    Rhetoric does not know truth or justice;
    it aims only at pleasing the audience.
  Rhetoric is to justice what cookery is to medicine:
    flattery that produces pleasure without health.

  The key distinction:
    REAL KNOWLEDGE → genuine good → genuine persuasion
    OPINION + RHETORIC → apparent good → manipulation

  Plato's charge: the Sophists don't teach virtue,
  they teach the appearance of virtue. They make the
  worse argument appear better. This is unjust because
  it displaces genuine knowledge with mere seeming.

PHAEDRUS (Plato's more nuanced view):
  A philosophical rhetoric IS possible but only if:
  1. The speaker has genuine knowledge of the truth.
  2. The speaker knows the soul (psychology) of the audience.
  3. The speaker can tailor the true argument to the
     specific soul-type of the specific audience.
  This "true rhetoric" would be inseparable from philosophy.
  In practice, Plato thinks no actual rhetoric meets this standard.

HISTORICAL IRONY:
  Plato wrote DIALOGUES — a literary form using
  characterization, narrative, and dramatic structure
  to make philosophical arguments. He used rhetoric
  (in the broad sense) to argue against rhetoric.
  As Derrida noted: Plato's Phaedrus uses writing
  to argue against writing. Same structural irony.
```

## Layer 2: Aristotle's Rhetoric — The Systematic Account

Aristotle's **Rhetoric** (c. 355–322 BCE, though the date is contested) is the foundational systematic treatment:

### Rhetoric as a Discipline

```
ARISTOTLE'S DEFINITION
-----------------------
"Rhetoric is the faculty of discovering in any particular
case all of the available means of persuasion."

KEY WORDS:
  "Faculty" (dynamis): a capacity or power, not a body of rules.
  "Discovering": inventio — rhetoric is a heuristic, not an algorithm.
  "In any particular case": it is always context-specific.
  "All of the available means": comprehensive, exhaustive.
  "Persuasion": not truth but effective communication.

RHETORIC'S RELATION TO OTHER DISCIPLINES:
  "Rhetoric is the counterpart (antistrophe) of dialectic."
  Dialectic: logical argument from probable premises (vs.
    demonstration from certain premises in analytics).
  Both deal with probable matters; both are available to all.
  Rhetoric adds: the audience, the speaker's character,
    the emotional state needed for persuasion.

  Rhetoric is also related to:
    Ethics and politics (it concerns human affairs)
    Logic (the enthymeme is the rhetorical syllogism)
    Psychology (pathos requires knowledge of the passions)
```

### The Enthymeme

The most important logical concept in Aristotle's Rhetoric:

```
THE ENTHYMEME (to enthymema)
-----------------------------
"The body of persuasion" (Aristotle).
The rhetorical equivalent of the syllogism.

A SYLLOGISM is a complete deductive argument:
  Major premise: All humans are mortal.
  Minor premise: Socrates is human.
  Conclusion: Therefore Socrates is mortal.

AN ENTHYMEME omits one premise because the audience
will supply it from common knowledge:
  "Socrates is mortal because he is human."
  The major premise (all humans are mortal) is left
  unstated; the audience fills it in.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
  1. Efficiency: rhetoric to an audience cannot be
     formal, complete, and slow. Enthymemes are fast.
  2. Engagement: when the audience supplies the missing
     premise, they participate in the argument.
     They persuade THEMSELVES. This is more powerful
     than being told the conclusion.
  3. Shared assumptions: the enthymeme only works if
     the missing premise is genuinely shared. If you
     supply a false or contested premise silently,
     you commit an error or a manipulation.

EXAMPLES:
  "She must be a good doctor — she went to Harvard Medical School."
  Missing premise: "Harvard Medical School graduates are good doctors."
  Whether this is a valid enthymeme depends on whether
  the missing premise is actually shared and defensible.

  "This policy will work — it worked in Germany."
  Missing premises: "What works in Germany works here"
  and "The conditions are comparable." These may not be shared.

The enthymeme can also be INVALID (the apparent enthymeme or
fallacious enthymeme) — when the missing premise is false,
contested, or exploits bias.
```

### The Topoi

```
THE TOPOI (TOPICS/COMMONPLACES)
--------------------------------
Topoi (singular: topos) = "places" — general lines of
argument that can be applied across many cases.

ARISTOTLE DISTINGUISHES:
  Special topoi (idioi topoi): arguments specific to
    a particular discipline (political science, ethics).
    The "facts" of the case.

  Common topoi (koinoi topoi): general argument-finding
    tools applicable to any case.

THE FOUR COMMON TOPOI (Rhetoric, Book II, Chapter 23):
  1. OPPOSITE: if X is true of one member of an opposition,
     the opposite is true of the other.
     "If war was the cause of our troubles, peace will
     be what makes them right."
  2. INFLECTION: use grammatical or conceptual variants.
     "If 'just' actions are not always good, 'justly'
     may not always be the right word for doing them."
  3. RELATIVE TERMS: same as inflection for relations.
  4. MORE AND LESS: if not even the more convincing case
     holds, the less won't either. "If not even the gods
     know everything, humans certainly don't."

Aristotle lists 28 common topoi and 10 fallacious topoi.
The full list covers most of the standard argumentative moves.

MODERN CONNECTION:
  The "topic" tradition continued through medieval education
  (Boethius, Cicero's De Topicis), into Renaissance rhetoric,
  into modern invention heuristics.
  Design thinking's "How Might We" questions are a modern
  inventio heuristic in the tradition of the topoi.
```

## Layer 3: Isocrates — Rhetoric as Education

Isocrates (436–338 BCE) is the other major figure in classical Greek rhetoric — less famous than Aristotle but hugely influential on the humanist educational tradition:

```
ISOCRATES — KEY POSITIONS
--------------------------
Against the Sophists (but also against Plato):
  The Sophists (especially Gorgias and the eristics):
    taught empty cleverness and argumentation without
    genuine ethical content.

  Plato: demanded certain philosophical knowledge as
    the prerequisite for true rhetoric; this is too
    demanding and otherworldly for practical education.

  Isocrates: practical wisdom and civic virtue are
    achievable through rhetorical education.
    The goal is the person of good judgment (phronesis),
    broad learning, civic virtue, and effective expression.

"Philosophy" in Isocrates = the study of wisdom through discourse.
  He means rhetoric + ethics + practical wisdom + broad culture.
  This is the "humanities" curriculum avant la lettre.

Against the Sophists (section 1):
  "I wish to inform those who hold wrong opinions of us,
  and to instruct those who are ignorant of what we do;
  I wish to do this for the sake of those young men
  who, instead of using their intelligence, have
  been deceived by those who profess to give them wisdom."

THE KAIROTIC DIMENSION:
  Isocrates emphasizes kairos (the right moment, the
  appropriate occasion). The good orator recognizes
  the moment and responds to it.
  No formula can produce the right speech for the
  right moment; only developed judgment (phronesis) can.
  This is irreducibly situational: rules can take you
  so far; judgment does the rest.

INFLUENCE:
  The Isocratean tradition → Roman education (Cicero,
  Quintilian) → Renaissance studia humanitatis
  (grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, moral philosophy)
  → modern liberal arts education
  → the "communication skills" component of management education
```

## Layer 4: The Aristotelian Emotions Catalog

Book II of the Rhetoric is a systematic account of the emotions — how they work and how to arouse them. This is the first systematic psychology in Western philosophy:

```
ARISTOTLE'S EMOTIONS CATALOG (Book II, Rhetoric)
--------------------------------------------------
Each emotion analyzed as:
  [emotional state] = definition + what produces it +
                      in whom + at whom

ANGER (orge):
  Definition: impulse, attended by pain, to conspicuous
    revenge for a conspicuous slight felt without justification.
  Produced by: feeling slighted, in circumstances where
    one expects respect.
  In whom: those with some sense of their own worth.
  At whom: those who slight without justification.
  To arouse: show the audience that they have been
    slighted in a way they did not deserve.
  To calm: show the accused did not intend the slight,
    or is suffering already, or is beneath consideration.

FEAR (phobos):
  Definition: pain or disturbance arising from imagining
    an imminent destructive or painful evil.
  Produced by: the object seeming close and powerful.
  Rhetoric: make the audience believe the danger is real
    and near, not distant and hypothetical.

PITY (eleos):
  Definition: pain felt at an apparently serious evil
    befalling a person who does not deserve it, where
    we expect it could befall us or those close to us.
  Condition: you must feel that you yourself could suffer.
  → People who think they are above suffering don't pity;
    people who are currently suffering don't pity
    (they're too focused on their own trouble).
  Rhetoric: make the audience identify with the sufferer
    and feel that comparable suffering could befall them.

INDIGNATION (nemesis, moral outrage):
  The emotion opposed to pity: pain at UNdeserved good
    fortune. The "it's not fair that they're doing well."

ENVY (phthonos):
  Pain at a neighbor's good fortune, because it
  diminishes the envier's own standing.

THE RHETORICAL USE:
  Every deliberate use of pathos requires understanding
  which emotion is appropriate (what the case requires)
  and how to produce it in this specific audience.
  The appeal to fear only works if the audience can
  imagine the feared object as real and near.
  The appeal to pity only works if the audience can
  identify with the suffering party.
  The Aristotelian account is a guide to audience analysis.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Sophist" is not an insult in the historical sense**
The Sophists were serious intellectual professionals who addressed genuine questions about knowledge, language, and justice. The pejorative meaning of "sophist" (clever but fallacious arguer) comes from Plato's polemical use. Protagoras and Gorgias were genuine thinkers; their relativism was a serious philosophical position.

**The enthymeme is not a "truncated syllogism" in a pejorative sense**
The omission of the premise is not sloppiness — it is appropriate to the medium (oral, real-time, to a non-specialist audience). The enthymeme is efficient and engages the audience's own reasoning. The problem arises when the missing premise is false or exploits bias; not when it is genuinely shared.

**Aristotle was not prescribing manipulation**
Aristotle's Rhetoric is descriptive (this is how persuasion works) and prescriptive (this is how to persuade well). The emotions catalog is not a manual for emotional manipulation — it is an analysis of how emotions function in rational persuasion. Aristotle insists that rhetoric is most effective when used in defense of truth (Book I, Ch. 1).

**Plato is more nuanced than "rhetoric bad"**
In the Phaedrus, Plato allows that a philosophical rhetoric is possible. His objection is to rhetoric divorced from genuine knowledge — which describes most actual rhetoric. The Gorgias is the more polemical text; the Phaedrus is more nuanced. Modern scholars (notably Jasper Neel) have argued that Plato actually USES sophisticated rhetoric to argue against rhetoric.
