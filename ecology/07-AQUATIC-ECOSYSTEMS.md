# Aquatic Ecosystems — Lakes, Streams, Estuaries, Coral Reefs

## The Big Picture

Aquatic ecosystems cover ~71% of Earth's surface (ocean) and host the majority of Earth's biodiversity at the phylum level. They share core ecological principles with terrestrial systems but differ fundamentally in: three-dimensional structure (water column), the dominance of dissolved nutrients and gases, the short lifespan of primary producers (phytoplankton turnover in days vs trees in centuries), and the role of stratification in controlling productivity and oxygen.

```
+---------------------------------------------------------------+
|              AQUATIC ECOSYSTEM TYPES                          |
```

## Lake Stratification — Physical Structure

Lakes stratify by temperature because water density is temperature-dependent:

```
SUMMER STRATIFICATION:

  EPILIMNION (surface layer):
  ~20–25°C; well-mixed by wind; high O₂; high light
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  THERMOCLINE (metalimnion):
  Steep temperature gradient; ~10–20°C
  ═════════════════════════════════════════
  HYPOLIMNION (bottom layer):
  Cold (4–8°C); dark; low O₂; nutrient-rich
  (sedimented nutrients can't mix up)

WATER DENSITY MAXIMUM AT 4°C:
  Ice (0°C) is LESS dense than water → floats
  → Lakes freeze from the top down → fish survive under ice
  → If ice were denser (like most materials), lakes would freeze solid

FALL OVERTURN:
  Surface cools → density increases → sinks
  → Whole lake mixes (fall turnover)
  → Nutrients from hypolimnion brought to surface
  → Autumn algal bloom common

FLUID DYNAMICS CONNECTION:
  Lake stratification is the same physics as atmospheric lapse rate: both are
  gravitationally stable density gradients (dense fluid below light fluid).
  The thermocline is the lake's equivalent of the atmospheric inversion layer —
  it suppresses vertical mixing (convective stability).
  Buoyancy frequency (Brunt-Väisälä): N² = -(g/ρ)(dρ/dz) > 0 → stable stratification
  When N² < 0 → convective instability → mixing (fall overturn is N² → 0 as surface cools)
  Same N² appears in internal wave theory, ocean circulation, and atmospheric science.

SPRING OVERTURN:
  Ice melts → 0°C water warms → reaches ~4°C density max
  → Sinks; second mixing event
  → Second algal bloom
```

**Dimictic lakes** — mix twice per year (spring + fall): common in temperate zones.
**Monomictic** — mix once (continuously cold or warm, mixes only once). **Meromictic** — permanently stratified (never mixes): dense salty bottom prevents turnover.

## Eutrophication — Lake Degradation

Nutrient enrichment → increased productivity → oxygen depletion:

```
OLIGOTROPHIC              MESOTROPHIC             EUTROPHIC
(nutrient-poor)           (moderate)              (nutrient-rich)

Clear water               Moderate clarity        Turbid (algae)
Deep light penetration    Moderate productivity   Shallow Secchi depth
Low algae                 Some algae              Algal blooms
High O₂ throughout        O₂ moderate below       Hypolimnion anoxic
                          thermocline              in summer
Salmonids                 Mixed fish               Carp, catfish
                          community                (hypoxia-tolerant)

                                                   HYPEREUTROPHIC:
                                                   Extreme; near-sterile
                                                   bottom; fish kills;
                                                   cyanobacteria blooms
                                                   (possible toxins)
```

**Phosphorus as key driver (Schindler's experiments):**
- Whole-lake experiments at ELA (Experimental Lakes Area), Ontario, 1972
- Half-lake fertilized with N+P+C → algal bloom; other half N+C only → no bloom
- Confirmed P as primary limiting nutrient in freshwater
- Led to phosphate bans in detergents (significant policy success)

**Cyanobacteria toxins** — at high eutrophication, cyanobacteria (blue-green "algae") may dominate. Some genera (Microcystis, Anabaena) produce microcystins (liver toxins), anatoxins (neurotoxins). Harmful algal blooms (HABs) → drinking water shutoffs, dog deaths, recreational closures.

## Open Ocean Ecology — Pelagic Zones and Productivity

```
PELAGIC ZONE DEPTH STRUCTURE:

EPIPELAGIC (0–200 m): Photic zone — light penetrates, photosynthesis possible
  Primary production by phytoplankton (diatoms, cyanobacteria, coccolithophores)
  ~50% of global NPP from phytoplankton despite very low standing biomass
  Dominated by microbial loop: bacteria → protists → zooplankton

MESOPELAGIC (200–1000 m): "Twilight zone" — dim light, no photosynthesis
  Highest biodiversity of pelagic zones; largest fish biomass by weight
  Diel vertical migrants: zooplankton and fish descend by day, ascend at night
  Biological pump: sinking organic particles carry carbon to depth

BATHYPELAGIC (1000–4000 m): No light; depends entirely on sinking particles
  Bioluminescence common; chemoreception dominates sensory ecology
  Low temperature (~4°C), high pressure, low food

ABYSSOPELAGIC (4000–6000 m): Abyssal plain; manganese nodules
  Extremely low productivity; food web dependent on "marine snow" input

HADALPELAGIC (6000–11,000 m): Ocean trenches
  Highest pressure environment on Earth; amphipod crustaceans dominant
```

**Ocean productivity patterns:**

```
LOW PRODUCTIVITY (oligotrophic gyres):
  Open ocean subtropical gyres: ~100 gC/m²/yr
  Nutrient-depleted: permanent thermocline prevents deep nutrient upwelling
  "Blue deserts" — clear blue water = low chlorophyll = low phytoplankton

HIGH PRODUCTIVITY ZONES:
  Coastal upwelling: ~400 gC/m²/yr (California, Peru/Humboldt, Benguela, Canary)
  → Trade winds drive Ekman transport → surface water pushed offshore
  → Deep, cold, nutrient-rich water rises to surface
  → Peru upwelling: ~10% of global fish catch from ~0.1% of ocean area

  Polar seas: seasonal high productivity (summer light + deep winter mixing = nutrients)
  Continental shelves: shallow + nutrient inputs from rivers = high productivity

EL NIÑO IMPACT:
  Normal: upwelling on, cold SST, high productivity, anchovy abundant
  El Niño: trade winds weaken, warm water floods eastern Pacific
  → Thermocline deepens → upwelling suppressed → nutrients cut off
  → Phytoplankton crash → anchovy crash → sea lion crash → seabird crash
  → Trophic cascade from ocean circulation change
```

**Thermohaline circulation (global ocean conveyor):**
```
MECHANISM:
  North Atlantic: warm surface water flows north → cools + becomes saltier by evaporation
  → Becomes dense → sinks (NADW: North Atlantic Deep Water)
  → Flows south along Atlantic floor → enters Southern Ocean → spreads globally
  → Upwells in Indian + Pacific → returns as surface current

TIMESCALE: ~1000 years for complete circuit
ECOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  Controls nutrient distribution globally
  AMOC (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation) — a regime shift risk:
  Freshwater from Greenland ice melt → reduces NADW formation → AMOC weakens
  → Less heat transport to Northern Europe → regional cooling
  → Altered monsoons, ocean productivity patterns
```

## Stream Ecology — River Continuum Concept

Rivers change in character from headwaters to mouth:

```
RIVER CONTINUUM CONCEPT (Vannote et al. 1980):

HEADWATERS (order 1-3):    MID-REACHES (4-6):    LOWER RIVER (7-12):

Shaded by riparian         Open canopy;           Large, turbid;
trees (allochthonous       autochthonous           floodplain dominated;
inputs: leaf litter)       production              fine particles;
                           dominates               backwater habitats
Invertebrate guilds:       P/R > 1                P/R < 1 (turbid)
"shredders" (leaves)       "collectors"
"scrapers" (algae)         "scrapers" dominate    "collectors"
                                                   "predators"

COARSE PARTICLE   →    FINE PARTICLE    →    VERY FINE PARTICLE
ORGANIC MATTER         ORGANIC MATTER         ORGANIC MATTER
(CPOM)                 (FPOM)                 (VFOM)
```

**Allochthonous vs autochthonous input:**
- Allochthonous = from outside the stream (leaf litter, woody debris) — dominates in shaded headwaters
- Autochthonous = produced within the stream (algae) — dominates in open mid-reaches

**Riparian zones** — critical for stream ecosystem function:
- Input of organic matter (leaves, wood)
- Temperature buffering (shading)
- Bank stability (roots)
- Nutrient uptake (riparian vegetation removes N + P before entering stream)
- Wildlife corridors

## Estuaries — Where Rivers Meet the Sea

Estuaries are semi-enclosed coastal water bodies where freshwater meets saltwater. Among the most productive ecosystems on Earth:

```
SALINITY GRADIENT:
River (0 ppt) ──────────────────→ Open ocean (35 ppt)
         Oligohaline  Mesohaline  Polyhaline
           (0-5)       (5-18)      (18-30+)

MIXING TYPES:
  Salt wedge: Saltwater intrudes along bottom (dense);
              Freshwater flows over top (light); minimal mixing
              (Strong river flow; Mississippi)

  Partially mixed: Wind/tidal mixing; some vertical gradient
                   (Most temperate estuaries; Chesapeake Bay)

  Well-mixed: Strong tides; nearly uniform salinity throughout
              (Small estuaries with strong tidal forcing)

KEY ESTUARY PROCESSES:
  Flocculation: Freshwater colloids aggregate when hit salt → particle sedimentation
  Nutrient trap: Filter particles from upriver; nutrient cycling hotspot
  Turbidity maximum: zone of highest turbidity near salt intrusion
```

**Why are estuaries so productive?** — Nutrient inputs from rivers + shallow depth (high light/m²) + tidal mixing maintaining nutrients in the photic zone. Chesapeake Bay: ~300 gC/m²/yr (very high).

**Estuary functions:** Spawning/nursery habitat for many marine fish (80% of commercial fish spend juvenile phase in estuaries). Nutrient processing. Carbon burial. Storm protection. Oysters = classic keystone filter feeder (historically filtered entire Chesapeake volume every 3–4 days; now takes a year due to population collapse).

## Deep Sea and Chemosynthetic Ecosystems

```
HYDROTHERMAL VENTS (discovered 1977, Galápagos Rift):
  Found at mid-ocean ridges: magma heats seawater → hydrothermal fluid
  Temperatures: 2°C ambient → 350–400°C at vent opening
  Chemistry: H₂S, CH₄, H₂ in vent fluid (from rock-water interaction)

CHEMOSYNTHESIS (base of vent food web):
  No sunlight → no photosynthesis
  Chemoautotrophic bacteria: oxidize H₂S → CO₂ + organic carbon
    H₂S + CO₂ + O₂ → organic carbon + H₂SO₄
  Bacteria either free-living or endosymbiotic in tube worms, clams, mussels

ECOLOGICAL STRUCTURE:
  Tube worms (Riftia pachyptila): up to 2 m; fastest-growing marine invertebrate
    → Endosymbiotic sulfur-oxidizing bacteria in trophosome organ
  Giant clams (Calyptogena): filter-feed bacteria + harbor endosymbionts
  Pompeii worm (Alvinella pompejana): lives at ~60°C at vent walls
  Vent shrimp (Rimicaris): clustered on vent walls; bacteria-grazing
  Octopus, crabs: higher-trophic-level predators

VENT ECOLOGY TIMELINE:
  Vents are ephemeral (active for decades; then shut off)
  Species must disperse to new vents → high isolation, high endemism
  Parallel evolution at geographically separated vent fields

COLD SEEPS (different energy source):
  Methane + sulfide from sediment → methane-oxidizing archaea (anaerobic)
  + sulfate-reducing bacteria → same chemosynthesis base
  Slower than vents; persist longer
  Methane hydrate (clathrate): ice-like solid at cold, high-pressure conditions
    → Vast stores in continental margins (possible energy resource / climate risk)
```

**Abyssal ecology:**
```
ABYSSAL PLAIN (3000–6000 m depth):
  ~60% of Earth's surface; least-studied biome
  Food source: marine snow (particle flux from surface, ~1% of surface production)
  Biodiversity surprisingly high: mega/macrofauna adapted to food scarcity
  Bioturbation: bottom-dwelling fauna mix sediments → affects carbon burial rates
  Manganese nodules: mineral concretions ~5–10 cm; rich in Mn, Ni, Co, Cu
    → Target of deep-sea mining proposals; ecological risk poorly understood
  Disturbance: extremely rare (centuries between physical disturbances)
    → Resilience very low; recovery from mining disturbance estimated >100 years
```

## Coral Reef Ecology

Coral reefs occupy <0.1% of ocean area but harbor ~25–30% of all marine species — the "rainforests of the sea":

```
CORAL BIOLOGY:
  Coral polyp: tiny animal (Cnidarian; like tiny anemone)
  Endosymbiosis: Zooxanthellae (dinoflagellate algae) live within coral tissue
  Zooxanthellae photosynthesize → provide ~70–90% of coral energy needs
  Coral provides: CO₂, N, P, shelter to algae

REEF STRUCTURE:
  Reef crest (high wave energy; coralline algae)
  Fore reef (diverse; hard corals; steep slope)
  Back reef / lagoon (patch reefs; soft corals; sediment)
```

**Coral bleaching:**
```
THERMAL STRESS (SST > ~1-2°C above seasonal max):
         ↓
Zooxanthellae stressed → produce reactive oxygen species
         ↓
Coral expels zooxanthellae → turns white ("bleaching")
         ↓
Without zooxanthellae: coral starves; survives weeks to months
         ↓
If stress passes: Zooxanthellae recolonize → coral recovers
If stress prolonged or severe: CORAL DEATH → algae overgrowth

BLEACHING SEVERITY:
  Degree Heating Weeks (DHW): cumulative thermal stress measure
  4 DHW → bleaching possible
  8 DHW → significant bleaching + mortality
```

**Mass bleaching events:**
- 1998: First global mass bleaching; ~16% of world's reefs died (strong El Niño)
- 2016: Most severe to date; ~50% of Great Barrier Reef northern section bleached
- 2023–2024: Fourth global mass bleaching event; ~54% of world's coral reef areas experienced bleaching

**Coral reef degradation drivers (multiple stressors):**
1. Ocean warming (bleaching)
2. Ocean acidification (lower pH → weaker carbonate skeletons; pH dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 since industrial revolution = 30% more acidic)
3. Overfishing (removes herbivores → algae overgrowth)
4. Eutrophication/sedimentation from runoff
5. Physical damage (ship groundings, anchor damage, crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks)

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Aquatic observation | Interpretation |
|--------------------|---------------|
| Lake epilimnion warm, hypolimnion cold + anoxic | Stratified summer lake; nutrients trapped below thermocline |
| Fall algal bloom in lake | Fall overturn mixing nutrients to surface |
| Lake turns green/turbid, fish dying | Eutrophication + hypoxia; check P and N inputs |
| Cyanobacteria bloom, blue-green scum | Hypereutrophic; possible cyanotoxins; public health hazard |
| Stream headwaters dominated by shredder invertebrates | Allochthonous-dominated; leaf litter input critical |
| Coral turned white but alive | Thermal bleaching; need temperature relief within weeks-months |
| Estuarine fish nursery function lost | Check turbidity, dissolved O₂, salinity changes from upstream alteration |

## Common Confusion Points

**Coral is an animal, not a plant** — Coral polyps are animals (Cnidarians). The color and much of their energy comes from symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae). Bleached coral is coral that lost its algal symbiont — the white is the coral's white calcium carbonate skeleton showing through.

**Eutrophication ≠ just green water** — The process includes nutrient enrichment → algal growth → decomposition → O₂ depletion → anoxia. The visible green water is just one symptom. The deadly part is the oxygen depletion in the hypolimnion that kills fish and benthic invertebrates.

**Estuaries are variable, not uniformly salty** — Salinity varies enormously by tide, season, storm, and location within the estuary. Organisms living there must be euryhaline (tolerant of wide salinity range) or restricted to specific salinity zones.

**Freshwater fish can't survive in ocean (and vice versa)** — Osmoregulation: freshwater fish are hypertonic to environment → excrete lots of dilute urine. Saltwater fish are hypotonic → drink constantly, excrete concentrated urine. Moving a freshwater fish to seawater → dehydration/death (and vice versa). Euryhaline species (salmon, eels, killifish) can regulate across the full range.
