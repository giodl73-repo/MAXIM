# Biogeochemical Cycles — Carbon, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Sulfur

## The Big Picture

While energy flows through ecosystems and is lost as heat, nutrients cycle. The same carbon atom might be in a plant, soil, atmosphere, ocean, and fossil fuel over millions of years. Human activities have dramatically accelerated most biogeochemical cycles — creating imbalances that drive climate change, eutrophication, ozone depletion, and ocean acidification.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              BIOGEOCHEMICAL CYCLE FRAMEWORK                       |
|                                                                   |
|  CYCLE    MAIN RESERVOIR    LIMITING    HUMAN PERTURBATION       |
```

## Carbon Cycle

### Natural Carbon Cycle

```
ATMOSPHERE
(870 PgC as CO₂, ~420 ppm)
     ↕                          ↕
     |  Photosynthesis (~120 PgC/yr) │ Respiration (~120 PgC/yr)
     ↓                          ↑
LAND BIOTA                     SOIL
(600 PgC vegetation)           (1500–2400 PgC organic matter)
     |                              |
     | Physical sedimentation       | Decomposition
     ↓                              ↓
FRESHWATER RIVERS → OCEAN (38,000 PgC, mostly DIC)
     |                              |
     | CaCO₃ sedimentation         | Marine photosynthesis
     ↓                              | (~50 PgC/yr)
SEDIMENTS → LITHOSPHERE            ↓ Marine respiration
(100,000,000+ PgC in fossil fuels, CaCO₃)
```

**Carbon residence times:**
- Atmosphere: ~5 years (fast exchange with land biota)
- Ocean surface: ~decades (fast exchange with atmosphere)
- Deep ocean: ~1000 years (thermohaline circulation)
- Soil organic matter: decades to centuries
- Sedimentary rock: millions of years

**The ocean as carbon sink:** ~2.5 PgC/yr net uptake (about 25% of anthropogenic CO₂ emissions). Solubility pump (CO₂ dissolves in cold, dense water → sinks) + biological pump (phytoplankton fix C → sink as particles).

### Human Perturbation

```
ANTHROPOGENIC CARBON FLUXES (approximate, early 2020s):
  Fossil fuel combustion:  +9–10 PgC/yr (↑ CO₂)
  Land use change:         +1.5–2 PgC/yr (deforestation)
  Total emission:          ~11–12 PgC/yr

  Land uptake:             ~3.5 PgC/yr (carbon sink)
  Ocean uptake:            ~2.5 PgC/yr (carbon sink)
  Atmospheric increase:    ~5–6 PgC/yr (↑ CO₂ ~2.5 ppm/yr)
```

CO₂ concentration trajectory: pre-industrial 280 ppm → 1960: 315 ppm → 2024: ~424 ppm. Annual rate of increase ~2.5 ppm/yr and accelerating.

**Permafrost carbon feedback** — ~1.5 trillion tonnes of C stored in Arctic permafrost (twice the current atmospheric stock). As permafrost thaws: microbial decomposition releases CO₂ and CH₄. A positive feedback that could double or triple the warming from anthropogenic emissions — the most important "tipping point" in the carbon cycle.

## Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is ~78% of the atmosphere but almost entirely unavailable as N₂ — the triple bond (N≡N) is extremely strong (941 kJ/mol). Biological productivity is often N-limited.

```
NITROGEN CYCLE:

       N₂ (atmosphere, vast reservoir)
        |
        | N-fixation: N₂ → NH₄⁺ (ammonium)
        | (requires nitrogenase enzyme + low O₂)
        | Biological: Rhizobium (legumes), cyanobacteria, free-living
        | Industrial: Haber-Bosch (N₂ + H₂ → NH₃; 400°C, 200 atm)
        ↓
   AMMONIUM (NH₄⁺) in soil/water
        |
        | Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻
        | (aerobic bacteria: Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter)
        ↓
   NITRATE (NO₃⁻)
        |        ↑ Plant uptake
        |        | (NH₄⁺ and NO₃⁻ both usable)
        |
        | Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂O → N₂ (returns to atmosphere)
        | (anaerobic bacteria; waterlogged/anoxic soils)
        ↓
   ORGANIC NITROGEN (in organisms)
        |
        | Ammonification (decomposition)
        ↓
   NH₄⁺ (back to ammonium pool)
```

**Nitrogen limitation:**
- Most terrestrial ecosystems are N-limited (adding N → increased plant growth)
- Human activities have roughly doubled global N fixation — now ~more N fixed by Haber-Bosch than by all natural processes combined

**Haber-Bosch impact (→ see agriculture/05-FERTILIZERS-PESTICIDES.md):**
- ~50% of N in human bodies today comes from synthetic fertilizer
- Enables food production for ~50% of current global population
- Also drives reactive nitrogen cascade (eutrophication, N₂O, acid rain, ozone)

**Nitrogen cascade:**
```
One N atom can:
  1. Acidify precipitation (as NOx → HNO₃)
  2. Fertilize forest (deposition)
  3. Cause eutrophication (runoff → stream/estuary)
  4. Contribute to ozone formation (NOx + VOC + sunlight)
  5. Contribute to N₂O greenhouse effect (soil denitrification)
  6. Acidify soil (nitrification → H⁺)
```

## Phosphorus Cycle

Phosphorus has NO atmospheric reservoir. The cycle is purely geological (weathering) + biological:

```
PHOSPHORUS CYCLE:

ROCK (P in apatite: Ca₅(PO₄)₃)
     |
     | Weathering (very slow; ~1 Tg P/yr natural)
     ↓
SOIL INORGANIC P (phosphate, PO₄³⁻)
     ↑           ↓ Plant uptake (mycorrhizae critical)
     |           ↓
     | Decomposition  BIOTA (organic P)
     ↑           ↓
     |           ↓ Runoff/erosion
     |           ↓
     |        RIVERS
     |           ↓
     |        OCEAN (sedimentation → rock, very slow)
     |           ↓
     +-------- (UPLIFT over geologic time → back to rock)

HUMAN INTERVENTIONS:
  Mining phosphate rock (finite resource; ~300-400 yr at current rates)
  Fertilizer application (much P lost to waterways)
  Eutrophication from P runoff
```

**P as the ultimate limiting nutrient:** In many freshwater lakes, P is the primary limiting nutrient for algal growth (Schindler 1974: whole-lake experiments). Reducing P inputs = primary strategy for controlling eutrophication.

**Ecological stoichiometry** — Redfield ratio (1934): marine phytoplankton have approximately constant C:N:P = 106:16:1 (by atoms). Deviations from this ratio indicate which element is limiting. Liebig's minimum applied to nutrient ratios.

Mass-balance connection: the Redfield ratio is a biological stoichiometric constraint, directly analogous to process mass balances in chemical engineering. A stoichiometric equation has fixed atom ratios; a biological organism has fixed elemental composition demands. If the environment supplies N:P at a ratio far above 16:1, phosphorus is limiting (excess N is unused). If far below 16:1, nitrogen is limiting. Monitoring the N:P ratio of inflowing water relative to Redfield 16:1 immediately identifies which element will limit phytoplankton growth — the same logic as checking limiting reagent in a chemical reaction.

## Nitrogen and Phosphorus: Human Perturbation and Eutrophication

```
EUTROPHICATION PROCESS:

Agricultural/urban/sewage N + P → enters water body
       ↓
Algal bloom (phytoplankton grow explosively)
       ↓
Algae die → detritus
       ↓
Bacterial decomposition consumes O₂
       ↓
HYPOXIA / ANOXIA (oxygen depletion)
       ↓
Dead zone (no fish, benthic invertebrates die)

EXAMPLES:
Gulf of Mexico dead zone: ~22,000 km² (Mississippi River N + P)
Baltic Sea: world's largest human-caused dead zone
Chesapeake Bay: phosphorus + nitrogen from Ag + urban runoff
China coastal waters: severe eutrophication from fertilizer
```

**Phosphorus control is more effective** in freshwater (lakes typically P-limited). **Nitrogen control** needed for coastal marine systems (typically N-limited). Requires both for estuaries.

## Sulfur Cycle

```
NATURAL SULFUR CYCLE:
  Volcanic emissions: SO₂ (natural aerosol; cooling)
  Marine DMS (dimethylsulfide): phytoplankton emission
    → oxidized to sulfate aerosols
    → cloud condensation nuclei (CLAW hypothesis: climate feedback)
  Weathering: sulfide minerals → sulfate in water

ANTHROPOGENIC PERTURBATION:
  Fossil fuel combustion: SO₂ (coal has 0.5–5% S by weight)
    → Acid rain: SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid)
    → pH drop in lakes/soils → fish kills, forest damage
    (1970s–1990s; largely controlled in developed world by scrubbers)
  Diesel engines: particulate sulfur (primary pollution)

SUCCESS STORY: US Clean Air Act SO₂ cap-and-trade
  SO₂ emissions peaked ~1970 (~30 Tg/yr) → now <5 Tg/yr
  Lake acidification reversed in many regions
```

## Nutrient Cycling in Ecosystems — Tight vs Leaky

**Hubbard Brook Watershed (Bormann & Likens, 1960s-70s):**
Classic ecosystem nutrient cycling experiment. Compared forested vs clear-cut watersheds:

```
INTACT FOREST:
  Nutrients taken up rapidly by vegetation
  Very little leakage to streams
  "Tight" cycling → nutrient-conservative

CLEAR-FELLED WATERSHED:
  No vegetation uptake
  Nitrate export increased 40-60× (from ~1 to ~50 kg/ha/yr)
  Ca, K, Mg exports also spiked
  pH of streams dropped
  LESSON: Vegetation is critical for nutrient retention
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Ecological context | Relevant cycle consideration |
|------------------|------------------------------|
| Freshwater lake turning green | P-limited eutrophication; control P inputs |
| Coastal dead zone | N + P from agriculture; N often limiting near coast |
| Why old-growth forest is C-neutral | NEP ≈ 0; decomposition matches production |
| Wetland as C sink | Anaerobic decomposition → slow; CH₄ flux offsets some |
| Boreal peatlands | Huge C stocks; slow decomposition; warming = C release risk |
| N₂O emissions from fertilized fields | Denitrification in waterlogged soil; N₂O = 273× CO₂ GWP |

## Common Confusion Points

**N₂ unavailability despite abundance** — 78% of air is N₂ but the triple bond is so strong that only nitrogenase (and the industrial Haber-Bosch) can break it. Organisms can't use N₂ directly any more than they can use O₂ from water.

**CO₂ vs carbon** — Be precise: "400 ppm CO₂" refers to CO₂ molecules; "400 GtC" refers to carbon atoms. CO₂ has molecular weight 44; C has atomic weight 12. To convert: 1 PgC = 3.67 Pg CO₂. Confusing C and CO₂ leads to factor-of-3.67 errors in carbon budget discussions.

**Phosphorus "peak phosphate"** — Phosphate rock is a finite, non-renewable resource (unlike C and N, there's no atmospheric P reservoir to draw from). Current minable reserves may last 300–400 years at current rates. Recycling P from sewage/food waste is an increasingly important sustainability challenge.

**Denitrification requires anoxic conditions** — Denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂) occurs in waterlogged, oxygen-depleted soils. Aerated soils don't denitrify. This is why wetlands are critical for N removal: they alternate between aerobic (nitrification) and anaerobic (denitrification) zones, completing the N cycle locally.
