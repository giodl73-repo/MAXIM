# Biogeochemical Cycles ‚ÄĒ Carbon, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Sulfur

## The Big Picture

While energy flows through ecosystems and is lost as heat, nutrients cycle. The same carbon atom might be in a plant, soil, atmosphere, ocean, and fossil fuel over millions of years. Human activities have dramatically accelerated most biogeochemical cycles ‚ÄĒ creating imbalances that drive climate change, eutrophication, ozone depletion, and ocean acidification.

## Carbon Cycle

### Natural Carbon Cycle

```
ATMOSPHERE
(870 PgC as CO‚āā, ~420 ppm)
     ‚Üē                          ‚Üē
     |  Photosynthesis (~120 PgC/yr) ‚Ēā Respiration (~120 PgC/yr)
     ‚Üď                          ‚ÜĎ
LAND BIOTA                     SOIL
(600 PgC vegetation)           (1500‚Äď2400 PgC organic matter)
     |                              |
     | Physical sedimentation       | Decomposition
     ‚Üď                              ‚Üď
FRESHWATER RIVERS ‚Üí OCEAN (38,000 PgC, mostly DIC)
     |                              |
     | CaCO‚āÉ sedimentation         | Marine photosynthesis
     ‚Üď                              | (~50 PgC/yr)
SEDIMENTS ‚Üí LITHOSPHERE            ‚Üď Marine respiration
(100,000,000+ PgC in fossil fuels, CaCO‚āÉ)
```

**Carbon residence times:**
- Atmosphere: ~5 years (fast exchange with land biota)
- Ocean surface: ~decades (fast exchange with atmosphere)
- Deep ocean: ~1000 years (thermohaline circulation)
- Soil organic matter: decades to centuries
- Sedimentary rock: millions of years

**The ocean as carbon sink:** ~2.5 PgC/yr net uptake (about 25% of anthropogenic CO‚āā emissions). Solubility pump (CO‚āā dissolves in cold, dense water ‚Üí sinks) + biological pump (phytoplankton fix C ‚Üí sink as particles).

### Human Perturbation

```
ANTHROPOGENIC CARBON FLUXES (approximate, early 2020s):
  Fossil fuel combustion:  +9‚Äď10 PgC/yr (‚ÜĎ CO‚āā)
  Land use change:         +1.5‚Äď2 PgC/yr (deforestation)
  Total emission:          ~11‚Äď12 PgC/yr

  Land uptake:             ~3.5 PgC/yr (carbon sink)
  Ocean uptake:            ~2.5 PgC/yr (carbon sink)
  Atmospheric increase:    ~5‚Äď6 PgC/yr (‚ÜĎ CO‚āā ~2.5 ppm/yr)
```

CO‚āā concentration trajectory: pre-industrial 280 ppm ‚Üí 1960: 315 ppm ‚Üí 2024: ~424 ppm. Annual rate of increase ~2.5 ppm/yr and accelerating.

**Permafrost carbon feedback** ‚ÄĒ ~1.5 trillion tonnes of C stored in Arctic permafrost (twice the current atmospheric stock). As permafrost thaws: microbial decomposition releases CO‚āā and CH‚āĄ. A positive feedback that could double or triple the warming from anthropogenic emissions ‚ÄĒ the most important "tipping point" in the carbon cycle.

## Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is ~78% of the atmosphere but almost entirely unavailable as N‚āā ‚ÄĒ the triple bond (N‚Č°N) is extremely strong (941 kJ/mol). Biological productivity is often N-limited.

```
NITROGEN CYCLE:

       N‚āā (atmosphere, vast reservoir)
        |
        | N-fixation: N‚āā ‚Üí NH‚āĄ‚Āļ (ammonium)
        | (requires nitrogenase enzyme + low O‚āā)
        | Biological: Rhizobium (legumes), cyanobacteria, free-living
        | Industrial: Haber-Bosch (N‚āā + H‚āā ‚Üí NH‚āÉ; 400¬įC, 200 atm)
        ‚Üď
   AMMONIUM (NH‚āĄ‚Āļ) in soil/water
        |
        | Nitrification: NH‚āĄ‚Āļ ‚Üí NO‚āā‚ĀĽ ‚Üí NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ
        | (aerobic bacteria: Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter)
        ‚Üď
   NITRATE (NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ)
        |        ‚ÜĎ Plant uptake
        |        | (NH‚āĄ‚Āļ and NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ both usable)
        |
        | Denitrification: NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ ‚Üí N‚āāO ‚Üí N‚āā (returns to atmosphere)
        | (anaerobic bacteria; waterlogged/anoxic soils)
        ‚Üď
   ORGANIC NITROGEN (in organisms)
        |
        | Ammonification (decomposition)
        ‚Üď
   NH‚āĄ‚Āļ (back to ammonium pool)
```

**Nitrogen limitation:**
- Most terrestrial ecosystems are N-limited (adding N ‚Üí increased plant growth)
- Human activities have roughly doubled global N fixation ‚ÄĒ now ~more N fixed by Haber-Bosch than by all natural processes combined

**Haber-Bosch impact (‚Üí see agriculture/05-FERTILIZERS-PESTICIDES.md):**
- ~50% of N in human bodies today comes from synthetic fertilizer
- Enables food production for ~50% of current global population
- Also drives reactive nitrogen cascade (eutrophication, N‚āāO, acid rain, ozone)

**Nitrogen cascade:**
```
One N atom can:
  1. Acidify precipitation (as NOx ‚Üí HNO‚āÉ)
  2. Fertilize forest (deposition)
  3. Cause eutrophication (runoff ‚Üí stream/estuary)
  4. Contribute to ozone formation (NOx + VOC + sunlight)
  5. Contribute to N‚āāO greenhouse effect (soil denitrification)
  6. Acidify soil (nitrification ‚Üí H‚Āļ)
```

## Phosphorus Cycle

Phosphorus has NO atmospheric reservoir. The cycle is purely geological (weathering) + biological:

```
PHOSPHORUS CYCLE:

ROCK (P in apatite: Ca‚āÖ(PO‚āĄ)‚āÉ)
     |
     | Weathering (very slow; ~1 Tg P/yr natural)
     ‚Üď
SOIL INORGANIC P (phosphate, PO‚āĄ¬≥‚ĀĽ)
     ‚ÜĎ           ‚Üď Plant uptake (mycorrhizae critical)
     |           ‚Üď
     | Decomposition  BIOTA (organic P)
     ‚ÜĎ           ‚Üď
     |           ‚Üď Runoff/erosion
     |           ‚Üď
     |        RIVERS
     |           ‚Üď
     |        OCEAN (sedimentation ‚Üí rock, very slow)
     |           ‚Üď
     +-------- (UPLIFT over geologic time ‚Üí back to rock)

HUMAN INTERVENTIONS:
  Mining phosphate rock (finite resource; ~300-400 yr at current rates)
  Fertilizer application (much P lost to waterways)
  Eutrophication from P runoff
```

**P as the ultimate limiting nutrient:** In many freshwater lakes, P is the primary limiting nutrient for algal growth (Schindler 1974: whole-lake experiments). Reducing P inputs = primary strategy for controlling eutrophication.

**Ecological stoichiometry** ‚ÄĒ Redfield ratio (1934): marine phytoplankton have approximately constant C:N:P = 106:16:1 (by atoms). Deviations from this ratio indicate which element is limiting. Liebig's minimum applied to nutrient ratios.

## Nitrogen and Phosphorus: Human Perturbation and Eutrophication

```
EUTROPHICATION PROCESS:

Agricultural/urban/sewage N + P ‚Üí enters water body
       ‚Üď
Algal bloom (phytoplankton grow explosively)
       ‚Üď
Algae die ‚Üí detritus
       ‚Üď
Bacterial decomposition consumes O‚āā
       ‚Üď
HYPOXIA / ANOXIA (oxygen depletion)
       ‚Üď
Dead zone (no fish, benthic invertebrates die)

EXAMPLES:
Gulf of Mexico dead zone: ~22,000 km¬≤ (Mississippi River N + P)
Baltic Sea: world's largest human-caused dead zone
Chesapeake Bay: phosphorus + nitrogen from Ag + urban runoff
China coastal waters: severe eutrophication from fertilizer
```

**Phosphorus control is more effective** in freshwater (lakes typically P-limited). **Nitrogen control** needed for coastal marine systems (typically N-limited). Requires both for estuaries.


## Nutrient Cycling in Ecosystems ‚ÄĒ Tight vs Leaky

**Hubbard Brook Watershed (Bormann & Likens, 1960s-70s):**
Classic ecosystem nutrient cycling experiment. Compared forested vs clear-cut watersheds:

```
INTACT FOREST:
  Nutrients taken up rapidly by vegetation
  Very little leakage to streams
  "Tight" cycling ‚Üí nutrient-conservative

CLEAR-FELLED WATERSHED:
  No vegetation uptake
  Nitrate export increased 40-60√ó (from ~1 to ~50 kg/ha/yr)
  Ca, K, Mg exports also spiked
  pH of streams dropped
  LESSON: Vegetation is critical for nutrient retention
```

## Common Confusion Points

**N‚āā unavailability despite abundance** ‚ÄĒ 78% of air is N‚āā but the triple bond is so strong that only nitrogenase (and the industrial Haber-Bosch) can break it. Organisms can't use N‚āā directly any more than they can use O‚āā from water.

**CO‚āā vs carbon** ‚ÄĒ Be precise: "400 ppm CO‚āā" refers to CO‚āā molecules; "400 GtC" refers to carbon atoms. CO‚āā has molecular weight 44; C has atomic weight 12. To convert: 1 PgC = 3.67 Pg CO‚āā. Confusing C and CO‚āā leads to factor-of-3.67 errors in carbon budget discussions.

**Phosphorus "peak phosphate"** ‚ÄĒ Phosphate rock is a finite, non-renewable resource (unlike C and N, there's no atmospheric P reservoir to draw from). Current minable reserves may last 300‚Äď400 years at current rates. Recycling P from sewage/food waste is an increasingly important sustainability challenge.

**Denitrification requires anoxic conditions** ‚ÄĒ Denitrification (NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ ‚Üí N‚āā) occurs in waterlogged, oxygen-depleted soils. Aerated soils don't denitrify. This is why wetlands are critical for N removal: they alternate between aerobic (nitrification) and anaerobic (denitrification) zones, completing the N cycle locally.
