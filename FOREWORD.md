# Foreword

---

This is a personal encyclopedia. It exists because I wanted one.

Not a textbook. Not a curriculum. Not a course you take and forget. A reference — the kind of book you keep on the shelf and pull down when you need to remember how something actually works. Fifty-two volumes covering the scope of what humans know, organized so you can find things and so the structure itself teaches you something about how knowledge fits together.

The library is organized as a deck of cards. Thirteen sections, four volumes each. Fifty-two volumes. The sections follow an arc from the immediate and tangible — what can I eat, what is this rock, how do I make things — through the formal and abstract — mathematics, computing, the philosophy of knowledge itself. People sit at the center: the Ace, the only rank that is both origin and culmination.

Every guide follows the same format. A landscape diagram first, so you know where you are. Then layers downward into detail. ASCII diagrams throughout — no images that can't be reproduced with a typewriter. Comparison tables. Decision cheat sheets. Bridges from older concepts to newer ones, because most knowledge didn't appear from nothing; it replaced or extended something that came before. No guide assumes you are a beginner. No guide assumes you already know the specific topic. The goal is always the same: give a capable reader enough structure to navigate a field they haven't studied, and enough depth that the guide remains useful after they have.

The name is MAXIM — a short statement expressing a general truth. It is also the name of a boxer who chose his owner, which is a different kind of truth.

## How to Use This Library

**If you are reading the physical volumes:** Start with Volume 0 (*Read This First*). It covers water, fire, shelter, food, healing, tools, numbers, community, machines, and how to copy these books by hand. Then follow the Origins Arc — rays 1 through 12 — or pick the section you need.

**If you are reading the digital version:** The MkDocs site is searchable and cross-linked. The Concept Index maps ideas across sections. The Reading Maps suggest thirteen curated paths through the library. The Prerequisites graph shows which volumes assume knowledge from others.

**If you are rebuilding:** Chapter 10 of Volume 0 explains how to make paper, ink, and pens from raw materials, and how to copy pages by hand, by woodblock, by gelatin duplicator, and by photography — all without modern industry. Copy the overview pages first. They are the table of contents for all human knowledge.

## How This Was Made

This library was written in February 2026 with AI assistance. The author directed the structure, the style, the audience, and the scope. The AI did the heavy lifting of composition across 217 directories and 2,178 files. Every guide was then reviewed, tagged for issues, and revised until clean. The result is neither purely human-written nor purely machine-generated — it is a collaboration, and it is better than either party could have produced alone.

The physical volumes are printed on acid-free paper rated to last five hundred years. The binding is Smyth-sewn — thread holds the signatures together even if the adhesive fails. Three archival sets exist in separate locations. The digital source is public and freely licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0, so that anyone can copy, translate, and redistribute the content without permission or payment.

The most durable backup is not the paper or the binding. It is the number of copies in the number of hands in the number of places. Make more.
