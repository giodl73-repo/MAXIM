# The Periodic Table — Mendeleev, Meyer, Moseley, Seaborg

## The Problem: Organizing 60+ Elements

By 1860 (after Cannizzaro's Karlsruhe lecture settled atomic weights),
chemists had a list of ~60 elements with reasonably accurate atomic masses
and known chemical behaviors. The question: is there a pattern?

```
THE PERIODICITY PROBLEM (1860s)
==================================

KNOWN: ~60 elements with atomic masses and properties.

OBSERVATIONS anyone could make:
  - Lithium (Li), sodium (Na), potassium (K) all behave similarly
    (soft alkali metals, react violently with water)
  - Beryllium (Be), magnesium (Mg), calcium (Ca) all behave similarly
    (alkaline earths, +2 charge)
  - Fluorine (F), chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), iodine (I) all similar
    (halogens, form salts, -1 charge)
  - Noble gases not yet known

DÉJÀ VU: These families had been noticed (Döbereiner's triads, 1829;
  Newlands's octaves, 1864). But nobody found the unifying principle.

THE KEY INSIGHT (Mendeleev/Meyer, independently, 1869–1870):
  Arrange by atomic mass → properties repeat PERIODICALLY.
  The periodic law: the properties of elements are a periodic function
  of their atomic weights.

WHY THIS WORKS (not understood until quantum mechanics, 1920s):
  Atomic mass ≈ proton number (approximately — hence Moseley's correction).
  Proton number = electron configuration = chemical behavior.
  The periodicity reflects filling of electron shells.
```

## Dmitri Mendeleev (1834–1907)

### Who He Was

Russian chemist, St. Petersburg. Legend has it he saw the complete periodic
table in a dream (probably apocryphal — he had been working on it for weeks
for his chemistry textbook). One of the most productive predictors in the
history of science: he left gaps in his table for undiscovered elements
and predicted their properties with startling accuracy.

He was passed over for the Nobel Prize (awarded 1906 to Moissan for fluorine;
Mendeleev died in 1907 before the 1908 prize was awarded).

### The Contribution: The Periodic Law and Its Predictions

**The 1869 Table**

```
MENDELEEV'S PERIODIC TABLE (1869)
===================================

Method:
  1. List all known elements by atomic mass.
  2. Arrange so that chemically similar elements fall in the same column.
  3. When the pattern breaks, leave a GAP rather than force an element
     into the wrong position.

Critical decision: LEAVE GAPS.
  This is what separated Mendeleev from Newlands (who forced elements
  into octaves without gaps, making his system less predictive).

Mendeleev's gaps (1871):
  He predicted three missing elements and their properties:
    "eka-boron" (below boron in the table)
    "eka-aluminum" (below aluminum)
    "eka-silicon" (below silicon)

  From the surrounding elements, he calculated:
    eka-aluminum: mass ~68, density ~6.0, valence 3, low melting point,
      would form M₂O₃ oxide, MCl₃ chloride, M(OH)₃ hydroxide.

CONFIRMATION:
  Gallium (Ga) discovered by Lecoq de Boisbaudran, 1875:
    mass = 69.7, density = 5.91, valence = 3, low mp (29.8°C)
    Ga₂O₃ oxide, GaCl₃ chloride — all as predicted.

  Scandium (Sc) discovered 1879 — eka-boron.
  Germanium (Ge) discovered 1886 — eka-silicon.
    Meyer himself said Mendeleev's eka-silicon prediction for Ge
    was "the most striking confirmation of the periodic law."
```

**Mendeleev's Corrections to Accepted Atomic Weights**

```
AUDACITY OF THE TABLE
=======================

Some elements didn't fit if placed by accepted atomic masses.
Mendeleev reversed the order of pairs where necessary:
  Cobalt (Co) and Nickel (Ni): Mendeleev placed Co before Ni
    even though Co's accepted atomic mass was slightly higher.
  Tellurium (Te) and Iodine (I): Te has higher mass but
    clearly belongs with oxygen/sulfur/selenium family;
    I clearly belongs with halogens.

He concluded: the accepted atomic masses must be wrong for these,
OR the periodic law applies to something deeper than mass.

MODERN UNDERSTANDING: He was right to trust the chemistry.
  The correct ordering variable is ATOMIC NUMBER (proton count),
  not atomic mass. For most elements these agree; for a few pairs
  (Co/Ni, Te/I, Ar/K) atomic mass order is inverted relative to
  atomic number order because of isotope distributions.
  Moseley resolved this completely (below).
```

**What Mendeleev Got Wrong**

```
MENDELEEV'S ERRORS
===================

1. Placed ether and "coronium" as elements in early versions.
   (Neither exists.)

2. Did not predict noble gases — the whole column was missing.
   When Argon was discovered (Rayleigh + Ramsay, 1894), it didn't fit
   his table. He initially resisted; eventually accepted a new column
   for inert gases.

3. The lanthanides caused trouble — 14 elements in a row with nearly
   identical chemistry. The current "f-block" accommodation was
   worked out gradually.

4. Thought the periodic law was empirical and fundamental.
   It IS fundamental — but its explanation (quantum mechanics) was
   30 years away.
```

## Lothar Meyer (1830–1895)

### Who He Was

German chemist, Tübingen. Independently developed a periodic arrangement
of the elements at nearly the same time as Mendeleev. Published his atomic
volume curve in 1870, after Mendeleev's 1869 table.

### The Contribution: Independent Periodic Arrangement and Atomic Volume Curve

```
MEYER'S APPROACH vs. MENDELEEV'S
==================================

SIMILARITIES:
  Both arranged elements by atomic mass.
  Both noticed periodicity of properties.
  Both published independently around 1869–1870.

DIFFERENCES:
  Mendeleev (1869):
    - Published a complete table with predictions
    - Made specific, testable predictions about missing elements
    - Willing to reverse accepted atomic masses when chemistry demanded
    - BOLDER: left gaps and made falsifiable predictions

  Meyer (1870):
    - Atomic volume curve: plotted atomic volume (mass/density)
    vs. atomic mass → dramatic periodic peaks
    - More cautious: did not make element predictions
    - Beautiful graphical demonstration of periodicity

MEYER'S ATOMIC VOLUME CURVE:
  x-axis: atomic mass
  y-axis: atomic volume (mass / density, in cm³/mol)

  The curve shows sharp peaks at alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs)
  and the same elements falling at the same positions on each cycle.

  This was a COMPELLING VISUAL argument for periodicity.
  The curve is still shown in chemistry textbooks.

CREDIT DISPUTE:
  Both share credit for discovering the periodic law.
  Mendeleev gets more credit because of the predictions.
  Meyer himself acknowledged Mendeleev's priority on predictions.
  Both received the Davy Medal (1882) jointly.
```

## Henry Moseley (1887–1915)

### Who He Was

English physicist. Worked in Rutherford's Manchester laboratory.
Determined the atomic number (nuclear charge) of elements by measuring
X-ray spectra. Killed in action at Gallipoli in 1915, at age 27 —
widely regarded as one of the greatest losses to science in history.
His death directly prompted the British government to stop sending
scientists to combat.

### The Contribution: Atomic Number — The True Ordering Variable

**X-Ray Spectroscopy and the Nuclear Charge (1913–1914)**

```
MOSELEY'S LAW
==============

CONTEXT: By 1913, Bohr's model had quantized atomic energy levels.
  Rutherford had established the nucleus (1911).
  But the nuclear charge was unmeasured for most elements.

MOSELEY'S EXPERIMENT:
  Bombard different elements with electrons → each element emits
  characteristic X-rays at specific frequencies.

  The frequencies of characteristic X-ray lines depend on Z
  (the nuclear charge / atomic number):

  √f = a(Z - b)

  where f is the X-ray frequency, Z is the atomic number,
  and a, b are constants (different for different X-ray series).

  This is Moseley's Law.

WHAT IT REVEALED:
  For each element, Z is an INTEGER: 1, 2, 3, 4, ...
  The elements can be ordered uniquely by this integer.
  The periodic table's ORDER should be Z, not atomic mass.

  Mendeleev's Co/Ni and Te/I inversions:
    Co (Z=27) before Ni (Z=28) — correct.
    Te (Z=52) before I (Z=53) — correct.
  The inversions were real and the atomic mass order was misleading.
```

**Gaps Are Real: Missing Elements Identified**

```
MOSELEY'S MISSING ELEMENTS
============================

By assigning Z to all known elements, Moseley identified
WHICH Z values were missing:

  Z=43: missing → eventually found as technetium (Tc), 1937
        (first synthetic element; no stable isotopes)
  Z=61: missing → promethium (Pm), 1945 (only from reactors/fission)
  Z=72: missing → hafnium (Hf), found 1923 by Coster + von Hevesy
        using Moseley-style X-ray methods
  Z=75: missing → rhenium (Re), found 1925

Before Moseley, chemists argued about whether elements were missing
or whether the table was complete.
After Moseley: a definite count — there are exactly Z=1 to 92
  naturally occurring elements (with gaps at 43, 61, 85, 87,
  which have no stable isotopes).

MOSELEY'S ACTUAL CONTRIBUTION:
  The periodic law is a law of ATOMIC NUMBER, not atomic mass.
  This placed the periodic table on a firm physical foundation.
  Quantum mechanics (1925–1930) then explained why Z determines
  electron configuration and hence chemical behavior.
```

## Glenn Seaborg (1912–1999)

### Who He Was

American nuclear chemist, Berkeley. Worked on the Manhattan Project
(one of the key designers of the plutonium bomb). Discovered or co-discovered
10 transuranic elements (elements beyond uranium, Z>92).
Element 106, seaborgium (Sg), was named for him while he was alive —
the only person to have an element named for them while living.
Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 1951 (shared with McMillan).

### The Contribution: Transuranics and the Actinide Concept

**The Actinide Series (1944)**

```
THE ACTINIDE CONCEPT
=====================

CONTEXT (1944): Several transuranic elements had been made artificially:
  Z=93 neptunium (Np), Z=94 plutonium (Pu), Z=95 americium (Am), etc.
  Where do they go in the periodic table?

  Standard assumption (wrong): they belonged in a 6th row of the d-block,
    analogous to hafnium, tantalum, tungsten...
  This predicted wrong chemical properties.

SEABORG'S INSIGHT (1944):
  The transuranics are filling the 5f electron subshell.
  They form an "actinide" series analogous to the lanthanides (4f subshell).
  The actinides should be a separate block at the bottom of the table —
    the f-block.

  This required COURAGE: he proposed a major reorganization of the
  periodic table, which many chemists initially resisted.

CONSEQUENCE:
  The modern periodic table with the f-block "island" at the bottom
  is Seaborg's. Without his actinide concept, the table's structure
  was wrong for all elements beyond barium.
```


**Plutonium and the Manhattan Project**

```
PLUTONIUM CHEMISTRY
====================

Pu-239 (fissile isotope):
  Created from U-238 + neutron → U-239 → Np-239 → Pu-239
  (via two beta decays, total ~2.4 days)

  Pu-239 undergoes fission with thermal and fast neutrons.
  Critical mass: ~10 kg (sphere the size of a grapefruit).

SEABORG'S CHALLENGE (1942):
  The first microgram of Pu-239 had been created.
  He had to determine the chemistry of plutonium with amounts
  too small to see — using radioactive counting rather than
  conventional analytical chemistry.

  He established: Pu is most stable as Pu⁴⁺ in nitric acid.
  The PUREX process for separating Pu from spent nuclear fuel
  (TBP extraction) descends directly from this work.

  The Fat Man bomb used a plutonium implosion design.
  The separation/purification chemistry was Seaborg's.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Mendeleev invented the periodic table"** — He invented the most useful early
version and made it predictive. Meyer independently developed a very similar
arrangement simultaneously. John Newlands (1864) and Alexandre-Émile Béguyer
de Chancourtois (1862) had earlier arrangements, but without the predictive
power of Mendeleev's gaps.

**"The periodic table is ordered by atomic mass"** — It is ordered by atomic
number. For almost all elements, these are in the same order (heavier atoms
have more protons). For a few pairs — Te/I, Co/Ni, Ar/K — they diverge, and
atomic number (not mass) gives the correct chemical ordering. Moseley
established this in 1913–1914. Mendeleev's table accidentally got these
right by trusting chemistry over the measured masses.

**"Noble gases ruined Mendeleev's table"** — He had to add a column,
which he initially resisted. But the discovery of argon (1894), helium
(1895), neon, krypton, xenon (all 1898) actually strengthened the
periodic table by revealing a whole new family with zero valence.
The inert gases are the clearest possible demonstration of chemical periodicity.

**"Moseley just confirmed Mendeleev"** — He solved a problem Mendeleev
couldn't solve: why periodicity exists and what the correct ordering variable
is. He also identified specific gaps. And he did it in about two years of
experimental work, from 1913 to 1914, before dying at 27. His death at
Gallipoli is cited as a concrete example of what the First World War cost science.

**"Seaborg's work is obscure nuclear chemistry"** — The f-block actinides
include thorium, uranium, and the entire family of nuclear reactor fuels
and weapons materials. The PUREX process for separating plutonium from
spent nuclear fuel is a direct descendant of Seaborg's wartime chemistry.
Every nuclear power plant's fuel cycle depends on actinide chemistry.
