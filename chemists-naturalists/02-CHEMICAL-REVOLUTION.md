# The Chemical Revolution — Lavoisier, Priestley, Scheele, Cavendish

## The Revolution: Overthrowing Phlogiston

Chemistry before 1770 was organized around **phlogiston** — a hypothetical substance
released during combustion. When something burned, it "lost" phlogiston. This explained
why metals form calxes (oxides) when heated and why charcoal burns away completely.

The theory worked well enough to be believed for a century.

```
THE PHLOGISTON THEORY (1667–1780)
===================================

Stahl's phlogiston (1703):
  Matter contains phlogiston, released during combustion/oxidation.
  Metal → calx (oxide) + phlogiston  (when heated in air)
  Calx + charcoal → metal  (charcoal is rich in phlogiston, restores it)
  Combustion = phlogiston release

  Why it "worked": explained qualitative observations.
  Problem: phlogiston has no positive mass; metals get HEAVIER when calcified.
    (Phlogiston defenders said phlogiston has NEGATIVE mass — bizarre.)

THE DESTROYERS:
  Priestley (1774): Isolates "dephlogisticated air" (oxygen)
  Scheele (~1772): Independently isolates oxygen ("fire air")
  Lavoisier (1774–1783): Identifies oxygen as the REAL explanation.
    Combustion = COMBINATION WITH OXYGEN, not release of phlogiston.

THE REVOLUTION:
  Lavoisier didn't just change the explanation of combustion.
  He changed CHEMISTRY ITSELF:
    New nomenclature (oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen — not "dephlogisticated air")
    Law of conservation of mass
    List of elements
    Quantitative methods
```

## Joseph Priestley (1733–1804)

### Who He Was

English minister, philosopher, and chemist. Strong religious nonconformist
and political radical — his house was burned by a mob in 1791 for supporting
the French Revolution. Emigrated to America in 1794 (Thomas Jefferson and John
Adams had invited him). Friend of Benjamin Franklin.

Discovered many gases — oxygen most importantly, but also ammonia, sulfur dioxide,
carbon monoxide, and what he called "fixed air" (CO₂), "dephlogisticated nitrous
air" (nitrous oxide, N₂O), and "inflammable air" (hydrogen, independently).

### The Contribution: Discovering Oxygen

**Isolating Oxygen (1774)**

```
PRIESTLEY'S OXYGEN EXPERIMENT (August 1, 1774)
================================================

Method: Heat mercuric oxide (HgO) with focused sunlight using a lens.
  The solid breaks down, releasing a gas.

  2HgO → 2Hg + O₂

What he observed:
  - A candle burned MORE BRIGHTLY in this gas
  - A mouse in this gas lived LONGER than in ordinary air
  - He breathed it himself: "I fancied that my breast felt peculiarly light
    and easy for some time afterwards"

What he called it: "Dephlogisticated air"
  (in phlogiston terms: air from which all phlogiston had been removed,
  so it could absorb more phlogiston from burning substances)

Lavoisier met Priestley in October 1774. Priestley described the experiment.
Lavoisier immediately repeated it and reinterpreted the results.
```

**Priestley's Resistance to the New Theory**

Priestley never accepted Lavoisier's oxygen theory. He died in 1804 still
defending phlogiston. This is a famous case of a discoverer who found the
right material but kept the wrong framework.

Priestley's comment: "Hitherto only two mice and myself have had the privilege
of breathing it." (On pure oxygen.)

## Carl Wilhelm Scheele (1742–1786)

### Who He Was

Swedish-German pharmaceutical chemist. Worked in a pharmacy in Köping, Sweden.
Extraordinary experimental chemist — isolated more new substances than any
chemist of his era. Died young (probably from his habit of tasting all compounds
he made, including mercury and arsenic compounds).

### The Contribution: Oxygen and a Dozen Other Discoveries

**Scheele's Priority Claim**

Scheele isolated oxygen around 1772 — before Priestley (1774) — but did not
publish until 1777. In publishing terms: Priestley first (1774), Scheele
second (1777), though priority of discovery may be Scheele's.

He called it "fire air" (eldluft) and correctly described:
- Its role in combustion
- Its role in respiration
- That air = fire air (oxygen) + "foul air" (nitrogen)
- Mercury calx (HgO) decomposition to release it

**Other Discoveries**

```
SCHEELE'S DISCOVERIES (partial list)
======================================

Gases and acids:
  Oxygen ("fire air") ~1772
  Chlorine ("dephlogisticated marine acid") 1774
  Nitrogen (independently) 1773
  Hydrogen fluoride 1771
  Hydrogen sulfide 1771
  Tartaric acid 1769
  Oxalic acid 1776
  Uric acid 1776
  Citric acid 1784
  Lactic acid 1780
  Malic acid 1785
  Glycerol 1779
  Prussian blue (from analysis) 1770
  Casein (from milk) 1780

He also discovered barium oxide (heavy earth), manganese dioxide,
and was the first to observe that silver halides darken in light
(photosensitivity — precursor to photography).

The element tantalum was named after Tantalus, but its discovery
was facilitated by Scheele's work on tungsten and molybdenum.

Scheele's "bad luck": Oxygen, chlorine, barium, manganese, molybdenum,
  tungsten — all partially or fully attributed to others because he
  published slowly or posthumously.
```

## Henry Cavendish (1731–1810)

### Who He Was

British scientist, heir to a large fortune. The most antisocial major scientist
in history — he barely spoke to other scientists, communicated mainly in writing,
and women were forbidden from his presence (he installed a back staircase to avoid
meeting female servants). Yet he was extraordinarily accurate — he measured the
density of the Earth to within 1% of the modern value.

### The Contribution: Hydrogen and Measuring the Earth

**Hydrogen (1766)**

```
CAVENDISH'S HYDROGEN DISCOVERY
================================

Cavendish studied "inflammable air" (hydrogen) released when metals
(iron, zinc, tin) react with acids.

  Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑

He measured: density, volume ratios, combustion products.

Key observation: When "inflammable air" (hydrogen) burns in air,
  it produces WATER.
  2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

He interpreted this as: "dephlogisticated air" (oxygen) + "inflammable air"
  (phlogiston-rich) → water (absorbs phlogiston).

Still using phlogiston framework. Lavoisier reinterpreted: hydrogen + oxygen → water.
Cavendish named it: "hydrogen" came from Lavoisier (Greek: "water-producer").

He also showed the atmosphere is 4/5 nitrogen and 1/5 oxygen with
extraordinary precision. And noticed a residual 1/120 of air that
wouldn't combine with oxygen — later found by Rayleigh and Ramsay to be argon.
(150 years before they discovered it.)
```

**Cavendish's Torsion Balance — Weighing the Earth (1798)**

```
CAVENDISH EXPERIMENT (1798)
============================

Setup: Two lead balls on a beam, suspended by a torsion fiber.
  Two large lead spheres brought close — gravitational attraction twists the fiber.
  Measure the twist → gravitational force between known masses → G.

  From G and Earth's gravitational acceleration g:
    g = GM_Earth/R_Earth²
    M_Earth = gR_Earth²/G

Cavendish measured G = 6.74 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²  (modern: 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹)
Earth's density: 5.45 g/cm³ (modern: 5.51 g/cm³) — within 1%.

He called it "weighing the Earth" — not determining G.
(Thinking of G as a separately measurable constant is later.)

The Cavendish experiment is still the standard laboratory method
for measuring G — the least precisely known fundamental constant
(known to only 4–5 significant figures, vs. 10–12 for others).
```

## Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier (1743–1794)

### Who He Was

French chemist and tax collector. Guillotined during the Terror in 1794,
at age 50. (A judge reportedly said: "The Republic has no need for savants.")
One year later he was posthumously exonerated. His wife Marie-Anne Paulze
translated English chemistry papers for him, assisted his experiments,
illustrated his works, and edited his memoirs.

### The Contribution: Founding Modern Chemistry

**The Oxygen Theory of Combustion (1774–1783)**

```
LAVOISIER'S PROGRAM
====================

Step 1 (1774): Meets Priestley, repeats his experiments.
  Mercury calx + heat → mercury + gas.
  The gas supports combustion better than ordinary air.

Step 2 (1776): Quantitative experiments with balances.
  Metal + air → calx.
  Weigh everything before and after.
  Total mass CONSERVED.
  The calx weighs MORE than the metal.
  → The metal has COMBINED WITH SOMETHING FROM THE AIR.
  → Not released phlogiston — absorbed something.

Step 3 (1778): Names the active component "oxygen" (Greek: "acid producer" — wrong,
  but the name stuck).

Step 4 (1783): Shows water = hydrogen + oxygen.
  Synthesizes water from the two gases.
  Decomposes water back to hydrogen + oxygen.

Step 5: Overthrows phlogiston entirely.
  New explanation: Combustion = oxidation = combining with oxygen.
  Calx = metal oxide. Reduction = removing oxygen.
```

**Conservation of Mass**

```
LAW OF CONSERVATION OF MASS
=============================

Lavoisier's famous statement (1789):
  "Nothing is created; nothing is destroyed. Before and after all operations,
  the quantity of matter in nature remains the same."

  Mass_reactants = Mass_products

Experimental basis:
  Combustion in a sealed vessel: weigh before and after.
  Total mass unchanged. The burned matter's mass equals the
  mass of the starting material plus the mass of oxygen consumed.

Lavoisier used quantitative balance measurements as the standard of chemistry.
This seems obvious now; before Lavoisier, chemists were qualitative.

(Mass-energy equivalence, E = mc²: technically mass is not conserved in
nuclear reactions. But the mass defect is ~10⁻¹⁰ of reactant mass for
chemical reactions — unmeasurable with any balance. So Lavoisier was
right for chemistry; Einstein showed the deeper law for nuclear processes.)
```

**The Chemical Nomenclature (1787)**

Lavoisier, with Berthollet, Fourcroy, and de Morveau, published a new chemical
nomenclature (*Méthode de Nomenclature Chimique*, 1787):

```
LAVOISIER'S NEW NAMING SYSTEM
================================

Old names: vitriol of Venus (copper sulfate), oil of vitriol (sulfuric acid),
  butter of antimony (antimony trichloride), phlogisticated air (nitrogen)

New systematic names:
  Name reflects COMPOSITION, not superficial properties.
  "Oxides" = combined with oxygen
  "Sulfate" = contains sulfur + oxygen
  "Hydrochloride" = hydrogen + chlorine

  Sulfuric acid: H₂SO₄ (two hydrogen, one sulfur, four oxygen)
  Copper sulfate: CuSO₄
  Carbon dioxide: CO₂ (not "fixed air" — name tells you what's in it)

This naming system, with modifications, is what we use today (IUPAC system).
Lavoisier's names are why we say "oxygen" not "dephlogisticated air"
and "nitrogen" not "phlogisticated air."
```

**List of 33 Elements (1789)**

In *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* (1789), Lavoisier listed 33 substances as
"elements" — things that could not be further decomposed. He defined an element
empirically (operationally): a substance that resists decomposition.

Some were wrong (light and caloric = heat were listed as elements).
But the list included: oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorus, carbon,
and most known metals. The operational definition of element as "irreducible
by chemistry" remained the standard until Moseley (atomic number, 1913).

## Common Confusion Points

**"Priestley discovered oxygen"** — He isolated it (1774) and made key observations.
Scheele possibly isolated it first (~1772). But Lavoisier understood what oxygen WAS —
the active principle in combustion and respiration. Priestley found the substance
without understanding it. Discovery without theoretical comprehension is partial
discovery; Lavoisier completed it.

**"Lavoisier was just a re-interpreter of others' work"** — His own experiments were
foundational: he quantified combustion with precise balances, proved conservation of mass,
showed the composition of water and air by synthesis and analysis, and developed the
nomenclature. He didn't just reinterpret Priestley — he ran the decisive experiments.

**"Cavendish was unusual only personally"** — His anomalous social behavior masked
extraordinary scientific insight. His residual argon observation (1/120 of the atmosphere
not combining) was 150 years ahead of its time. His electrical experiments anticipated
Coulomb's law and the concept of electrical potential. He simply didn't publish.
Maxwell edited and published Cavendish's electrical papers posthumously (1879), revealing
results decades ahead of their published "discoveries."

**"The phlogiston theory was completely wrong"** — It was structurally wrong (combustion
is oxidation, not phlogiston release), but the observations phlogistonists made were
real. The problem was that "phlogiston" was an unobservable entity defined to explain
combustion — unfalsifiable in principle until Lavoisier showed a positive, quantifiable
alternative. The history of phlogiston is a case study in how theories die: not from
one decisive experiment, but from the weight of accumulated anomalies + a better theory.
