# Exploration Naturalists — Humboldt, Banks, Bates, Cook (Scientific), Wallace

## The Program: Science by Going There

Natural history was transformed in the 18th and 19th centuries by
systematic collection voyages. The combination of global travel,
precise measurement, and massive specimen collection changed biology,
geography, and ecology from descriptive local disciplines into global
comparative sciences.

```
THE EXPLORATION TRADITION
===========================

PROBLEM (1750):
  Natural history was local. European naturalists knew European species.
  Travelers had described exotic organisms but without systematic precision.
  No quantitative biogeography. No ecological comparison across continents.
  No understanding of why different regions have different biotas.

THE PROGRAM:
  Collect: bring back specimens (dead and living), seeds, fossils.
  Measure: record temperatures, pressures, magnetic declinations, elevations.
  Map: record geographic coordinates precisely.
  Compare: relate what you find to what's known from other regions.

KEY VOYAGES:
  Cook's Endeavour voyage (1768–1771): Banks onboard — Pacific/Australia
  Humboldt in South America (1799–1804): 6,000 new species + quantitative ecology
  Darwin on the Beagle (1831–1836): Galápagos and evolution
  Bates in the Amazon (1848–1859): 14,000 species, 8,000 new; mimicry
  Wallace in the Malay Archipelago (1854–1862): Wallace Line; 125,000 specimens

THE OUTPUT:
  Biogeography as a quantitative science
  Understanding of tropical diversity (why tropics have more species)
  Mimicry and the first explanation of warning coloration
  The foundations of ecology (Humboldt's isotherms, vegetation zones)
```

## Alexander von Humboldt (1769–1859)

### Who He Was

German naturalist and geographer. Independently wealthy (funded his own
expeditions). The most famous scientist in the world for much of his lifetime
— arguably more famous than Napoleon in 1800. Influenced Darwin directly
(Darwin read Humboldt's *Personal Narrative* obsessively before the Beagle voyage).

His 1799–1804 expedition to Spanish America (Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador,
Peru, Cuba, Mexico) with Aimé Bonpland covered 9,650 km on foot and by canoe.
He sent back 60,000 plant specimens.

### The Contribution: Quantitative Ecology and the Geography of Plants

**Isotherms and Vegetation Zones (1817–1845)**

```
HUMBOLDT'S MAJOR INNOVATIONS
===============================

1. ISOTHERMS (1817):
   Lines connecting points of equal average temperature on a map.
   Humboldt invented this as a tool for comparing climate across continents.
   Before isotherms: temperature was described verbally for specific cities.
   After isotherms: global temperature patterns became visible on a map.

   The isotherm concept immediately revealed that:
   - Temperature zones are NOT simply lines of latitude
   - Ocean currents strongly modify continental climates
   - The same latitude in Asia vs. North America has different climates

2. VEGETATION ZONES ON CHIMBORAZO (1802):
   Humboldt climbed Chimborazo (Ecuador, 6,263m, then thought the world's
   highest peak) and mapped vegetation:

       SUMMIT: snow/ice, no life
       5000m: lichens only
       4000m: alpine meadows (páramo)
       3000m: shrubs, polylepis trees
       2000m: cloud forest
       1000m: tropical forest
       Coastal: mangroves/tropical vegetation

   AND: each vegetation zone corresponds to a climate zone that occurs
   at higher latitudes:
     Tropical forest = European temperate climate
     Alpine meadow = European subalpine
     Summit zone = Arctic tundra

   This is the discovery that ALTITUDE and LATITUDE produce equivalent
   climate zones — and thus equivalent vegetation zones.

   The diagram (Tableau Physique des Andes, 1805) is the first visual
   statement of ecological zonation. It is still reproduced in textbooks.

3. COMPARISON ACROSS CONTINENTS:
   Humboldt compared Africa, Europe, and South America systematically:
   similar climates produce similar vegetation forms even if different
   species are present (convergent evolution of form to climate).
   He called this "plant geography" — the precursor to ecology.
```

**Humboldt's Influence on Darwin and Later Scientists**

```
HUMBOLDT'S INTELLECTUAL NETWORK
==================================

Darwin:
  Darwin's copy of Humboldt's Personal Narrative was heavily annotated.
  Darwin's style of describing landscapes (Voyage of the Beagle) directly
  imitates Humboldt's combination of aesthetics and measurement.
  Darwin credited Humboldt with inspiring his scientific career.

George Perkins Marsh:
  Humboldt's observation that deforestation changed local climates
  influenced Marsh's Man and Nature (1864) — the founding text of
  American environmentalism.

Modern ecology:
  The concept of vegetation zones (biomes) is Humboldtian.
  The Amazon biome, temperate forest biome, tundra biome, etc.
  descend directly from Humboldt's plant geography.

Humboldt's Cosmos (1845–1862):
  A 5-volume attempt to describe the entire physical world as an
  interconnected system. Proposed the unity of nature.
  The word "cosmos" for the ordered whole universe/world is Humboldt's.
```

## Joseph Banks (1743–1820)

### Who He Was

English naturalist and botanist. Independently wealthy. Sailed on Cook's
first voyage (Endeavour, 1768–1771) as the civilian head of science,
at his own expense. Returned with the largest botanical collection from
the Pacific then assembled. President of the Royal Society for 42 years
(1778–1820) — an unprecedented tenure that made him the central node of
British science for decades.

### The Contribution: The Endeavour Voyage and Institution-Building

**The Endeavour Voyage (1768–1771)**

```
COOK'S ENDEAVOUR VOYAGE (1768–1771)
======================================

PRIMARY MISSION: Observe the Transit of Venus from Tahiti
  (June 3, 1769 — used to measure the Earth-Sun distance)

SECONDARY MISSION: Investigate the "unknown southern continent"
  (Terra Australis Incognita — hypothesized since antiquity)

BANKS'S CONTRIBUTION (not Cook's):
  Banks brought: himself, 2 naturalists, 2 artists, 3 servants, a greyhound.
  Budget: £10,000 of his own money (enormous sum).

SCIENTIFIC RESULTS:
  New Zealand: first European circumnavigation; first systematic natural
    history. Banks collected ~400 plant species previously unknown to science.
  Australia (Botany Bay, April 1770):
    Named for the extraordinary plant diversity Banks found there.
    Banks collected ~1,400 plant specimens from Australia.
    Returned with ~30,000 pressed plant specimens from the entire voyage.

TAXONOMIC OUTPUT:
  The voyage produced material for decades of taxonomic work.
  Solander (Banks's Swedish naturalist, student of Linnaeus) described
  many new genera and species.
  The genus Banksia (Australian proteaceous plants) is named for Banks.
```

**Institution Building: Kew Gardens and the Royal Society**

```
BANKS AS SCIENCE ADMINISTRATOR
================================

KEW GARDENS:
  Banks became unofficial director of the Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew
  (from 1773 until his death, 1820).
  He transformed Kew from a royal pleasure garden into a global botanical
  research and plant-exchange institution.

  Banks organized sending plant collectors to all continents.
  He deliberately moved economically useful plants between colonies:
  - Breadfruit from Tahiti to Caribbean (HMS Bounty mission — the one
    with the mutiny; second mission succeeded)
  - Tea from China to India (via Kew)
  - Rubber from Brazil to Southeast Asia (later, building on Banks's network)

  These "botanical imperialism" transfers had enormous economic consequences.

ROYAL SOCIETY (President, 1778–1820):
  Banks controlled access to the British scientific establishment.
  He recommended positions, distributed patronage, approved expeditions.
  His network extended to Cook (geography), Cavendish (physics),
  Herschel (astronomy), and the botanists he deployed worldwide.

THE NETWORK:
  Banks is less a discoverer than an organizer:
  he turned British natural history from a hobby of gentlemen into
  an institutionalized, globally connected enterprise.
```

## Henry Walter Bates (1825–1892)

### Who He Was

English naturalist. Self-educated, worked in a hosiery factory before
dedicating himself to natural history. Spent 11 years in the Amazon
(1848–1859) — the longest individual field expedition of the 19th century.
Collected approximately 14,700 species, of which ~8,000 were new to science.
His analysis of this material produced one of the most important ideas in
evolutionary biology: Batesian mimicry.

### The Contribution: Batesian Mimicry

**The Amazon Collection (1848–1859)**

```
BATES IN THE AMAZON
====================

LOGISTICS:
  Wallace accompanied him for the first 4 years (1848–1852).
  After Wallace left, Bates continued alone for 7 more years.
  He collected ~14,700 species total:
    Insects: ~7,500 butterfly/moth species; several thousand beetles
    ~8,000 were new to science

  He personally knew by sight thousands of species of Amazonian butterflies.

THE OBSERVATION:
  In the Amazon, some butterflies that are palatable (edible to birds)
  closely resemble unpalatable species (which birds avoid).

  Specifically: certain edible Pieridae butterflies look strikingly similar
  to unpalatable Heliconius butterflies.
    - Same wing color patterns
    - Same flight behavior
    - Different species, different families

  Why would natural selection produce a palatable butterfly that resembles
  a poisonous one?
```

**Batesian Mimicry (1862)**

```
BATESIAN MIMICRY
=================

BATES'S EXPLANATION (1862):
  If a predator (bird) learns to avoid the unpalatable MODEL species
  (say, Heliconius), then a palatable MIMIC that resembles the model
  will also be avoided — gaining protection by deception.

  Natural selection would favor increasingly accurate mimics:
    Slightly better mimics survive more → leave more offspring →
    mimicry improves over generations.

This is one of the clearest demonstrations of natural selection in action:
  - The selective pressure (predation) is identifiable
  - The adaptation (resemblance) is measurable
  - The mechanism (predator avoidance learning) is testable
  - The quantitative prediction (mimic should be rarer than model,
    because if mimics outnumber models, birds don't learn to avoid) is correct

MATHEMATICAL PREDICTION:
  For mimicry to work: model must be more abundant than mimic.
  If mimics are too common, birds encounter non-poisonous mimics often,
  don't learn the avoidance → mimicry breaks down.

  Confirmed in field and experimental studies.

DISTINCTION FROM MÜLLERIAN MIMICRY:
  Müllerian mimicry (Fritz Müller, 1878): TWO UNPALATABLE species
  resemble each other. Both gain because predators learn avoidance
  faster if both look the same.
  Müllerian: mutually beneficial (both unpalatable)
  Batesian: parasitic (mimic exploits model's reputation)
```

## James Cook (1728–1779) — The Scientific Voyages

### Who He Was

English navigator and explorer. Three Pacific voyages (1768–1771,
1772–1775, 1776–1779). Killed in Hawaii, 1779. The scientific content
of his voyages was contributed largely by the naturalists he carried;
Cook's own contribution was navigation and logistics.

### The Scientific Legacy of Cook's Voyages

**Three Voyages**

```
COOK'S THREE VOYAGES
=====================

FIRST VOYAGE (1768–1771, HMS Endeavour):
  Naturalists: Banks + Solander
  Achievements:
    - First transit of Venus observation from Tahiti
    - First European circumnavigation of New Zealand
    - First European landfall on eastern Australia (Botany Bay)
    - Disproval of Terra Australis as habitable continent
      in the southern temperate zone (it existed, but was ice)
  Collections: ~30,000 plant specimens; hundreds of new species

SECOND VOYAGE (1772–1775, HMS Resolution):
  Naturalists: Forsters (Johann Reinhold + Georg)
  Achievements:
    - First crossing of the Antarctic Circle
    - Established that no large habitable southern continent exists
      (Antarctica is inhospitable ice; not the fertile land imagined)
    - Longitude: tested Harrison's H4 chronometer — worked to 8 miles
      after a 100,000-mile voyage. Navigation revolution.
  The younger Forster (Georg) later influenced Humboldt directly.

THIRD VOYAGE (1776–1779):
  Mission: find the Northwest Passage from the Pacific side
  Charted much of the North Pacific and Arctic coasts
  Cook killed in Kealakekua Bay, Hawaii, February 14, 1779

NAVIGATION CONTRIBUTION:
  Harrison's chronometer (tested on Cook's 2nd voyage) solved longitude.
  Combined with astronomical methods → precise global charts.
  This is a technology story as much as a science story:
    Before chronometer: longitude error of 300+ miles common
    After: errors of a few miles — ocean navigation transformed
```

## Alfred Russel Wallace (Amazon and Malay Archipelago)

*Note: Wallace appears in File 07 for natural selection; here the focus
is on his fieldwork.*

```
WALLACE'S FIELD EXPEDITIONS
============================

AMAZON (1848–1852, with Bates):
  Collected widely in the Amazon basin.
  On return: his ship caught fire and sank.
  He lost almost all his specimens after 4 years of collection.
  His notes and some sketches survived.
  Published Palm Trees of the Amazon (1853) and
  A Narrative of Travels on the Amazon and Rio Negro (1853)
  from memory and surviving notes.

MALAY ARCHIPELAGO (1854–1862):
  8 years, 22,000 miles within the archipelago.
  ~125,000 specimens collected (14,000 beetles, 8,050 butterflies/moths).
  Many specimens sold to collectors/museums to fund the expedition.

THE WALLACE LINE:
  See File 07 for discussion of the faunal boundary.

BIOGEOGRAPHY AS A BOOK:
  The Geographical Distribution of Animals (1876) — the founding text
  of scientific biogeography.
  Divided the world into 6 major biogeographic regions (still used
  in modified form): Palaearctic, Nearctic, Neotropical, Ethiopian,
  Oriental, Australasian.
  Wallace established the methods (comparing faunal lists by region,
  tracking distribution boundaries) that define biogeography.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Cook was primarily a scientist"** — Cook was primarily a navigator and naval
officer. He is in the scientific naturalist tradition because he carried
naturalists and the primary scientific output of his voyages came from them
(Banks, Solander, the Forsters). His own contribution was enabling the science —
extraordinarily well, over three voyages covering most of the Pacific.

**"Humboldt founded ecology as a discipline"** — He coined the practices and
concepts of plant geography that became ecology; the word "ecology" was coined
by Haeckel (1866). Humboldt's contribution was the quantitative, comparative,
cross-continental approach. Every modern biome map descends from his 1805
Tableau Physique.

**"Banks was an imperial agent, not a scientist"** — Both are true. He
organized plant transfers (breadfruit, tea, rubber) that served British
imperial interests. He also built the world's best botanical collection,
transformed Kew into a global research center, and ran the Royal Society
for 42 years building scientific institutions. The scientific and imperial
roles were intertwined in his era; separating them requires anachronistic
judgment.

**"Batesian mimicry is just camouflage"** — No. Camouflage makes an organism
look like its background (invisible). Batesian mimicry makes a palatable
species look like a poisonous species that predators have LEARNED to avoid.
It requires predator learning; camouflage does not. They are mechanistically
distinct selective pressures.

**"The explorations were mainly about finding new lands"** — The 18th-century
voyages had mixed motives: territorial claim, trade route discovery, and
science. Cook's first voyage was explicitly organized around the Transit of
Venus (astronomical). The scientific programs were often the pretext for
funding that the Admiralty and governments provided. Banks spent £10,000 of
his own money on the Endeavour voyage specifically because he wanted the
natural history.
