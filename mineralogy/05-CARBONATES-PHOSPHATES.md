## The Carbonate Minerals

### Calcite Group

```
CALCITE: CaCO₃ (trigonal, R3̄c)
  Most abundant carbonate; forms at Earth surface conditions
  Hardness: 3 (scratched by a penny; scratches fingernail)
  Perfect rhombohedral cleavage: {1011} — three planes at ~75°
  Effervesces vigorously in cold dilute HCl (definitive field test)
  Strongly birefringent: "double refraction" — two images through crystal
  Isotropy: Iceland spar (optical calcite) used in Nicol prisms

FORMS OF CALCITE:
  Marble: metamorphosed limestone; coarser crystal mosaic
  Travertine: hot-spring deposited; layered, often tan/orange
  Tufa: cold-spring deposited; porous; towers in saline lakes
  Cave speleothems: stalactites, stalagmites, flowstones, cave pearls
  Chalk: soft, porous marine limestone; coccolithophore shells
  Bioclastic limestone: fragments of shells, corals, foraminifera
  Oolite: concentric layers around grain nuclei; shallow-marine carbonate

CALCITE POLYMORPHS:
  Calcite (trigonal): stable at low T/P (Earth surface)
  Aragonite (orthorhombic): stable at higher P; in subducting slabs
  Vaterite: metastable hexagonal polymorph; rare; in some biominerals
  → All have formula CaCO₃; different crystal structures
```

### Dolomite

```
DOLOMITE: CaMg(CO₃)₂ — ordered alternation of Ca and Mg layers
  Rhombohedral; very similar to calcite but Mg-rich
  Reacts with HCl only when powdered or in hot acid (not cold → test!)
  "The dolomite problem": how does dolomite form?
    Primary dolomite precipitation is kinetically inhibited at low T
    Most ancient dolomite = secondary (dolomitization of calcite)
    by Mg²⁺-rich brine replacing Ca²⁺: CaCO₃ + Mg²⁺ → CaMg(CO₃)₂ + Ca²⁺
  Major reservoir rock for oil and gas (dolomite porosity)
  Named for Dolomieu (French geologist); the Dolomites (mountains) named
    for the mineral, which is unusually prominent there

MAGNESITE: MgCO₃
  Trigonal; white; cleavage like calcite
  Refractory material (high melting point → furnace linings)
  Magnesium ore
```

### Calcium-Barium-Lead Carbonates

```
ARAGONITE: CaCO₃ (orthorhombic)
  Formed at higher P than calcite; in deep-sea sediments, high-P veins
  Also in some mollusc shells (nacre/mother of pearl is aragonite)
  Metastable at Earth surface → slowly converts to calcite
  Ancient shell fossils: aragonite → calcite conversion over time
    → Loss of original isotope/trace element chemistry

SIDERITE: FeCO₃
  Iron carbonate; rhombohedral; brown; in BIF sequences and ore deposits
  Can be an iron ore but low grade; mainly indicator mineral

RHODOCHROSITE: MnCO₃
  Trigonal; beautiful pink/red banding; Mn ore and collector mineral
  Stalactitic forms from hydrothermal deposits (Capillitas, Argentina)

SMITHSONITE: ZnCO₃
  Secondary zinc mineral; "dry-bone ore"; variable color; Zn ore

MALACHITE: Cu₂CO₃(OH)₂
  Vivid green; monoclinic; botryoidal masses; secondary copper mineral
  Stalactitic masses; banding (malachite patterns = recognizable)
  Green pigment historically; Russian malachite columns (Hermitage)

AZURITE: Cu₃(CO₃)₂(OH)₂
  Vivid blue; monoclinic; secondary copper mineral
  Converts to malachite over time (azurite → malachite dehydration)
  Blue pigment in medieval paintings (unstable; fades to green)

WITHERITE: BaCO₃
  Orthorhombic (isostructural with aragonite)
  Barium carbonate; dense; minor Ba ore

CERUSSITE: PbCO₃
  Orthorhombic (aragonite structure); dense (SG 6.5)
  Secondary lead mineral in oxidized ore zones
```

## The Carbonate System and Carbon Cycle

```
EARTH'S CARBON RESERVOIRS (approximate, in Gt C)
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  Marine carbonates (CaCO₃)          60,000,000  ← dominant     |
|  Kerogen + coal (sedimentary organic)  15,000,000                |
|  Mantle                              ~100,000,000 (poorly known) |
|  Terrestrial biomass                      550                    |
|  Soil carbon                            1,500                    |
|  Ocean (dissolved)                      38,000                   |
|  Atmosphere (CO₂)                          800  ← small!         |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

The carbonate-silicate cycle (Urey reaction):
  CaSiO₃ + CO₂ ⇌ CaCO₃ + SiO₂
  Over millions of years this sequesters atmospheric CO₂ into rock
  Reverse reaction (volcanic outgassing) returns CO₂ to atmosphere
  → Earth's long-term thermostat mechanism
```

**Cave Speleothems**: Calcite precipitates from CO₂-charged groundwater dripping into a cave air space:

```
CaCO₃ (limestone) + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ (dissolution in soil)
Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ → CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O (precipitation in cave)

Stalactite: hangs from ceiling (drip at tip)
Stalagmite: grows from floor (drip at base)
Memory aid: stalacTITE = ceiling; stalaGMITE = ground
Column: stalactite + stalagmite meet
Flowstone: sheet deposits on floors and walls
Cave pearl: concentric calcite around grain in pool
```

**Speleothem paleoclimate records**: Oxygen isotope ratios (δ¹⁸O) in speleothem calcite record past rainfall amount and source, with a temperature component. Uranium-thorium (U-Th) dating gives precise ages independent of the climate signal — unlike ice cores (layer counting) or ocean sediment cores (orbital tuning). Together they produce high-resolution climate records extending back 500,000+ years, and some Chinese cave records reach 640,000 years.

The three major Quaternary climate archives — speleothems, ice cores, and marine sediment cores — record overlapping but distinct signals:
- Marine forams (δ¹⁸O): global ice volume + deep-ocean temperature; continuous record but lower temporal resolution (~1–5 kyr)
- Ice cores (δ¹⁸O, δD): polar atmospheric temperature; annual layers preserved; limited to ~800 kyr in Antarctica
- Speleothems (δ¹⁸O, δ¹³C): regional hydroclimate (monsoon strength, rainfall); U-Th absolute ages with ±100–500 yr precision; strong monsoon = more negative δ¹⁸O in Asian cave records (amount effect dominates)

These archives cross-calibrate each other: the Dongge Cave (China) speleothem δ¹⁸O record correlates with the Greenland ice-core stadial/interstadial sequence across the last glacial cycle, demonstrating teleconnected global climate reorganizations.

## The Phosphate Minerals

### Apatite Group

```
APATITE: Ca₅(PO₄)₃(F,OH,Cl) — the most important phosphate mineral

Three end-members (continuous substitution at the X site):
  Fluorapatite: Ca₅(PO₄)₃F — most common; volcanic and metamorphic
  Hydroxyapatite: Ca₅(PO₄)₃(OH) — biological apatite (bones and teeth)
  Chlorapatite: Ca₅(PO₄)₃Cl — less common; halide-rich environments

Biological apatite: bones and teeth are composite materials
  Hydroxyapatite (70% by weight in bone) + collagen (organic framework)
  Teeth enamel: 97% fluorapatite by weight (the hardest biological material)
  Dental fluoride treatment: converts hydroxyapatite → fluorapatite
    → Fluorapatite is harder and less acid-soluble

Geochronological applications:
  Fission track dating: uranium in apatite undergoes fission → tracks in crystal
    Heating anneals tracks → track density records time since last thermal event
    Used for: cooling histories, exhumation rates, landscape evolution
  U-Th-He dating (apatite): He from U/Th decay retains above ~70°C
    → Closure temperature system for very shallow, low-T thermochronology

Hardness: 5 on Mohs scale (the reference mineral for that level)
Hexagonal; six-sided prisms with flat terminations
Colors: blue, green, yellow, purple, colorless (Mn/Fe/etc. impurities)
```

### Phosphorite — Economic Phosphate

```
PHOSPHORITE (rock): high-P sedimentary deposits
  Mainly microcrystalline apatite (carbonate-fluorapatite)
  Forms in upwelling zones: organic-rich sediment → bacterial decomposition
    → PO₄³⁻ released → precipitates as apatite in pore waters

ECONOMIC SIGNIFICANCE:
  All agricultural fertilizer phosphate comes from phosphorite mining
  No phosphorus → no crop yield → no food security
  Phosphorus is not cycled efficiently (unlike N₂) — it's a one-way flux
  Haber-Bosch solved nitrogen; phosphorus has no equivalent fix
  Major deposits: Morocco (Western Sahara ~75% of world reserves)
    Florida phosphate district (mined out or depleting)
    China, Russia (significant reserves)

PEAK PHOSPHORUS:
  Phosphate rock is a non-renewable resource on human timescales
  Recoverable reserves: ~50–300 years at current consumption
  No substitute in fertilizer; no industrial synthesis from non-mineral P
  → Genuine long-term strategic resource risk

  The supply concentration parallels the rare earth situation (see 08-ECONOMIC-GEOLOGY):
  Morocco controls ~75% of world phosphate reserves (Western Sahara included),
  creating a single-country chokepoint for global food production. Unlike REE,
  there is no recycling pathway — phosphorus dispersed into agriculture
  eventually reaches the ocean as dissolved phosphate. Recovery from wastewater
  (struvite precipitation) exists but operates at a small fraction of the needed scale.
```

### Other Phosphate Minerals

```
MONAZITE: (Ce,La,Nd,Th)PO₄
  REE + Th phosphate; monoclinic; yellow-brown; dense
  Major rare earth ore mineral (especially for Ce, La, Nd)
  Contains significant Th → radioactive → handling regulations
  U-Th-Pb geochronometer: robust system (high closure T ~900°C)
    → Dates high-grade metamorphic events

XENOTIME: YPO₄
  Yttrium + heavy REE phosphate; tetragonal (isostructural with zircon)
  Geochronometer: contains U → U-Pb dating of metamorphism

TURQUOISE: CuAl₆(PO₄)₄(OH)₈·4H₂O
  Sky blue to green; secondary; in oxidized Cu deposits in arid regions
  Prized as gemstone by pre-Columbian Americans, Persians, Egyptians
  Hardness 5–6; waxy to dull luster
  Most "turquoise" in jewelry is stabilized (resin-impregnated) low-grade

VIVIANITE: Fe₃(PO₄)₂·8H₂O
  Blue-green; forms in reducing sediments (low-O peat, lake muds)
  Indicator of preservation conditions for organic material

LAZULITE: MgAl₂(PO₄)₂(OH)₂
  Blue phosphate; rare metamorphic mineral; sometimes gem quality

WAVELLITE: Al₃(PO₄)₂(OH)₃·5H₂O
  Radial fibrous aggregates; secondary in weathering profiles
```

## Biogenic Carbonates — Shells to Chalk

```
BIOMINERALIZATION: organisms precipitate minerals
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|  Foraminifera: CaCO₃ (calcite) shells → chalk, limestone         |
|  Coccolithophores: CaCO₃ plates → pelagic chalk               |
|  Corals: CaCO₃ (aragonite) skeletons → reef limestone          |
|  Bivalves: CaCO₃ (calcite + aragonite) shells                  |
|  Echinoderms: single calcite crystals → crinoidal limestone      |
|  Siliceous organisms: SiO₂ (amorphous) → chert, diatomite      |
|  Phosphatic organisms: Ca₅(PO₄)₃(OH) in bones and teeth        |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

SHELL CHEMISTRY AS PROXY:
  δ¹⁸O in foram shells → sea surface temperature and ice volume
  Mg/Ca ratio in foram shells → temperature
  δ¹³C → carbon cycle, productivity, circulation
  Sr/Ca → temperature
  → Paleoclimate proxy archive in marine sediment cores
```

## Cave Mineral Diversity

Beyond common calcite speleothems:

| Mineral | Formula | Formation | Notes |
|---------|---------|-----------|-------|
| Aragonite | CaCO₃ | Mg-rich waters | Converts to calcite with time |
| Gypsum | CaSO₄·2H₂O | Oxidation of sulfides | Selenite crystals in Naica cave (Mexico) |
| Halite | NaCl | Evaporation in arid caves | Salt caves in Israel |
| Moonmilk | CaCO₃ variants | Bacterial activity | Soft, white, pasty |
| Huntite | CaMg₃(CO₃)₄ | Low T, high Mg | White coating |
| Hydromagnesite | Mg₅(CO₃)₄(OH)₂·4H₂O | Oxidation of mafic rocks | Speleothems in lava tubes |

**Naica Cave (Chihuahua, Mexico)**: gypsum crystals (selenite, CaSO₄·2H₂O) up to 11 meters long, 55 tonnes — formed over ~600,000 years in constant 58°C, 100% humidity conditions. The largest natural crystals ever found.

## Common Confusion Points

**Calcite vs. aragonite tests**: Both CaCO₃; both effervesce in HCl. Distinguish by: crystal form (rhombohedra vs. orthorhombic tabular), specific gravity (2.71 vs. 2.93 — aragonite denser), and Feigl's solution (stains aragonite black, not calcite) in the lab.

**Dolomite and the cold HCl test**: Many students expect carbonates to always effervesce. Dolomite does not effervesce noticeably in cold acid — this is a diagnostic point. Grinding a sample dramatically increases reaction rate.

**Apatite's variable chemistry**: The apatite group spans fluorapatite, hydroxyapatite, and chlorapatite, all with continuous substitution. When a geologist says "apatite," they usually mean calcium phosphate with some F/OH/Cl — rarely a specific end-member.

**Phosphorus cycle is not like carbon cycle**: Carbon cycles between atmosphere, ocean, and biomass relatively quickly. Phosphorus released from rocks enters the ocean and largely settles into sediment — it returns to the atmosphere/biosphere only through tectonic uplift and weathering. There's no atmospheric phosphorus gas phase. This is why phosphate rock is a genuinely non-renewable fertilizer resource.

**Monazite is radioactive**: Thorium content in monazite is substantial (~5–10% ThO₂). Beach sands concentrated in monazite (e.g., Kerala coast, India) are measurably radioactive. Handling regulations apply to monazite ore concentrates. This doesn't make the rock dangerous — the Th is embedded in the crystal lattice and insoluble — but dust and processing require precautions.
