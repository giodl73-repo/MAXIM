# Eusociality: Ants, Bees, Wasps, and Termites

## The Big Picture

Eusociality is the highest level of social organization in the animal kingdom: overlapping generations living together, cooperative brood care, and reproductive division of labor. It has evolved independently ~15 times in insects, and the majority of insect biomass on Earth is eusocial. Understanding it requires grasping both the evolutionary logic (Hamilton's kin selection) and the proximate mechanisms (pheromones, caste determination).

```
EUSOCIALITY DEFINITION (Michener 1969, refined)
=================================================

THREE CRITERIA (all three required):
  1. Overlapping generations         -- parents + offspring cohabit
  2. Cooperative brood care          -- workers help raise young not their own
  3. Reproductive division of labor  -- some individuals forgo own reproduction
                                        (workers) while others specialize
                                        (queens, reproductives)

GRADES OF SOCIALITY:
  Solitary ---------> Subsocial --> Semisocial --> Primitively eusocial --> Advanced eusocial
  (no cooperation)   (parent        (castes,       (castes + overlap;       (morphological
                      feeds young)   same gen)      small colony)            castes; large
                                                                             colony)

EUSOCIAL INSECTS:
  Hymenoptera:  ants (all), many bees, some wasps -- ~20K eusocial spp
  Blattodea:    termites (all, ~3,500 spp) -- evolved independently
  Thysanoptera: some gall thrips (Kladothrips) -- unusual case
  Hemiptera:    aphid soldiers in some species -- partial eusociality
```

## Hamilton's Kin Selection: The Evolutionary Engine

```
HAMILTON'S RULE
================

  rb > c

  r = relatedness coefficient (probability of sharing allele by descent)
  b = benefit to recipient (in units of offspring)
  c = cost to actor (in reproductive offspring foregone)

  An altruistic behavior (working for others, not reproducing) is
  favored by natural selection when the INDIRECT FITNESS gained
  (r x b) exceeds the DIRECT FITNESS lost (c).

HAPLODIPLOIDY AND HYMENOPTERA
================================

Hymenoptera sex determination:
  Females: diploid (2n) -- from fertilized eggs
  Males:   haploid (n)  -- from unfertilized eggs

Relatedness in a colony with single-mated queen:
  r(sister-sister) = 3/4
  r(mother-daughter) = 1/2

  Compare to diploid species:
  r(sister-sister) = 1/2
  r(mother-daughter) = 1/2

IMPLICATION:
  A female worker shares 3/4 of her genes with her sisters.
  She shares only 1/2 with her own daughters (if she reproduced).
  Therefore helping rear sisters is MORE inclusive-fitness-efficient
  than reproducing directly -- PROVIDED the colony produces enough sisters.

  This is why eusociality evolved many times in Hymenoptera but
  only ONCE (termites) in diploid insects -- haplodiploidy tips rb > c.

CAVEAT (modern view):
  Haplodiploidy is predisposing but not sufficient.
  Multiple mating (polyandry) reduces relatedness: if queen mates with
  10 males, worker relatedness to sisters = ~0.25 -- same as diploid.
  Modern view: kin selection + ecological factors + monogamy hypothesis
  (Boomsma 2009: eusociality requires lifetime monogamy in founders)
```

## Ants (Formicidae): The Dominant Eusocials

```
FORMICIDAE -- ~22,000 SPECIES, 100% EUSOCIAL
==============================================

COLONY STRUCTURE:
  Queen(s): reproductive; egg-laying
    Monogyne: one queen per colony (Solenopsis invicta solitary type)
    Polygyne: multiple queens (Solenopsis invicta invasive type)
  Workers: sterile females; all tasks except reproduction
    Monomorphic: one worker caste (Camponotus)
    Polymorphic: multiple size classes (Atta, Pheidole)
      Minor workers: general tasks
      Major workers / soldiers: defense, seed milling
      Super-majors (Pheidole megacephala): extreme size polymorphism
  Males: haploid; short-lived; mate during nuptial flight, die

CASTE DETERMINATION:
  All females start equivalent (fertilized egg)
  Queen vs worker: determined by larval nutrition/physiology
  NOT genetic (in most ants) -- epigenetic/developmental
  Exception: some species have genetic caste determination (Pogonomyrmex)

REPRODUCTIVE FLIGHT (NUPTIAL FLIGHT):
  New queens + males emerge simultaneously (sometimes thousands of colonies)
  Synchronization: weather cues (temperature, humidity, photoperiod)
  Mating: mid-air; multiple males per queen (polyandry)
  Queens: store sperm in spermatheca for 15-20 year colony life
  Males: die within hours-days of mating (no other function)
```

### Ant Subfamilies and Ecology

```
ANT SUBFAMILY DIVERSITY
========================

Myrmicinae:  ~6,000 spp; Atta (leafcutter), Solenopsis (fire ants),
             Pheidole, Pogonomyrmex
Formicinae:  ~3,000 spp; Camponotus (carpenter ants), Formica,
             Lasius (wood ants), Polyrhachis
Dolichoderinae: ~1,000 spp; Linepithema, Tapinoma; dolichoderine acid defense
Ponerinae:   ~1,600 spp; primitive; large-bodied; venomous; small colonies
Dorylinae:   ~700 spp; army ants (Eciton, Dorylus); nomadic swarm raids

ECOLOGICAL ROLES:
  Leafcutter ants (Atta, Acromyrmex):
    Cut vegetation -> cultivate Leucoagaricus fungus in underground gardens
    Oldest known agriculture (~50 Mya origin)
    Attini = obligate mutualism with fungus (fungal lineages never found outside ant nests)

  Army ants (Eciton burchellii):
    Nomadic colonies up to 500,000 workers
    Swarm raids kill all arthropods in path (100-200m^2/hr)
    Bivouacs: living nest made of interlocked ant bodies
    Antbird following: >50 bird species follow raids for flushed arthropods

  Honeypot ants (Myrmecocystus):
    Repletes: workers gorged with nectar until abdomen distends
    Living food storage for colony during drought

  Weaver ants (Oecophylla):
    Build arboreal nests by pulling leaves together
    Larvae used as "living glue" dispensers -- workers hold larvae
    against leaf edges while larvae spin silk
```

## Honeybees (Apis mellifera): Best-Studied Eusocial

```
APIS MELLIFERA COLONY STRUCTURE
=================================

  QUEEN (1 per colony):
    Diploid female; sole egg layer
    Mates once in life (nuptial flight; multiple drones)
    Stores ~6 million sperm in spermatheca
    Lives 3-5 years
    Pheromone: Queen Mandibular Pheromone (QMP) suppresses worker ovary

  WORKERS (50,000-80,000 females):
    Sterile under normal conditions; ovaries activated only in queenless colony
    Age polyethism: task changes with age
      Days 1-3:   cell cleaning
      Days 3-10:  nurse bees (royal jelly/brood food production)
      Days 10-18: wax production, comb building
      Days 18-21: guard duty (at hive entrance)
      Days 21+:   foraging
    Live ~40 days in summer; several months in winter

  DRONES (~500-1000 males):
    Haploid; no sting; no wax glands; no pollen baskets
    Sole function: mate with virgin queens from other colonies
    Expelled before winter (workers throw them out to die)
```

### Waggle Dance: A Symbolic Communication System

```
WAGGLE DANCE COMMUNICATION
============================

Discovery: Karl von Frisch (Nobel Prize 1973)

ROUND DANCE: food source < 50m from hive
  Worker dances in circles; recruits search nearby

WAGGLE DANCE: food source > 50m from hive
  Worker runs a figure-8 with waggle run in middle

INFORMATION ENCODED:
  Direction: angle of waggle run relative to vertical
             = angle of food relative to sun's azimuth
  Distance:  duration of waggle run
             1 second waggle ~ 1km

  +-----------------------------------+
  | Waggle run at 60 deg from vertical|
  | -> food is 60 deg to RIGHT of sun |
  |                                   |
  | Waggle run for 2 seconds          |
  | -> food is ~2 km away            |
  +-----------------------------------+

This is the ONLY known symbolic communication in invertebrates:
  - Uses a reference (the sun) not currently visible
  - Encodes two independent variables (direction + distance)
  - Decoded correctly by other workers in the dark hive
```

### Caste Determination: Queen vs Worker

```
HONEY BEE CASTE DETERMINATION
===============================

All female larvae initially identical
  |
  Brood cell type:
    Worker cell (hexagonal, small) -> worker
    Queen cell (vertical, large)   -> queen (if royally fed)

  Royal jelly feeding:
    All larvae fed royal jelly first 3 days
    Worker larvae: switched to beebread (pollen + honey) after day 3
    Queen larvae:  royal jelly throughout larval development

  Molecular mechanism (discovered 2008):
    Royal jelly -> royalactin protein -> activates EGFR/TOR pathways
    -> accelerated growth + ovary development + longevity

  Result:
    Worker larva: ~6 days larval + 12 days pupa -> worker
    Queen larva:  ~5 days larval + 8 days pupa -> larger, fertile queen
    SAME GENOME; entirely different organisms via epigenetic regulation
```

## Social Wasps (Vespidae)

```
VESPIDAE SOCIAL SPECTRUM
==========================

Primitively eusocial (Polistes paper wasps):
  Small colonies, 10-100 workers
  No morphological queen/worker distinction
  Queen established by dominance behavior (not pheromone)
  Foundress acts as queen; workers behaviorally subordinate
  Workers can reproduce if queen removed

Highly eusocial (Vespula, Dolichovespula, Vespa):
  Large colonies, 5,000-15,000 workers (Vespa mandarinia: up to 300 workers)
  Paper nests (chewed wood fiber)
  Annual colonies in temperate zones
  Morphological queen (larger, distinct) vs workers

Vespa mandarinia (giant Asian hornet):
  Can kill ~300 honey bees in 90 minutes (30 hornets vs 30,000 bees)
  Japanese honey bees (Apis cerana japonica) counter: heat-ball defense
    Workers surround hornet, vibrate, raise ball to 47 C -> hornet dies
  European honey bee (Apis mellifera): no evolved defense; vulnerable
```

## Termites (Blattodea: Termitoidae): Independent Eusocial Evolution

```
TERMITE COLONY STRUCTURE
==========================

DIPLOID (both sexes work -- unlike Hymenoptera where all workers female)

  QUEEN: morphologically distinct; physogastric (enormously enlarged
         abdomen for egg production); up to 2,000 eggs/day (Macrotermes)
         Lives 20-50 years in some species

  KING:  remains with queen; mates repeatedly throughout life
         (unlike Hymenoptera where males die after mating)

  WORKERS: both male and female; sterile; do all labor
            (no haplodiploidy -- kin selection less predicted here)

  SOLDIERS: morphologically modified for defense
    Mandibulate: enlarged mandibles (Nasutitermes: mandible-bearing)
    Nasute:      rounded head; spray terpene compound from nasus
    Some species: soldiers with autothysis (self-explosion)
      Camponotus saundersi (not termite): but see Neocapritermes taracua
      Worker carries explosive glands; ruptures when threatened

CELLULOSE DIGESTION:
  Lower termites: protozoan hindgut symbionts (Trichonympha)
    (inherited by trophallaxis -- proctodeal feeding)
  Higher termites (Termitidae): bacterial microbiome only
    Macrotermes: fungus-farming (parallel to Atta ants!)

MOUND ENGINEERING:
  Macrotermes bellicosus mounds: 3m tall, 300kg, maintain 30C internal
  while external T varies from 6-40C; CO2/O2 regulation via diffusion
  through porous outer mall; fungus gardens at 29-31C; underground water
```

## Pheromone Communication in Eusocial Insects

```
PHEROMONE CLASSES IN SOCIAL INSECTS
=====================================

RELEASER PHEROMONES (immediate behavioral effect):
  Alarm: isoamyl acetate (Apis); formic acid (Formica)
  Recruitment/trail: pheromone glands varies by species
    Atta: metapleural gland; Solenopsis: Dufour's gland
  Aggregation: recruit nestmates to resource
  Sex/mating: queens release compounds attracting drones

PRIMER PHEROMONES (physiological effect, longer-term):
  Queen Mandibular Pheromone (QMP, Apis):
    5-component blend; suppresses worker ovary development
    Attracts drones during nuptial flight
    Maintains colony cohesion
  Queen recognition: colony specific hydrocarbon blends on cuticle
    Colony members recognized by CHC (cuticular hydrocarbon) profile
    Non-nestmates: attacked or expelled

TRAIL PHEROMONES (recruitment):
  Ant trail: volatile + semi-volatile compounds in combination
    Short-range: highly volatile (evaporates fast) -> directs near trail
    Long-range: less volatile -> persists as broad plume
    Interrupted trail: recruits stop -> foragers abandon trail
  Bee trail:  Nasonov gland pheromone (geraniol + nerol + citral)
    "Hive scent" -- orients bees to hive entrance

ALARM PHEROMONES:
  Release: mandibular gland compounds (many ants)
  Formic acid: Formica ants; also defensive spray
  Venom alkaloids (piperidine): Solenopsis fire ants
  Dose-dependent: low dose attracts; high dose releases biting/stinging
```

### Engineering Bridges

Eusocial insect colonies are distributed systems running without central coordination. Three architectural patterns from CS map directly:

**Stigmergy as indirect message passing**: ant trail pheromones are not signals between agents — they are state written to shared medium (the substrate) that other agents read and modify. No ant communicates directly with another to recruit to a food source; each ant reads the pheromone gradient and writes more pheromone on its return trip. The trail emerges from this read-write process without any agent having global knowledge. This is the actor model with persistent shared state: agents are stateless, the environment is the state, messages are indirect. It is also identical to how distributed cache invalidation works via shared counters — no direct peer communication, just writes to shared state that others poll.

**Queen pheromone as distributed leader-lease**: the queen produces Queen Mandibular Pheromone (QMP) that suppresses worker ovary development and worker queen-rearing behavior. As long as QMP is detectable at sufficient concentration throughout the colony, the queen's "lease" is active — no workers attempt to supersede her. When QMP concentration drops (queen aging, death, or removal), the lease expires and the colony initiates emergency queen rearing or swarms. This is a heartbeat-based leader election protocol: the leader broadcasts a keep-alive signal; if the signal drops below threshold, followers initiate election. The pheromone gradient from queen to peripheral workers provides the implicit TTL — workers furthest from the queen detect pheromone loss first.

**Task allocation as distributed load balancing**: a honey bee colony allocates workers to tasks (nursing, foraging, guarding, building) without any central scheduler. Each worker's probability of accepting a task increases when: (a) the task-associated stimulus is above threshold and (b) the worker's response threshold for that task is low (workers vary in individual response thresholds). High-demand tasks (many larvae needing nursing) raise stimulus signals; workers with low nursing thresholds respond first; as they respond, stimulus drops and others stop. This is demand-responsive load balancing without a coordinator — each worker is a self-scheduling agent that responds to load signals in proportion to its threshold.

## Common Confusion Points

**Haplodiploidy is not sufficient for eusociality**: Many haplodiploid Hymenoptera (most parasitoid wasps, many bee species) are solitary. Haplodiploidy predisposes but does not cause eusociality. The monogamy hypothesis (Boomsma) adds that lifetime monogamy of the founding pair is the key common condition.

**Workers are not sterile by genetics**: Workers can develop ovaries and lay unfertilized (male) eggs if the queen dies or is removed. In honeybees, this causes "laying worker" colonies. Worker sterility is maintained by queen pheromone (QMP) and social dominance, not by genetics.

**Termite "workers" are different from ant/bee workers**: In Hymenoptera, workers are reproductively suppressed adult females. In termites, "workers" are often immature instars (pseudergates) that can molt into reproductives under certain conditions — not permanently sterile in many primitive species.

**Queen bee vs queen ant**: In Apis, queens are continuously produced from worker larvae when a new queen is needed (swarming, supersedure). In ants, new queens (gynes) are produced only seasonally during nuptial flight period and are morphologically distinct from birth.

**Trophallaxis is a communication channel**: Liquid food exchange (mouth-to-mouth or anus-to-mouth) among nestmates is not just nutrition transfer. It transmits pheromones, symbionts (in termites), and information about colony nutritional state.
