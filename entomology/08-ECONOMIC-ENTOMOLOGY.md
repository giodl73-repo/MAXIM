# Economic Entomology: Pests and Biocontrol

## The Big Picture

Economic entomology is applied insect ecology: quantifying when insect populations cause economic harm and deploying biological, chemical, and cultural tools to manage them below damage thresholds. The dominant framework — Integrated Pest Management (IPM) — emerged in the 1960s as a reaction to the chemical pesticide paradigm and treats pest management as an optimization problem with ecological, economic, and toxicological constraints.

```
ECONOMIC ENTOMOLOGY FRAMEWORK
================================

                    PEST POPULATION
                         |
            +------------+------------+
            |                         |
    Below threshold             Above threshold
            |                         |
    No intervention          INTERVENTION OPTIONS:
                             +---------+-----------+
                             |         |           |
                         Cultural  Biological  Chemical
                         control   control     control
                             |         |           |
                             +----+----+           |
                                  |                |
                             INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT
                             (IPM: combine intelligently)

ECONOMIC THRESHOLDS:
  EIL = Economic Injury Level: density at which crop loss = cost of control
  ET  = Economic Threshold: action level (apply control BEFORE EIL to prevent crossing it)
  ET always < EIL (by definition -- act before injury, not at injury)
  ET varies by: crop value, control cost, pest growth rate, timing
```

## Major Crop Pests by System

```
MAJOR INSECT PEST GROUPS
==========================

APHIDS (Hemiptera: Aphididae)
  Direct damage: phloem feeding -> wilt, honeydew -> sooty mold
  Indirect damage: virus vectors (PVY, CMV, BYDV, CTV)
  Life history: parthenogenetic explosive growth; winged dispersal
  Key species: Myzus persicae (green peach), Aphis gossypii (cotton),
               Rhopalosiphum padi (bird cherry-oat), Acyrthosiphon pisum (pea)
  Control: selective aphicides, parasitoid wasps (Aphidius), lacewings

WHITEFLIES (Hemiptera: Aleyrodidae)
  Bemisia tabaci (biotype B = silverleaf whitefly):
    Phloem feeder + major virus vector (Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus, etc.)
    B biotype: more virulent, more pesticide resistant than A biotype
    Invasive globally (tropical origin; spread via ornamental trade)
  Trialeurodes vaporariorum: greenhouse whitefly; temperate
  Control: Encarsia formosa parasitoid, Beauveria bassiana, neonicotinoids

THRIPS (Thysanoptera: Thripidae)
  Frankliniella occidentalis: western flower thrips
    Feeding damage + Tospoviruses (TSWV, INSV) -- major greenhouse pest
    Cryptic behavior: flowers, leaf folds; difficult to monitor
  Thrips palmi: tropical; melon, pepper
  Control: blue/yellow sticky traps; predatory mites (Amblyseius); spinosad

SPIDER MITES (Arachnida, not insects, but managed in economic entomology)
  Tetranychus urticae: two-spotted spider mite
    Chelicerate; not Insecta -- but canonically included in IPM textbooks
    Cell-content feeders; thrive in hot/dry conditions; rapid resistance
```

### Caterpillars (Lepidoptera)

```
LEPIDOPTERA PESTS
==================

NOCTUIDAE (owlet moths) -- most damaging family:
  Helicoverpa armigera (cotton bollworm): global; polyphagous; Bt resistant
  Heliothis virescens (tobacco budworm)
  Spodoptera frugiperda (fall armyworm): major grass/corn pest; Africa invasion 2016
  Spodoptera exigua (beet armyworm): vegetable crops
  Mythimna separata (armyworm): small grains, Asia

PYRALIDAE / CRAMBIDAE:
  Ostrinia nubilalis (European corn borer): single largest pest in US corn
  Chilo suppressalis (striped rice borer): Asia
  Plutella xylostella (diamondback moth): Brassica specialist; Bt resistant in field

TORTRICIDAE (leafrollers):
  Cydia pomonella (codling moth): apple + pear; key managed pest
  Archips spp.: broad leafrollers in orchards

SPHINGIDAE (hornworms):
  Manduca quinquemaculata/sexta: tobacco/tomato; Braconid parasitoid wasp models

MANAGEMENT:
  Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis): Cry toxins; highly specific to Lepidoptera
  Pheromone traps: monitoring emergence, mating disruption
  Nuclear polyhedrosis virus (NPV): Helicoverpa, Spodoptera
  Trichogramma wasps: egg parasitoids; widely released
```

### Beetles (Coleoptera)

```
COLEOPTERA PESTS
=================

ROOT/SOIL PESTS:
  Diabrotica virgifera (western corn rootworm):
    Most expensive pest in US agriculture; ~$1B/yr damage + control
    Larvae: attack corn roots
    Adults: silk feeding + leaf damage; vector of bacterial wilt
    Resistance: to soil insecticides, Bt Cry3Bb1, crop rotation
  Agriotes spp. (wireworms): click beetle larvae; soil; root crops

STORED GRAIN PESTS:
  Sitophilus granarius/oryzae (grain weevil): inside grain kernels
  Tribolium castaneum/confusum (flour beetles): surface feeders
  Callosobruchus maculatus: legume bruchid; endemic in tropical storage

BARK BEETLES (Curculionidae: Scolytinae):
  Dendroctonus frontalis (southern pine beetle): epidemic outbreaks kill stands
  Dendroctonus ponderosae (mountain pine beetle): 50M ha killed in BC alone
  Ips typographus (European spruce bark beetle): post-windstorm outbreaks
  Mechanism: pheromone mass attack; blue-stain fungus carried by beetle
  Threshold: trees withstand <10 attacks/m^2 bark; mass attack overwhelms resin

INVASIVE BORERS:
  Agrilus planipennis (emerald ash borer): all North American Fraxinus
    ~99% ash mortality within 10 years of infestation
  Anoplophora glabripennis (Asian longhorned beetle): broadleaf species
  Bactrocera dorsalis (oriental fruit fly): tropical fruits
```

## Biological Control

```
BIOLOGICAL CONTROL OVERVIEW
==============================

DEFINITION: Use of living organisms to suppress pest populations

TYPES:
  CLASSICAL BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
    Import natural enemy from pest's native range
    Release into new range (where pest has "escaped")
    Target: invasive pests with no local natural enemies
    Example: Aphytis melinus (chalcid wasp) vs California red scale
             Rodolia cardinalis (ladybeetle) vs cottony cushion scale (1888)
             Rodolia: first successful biocontrol; saved California citrus

  AUGMENTATIVE BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
    Mass-rear and periodically release natural enemies
    Inundative: large single releases (flood pest with enemies)
    Inoculative: small release; establish self-sustaining population
    Examples:
      Encarsia formosa: greenhouse whitefly (widely commercial)
      Trichogramma wasps: egg parasitoids for Lepidoptera
      Phytoseiid mites: spider mite control in glasshouses
      Amblyseius cucumeris: western flower thrips

  CONSERVATION BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
    Modify environment to enhance existing natural enemies
    Wildflower strips: parasitoid wasp nectar
    Reduced tillage: ground beetle populations
    Hedgerows: overwintering beneficial insects
    Avoid broad-spectrum insecticides during peak enemy activity
```

### Key Natural Enemy Families

```
NATURAL ENEMY GROUPS
=====================

PARASITOID HYMENOPTERA:
  Ichneumonidae (~24,000 spp): larval/pupal parasitoids of Lepidoptera
  Braconidae (~17,000 spp):    Apanteles, Cotesia (Lepidoptera);
                                Aphidius, Praon (aphids)
  Pteromalidae:                pupal parasitoids
  Trichogrammatidae:           egg parasitoids (1mm body; widely released)
  Chalcidoidea:                scale insect parasitoids

PARASITOID DIPTERA:
  Tachinidae (~8,500 spp): largest parasitoid fly family
    Compsilura concinnata: polyphagous; imported for gypsy moth;
    unintended effect on native Lepidoptera (classic non-target risk)

PREDATORY BEETLES:
  Coccinellidae (ladybeetles): aphid + scale predators
    Hippodamia convergens, Harmonia axyridis (invasive; outcompetes natives)
  Carabidae (ground beetles): generalist; soil-surface prey
    Poecilus cupreus, Bembidion spp.: aphid eggs, small larvae

PREDATORY BUGS:
  Orius spp. (pirate bugs): thrips + whitefly eggs
  Macrolophus pygmaeus: aphid + whitefly (Mediterranean tomato biocontrol)

ENTOMOPATHOGENIC FUNGI:
  Beauveria bassiana: broad spectrum; contact; spore penetrates cuticle
  Metarhizium anisopliae: soil pests; commercially produced
  Lecanicillium muscarium: whitefly
```

## Microbial Insecticides

```
MICROBIAL PEST CONTROL AGENTS (MPCAs)
========================================

Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt):
  Gram-positive sporulating bacteria
  Produces protoxin crystals (Cry proteins) in spore
  Ingested: midgut alkaline pH activates toxin
  Cry toxin: binds midgut receptor -> pore formation -> cell lysis

  SEROTYPES AND TARGETS:
  Bt kurstaki (Btk):   Lepidoptera (most commercial uses)
  Bt israelensis (Bti): Diptera (mosquito larvae, blackflies)
  Bt tenebrionis (Btt): Coleoptera (Colorado potato beetle)
  Bt aizawai:           Lepidoptera (diamondback moth, armyworm)

  TRANSGENIC Bt CROPS (Bt crops):
    Cry gene inserted into plant genome
    Plant produces Bt toxin in all tissues (or tissue-specific)
    Events: MON810 (corn, Cry1Ab), Bt Bollgard II (cotton, Cry1Ac+Cry2Ab)
    Resistance management: HIGH-DOSE + REFUGE (structured refuge)
      -> susceptible genotypes survive in refuge -> dilute resistance alleles

NUCLEAR POLYHEDROSIS VIRUSES (NPV/baculoviruses):
  Highly specific (usually genus/species level)
  Ingested by larva -> virus replicates -> larva liquefies
  Applied at high larval density; slow (5-10 days vs 24hr Bt)
  Examples: Helicoverpa NPV; Spodoptera exigua NPV

ENTOMOPATHOGENIC NEMATODES (EPNs):
  Steinernema, Heterorhabditis species
  Carry symbiotic bacteria (Xenorhabdus, Photorhabdus)
  Enter insect through cuticle/orifices -> release bacteria -> insect dies
  Applied: soil drenches for soil/root pests (vine weevil, chafer grubs)
  Short persistence; need moist conditions
```

## IPM: The Integrated Framework

```
IPM DECISION FRAMEWORK
========================

1. PEST IDENTIFICATION
   Correct species ID -> correct biology -> correct management
   Common misidentification: aphid vs leafhopper; thrips vs mite damage

2. POPULATION MONITORING
   Sticky traps: flying insects; track emergence timing
   Pheromone traps: sex lure; moth flight activity
   Beat tray: dislodge insects from foliage; count
   Visual counts: aphids/leaf; whitefly adults/leaf
   Degree-day models: predict emergence from weather data

3. THRESHOLD DETERMINATION
   EIL: calculate from crop value, pest damage coefficient, yield loss
   ET: set below EIL (typically 50-80% of EIL)
   Dynamic thresholds: account for crop growth stage, season timing

4. CONTROL SELECTION MATRIX
   +------------------+----------+----------+---------+---------+
   | Control method   | Speed    | Specificity| Cost   | Risk    |
   +------------------+----------+----------+---------+---------+
   | Chemical (broad) | Fast     | Low      | Low     | High    |
   | Chemical (select)| Fast     | Moderate | Medium  | Medium  |
   | Biological augm. | Slow     | High     | Medium  | Low     |
   | Biological class.| Very slow| High     | One-time| Low     |
   | Cultural         | Slow     | Moderate | Low     | None    |
   | Pheromone        | Slow     | Very high| Medium  | None    |
   +------------------+----------+----------+---------+---------+

5. EVALUATION
   Monitor post-application -> confirm below EIL
   Assess natural enemy impact -> conservation value
   Resistance monitoring -> rotate modes of action
```

## Pesticide Resistance: The Evolutionary Arms Race

```
PESTICIDE RESISTANCE EVOLUTION
==================================

MECHANISM:
  Initial population: rare resistant alleles at low frequency
  Selection pressure (pesticide): susceptibles die
  Resistant alleles increase in frequency rapidly
  At >1% resistance frequency: resistance practically significant

RESISTANCE MECHANISMS:
  Metabolic:
    CYP450 upregulation -> faster oxidation of insecticide
    Esterase amplification -> faster hydrolysis
    GST upregulation -> faster conjugation
  Target site:
    kdr mutation (knock-down resistance): sodium channel L1014F/S
    -> pyrethroids ineffective
    AChE insensitivity (Ace mutation): organophosphate/carbamate resistance
    nAChR mutation: neonicotinoid resistance in some Apis, Bemisia
  Cuticular:
    Thickened cuticle -> reduced penetration
  Behavioral:
    Avoidance of treated surfaces (cockroaches, bed bugs)

RESISTANCE MANAGEMENT:
  Rotate modes of action (IRAC classification: Group 1, 2, 3...)
  Mixing: rarely recommended (selects for multiple resistance)
  High-dose/refuge (Bt): structured refuges maintain susceptible insects
  Refugia: natural untreated habitat near crops
  Monitoring: bioassays + molecular markers for early detection
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Pest type | First-line IPM | Biological agent | Key threshold concept |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Aphids | Beneficial insect conservation; selective aphicide | Aphidius wasps; Orius | >10 aphids/leaf (varies by crop) |
| Whitefly | Yellow sticky traps (monitoring) | Encarsia formosa | 3-4 adults/leaf in greenhouse |
| Thrips | Blue sticky traps; reflective mulch | Amblyseius cucumeris | TSWV risk > density threshold |
| Caterpillars | Pheromone traps; Bt kurstaki | Trichogramma (egg); NPV | Egg mass/frass counts |
| Corn borer | Bt crop or Trichogramma release | Trichogramma ostriniae | >25% plants with eggs |
| Bark beetle | Sanitation; pheromone traps | Natural (woodpeckers, parasitoids limited) | Prevent mass attack synchronization |
| Soil pests | Crop rotation; EPN | Steinernema/Heterorhabditis | Larvae/m^2 count |

## Common Confusion Points

**EIL vs ET**: EIL = the density at which damage = control cost. ET = the density at which you should apply control to prevent reaching EIL. ET < EIL always. ET is the action threshold; EIL is the economic definition.

**Classical vs augmentative biocontrol**: Classical = import and release foreign natural enemy (one-time event; aims for permanent establishment). Augmentative = mass-rear and repeatedly release native or commercial natural enemies (like applying a biological insecticide). The business model for augmentative biocontrol is ongoing sales; for classical, it's a one-time government program.

**Bt crops vs Bt sprays**: Bt sprays (Btk, Bti) are OMRI-approved in organic farming. Bt crops (transgenic; Cry gene in plant) are not approved for organic. Both use Cry proteins, but organic status is determined by the production method (genetic transformation), not the molecule.

**Resistance management**: IRAC mode-of-action groups define chemical classes. Rotating between groups (not just products) is the key principle. Group 4 = neonicotinoids; Group 3 = pyrethroids; Group 1 = organophosphates. Using two Group 4 products in rotation does NOT constitute rotation.

**Non-target effects of classical biocontrol**: Generalist natural enemies imported for one pest can attack non-target species. Compsilura concinnata (tachinid fly) imported for gypsy moth has reduced native silk moth and giant silk moth populations in N. America. Risk assessment now required before USDA-APHIS approval.
