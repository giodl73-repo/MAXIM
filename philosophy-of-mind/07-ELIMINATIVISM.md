## Folk Psychology as Theory

### The Theoretical Character of Belief/Desire Explanation

```
FOLK PSYCHOLOGY AS THEORY (Sellars, Dennett, Churchland)
=========================================================

FOLK PSYCHOLOGY:
  The practice of explaining and predicting behavior by attributing
  propositional attitudes:
  - Beliefs: "She believes it will rain"
  - Desires: "He wants coffee"
  - Intentions: "They intend to leave"
  - Hopes, fears, expectations, etc.

  This is not just a description of how we talk.
  It's a THEORY of human behavior.
  It makes predictions:
  "She believes it will rain AND wants to stay dry
   THEREFORE she will take an umbrella."
  This is a law-like generalization.

WILFRID SELLARS: "THE MYTH OF THE GIVEN" (1956)
  Our access to our own mental states is not "given" immediately.
  Mental state vocabulary is learned within a social practice.
  "Pain" doesn't refer to a raw given -- it's a concept embedded
  in a theory of how people behave when hurt.
  Mental concepts are theoretical concepts, like "electron."

IF FOLK PSYCHOLOGY IS A THEORY, IT CAN BE:
  1. REDUCED to neuroscience (reduction -- standard view)
     Folk concepts map onto neural concepts.
  2. REPLACED by a better theory (eliminativism)
     Folk concepts have no neural analogs -- they're replaced.
  3. VINDICATED as is (folk psychology is approximately correct)
  4. Used as a useful but literally false heuristic (Dennett)
```

## Churchland's Eliminative Materialism

```
PAUL CHURCHLAND: "MATTER AND CONSCIOUSNESS" (1984)
====================================================

THESIS: Folk psychology is a radically false theory.
Beliefs, desires, pains, memories, emotions as folk-theoretically
conceived do not exist.
Neuroscience will not reduce folk psychology to neural states.
It will REPLACE folk psychology with a better theory,
just as chemistry replaced phlogiston theory, not reduced it.

THE PHLOGISTON ANALOGY:
  Phlogiston theory: burning = release of phlogiston.
  Chemistry: burning = oxidation. (No phlogiston.)
  We didn't reduce "phlogiston" to "oxygen deficiency."
  We eliminated "phlogiston" entirely from our vocabulary.

  ANALOGOUSLY:
  "Belief" may not correspond to any natural kind in neuroscience.
  The neural network of a brain may not have states that
  map cleanly onto the belief/desire taxonomy.
  Just as the brain doesn't have phlogiston,
  it may not have "beliefs" as folk psychology conceives them.

WHY FOLK PSYCHOLOGY IS FAILING:
  1. It has been stagnant for millennia.
     No major theoretical advances in the folk psychological
     account of cognition since ancient Greece.
     Compare: chemistry, physics -- radical revolutions.

  2. It fails to connect with neuroscience.
     No principled bridge between belief/desire vocabulary
     and neural vocabulary.
     No neural state that is clearly "the belief that P."

  3. Many mental phenomena are poorly explained by folk categories:
     Mental illness (what makes depression a "belief-desire" failure?)
     Sleep, learning, attention, creativity, perception
     are all poorly served by folk psychological categories.

  4. Folk psychology DOES explain behavior surprisingly well.
     Churchland: this is because we've adapted our behavior
     to fit folk psychological descriptions, not because folk
     psychology is accurate about inner states.
```

### Patricia Churchland's Neurophilosophy

```
PATRICIA CHURCHLAND: "NEUROPHILOSOPHY" (1986)
===============================================

Extends Paul's program to a positive research agenda:
  Philosophy should become continuous with neuroscience.
  Philosophical problems about mind should be addressed with
  neuroscientific tools, not a priori armchair reasoning.

KEY PREDICTIONS:
  1. Connectionist (neural network) models of cognition
     may not preserve propositional attitude structure.
     (Vindicated partially: modern AI represents knowledge
      in distributed vector embeddings, not discrete propositions.)

  2. Consciousness and intentionality will be explained by
     neural processes without residue.

  3. Introspection is unreliable: we don't have privileged access
     to our own mental states. Introspective reports are theories
     about our states, not direct readings.

MODERN RELEVANCE:
  LLM internal representations are not propositional.
  Beliefs in LLMs are not stored as "I believe P."
  They are distributed over billions of weights.
  This is consistent with the Churchland prediction.
  The folk psychological vocabulary ("the model believes X")
  is a useful shorthand applied to systems that don't
  have propositional attitude structure internally.
```

## Arguments Against Eliminativism

### The Success of Folk Psychology

```
THE PREDICTIVE SUCCESS OBJECTION
===================================

OBJECTION: Folk psychology works.
  It predicts behavior astonishingly well.
  I predict that you will reach for the coffee if you want coffee.
  This prediction succeeds across billions of people, every day.

  The best explanation of folk psychology's success is that
  it accurately tracks real psychological states.
  Successful theories are (approximately) true.
  THEREFORE: folk psychology is approximately true.
  THEREFORE: eliminativism is false.

CHURCHLAND'S RESPONSE:
  Folk psychology's success is limited.
  It works for normal, simple behavior.
  It fails for mental illness, developmental psychology,
  cognitive biases, perceptual illusions, sleep/dreaming,
  the neuroscience of memory, attention, learning.
  The batting average is lower than it seems because
  we notice successes and ignore failures.

DENNETT'S RESPONSE:
  Folk psychology's success is explained by the fact that
  REAL PATTERNS in behavior exist.
  These patterns are real enough that folk psychological
  vocabulary tracks them.
  But the patterns are not what folk psychology SAYS they are.
  Beliefs are real patterns, not discrete propositional states.
```

### The Self-Refutation Objection

```
SELF-REFUTATION OBJECTION
===========================

OBJECTION:
  Churchland BELIEVES that eliminative materialism is true.
  He DESIRES that others accept it.
  He has formed INTENTIONS to argue for it.
  He HOPES his arguments will be convincing.

  If eliminative materialism is true, beliefs don't exist.
  But Churchland's advocacy of eliminative materialism
  USES folk psychological concepts.
  "Churchland believes eliminativism" uses the belief concept.
  A theory that eliminates its own premises is self-refuting.

CHURCHLAND'S RESPONSE:
  This is a petitio principii -- it assumes folk psychological
  vocabulary cannot be replaced.
  Before we had kinetic theory of heat, no one could describe
  temperature without mentioning "phlogiston" concepts.
  But this doesn't mean thermal physics is self-refuting.
  Similarly: describing eliminativism in folk vocabulary
  doesn't mean folk vocabulary is irreplaceable.
  We would describe eliminativism differently once we have
  the replacement vocabulary.

  "I hold [whatever neural state corresponds to] eliminative materialism."
  The neural vocabulary doesn't yet exist, but that's an
  empirical limitation, not a logical refutation.

ASSESSMENT:
  Churchland's response is coherent.
  The self-refutation objection is weaker than it appears.
  But the demand for the replacement vocabulary is demanding:
  what IS the replacement vocabulary, and when will it arrive?
```

### The Ineliminability of Moral Discourse

```
MORAL DISCOURSE OBJECTION
===========================

OBJECTION: Even if folk psychology is replaceable descriptively,
it seems INELIMINABLE in moral contexts.

  "You believed you were doing the right thing" -- relevant to
  moral assessment.
  "She intended to harm him" -- relevant to criminal culpability.
  "He acted from desire for profit" -- relevant to ethical evaluation.

  If beliefs and desires don't exist:
  What is the basis for criminal law?
  What is the basis for moral responsibility?
  What is the basis for distinguishing voluntary from involuntary actions?

CHURCHLAND'S RESPONSE:
  The replacement vocabulary would serve these functions too.
  We don't need "belief" specifically -- we need whatever
  neural state plays the role that makes actions culpable vs. innocent.
  That neural state exists; we just don't have the right vocabulary yet.

  IMPLICATION: If eliminativism is true, criminal law and ethics
  need to be rebuilt on neuroscientific foundations.
  This is radical but not logically impossible.
```

## Dennett's Intentional Stance

```
DENNETT: "THE INTENTIONAL STANCE" (1987)
==========================================

POSITION: Middle path between folk psychology realism
and Churchland's eliminativism.

THE THREE STANCES:
  1. PHYSICAL STANCE:
     Describe a system in terms of its physical/chemical constituents.
     Predict its behavior from physical laws.
     Always correct in principle. Often useless in practice.

  2. DESIGN STANCE:
     Describe a system in terms of its design and purpose.
     "The thermostat is designed to keep room at 68 F."
     Works for designed artifacts. Fast and useful.

  3. INTENTIONAL STANCE:
     Describe a system as if it had beliefs, desires, and rationality.
     "The mouse wants to get to the cheese."
     "The chess program believes it needs to protect its queen."
     Works for complex systems with complex behavior.

THE KEY CLAIM:
  Adopting the intentional stance is not merely convenient shorthand.
  Beliefs and desires are REAL PATTERNS in behavior.
  They are real in the same way that the center of gravity is real
  (useful idealizations that correspond to something in nature).

  They are NOT:
  - Discrete propositional states stored in specific locations
  - Rich inner objects with phenomenal character

  DENNETT IS NOT A REALIST about folk psychological states:
  There is no one place in the brain where "the belief that P" lives.
  Beliefs are patterns across the system.

  DENNETT IS NOT AN ELIMINATIVIST:
  The patterns are real.
  "The chess program believes it needs to protect its queen" is TRUE
  -- as true as any real-pattern description can be.

THE HETEROPHENOMENOLOGY:
  Dennett's method for studying consciousness:
  Take subjects' verbal reports seriously as DATA.
  But don't assume they accurately describe inner states.
  Third-person methodology applied to first-person reports.
  Avoids both the Scylla of dismissing reports and
  the Charybdis of taking them at face value.
```

## AI and Folk Psychology

```
FOLK PSYCHOLOGY FOR AI SYSTEMS
================================

When we say "ChatGPT believes the capital of France is Paris":

  REALIST VIEW:
  LLMs have beliefs stored in their weights.
  The pattern "capital of France = Paris" is a belief.
  Distributed, but real.
  Folk psychology is vindicated for AI.

  ELIMINATIVIST VIEW:
  LLMs don't have beliefs. They have weighted connections.
  "Belief" is a folk concept that doesn't map onto
  transformer weight matrices.
  We should describe LLM behavior in neural/computational terms,
  not folk psychological terms.

  DENNETT'S VIEW (most natural for AI):
  The intentional stance is USEFUL for LLMs.
  Saying "GPT-4 believes X" predicts its outputs reliably.
  It's a useful stance.
  But: the "belief" is a real pattern in weight space, not a
  discrete propositional state.

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS FOR SENIOR ENGINEERING LEADERS WORKING WITH AI:
  When your team says "the model understands X" or "the model thinks Y":
  - This is intentional stance talk
  - It works for communication and product planning
  - It does NOT imply the model has propositional attitude states
  - It does NOT imply it is conscious or has interests
  - The underlying reality is weight matrices and attention patterns
  But: the intentional vocabulary is USEFUL and not wrong
  (for Dennett), or is STRICTLY FALSE but useful as shorthand
  (for eliminativists like Churchland).
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| When someone says... | The right framing is... |
|---|---|
| "The model believes X" | Intentional stance (Dennett): a real pattern in weight space that reliably predicts output. Useful and not strictly false. Does NOT imply a discrete propositional state stored anywhere, or that the model has interests in the belief being correct. |
| "The model understands this topic" | Warranted if the model's behavior across varied inputs is reliably appropriate; not warranted as a claim about semantic understanding (Chinese Room applies). Dennett: understanding is the intentional stance at high confidence. Churchland: the word "understands" may not carve nature at its joints. |
| "The model doesn't really believe anything — it's just statistics" | Eliminativist move. Possibly correct (Churchland), or possibly a misdescription that throws away real patterns (Dennett). "Just statistics" applied to human neurons would be equally dismissive. The interesting question is whether the computational structure constitutes beliefs in the relevant sense, not whether to use the word. |
| "I want to say the model thinks X without implying consciousness" | Dennett's intentional stance is designed exactly for this: adopt the stance, gain predictive power, make no phenomenal commitment. The stance is useful even if no propositional attitudes literally exist. |
| "We should eliminate talk of 'model beliefs' from our engineering discussions" | Churchland's position: if belief-talk doesn't map onto what the model actually does (distributed vector representations, not discrete propositions), the vocabulary misleads product decisions. But: Dennett's counter — the patterns are real enough that intentional vocabulary tracks them reliably, and replacing it with computational vocabulary everywhere would be impractical. |
| "The model has preferences / values" | More cautious than "beliefs": RLHF training shapes output distributions in ways that behave like preferences (consistent orderings over outputs). Whether these are "genuine" preferences depends on what preferences are — which is exactly the folk psychology debate. |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Eliminativism says mental states don't exist."**
Strictly: eliminativism says mental states as *folk psychology conceives them* — discrete propositional attitudes with semantic content — don't exist. It doesn't say there's "nothing going on" in the brain. It says the "going on" should be described in neural/computational terms, not in folk-psychological terms. The neural reality exists; the folk-psychological description is inaccurate.

**"Dennett is an eliminativist."**
Dennett is often read as eliminativist about phenomenal consciousness (qualia) specifically. He is NOT eliminativist about beliefs and desires in the way Churchland is. His intentional stance view is that beliefs and desires are real patterns, not fictional posits. He argues *against* Churchland's eliminativism for propositional attitudes.

**"Folk psychology is just commonsense, not a theory."**
Sellars' point is that commonsense psychological concepts are embedded in a network of law-like generalizations and inferential commitments that constitute a theory. "He wants X and believes Y is the way to get X, so he will do Y" is a law-like generalization with predictive content. This is what a theory does. The fact that it's "common sense" doesn't disqualify it from being theoretical.

**"If eliminativism is true, we can't hold anyone responsible."**
Churchland would say: responsibility would be reconstructed on neuroscientific grounds. We would speak of neural states that warrant treatment-style responses vs. states that warrant reactive attitudes. The vocabulary changes; the practice might survive in modified form. Whether moral discourse can survive the elimination of beliefs and desires is a genuine open question, not a quick reductio.

**"Neural networks (deep learning) vindicate eliminativism."**
Partly consistent with eliminativism: modern AI systems store knowledge in distributed weighted connections, not as discrete propositional databases. This is what the Churchlands predicted would happen with connectionist models. But: it doesn't prove human cognition lacks propositional structure — just that one way of implementing AI doesn't preserve it. And folk psychological vocabulary ("the model believes X") remains useful for describing AI behavior, consistent with Dennett's stance.
