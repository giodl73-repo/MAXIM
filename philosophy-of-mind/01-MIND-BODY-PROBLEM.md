## Descartes' Substance Dualism

### The Setup

```
DESCARTES' ARGUMENT (Meditations on First Philosophy, 1641)
============================================================

STARTING POINT: Methodological doubt.
  What can I know with certainty?
  I can doubt the external world.
  I can doubt my body.
  But can I doubt that I am thinking, right now, as I doubt?
  NO. Cogito ergo sum: "I think, therefore I am."

CONSEQUENCE:
  My existence as a thinking thing is certain.
  My body's existence can be doubted.
  Therefore: I (the thinking thing) am distinct from my body.
  Mind (res cogitans -- thinking substance) is distinct from
  body (res extensa -- extended substance).

  RES COGITANS:        RES EXTENSA:
  - Thinking           - Extended in space
  - Unextended         - Divisible
  - Indivisible        - Mechanical
  - Immaterial         - Material
  - The seat of the self - The body, the world
```

### The Interaction Problem

```
DESCARTES' PROBLEM: HOW DO THEY INTERACT?
==========================================

If mind is non-physical and body is physical:
  How does mind cause the arm to move?
  How does a pinprick cause mental pain?

  Physical causation: requires spatial contact, forces, mechanisms.
  Non-physical mind has no spatial location.
  Therefore: how?

DESCARTES' ANSWER: The pineal gland.
  A small gland at the center of the brain.
  Descartes thought it was the unique unpaired structure.
  (Wrong -- it's not; but this was 17th century anatomy.)
  The soul is "closely joined" to the pineal gland.
  Fluid in the brain's ventricles transmits soul's intentions to body.

WHY THIS FAILS:
  It just pushes the problem back.
  How does the soul interact with the pineal gland specifically?
  The problem of interaction is not solved by naming a location.
  Any proposed mechanism must eventually invoke
  a soul-to-matter causal contact, which is the original problem.

PRINCESS ELIZABETH'S CHALLENGE (1643):
  Descartes corresponded with Princess Elizabeth of Bohemia.
  She pressed him: how can an unextended substance exert force
  on a body, as all force requires contact and area?
  Descartes had no satisfactory answer.
  This remains the core problem.
```

## Alternatives to Cartesian Dualism

### Occasionalism (Malebranche)

```
OCCASIONALISM (Nicolas Malebranche, 1670s)
==========================================

Mind-body interaction doesn't actually occur.
God intervenes on every occasion:
- When your mind "wills" to move the arm,
  God causes the arm to move.
- When a pin pricks the body,
  God causes the mental sensation of pain.

Mind and body are causally isolated.
God mediates all apparent interactions.

ASSESSMENT: Theologically convenient; philosophically unsatisfying.
Requires a constant divine intervention for all apparent causation.
Not accepted outside specific theological contexts.
```

### Leibnizian Pre-Established Harmony

```
LEIBNIZ: PRE-ESTABLISHED HARMONY (1695)
=========================================

Monads are the fundamental units of reality.
Each monad is a perceiving thing (no two are exactly alike).
Monads don't interact causally -- they are "windowless."
But they run in perfect synchrony, pre-established by God.

Mind and body are like two clocks that were wound up together
to run in perfect synchrony, showing the same time, without
one affecting the other.

The apparent interaction between mind and body is not
real causation but synchronized parallelism.

ASSESSMENT: Pre-established harmony avoids the interaction problem
by eliminating genuine interaction.
Leads to epiphenomenalism concerns (mental events are causally inert).
The God requirement makes it theologically dependent.
```

### Spinoza's Monism / Dual-Aspect Theory

```
SPINOZA (Ethics, 1677): NEUTRAL MONISM
========================================

There is only ONE substance: God / Nature (Deus sive Natura).
Mind and body are two ATTRIBUTES of the one substance.
The same substance viewed under different aspects.

  One substance
  /            \
Thought        Extension
(mind)         (body)

Not two substances interacting.
Not one reducing to the other.
Two aspects of the same underlying reality.

MODERN DESCENDANT: Dual-aspect theory, neutral monism.
Remains a minority position in academic philosophy.
Russell's neutral monism (1921): the fundamental stuff is neither
mental nor physical; both mind and matter are constructed from it.
```

## Ryle's Category Mistake

```
GILBERT RYLE: "THE GHOST IN THE MACHINE" (Concept of Mind, 1949)
=================================================================

RYLE'S DIAGNOSIS: Descartes made a category mistake.
He treated mind as if it were a thing of the same kind as body,
just of a different (non-physical) type.

THE CATEGORY MISTAKE ANALOGY:
  A visitor to Oxford is shown all the colleges, libraries,
  playing fields, scientific departments.
  "But where is the University?" the visitor asks.
  The University is not another building alongside the others.
  The University IS the organized whole of the institutions shown.
  Asking "where is the University?" treats a relation or organization
  as if it were another entity in the series.

APPLIED TO MIND:
  We observe human behavior: speaking, reasoning, solving problems.
  Descartes says: "But where is the MIND that does all this?"
  Ryle: The mistake is treating "mind" as if it were a ghostly
  behind-the-scenes cause, parallel to but separate from all these activities.
  Mind is not a hidden substance -- it's the organization of capacities
  and dispositions that constitutes intelligent behavior.

  "The dogma of the Ghost in the Machine" -- Descartes' picture.

DISPOSITIONALISM:
  Mental states are dispositions to behave in certain ways.
  "Knows how to multiply" = disposition to produce correct products.
  Not a mental event happening in a Cartesian theater.

INFLUENCE:
  Ryle influenced behaviorism and later functionalism.
  He did NOT think mental states were identical to behaviors (that's
  logical behaviorism, which he also criticized).
  Mental concepts describe dispositions, not occurrences.
```

## Identity Theory

```
MIND-BRAIN IDENTITY THEORY (Place 1956, Smart 1959)
======================================================

THESIS: Mental states are IDENTICAL to brain states.
Not correlated with, not caused by, not emergent from --
literally identical.

Pain = C-fiber firing
  (where C-fibers are peripheral nociceptors -- simplified)

Thought about Paris = some complex neural activation pattern P

TYPES OF IDENTITY:
  TYPE IDENTITY: Every token of mental type M = some physical type N
  Example: every pain = C-fiber firing
  Strong claim: pain and C-fiber firing are the same physical property.

  TOKEN IDENTITY: Every token of any mental state = some physical token
  but different tokens of the same mental type may be different physical tokens.
  Less restrictive.

SENSATION LANGUAGE vs. BRAIN PROCESS LANGUAGE:
  Smart (1959): "When I say I have a sensation of X, I am reporting
  an event or process in my brain."
  Mental vocabulary and brain vocabulary are two ways of describing
  the same thing, just as "the morning star" and "the evening star"
  turned out to be two names for the same thing (Venus).
  An a posteriori identity, discovered through science.

ADVANTAGES:
  - Avoids interaction problem (mind = brain, no separate interaction)
  - Causal efficacy: mental events cause things because they ARE
    physical events
  - Scientifically tractable in principle
```

### The Multiple Realizability Objection

```
MULTIPLE REALIZABILITY OBJECTION (Putnam 1967)
================================================

If mental states are TYPE IDENTICAL to brain states:
  Pain = C-fiber firing (in humans)
  But: octopuses have pain but no C-fibers.
  Martians could have pain implemented in completely different
  physical structures.

  If pain = C-fiber firing in humans,
  then what octopuses and Martians have is NOT pain (by definition).

  But they clearly can have pain!

THEREFORE: Type identity theory is too restrictive.
Mental kinds cannot be mapped onto physical kinds type-by-type.
Mental states are MULTIPLY REALIZABLE in different substrates.

This is the founding argument of FUNCTIONALISM.
(See 02-FUNCTIONALISM.md for full treatment.)
```

## Davidson's Anomalous Monism

```
DONALD DAVIDSON: ANOMALOUS MONISM (1970)
==========================================

PROBLEM: Mental causation + type identity rejected + physicalism?

DAVIDSON'S SOLUTION:
  Token identity: each mental event = some physical event (token)
  But: no strict psychophysical laws (anomalous)
  Mental vocabulary is not type-reducible to physical vocabulary.

THREE PRINCIPLES DAVIDSON HOLDS:
  1. Causal interaction: mental and physical events causally interact.
  2. Nomological character of causality: causation requires strict laws.
  3. Anomalism of the mental: there are no strict mental/physical laws.

RESOLUTION:
  Mental events are physical (token identity) -- so principle 1 OK.
  Mental events ARE physical events that fall under physical laws.
  But: they cannot be described qua mental by physical laws.
  Mental descriptions pick out events differently than physical descriptions.

  It's like saying: "The President's decision caused the stock crash."
  The decision is a physical event (neural firing).
  The physical laws govern the physical event.
  But there's no law of the form "All Presidential decisions cause stock crashes."
  The mental description doesn't support strict laws.

CRITICISM (Kim):
  If mental properties are not type-identical to physical properties,
  then mental properties are "causally inert."
  The causal work is done by the physical properties.
  The mental description is just an overlay.
  This leads to epiphenomenalism about mental properties (not mental tokens).
```

## The Causal Exclusion Problem (Kim)

```
KIM'S CAUSAL EXCLUSION ARGUMENT (1989)
========================================

SETUP:
  P1 -- physical state at time t1
  M1 -- mental state at time t1 (supervenient on P1)
  P2 -- physical state at time t2
  M2 -- mental state at time t2 (supervenient on P2)

    M1 ----causes?----> M2
     ^                   ^
     |                   |
     | (supervenience)   | (supervenience)
     |                   |
    P1 ----causes------> P2
           (physical causation, complete)

CAUSAL EXCLUSION:
  If physical causation (P1 --> P2) is causally sufficient for P2,
  and M1 is supervenient on P1 (not identical),
  then what causal work does M1 do?
  P2 is already fully caused by P1.
  M1's contribution to M2 is "excluded" by P1's contribution to P2.

  If M1 =/= P1 (not identical), M1's causal contribution is redundant.
  Either: M1 is causally inert (epiphenomenalism).
  Or: There is overdetermination (both P1 and M1 cause P2) -- implausible.
  Or: M1 = P1 (identity theory) -- which Putnam's argument contests.

KIM'S CONCLUSION:
  Non-reductive physicalism (holds mental =/= physical but supervenes)
  cannot explain how mental properties are causally effective.
  Either identity theory (mental = physical), or mental states are epiphenomenal.

THE STANDARD RESPONSE:
  Functionalists: mental properties just ARE certain functional/causal
  roles, so they have genuine causal power via those roles.
  Kim: the causal power comes from physical implementation, not from
  the functional description.
  This debate is ongoing.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Position | Core claim | Key problem |
|---|---|---|
| Substance dualism (Descartes) | Mind and matter are distinct substances | Interaction problem |
| Occasionalism (Malebranche) | God mediates all mind-body events | Requires constant divine intervention |
| Pre-established harmony (Leibniz) | Mind and body synchronized, no interaction | Eliminates genuine causation |
| Dual-aspect theory (Spinoza) | Mind and matter are aspects of one substance | How does "one substance" explain divergence? |
| Category mistake view (Ryle) | "Mind" is a misdescription of behavior/disposition | Doesn't explain phenomenal experience |
| Type identity theory (Place/Smart) | Pain = C-fiber firing | Multiple realizability objection |
| Token identity (Davidson) | Each mental event = some physical event | Kim's causal exclusion problem |
| Functionalism (Putnam) | Mental states defined by functional role | Chinese Room, absent qualia |
| Property dualism (Chalmers) | Mental properties are non-physical | Hard problem still present |
| Eliminative materialism (Churchland) | Mental states don't exist | Self-refutation worry |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Descartes was obviously wrong, we can move on."**
Substance dualism is almost universally rejected. But the *motivation* for dualism — the apparent irreducibility of subjective experience to physical description — is not resolved by rejecting substance dualism. Property dualism (Chalmers) is a contemporary, defended position with the same motivation but without the interaction problem. The core intuition that generated Cartesian dualism is not obviously wrong.

**"The mind-brain identity theory was proven by neuroscience."**
Neuroscience demonstrates correlations between mental states and brain states. Correlation is not identity. The identity claim is a philosophical thesis that requires interpretation of neuroscientific data. And Putnam's multiple realizability argument remains a serious objection to *type* identity theory specifically.

**"Multiple realizability means computers can think."**
Multiple realizability says that *if* silicon can instantiate the right functional states, it has the right mental states. It doesn't say silicon *does* instantiate those states. The argument is about the criteria for mental states, not about whether any particular system meets those criteria. Whether LLMs instantiate the relevant functional organization is a separate question.

**"Davidson's anomalous monism is just functionalism in disguise."**
They are related but distinct. Functionalism defines mental states by causal/functional roles. Davidson's anomalous monism says mental events are physical tokens but mental descriptions don't reduce to physical descriptions. Davidson doesn't commit to functionalism's characterization of what makes a mental state what it is — he just says each mental token is some physical token.

**"Kim proved non-reductive physicalism is false."**
Kim argued that non-reductive physicalism has a serious problem (causal exclusion). Most philosophers find this problem significant. There are responses (many kinds of causation, network causation, multiple realization at the property level), but none are universally accepted. The problem is serious enough to have reshaped the debate, but "proved false" overstates it.
