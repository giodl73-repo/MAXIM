## The Positions in Detail

### Hard Determinism

```
HARD DETERMINISM
=================

THESIS: Every event, including every human action and decision,
is causally necessitated by prior events + laws of nature.
Free will (in any sense requiring anything other than determinism)
is impossible.
Moral responsibility is therefore a fiction.

SUPPORT FROM PHYSICS:
  Classical mechanics: complete state at t0 + laws determines all future states.
  (Laplace's demon: infinite intelligence knowing all positions and
   momenta could compute all future events.)

  QUANTUM CHALLENGE:
  Quantum indeterminacy makes strict classical determinism false.
  Does this help? Hard determinists say no:
  Random quantum events don't give you control.
  If my decision to raise my hand is caused by a random quantum
  fluctuation in my neurons, that's not free will -- it's randomness.
  Freedom requires control, not mere randomness.

MANIPULATION ARGUMENT SUPPORT:
  Imagine a neuroscientist who directly manipulates your brain
  to produce a decision. You acted -- but not freely.
  The hard determinist notes: the laws of nature + prior causes
  stand to your decisions exactly as the neuroscientist does.
  Both determine your decision without your control.

  [Note: this is actually used differently by different philosophers --
  some use it against compatibilism, others to motivate it]
```

### Compatibilism

```
COMPATIBILISM
=============

THESIS: Free will is compatible with determinism.
"Free will" properly understood does NOT require the ability
to have done otherwise in the libertarian sense.

What compatibilism REDEFINES:
  Free will = acting in accordance with your own desires,
              reasons, values -- not compelled by external force
              or internal compulsion.

  The opposite of free will is not determinism.
  The opposite of free will is COMPULSION (being coerced,
  having a phobia, being hypnotized, being literally forced).

  A determined act CAN be free: "He freely chose to donate."
  = His action was caused by his own values and reasoning,
    not by external compulsion.
  = He would have responded to contrary reasons.

HUME: Actions are free if caused by the agent's desires and character.
KANT (compatibilist reading): Rational autonomy = acting on universal laws
      you give yourself -- this is compatible with natural causation.
MILL: Free action = action caused by the person's motives, not coercion.
```

### The Reasons-Responsive Account (Fischer)

```
FISCHER'S SEMI-COMPATIBILISM
==============================

JOHN MARTIN FISCHER (1994, 2006): The most developed contemporary
compatibilism.

TWO MECHANISMS:
  1. REASONS-RESPONSIVE MECHANISM:
     A mechanism that responds to reasons for and against actions.
     The agent would have acted differently if given sufficient
     reasons to act differently.

  2. REASONS-RECEPTIVE MECHANISM:
     More demanding: the mechanism appropriately responds to the full
     range of reasons (including moral reasons).

MORAL RESPONSIBILITY:
  Requires: guidance control (acting via reasons-responsive mechanism).
  Accountability: requires reasons-receptive mechanism.

The mechanisms must be "mesh" -- the agent's own mechanisms,
not hijacked, not the result of global manipulation.

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR ALTERNATE POSSIBILITIES:
  Fischer + Ravizza: moral responsibility does NOT require the ability
  to have done otherwise.
  Requires only that the action flowed from the agent's own
  reasons-responsive mechanism.
```

## Frankfurt Cases

```
FRANKFURT CASES (Harry Frankfurt 1969)
========================================

PURPOSE: Attack the Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP).
PAP: A person is morally responsible for an action only if
     they could have done otherwise.

FRANKFURT'S COUNTEREXAMPLE:
  Set up:
  - Black wants Jones to vote Democrat.
  - Black has implanted a device in Jones's brain.
  - The device monitors Jones's decision-making.
  - IF Jones is about to decide to vote Republican, Black activates
    the device and forces Jones to vote Democrat.
  - IF Jones decides on his own to vote Democrat, Black does nothing.

  WHAT HAPPENS:
  Jones, on his own, decides to vote Democrat.
  Black does nothing (no need to intervene).
  Jones votes Democrat entirely on his own.

  CAN JONES HAVE DONE OTHERWISE? NO.
  If he had started to decide differently, Black would have intervened.
  He could NOT have done otherwise (there was no alternate possibility).

  IS JONES MORALLY RESPONSIBLE FOR VOTING DEMOCRAT? YES.
  He acted entirely on his own. Black never intervened.
  His action was caused by his own deliberation.

  THEREFORE: PAP is false.
  Moral responsibility does NOT require the ability to have done otherwise.

COMPATIBILIST TAKEAWAY:
  What matters for responsibility is how the action was caused,
  not whether alternatives existed.
  If caused by the agent's own reasons-responsive mechanism
  (not compulsion, not hypnosis, not direct manipulation):
  responsible.
```

## Libertarianism About Free Will

```
FREE WILL LIBERTARIANISM (not political libertarianism)
========================================================

THESIS: Free will is real and requires genuine indeterminism
in the decision-making process.

WHY INDETERMINISM?
  If determinism is true, all my actions are fixed by prior causes.
  If prior causes trace back to before my existence:
  I am not the "ultimate originator" of my actions.
  I am just a node in a causal chain that began long before me.
  This undermines responsibility.

  Libertarianism requires: some actions originate with the AGENT
  in a way not fully determined by prior causes.

CHALLENGES:
  1. HOW does the agent "originate" an action?
     If actions are partially random (quantum), that's not control.
     If actions are neither determined nor random -- what is the
     third option?
     "Agent causation" (Chisholm): agents cause actions directly,
     as a special kind of causation not reducible to event causation.
     Problem: agent causation is metaphysically obscure.

  2. Why does quantum indeterminism help?
     Randomness in neurotransmitter release ≠ free will.
     Freedom requires CONTROL. Randomness is not control.
     Adding random noise to a deterministic system doesn't
     give the system control over the noise.
```

## Strawson's Reactive Attitudes

```
P.F. STRAWSON: "FREEDOM AND RESENTMENT" (1962)
================================================

STRAWSON'S MOVE: Sidestep the metaphysics.

The practice of holding people responsible is constitutive of
human relationships, not derived from metaphysical facts about
determinism or indeterminism.

REACTIVE ATTITUDES:
  Gratitude: "Thank you for helping me."
  Resentment: "You deliberately hurt me, I am angry."
  Indignation: "You wronged someone, I am outraged."
  Love, hurt, anger, appreciation.

  These are responses to the WILL of other persons as expressed
  in their actions. They are not just causal-historical tracking.

EXEMPTIONS FROM REACTIVE ATTITUDES:
  1. Excuses: "He stepped on my foot but didn't intend to."
     We suspend resentment -- the action didn't manifest
     ill-will or indifference.

  2. Exemptions: "She has a severe mental illness."
     We adopt an OBJECTIVE STANCE -- viewing the person as an
     object of treatment, not as a full member of the moral community.
     Not because we think differently about physics,
     but because we adjust the PRACTICE of holding responsible.

STRAWSON'S CONCLUSION:
  The question "but does determinism really permit resentment?"
  is beside the point.
  The practice of reactive attitudes is too fundamental to human
  relationships to be overturned by a physics argument.
  Even a convinced hard determinist continues to love, thank, and
  resent people in practice.
  The practice is prior to the philosophical theory.

  Free will and responsibility are not metaphysical discoveries
  but practices constitutive of human communal life.
```

## Neuroscientific Challenges

```
LIBET EXPERIMENTS (1983, 1985)
================================

SETUP:
  Subject told: move your wrist whenever you feel like it.
  EEG measures brain activity.
  Subject reports when they consciously "decided" to move
  (using a clock with a rotating dot; report the dot's position
  when you first felt the urge to move).

  RESULTS:
  Readiness Potential (RP) begins: ~550 ms before movement
  Conscious "decision" (W): ~200 ms before movement
  Movement: 0 ms

  BRAIN ACTIVITY PRECEDES CONSCIOUS AWARENESS BY ~350 ms.

  INITIAL INTERPRETATION:
  The brain "decides" before you consciously decide.
  Therefore: conscious decisions are not the cause of actions.
  Conscious will is an epiphenomenal afterthought.

CRITIQUES:
  1. W measurement is unreliable:
     Asking subjects to report clock position when they "notice"
     an urge conflates multiple things (when did the urge begin?
     when did attention shift to the clock? etc.)

  2. Schurger (2012) reanalysis:
     The RP may not be a specific "decision signal."
     It may reflect background fluctuations in motor preparation.
     A stochastic accumulator model: movement occurs when
     random neural noise crosses a threshold.
     The RP is the noise approaching threshold -- not the decision.

  3. "Veto" power:
     Libet himself noted: subjects could "veto" the movement
     in the 200ms between W and movement.
     If conscious will can veto, it has some causal role.
     Hard to square with pure epiphenomenalism.

  4. Scope:
     Simple wrist flick is not a good model of deliberate decision-making.
     We rarely deliberate about wrist flicks.
     "Deciding when to move your wrist" may not generalize to
     "deciding whether to quit your job."

VERDICT:
  The Libet experiments do not demonstrate that free will is illusory.
  They are interesting findings about neural timing.
  They do not resolve the philosophical question.
```

## AI and Free Will

```
FREE WILL AND AI SYSTEMS
=========================

DETERMINISTIC AI:
  Most neural networks are deterministic given fixed weights + seed.
  Sampling with temperature adds stochasticity.
  Does an LLM "decide" anything?

  Under hard determinism: no human decides either.
  Under compatibilism: "decision" means something about the
  causal structure, not indeterminism.
  An LLM acts on inputs + weights. Are these its "own desires"?

  This maps to Frankfurt's concept:
  Was the action caused by the system's own reasons-responsive mechanism?
  For an LLM: it's unclear whether there's a self-owned mechanism
  vs. an imposed one (training).
  The manipulation argument pressure returns:
  If a corporation trained your beliefs and values into you,
  are they "your own"?

MORAL RESPONSIBILITY FOR AI SYSTEMS:
  Current practice: attribute responsibility to developers and deployers,
  not to AI systems themselves.
  This is appropriate given: AI systems are tools, no legal personhood,
  no independent interests in the relevant sense.

  Future: if AI systems acquire something like preferences, values,
  self-conception -- the free will debate becomes practically urgent.
  What causal structure would make an AI responsible?
  Compatibilist answer: acting via its own reasons-responsive mechanism.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Quantum mechanics proves free will."**
Quantum indeterminacy introduces random events at the microscale. Random events are not controlled events. Free will requires the agent to be the source and controller of their decisions. Randomness doesn't provide this. Libertarians who appeal to quantum mechanics need to explain how randomness gives rise to self-determined control, which is the unresolved challenge.

**"Compatibilism is just giving up on real free will."**
Compatibilists say their concept of free will is the only coherent one. The libertarian notion of "could have done otherwise in the same physical circumstances" is, for compatibilists, incoherent — there's no clear content to it. Whether compatibilism "gives up" or "clarifies" free will depends on which concept you think is the right one. This is the core dispute.

**"The Libet experiments prove we have no free will."**
They show that neural activity precedes the reported moment of conscious decision. This is interesting. It does not show that conscious deliberation never influences action, that all decisions work this way, or that the philosophical free will question is settled. The Schurger reanalysis casts doubt on the specific interpretation. Neuroscience cannot settle the philosophical question.

**"Frankfurt cases are contrived and irrelevant."**
They are contrived — that's the point of thought experiments. The question is whether they reveal something about the concept of moral responsibility. If an intuitive principle (PAP) can be shown to fail in a clearly worked-out case, the principle is suspect. The intuition that Jones is responsible (even though he couldn't have done otherwise) either has to be rejected or the principle has to be revised.

**"Compatibilism was Kant's view."**
Kant held a complex position. He believed in transcendental freedom (the noumenal self is free in a sense not subject to natural causation) and empirical determinism (the phenomenal self is subject to natural laws). This is sometimes called Kantian compatibilism, but it's closer to a two-levels view (noumenal/phenomenal) than standard compatibilism. Contemporary compatibilists mostly work within a naturalistic framework without noumenal selves.
