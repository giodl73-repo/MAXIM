## Domain Map

### Domain 1 — Tree Biology (Guides 01–02)

What a tree *is* at the cellular and tissue level.

```
TREE CROSS-SECTION (schematic, not to scale):

         BARK (periderm + phloem)
      +----------------------------------+
      |  OUTER BARK (periderm/cork)      |  <-- dead tissue; moisture barrier
      |  INNER BARK (phloem)             |  <-- sugars move DOWN from leaves
      +--+----------------------------+--+
         |   VASCULAR CAMBIUM         |     <-- meristematic ring; 1 cell thick
         |   (one cell layer thick)   |     <-- adds xylem inward, phloem outward
      +--+----------------------------+--+
      |  SAPWOOD (functional xylem)      |  <-- water + mineral transport upward
      |  (outermost N growth rings)      |  <-- living ray parenchyma still active
      |----------------------------------|
      |  HEARTWOOD (non-functional)      |  <-- dead xylem; extractives deposited
      |  (inner xylem)                   |  <-- denser, often darker, rot-resistant
      +----------------------------------+
              |
          PITH (dead parenchyma, structural center — first year's growth)
```

Key cellular structures:
- **Tracheids** (gymnosperms): long, tapered, imperforate cells — water transport and structural support combined in one cell type
- **Vessel elements** (angiosperms): short, wide, perforated end walls — form vessels (tubes) for rapid water transport; separate fibers handle mechanical load
- **Ray cells**: radial parenchyma oriented perpendicular to grain — lateral transport of nutrients and water storage
- **Resin canals**: epithelial-lined channels in conifers — constitutive and inducible defense

### Domain 2 — Wood Science (Guide 02)

Anisotropic material with three principal axes:

```
  LONGITUDINAL (L)  -- along fiber axis       -- strongest in tension/compression
  RADIAL (R)        -- pith-to-bark direction  -- intermediate
  TANGENTIAL (T)    -- tangent to growth rings -- widest shrinkage, flattest-sawn face

  Shrinkage order:  L << R < T     (L barely moves; T is ~2× R)
  Strength order:   L >> R >= T
  Modulus order:    EL >> ER > ET
```

### Domain 3 — Dendrochronology (Guide 03)

Trees as climate archives:

```
  EARLYWOOD (wide, thin-walled cells)  <-- formed during rapid spring growth
  LATEWOOD (narrow, thick-walled cells) <-- formed during summer stress / season end

  One RING = one year (in temperate, seasonal climates — not universal)
  CROSS-DATING = pattern-matching ring-width sequences across specimens
  MASTER CHRONOLOGY = composite record extending thousands of years
  CALIBRATION CURVE (IntCal) = bridges radiocarbon dates to calendar years
```

### Domain 4 — Forest Ecology (Guide 04)

Stand-level and landscape-level dynamics:

```
  CANOPY STRATIFICATION (temperate deciduous):
  Canopy layer      15–35 m   -- dominant/codominant crowns intercept 80–90% light
  Sub-canopy        5–15 m    -- shade-tolerant species; suppressed trees
  Shrub layer       1–5 m
  Herb/ground       0–1 m     -- ferns, forbs; ephemeral spring bloomers

  SUCCESSION SEQUENCE:
  Disturbance --> Pioneer (intolerant) --> Mid-seral --> Late-seral
  Gleasonian: no deterministic climax; species respond individualistically to gradients
```

### Domain 5 — Silviculture (Guide 07)

Intentional manipulation of forest stand structure:

```
  EVEN-AGED:           UNEVEN-AGED:
  Clearcut             Single-tree selection
  Seed tree            Group selection (0.1–2 ha openings)
  Shelterwood          Continuous cover forestry

  COPPICE: cut to stump; regrowth from root sprouts (oaks, hazel, chestnut)
  POLLARD: cut above browse height, repeated (willow, beech; traditional European)
```

### Domain 6 — Forest Economics and Conservation (Guides 08–09)

```
  TIMBER:       stumpage price (standing tree) -> log price -> lumber price
  CARBON:       VCS protocols, REDD+, voluntary carbon markets
  CONSERVATION: old-growth metrics, fragmentation, deforestation rates, rewilding
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Hardwood" and "softwood" are anatomical, not mechanical designations.**
Balsa (*Ochroma pyramidale*) is a hardwood (angiosperm, has vessels). Yew (*Taxus*) is a softwood (gymnosperm, tracheid-only) yet is mechanically harder than most hardwoods. These terms describe reproductive/anatomical category, not density or Janka hardness.

**Growth rings are not universally annual.**
In tropical climates lacking strong seasonal temperature or rainfall contrast, some species produce multiple rings per year or produce indistinct rings. Dendrochronology requires confirmed annual ring formation — calibrated by "pointer years" and supported by anatomical analysis.

**Heartwood is not decay.**
Heartwood formation is a programmed developmental transition: the tree actively deposits extractive compounds (tannins, phenolics, silica, oils, resin acids) into dying xylem cells. It is metabolically deliberate, not passive rot.

**Sapwood width is not a reliable age indicator across species.**
Some species maintain only 2–3 rings of sapwood (black locust); others keep 50+ (maples). The boundary is physiologically regulated, not simply time-based.

**Mycorrhizal networks do not function like a communications system.**
Carbon and nutrient transfer through fungal mycelium is documented biochemically but ecologically contested in terms of net benefit direction and magnitude. The "wood wide web" metaphor overstates intentionality and ignores that fungi are metabolic agents with their own fitness interests.
