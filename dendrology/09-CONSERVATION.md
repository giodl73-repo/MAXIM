# Forest Conservation: Old-Growth, Deforestation, Reforestation

## The Big Picture

Forest conservation spans three interconnected challenges: defining and protecting what remains of natural forest (old-growth metrics), understanding and reversing deforestation, and rebuilding forest cover through reforestation and rewilding. The third leg — rewilding — has shifted from theoretical to practical with measurable ecological outcomes in the last 20 years.

## Section 1 — Defining Old-Growth Forest

"Old-growth" is not simply "old trees" — it is a suite of structural characteristics that arise only after centuries of ecological development without stand-replacing disturbance.

### Structural Definition (North American standard)

```
  OLD-GROWTH STRUCTURAL ATTRIBUTES:

  1. LARGE LIVE TREES
     Presence of trees > 400 yr age (temperate) or > 200 yr (boreal)
     Multiple canopy layers; large diameter variance in stand
     DBH distribution extends > 60–80 cm for dominant canopy species

  2. LARGE SNAGS (standing dead trees)
     > 50 cm DBH, > 7 m height; decay class 2–4
     Critical: nest sites for cavity-nesters (woodpeckers, owls, martens)
     Density: > 2–4 snags/ha of large size

  3. LARGE DOWNED LOGS (coarse woody debris, CWD)
     Logs > 60 cm diameter in advanced decay
     Volume: > 50–100 m³/ha CWD
     Function: salamander habitat, fungal diversity, nurse log regeneration

  4. CANOPY COMPLEXITY
     Multiple canopy layers (vertical heterogeneity)
     Canopy gaps of various sizes (gap mosaic)
     Mixed crown architecture; uneven-aged structure

  5. TREE SPECIES COMPOSITION
     Late-successional species dominant (shade-tolerant)
     Often multispecies; not monocultures

  6. FOREST FLOOR COMPLEXITY
     Pit-and-mound topography from tip-up mounds of windthrown trees
     Deep, complex litter layer with multiple decomposition stages
```

### Old-Growth Metrics by Region

| Region | Primary Species | Old-Growth Age Threshold | Key Structural Indicators |
|--------|----------------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Pacific NW, USA | Douglas-fir, Sitka spruce, western red cedar | > 200 yr; often 400–800 yr | Snags > 50 cm, CWD > 75 m³/ha |
| Eastern USA | Beech, sugar maple, hemlock | > 200 yr (hemlock 300+) | Multi-layer; lichen diversity |
| Boreal | Black spruce, balsam fir, jack pine | > 80–150 yr | Snag density; CWD; lichen |
| European temperate | Beech, pedunculate oak | > 250 yr | Hollow trees; saproxylic beetle diversity |
| Tropical Amazon | Multi-species hardwood | > 400 yr (no typical ring count) | Emergent density; epiphyte load |

### Global Old-Growth Extent

```
  GLOBAL OLD-GROWTH / PRIMARY FOREST:
  ~1.11 billion ha remain (FAO 2020 estimate)
  ~27% of remaining forest is old-growth / primary
  Concentrated in: Russia (boreal), Canada (boreal + temperate)
                   Amazon basin, Congo basin, SE Asia

  LOSS RATE: ~3.3–4.9 M ha/yr of primary forest (Hansen et al.)
  Includes conversion to agriculture, degradation, fire

  US OLD-GROWTH (lower 48 states):
  ~5% of original old-growth forest remains
  Most in national parks, wilderness areas, some national forests
  Pacific NW: ~10–20% of original Douglas-fir old-growth intact
  Eastern USA: < 1% of original eastern old-growth remains
```

## Section 2 — Fragmentation Effects

Habitat fragmentation creates isolated forest patches in an inhospitable matrix. Area alone does not predict ecological function — edge effects, connectivity, and minimum patch size are critical.

```
FRAGMENTATION EFFECTS:

  ORIGINAL FOREST             FRAGMENTED LANDSCAPE
  +--------------------+      +-+--+----+-+--+--+-+
  |                    |      | |  |    | |  |  | |
  |   Intact interior  |  --> +-+  +----+ +--+  +-+
  |   No edge effects  |      Matrix (agriculture,
  |                    |      roads, development)
  +--------------------+      Small isolated patches

  EDGE EFFECTS:
  +-- Increased wind, temperature, VPD at forest edge
  +-- Invasive species penetrate from matrix
  +-- Nest predation increases at edges (generalist predators)
  +-- Interior-dependent species lose habitat even as total area is retained

  MINIMUM VIABLE PATCH SIZE (examples):
  Spotted owl (Strix occidentalis): ~1,000–1,200 ha territory
  Marbled murrelet: old-growth forest within 15 km of ocean
  Wild boar: continuous corridor > 2 km wide
  Many insects: patches > 100 ha required for viable populations
```

### Edge Effect Penetration Depth

| Effect | Penetration Depth from Edge |
|--------|----------------------------|
| Wind penetration (blowdown risk) | 100–500 m |
| Air temperature/humidity change | 30–100 m |
| Nest predation increase | 300–600 m |
| Invasive plant colonization | 200–400 m |
| "Interior habitat" definition | > 600 m from edge (conservative) |

A 200-ha square patch (~450 m side) has essentially **no interior habitat** if interior is defined as > 300 m from edge.

## Section 3 — Deforestation: Rates, Drivers, Hotspots

### Global Deforestation Data

```
  GROSS FOREST LOSS (Global Forest Watch, Hansen et al., 2001–2022):
  2001–2010: ~7.4 M ha/yr tropical tree cover loss
  2015–2022: ~10–15 M ha/yr tropical tree cover loss

  IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:
  Tree cover loss ≠ deforestation
  Plantation harvest = tree cover loss but is NOT deforestation
  ~50% of global tree cover loss is from plantation harvest (especially SE Asia)
  ~25–30% is genuine tropical primary forest deforestation

  PRIMARY DRIVERS BY REGION (Pendrill et al., Nature Communications 2022):
  Amazon Brazil:    67% cattle pasture, 17% soy, 5% other crops
  SE Asia:          40% oil palm, 30% other ag, 15% pulpwood
  Congo DRC:        75% small-scale subsistence agriculture
  Global tropics:   Cattle/soy (S. America), oil palm (SE Asia),
                    smallholder ag (Africa/SE Asia)
```

### Frontier Deforestation Dynamics

```
  DEFORESTATION FRONTIER PROCESS:
  1. Road construction (logging road opens access)
  2. Selective logging (high-value timber extracted)
  3. Slash-and-burn by following settlers
  4. Cattle ranching (extensive; low productivity)
  5. Soy/palm conversion of degraded land
  6. Frontier advances further into remaining forest

  FEEDBACK LOOP (Amazon "dieback" concern):
  Deforestation --> reduced evapotranspiration --> reduced rainfall
  --> forest stress and mortality --> more deforestation
  Tipping point analysis (Lovejoy & Nobre 2018): ~20–25% Amazon deforestation
  may trigger self-reinforcing transition to savanna
  Current level: ~17–20% Amazon already deforested
```

## Section 4 — Reforestation vs. Afforestation

These terms are distinct and the distinction matters for carbon accounting and ecological outcomes.

```
  REFORESTATION:                     AFFORESTATION:
  Replanting forest on land that      Establishing forest on land that
  was PREVIOUSLY FORESTED but         has NOT been forested in RECENT
  recently cleared/disturbed          historical time (50–100 yr threshold)

  +-- Restores original habitat type  +-- Creates new forest
  +-- Appropriate seed sources        +-- May introduce non-native species
  +-- Pre-existing soil seed bank     +-- Different soil, hydrology
  +-- Typically higher ecological     +-- Can drain peatlands, wetlands
      value                               (convert good carbon sinks to
  +-- Shorter recovery time               sources) if sited incorrectly
  Examples: replanting after fire,    Examples: tree planting on moorland,
  clearcut; river corridor restoration  grasslands, peatlands, savannas
```

### Cautionary Note on Large-Scale Afforestation

```
  POTENTIAL NEGATIVE OUTCOMES OF AFFORESTATION:
  +-- Peatland afforestation: peat oxidation releases more carbon
      than trees will ever sequester; catastrophic carbon mistake
      (UK example: afforestation of flow country, Scotland)
  +-- Natural grassland conversion: savannas and grasslands
      are ancient ecosystems with high endemic biodiversity;
      planting trees destroys them
  +-- Alien monocultures: eucalyptus in non-native range drains
      groundwater, outcompetes native flora
  +-- Wrong species for location: fails or impedes natural regen.

  RULE: Restore where forest BELONGS; do not plant trees to
        "do something" on non-forest land for political credit
```

## Section 5 — Rewilding Evidence

Rewilding is the passive or active return of ecological function to degraded landscapes. Active rewilding includes reintroduction of keystone species; passive is simply removing human pressure.

### Passive Rewilding: Natural Forest Return

```
  SUCCESSION RATE:
  Abandoned agricultural land (temperate Europe/N. America):
  Year  0–5:  Annual weeds, early herbaceous perennials
  Year  5–20: Shrubs, pioneer trees (birch, aspen, pin cherry)
  Year 20–50: Young canopy establishing; shade-tolerant understory
  Year 50–100: Forest structure; mid-seral species dominant
  Year 100+:  Late-seral approaching; CWD accumulating
  Year 300+:  Old-growth structural attributes beginning

  CARBON SEQUESTRATION RATE (regrowing temperate forest):
  ~1.5–3.0 Mg C/ha/yr for first 50 yr
  ~0.5–1.5 Mg C/ha/yr thereafter as forest matures
  Old-growth continues to accumulate carbon at 0.3–0.5 Mg C/ha/yr
```

### Trophic Cascade: The Yellowstone Wolf Experiment

The reintroduction of gray wolves (*Canis lupus*) to Yellowstone National Park (1995) is the most studied example of top-down trophic cascade effects.

```
  WOLVES REINTRODUCED (1995)
         |
         v
  ELK BEHAVIOR CHANGED            ELK NUMBERS REDUCED
  (avoid riparian zones)          (direct predation)
         |                               |
         v                               v
  RIPARIAN VEGETATION             GRAZING PRESSURE REDUCED
  RECOVERED (willows, aspen,      on vegetation broadly
  cottonwood; elk previously
  overbrowsed to bare soil)
         |
         v
  BEAVER RECOLONIZATION           SONG BIRD DIVERSITY INCREASED
  (dam building returned)         (riparian vegetation restored)
         |
         v
  STREAM MORPHOLOGY CHANGED       SONGBIRD / RAPTOR BREEDING
  (beaver ponds slow water,       habitat expanded
   reduce erosion, raise water
   table -- "geomorphic cascade")

  KEY FINDING: Loss of apex predator caused GEOMORPHIC changes to
               river channels; restoration reversed them within 15 yr
```

### Rewilding Evidence — Selected Case Studies

| Project | Location | Species | Outcome |
|---------|----------|---------|---------|
| Wolf reintroduction | Yellowstone NP, USA | Gray wolf | Trophic cascade; riparian recovery; geomorphic |
| Lynx reintroduction | Various European countries | Eurasian lynx | Roe deer reduction; forest understory recovery (partial) |
| Sea otter recovery | Pacific NW coast | Sea otter | Kelp forest recovery (otter controls sea urchins) |
| Wild boar return | European forests | Sus scrofa | Soil disturbance; acorn dispersal; biodiversity benefits |
| Beaver reintroduction | UK rivers | Eurasian beaver | Dam building; wetland restoration; flood attenuation |
| Rewilding of Knepp Estate | UK (farmland) | Multiple ungulates, predators absent | Spontaneous biodiversity recovery; nightingales, purple emperor butterflies |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Reforestation" in policy pledges often means plantation, not forest restoration.**
Major government and corporate "billion tree" pledges frequently fulfill targets with fast-growing monoculture plantations (eucalyptus, teak, pine) that have limited biodiversity value and may even be carbon-negative if replacing native savanna or grassland.

**Protected area designation is not protection.**
At least 30–40% of global protected areas lack effective management enforcement (Geldmann et al. 2019). "Paper parks" exist on maps but have deforestation rates similar to unprotected areas in countries with weak governance.

**Old-growth is not the only conservation-valuable forest.**
Secondary forests (20–80 yr old) are recovering significant biodiversity and carbon stock. Landscape-scale conservation requires a matrix of old-growth, late-seral, and recovering secondary forest — not just isolated old-growth reserves.

**Fragmentation effects operate at larger scales than intuitive.**
A patch that looks "large" on a map (5,000 ha) may function as a series of small patches if bisected by roads. The functional connectivity, not just total area, determines ecological viability for area-sensitive species.

**The Amazon tipping point is a scientific hypothesis with uncertainty ranges, not a confirmed cliff-edge.**
Lovejoy and Nobre's 2018 estimate of 20–25% deforestation as a threshold for self-reinforcing savannization is derived from modeling with substantial uncertainty. The current ~17–20% deforestation puts us in the uncertainty range — which is exactly why precautionary action is warranted, not because the cliff edge is precisely known.
