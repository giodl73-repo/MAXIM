# Tree Anatomy: Vascular Cambium, Sapwood, Heartwood, Bark

## The Big Picture

A tree is built from four concentric tissue systems produced by two meristematic zones. Understanding which cells do what — and where they come from — is the foundation of all wood science and tree physiology.

```
TREE CROSS-SECTION — FULL TISSUE MAP
(transverse view, temperate hardwood)

  Outermost
  |
  |  [1] RHYTIDOME (outer bark)
  |      Layers of dead periderm (cork) + compressed dead phloem
  |      Moisture barrier; insulation; pest/fire resistance
  |
  |  [2] INNER BARK (functional phloem)
  |      Sieve tubes + companion cells: sugar transport downward
  |      Phloem parenchyma stores starch; short-lived (1–3 yr active)
  |
  |  [3] VASCULAR CAMBIUM  <--- PRIMARY BOUNDARY
  |      One-cell-thick meristematic cylinder
  |      Fusiform initials --> xylem (inward) + phloem (outward)
  |      Ray initials --> wood rays (radial parenchyma)
  |
  |  [4] SAPWOOD (functional xylem: outermost N rings)
  |      Water + mineral transport (xylem vessels or tracheids)
  |      Living ray parenchyma: starch storage, heartwood precursors
  |      Xylem fibers: mechanical support
  |
  |  [5] HEARTWOOD  <--- TRANSITION ZONE
  |      Dead xylem saturated with extractive compounds
  |      Tylose formation in vessels (blocks conductivity deliberately)
  |      Color from phenolics, tannins, oils, silica
  |
  |  [6] PITH
  |      Dead parenchyma at geometric center
  |      First-year's primary growth; often hollow in older stems
  |
  Innermost
```

## Section 1 — The Vascular Cambium

The cambium is the primary engine of secondary growth (girth increase). It is a single meristematic cylinder producing new cells on both its inner and outer faces.

```
CAMBIAL ACTIVITY:

  PHLOEM SIDE                    XYLEM SIDE
  (outward additions)            (inward additions)
  +------------------+           +------------------+
  | sieve tube       |           | vessel element   |
  | companion cell   |<-CAMBIUM->| tracheid         |
  | phloem fiber     |           | wood fiber       |
  | phloem parenchyma|           | ray parenchyma   |
  +------------------+           +------------------+

  Ratio: ~4–10 xylem cells added for every 1 phloem cell
  Xylem accumulates; phloem is continuously shed outward as bark
```

### Cambial Cell Types

| Cambial Initial | Products | Orientation |
|-----------------|----------|-------------|
| **Fusiform initial** | Axially-oriented xylem and phloem cells | Vertical axis |
| **Ray initial** | Ray parenchyma cells (xylem and phloem rays) | Radial axis |

Fusiform initials are long, tapering cells. In conifers they are very long (2–8 mm); in angiosperms shorter (0.2–2 mm). This translates directly into fiber length, which determines paper-making quality.

### Seasonal Cambial Activity

```
  SPRING:   Auxin wave from expanding buds activates cambium
            --> wide-lumen, thin-walled cells (earlywood)
            --> low-density wood, light color

  SUMMER:   Water stress, decreasing day length
            --> narrow-lumen, thick-walled cells (latewood)
            --> high-density wood, dark color

  AUTUMN:   Cambium goes dormant (cold/dry signal)

  WINTER:   Quiescent; cells compressed between previous year's
            latewood and next year's future earlywood

  One earlywood/latewood pair = one annual ring
  (temperate, seasonal climates; not universal tropically)
```

## Section 2 — Xylem: Tracheid vs. Vessel Element

The most important anatomical difference between gymnosperm (softwood) and angiosperm (hardwood) wood lies in xylem organization.

```
GYMNOSPERM (Softwood) XYLEM:        ANGIOSPERM (Hardwood) XYLEM:

  TRACHEID only                       VESSEL ELEMENT + FIBER + PARENCHYMA

  [Tracheid]                          [Vessel element]  [Fiber]
  +---------+                         +-----------+     +------+
  |         |   Long, tapered cell    |           |     |  //  |
  |    //   |   both conducts water   | open end  |     |  //  |
  |    //   |   AND provides support  |  (perforation)|  |  //  |
  |    //   |                         |           |     |  //  |
  +---------+                         +-----------+     +------+
  ~1–8 mm long                        Short (0.1–1.2mm) ~1–2 mm long
  Pit pairs connect                   Stack into vessels thick-walled
  adjacent cells                      (can be meters long) no lumen

  Water crosses via                   Water flows through  Mechanical
  bordered pits                       open perforations    support only
```

### Bordered Pits (Conifer Mechanism)

Conifers have no vessel perforation plates. Water moves between tracheids through **bordered pits** — circular, valve-like structures with a torus (thickened central disc) that can aspirate (seal) under drought stress, preventing embolism propagation. This makes conifer wood resistant to runaway hydraulic failure.

### Vessel Types in Angiosperms

```
RING-POROUS woods (oak, ash, elm, hickory):
  Large earlywood vessels visible to naked eye as ring at ring boundary
  Efficient rapid transport when active; high vulnerability to freezing embolism
  --> Produce new vessels EVERY spring from scratch

DIFFUSE-POROUS woods (maple, beech, birch, cherry, poplar):
  Vessels uniformly distributed; no visible ring to naked eye
  More embolism-resistant; vessels may remain functional 2+ years
  --> Better suited to cold climates where late-season freezing is common
```

| Feature | Ring-porous | Diffuse-porous |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Vessel diameter (earlywood) | 200–500 µm | 20–100 µm |
| Vessel functionality | Single growing season | 1–3 growing seasons |
| Freeze-thaw vulnerability | High | Moderate |
| Examples | Oak, ash, elm, hickory | Maple, birch, beech, poplar |

## Section 3 — Ray Cells

Rays are sheets of parenchyma running radially from the cambium inward (through sapwood and into heartwood) and outward (through phloem). They are the radial axis of the tree.

```
RADIAL VIEW (cross-cut tangential section):

  RAY (horizontal plate of parenchyma cells)
  +-------------------------------------------------------+
  |  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  o  |  <-- uniseriate ray (1 cell wide)
  +-------------------------------------------------------+
  or
  +===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+  <-- multiseriate ray (oak: up to 30+ cells wide)
  +===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+===+
```

### Ray Functions

| Function | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| Lateral water transport | Aquaporins in ray cell walls move water radially |
| Carbohydrate storage | Starch granules in ray parenchyma (winter reserves) |
| Heartwood formation | Ray cells secrete extractive precursors inward |
| Wound response | Ray parenchyma differentiates into wound wood / traumatic resin canals |
| Defense signaling | Rapid oxidative burst through ray network |

**Decorative value:** In oak (*Quercus*), the very large multiseriate rays appear as "silver grain" or "ray fleck" on quartersawn lumber — the most visually distinctive feature of English/American white/red oak and a major determinant of panel value.

## Section 4 — Bark: Phloem + Periderm

Bark is not a single tissue. It is a compound structure:

```
FROM CAMBIUM OUTWARD:

  CAMBIUM
     |
     v
  [Inner bark / Functional phloem]
  Sieve tubes (sugar transport) -- alive, functional
  Companion cells (metabolic support for sieve tubes)
  Phloem parenchyma (storage)
  Phloem fibers (mechanical support)
     |
     v  (phloem pushed outward, crushed by new additions)
  [Phelloderm] -- living parenchyma inside cork
  [Phellogen / Cork Cambium] -- produces cork outward, phelloderm inward
  [Phellem / Cork] -- dead, suberized cells; gas-impermeable
     |
     v
  [Rhytidome] -- all dead tissue outside the innermost functional periderm
  In furrowed bark (oak, pine): layers of periderms + dead phloem stacked
  In smooth bark (beech, birch): thin; single or few periderms
```

### Bark Adaptations

| Bark Type | Examples | Adaptive Function |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Thick, corky | Cork oak (*Quercus suber*), giant sequoia | Fire insulation; harvested every 9 yr |
| Smooth, thin | Beech (*Fagus*), birch (*Betula*) | Photosynthetically active (chlorophyll in phloem); heat re-radiation |
| Papery, exfoliating | River birch, sycamore | Lichen/parasite exclusion; water shedding |
| Resinous | Pines, spruces | Pitch tubes seal wounds; insect/fungal defense |
| Armored, furrowed | Douglas-fir, ponderosa pine | Deep furrows insulate against surface fire |

## Section 5 — Resin Canals (Conifers)

Resin canals are epithelium-lined channels running both longitudinally (in wood and bark) and radially (through wood rays). They contain oleoresin — a mixture of volatile monoterpenes and nonvolatile diterpene acids.

```
LONGITUDINAL CANAL (vertical section):

  Epithelial cells                Central lumen
  +---+---+---+---+              +===============+
  | E | E | E | E |              |  RESIN        |
  +---+---+---+---+  <-- cells   |  (terpenes +  |
  | E |               secrete   |   resin acids)|
  +---+               oleoresin  +===============+
  | E |              into lumen
```

**Constitutive canals**: Always present (*Pinus*, *Picea*, *Larix*).
**Traumatic resin canals**: Formed in response to wounding, insects, fungal attack. Often in tangential rows, visible macroscopically in spruce attacked by bark beetles.

## Section 6 — Meristematic Zones: Primary vs. Secondary Growth

```
PRIMARY GROWTH:                    SECONDARY GROWTH:
(apical meristems)                 (lateral meristems)

  Shoot apical meristem (SAM)        Vascular cambium
  --> elongation of stem/branches    --> increases stem DIAMETER
  --> nodes, internodes              --> adds wood (xylem) and bark (phloem)

  Root apical meristem (RAM)         Cork cambium (phellogen)
  --> root elongation                --> produces bark periderm
  --> root cap protection            --> replaces epidermis in woody stems

  PRIMARY BODY:         SECONDARY BODY:
  Epidermis            Bark (periderm + phloem)
  Cortex               Wood (secondary xylem)
  Endodermis           Both produced annually in seasonal climates
  Pith
  Primary vascular
  bundles
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What produces new wood every year? | Vascular cambium (one cell thick) |
| Why is conifer wood structurally simpler? | Tracheids do double duty (water + support); no fibers or vessels needed |
| What distinguishes ring-porous from diffuse-porous? | Vessel size distribution relative to ring boundary |
| Why does beech have prominent ray flecks? | Large multiseriate rays; visible on quartersawn surfaces |
| What causes pitch pockets in pine lumber? | Resin canals (traumatic) enclosed in harvested wood |
| Why does oak resist rot without treatment? | Heartwood tannins, tyloses blocking vessels, silica deposits |
| What is the "silver grain" on quartersawn oak? | Ray cells oriented radially; flat face of large ray sheets |
| Where does the tree "know" to stop making sapwood? | Ray cells signal extractive deposition; controlled, not passive |

## Common Confusion Points

**The cambium does not "grow" — it divides.**
The cambium is a single-cell-thick cylinder. It does not enlarge; it divides tangentially, pushing new cells inward (becoming xylem) and outward (becoming phloem). The cambium itself stays the same thickness throughout the tree's life.

**Bark thickness is not cambium thickness.**
The cambium is microscopically thin. The bark you see — potentially centimeters thick — is dead periderm (rhytidome) accumulated over decades, plus the thin inner functional phloem.

**Sapwood depth is species-specific, not age-specific.**
Black locust may have 2–3 rings of sapwood; silver maple may have 20–30. The width of the sapwood zone is regulated by the tree's hydraulic demand and species genetics, not simply by the tree's age.

**Heartwood color does not predict hardness or density.**
Osage orange heartwood is bright orange-yellow and extremely dense. Poplar heartwood is gray-green and light. Color reflects chemistry (extractives) not mechanical properties.

**Tyloses are not disease.**
Tyloses are bubble-like ingrowths from adjacent parenchyma cells into vessel lumens, deliberately blocking them during heartwood formation. In white oak they make the wood impermeable (hence barrel-making use). They are a feature, not a defect.
