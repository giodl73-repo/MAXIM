# Silviculture: Plantation, Selection Cutting, Clear-Cut, Coppice

## The Big Picture

Silviculture is the deliberate manipulation of forest composition, structure, and growth to achieve defined objectives. Every silvicultural system makes a fundamental choice: how much canopy to remove, when, and what regeneration source to rely on.

```
SILVICULTURAL SYSTEMS — DECISION SPACE

  REGENERATION      STAND           CUTTING
  METHOD            STRUCTURE       SYSTEM
  +-------------+   +----------+   +------------------+
  | Natural seed |   | Even-aged|   | Clearcut          |
  | (from above) |   | one cohort|  | Seed tree         |
  +-------------+   +----------+   | Shelterwood       |
                                   +------------------+
  +-------------+   +----------+   +------------------+
  | Advance      |   |Uneven-aged|  | Single-tree sel.  |
  | regeneration |   | multiple  |  | Group selection   |
  | (established |   | cohorts   |  +------------------+
  | before cut)  |   +----------+
  +-------------+
                                   +------------------+
  +-------------+                  | Coppice/Pollard   |
  | Vegetative   |                 | Conversion        |
  | sprouts      |                 | Strip cutting     |
  +-------------+                  +------------------+

  KEY AXIS: Light-demanding (pioneer) species --> even-aged clearcut
            Shade-tolerant species --> uneven-aged selection
```

## Section 1 — Even-Aged Systems

Even-aged systems create a stand of trees all the same approximate age by removing the existing overstory completely or nearly so. They are favored for light-demanding species that cannot regenerate under an existing canopy.

### Clearcut

```
  CLEARCUT:
  Before:  Mature stand (70–100 yr)
  |||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||  (canopy trees)

  After harvest:
  _____________________________________________  (bare site)
  Seeds fall from: adjacent stand edges, residual seed bank, direct seeding

  REGENERATION: depends on wind-dispersed species (pine, birch, aspen),
                retained root systems (aspen sprouting from roots),
                direct seeding, or planting

  PROS: Cheapest to execute; most efficient for pioneer species;
        maximizes light for shade-intolerant seedlings;
        complete site preparation possible
  CONS: Erosion risk; aesthetically disruptive; loss of habitat complexity;
        site must have adequate seed source or require replanting;
        difficult to justify ecologically in slope/stream contexts

  REGULATORY: Most regulated harvesting method globally;
              often restricted near streams, slopes > 30%, sensitive soils
```

### Seed Tree

```
  SEED TREE:
  Before: Mature stand
  ||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||

  After: Clear most stand; retain 2–10 "seed trees" per hectare
         |          |          |          (3–5% of original stand)
  _______|__________|__________|________

  Seed trees: selected for seed production, wind-firmness, and genetics
  After regeneration established: seed trees often harvested

  PROS: Natural regeneration from local seed; cheap
  CONS: Limited genetic diversity from few trees; windthrow risk to seed trees;
        irregular regeneration if seed trees die or fail to seed
  BEST FOR: Pines (large seed crops, wind-dispersed)
```

### Shelterwood

The most biologically sophisticated even-aged system. Removes the overstory in two or three cuts over 5–20 years, allowing gradual light increase that favors advance regeneration.

```
  SHELTERWOOD SEQUENCE:
  YEAR 0: Preparatory cut — thin overstory to ~60% of original BA;
          encourage advance regen; improve wind-firmness
  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  (60% retained)

  YEAR 5–10: Establishment cut — remove to ~30–40% canopy;
             advance regeneration now growing; seedlings establishing
     |    |    |    |    (30% retained)

  YEAR 10–20: Removal cut — harvest remaining overstory;
              regeneration now 10–20+ yr old and self-sufficient
  _____________________________________ (new even-aged stand)
  ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~ (young cohort)

  PROS: Best light management for shade-tolerant and intermediate species;
        protects soil and regeneration during establishment;
        aesthetically gradual; multiple income harvests
  CONS: Complex; requires multiple entries; more logging roads;
        residual stand damage from repeated logging operations
  BEST FOR: Douglas-fir, oak, ash, beech, yellow birch
```

## Section 2 — Uneven-Aged Systems

Uneven-aged management maintains three or more distinct age/size classes simultaneously. No site-level clearcut ever occurs. The "forest" is always there.

### Single-Tree Selection

```
  SINGLE-TREE SELECTION:
  Before (cross-section of stand):
  Big|  med  |  small  | large|  med |  small|  Big
  ---|-----  | ------- | -----|  --- | ------|  ---

  After selection cutting:
  Remove trees individually based on:
  - Physiological maturity (target diameter reached)
  - Poor form, disease, suppression
  - "Cull" trees blocking regeneration
  - Maintaining target diameter distribution

  TARGET DIAMETER DISTRIBUTION (q-factor / Liocourt's Law):
  +
  | ***
  |     ***
  Count|        ***
  |            ***
  |                ***
  +-----+---+---+---+---> DBH class
  Small  Med  Large  Large  V. large

  Reverse J-curve: many small, fewer large trees
  q = ratio of trees in successive DBH classes (typically 1.2–1.5)

  PROS: Continuous cover; aesthetically continuous forest;
        perpetual shade-tolerant species dominance;
        good for water quality protection, wildlife habitat
  CONS: Complex marking; expensive per unit volume; high residual damage;
        NOT suitable for light-demanding species (too shady for pioneers)
  BEST FOR: Sugar maple, beech, hemlock, spruce-fir
```

### Group Selection

```
  GROUP SELECTION:
  Creates gaps of 0.1–2.0 ha by removing groups of trees
  Intermediate between clearcut and single-tree selection

  Gap size determines species favored:
  < 0.1 ha (200 m²)  --> shade-tolerant species only (advance regen)
  0.1–0.5 ha         --> intermediate species (oak, ash)
  0.5–2.0 ha         --> light-demanding species at gap center

  PROS: Biodiversity benefits from gap size spectrum;
        intermediate species can regenerate;
        continuous canopy matrix maintained
  CONS: More complex to manage; requires careful gap placement
```

## Section 3 — Coppice and Pollard

Vegetative regeneration systems that exploit the ability of certain broadleaved species to resprout vigorously from cut stumps or main stems.

```
  COPPICE:                              POLLARD:
  Cut to near-ground level (stool)      Cut at 2–4 m height on main stem

  +---+                                  +---+
  |   |  Sprouts from root collar        |   |  Sprouts from pollard head
  +---+  and cambium                     |   |  (above browse height)
  STOOL                                  |   |
  |   |                                  |   |
  ROOTS (extensive; old)                 |   |
                                         ROOT

  Regrowth is FASTER than seed-grown     Used where livestock/deer
  trees because of established root      grazing would destroy
  system                                 coppice regrowth

  SPECIES: Hazel, sweet chestnut,        SPECIES: Willows (willow pollard),
           oak, lime, alder              beech (traditional English),
           ash (charcoal production)     ash, hornbeam

  ROTATION: 7–25 yr for coppice;        ROTATION: 5–20 yr for willow;
            traditional charcoal = 15 yr  30–50 yr for beech pollard
```

### Coppice-with-Standards

A multi-story variation: retain selected trees (standards) as irregular overstory while coppicing the understory. The standards provide timber at longer rotations (60–120 yr) while coppice yields regular small-diameter material. Traditional English woodland management from medieval period.

```
  COPPICE WITH STANDARDS:
  Year  0: Clear coppice; retain 40–80 standards/ha
  Year  7: Harvest coppice; retain standards
  Year 14: Harvest coppice; retain + recruit standards
  Year 70: Standards reach timber size; harvest; recruit new standards

  Product:  Coppice = fencing, charcoal, basket material, fuel
            Standards = construction timber, shipbuilding timber
```

## Section 4 — Rotation Age and Economic Optimization

### Biological Rotation vs. Economic Rotation

```
  BIOLOGICAL ROTATION: Age at which stand Mean Annual Increment (MAI) peaks
  MAI = total volume / age at harvest

  GROWTH CURVE:
  Volume
    ^            /-------\   <-- Current Annual Increment (CAI) peaks early
    |           /         \
    |          /           \___
    |         /                \___
    |    ___/                       \___
    |___/
    +------+----------+--------+---------> Age
    Pioneer   CAI peak  MAI peak   Senescence
              (20-30yr) (50-80yr)   (100-200yr+)

  MAI maximum = BIOLOGICALLY optimal rotation
  (maximizes long-run average yield per unit time)

  CAI = MAI when MAI is at maximum
  (rule: harvest when current growth = average past growth)
```

### Economic Rotation (Faustmann Formula)

Economically optimal rotation accounts for land opportunity cost. It is typically **shorter** than biological rotation because money tied up in standing timber has opportunity cost.

```
  FAUSTMANN ROTATION:

  LEV (Land Expectation Value) = NPV of all future rotations on the site

  LEV = (R - C) × e^(-ir) / (1 - e^(-ir))

  Where:
    R = revenue at harvest (stumpage × volume)
    C = costs (planting, management, taxes)
    i = discount rate
    r = rotation length (years)

  Optimal rotation: maximize LEV with respect to r
  --> Shorter rotation than MAI maximum when:
      - discount rate is high
      - timber prices are expected to fall
      - land has high alternative use value

  EXAMPLE: Douglas-fir coastal Pacific NW
  Biological rotation (MAI max): ~60–80 yr
  Economic rotation at 5% discount rate: ~40–50 yr
  Economic rotation at 8% discount rate: ~30–35 yr
```

## Section 5 — Plantation Forestry

Plantations are intensively managed monocultures (or simple mixtures) of single species, typically from selected genetic material, grown on short rotations.

```
  PLANTATION CHARACTERISTICS:
  +----------------------------------------------+
  | Species: typically 1–3 species per stand      |
  | Origin: seedlings from seed orchards (bred    |
  |         for growth rate, form, disease resist.)|
  | Spacing: regular, designed for harvest machine|
  | Thinning: 1–3 commercial thinnings scheduled  |
  | Rotation: short (eucalyptus: 7 yr; pine: 20 yr;|
  |           hardwood: 25–40 yr)                 |
  | Yield: 2–10× natural forest (site-prepared,   |
  |         fertilized, weed-controlled)          |
  +----------------------------------------------+

  GLOBAL PLANTATION AREA: ~294 M ha (~7% of global forest area)
  Supplies ~40% of world's industrial timber from 7% of forest area

  KEY PLANTATION SPECIES:
  Softwood: Pinus radiata (NZ, AU, Chile); Pinus taeda/elliottii (SE USA);
            Picea sitchensis (UK); Larix (Japan); Eucalyptus (rotation 7 yr, fiber)
  Hardwood: Tectona grandis (teak plantations); Populus (hybrid poplar, 10–15 yr)
            Eucalyptus (fiber; pulp; 7–12 yr rotation)

  YIELD COMPARISON:
  Natural forest (temperate): 2–5 m³/ha/yr
  Managed natural forest:    5–10 m³/ha/yr
  Plantation (pine):        10–20 m³/ha/yr
  Plantation (eucalyptus):  20–50 m³/ha/yr in tropics
```

## Section 6 — Certification: FSC and PEFC

Forest certification provides third-party verification that timber comes from responsibly managed forests.

```
  FSC (Forest Stewardship Council):
  +-- International non-governmental; founded 1993
  +-- 10 Principles + 70 Criteria covering:
      social equity, indigenous rights, workers rights,
      ecological function, monitoring, legal compliance
  +-- Chain of Custody (CoC) certification tracks wood through supply chain
  +-- ~220 M ha certified globally (2024)
  +-- Accredited certifiers: SmartWood, SGS, Bureau Veritas, etc.
  +-- Considered more stringent (environmental NGO support)

  PEFC (Programme for Endorsement of Forest Certification):
  +-- Umbrella organization endorsing national standards
  +-- Founded 1999; endorsed national schemes from >50 countries
  +-- ~340 M ha certified (2024) -- largest area globally
  +-- Considered industry-friendly; less stringent than FSC per some analyses
  +-- Strong in Europe (SFI North America is PEFC-endorsed)

  COMPARISON:
  +------------------+----------+----------+
  | Feature          | FSC      | PEFC     |
  +------------------+----------+----------+
  | Founded          | 1993     | 1999     |
  | Area certified   | 220 M ha | 340 M ha |
  | Governance       | Multi-   | National |
  |                  | stakehold| schemes  |
  | GMO timber       | Banned   | Allowed  |
  | Controversial    | Various  | Less     |
  | chemicals        | banned   | restricted|
  | Acceptance       | NGOs     | Industry |
  +------------------+----------+----------+
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | System |
|-----------|--------|
| Pioneer species (pine, aspen, birch) on flat terrain | Clearcut + replanting or seed source |
| Douglas-fir, mixed shade-tolerant + intolerant | Shelterwood (3 entries over 15–20 yr) |
| Sugar maple, beech, hemlock — maintain forest structure | Single-tree selection |
| Broadleaved species for regular fuel/material supply | Coppice on 15–25 yr rotation |
| High-altitude watershed; visual sensitivity | Uneven-aged / group selection; no clearcut |
| Maximum timber volume production (plantation) | Monoculture + thinning schedule; Faustmann rotation |
| Certification requirement for export market | FSC Chain of Custody required for most EU markets |

## Common Confusion Points

**Selection cutting is not high-grading.**
High-grading removes the best trees, leaving the worst (the opposite of what selection silviculture prescribes). Properly executed single-tree selection removes trees based on a diameter distribution target and forest health criteria — not just the highest-value stems. High-grading is the historic abuse, not the system.

**Clearcut is not inherently non-sustainable.**
In appropriate ecological contexts (boreal forest with fire ecology, lodgepole pine, aspen) clearcut mimics stand-replacing fire and is a valid regeneration method. The problem is application in inappropriate contexts: steep slopes, sensitive soils, riparian zones, shade-tolerant species requiring advance regeneration.

**Rotation age optimized for volume is not optimized for carbon sequestration.**
Carbon storage in standing timber increases indefinitely (old-growth stores far more carbon than plantation). The Faustmann rotation optimizes economic return, not ecological function. Carbon credit protocols may justify longer rotations by monetizing the additional carbon storage.

**Certification does not guarantee legality of origin everywhere.**
FSC or PEFC certification applies only to certified operations. Laundering of uncertified or illegal timber into certified supply chains has been documented. Due diligence on supply chain origin (EUDR — EU Deforestation Regulation, 2023) is increasingly required for European market access.
