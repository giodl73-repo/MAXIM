# Tropical Tree Diversity: Rainforest Structure, Emergents, Epiphytes

## The Big Picture

Tropical rainforests cover ~7% of Earth's land surface but contain ~50% of terrestrial species. Their architecture is vertical — multiple strata from ground to emergent crowns — and their diversity is extreme: a single hectare of Amazonian forest may contain 300+ tree species, compared to ~30 in a temperate forest hectare. The drivers of this diversity and the structural features that sustain it are the focus of this guide.

```
TROPICAL RAINFOREST — VERTICAL STRUCTURE

  Height
  50m+  +-[EMERGENT LAYER]------+   Giant isolated crowns above main canopy
        | Exposed; wind-pollinated|   Dipterocarps, Brazil nut, Emergent figs
        | or animal-dispersed    |   Crown fire does not spread to this height
        | seeds; thermally       |   Rain initiates mass synchronized flowering
        | extreme conditions     |

        | Closed or near-closed  |   Main interception layer; temperature

        | for birds + epiphytes  |

        | Deep shade <5% full sun|   Adapted: large leaves, horizontal spread
```

## Section 1 — Structural Features of Tropical Trees

### Buttress Roots

Buttress roots are plank-like lateral extensions from the trunk base, radiating outward across the soil surface. They are ecologically diagnostic of tropical lowland forest.

```
BUTTRESS ROOT CROSS-SECTION (plan view):

            N
            |
       ===/ | \===    Plank-like flanges
      /     |     \   extend outward and
     =      TRUNK   =  downward from trunk
      \     |     /   base; often 3–8 m tall
       ===\ | /===
            |
            S

  HEIGHT: typically 3–10 m; record: ~9 m (some Ficus)
  WIDTH: planks 10–30 cm thick
  EXTENT: radially 2–5 m from trunk
```

**Why buttress roots exist:**
1. **Mechanical stability hypothesis**: Shallow soils (oxisols, ultisols) provide poor anchorage for deep tap roots. Buttresses spread the load over a wider area, increasing stability against wind throw in large-crowned emergents.
2. **Nutrient acquisition hypothesis**: Buttresses extend root surface area into the nutrient-rich O/A horizon where litter decomposes, effectively enlarging the foraging area for P and K.
3. Both hypotheses have supporting evidence; likely both are true.

### Cauliflory

Cauliflory = flowers and fruits borne directly on the trunk or major branches, rather than on young twig tips.

```
  TWIG-FLOWERING (most temperate trees):     CAULIFLOROUS (many tropical trees):
  Flowers at branch tips, shaded by leaves   Flowers on trunk/main branch directly

  Why cauliflory in tropics?
  --> Large-bodied frugivores (tapirs, peccaries, agoutis, large bats)
      cannot reach twig tips but can access trunk
  --> Large heavy fruits (cacao, breadfruit, jackfruit) too heavy for twigs
  --> Bat pollination: trunk flowers are more accessible in flight corridor

  EXAMPLES: Cacao (Theobroma cacao), jackfruit (Artocarpus),
            Ficus (some), durian, some Leguminosae
```

## Section 2 — Epiphytes

Epiphytes are plants that grow on other plants for structural support but derive no water or nutrients from the host (not parasites). In tropical rainforests they contribute 25–35% of total vascular plant diversity and add 1–5 tonnes of biomass per hectare.

```
EPIPHYTE LOAD ON TROPICAL TREE:

  Crown (top to bottom):

    Bromeliad tanks (Tillandsia, Vriesea):
      Water-storing leaf rosettes; accumulate water and debris.
      Miniature aquatic ecosystems (frogs, insects, bacteria).

    Orchids:
      ~20,000 epiphytic sp. globally; dominant in mid-canopy.

    Ferns (Asplenium, Platycerium, including bird's-nest ferns):
      Accumulate detritus (humus pockets).

    Mosses/Liverworts (Bryophytes):
      Dominant in high-humidity, high-altitude cloud forest.

  Trunk:
    Typically bare of epiphytes in lowland;
    colonized in montane/cloud forest (permanently wet).
```

**Ecophysiology of epiphytes:**
- Water acquisition: bromeliad tanks, CAM photosynthesis, velamen (spongy root tissue in orchids absorbs water vapor and rain)
- Nutrient acquisition: from rainwater throughfall, canopy leachate, fog, decomposing debris in humus pockets
- No soil access — must be entirely self-sufficient for water and nutrient between rain events

## Section 3 — Drivers of Tropical Tree Diversity

### The Janzen-Connell Hypothesis

Proposed independently by Daniel Janzen (1970) and Joseph Connell (1971): seeds and seedlings directly beneath a parent tree experience higher mortality from specialized seed predators and pathogens than those that disperse away from the parent.

```
  JANZEN-CONNELL EFFECT:

  SEED SHADOW               MORTALITY FUNCTION         NET RECRUITMENT

  Most seeds fall           Highest mortality near      Peak recruitment
  near parent tree          parent (specialized         AWAY from parent
  (negative exponential     seed predators,
  dispersal curve)          host-specific pathogens)

  Seed density              Survival = dispersal        Creates "safe sites"
     ^                      probability × inverse       at intermediate
     |   *                  mortality probability       distances
     |    **
     |      ***                                         --> favors DIFFERENT
     +---------->           --> prevents monocultures   species establishing
       distance                                         near each parent
```

**Implication:** Because each species is most suppressed near its own conspecifics, rare species have a competitive advantage over common species. This frequency-dependence stabilizes high diversity — negative feedback prevents any single species from dominating.

## Section 4 — Dipterocarp Dominance (SE Asia)

The Dipterocarpaceae family dominates the canopy of Southeast Asian lowland rainforests in a way without parallel elsewhere. In Borneo and Sumatra, dipterocarps may constitute 50–80% of canopy trees by basal area.

```
  DIPTEROCARP CHARACTERISTICS:
  +-----------------------------------------+
  | Family: Dipterocarpaceae                |
  | Genera: Shorea, Dipterocarpus,          |
  |          Dryobalanops, Hopea, Vatica    |
  | Height: up to 70+ m (emergents)         |
  | Bark: often furrowed; resin-producing   |
  | Fruit: 2–5 winged "helicopter" seeds    |
  |        (1-2 petals enlarged as wings)   |
  | Dispersal: wind-assisted autorotation   |
  +-----------------------------------------+

  MAST SEEDING (supra-annual synchrony):
  Dipterocarps do NOT flower every year.
  Synchronized flowering across all dipterocarp species in a region
  occurs every 2–10 years, triggered by:
    --> El Niño / ENSO drought events (thermal stress cue)
    --> Temperature anomaly: ~1°C drop following warm period

  ECOLOGICAL EFFECT:
  +-- Massive simultaneous seed production overwhelms all seed predators
  +-- Most seeds escape predation (predator satiation strategy)
  +-- Massive synchronized regeneration cohort after mast event
  +-- Intervals between masting starve specialist seed predators
       (population crashes between mast events)
```

## Section 6 — Carbon and Conservation Context

```
  TROPICAL FOREST CARBON:

  Above-ground biomass:   ~120–180 Mg C/ha (Amazon, intact)
  Below-ground biomass:   ~25–35 Mg C/ha (roots)
  Soil organic carbon:    ~100–150 Mg C/ha (0–30 cm depth)

  Deforestation releases approximately:
  Amazon clearcut:        ~200–250 Mg CO2/ha immediate
  Peat-swamp forest (SE Asia): up to 1,500–3,000 Mg CO2/ha
  (peat oxidation can continue for decades)

  DEFORESTATION RATES (2015–2020, FAO 2022 data):
  Brazil:               ~1.5 M ha/yr gross tropical forest loss
  Democratic Rep. Congo: ~0.9 M ha/yr
  Indonesia:            ~0.5 M ha/yr (reduced from ~1 M ha/yr, 2001–2012)
  Bolivia:              ~0.3 M ha/yr
  Global tropical:      ~8–10 M ha/yr gross loss
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why do large tropical trees have buttresses but temperate trees rarely do? | Shallow tropical soils + large crown area; load-spreading and nutrient foraging |
| Why does dipterocarp masting occur irregularly, not annually? | ENSO-triggered; predator satiation requires long intervals to reset predator populations |
| What makes teak the benchmark outdoor wood? | Oil content + silica = Class 1 durability; dimensional stability; silica damages tools |
| Why is Janzen-Connell important for conservation? | Diverse forests need dispersal distances; fragmentation isolates trees from dispersers, collapses the mechanism |
| Why is balsa classified as a hardwood? | It is an angiosperm (has vessels) = hardwood by definition; density/hardness are separate properties |
| Why are tropical ring-porous species rare? | Strong seasonal signal needed for annual rings; few tropical species have strong enough seasonality |

## Common Confusion Points

**CITES and tropical timber are not equivalent.**
Not all tropical timber is CITES-protected. Teak, ipe, and iroko are widely traded. *Dalbergia* (rosewoods) are broadly listed under CITES II (permit required) or I (commercial trade banned). Checking CITES Appendix status before specifying tropical species is essential.

**"Mahogany" is a marketing term, not a botanical one.**
True Honduran mahogany (*Swietenia macrophylla*) is CITES II listed. What is sold as "mahogany" may be *Khaya* (African mahogany), *Entandrophragma* (sapele), *Toona* (Australian cedar), or various other meliaceous species. Properties differ.

**Rainforest soils are not rich — the forest is.**
Tropical oxisols and ultisols are extremely low in nutrients. The nutrient capital is in the standing biomass and surface organic matter layer, not the mineral soil. This is why slash-and-burn agriculture exhausts fertility in 2–3 seasons and why selective logging damage to the residual forest can be far worse than the timber removed.

**Emergent trees are not more exposed to stress — they are adapted to it.**
Emergent species evolved for high light, high wind, and high VPD in their crown environment. They are not misplaced — they are the specialists for that niche.
