# Forest Ecology: Succession, Gap Dynamics, Nutrient Cycling

## The Big Picture

A forest is not a stable assemblage — it is a system perpetually recovering from disturbance at multiple spatial and temporal scales. The central organizing frameworks are: (1) succession theory (directional change after disturbance), (2) gap dynamics (the forest as a mosaic of patches at different successional stages), and (3) nutrient cycling (the biogeochemical loops that sustain productivity).

## Section 1 — Succession Theory

### Clementsian vs. Gleasonian Succession

Two fundamentally different models of how vegetation changes over time following disturbance:

```
CLEMENTSIAN MODEL (Clements, 1916):                GLEASONIAN MODEL (Gleason, 1926):

  Vegetation = superorganism                          Each species responds INDEPENDENTLY
  Succession = deterministic progression              to environmental gradients
  Endpoint = stable CLIMAX community                 No climax — just current equilibrium
                                                      given current climate and disturbance
  +---> pioneer --> seral A --> seral B --> CLIMAX    +---> probabilistic assemblage
                                                       determined by propagule arrival,
  The climax community is THE ENDPOINT                 site history, and luck
  for that climate zone

  Modern view: Clementsian useful as heuristic;      Gleasonian now dominant in ecology
               not mechanistically accurate           ("individualistic concept")
```

### Disturbance-Succession Framework

```
  [Major disturbance: fire, clearcut, windthrow]
          |
          v
  [Open, high-light environment]
          |
          v
  [Pioneer species] -- fast-growing, light-demanding, short-lived
  Examples: aspen (Populus), birch (Betula), pin cherry (Prunus pensylvanica)
  Strategy: high seed output, wind dispersal, rapid germination
          |
          | (10–30 yr)
          v
  [Early-seral] -- intermediate species establish
  Examples: tulip poplar, red maple, white ash
          |
          | (30–80 yr)
          v
  [Mid-seral] -- shade-tolerant species emerge in understory
  Examples: sugar maple, beech, hemlock
          |
          | (80–200+ yr)
          v
  [Late-seral / old-growth] -- structurally complex; large trees, snags, logs
  Examples: depends on region; beech-maple in Great Lakes; spruce-fir boreal
```

## Section 2 — Gap Dynamics

Gap dynamics reconceptualizes the forest as a spatial mosaic of patches at different successional stages, driven by individual tree death opening canopy gaps.

```
GAP DYNAMICS — FOREST MOSAIC

  [FOREST STAND viewed from above]

  +-----+----+-------+---------+----+------+
  |Gap C|    | Bldng |         |Gap |      |
  |small|Bldg|phase  | Mature  | A  |Mature|
  |(2yr)|    |(15yr) | forest  |big |forest|
  +-----+----+-------+---------+----+------+
  |       Mature      |Gap B  |             |
  |       forest      |medium |   Mature    |
  |                   |(5yr)  |             |
  +-------------------+-------+-------------+

  Each gap is at a different successional stage simultaneously
  Stand-level diversity maintained by gap mosaic
```

### Gap Characteristics

| Parameter | Value Range | Ecological Effect |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Gap size (small, single tree) | 10–200 m² | Favors shade-tolerant species; advance regeneration |
| Gap size (medium, group) | 200–2000 m² | Mixed; intermediate species benefit |
| Gap size (large, stand) | > 2000 m² | Favors light-demanding pioneers |
| Gap turnover rate (temperate) | 1–2% canopy area/yr | 50–100 yr mean return interval |
| Gap fill time (temperate) | 10–40 yr | Depends on species and gap size |

### Advance Regeneration

In shade-tolerant species (beech, hemlock, sugar maple), seedlings establish in the understory and persist for decades in a suppressed state — waiting for a gap. When a gap opens, they are already in position and accelerate rapidly. This "advance regeneration" is the mechanism by which shade-tolerant species dominate late-seral forests.

```
  HEIGHT GROWTH MODEL:
  Without gap: suppressed sapling, < 5 cm/yr growth
  After gap opens: release response, 30–60 cm/yr for 5–10 yr
  The suppressed period can last 50–150 yr for shade tolerants
```

## Section 4 — Nutrient Cycling

Forest nutrient cycling involves tightly coupled biotic and abiotic processes. The key nutrients are nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), calcium (Ca), potassium (K), and silicon (Si in some systems).

### The Nitrogen Cycle in Forests

```
NITROGEN CYCLE (forest ecosystem):

  ATMOSPHERE (N2 pool: 78%)
     |
     | N2 FIXATION (prokaryotes only)
     | Rhizobium (legumes), Frankia (alder, bayberry -- actinorhizal)
     | Free-living: Azotobacter, Clostridium, Cyanobacteria
     | Rates: 1–300 kg N/ha/yr (alder > Rhizobium > free-living)
     |
     v
  ORGANIC N (litter, soil organic matter)
     |
     | MINERALIZATION (decomposers: fungi + bacteria)
     | Ammonification: organic-N --> NH4+
     | Rate limited by C:N ratio of substrate
     v
  AMMONIUM (NH4+) in soil solution
     |
     | NITRIFICATION (Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter -- aerobic only)
     | NH4+ --> NO2- --> NO3-
     v
  NITRATE (NO3-) -- mobile, readily leached
     |
     | PLANT UPTAKE (both NH4+ and NO3-)
     | DENITRIFICATION (Pseudomonas, anaerobic conditions)
     | NO3- --> N2O --> N2 (gases; lost to atmosphere)
     | LEACHING (NO3- to groundwater)
     v
  LOSS PATHWAY
```

### The Phosphorus Cycle

Unlike N, phosphorus has **no atmospheric reservoir**. Its cycle is a closed loop:

```
  PRIMARY SOURCE: weathering of parent material (apatite, other P minerals)
  Rate: very slow (geological timescales)

  P CYCLE IN FOREST:
  [Plant uptake] <-- [Soil solution P (very low concentration)] <-- [P desorption]
       |                                                                    ^
       v                                                                    |
  [Litter fall]                                                    [P sorption to
       |                                                            Fe/Al oxides]
       v
  [Decomposition] --> [Mineralized P] --> [Soil solution] or [Re-sorbed]

  MYCORRHIZAL P ACQUISITION:
  AM and EM fungi extend effective root surface area 100–1000×
  Hyphal networks reach P-depleted zones around roots
  ~70–80% of P in some forests moves through mycorrhizal pathway
```

### The Calcium Cycle

Calcium is particularly important in temperate forests and is the nutrient most affected by acid deposition:

```
  Ca SOURCES: weathering of Ca-silicate and Ca-carbonate minerals
  Ca SINKS:   leaching (Ca2+ is mobile at low pH), biomass uptake

  Ca CYCLE:
  Soil Ca2+ <--> Exchange sites (clay, organic matter) <--> Soil solution
       ^                                                          |
       | weathering                                               | plant uptake
       |                                                          v
  Mineral pool                                           Stem wood (long-term storage)
  (slow release)                                         Foliage (annual cycling)
                                                                  |
                                                                  | litter fall
                                                                  v
                                                          Decomposition --> soil Ca2+

  ACID RAIN EFFECT:
  H+ (from SO4²- + NO3- deposition) displaces Ca2+ from exchange sites
  Ca2+ leaches to streams; Al3+ (toxic to fine roots) released
  Hubbard Brook (NH): ~60% Ca depletion in exchangeable pool since pre-industrial
```

## Section 5 — Mycorrhizal Networks

Two major types of mycorrhizal associations in forests:

```
ECTOMYCORRHIZAL (EM):                    ARBUSCULAR MYCORRHIZAL (AM):

  Fungal hyphae form a mantle           Hyphae penetrate cell walls
  around root tip                       forming arbuscules inside cells

  Hartig net: hyphae between            No cell wall penetration
  cortical cells (intercellular)        (vs. EM where hyphae stay between cells)

  Forest trees: oaks, beeches,          Agricultural crops + many tropical trees
  birches, pines, spruces, firs         Diverse tropical hardwoods; maples

  ~6,000 fungal species                 ~220 species (Glomeromycota)
  (Ascomycetes + Basidiomycetes)        (far fewer; obligate symbiont)

  Dominant in temperate/boreal          Dominant in tropical and agricultural
  forests; high N-mineralization        forests; low N conditions
  environments

  Fungi receive ~10–20% of tree's       Fungi receive ~5–20% photosynthate
  photosynthate
```

### Network Evidence and Limits

Inter-tree carbon and nutrient transfer through shared mycorrhizal networks is documented (Klein et al. 2016; Simard et al. 1997) but contested in ecological significance:

- **Documented**: 13C-labeled carbon moves from large donor tree to seedling via shared network
- **Contested**: whether transfer is net beneficial to recipient (vs. fungus extracting cost from both)
- **Contested**: frequency of transfer under natural conditions
- **Not demonstrated**: directional "choice" or "communication" — these are anthropomorphizations

## Section 6 — Canopy Stratification

```
CANOPY PROFILE (temperate deciduous, mature):

  Height
  35m  +---[Emergent/dominant crowns]---+   White oak, tulip poplar
       |   Full sun; high photosynthesis|
  20m  +---[Canopy layer]--------------+   Sugar maple, red oak, black cherry
       |   Intercepts 70-80% of light  |
  10m  +---[Sub-canopy]---------------+   Flowering dogwood, ironwood
       |   Diffuse light; shade adapt. |
  3m   +---[Shrub layer]-------------+    Spicebush, viburnums, elderberry
       |   1–5% of full sunlight      |
  0m   +---[Ground layer]------------+    Trillium, mayapple, ferns; spring ephemerals
       | Ephemeral light window before |
       | canopy closes in spring       |

  LIGHT ATTENUATION: approximately 1% of full sunlight reaches forest floor
  Beer-Lambert analog: I = I0 × e^(-k × LAI)
  k = light extinction coefficient (~0.5 for broad-leaved canopy)
  LAI = leaf area index (m² leaf / m² ground; temperate forest: 4–8)
```

## Section 7 — Fire Ecology

Fire is an ecological process, not just a disturbance:

```
FIRE REGIMES:

  LOW-SEVERITY / FREQUENT                HIGH-SEVERITY / INFREQUENT
  (surface fire)                         (crown fire)

  Ponderosa pine forests (SW USA)        Lodgepole pine forests
  1–20 yr return interval                100–300 yr return interval
  Burns understory; kills young stems    Kills all or most trees
  Opens nutrient pools; removes          Complete stand-replacing disturbance
  competing vegetation
  Bark-adapted trees survive             Serotinous cones (lodgepole, jack pine)
                                         open after fire; massive regeneration

  Fire exclusion (20th C policy) -->     Fuel accumulation --> catastrophic
  buildup of fuels in ponderosa pine     megafire outside historical regime
  forests

  NUTRIENT PULSE POST-FIRE:
  Ash releases Ca, Mg, K, P immediately
  Vegetation-free period --> N loss by leaching (no uptake)
  Mycorrhizal network destroyed; recolonization from soil propagules
  N-fixing species (ceanothus, lupine) increase post-fire
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Concept |
|----------|---------|
| Why do shade-tolerant species dominate old-growth? | Long-term gap accumulation selects for advance-regeneration strategy |
| Why does an aspen stand regenerate after clearcut? | Clonal root sprouting; intolerant pioneers have root reserves |
| Why does old-growth hemlock resist beech regeneration? | Deep shade; hemlock creates its own perpetuating shade niche |
| Why is N the primary limiting nutrient in boreal forests? | Low temperature slows N mineralization; decomposition bottleneck |
| Why is P primary limiting in many tropical soils? | Old, weathered oxisols with P sorbed to Fe/Al oxides; no geologically fresh parent material |
| Why did acid rain devastate sugar maple in NE USA? | Ca depletion from soil exchange complex; fine root damage from Al3+ |
| How do gap-phase dynamics maintain diversity? | Different gap sizes favor different shade tolerance groups simultaneously |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Climax forest" is not a real endpoint in the Gleasonian sense.**
No forest community is truly stable indefinitely — climate change, pest cycles, fire regimes, and species invasions continuously shift the equilibrium. What looks like "climax" is simply a slow-enough rate of change to appear stable on human timescales.

**Mycorrhizal networks do not make trees altruistic.**
Trees do not "choose" to send resources to neighboring trees. Fungi are autonomous metabolic agents extracting carbon from multiple hosts. Inter-tree C transfer is real but is a byproduct of fungal foraging behavior, not a tree-level cooperative strategy.

**Succession is not always progressive.**
Retrograde succession (reversal to earlier states) can occur under sustained stress: drought, soil compaction, invasive species. "Moving toward old-growth" is a tendency, not a guarantee.

**Nutrient limitation can shift with ecosystem development.**
Early post-glacial succession is typically P-limited (no organic matter, low microbial activity). After N-fixers colonize, N becomes less limiting; P limitation may persist. Late-successional forests vary by parent material and climate in whether N or P limits productivity.
