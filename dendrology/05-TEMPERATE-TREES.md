# Temperate Tree Species: Oak, Maple, Beech, Ash, Birch, Conifer Families

## The Big Picture

Temperate forest trees divide cleanly into two anatomical kingdoms (gymnosperms and angiosperms) and then into major families that share wood anatomy, ecology, and timber characteristics. This guide covers the principal species groups of North American and European temperate forests with identifiable traits and wood properties.

```
TEMPERATE TREE TAXONOMY MAP

  GYMNOSPERMS (softwoods — tracheids only, no vessels)
  +------------------------------------------------------------------+
  | Pinaceae            Cupressaceae         Taxaceae                |
  | Pine, Spruce,       Cedar, Juniper,      Yew                     |
  | Fir, Larch,         Cypress, Redwood,    (poisonous + bow wood)  |
  | Hemlock, Doug-fir   Arborvitae                                   |
  +------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Section 1 — Gymnosperm vs. Angiosperm Wood Anatomy

| Feature | Gymnosperm (Softwood) | Angiosperm (Hardwood) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Water transport | Tracheids (bordered pits) | Vessels (perforated plates) |
| Mechanical support | Same tracheids | Separate fibers (libriform) |
| Ray width | Uniseriate (1 cell wide) mostly | Variable: 1 to 30+ cells wide |
| Resin canals | Present (Pinus, Picea, Larix) | Absent (except traumatic in some) |
| Ring visibility | Clear (density contrast EW/LW) | Variable (ring-porous vs. diffuse) |
| Machining | Easier; less dulling | Harder; vessel tearing an issue |
| Drying | Generally faster; less checking | Many species prone to degrade |

## Section 2 — Oaks (Quercus, Fagaceae)

Oaks are ecologically and economically the most important deciduous hardwood genus in the Northern Hemisphere.

### Red Oak Group vs. White Oak Group

```
  RED OAK GROUP:                        WHITE OAK GROUP:
  (Q. rubra, velutina, coccinea, etc.)  (Q. alba, bicolor, macrocarpa, etc.)

  Leaf lobes with bristle tips          Leaf lobes rounded, no bristle tips
  Acorns mature in 2 years              Acorns mature in 1 year
  NO tyloses in vessels                 TYLOSES block vessels (watertight)
  Vessels open, permeable               Impermeable to water/gas
  NOT suitable for barrel-making        WHITE OAK required for wine/whiskey barrels
  Open-grain; checks easily             Closed-grain; watertight staves

  Both: ring-porous, large EW vessels   Both: large rays visible as "silver grain"
```

### Oak Wood Properties

| Property | Red Oak | White Oak |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Janka hardness | 1290 lbf | 1360 lbf |
| Density (air-dry) | 770 kg/m³ | 770 kg/m³ |
| Tangential shrinkage | 8.6% | 10.5% |
| Durability (heartwood) | Class 3–4 | Class 2–3 |
| Vessel permeability | Open | Closed (tyloses) |
| Tannin content | High | Very high |
| Key uses | Flooring, furniture, cabinets | Barrels, ship planking, furniture |

## Section 3 — Maples (Acer, Aceraceae/Sapindaceae)

Economically dominant in the eastern North American hardwood market; all maples are diffuse-porous.

| Species | Common Name | Janka | Notes |
|---------|------------|-------|-------|
| *A. saccharum* | Sugar / Rock maple | 1450 lbf | Hardest domestic species; "hard maple"; flooring, bowling lanes |
| *A. saccharinum* | Silver maple | 700 lbf | "Soft maple"; fast-growing; machining wood |
| *A. rubrum* | Red maple | 950 lbf | "Soft maple"; commercial mixed-species lumber |
| *A. platanoides* | Norway maple (invasive) | ~1010 lbf | Displaces native maples; wood similar to sugar maple |
| *A. pseudoplatanus* | Sycamore maple (EU) | ~1020 lbf | "Fiddleback" figure; musical instruments |

**Sugar maple special properties:**
- Curly, bird's-eye, and quilted figure forms — premium market value
- Sapwood is cream-white with almost no heartwood discoloration — favored for flooring
- Very high silica content → dulls cutting edges faster than most species

## Section 4 — Beeches (Fagus, Fagaceae)

*Fagus sylvatica* (European beech) and *F. grandifolia* (American beech) are structurally similar:

| Property | European Beech | American Beech |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| Janka hardness | ~1300 lbf | ~1300 lbf |
| Density (air-dry) | ~720 kg/m³ | ~740 kg/m³ |
| Ring-porous? | No — diffuse-porous | No — diffuse-porous |
| Rays | Very large, multiseriate | Large |
| Shrinkage | High (T: 11.8%, R: 5.8%) | High (T: 11.9%) |
| Durability | Class 5 (not durable) | Class 5 |
| Key uses | Furniture, plywood, turned ware | Furniture frames, veneer |

**Beech ecology note:** American beech is highly shade-tolerant and a persistent late-seral dominant in the Great Lakes / Appalachian region. It reproduces vegetatively by root sprouts, creating dense clonal groves. Currently devastated by Beech Bark Disease (BBD) — *Nectria* fungus introduced by European beech scale.

## Section 5 — Ashes (Fraxinus, Oleaceae)

Ashes are ring-porous, with large earlywood vessel rings and diffuse latewood fibers.

| Property | White Ash | Black Ash | European Ash |
|----------|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Janka hardness | 1320 lbf | 850 lbf | ~1260 lbf |
| Density | ~660 kg/m³ | ~540 kg/m³ | ~720 kg/m³ |
| Toughness | Very high | High | Very high |
| Key uses | Tool handles, baseball bats, oars | Basket splints, furniture | Sports equipment, furniture |

**Ash toughness:** Ash has exceptionally high energy absorption before failure — the reason for tool handles, hockey sticks, oars, and historically in bow-making. The ring-porous structure with pronounced latewood bands contributes to this.

**Emerald Ash Borer (EAB):** *Agrilus planipennis* (introduced from Asia, first detected Michigan 2002) has killed billions of ash trees across North America and is now established in Europe. Effectively eliminates *Fraxinus* from affected stands unless treated annually. A genuinely catastrophic ecological event comparable to chestnut blight.

## Section 6 — Birches (Betula, Betulaceae)

Birches are diffuse-porous pioneers with short-lived dominance after disturbance.

| Species | Notes | Wood Use |
|---------|-------|----------|
| *B. papyrifera* (Paper birch) | White bark; N. American pioneer; very short-lived (70–80 yr) | Pulp, plywood, veneer, turned goods |
| *B. alleghaniensis* (Yellow birch) | Shade tolerant; long-lived; cherry-like figure | Furniture, cabinetry, flooring |
| *B. pendula* (Silver birch EU) | Short-lived; pioneer; thin white bark | Ply, pulp; important culturally (Finland, Russia) |
| *B. lenta* (Sweet birch) | Methyl salicylate scent; dense (820 kg/m³) | Premium furniture; flooring |

**Alnus (Alder)** in the same family: the nitrogen fixer. *A. rubra* (red alder, Pacific NW) is an actinorhizal species that fixes atmospheric N at up to 60–130 kg N/ha/yr through Frankia symbiosis. Ecologically critical in riparian zones and early post-disturbance succession.

## Common Confusion Points

**Red oak vs. white oak in commerce.**
At the lumberyard, "red oak" and "white oak" are sold as separate species groups. Never mix them for the same project — their shrinkage, figure, and especially vessel structure are incompatible if watertightness matters (e.g., barrel staves, boat planking).

**Eastern red cedar is a juniper, not a cedar.**
*Juniperus virginiana* (eastern red "cedar") is in Cupressaceae. True cedars (*Cedrus*) are in Pinaceae and are not native to North America. This naming confusion extends to western red "cedar" (*Thuja plicata*), also in Cupressaceae, not a true cedar.

**Douglas-fir is not a true fir.**
*Pseudotsuga menziesii* is in Pinaceae but in its own genus. It is not an *Abies* (true fir), not a *Pinus* (pine), not a *Tsuga* (hemlock). The hyphenated name is intentional in technical use to flag this.

**"Poplar" in North America vs. Europe.**
In North America, "poplar" in the lumber trade usually means tulip poplar (*Liriodendron tulipifera*, Magnoliaceae) — completely unrelated to European poplars (*Populus*, Salicaceae). Tulip poplar is not a true poplar at all.

**Teak's silica content destroys cutting tools.**
Teak (*Tectona grandis*) contains up to 1.4% amorphous silica. Carbide-tipped tools are mandatory; HSS tooling dulls almost immediately. This is a significant machining cost factor.
