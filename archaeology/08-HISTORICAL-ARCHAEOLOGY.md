# Historical Archaeology

## The Big Picture

**Documents-vs-material record as logs-vs-telemetry**: The central methodological insight of historical archaeology — that material culture often contradicts, supplements, or reveals what written records omit — is structurally identical to the gap between reported behavior and observed telemetry in production systems. What teams say they do (retrospective documentation, incident reports, architecture diagrams) consistently diverges from what the system actually does (distributed traces, heap dumps, network captures). William Rathje's Garbage Project (Tucson, 1970s–90s) is a direct parallel to production observability: participants consistently self-reported consuming less alcohol and more healthy food than their garbage documented. The material record doesn't lie — it records what was actually discarded, not what people wished to report. This is the same logic as "trust the logs, not the status page." Historical archaeology's value proposition is that written sources are authored (someone chose what to record and how) while material culture is deposited (it accumulates through behavior, not representation).

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|           HISTORICAL ARCHAEOLOGY: SCOPE AND SIGNIFICANCE        |
|                                                                  |
|  DEFINITION: Archaeology of societies that have written records |
|  PARADOX: Why dig if we have documents?                         |
|  ANSWER: Documents record what their authors chose to record.   |
```

## Colonial Contact Archaeology

When European colonizers encountered indigenous populations, the contact process left material traces on both sides.

```
CONTACT PERIOD SIGNATURES:
  European goods in indigenous contexts:
    Glass beads: exchanged in early contact → date indigenous sites
    Metal tools (axes, knives): replace stone → technological change
    Brass kettles: cut up and reworked into ornaments (not just used as pots)
    European ceramics: occasionally in indigenous assemblages

  Indigenous goods in European contexts:
    Native pottery: service in colonial kitchens
    Native food crops: maize replaces European grain
    Architectural adaptations: European floor plans with native techniques

  THE TRANSFORMATION PROCESS:
    Indigenous peoples did not simply adopt European goods wholesale
    → Beads reworked for indigenous aesthetic and symbolic systems
    → Copper (European) conflated with indigenous copper as prestige good
    → Horses: transformed Plains Indian societies in 1-2 generations

  EXAMPLE: Roanoke Colony (1585-90, lost colony):
    Archaeological site X-1 (Hatteras Island, NC): finds suggest
    some colonists survived by integrating with Croatoan people
    → Material evidence of cultural fusion; written record silent
```

### Mission Archaeology

```
SPANISH COLONIAL MISSIONS (16th–18th c., Americas + Philippines):
  Physical form: church + convento + indigenous quarters
  Goal: Convert indigenous population; extract labor (mita/encomienda)

WHAT ARCHAEOLOGY SHOWS:
  Diet change at missions: indigenous diet shifts toward European crops
  But: maize and local plants persist → partial assimilation
  Skeletal health: DETERIORATED at missions
    → Crowding → infectious disease; hard labor → skeletal stress
    → Reduced dietary diversity → nutritional deficiencies
    → Life expectancy shorter at mission than pre-contact
  Written mission records: describe conversion success, spiritual progress
  Archaeology: describes disease, malnutrition, shortened lives
  → The gap between document and material record is stark

CALIFORNIA MISSIONS (21 missions, 1769–1833):
  La Purísima (Lompoc): extensively excavated + reconstructed
  Santa Barbara, San Juan Capistrano: active archaeology ongoing
  → Indigenous Chumash, Ohlone, and others: devastating depopulation
```

## Plantation Archaeology and the Archaeology of the Enslaved

This is among the most politically significant subfields of historical archaeology — because the written record was produced by enslavers, not the enslaved.

```
THE DOCUMENTARY RECORD PROBLEM:
  Plantation records: inventories of enslaved people as property
  → Names, ages, "values," tasks assigned
  → Written by enslavers from an ownership perspective
  → Almost never from the perspective of enslaved individuals

  Enslaved people left almost no written record (literacy suppressed;
    few exceptions: Frederick Douglass, Harriet Jacobs, etc.)

  MATERIAL CULTURE: the only non-enslaver record of enslaved life
  → Spatial organization of quarters (how much space, what privacy)
  → Diet (what bones, what pots — compare plantation records vs. actual)
  → Spiritual practices (African-derived ritual objects)
  → Material resistance and agency (hidden objects, alternative economies)
```

### Slave Quarter Archaeology

```
SLAVE QUARTER FINDS:
  SPATIAL ORGANIZATION:
    Location on plantation: distance from main house
    House form: single-room vs. multi-room; flooring; chimney
    Clustering: family units vs. dormitory-style
    Privacy: gardens, yard areas (personal agricultural plots)

  DIET ANALYSIS:
    Faunal assemblage: plantation records may say 1 lb pork/week
    → Archaeology shows: supplemental hunting (deer, opossum, turkey),
       fishing, foraging, personal gardens → much richer diet than records
    → Enslaved people actively supplemented inadequate rations
    → Dietary independence = economic resistance to plantation control

  SPIRITUAL PRACTICE:
    "Colonoware": low-fired pottery, hand-built; African-derived techniques
    → Found in South Carolina; traditionally attributed to Native Americans
    → Now understood: partly produced by enslaved Africans; hybrid tradition
    Bundles (mojo bags, caches): hidden under hearths, in walls
    → Materials: crystals, pins, animal bones, ash, coins in patterned assemblage
    → Associated with African-derived spiritual practices (Bakongo cosmogram)
    → Completely absent from written plantation records

  RESISTANCE MATERIAL:
    Hidden caches of alcohol, tobacco, food
    Tools owned independently (off-the-books economy)
    Catholic religious items in Protestant slave states → syncretic practice
```

**Monticello (Jefferson's plantation)**:

```
MONTICELLO SLAVE QUARTERS (excavation ongoing since 1979):
  Main house: Thomas Jefferson's neoclassical mansion (well documented)
  Mulberry Row: workshops + slave quarters along service road

  Archaeological finds:
  → Glass from broken bottles → possible drinking
  → Pins, buttons, ceramics → personal possessions beyond work tools
  → Cowrie shells (West African symbolic significance) in Hemings family area
  → Food remains: variety greater than plantation ledgers suggest

  Sally Hemings room (2017 excavation):
    Jefferson's enslaved concubine; relationship confirmed genetically
    (Jefferson Y-chromosome in Hemings descendants)
    Room identified adjacent to Jefferson's bedroom
    → Material evidence of documented but long-denied relationship
```

## Industrial Archaeology

```
SCOPE:
  Physical remains of industrial processes (16th c. to present)
  Bridges, canals, railways, factories, mines, ports
  Recording before demolition; heritage preservation

IRONBRIDGE GORGE (Telford, UK):
  Abraham Darby's blast furnace (1709): first coke-smelted iron
  Iron Bridge (1779): first cast-iron bridge
  Multiple adjacent industrial sites in UNESCO World Heritage designation
  Archaeological recording of blast furnace sequence

COAL MINING ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Shaft heads, engine houses, spoil heaps
  Cornish engine houses: distinctive architecture; hundreds on landscape
  Colliery villages: planned settlement for mine workers
  Evidence of child labor in pre-1842 period (bone pathology in miners)

TEXTILE MILLS:
  Arkwright's Cromford Mill (1771): first water-powered spinning mill
  Multi-story mill buildings: specific structural requirements (iron columns)
  Power transmission: waterwheel → gear → line shafting → machines
  Millworker housing: planned terraces adjacent to mills

RAILWAYS:
  Cuttings, embankments, tunnels, viaducts: large landscape impact
  Station archaeology: change from wooden to masonry structures
  Locomotive roundhouses: engineering workshops

INDUSTRIAL ARCHAEOLOGY METHODOLOGY:
  Standing building recording: photogrammetry + measured drawing
  Earthwork survey: LiDAR for landscape remains
  Targeted excavation: specific features (furnace base, engine bed)
  Oral history: living memory of recent industrial sites
  Archive research: maps, plans, photographs, industrial records
```

## Shipwreck Archaeology (Post-1500)

```
HISTORICAL PERIOD WRECKS:
  Mary Rose (1545): Henry VIII warship; raised 1982
    → Artifacts: bows, arrows, clothing, personal effects, cannon
    → Bioarchaeology of crew: health, origins, diet (Sr isotopes)
    → Wrecks as sealed deposits: no later intrusion after sinking

  Vasa (1628): Swedish warship; sank on maiden voyage; raised 1961
    → Extraordinary preservation in Baltic low-salinity water
    → Crew remains; cargo; full rig partially reconstructed
    → Design flaw identified: too narrow relative to gun deck height

  East Indiamen: Dutch, British VOC/EIC ships
    → Cargo manifests + archaeology: verify what was traded
    → Cargo distribution among crew vs. company goods (smuggling)

  SLAVE SHIP ARCHAEOLOGY:
    Henrietta Marie (1700): slaved; sank off Florida
    → First North American slave ship archaeology
    → Iron shackles, trading goods; confronting material evidence
    São José (1794): Portuguese slave ship sank off Cape Town
    → 212 enslaved Mozambicans drowned; first identified slave ship
    → Memorialization ethics: who decides what to do with the wreck?

  TITANIC (1912): Deepwater wreck ~3,800 m
    → RMS Titanic designated as protected site
    → Ongoing photogrammetry (2023: complete 3D digital twin)
    → Rapid deterioration from iron-eating bacteria
```

## Conflict Archaeology

```
BATTLEFIELD ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Metal detecting + GIS mapping + excavation
  → Reconstruct troop movements from artifact distribution

  LITTLE BIGHORN (1876):
    Surface and subsurface metal detecting → cartridge cases, bullets
    Ballistic matching: specific firearms → unit positions
    → Spatial pattern contradicts 19th c. narratives
    → Revealed Custer's command was more dispersed than accounts suggested

  FIRST WORLD WAR:
    Western Front: extensive surviving trench systems (preserved farmland)
    Flanders: human remains regularly recovered during development
    Belgian Joint Task Force: ongoing recovery and identification
    → DNA identification of individual soldiers from bones
    → Artillery shell fragments: metallurgical analysis → factory of origin

  MASSACRE SITES:
    Srebrenica (1995): forensic archaeology for evidence of war crimes
    → Body recovery + DNA identification (ICMP)
    → Stratigraphic evidence of primary vs. secondary mass graves
    → Bullets, ligatures, blindfolds: physical evidence of execution

FORENSIC ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Applies archaeological recording methodology to crime scenes
  Mass graves: stratify, record, recover evidence; chain of custody
  Evidence for war crimes tribunals: ICTY, ICC
  Cold cases: long-term burial recovery and analysis
```

## Contemporary and Modern Archaeology

```
GARBAGE PROJECT (William Rathje, 1973+):
  Archaeological methods applied to modern landfills
  → What do people throw away vs. what they say they consume?
  → Self-reported alcohol consumption: underestimated by 40-60%
  → Food waste: much more than people report
  → Newspapers from 1960s still legible in landfill (not biodegraded)
  → Hot dogs, grass clippings, disposable diapers: still recognizable after 25 years

DISASTER ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Pompeii effect: sudden events create frozen-in-time deposits
  World Trade Center (9/11): rapid archaeological recording of the site
  → Stratigraphic sequence of collapse documented
  → Objects recovered and catalogued as heritage

DIGITAL HERITAGE:
  3D scanning: museums digitize collections (British Museum, Smithsonian)
  LiDAR: record standing structures non-invasively
  Satellite archaeology: SpaceNet, Planet Labs, CORONA declassified imagery
    → CORONA spy satellite images (1960s): revealed site patterns now destroyed
    → Tell sites in Mesopotamia: looting visible from orbit post-2003
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Use this when... | Method / approach | Notes |
|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| The written record is exclusively from the dominating group's perspective | Plantation archaeology / archaeology of the enslaved | Documents are authored by enslavers; slave quarter excavation recovers the material record of enslaved people's own lives — spiritual objects, food supplementation, personal items, evidence of resistance |
| Troop movements and battle dynamics need reconstruction beyond official accounts | Conflict archaeology: metal detecting + GIS + projectile scatter analysis | Spent cartridge and bullet distribution maps troop positions and movement; official battle accounts are often post-hoc narratives |
| Self-reported consumption data seems unreliable | Garbage Project methodology (material discard vs. self-report) | Material record corrects for social desirability bias; works for food, alcohol, household consumption patterns |
| Contact-era cultural mixing needs to be understood | Colonoware and hybrid material culture analysis | Mixed-tradition ceramics (African + European techniques) reveal cultural synthesis that neither African nor European documentary sources describe |
| Industrial processes need reconstruction | Industrial archaeology: fabric analysis + experimental replication | Machinery, production waste, spatial layout of factory floors preserve process knowledge not recorded in manuals |
| Mass grave evidence is needed for legal proceedings | Forensic archaeology: chain-of-custody recording + aDNA identification | Methods identical to standard excavation but with legal evidentiary standards; supports war crimes tribunals |
| The gap between official account and material record is the finding | Documents-vs-material cross-analysis | When they agree: corroboration; when they diverge: the divergence is the data — what was systematically omitted or misrepresented |

## Common Confusion Points

**Historical archaeology ≠ just digging up old stuff with documents**: The discipline developed partly as a critique of the textual record — specifically to document the lives of people excluded from written history. The "historical" in the name refers to the existence of a written record, not the discipline's reliance on it.

**Plantation archaeology is not about slavery tourism**: The field emerged from serious scholarship about African American material culture and history. The political stakes — whose history gets told, by whom, with what resources — are active debates within the discipline.

**The "Columbian Exchange" is material**: European contact with the Americas involved massive two-way exchange: European plants, animals, and diseases to Americas; New World crops (maize, potato, tomato, tobacco) to Europe and Asia. This is traceable in the archaeological record — through botanical remains, faunal assemblages, skeletal disease evidence (treponematosis in pre-Columbian vs. post-contact skeletons), and trade goods.

**Forensic archaeology ≠ archaeology of crime generally**: Forensic archaeology specifically refers to application of archaeological methods to legal contexts (crime scenes, mass graves, body recovery). It requires additional training in chain-of-custody procedures, legal standards of evidence, and coordination with law enforcement and human rights organizations.
