## The Lower Paleolithic

### Tool Industries

```
STONE TOOL TECHNOLOGY (Lithic Modes):

MODE 1 — OLDOWAN (2.6–1.5 Ma):
  Earliest recognized stone tools
  Simple flaking: core + flakes (unmodified)
  Requires identifying suitable stone, percussion flaking
  No formal shape; expedient
  Made by: Homo habilis, Paranthropus (tool use preceded genus Homo?)

MODE 2 — ACHEULEAN (1.76 Ma – 300 Ka):
  Handaxe: bifacially worked, teardrop/oval form
  Same basic form maintained for ~1.5 million years
  Requires mental template: working toward a predetermined form
  Made by: Homo erectus and successors
  Spread: Africa, Europe, South Asia; not East Asia (different tradition)

  Why did the handaxe persist unchanged for 1.5 Ma?
  Possible answers:
    → Worked well (no pressure to change)
    → Culturally conservative transmission
    → Neurological constraint on innovation
    → Actually not unchanging (subtle variation by time/region)
  Still debated
```

### Homo erectus and Dispersals

```
HOMO ERECTUS (1.89 Ma – 100 Ka):
  First hominin to leave Africa: Dmanisi, Georgia (~1.8 Ma)
  Spread across Asia (Java Man: 1.5 Ma; Peking Man: 0.5 Ma)
  Developed Acheulean handaxe technology
  Control of fire: evidence at Wonderwerk Cave (1 Ma), Zhoukoudian (~0.4 Ma)
  Variable body size; larger than earlier hominins
  Encephalization: brain ~850–1100 cc (Homo sapiens: ~1350 cc)
```

## The Middle Paleolithic

### Prepared-Core Technology (Levallois)

```
LEVALLOIS TECHNIQUE (Mode 3, ~300–30 Ka):
  Prepare a core platform carefully → detach a predetermined flake shape
  Planned → the shape of the flake is controlled BEFORE detachment
  This is a different cognitive architecture from expedient flaking

CONCEPT:
  1. Select suitable nodule
  2. Prepare the core by shaping the dome (upper surface)
  3. Create a striking platform (lower edge)
  4. Detach a Levallois flake of predetermined form

  The prepared-core method = planning ahead; the operator holds a
  mental model of the final product BEFORE the decisive strike
  → Computational analogy: specifying the output type in advance

MAKERS: Neanderthals (Europe/Near East); Modern humans (Africa/Levant)
  Both produced Levallois flakes with indistinguishable form
  → The technique alone doesn't distinguish species
```

### Neanderthals

```
NEANDERTHALS (Homo neanderthalensis, ~400–40 Ka):
  Europe and western Asia
  Brain size: ~1500 cc (larger than modern human average)
  Cold-adapted anatomy: large nose (warm/moisten cold air), stocky build
  Mousterian stone industry (flakes, scrapers, points)
  Symbolic behavior: ochre use, perforated shells, feather ornaments
  Interbreeding with modern humans: ~2% of non-African genomes
  Extinction: ~40 Ka; coincides with modern human arrival in Europe

  NEANDERTHAL ART?
  U-series dating on cave art in Spain: 65,000 BP
  Modern humans in Spain: arrived ~45 Ka
  → If reliable, Neanderthals made cave art before modern human arrival
  → Very contested; ongoing debate about dating accuracy

  WHAT KILLED THEM?
  Competition with modern humans (resource competition)
  Climate change (cold oscillation at end)
  Disease from modern humans (no immune adaptation)
  Demographic absorption (interbreeding, not extinction)
  Likely: combination of all above; no single cause
```

## The Upper Paleolithic Revolution

~45,000 BP in Europe (100–75 Ka in Africa for some behavioral markers):

```
BEHAVIORAL MODERNITY PACKAGE (debated):
  → Blade and bladelet technology (long, narrow, standardized flakes)
  → Systematic use of bone, antler, ivory for tools
  → Figurative art (Venus figurines, cave paintings, portable art)
  → Personal ornaments (beads, pendants, body painting)
  → Long-distance exchange networks (shells far from coast)
  → Structured living spaces (evidence of hearth use, storage areas)
  → Complex hunting strategies (seasonal, targeted)
  → Possibly: language (no direct evidence; inferred from behavior)

CAVE ART:
  Lascaux (France): ~17,000 BP — detailed polychrome animals
  Altamira (Spain): ~18,000 BP — ceiling bison, classic
  Chauvet (France): ~37,000 BP — oldest in Europe; sophisticated technique
  Sulawesi (Indonesia): >45,000 BP — pig and hand stencils; oldest figurative
  → Cave art is NOT just European; it was a global phenomenon
```

## Out-of-Africa Dispersals

```
MODERN HUMAN DISPERSAL MODEL (current consensus, but evolving):
  Anatomically modern Homo sapiens originated in Africa (~300 Ka)
  Multiple early dispersals that mostly didn't persist
  Main successful dispersal: ~60–70 Ka (coastal route? Arabia?)
  By 45 Ka: in Europe (replacing Neanderthals)
  By 50 Ka: in Australia (Madjedbebe site: 65 Ka disputed)
  By 15 Ka: in Americas (Beringia land bridge; earlier dates contested)
  By 13 Ka: at Monte Verde, Chile (southernmost S. America)

  GENETIC EVIDENCE:
  Non-African populations descend from a bottleneck (small founder group)
  → Less genetic diversity than African populations (founder effect)
  → ALL non-African populations are a subset of African diversity

  MULTIPLE DISPERSALS:
  aDNA shows complexity — multiple waves, replacements, admixture
  First Americans: at least 3 distinct founding populations
  Australia: evidence of Indian ancestry influx ~4 Ka
  Europe: completely replaced by farmers, then steppe migrants
  → Population history = continuous flux, not single events
```

## Megafauna Extinctions — The Pleistocene Debate

```
PLEISTOCENE MEGAFAUNA EXTINCTION:
  40,000–10,000 BP: wave of large animal extinctions globally
  Australia: 85 genera lost (wombats as large as hippos, giant kangaroos)
  Americas: mammoths, mastodons, horses, giant ground sloths, camels
  Eurasia: woolly mammoth, woolly rhinoceros, cave bear, cave lion
  Africa: least affected (co-evolved with hunters?)

THREE MAIN HYPOTHESES:
  1. OVERKILL (Paul Martin, 1973):
     Human arrival coincides with extinction on each continent
     Naive prey (never hunted before) → unsustainable harvest
     Circumstantial: timing correlation is strong

  2. CLIMATE CHANGE:
     Pleistocene-Holocene transition: rapid climate shift
     Vegetation zone changes → habitat loss
     Humans not necessary; climate sufficient?
     Counter: prior ice-age transitions didn't cause mass extinction

  3. COMBINED (current best evidence):
     Climate change weakened populations
     Human pressure (even low-level hunting) pushed over edge
     "Synergy" model: neither alone was sufficient
     Continental differences reflect different human arrival timing

  MAMMOTH SURVIVAL:
  Wrangel Island (Arctic): mammoths survived until 4,000 BP
  → Isolated from humans; supports human pressure as key factor
  → Also shows islands can preserve megafauna far longer than mainland
```

## The Neolithic Revolution

```
AGRICULTURAL ORIGINS (polycentric, not single event):

SOUTHWEST ASIA (Fertile Crescent, ~10,000 BC):
  Pre-Pottery Neolithic A (PPNA): sedentary villages without ceramics
  Göbekli Tepe: monumental T-pillar enclosures; hunter-gatherers
    built this BEFORE farming → revises simple narrative
  Wild einkorn wheat → domesticated ~9500 BC (Karaçadağ, Turkey)
  Wild barley, lentil, pea, flax also domesticated
  Sheep, goat (~8000 BC), cattle, pig
  Spread: Anatolian farmers expanded into Europe by ~6000 BC
    → Replaced most European hunter-gatherers genetically

EAST ASIA (~7000 BC):
  Rice (Oryza sativa) domesticated: Yangtze River valley
  Millet (N. China): Yellow River valley
  Pig domestication: multiple centers
  Chicken: Southeast/South Asia

AMERICAS:
  Maize (teosinte → maize): Balsas region, Mexico; ~8700 BC
    (Teosinte looks nothing like corn; domestication = massive morphology change)
  Potato, quinoa, llama/alpaca: Andes
  Sunflower, squash: Eastern North America

AFRICA:
  Sorghum, pearl millet: Sahel/sub-Saharan Africa (~4000 BC)
  African rice (O. glaberrima): West Africa
  Yam: West Africa

KEY PATTERNS:
  Independent multiple origins → agriculture was a convergent solution
  Not a sudden "revolution" — gradual intensification over millennia
  Agriculture initially: more work for lower nutrition per hour than hunting
  But: supports higher population density; enables storage; enables cities
```

### The Secondary Products Revolution

Andrew Sherratt's model (1981): ~4500 BC, new uses of domesticated animals emerge beyond primary products (meat, hide, bone):

```
SECONDARY PRODUCTS REVOLUTION (~4500–3000 BC Europe/Near East):
  PRIMARY (from killing): meat, hide, bone, horn
  SECONDARY (from living animals):
    Milk/dairy: hard evidence from lipid residues in pottery ~6000 BC
    Wool: sheep bred for fleece (not just meat); ~4000 BC
    Traction: cattle used to pull plows → greatly expands agriculture
    Riding: horse riding; ~3500–3000 BC (Eneolithic steppe cultures)
    Pack transport: llama (Americas), donkey, camel

  Impact: traction plow → cultivate heavier soils → agricultural expansion
  Wheeled transport: correlated with horse/ox traction; ~3500 BC
  → Secondary products = the innovation that enabled Bronze Age economies
```

## Bronze Age Metallurgy

```
METALWORKING SEQUENCE:
  Native copper use: ~9000 BC (cold hammering of naturally occurring Cu)
  Copper smelting: ~5000 BC (Rudna Glava, Serbia; requires reduction with charcoal)
  Arsenical copper: ~3500 BC (arsenic impurities improve hardness; natural alloy)
  Bronze (Cu + Sn): ~3300 BC (Near East); requires tin from specific sources
    → Long-distance tin trade becomes critical supply chain
    → Tin sources: Cornwall (UK), Erzgebirge (Germany), Afghanistan, Iberia
  Iron: ~1200–1000 BC widespread (iron-smelting requires higher temperatures)

THE BRONZE AGE SUPPLY CHAIN:
  Copper ore + tin ore → smelt separately → alloy → cast → tools/weapons
  Copper sources: Cyprus (= "Cuprum" origin of "copper"), Sinai, Iberia
  Tin sources: distant; created long-distance exchange networks

BRONZE AGE COLLAPSE (~1200 BC):
  Synchronized collapse of interconnected Bronze Age palace economies
  Egypt, Mycenae, Hittites, Ugarit all destabilized in ~50 year window
  Evidence: burned palace sites, disrupted trade (no more oxhide ingots)
  Causes debated: "Sea Peoples" invasion, climate disruption, internal revolt,
    supply chain disruption (tin trade breakdown), epidemic
  Survivors: Egypt weakened; Assyria persisted; Phoenician cities
  → Followed by Iron Age development (iron easier to source locally than tin)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Use this when... | Method / concept | Notes |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------|
| Dating pre-ceramic sites (no pottery, no carbon in useful contexts) | Lithic typology + OSL/TL dating | Typological sequences (Oldowan → Acheulean → Levallois) establish relative chronology; OSL dates sediment deposition directly |
| Tracking population movements across continents | Ancient DNA (aDNA) | Haplogroup distribution + admixture modeling; can separate migration from cultural diffusion |
| Reconstructing diet across individual lifetimes | Stable isotopes (δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N) from bone collagen | Bone collagen records ~10-year dietary average; tooth enamel records childhood; hair/nail record months |
| Distinguishing independent invention from diffusion | Comparative typology + chronological distribution maps | Simultaneous appearance in distant regions with no plausible contact = independent invention; sequential spread along routes = diffusion |
| Dating a site with abundant charcoal or short-lived organic material | Radiocarbon (AMS) | Precision ~±20–50 years for Holocene; becomes imprecise beyond ~50 Ka |
| Establishing that humans caused megafauna extinction vs. climate | Chronological overlap testing | Compare extinction date to first human arrival date and to climate event date; if extinction tracks human arrival more tightly than climate shifts, human causation is implicated |
| Understanding Bronze Age exchange networks | Tin and copper isotope provenancing | Tin isotopes fingerprint ore sources; matching finished bronze to ore region maps trade routes |

## Common Confusion Points

**Anatomically modern vs. behaviorally modern**: Anatomically modern Homo sapiens (modern skeleton) appears ~300 Ka. Behavioral modernity (art, complex tools, symbolic behavior) emerged gradually — some elements earlier, "full package" by ~45 Ka. The terms don't describe the same transition.

**Out-of-Africa is not just one migration**: There were multiple dispersals over time. The main successful wave was ~60–70 Ka, but earlier dispersals occurred (and left little genetic trace in modern populations). The Americas were populated multiple times, with evidence for at least 3 founding genetic lineages.

**Neolithic does not mean "same time everywhere"**: Neolithic (farming) started ~10,000 BC in SW Asia. It reached Britain ~4000 BC. It reached parts of Australia... never (hunting-gathering persisted until European contact). Neolithic is a technological stage, not a global time horizon.

**Göbekli Tepe upends the standard narrative**: The standard story was: farming → surplus → permanent settlement → monuments. Göbekli Tepe (complex monuments built by pre-agricultural hunter-gatherers) suggests the causality might be reversed or more complex: ritual/social aggregation → settled community → agriculture to feed the gatherers.

**Bronze Age collapse**: The interconnected palace economy collapse ~1200 BC demonstrates how supply chain fragility and systems complexity can create cascade failure — relevant as a historical analog for modern globalized supply chains.
