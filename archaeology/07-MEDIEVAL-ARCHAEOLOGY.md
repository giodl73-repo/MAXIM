## Settlement Archaeology

### Villages and Rural Settlement

```
MEDIEVAL VILLAGE FORMS:
  NUCLEATED VILLAGES (common in England, N. France, N. Germany):
    Clustered houses around a green, church, or manor
    Open-field system: strips in communal fields; complex rotation
    Deserted medieval villages (DMVs): entire village abandonment
    → Causes: Black Death (1348–50); enclosure; bad harvests; landlord clearing
    → UK: 3,000+ DMVs identified; many from aerial photography

  DISPERSED SETTLEMENT (common in SW England, Wales, Ireland):
    Individual farmsteads; enclosures; no central nucleation
    More Celtic/Romano-British continuity

DESERTED MEDIEVAL VILLAGES:
  Wharram Percy (Yorkshire, UK): most excavated DMV
    Excavated 40+ years (Beresford and Hurst)
    → 12 longhouses + church + manor; peaked at ~40 families
    → Abandoned c. 1500 as sheep farming replaced grain
    → Bioarchaeology: population health, diet, disease from skeletons

LONGHOUSE:
  Standard vernacular house: humans and livestock under one roof
    → Warmth from animals; shared entrance; byre at one end
  Wall: timber, cruck frame, or stone foundation
  Roof: thatch or turf; leaves minimal trace archaeologically
  → Identified by: wall slot/post-holes, hearth, midden (refuse) deposit
```

### Castles and Manorial Sites

```
CASTLE CONSTRUCTION SEQUENCE:
  Norman motte-and-bailey (post-1066 in England):
    Motte: earthen mound with timber tower
    Bailey: enclosed courtyard below
    Quick to build; earth + timber
    → Replaced by stone in 12th c. as permanence required

  Stone keeps:
    Tower of London (1078, William I): White Tower
    Shell keep: stone ring wall on motte (Arundel, Windsor)
    Great keep: free-standing square tower (Rochester, Dover)

  LATER CASTLES (12th–15th c.):
    Concentric design: multiple rings of walls
    Cylindrical towers (better angle of fire)
    Gatehouse towers
    Water defenses: moats, wet ditches
    Edward I's Welsh castles: Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech
    → Military engineering at state scale; financed by royal government

  CASTLE ARCHAEOLOGY:
    Stratigraphic excavation of complex multi-period sites
    Finds: pottery sequence dates occupation phases
    Documentary records: building accounts survive for some (Tower of London)
    Dendrochronology of surviving timbers → precise construction dates
```

### Cathedral and Church Construction

```
CATHEDRAL CONSTRUCTION (long-term building project):
  Duration: Salisbury (started 1220, substantially complete 1320) = 100 years
  Winchester: started 1079; work continued to 16th century
  Building accounts survive for some: wage records, material costs

  Construction sequence:
  → Choir and east end first (liturgical priority)
  → Nave last (congregation space)
  → Tower: often added/rebuilt multiple times (Ely octagon after 1322 collapse)

DENDROCHRONOLOGY IN CHURCH TIMBERS:
  Medieval roofs often retain original timbers
  → Dendro dates the original roof precisely
  Westminster Hall hammerbeam roof (1394–1399): oak from multiple counties
  → Ring patterns match English oak chronology exactly
  → Construction accounts match dendro dates → mutual corroboration

CHURCH ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Every church is a palimpsest of rebuilding
  Ground-penetrating radar: preceding structures under floor
  Floor tiles: Flemish, local tiles dated by motif sequences
  Human burials (within church): rank-ordered burial positions
  → Chancel: high-status; nave: merchant; churchyard: everyone else
```

---

**Supply chain and versioned infrastructure bridges**: The Hanseatic League (13th–17th c.) is a medieval supply chain network that solves the same problems as modern logistics: standardization (Lübeck barrel sizes for herring; Hamburg barrel sizes for beer), trust without central authority (merchant reputation systems enforced by exclusion from the network), multi-hub redundancy (Bergen, Bruges, London, Novgorod as the four Kontore), and network effects (monopoly pricing leverage through collective negotiation with rulers). The League's collapse follows the same pattern as platform lock-in failure: when alternative routes opened (Dutch bypassed the League; English merchants organized their own networks), the switching cost dropped below zero and the network disaggregated rapidly. Dendrochronology as a dating method has a direct engineering parallel: a dendro-dated timber is an immutable artifact with a tamper-evident timestamp (tree-ring sequences cannot be forged without growing the tree). Buildings containing dendro-dated timbers have an audit trail with single-year precision — closer to commit-stamped version control than to any document-based dating method. The "building biography" (combining dendro + documentary sources + architectural fabric analysis) is the medieval equivalent of reconstructing a service's deployment history from git log + monitoring data + infrastructure-as-code.

## Trade and Exchange

```
CERAMIC SEQUENCE AND TRADE:
  Pottery is the dating currency of medieval archaeology
  Local wares: can be sourced to specific production centers
  Imported wares: map trading networks

  KEY MEDIEVAL POTTERY:
  Developed Stamford ware (10th–12th c.): lead-glazed; first English glazed ware
  Medieval splash-glazed jugs: various local productions (London, Coventry, etc.)
  Saintonge ware (France): distinctive green-glazed; trade goods
  North German stoneware: Siegburg, Raeren; tough grey stoneware
    → Found across North Sea trade networks (Hanseatic League routes)
  Rhenish stoneware (Bellarmine jugs): 16th–17th c.; ubiquitous in N. Europe

HANSEATIC LEAGUE ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Lubeck, Hamburg, Bruges, London: German merchant trading network
  Bergen Bryggen: UNESCO site; timber waterfront buildings preserved
  → Dendrochronology of building timbers → precise dates
  → Goods found: herring barrels, wax seals, weights, Baltic amber
  → Written accounts + archaeology corroborate each other precisely

BULK GOODS:
  Salt: essential pre-refrigeration preservation; massive trade
  Wool: English wool → Flemish looms; staple wool trade
  Herrings: North Sea herring; preserved in salt; sold across Europe
  Grain: Baltic grain → western deficit areas
  → Medieval trade = bulk commodities, not luxury goods only
```

## Zooarchaeology of Medieval Economy

```
WHAT ANIMAL BONES TELL US:
  SPECIES PROPORTIONS:
    Cattle: meat + traction + dairy; expensive to raise
    Sheep: wool + milk + meat; common on all sites
    Pig: meat only (no secondary products); urban sites prefer
    Chicken, goose: domestic fowl; ubiquitous small scale
    Fish: marine and freshwater; increases through medieval period
    → Urban sites: more pig + fish (no pasture; import protein)
    → Rural sites: more cattle + sheep (local pasture)

  MORTALITY PROFILES:
    Cattle killed old → traction animals worked first; dairy cows
    Sheep: complex age profiles → combination of wool + meat strategies
    Cattle foot bones: suggest town disposal of hides (cobbler's waste)
      → Many feet from one site = hide-working nearby

  BUTCHERY EVIDENCE:
    Cut marks: position tells which joints, what order
    Cleaver vs. knife marks: volume processing vs. fine jointing
    Boiling vs. roasting indicators

  FISH BONES:
    Herring: split and salted; split bones = processing evidence
    Cod: heavily processed (stockfish = dried); vertebrae in London
    → Rises through medieval period as Christianity demands more fish days
    → Fasting practice → fish consumption → archaeological visibility
```

## The Written Record as Complement and Check

Medieval archaeology has more written records than prehistory — and the relationship between material culture and documents is complex.

```
WHERE DOCUMENTS AND ARCHAEOLOGY AGREE:
  Building accounts + dendro dates: construction timing
  Tax records (Domesday, 1086): settlement location + population
  Manor accounts: stock numbers, crops → compare with faunal assemblage
  Trade records: identify origin of goods found archaeologically

WHERE THEY CONTRADICT OR COMPLEMENT:
  Documents: usually describe elite and administrative life
  Archaeology: captures what was actually consumed, discarded, built
  → Manor accounts record grain stored; archaeology shows what was eaten
  → Church documents record religious life; archaeology shows ritual practice
    including folk practices (buried objects, unusual deposits) not in texts
  → Social history of peasant, artisan, servant: mainly archaeological
```

## The Black Death — Material Record

```
THE BLACK DEATH (1347–1353):
  Yersinia pestis (pneumonic + bubonic); killed 30–60% of European population
  Pandemic reached: Crimea → Sicily → France → England (1348)

ARCHAEOLOGICAL EVIDENCE:
  Mass graves: East Smithfield (London): emergency cemetery 1348–49
    → Rapid burial in rows, not randomly stacked
    → aDNA confirms Yersinia pestis in teeth (2011, 2016 studies)
    → Isotope analysis: local Londoners AND migrants from elsewhere
  Charterhouse Square (London, Crossrail): additional Black Death burials
  Danish mass graves: aDNA confirms Black Death variant

  POST-PLAGUE LANDSCAPE CHANGE:
    Settlement abandonment: many villages halved or disappeared
    Labor scarcity → peasant wages rose → Peasants' Revolt (1381) context
    Land use change: sheep (low labor) replaced arable (high labor)
    → Deserted medieval village wave → Wharram Percy type sites
    Skeletal evidence: skeletal stress markers in pre-plague populations
      → Signs of nutritional stress (famine of 1315–22) before plague
```

## Key Site Types

```
WATERLOGGED URBAN DEPOSITS:
  London (Southwark, Thames waterfront, City): Roman to medieval
    Preserved wooden structures, leather, plant remains
  York (Jorvik, Coppergate): Viking-age urban deposits
    → Excavated 1976–81; 35,000 objects; food remains; ectoparasites
    → Preserved fecal material → intestinal parasites (whipworm, roundworm)
    → Dietary reconstruction from the most intimate evidence

  Dublin (Wood Quay): 10th–12th c. Viking and Irish town
    → Waterlogged organic material; dendro dates for timber structures
    → Pre-Norman Dublin reconstructed from archaeology

STANDING BUILDING RECORDING:
  Many medieval structures still standing → "buildings archaeology"
  Survey + dendro + document research = building biography
  English Heritage (now Historic England): systematic recording
  Archaeological interventions during conservation → stratigraphic sections
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Use this when... | Method / concept | Notes |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------|
| A medieval timber structure survives (roof truss, floor beam, wattle) | Dendrochronology | Single-year precision; master sequences for NW Europe, Scandinavia, N. America; requires ~50+ rings for reliable match |
| Dating occupation layers in a medieval building | Pottery sequence analysis | Ceramic production centers, glaze types, and form sequences provide TPQ dates; pottery is the "commit timestamp" of medieval stratigraphy |
| Identifying the pathogen in a mass burial | aDNA from dental pulp (Yersinia pestis for Black Death) | Dental pulp survives well; pathogen DNA distinguishable from host; confirmed Black Death cause at East Smithfield (London) cemetery |
| Reconstructing medieval trade networks beyond documentary evidence | Distribution maps of imported pottery + coins + stable isotopes | Saintonge ware from SW France in English sites; North German stoneware across North Sea routes — maps Hanseatic trading geography |
| Understanding rural social history without elite documents | Deserted Medieval Village (DMV) excavation | Village layout, house sizes, animal bones → subsistence economy, social stratification without a single written source |
| Understanding why a village was abandoned | Multi-proxy analysis (pollen + zooarchaeology + documentary search) | Black Death, enclosure for sheep farming, and flooding are all archaeologically distinguishable by different assemblage signatures |
| Investigating a standing building's construction history | Buildings archaeology: dendro + architectural fabric analysis + documentary research | The building itself is the primary source; fabric phasing reads like version control |

## Common Confusion Points

**Medieval ≠ Dark Ages**: "Dark Ages" is an obsolete pejorative term for the Early Medieval period (c. 500–1000 AD). The period saw sophisticated trade networks, complex religious culture, technological innovation (heavy plow, horse collar, windmill), and political organization. "Dark" because of limited Latin literacy compared to Rome, not actual cultural darkness.

**Motte-and-bailey → stone keep**: The Norman conquest of England (1066) did not immediately produce stone castles. Initial conquest used rapid-build earth and timber mottes. Stone construction followed as permanence was needed. The sequence matters for interpreting early medieval fortification.

**Documentary record bias**: All medieval documents were written by literate people (clergy, royal administrators, merchants). Women, peasants, the enslaved, heretics, and Jews left few written records. The archaeological record partially corrects this — but also has its own biases toward durable materials.

**Waterlogged sites preserve what dry sites lose**: Leather, wood, basketry, textile, seeds, insects, parasites all survive in anaerobic waterlogged conditions. The Jorvik site would be nearly invisible on a dry site — just post-holes and a few metal objects. Excavation conditions radically affect what survives, not just what existed.
