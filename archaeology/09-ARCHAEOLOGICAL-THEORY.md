## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|           PARADIGM SHIFTS IN ARCHAEOLOGICAL THOUGHT             |
|                                                                  |
|  CULTURE HISTORY         PROCESSUALISM          POST-PROCESSUALISM|

  Each shift = anomalies accumulate → crisis → new paradigm
```

## Culture History (1880s–1960s)

### Core Assumptions

```
CULTURE HISTORY FRAMEWORK:
  "Culture" = a people defined by a set of shared artifact types
  → Pottery types, tool types, burial customs = "culture"
  → Different culture = different people
  → Culture change = migration or diffusion from another culture

  The "normative" view:
    Cultures have norms; artifacts conform to norms
    → Variation is deviation from a norm (problematic assumption)
    → Groups are internally homogeneous (also problematic)

  Chronological method: SERIATION
    Types appear, peak, decline in frequency
    → Used to order assemblages chronologically without absolute dates

  Spatial method: DISTRIBUTION MAPS
    Map where artifact types occur → culture area = people area
    → Cultura → people; artifact = ethnic marker
```

### Major Figures and Contributions

**V. Gordon Childe** (1892–1957): the synthesizer of European prehistory. Defined the "Neolithic Revolution" and "Urban Revolution" as major transformative stages. Despite Marxist sympathies, used largely diffusionist explanations in practice.

**Mortimer Wheeler** (1890–1976): standardized excavation technique; developed the Wheeler box-grid method; made archaeology public-facing (BBC television, India, Pakistan excavations).

**Franz Boas** (anthropological side): critiqued racial evolutionary theories; argued for cultural historical explanation of difference rather than biological hierarchy.

### Critique

```
WHAT CULTURE HISTORY GOT WRONG:
  → Artifact types ≠ ethnic groups (same artifact can be made by different peoples)
  → Change ≠ migration (local innovation possible)
  → Cultures are not internally homogeneous (variation is meaningful, not noise)
  → Diffusion is not explanation (WHY did a trait spread? what conditions enabled it?)

  But culture history produced: systematic sequences, typologies, regional syntheses
  → Many still used today as the chronological scaffolding
```

## Processualism / New Archaeology (1960s–1980s)

### The Revolution

**Lewis Binford** (1931–2011) is the central figure. His 1962 paper "Archaeology as Anthropology" is the manifesto:

```
BINFORD'S CRITIQUE OF CULTURE HISTORY:
  "The loss of motion in cultural systems should not be equated with
  an inevitable loss of information in the archaeological record."
  → Archaeology can study behavior, not just classify things

PROCESSUAL PROGRAM:
  1. Archaeology should be a science with generalizing laws
  2. Hypotheses should be explicitly stated and tested
  3. Culture = humanity's way of adapting to the environment
  4. Cultural change = adaptive response to environmental and demographic factors
  5. The archaeological record is a direct reflection of past behavior
     (the "Pompeiian premise" — overstated but influential)
```

### Systems Theory and Ecology

```
SYSTEMS APPROACH (David Clarke, Kent Flannery):
  Culture as a system: multiple interacting subsystems
  → Technological, social, religious, economic subsystems
  → Change in one affects others (feedback loops)
  → Adaptive behavior: negative feedback = stability; positive = change

  Flannery's "broad spectrum revolution" model:
    Pre-Neolithic: humans expanded dietary range (stressed resources)
    → Broad-spectrum exploitation → intensification → domestication
    → Quantitative/ecological model of agricultural origins

MIDDLE RANGE THEORY (Binford):
  The gap between observable present (living societies, modern refuse)
  and past archaeological record
  → Ethnoarchaeology: study living societies to understand past material
    culture formation processes
  → Taphonomy: understand post-depositional transformation of sites
  → Binford among Nunamiut Eskimo: understand bone assemblages from
    actual hunting and butchery behavior
```

### Quantitative Methods

Processualism brought statistics into archaeology:

```
QUANTITATIVE APPROACHES:
  Seriation as statistical matrix analysis
  Multivariate analysis (PCA, cluster analysis) of artifact assemblages
  Spatial statistics: site catchment analysis, Thiessen polygons, nearest neighbor
  Sampling theory: how much of a site must you excavate to be representative?
  Settlement pattern analysis: site hierarchy, rank-size rules
  GIS: computerized mapping (inherits processualist spatial focus)

  RANK-SIZE RULE (Zipf's law analog):
    In many settlement systems, city sizes follow a power law:
    rank × population = constant
    → Primate: one dominant center + much smaller followers
    → Log-normal: smooth distribution
    → Which pattern tells you about political organization
```

### Critique of Processualism

```
WHAT PROCESSUALISM GOT WRONG:
  → Culture ≠ just adaptation; humans have ideology, ritual, identity
  → The "Pompeiian premise": sites are NOT frozen behavior; taphonomy matters
  → Male-biased: processual models assumed male-produced material culture
  → Ignored agency: individuals and groups have choices, not just system responses
  → Claimed universalism while representing Western academic perspective
  → Treated indigenous peoples as data (ethnographic analogy) not as collaborators
```

## Post-Processualism (1980s–Present)

### Origins and Core Claims

Ian Hodder at Cambridge in the early 1980s developed the critique. His excavation at Çatalhöyük became the test case for post-processual methodology in practice.

```
POST-PROCESSUAL CORE CLAIMS:
  1. Objects have MEANING that cannot be read directly from function
     → An object = a message in a social context; meaning is constructed
  2. Individuals have AGENCY — they make choices that shape social structures
     → Not just system nodes; active social beings
  3. PAST PERSPECTIVE matters: we must try to understand past people's own
     categories and meanings, not impose ours
  4. ALL KNOWLEDGE is situated: the archaeologist's perspective (gender, race,
     class, nationality) shapes interpretation
     → "Reflexivity": acknowledge your position
  5. POLITICS ARE INESCAPABLE: whose past is it? who has rights to it?
     → Indigenous communities have claims on their ancestors' material culture
```

### Key Figures

**Ian Hodder** (1948–): developed contextual archaeology; Çatalhöyük excavation (Turkey, 1993–present) as methodological experiment.

**Michael Shanks and Christopher Tilley** (1987): "Re-Constructing Archaeology" — the most radical critique; argued archaeology is inherently political and cannot be objective.

**Henrietta Moore and colleagues**: feminist archaeology; gender as category of analysis; critiquing male-centric interpretations.

**Julian Thomas**: phenomenological approach; landscape and embodied experience.

### Çatalhöyük as Post-Processual Laboratory

```
ÇATALHÖYÜK (Turkey, 7500–5700 BC):
  Large Neolithic town (~3,500–8,000 people)
  No streets: houses accessed via rooftops through openings
  Houses contain burials under floors: ancestors in the house

  PRE-HODDER INTERPRETATION (culture history/processual):
    → Shrine rooms with bull heads = religious buildings
    → Social stratification evidence

  HODDER'S RE-INTERPRETATION (post-processual):
    → No clear public/private distinction; house = social unit
    → Bull heads: symbols within domestic ritual; not separate shrines
    → Burial under floors: living with ancestors
    → Multiple interpretations valid simultaneously (collaborative publication)

  METHODOLOGICAL INNOVATION:
    "Reflexive" recording: interpretations changed as excavation progressed
    Multiple specialists integrated from day 1 (not post-ex only)
    Local community and descendants included in research design
    Published on web in real time (early attempt at open archaeology)
```

## Indigenous Archaeology and NAGPRA

```
NAGPRA (Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990):
  US federal law requiring:
  → Federal institutions holding Native American cultural objects +
    human remains must inventory them
  → Consult with affiliated tribes
  → Repatriate objects/remains upon request

KENNEWICK MAN (1996):
  9,000-year-old skeletal remains found on Columbia River, Washington
  Claimed by several Native tribes for reburial; scientists wanted study
  Legal battle: resolved 2015; DNA study showed Native American ancestry
  → Repatriated to tribes; full study published before repatriation
  → Demonstrated: consultation possible + science + repatriation not mutually exclusive

BROADER PRINCIPLES:
  Indigenous communities are stakeholders in archaeology of their past
  "Community archaeology": descendant communities co-design research
  Repatriation: ongoing globally (Benin bronzes, Elgin Marbles, Maori heads)
  → Institutional resistance from museums holding major collections

POSTCOLONIAL ARCHAEOLOGY:
  Recognize colonial context of archaeological practice
  → 19th c. archaeology embedded in empire: collecting, classifying, controlling
  → Knowledge production about colonized peoples by colonizers
  → Decolonizing: local archaeologists, community control, local publication
```

## Current Theoretical Landscape

```
PLURALISM: no single dominant paradigm
  → Contextual (Hodder), feminist, indigenous, cognitive, evolutionary,
    Marxist, environmental, landscape, biographical approaches coexist

COGNITIVE ARCHAEOLOGY (Renfrew, Mithen):
  How did human cognitive evolution produce archaeological record?
  Symbolic thought, language, planning depth
  → Study material culture as evidence of cognitive capability

EVOLUTIONARY/BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY:
  Processualist-flavored; formal models of optimal foraging,
  patch choice, risk management
  → Quantitative but from adaptation logic; opposes pure post-processualism

BIOGRAPHICAL APPROACH:
  Objects have "biographies" (life histories): made, used, curated, discarded
  → Kopytoff: objects move through social contexts; meaning changes
  → Track object from production to deposition = full social life

DIGITAL AND COMPUTATIONAL:
  Machine learning applied to artifact classification
  Agent-based modeling: simulate social processes
  Network analysis: social network theory applied to artifact distribution
  → Processualism's quantitative impulse with modern tools
```

## Theory and Method: The Relationship

```
THEORY → METHOD → INTERPRETATION CHAIN:

  Culture History:
  Theory: people = artifact types → Method: typology + distribution maps
  → Interpretation: culture A spread to region B

  Processualism:
  Theory: culture = adaptation → Method: systems analysis, hypothesis testing
  → Interpretation: population stress caused dietary shift

  Post-Processualism:
  Theory: objects have contextual meaning → Method: contextual analysis,
    multiple specialist integration, reflexive recording
  → Interpretation: this deposit represents a ritual practice embedded in
    a specific cosmological framework

  The choice of theory determines what questions you can even ask.
  What looks like methodological debate is often theoretical debate in disguise.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| School | Key question | Core method | Key weakness |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Culture History | What? When? Where? | Typology + seriation | Artifact ≠ ethnic group |
| Processualism | Why? How? | Hypothesis testing + systems | Ignored meaning, agency |
| Post-Processualism | Whose meaning? Whose past? | Contextual, reflexive | Extreme relativism; hard to test |
| Current pluralism | All of the above | Mixed methods | No unifying framework |

## Common Confusion Points

**Paradigm shift does not mean earlier work was worthless**: Culture-historical sequences are still used. Processual quantitative methods are still used. Post-processual sensibility about politics and meaning is now mainstream. Paradigm shifts in archaeology add layers rather than replace everything.

**Post-processualism ≠ "anything goes"**: The extreme relativist position ("all interpretations are equally valid") was argued by Shanks and Tilley but widely critiqued even within the post-processual school. Hodder's contextual approach still privileges evidence; it just acknowledges multiple valid interpretations consistent with that evidence.

**Processualism was not neutral**: Processual claims to scientific objectivity masked assumptions: adaptation models often assumed male-dominated subsistence; systems models assumed equilibrium (stability as normal state); evolutionary analogies imported questionable assumptions from biology.

**NAGPRA applies only to federally funded institutions**: The law applies to federally funded museums and federal agencies. Private collections are not covered (a significant loophole). Non-US institutions are entirely outside NAGPRA (hence the Elgin Marbles debate is a different legal framework — no binding international equivalent of NAGPRA).

**The "Pompeiian premise" criticism**: Processualists sometimes wrote as if the archaeological record was a direct snapshot of behavior (like Pompeii — but even Pompeii is not simple). Post-processualists emphasized that the record is filtered through: taphonomy, burial practice, selection of what to keep, later disturbance. What you find ≠ what was there; what was there ≠ what people did.
