Classical archaeology sits at the intersection of material culture and textual record. The tension between the two is often the most productive space.

## Greek Urban Form

```
GREEK POLIS (CITY-STATE) PHYSICAL STRUCTURE:
  ACROPOLIS: fortified high city
    Sanctuary zone: temples to city's tutelary deity
    Athens: Parthenon (Athena), Erechtheion, Propylaia
    Visibility: deliberate siting for visual dominance of landscape

  AGORA: civic/commercial center below
    Open space for assembly and market
    Surrounded by: stoa (colonnaded halls), bouleuterion (council house),
      prytaneion (executive building), temples, law courts
    Athenian Agora: excavated by American School since 1931

  SANCTUARY (extra-urban):
    Panhellenic sanctuaries: Olympia, Delphi, Isthmia, Nemea
    → Where all poleis sent dedications; common religious identity
    → Olympia: 4-year Olympiad games since 776 BC (traditional)
    → Delphi oracle: political intelligence network; controlled by Delphic Amphictiony

TEMPLE ARCHITECTURE:
  Order: Doric (sturdy, no base), Ionic (scroll capitals), Corinthian (acanthus)
  Optical corrections: entasis (column swelling), stylobate curvature
    → Compensate for optical illusions at large scale
  Original color: temples were brightly painted; white marble is post-antique stripping

POTTERY CHRONOLOGY:
  Attic pottery: finest quality; widely exported; sequence well-dated
  Black-figure: dark figures on red ground (~630–480 BC)
  Red-figure: red figures on black ground (~530–350 BC; inverted technique)
  White-ground: white background; funeral use
  Stamp typologies and painter styles → decade-level dating
  → Find a red-figure amphora → you have a TPQ date for the context
```

## Roman Urban Form

```
ROMAN CITY PLANNING (standardized grid):
  CARDO MAXIMUS: N-S main street
  DECUMANUS MAXIMUS: E-W main street
  Intersection: forum (civic center)
  Insulae: city blocks between streets (apartment blocks)

FORUM: Roman equivalent of agora
  Basilica: covered hall for law courts and commerce
    → Christian basilica borrowed this secular building type
  Capitolium: temple to Jupiter, Juno, Minerva (state religion)
  Curia: senate house
  Rostra: speaker's platform
  Arches, columns: commemorative monuments

ROMAN INFRASTRUCTURE (engineered city):
  AQUEDUCTS: gravity-fed; Pont du Gard (Nîmes): 50 km system
    Gradient: ~0.3 m per km (precise slope maintenance)
    Underground tunnel + arcaded bridge sections
    Rome's 11 aqueducts: ~1 million m³/day delivery
  SEWERS: Cloaca Maxima (Rome): still operational
  HEATING: hypocaust (underfloor hot air) → bathhouses, wealthy homes
  ROADS: concrete core + stone surface; still used routes 2000 years later
    Milestones: distances from Forum Romanum in miles (1 mile = 1,000 paces)

INSULA (apartment block):
  Multi-story (4–7 stories); ground floor commerce; upper floors rental
  Built of concrete (opus caementicium) with brick facing
  Fire risk: Nero's fire (64 AD) destroyed much of Rome
  → Rebuilt to new fire code (Nero's building regulations)
  → One of earliest documented building codes
```

### Pompeii and Herculaneum — The Laboratory

```
VESUVIUS ERUPTION (79 AD):
  August 24–25, 79 AD: Plinian eruption
  Pompeii: buried under 4–6 m of volcanic ash and lapilli (airfall)
  Herculaneum: buried under 20 m of volcanic surge and pyroclastic flow
  Preservation: organic materials, food, graffiti, wall paintings, furniture

WHAT POMPEII SHOWS:
  Entire city plan excavated (1748–present)
  House of the Faun: wealthy private house with Alexander mosaic
  Street grid, fountains, stepping stones, ruts in road
  Graffiti: election advertisements, obscenities, love messages
    → First urban graffiti archive; direct voice of non-elite Romans
  Bakeries: loaves still in ovens; carbonized bread (Herculaneum)
  Thermopolia: street food counters with warm food niches (fast food bars)
  Brothel (lupanar): graffiti and paintings document services
  Garden remains: pollen + phytoliths reconstruct planted species

HERCULANEUM:
  Fewer excavations (town under modern Ercolano)
  Better preservation of organic material: wooden furniture, scrolls
  Villa dei Papiri: 2,000 papyrus scrolls (mostly Epicurean philosophy)
    → Multispectral imaging + machine learning (2023): reading without unrolling

PROBLEM: plaster casts
  Soft ash around decomposed bodies left voids → Fiorelli (1860s) injected plaster
  → Famous "body casts" are the shape of victims, not bones
  CT scanning casts + analysis of bones inside

CRITICAL LIMITATION:
  Pompeii is a frozen moment in time (79 AD) but often used to represent
  Roman urbanism generally → sampling bias: one city, one date, one eruption type
```

## Epigraphy

The study of inscriptions on durable materials (stone, metal, wood, ceramic).

```
EPIGRAPHIC CATEGORIES:
  MONUMENTAL: dedications, building inscriptions, honorific statues
    → Who funded what; political relationships; chronology
  LEGAL: laws, treaties, contracts on stone or bronze tablet
  FUNERARY: epitaphs; largest category; social history of names
  INSTRUMENTUM DOMESTICUM: stamps on pottery, tiles, lead pipes, amphorae
    → Potter stamps date pottery production; lead pipe stamps name plumbers
  DIPINTI/GRAFFITI: painted or scratched on walls
    → Pompeii graffiti: most complete ancient graffiti record

DATING BY LETTER FORMS:
  Greek lettering changed across centuries (Archaic → Classical → Hellenistic)
  Roman cursive on wax tablets vs. monumental incision differ
  Experienced epigraphers can date inscriptions ±25–50 years by style alone

LINGUISTIC CONTENT:
  Greek papyri (Egypt): everyday documents in Koine Greek
    → Tax receipts, personal letters, contracts, literary fragments
  Linear B tablets (Mycenae, Pylos): administrative records only
    → Inventories of goods, personnel, flocks → not literature
    → Deciphered 1952 by Michael Ventris (architect, autodidact)
    → Showed Mycenean Greeks spoke proto-Greek before Homer
```

## Numismatics

Coins are the most reliable TPQ (terminus post quem) dating tool in historical archaeology.

```
ROMAN COINS AS TPQ:
  Each coin was minted during a specific emperor's reign
  Coin can't be in a layer older than its mint date
  But: coins circulate for years/decades; worn coins may be much older than
    the layer they're found in (remember: TPQ not absolute date)

COIN HOARDS:
  Buried for safety; if never recovered → complete snapshot of coins in use
  HOARDS DATE EVENTS:
    British Domitian hoards (late 1st c. AD): Flavian distribution of money
    4th century hoards: barbarian raiding → wealth hidden, owner died
    Terminal Roman hoards: date of abandonment of Roman Britain (post 408 AD)

FINDS DISTRIBUTION MAPS:
  Map all findspots → visualize trade/administrative networks
  Roman coins in India → direct trade beyond written record
  Roman coins in Scotland → not occupation; gift exchange? loot?

DENARIUS DEBASEMENT:
  Silver content dropped: Augustus (95%) → Severus (50%) → Gallienus (<5%)
  XRF analysis of coins → track debasement over time
  → Economic history of Roman 3rd century crisis measurable in silver %
```

## Underwater Archaeology

```
OVERVIEW:
  Ships carried goods → sank → preserved under anaerobic seafloor sediments
  Submerged landscapes: coastal areas flooded post-LGM (sea level rose 120 m)
  Ports: silted up and submerged (Alexandria, Caesarea)

ANTIKYTHERA MECHANISM (c. 100 BC):
  Recovered by sponge divers, 1901, from shipwreck off Antikythera (Greek islands)
  First recognized as complex: X-ray/CT scanning 1950s–2006
  Result: an analog computer with 37 gears
  Function: predict astronomical positions, eclipses, Olympic cycle
  → Demonstrates Greek mechanical sophistication unknown from texts
  → No equivalent device appears in any other context for 1,000+ years
  → Reconstructed by Freeth, Wright et al.; mechanism still debated in detail

METHODOLOGY:
  SCUBA diving: depth limited (~40 m practical)
  Saturation diving: extended periods at depth
  ROV and AUV: robotic survey at abyssal depths
  Photogrammetry: 3D recording of entire wreck site from overlapping photos
  Site grid: same as terrestrial, underwater
  In situ recording: context = depth below seafloor + spatial relationship

MAJOR WRECKS:
  Uluburun (Turkey, 1400 BC): Bronze Age cargo; 10 tons copper ingots,
    tin ingots, glass ingots, Canaanite jars, ebony, ivory, Egyptian scarabs
    → Best single snapshot of Late Bronze Age trade network
  Antikythera: Greek luxury goods (bronze statues, mechanism); 1st c. BC
  Vasa (Sweden, 1628): complete warship; raised 1961; preserved in museum
  Mary Rose (England, 1545): Henry VIII flagship; raised 1982; museum in Portsmouth
  Black Sea Project: Bulgarian coast; oxygen-free water → extraordinary preservation
    (7,000-year-old hulls)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Classical texts are not truth**: The relationship between written sources and the archaeological record is complex. Texts were written by specific people with specific agendas. Tacitus wrote Roman imperial propaganda from a senatorial perspective. Thucydides was an exile from Athens. Always ask: who wrote this, for whom, with what purpose?

**Pompeii as "typical" Roman city**: Pompeii was a middle-tier Italian provincial town, not representative of Rome (massive), provincial cities (different), or the eastern empire (very different). Pompeii's excellent preservation has distorted the field toward Italian Roman urbanism.

**Optical corrections in Doric temples**: The Parthenon has no straight lines. The stylobate (floor) curves upward in the center; columns have entasis (swelling at 1/3 height) and lean inward slightly. These corrections are so subtle they're invisible to the casual observer but measurable. They were deliberate — either for visual reasons or structural stiffening (debate continues).

**Linear B is not the same as Greek**: Linear B records Mycenean Greek (~1400–1200 BC), which is ancestral to but distinct from Classical Greek. Homer (c. 800 BC) is in a different form. There's a 400-year dark age (1200–800 BC) during which writing was lost in the Aegean — Linear B was completely forgotten; the Classical Greek alphabet came from a different source (Phoenician alphabet adoption).
