## The Underground Comix Breakthrough (1968)

### Context and Emergence

```
1968: THE YEAR
  Vietnam War; assassinations (MLK, RFK); student uprisings (Paris, Columbia)
  San Francisco: Haight-Ashbury; Summer of Love ending; psychedelic culture
  Underground press: Berkeley Barb, East Village Other -- alternative newspapers
  Head shops: alternative distribution outside newsstand (no CCA enforcement)

ZAP COMIX #1 (ROBERT CRUMB, 1968)
  Crumb printed it himself; sold 5,000 copies from a baby carriage
  in Haight-Ashbury with his pregnant wife Dana
  Content: Fritz the Cat (satiric ); Mr. Natural (dropout sage); personal
  Drawing style: pen and ink, cross-hatching, cartoony grotesque
  Subject matter: sex, drugs, body horror, racial caricature
  (Crumb's racial caricatures remain deeply controversial and unresolved)

  WHAT ZAP PROVED:
  You could make comics outside the CCA system
  Distribution: head shops, alternative bookstores
  Audience: adults who grew up on comics and had no adult comics to read
  Economics: small press, self-financed, community-supported
```

### R. Crumb: The Contradiction

```
R. CRUMB (b. 1943)
  The central figure and the most contested one.

  INNOVATIONS:
  Autobiographical mode: the cartoonist's life as subject
  (not a hero, a normal anxious person)
  Raw honesty about sexuality, anxiety, self-loathing
  Dense, obsessively detailed drawing style
  "Confessional comics" as genre

  PROBLEMS:
  Racial caricature: Crumb drew Black characters in Sambo tradition
  Depictions of women: misogynistic; often degrading
  Crumb himself: acknowledges this; does not fully disavow

  THE CRITICAL PROBLEM:
  Crumb opened formal possibilities that led directly to:
  Spiegelman, Bechdel, Ware, Brown, Seth -- all learned from him
  You cannot write the history of literary comics without Crumb
  You cannot write it as celebration without the racial/gender problems
  Most serious critics do both simultaneously.
```

## Feminist Underground Comics

```
IT AIN'T ME BABE (1970)
  First all-women underground comix anthology
  Edited by: Trina Robbins

WIMMEN'S COMIX (1972-1992)
  Ongoing anthology; rotating editorial collective
  Over 20 issues; multiple cartoonists
  Content: women's experience; sexuality; anger; humor
  NOT available on newsstands; underground distribution only

WOMEN AND GIRLS CARTOONISTS (KEY FIGURES)
  Trina Robbins: political; historical; feminist fairy tales
  Aline Kominsky-Crumb: autobiographical; uncomfortable self-portrait
  Lynda Barry: childhood; memory; mixed text-image-drawing
  Mary Wings: lesbian detective fiction in comics
  Lee Marrs: political satire

  THE DOUBLE EXCLUSION:
  Underground comix: excluded women (male-dominated)
  Mainstream comics: excluded women except as secondary characters
  Women underground: neither underground nor mainstream
  Self-published outside both exclusions
```

## RAW Magazine and High Art Pretensions (1980s)

```
RAW MAGAZINE (1980-1991)
  Edited: Art Spiegelman + Francoise Mouly (New Yorker art director)
  Format: oversized; each issue different; luxurious printing
  Tone: "the graphix magazine for damned intellectuals"

  CONTENT:
  Serialized: "Maus" (Spiegelman's own work, starting 1980)
  International contributors: European BD, Japanese, experimental
  Gary Panter: psychedelic; punk-inflected; raw surface
  Ben Katchor: Julius Knipl, Real Estate Photographer; New York nostalgia
  Charles Burns: Black Hole; body horror; suburban dread
  Chris Ware: first RAW contributions

  SIGNIFICANCE:
  Made the argument: comics can be high art
  Connected: American alternative to European and Japanese traditions
  Trained the generation that would produce 2000s literary comics

MATT GROENING'S "LIFE IN HELL" (1977-2012)
  Self-published, then syndicated
  Binky the rabbit; Akbar and Jeff
  Strip that ran in alternative newspapers
  Led directly to: The Simpsons (Fox, 1989)
  First instance of alternative comics sensibility in mainstream animation
```

## Fantagraphics and the Literary Press (1980s-present)

```
FANTAGRAPHICS BOOKS (founded 1976 as magazine; press 1980s)
  Gary Groth and Kim Thompson; Seattle

  LOVE AND ROCKETS (LOS BROS HERNANDEZ, 1982-)
  Jaime Hernandez: Hoppers, California; Chicano working class; women characters
  Gilbert Hernandez: Palomar, Latin American magical realism in comics
  Punk rock culture; sexuality; aging; politics; community
  Published continuously 40+ years; genuinely literary serial comics

  HATE (Peter Bagge, 1990-1998)
  Buddy Bradley: Generation X slacker; Seattle; relationships
  Angry and funny; the 1990s alternative culture in comics

  DICK TRACY COMPLETE COLLECTIONS (Chester Gould): classic reprints
  Also: Prince Valiant, Popeye, Gasoline Alley -- archival function

  FANTAGRAPHICS AS INSTITUTION:
  Published: the complete Peanuts (25 volumes; definitive edition)
  Championed: work that mainstream publishers wouldn't touch
  Controversy: sued by Neal Adams (1983); survived industry pressure
  Critical role: reviewed comics seriously (The Comics Journal)
```

## Autobiographical Comics: The Major Tradition

```
WHY AUTOBIOGRAPHY BECAME THE DOMINANT ALTERNATIVE MODE

  INFLUENCE OF CRUMB: your life is the subject; no need for genre
  FEMINIST TRADITION: personal is political (Betty Friedan etc.)
  PUNK INFLUENCE: DIY; authenticity over polish; your voice over craft
  PRINT-ON-DEMAND / MINI-COMICS: cheap production; direct distribution

KEY AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL WORKS
  Chester Brown: "Yummy Fur" -> "I Never Liked You" (1994)
                 Childhood; sexuality; dissociation
                 "Louis Riel" (2003): Canadian history as comics biography

  Seth (Gregory Gallant): "It's a Good Life If You Don't Weaken" (1996)
                          Searching for a forgotten cartoonist; nostalgia; loss
                          Aesthetic: 1940s visual culture; slow pace

  Joe Matt: "Peepshow" -> "Spent" (2000)
            Pornography addiction; self-lacerating; deeply uncomfortable
            Matt as unreliable narrator of his own worst self

  Jeff Brown: "Clumsy" (2002): awkward romance; Drawn & Quarterly

  Adrian Tomine: "Summer Blonde" (2002); "Shortcomings" (2007)
                 Asian-American experience; urban alienation; realism

  Lynda Barry: "One Hundred Demons" (2002); "What It Is" (2008)
               Childhood; creativity; memory; mixed text and image
               Taught university courses on comics as creativity practice
```

## Memoir Comics and Cultural Expansion

```
THE MEMOIR BOOM (2000s)
  After Maus, personal and political memoir became the dominant
  literary comics form.

  PERSEPOLIS (Mariane Satrapi, 2000-2003)
  Iranian childhood; Islamic Revolution; exile in Europe
  Black and white; graphic novel format; published by L'Association (FR)
  English translation: Pantheon (2003-2004)
  Film adaptation (2007)
  Brought: non-Western political experience to global audience

  FUN HOME (Alison Bechdel, 2006)
  Bechdel: author of "Bechdel Test" (the 1985 strip criterion: movie with
           two named women who talk to each other about something other than a man)
  Fun Home: father's suicide; coming out as lesbian; family history
  Literary allusions: Joyce, Fitzgerald, Proust; deliberate
  Won National Book Critics Circle Award
  Musical adaptation (Broadway, 2015): first lesbian love story on Broadway

  BLANKETS (Craig Thompson, 2003)
  First love; evangelical Christian upbringing; loss of faith
  600pp; continuous warm drawing style; breakthrough for comics in bookstores

  THE BEST WE COULD DO (Thi Bui, 2017)
  Vietnamese refugee experience; intergenerational trauma; parenthood
  Watercolor; memoir; immigration story

  ARE YOU MY MOTHER? (Bechdel, 2012)
  Sequel to Fun Home; psychoanalysis; mother-daughter relationship
  Winnicott and object relations theory embedded in the narrative
```

## Punk Comics and Aesthetic Movements

```
GARY PANTER (b. 1950)
  Punk aesthetic applied to comics
  "Jimbo" (character); RAW magazine contributions; Pee-wee's Playhouse (sets)
  Scratchy, jagged, deliberately anti-clean line
  Response to: slick commercial comics and clean-line BD
  Influence: alternative cartoonists ever since

  THE PUNK AESTHETICS IN COMICS:
  Rough drawing valued over finish
  Personal expression over craft
  Ugliness as honesty
  Accessibility: anyone can make a mini-comic

CHARLES BURNS: "BLACK HOLE" (1995-2005)
  Teens; STD that produces mutations; suburban horror
  Pristine black-and-white technique; hyper-clean line (anti-punk)
  Subject matter: horror; sexual anxiety; conformity vs. freakdom
  Technically opposite to Panter in approach; similar in underground ethos

MINI-COMICS AND ZINE CULTURE
  Xerox machine + stapler = publishable comic
  Mini-comics: staple-bound; small print runs; passed hand to hand
  Active in 1980s-2000s; now partly replaced by webcomics
  Physical object; community artifact; not for mainstream
  Many now-canonical cartoonists started in mini-comics:
  Chris Ware; Jeffrey Brown; Lynda Barry; Tom Hart
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Alternative comics = political"** -- Some are explicitly political (feminist
underground, Persepolis). Many are not: Seth's nostalgic formalism, Chester
Brown's sexual anxiety, and Craig Thompson's evangelical memoir are personal
not politically programmatic. "Alternative" = outside the genre mainstream,
not necessarily engaged with identity politics.

**"Underground comix were marginal"** -- Zap Comix sold millions of copies
through head shops in the 1960s-70s. They were mainstream entertainment for
a counterculture audience of millions. "Underground" refers to the distribution
system (not through Comics Code-compliant newsstand distribution), not to
limited readership.

**"R. Crumb's racial caricatures should be excised from history"** -- They
cannot be excised; they are woven into the formal innovation. The critical
reading must hold both: the formal breakthrough AND the racism, without
resolving the tension by ignoring either. This is the standard challenge
of historically significant art made by complicated people.

**"Memoir comics are navel-gazing"** -- At their worst, yes. At their best
(Maus, Fun Home, Persepolis), the personal story opens onto larger historical
and political realities. The argument FOR autobiography in comics: the formal
constraint (you can only draw what you experienced, roughly) creates credibility
and honesty unavailable to genre fiction.
