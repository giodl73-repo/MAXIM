## Pre-History: Sequential Images Before Comics

Comics did not arrive fully formed in 1895. The form has precursors reaching back
centuries.

```
BAYEUX TAPESTRY (c. 1077)
  Embroidered linen, ~70 meters long
  Sequential narrative: 1066 Norman Conquest of England
  Left to right reading direction; Latin captions
  Continuous narrative (not panel-separated)
  Not a comic: no clear panel divisions, no gutter
  BUT: sequential visual narrative; text + image; read in order

WILLIAM HOGARTH (1697-1764)
  "A Rake's Progress" (1735): 8 paintings in sequence
  "Marriage A-la-Mode" (1743-45): 6 paintings
  Designed as series to be read sequentially
  Moral satire through visual narrative
  Engraved and sold widely: mass distribution of sequential narrative
  McCloud cites Hogarth as foundational precursor

RODOLPHE TOPFFER (1799-1846) -- THE REAL ORIGIN POINT
  Swiss cartoonist and educator
  "Histoire de Mr. Jabot" (1827, published 1833)
  "Dr. Festus" (1829); "Monsieur Cryptogame" (1830)
  WHAT HE INVENTED:
  - Panels with clear borders
  - Captions below images
  - Simple, expressive line drawing
  - Narrative sequence driven by image, not text
  Goethe praised his work; Topffer = legitimate precursor of modern comics
  His albums were widely pirated in US and Europe (first comics piracy)
```

## The Newspaper Strip Era (1895-1940s)

### The Yellow Kid and the Origin Story

```
RICHARD OUTCAULT'S "YELLOW KID" (1895)
  New York World (Pulitzer); then New York Journal (Hearst)
  Character: bald child in yellow nightshirt; urban slum setting
  Was this the first comic strip?
  Arguments for: speech balloon (in color; character's nightshirt)
                 sequential if read across runs
  Arguments against: early strips were single images, not panels

  IMPORTANCE: the "Yellow Kid Wars" between Pulitzer and Hearst
  showed that popular characters could sell newspapers.
  The economics of comics = newspaper circulation weapon.
  This never changed: media uses comics as reader-magnets.
```

### The Great Newspaper Strips

```
LITTLE NEMO IN SLUMBERLAND (Winsor McCay, 1905-1914, 1924-1926)
  New York Herald; New York American
  About: a boy's dreamscapes; wakes up last panel always
  Significance: Visually extraordinary; architectural dream imagery
               McCay = one of the greatest visual imaginations in comics
               Each page complete; not narrative serial

  KRAZY KAT (George Herriman, 1913-1944)
  New York Evening Journal
  About: Kat loves Ignatz Mouse; Ignatz throws bricks at Kat;
         Officer Pupp tries to arrest Ignatz; Kat loves being hit
  Significance: Language play, poetry, surrealism; racial ambiguity
               E.E. Cummings wrote an essay praising it
               Gilbert Seldes called it the greatest American art form
               Ran until Herriman's death; Hearst personally kept it running
               despite limited circulation

  GASOLINE ALLEY (Frank King, 1918-present)
  First strips to age characters in real time
  Characters born; grew up; had children
  Narrative continuity across decades

  PEANUTS (Charles Schulz, 1950-2000)
  Charlie Brown's existential uncertainty; Snoopy's fantasy life
  Ran 17,897 strips in 50 years
  Schulz drew every strip himself until his death
  Philosophical depth in 3-4 panels; profound loneliness of children
```

## The Comic Book Era (1938-)

### The Advent of the Superhero

```
ACTION COMICS #1 (Jerry Siegel / Joe Shuster, June 1938)
  Superman: first superhero as understood by the genre
  Origin: mild-mannered reporter; disguise; super-powers; fights crime
  Costume: visual distinction from civilian identity
  Jewish immigrant creators; fantasy of strength against oppression
  Historical reading: created while Hitler rose; wish-fulfillment

  WHAT SUPERHERO ADDED TO COMICS:
  Serialized ongoing characters
  Shared universe (eventually)
  Power fantasy tied to moral framework
  Visual iconography (cape, mask, logo) as commercial asset

WORLD WAR II COMICS
  Captain America #1 (1941): punches Hitler on cover
  Comics: central wartime entertainment medium
  Sold millions of copies
  Military: distributed to troops (cheap, portable)
  Post-war: market collapses; genres diversify (horror, crime, Western)
```

### Fredric Wertham and the Comics Code Authority

```
FREDRIC WERTHAM'S "SEDUCTION OF THE INNOCENT" (1954)
  Psychiatrist; argued comics caused juvenile delinquency
  Batman and Robin: "homosexual fantasy"
  Wonder Woman: lesbian subtext; sadism
  Horror comics (EC Comics, William Gaines): caused violence
  Evidence: cherry-picked; methodology later discredited
  But: created a moral panic

  1954: US Senate Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency
  Testimony by Gaines and others; enormous media coverage

  COMICS CODE AUTHORITY (CCA, October 1954)
  Self-regulatory body; created by publishers to avoid legislation
  Rules: no crime "pay off"; no disrespect for authority;
         no "walking dead" / vampires / werewolves in titles;
         marriage respected; no sexual deviation;
         drugs shown only negatively

  RESULT:
  EC Comics (Gaines): cancelled all horror/crime comics
  -> Founded Mad Magazine (satire, not comics-code-covered)
  -> Horror comics revived only in underground (1960s)

  Comics market shrinks; superhero remains as "safe" genre
  The Code castrated the medium for 15+ years.
```

## The Underground Comix Revolution (1960s-1970s)

### The Environment

```
CONTEXT
  1960s counterculture; Vietnam War; civil rights movement
  Hippie press, alternative newspapers, head shops
  "Off-underground" distribution (record stores, head shops)
  No Comics Code Authority enforcement outside newsstand distribution

  R. CRUMB (b. 1943)
  "Zap Comix #1" (1968); self-published; sold from a baby carriage
  in Haight-Ashbury, San Francisco
  Fritz the Cat; Mr. Natural; Crumb himself as character
  Explicitly sexual; drug-related; autobiographical
  Raw, confessional, self-lacerating
  Crumb: the superhero tradition was false; my life is my subject

  EXPLICIT CHALLENGES TO CCA:
  Sexual content: forbidden by Code
  Drug culture: Code required negative portrayal; undergrounds celebrated it
  Authority figures: satirized/mocked; Code required respect
  The underground was specifically against the Code's values

  OTHER UNDERGROUND CARTOONISTS:
  Gilbert Shelton: The Fabulous Furry Freak Brothers
  Robert Williams: psychedelic machinery
  Spain Rodriguez: motorcycle gangs; political radicalism
  Trina Robbins: women underground cartoonists; feminist perspective
```

## European BD: A Parallel Tradition

```
TINTIN (Herge / Georges Remi, 1929-1986)
  Published in: Le Vingtieme Siecle (Catholic newspaper), Belgium
  Boy reporter; global adventures; Snowy the dog; Captain Haddock
  Line clair (clear line) style: clean outlines; flat color; no hatching
  Highly influential on all European comics that followed
  Problems: early Tintin albums = racist colonial-era caricatures
           (Congo, America) -- Herge later acknowledged and partially revised

ASTERIX (Goscinny / Uderzo, 1959-present)
  Village of Gauls resists Roman occupation; Asterix + Obelix
  Satire of contemporary French society via historical setting
  Sold 380+ million albums; second most sold European comic character

MOEBIUS (Jean Giraud, 1938-2012)
  "Blueberry" (Western; 1963+) under Giraud; SF/fantasy as Moebius
  "The Incal" with Jodorowsky (1980): SF classic
  Visual influence: every SF concept artist since; Star Wars, Alien, Tron
  Dense detail; surreal color; philosophical SF

BD FORMAT:
  Hardcover albums (48pp usually); full color; A4 size
  Published as complete albums (not serialized floppies first)
  Different commercial model: bookstore distribution
  France: annual BD festival (Angouleme) = most prestigious comics award globally
```

## The Literary Turn (1978-1992)

```
WILL EISNER'S "A CONTRACT WITH GOD" (1978)
  First book to use "graphic novel" on the cover
  Four interconnected stories; Depression-era Bronx; Jewish immigrant life
  No superheroes; no genre convention; adult subject matter
  Eisner's argument: comics can carry literary content

  EISNER'S EARLIER WORK:
  "The Spirit" (1940-1952): newspaper supplement; crime stories
  Innovative splash pages; expressionistic; film noir influence
  Considered foundational work for visual storytelling technique

WATCHMEN (Alan Moore / Dave Gibbons, 1986-87, DC Comics)
  Superhero deconstruction: what if superheroes existed?
  Cold War nuclear anxiety; moral complexity; non-linear structure
  9-panel grid used systematically for control / counterpoint
  Time Magazine "100 Best Novels" list (English language, 20th century)

DARK KNIGHT RETURNS (Frank Miller, 1986, DC)
  Aged, authoritarian Batman in near-future
  Violent; ideologically complex; not for children
  Reagan-era America critiqued through superhero lens
  With Watchmen: broke assumption that comics = juvenile

  RESULT OF 1986-1992:
  Comics entered literary/cultural discourse
  "Graphic novel" = shorthand for "comics taken seriously"
  Mass-market bookstores began stocking comics
  Pulitzer for Maus (1992): ultimate cultural legitimation
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Comics started with superheroes"** -- Newspaper strips predate comic books by
decades. Rodolphe Topffer's proto-comics predate newspaper strips by 60 years.
The form has a much longer history than the superhero genre suggests.

**"The Comics Code Authority destroyed bad comics"** -- The CCA suppressed
genuinely interesting adult material (EC's horror and crime comics, which were
well-crafted) and protected a narrow ideology of American normality. It was
censorship with commercial enforcement.

**"Tintin is a children's comic"** -- Tintin was first published in an adult
Catholic newspaper aimed at adult readers. It became associated with children
in subsequent decades. The same is true of much "children's literature": the
audience category was assigned retrospectively.

**"Underground comix were just pornographic"** -- Underground comix produced
genuinely innovative formal work. Crumb's autobiographical mode opened avenues
that led directly to Art Spiegelman, Alison Bechdel, Chris Ware. The sex and
drugs were part of the rejection of CCA authority; the formal innovation was
equally important.
