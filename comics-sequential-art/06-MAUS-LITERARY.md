# Maus and Comics' Literary Turn

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                   MAUS: KEY FACTS                                 |
|                                                                  |
|  CREATOR: Art Spiegelman (b. 1948, Stockholm; grew up NYC)       |
|  SERIALIZATION: RAW Magazine, 1980-1991                          |
|  PUBLISHED: Vol 1 (1986, Pantheon); Vol 2 (1991, Pantheon)       |
|  AWARD: Pulitzer Prize Special Citation (1992)                   |
|         First (and for years only) comic to win a Pulitzer       |
|                                                                  |
|  SUBJECT: Vladek Spiegelman's Holocaust survival (Auschwitz)     |
|           AND: Art interviewing his elderly father in the 1970s  |
|           DUAL NARRATIVE: past and present simultaneously        |
|                                                                  |
|  DEVICE: Ethnic groups as animals                                |
|           Jews = mice; Nazis = cats; Poles = pigs                |
|           Americans = dogs; French = frogs                       |
|                                                                  |
|  STRUCTURE: A comic about Holocaust AND about making a comic      |
|             about the Holocaust                                   |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Spiegelman's Context: Before Maus

```
ART SPIEGELMAN'S BACKGROUND
  Son of Polish Holocaust survivors
  Mother: Anja Spiegelman; committed suicide in 1968
  Father: Vladek Spiegelman; survived Auschwitz; remarried

  BREAKDOWNS (1972)
  Three-page comic about his mother's suicide
  Autobiographical; psychologically raw; proto-Maus mode
  Important early work establishing: your life is your subject

  UNDERGROUND COMIX GENERATION
  Spiegelman contributed to: Short Order Comix, Bijou Funnies
  Influenced by: Crumb; but more interested in formal experiment
  RAW MAGAZINE (with Francoise Mouly): launched 1980
  Platform for: himself and the generation that would become
  literary comics' primary authors

  "MAUS" FIRST VERSION (1972-73)
  Three-page strip in "Funny Aminals" (Apex Novelties)
  Short; proof of concept
  The animal metaphor was already there
  The interview structure (son talks to father) was not yet developed
```

## The Structure of Maus

### The Double Narrative

```
PRESENT STORY (1970s-80s)
  Art visits his father Vladek in Rego Park, Queens
  Records conversations (Art has a tape recorder)
  Vladek talks; Art draws what Vladek says happened
  Art deals with: difficult father; grief for mother; his own Jewishness
  The framing narrative is ALSO about the process of making Maus

PAST STORY (Poland, 1930s-1945)
  Vladek's story: pre-war Sosnowiec; courtship of Anja; persecution
  Business, family, survival strategies; hiding; ghetto; Auschwitz
  Precise; detailed; obsessive memory (Vladek remembers everything)

THE FORMAL INSIGHT:
  Both stories are present on the page simultaneously.
  Past and present are both happening NOW, for the reader.
  The frame story is NOT separate from the Holocaust story.
  They contaminate each other:
  Art's difficulty with his father (present) informs
  the reader's understanding of Vladek's survival psychology (past).
  The Holocaust shaped Vladek; Vladek shaped Art.
  The comic is the circuit.
```

### The Animal Metaphor

```
WHY ANIMALS?
  The standard reading: using Nazi racial classification as
  visual metaphor to examine the logic of racial classification.
  If Jews are mice and Nazis are cats -- it's obvious nonsense.
  The animals externalize the absurdity of racial essentialism.

  BUT SPIEGELMAN KNEW IT WAS MORE COMPLICATED:
  Volume 2, "And Here My Troubles Began":
  A French woman is shown as a mouse (because she is Jewish)
  A Pole who helps them: also a mouse when playing a Jew
  Spiegelman is shown sitting at his drawing board, surrounded by
  media attention, wearing a mouse mask over his human face.

  THE METACOMMENTARY:
  "Am I a mouse because I'm Jewish?"
  "What does it mean to draw my father as a mouse?"
  "Is this appropriating his experience?"
  "The mouse mask hides my human face -- what does that mean?"

  Spiegelman refuses to let the animal metaphor be simple.
  The book critiques its own formal device from within.
```

## Spiegelman vs. "Survivor Guilt" by Proxy

```
THE SON-OF-SURVIVORS PROBLEM
  Spiegelman did not survive Auschwitz. His father did.
  What is his right to tell this story?
  Is he using his father's suffering for his own artistic project?
  Does his work simplify and aestheticize what should be unspeakable?

  SPIEGELMAN ADDRESSES THIS IN THE TEXT:
  Chapter "Prisoner on the Hell Planet" (Vol 1):
  A different style (expressionist; woodcut aesthetic)
  Art in a striped prison uniform: "PRISONER OF WAR"
  About his mother's suicide: Art is also a prisoner of the Holocaust
  even without having been there.

  "MEIN KAMPF" (German title; Spiegelman's pun / provocation):
  Volume 2's original title referencing Hitler's book
  Spiegelman: the act of representing the Holocaust in comics
  is fraught; he is owning the fraught-ness.

THE METAFICTIONAL CHAPTER (Vol 2, Chapter 2)
  Spiegelman is shown at his drawing board, overwhelmed by media attention.
  He is wearing a mouse mask. Real mice are on his desk.
  Critics are calling Maus everything from brilliant to exploitative.
  His therapist is himself a Holocaust survivor.
  Vladek is dead; Art is drawing from memory and tape recordings now.
  The chapter is about: what does it mean to make this book?
  This self-interrogation is what distinguishes Maus from memoir-as-therapy.
```

## The Pulitzer and What It Meant

```
1992 PULITZER PRIZE SPECIAL CITATION
  First time a comic received a Pulitzer
  The Pulitzer board: did not fit existing categories (fiction, biography)
  Created a "Special Citation" for Maus

  WHAT IT SIGNALED:
  Comics can carry serious cultural weight
  The literary establishment was acknowledging the form
  Not just "this content happens to be serious" but
  "this form is serious when used with sufficient ambition"

  WHAT IT DID NOT SETTLE:
  The Pulitzer is cultural capital within a specific (American, literary) field.
  Comics were already respectable in France, Belgium, Japan.
  The US literary establishment was catching up.

POST-MAUS PUBLISHING SHIFT
  Pantheon (mainstream literary press) had published Maus as a book.
  After the Pulitzer:
  Mainstream publishers began acquiring literary comics
  Bookstores created "graphic novel" sections
  Marjane Satrapi's Persepolis (2000-2003): published by L'Association (FR),
  then Pantheon English translation (2003-2004)
  Alison Bechdel's Fun Home: Houghton Mifflin (2006)
  The literary press accepted comics as literary content delivery.
```

## The Holocaust Representation Problem

```
THEODOR ADORNO'S CHALLENGE (paraphrased):
  "To write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric."
  (From "Prisms", 1955)
  The meaning: can art represent the unrepresentable?
  Does aestheticizing atrocity reduce and falsify it?
  Does making the Holocaust into a story make it "palatable"?

CLAUDE LANZMANN AND "SHOAH" (1985)
  9.5-hour documentary film; NO archival footage
  Lanzmann's position: showing images of the camps aestheticizes them
  Only survivor and bystander testimony; present-day landscape
  This was the "proper" way to represent the Holocaust?

SPIEGELMAN'S COUNTER-POSITION (through Maus):
  Comics' gutter = space where the unrepresentable lives
  You cannot depict the gas chambers directly; Maus doesn't
  The mouse metaphor creates DISTANCE that allows approach
  (Direct depiction may traumatize; the animal mask permits
   approach to what would otherwise be unbearable)
  The frame story (present-day Art) constantly reminds the reader
  this is mediated; constructed; uncertain

  MAUS DOES NOT CLAIM TO SHOW THE HOLOCAUST.
  It claims to show Art talking to Vladek about Vladek's memory of it.
  Three removes from the event: the event -> Vladek's memory ->
  Art's reconstruction -> the comic you're reading.
  This epistemological honesty is the ethical response to Adorno.
```

## The Bequest: What Maus Made Possible

```
DIRECT INFLUENCES
  Persepolis (Satrapi, 2000): political memoir; foreign country; animals absent
  Maus showed: non-fiction personal-political memoir is a comics genre
  Persepolis uses B&W; clean style; Persian decorative patterns as texture

  Fun Home (Bechdel, 2006): literary allusion; complex temporality; self-examination
  Maus showed: comics can carry literary apparatus (allusion, irony, framing)
  Fun Home = Maus + feminist literary tradition

  Jimmy Corrigan (Ware, 2000): formal complexity; painful psychology; fragmentation
  Maus opened the door to: serious formal experiment with painful content

  March (John Lewis / Andrew Aydin / Nate Powell, 2013-2016)
  Civil rights memoir; official Congressional Medal of Honor reading
  Maus as template: historical memoir from within; dual temporality

THE CRITICAL CONSENSUS BY 2024:
  Maus is the Ulysses of comics:
  Not because everyone reads it (they don't)
  But because you cannot understand the form's possibilities
  without understanding what it proved about those possibilities.
```

## The Tennessee School Board Incident (2022)

```
JANUARY 2022
  McMinn County Board of Education (Tennessee) voted 10-0 to remove
  Maus from an 8th-grade Holocaust curriculum.

  REASONS CITED:
  - Profanity (8 specific curse words)
  - Nudity (a drawing of Spiegelman's mother nude in a bathtub
    -- from the suicide chapter)

  ACTUAL EFFECT:
  International news coverage
  Maus sales spiked immediately to #1 on Amazon
  Publisher Pantheon ran a special edition
  Tennessee residents donated copies to libraries
  This is not the school board's intended outcome.

  SIGNIFICANCE:
  Censorship attempts on serious Holocaust education comics
  continue a tradition of literary suppression
  that historically only increases readership and legitimacy.
  The incident demonstrated: Maus remains culturally charged,
  40+ years after its first publication.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Maus is a Holocaust memoir"** -- Maus is a memoir about Art interviewing his
father about the Holocaust. The subject is not only Vladek's Holocaust experience;
it is equally Art's reconstruction of that experience, his difficult relationship
with his father, and the ethics of Holocaust representation in comics. All of
this is equally "the book."

**"The animal metaphor makes the Holocaust cute or simple"** -- The animal
metaphor makes it bearable to read, then immediately questions whether "bearable"
is appropriate. Spiegelman uses the device and interrogates the device in the
same work.

**"The Pulitzer made comics legitimate"** -- The Pulitzer made comics legitimate
to American literary culture specifically. European BD and Japanese manga had
different legitimacy structures. The Pulitzer was a late arrival to a form that
had been serious elsewhere for decades.

**"You can use Maus as a template for serious subjects in comics"** -- You can
learn from it. But Maus is also about what makes Maus possible: Spiegelman's
specific relationship to the specific subject of the Holocaust and to his specific
father. Copying its structure without equivalent subject-matter specificity
produces imitation, not equivalent depth.
