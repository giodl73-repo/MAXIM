# McCloud's "Understanding Comics": Closure and Transitions

## The Big Picture

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|          SCOTT McCLOUD'S "UNDERSTANDING COMICS" (1993)           |
|                                                                  |
|  FORMAT: A comic book about comics (recursive self-reference)    |
|  METHOD: Demonstrates claims through the medium it analyzes      |
|                                                                  |
|  CENTRAL CLAIMS:                                                 |
|  1. Comics = "juxtaposed pictorial and other images in           |
|     deliberate sequence" (definition)                            |
|  2. Closure: reader fills the gap between panels                 |
|  3. Panel transitions: 6 types; varying cognitive demand         |
|  4. Blood in the gutter: violence as participatory experience    |
|  5. Time and space: same thing in comics                         |
|  6. Icon vs. reality: abstraction amplifies identification       |
|                                                                  |
|  SIGNIFICANCE: First systematic theory of the medium             |
|  Limitation: Written before manga globalization; internet comics  |
|  Sequels: "Reinventing Comics" (2000); "Making Comics" (2006)    |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## The Definition Problem

McCloud begins by defining comics precisely because the medium lacked a theoretical
account of itself.

```
McCLOUD'S DEFINITION CONSTRUCTION
  Start: "Comics: Plural noun. Magazines and newspapers
          containing comic strips." (dictionary definition)

  Problem: this defines by historical instance, not by form.
  Hogarth's prints: sequential. Are they comics?
  Hieroglyphs: sequential images with text. Are they comics?
  Wordless picture books: sequential images. Are they comics?
  Film: sequential images, 24/second. Are they comics?

  McCLOUD'S MOVE:
  Focus on the formal principle that makes comics distinctive:
  JUXTAPOSITION: the images are next to each other on the page
  (or screen; or surface)
  SEQUENCE: they are read in order
  DELIBERATE: the arrangement is intended to convey meaning

  EXCLUDES:
  Film (sequential but temporal, not spatial)
  Single illustrations (spatial but not sequential)
  Photo series (sequential if deliberate -- could be comics)

  INCLUDES:
  Bayeux Tapestry
  Hogarth's moral progress series
  Ancient Egyptian friezes (possibly)
```

## Closure: The Central Concept

Closure is the most important theoretical claim in "Understanding Comics."

```
DEFINITION:
  "The phenomenon of observing the parts but perceiving the whole."

  You see: Panel A (person raises fist) and Panel B (person on floor)
  You infer: the punch, the moment of impact, the fall
  That inference is CLOSURE.

  The reader CREATES the action in the gutter.
  The gutter = reader participation space.
```

### The Blood in the Gutter

```
McCLOUD'S EXAMPLE:
  Panel 1: Man with raised axe says "Now you die!"
  Panel 2: Night sky over a city; distant scream
  [Between panels: nothing]

  What happened in the gutter?
  The reader's mind completed the murder.
  The READER killed the man.

  McCLOUD'S CLAIM:
  "I did it!" (meaning: the reader participated)
  In film: the act is shown; viewer is passive
  In comics: the reader is an ACTIVE PARTICIPANT in the narrative

  This is why:
  - Comics violence can feel more intense than film violence
    (you completed it; it's yours)
  - Horror comics work differently from horror film
  - Intimacy between reader and narrative is structural
```

## The Six Panel Transitions

McCloud identifies six types of transitions between panels, ranging from highest
to lowest reader cognitive participation:

```
TYPE 1: MOMENT-TO-MOMENT
  From one action to the very next instant.
  Example: a ball in flight -> same ball slightly further
  Reader participation: minimal (nearly film-like)
  Use: slow down time; show detail; pause for emphasis
  Common in: manga (more common than Western comics)

TYPE 2: ACTION-TO-ACTION
  Single subject; one action to the next action
  Example: pitcher winds up -> ball in flight
  Reader participation: low-moderate
  THE MOST COMMON TYPE in Western comics
  Time compressed; efficient narration

TYPE 3: SUBJECT-TO-SUBJECT
  Different subjects; within a scene or idea
  Example: A speaking -> B listening; crime scene -> detective
  Reader participation: moderate
  Forces reader to infer relationship between subjects
  THE MOST COMMON TYPE in manga

TYPE 4: SCENE-TO-SCENE
  Different locations and/or time periods
  Example: last panel in city -> first panel in jungle (years later)
  Reader participation: high (infer duration, cause, relationship)
  Narrative time compression; common in literary comics

TYPE 5: ASPECT-TO-ASPECT
  Different aspects of a scene; no clear time progress
  Example: clock, window, coffee cup, street -- establishing a mood
  Reader participation: high (reader builds spatial/temporal impression)
  RARE in Western comics; COMMON in manga (particularly Otomo, Miyazaki)
  Creates atmosphere; slows time nearly to stop

TYPE 6: NON-SEQUITUR
  No logical or narrative connection
  Example: random images in sequence
  Reader participation: very high (reader must construct meaning)
  Rare; experimental; Dada-adjacent when used deliberately
```

## Time and Space: The Same Thing in Comics

```
TIME IS SPACE IN COMICS
  In film: time passes as you watch; you cannot control pace
  In prose: duration is implied by text quantity and tense
  In comics: more panels = more time; fewer panels = less time
             but BOTH exist on the page simultaneously

  A 6-panel page with 3 caption boxes of text = different duration
  than a 6-panel page with no text.
  The reader controls pace; can re-read; can linger; can skip.

PANEL SIZE AND DURATION
  Large panel: implies longer moment; important; slow time down
  Small panel: brief; quick; trivial detail
  Narrow horizontal panel: a flash; blink; reaction
  Tall vertical panel: falling; height; power differential

  EXCEPTION: McCloud notes this is not absolute.
  A large silent panel can be a very short moment.
  A small panel can contain an entire year's summary.
  Size signals importance more reliably than duration.

BLEEDING PANELS
  A panel that extends to the page edge
  = no boundary = timeless moment
  OR: extending beyond the frame of the page = beyond reality
  Used for: splashes of action; dreams; deaths; overwhelming events
```

## The Triangle of Abstraction

McCloud proposes a vocabulary map for visual styles:

```
McCLOUD'S IMAGE TRIANGLE

        REALISTIC IMAGES
             /\
            /  \
           /    \
          /      \
    ABSTRACT ---  ICONIC (simplified)
    (non-        (cartoon faces,
    representational) symbols)

The triangle describes where any visual representation falls.

REALISTIC: detailed, photorealistic
  Example: Alex Ross paintings; photo-realistic illustration
  Reader identifies less with the character
  (it looks like a specific person, not like "a person")

ICONIC / SIMPLIFIED:
  Big eyes, small nose; simple shapes; Peanuts characters
  Reader projects their own face onto the character
  "The more cartoony a face, the more people it could be"
  This is why: Mickey Mouse; Tintin; Charlie Brown
  are universally identifiable despite minimal detail

ABSTRACT:
  Non-representational; pure design
  Requires different cognitive mode to interpret

McCLOUD'S CLAIM:
  Simplification -> identification
  The less specific the face, the more universal
  This explains why cartoons have broader audience appeal
  than realistic illustration (for character comics)
```

## The Icon as Meaning-Machine

```
PICTORIAL ICONS vs SYMBOLIC ICONS

  Pictorial icon: resembles what it represents
  (a drawing of a house = a house)

  Symbolic icon: arbitrary convention
  (a $ sign = money; not visually similar to money)

  Language: purely symbolic
  Comics uses BOTH simultaneously

  THE TENSION:
  When you read the word "TREE" you perceive an abstract symbol
  When you see a drawing of a tree you perceive a visual resemblance

  Comics combines both in the same reading experience.
  This is a unique cognitive demand.
  The reader is switching between modes (symbolic / iconic)
  panel to panel, word to image.
```

## Word-Image Relationships (McCloud's 7 Combinations)

In panels containing both words and images, McCloud identifies how they combine:

```
1. WORD-SPECIFIC: Images illustrate text; words carry narrative weight
   Text: "The angry man..." ; Image: man looking angry
   Images could be replaced; words do the work

2. PICTURE-SPECIFIC: Words function as soundtrack to images
   Image: chase sequence; Text: "Help!"
   Images could work alone

3. DUO-SPECIFIC: Both say the same thing (redundant)
   Text: "I punched him"; Image: punching him
   Common in amateur comics; weak

4. ADDITIVE: Words amplify or elaborate on images
   Image: person at window; Text: "She waited for hours"
   Duration added to image by words

5. PARALLEL: Words and images pursue separate paths
   Image: one scene; Text: caption about something else
   Creates irony, counterpoint, complexity

6. MONTAGE: Words as visual elements of the image
   Onomatopoeia as design; speech balloon as part of composition

7. INTERDEPENDENT: Neither word nor image makes sense alone
   Neither could communicate alone; together they do
   This is the highest (most distinctly comics) combination
```

## Criticism and Extensions of McCloud

```
LIMITATIONS / CRITIQUES
  Pre-digital: "Understanding Comics" (1993) preceded
               webcomics, infinite canvas, webtoons
               McCloud addressed this in "Reinventing Comics" (2000)

  Western bias: Six transitions derived mostly from Western comics
               Manga's aspect-to-aspect and moment-to-moment are
               more frequent than McCloud's taxonomy suggested
               as dominant

  Simplification: The triangle abstraction is a useful model, not
                  a complete theory of visual representation
                  It elides: style, color, line quality

  Normative smuggling: McCloud sometimes describes what comics
                       DO as what comics SHOULD DO
                       (interdependent word-image = highest combination)
                       This is a value judgment dressed as theory

EXTENSIONS BY OTHERS
  Thierry Groensteen: "The System of Comics" (2007)
  French theoretical tradition; more formal; less accessible
  "Arthrology": non-contiguous panel relationships across a page

  Hillary Chute: comics and witness; memoir comics; Holocaust testimony
  (extends McCloud's gutter concept to trauma)

  Neil Cohn: cognitive neuroscience of comics reading
  "Visual Language Theory" -- the language syntax model
```

## Why McCloud Matters

```
BEFORE McCLOUD (1993)
  Comics criticism: plot summaries + "is this art?" debates
  No systematic vocabulary for HOW comics work
  No theory of why panels and gutters function as they do

AFTER McCLOUD
  Comics studies: legitimized as academic field
  Art schools teach comics formally using McCloud's vocabulary
  Comics creators: can articulate what they're doing
  Readers: have a theoretical framework to understand their experience

  McCLOUD demonstrated his theory using the medium he theorized.
  "Understanding Comics" is itself a proof-of-concept:
  comics can carry theoretical argument.
  This was itself a literary/critical breakthrough.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Closure is just inference"** -- Closure in McCloud's sense is a specific
participatory act where the reader's imagination creates action that the comic
does not depict. "Inference" implies passive processing; McCloud's closure is
active co-creation of the narrative.

**"All panel transitions are equal"** -- No. Moment-to-moment transitions
demand little from the reader (like film); non-sequitur demands maximum
participation. McCloud argues that Western comics underuse aspect-to-aspect
(common in manga) which creates atmosphere rather than advancing action.

**"The definition excludes film"** -- Yes, deliberately. McCloud defines comics
by spatial juxtaposition (images exist simultaneously on a surface) versus
film's temporal projection (images appear and vanish in time). This is a
formal distinction, not a quality judgment.

**"Simple drawing = less skill"** -- McCloud argues the opposite: simplification
is harder and more demanding than detailed realism. Distilling a face to 5 lines
requires understanding what is essential. Peanuts looks "easy" and is extremely
difficult to do well.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| McCloud concept                           | Application in practice                 |
|-------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Closure                                   | Leave readers space to participate      |
| Blood in the gutter                       | Don't show violence -- imply it         |
| Panel transition choice                   | Vary type; avoid all action-to-action   |
| Time as space                             | Panel size signals importance/duration  |
| Image triangle                            | Simplified icons = wider identification |
| Interdependent word-image combination     | Highest comics-specific achievement     |
| Aspect-to-aspect transitions              | Atmosphere; pause; Japanese aesthetic   |
