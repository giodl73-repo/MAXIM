## The Form: What Makes Comics Comics

The question "what is a comic?" has more substance than it appears.

```
DEFINITIONS IN COMPETITION
  Will Eisner: "sequential art"
  Scott McCloud: "juxtaposed pictorial and other images in
                  deliberate sequence" (from "Understanding Comics")
  Key debate: must it include text? (no -- wordless comics exist)
              must it be sequential? (yes, by definition)

  WHAT COMICS UNIQUELY DOES:
  Time is spatial: earlier panels = left / above; later = right / below
  The gutter (space between panels) is where time lives
  The reader's mind completes the motion between frames
  (closure: inferring what happened in the gap)

  NO OTHER MEDIUM DOES THIS:
  Film: time is real; you cannot linger, skip, re-read
  Prose: time is language (past tense, duration, sequence)
  Painting: single moment; no sequence
  Comics: durational time encoded as spatial sequence;
          reader controls pace
```

## The American Tradition: Commercial History

```
NEWSPAPER COMIC STRIPS (1890s-present)
  Yellow Kid (Richard Outcault, 1895): often cited as first
  Krazy Kat (George Herriman, 1913): surrealism, language play
  Little Nemo in Slumberland (Winsor McCay, 1905): dreamscapes
  Peanuts (Charles Schulz, 1950-2000): philosophy + melancholy
  Doonesbury (Garry Trudeau, 1970-): political satire
  These ran in newspapers (massive distribution) weekly or daily.

COMIC BOOKS (1930s-)
  Not comic strips bound -- different format: monthly, direct sales
  Pulp paper; color cover; 22-32pp interior
  Early: humor, adventure, crime, Western
  1938: Action Comics #1 (Superman): superhero genre dominates

DISTRIBUTION MODEL (key to economics)
  Publisher -> Distributor (effectively monopoly: Diamond, 1990s)
  -> Comic book shops (direct market, 1970s+)
  Previously: newsstands (mass market; higher circulation)
  Newsstand collapse -> direct market dependence
  -> Reader demographics: older, dedicated, declining new reader pipeline
```

## Formal Elements of Sequential Art

```
THE VOCABULARY
  Panel:    Individual frame; bounded image space
  Gutter:   Space between panels; where time lives
  Tier:     Horizontal row of panels
  Bleed:    Panel extends to page edge (no border)
  Splash:   Full-page single image
  Double-page spread: Two pages as single image
  Caption:  Narration box (usually top of panel; rectangular)
  Balloon:  Speech or thought (oval; tail points to speaker)
  Motion lines: Lines indicating movement or speed
  Sound effects: Onomatopoeia as graphic element (POW! CRASH!)

READING PATH
  Western comics: left to right, top to bottom
  Manga: right to left (reading direction of Japanese text)
  Webtoon: top to bottom (infinite vertical scroll)
  Each convention: affects narrative pacing; panel arrangement strategy

PANEL SIZE AND TIME
  Large panel: longer duration (or important moment)
  Small panel: brief moment, quick action
  Narrow horizontal panel: a flash, a reaction
  Vertical panel: height, falling, surveillance from above
  Gutterless panels (no gutter): simultaneous or continuous
```

## The Critical Moment: Comics Seeking Legitimacy

```
CULTURAL STATUS PROBLEM (through 1980)
  Comics = children's entertainment (USA)
  Comics Code Authority (1954): self-censorship after Wertham
  Comics = juvenile = low culture

  EUROPE: comics were not low culture
  Tintin: published in Le Vingtieme Siecle (adult newspaper)
  French BD: respected cultural form; published as hardcover albums

  JAPAN: manga = mainstream; not children's genre exclusively
  Adults reading manga on commuter trains: normal; not stigmatized

THE TURNING POINT (1986-1992)
  1986: "Maus" serial completion; "Watchmen" and "Dark Knight Returns"
  1987: "Maus" in New York Times Book Review
  1991: "Maus" collected volume wins Pulitzer Prize (special citation)
  1992: Vertigo (DC imprint) launched; literary superhero adjacents

  These events repositioned comics within Anglophone cultural discourse.
  "Graphic novel" term: signal of ambition + literary intent
  The form proved it could carry serious subjects.
```

## How Comics Relate to Adjacent Forms

```
COMICS vs FILM
  Both: visual storytelling; sequential
  Film: time is real (24fps); reader passive
  Comics: time is spatial; reader active (sets pace; re-reads)
  Comics: cheaper to produce at any level
  Film: visual + audio; comics: silent (sound is described, not heard)

COMICS vs ILLUSTRATION / PICTURE BOOKS
  Picture book: illustration supports text (often)
  Comics: image IS the primary narrative vehicle
  Wordless picture books blur this
  Comics theory defines by sequence, not text/image ratio

COMICS vs ANIMATION
  Animation: the frames are filled in (no gutters)
  Comics: reader fills the gaps
  McCloud's insight: the reader is the author of the gutter

COMICS AND PROSE
  Comics can achieve: compressed narrative (years in 3 pages)
  Comics struggles with: extended interiority, complex argument
  Prose can go anywhere in consciousness; comics are present-tense
  visual (depicting what characters SEE, not FEEL, unless visualized)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Comic book = superheroes"** -- In the American context, the superhero genre
dominated the market for 60+ years. But: European BD, manga, underground comix,
literary graphic novels, webcomics -- none are primarily superhero. The format
is not the genre.

**"Graphic novel = long comic book"** -- "Graphic novel" is a marketing/prestige
term as much as a formal description. "Maus" is a graphic novel. "The Complete
Peanuts" collected strips are also sometimes called a graphic novel. The term
signals literary ambition; it does not specify a formal category precisely.

**"Comics are simple"** -- Comics require the reader to participate in narrative
construction. The gutter-filling process is cognitively active. The form can
be simple; the best comics are not. McCloud's theoretical account shows the
complexity is structural, not accidental.

**"Manga is a genre"** -- Manga is a medium (Japanese comics). Within manga:
shonen (boys), shojo (girls), seinen (adult men), josei (adult women), horror,
romance, sports, history, literary -- the full range of fiction genres exists.
"I like manga" is like saying "I like American books."
