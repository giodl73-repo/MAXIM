## Historical Development

### Osamu Tezuka: The God of Manga

```
OSAMU TEZUKA (1928-1989)
  Called: "the god of manga" and "the godfather of anime"
  Created the modern manga language

  KEY INNOVATIONS:
  Cinematic storytelling: used film grammar (wide shots, close-ups,
  tracking shots, time compression) in comics panels
  Influenced by: Bambi (Disney); Fritz Lang's "Metropolis";
                 Eisenstein's montage theory
  Emotional faces: large eyes (Disney influence); expressive;
                   the "big eyes" became the manga visual convention

  KEY WORKS:
  "Astro Boy" (Tetsuwan Atom, 1952-68): robot boy with human emotions;
  post-Hiroshima Japan; atomic age anxiety and hope
  "Kimba the White Lion" (Jungle Emperor Leo, 1950-54): Disney-adjacent
  "Black Jack" (1973-83): illegal surgeon; medical ethics; brilliant
  "Phoenix" (Hi no Tori, 1967-88): life/death/rebirth cycle; philosophical
  "Buddha" (1972-83): biography of Siddhartha as manga epic

  PRODUCTION METHOD:
  Tezuka ran a studio (Mushi Production); industrial manga production
  Assistants for backgrounds; Tezuka: faces, storytelling decisions
  This assistant model became standard for manga production
```

## The Serialization System

### How Manga Is Made and Distributed

```
MAGAZINE SERIALIZATION
  Weekly: Weekly Shonen Jump (Shueisha), Weekly Shonen Magazine,
          Weekly Young Magazine
  Monthly: Shonen Sunday, Afternoon, etc.

  A MANGA ARTIST'S SCHEDULE:
  Weekly serialization = 18-20 pages per week
  Deadline: Friday (or earlier for final editing)
  Creator + 2-4 assistants working essentially nonstop
  Many mangaka report: sleep 3-4 hours per night during serialization

  EDITOR RELATIONSHIP:
  Jump: strong editor involvement in story decisions
  (editors can request changes; in extreme cases: end the series)
  Editor-creator relationship: crucial; like showrunner-writer

TANKOBON (COLLECTED VOLUMES)
  Magazine serialization -> collected into tankobon volumes
  Each volume: ~200 pages; 8-10 chapters
  Price: ~450-600 yen in Japan
  Global: translated tankobon are the primary format for non-Japanese readers

  JUMP'S SELECTION MECHANISM:
  Weekly popularity voting by readers (postcards, now digital)
  Low-ranked series: warned; if sustained: cancelled
  High-ranked: given more chapters
  This is a continuous market test.
  Naruto: 700 chapters over 15 years (winner)
  Many series: cancelled at chapter 20-30 (market losers)
```

## Demographic Genres

### Shonen: Boys' Action

```
SHONEN (少年 = "boy")
  Target: boys, broadly 10-18; actual readership: wider
  Published in: Weekly Shonen Jump (most famous shonen magazine)

  THEMES:
  "Friendship, effort, victory" (nakama, doryoku, shori) -- Jump's editorial trinity
  Protagonist: underdog; grows through challenges; bonds with friends
  Power escalation: each arc's final antagonist surpasses previous
  "Tournament arcs" and "training arcs" are structural conventions

  CANONICAL EXAMPLES:
  Dragon Ball (Toriyama, 1984-95): martial arts; power escalation template
  Naruto (Kishimoto, 1999-2014): ninja; belonging; identity
  One Piece (Oda, 1997-ongoing): pirates; freedom; friendship; 1,000+ chapters
  My Hero Academia (Horikoshi, 2014-ongoing): superhero school
  Demon Slayer / Kimetsu no Yaiba (Gotouge, 2016-20): fastest-selling manga ever
  Attack on Titan / Shingeki no Kyojin (Isayama, 2009-2021): dark; political

  VISUAL CONVENTIONS:
  Action scenes: speed lines; motion blur; impact effects
  Emotional scenes: large panels; close-up faces; sweat drops
  Power-up: glowing; screaming; clothes tearing; muscle expansion
```

### Shojo: Girls' Romance and Emotion

```
SHOJO (少女 = "girl")
  Target: girls broadly 10-18; actual readership: includes adults
  Published in: Ribon, Margaret, Nakayoshi

  THEMES:
  Romance; identity; emotional complexity; relationships
  More interior; psychological depth; page aesthetics different from shonen

  VISUAL CONVENTIONS:
  Flowers; sparkles; screen tones for emotion
  Close-ups on eyes (particularly expressive)
  Non-narrative decorative elements on the page
  Pages sometimes more visually "noisy" with emotion indicators

  CANONICAL EXAMPLES:
  Sailor Moon (Takeuchi, 1991-97): magical girl; iconic globally
  Fruits Basket (Takaya, 1998-2006): zodiac curse; romance; trauma
  Nana (Yazawa, 2000-ongoing, hiatus): two women named Nana; music; identity
  Cardcaptor Sakura (CLAMP, 1996-2000): magical girl
  Ouran High School Host Club (Hatori, 2002-10): gender comedy; class

  SHOJO INNOVATIONS:
  Multiple simultaneous flashbacks (not sequential time; emotional simultaneity)
  Text outside panels (floating words; typographic emotion)
  More experimental page design than mainstream shonen
```

### Seinen and Josei

```
SEINEN (青年 = "young man")
  Older male readership; more graphic; more complex
  Published in: Young Jump, Morning, Afternoon, Big Comic Original
  Content: violence, sexuality, philosophical; not censored as shonen

  CANONICAL EXAMPLES:
  Berserk (Miura, 1989-2021, posthumous continuation): dark fantasy
            Guts: betrayal; survival; revenge; medieval violence
            Art: extraordinary; highly influential
  Vagabond (Inoue, 1998-2015 ongoing, long hiatus): Miyamoto Musashi; swordsmanship
  Vinland Saga (Yukimura, 2005-ongoing): Vikings; war; pacifism; Thorfinn
  Akira (Otomo, 1982-1990): cyberpunk; Neo-Tokyo; psychic powers
            Visually ahead of its time; influenced: Matrix aesthetic, cyberpunk overall
  Ghost in the Shell (Shirow, 1989-1990): cyborg bodies; identity; surveillance

JOSEI (女性 = "woman")
  Adult women; realistic romance; emotional depth
  Less fantasy than shojo; more adult relationships
  Nana: crosses shojo/josei; widely read by adult women
```

## Manga's Visual Language

### Key Visual Conventions

```
BIG EYES
  Tezuka's Disney influence; emotional windows
  Critique: Westernization; aspiration to non-Japanese appearance
  Counter: The eyes are often unrealistic in many directions;
           large eyes = emotional emphasis, not racial aspiration
           Japanese people with naturally large eyes also drew this way
           The debate: legitimate; ongoing

SCREEN TONES (N-tones)
  Pre-digital: physical adhesive patterns applied to artwork
  Post-digital: software patterns
  Used for: textures, backgrounds, emotional moods
  Gray gradients created without gray ink (B&W pages)
  Different dot patterns = different textures
  A major component of manga's distinctive visual texture

SUPER-DEFORMATION (SD / "Chibi")
  Extreme simplification of characters for comedy/cuteness
  Normal character: realistic proportions
  Same character "SD": head 1/3 of body; dots for eyes; cartoony
  Switches between modes within the same page
  A visual vocabulary signal: "this is funny" or "this is cute"

VISUAL ONOMATOPOEIA
  More extensive than Western comics
  Sound effects integrated into page design as visual elements
  Silence has a sound effect in Japanese manga (shi-n or "...)
  Blushing, sweating, trembling: all have visual codified expressions
```

## Global Expansion of Manga

### The Translation Wave

```
EARLY US TRANSLATIONS (1980s)
  First Contact Comics: Barefoot Gen (1978): Hiroshima survivor account
  Eclipse and Viz (1987): Golgo 13, Area 88 -- initial experiments

  ZEN INTERGALACTIC NINJA (1988); NAUSICAA (1988-1997) -- Viz
  Gen of Hiroshima: important early localization

VIZ MEDIA (1987-present)
  Joint venture: Shueisha (Jump) + Shogakukan + Viz
  Dominant US manga publisher; publishes Jump manga in US
  Switched to unflipped (right-to-left) in 2003
  Online: Manga Plus platform (legal, free, simultaneous release)

TOKYOPOP (1997-2011; revived)
  "100% Authentic Manga" -- unflipped reading direction
  Normalized right-to-left for American readers
  Published: large catalog; collapsed 2011; properties scattered

SIMULTANEOUS WORLDWIDE RELEASE (current)
  Shueisha: Manga Plus app
  Free, legal, simultaneous with Japanese release
  Partial solution to piracy (major problem pre-2020)

SCANLATION (fan translation + scanning)
  Underground fan translations of untranslated manga
  Legally questionable; culturally significant
  Many manga fans learned Japanese to read ahead of translations
  Legal platforms reduced but didn't eliminate scanlation
```

## Manga vs. American Comics: A Structural Comparison

```
+---------------------+------------------------+------------------+
| DIMENSION           | MANGA                  | AMERICAN COMICS  |
+---------------------+------------------------+------------------+
| Format              | Tankobon (200pp); B&W   | 22pp monthly; color|
| Reading direction   | Right to left           | Left to right    |
| Serialization       | Weekly/monthly magazine | Monthly direct   |
| Creator ownership   | Publisher owns          | Publisher owns   |
|                     | (Shueisha owns Jump IP) | (Marvel/DC own)  |
| Creator income      | Serialization fee +     | Page rate +      |
|                     | tankobon royalties      | limited royalties|
| Genre range         | All genres              | Superhero dominant|
| Female readership   | 45%+ of manga readers   | ~25% of US comics|
|                     | (shojo; josei)          | (growing)        |
| Cultural status     | Mainstream; trains, etc | Historically low |
| Production speed    | 18-20pp/week            | 22pp/month       |
| End of story        | Planned endings common  | Ongoing indefinitely|
+---------------------+------------------------+------------------+
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Manga is a genre"** -- Manga is a medium (Japanese comics), not a genre.
Every literary genre exists within manga: action, romance, horror, literary
fiction, history, biography, cooking, sports, philosophy. Saying "I like manga"
is as imprecise as "I like American books."

**"Shonen and shojo are about the content"** -- Shonen and shojo are Japanese
magazine demographics (boys and girls). These determine where the manga is
published and marketed. In practice: shonen tends to action; shojo tends to
romance; but there are exceptions. It is a marketing category, not a content
description.

**"Manga art is simpler than American comics"** -- Some manga is minimal (Tezuka);
some is extraordinarily detailed (Berserk; Vagabond). Miura's Berserk contains
some of the most detailed artwork in any comics tradition. The visual conventions
(screen tones, big eyes) are different, not simpler.

**"All anime is based on manga"** -- Many anime adaptations are original
(Ghost in the Shell film is different from manga; most Ghibli films are original).
Many anime are based on light novels (Sword Art Online). Manga-to-anime is the
dominant pipeline but not the only one.

---

Jump's weekly reader popularity voting is **real-time continuous market testing at scale**: the bottom-ranked series get cancelled, the top-ranked get more pages, and each week's data directly influences product decisions (story direction, chapter length, continuation). This is the same mechanism as feature flags + usage metrics driving product roadmap in software — except the feedback loop is weekly rather than per-release, and the stakes are existential for each series.
