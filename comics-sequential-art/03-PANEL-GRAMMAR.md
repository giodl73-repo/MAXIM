## The Panel

The panel is the basic unit of comics. Every formal decision in panel design is
also a narrative decision.

```
PANEL BOUNDARIES
  Bounded panel (border/frame):
    Contained moment; definite temporal unit
    Most common in Western genre comics

  Borderless panel:
    Same image; but without container
    Effect: the moment is timeless, or the boundary between reader
            and scene is thinner

  Bleed (panel extends to page edge):
    No boundary = no containing frame
    Effect: overwhelming, boundless, or transcendent moments
    Violence, sex, death, vast landscape often use full bleed
    "The scene escapes its frame"

PANEL SHAPE
  Rectangular (standard):
    Most common; neutral; clean
    Ratio varies: wide landscape panels; tall portrait panels

  Irregular shape (splash page insets, etc.):
    Signals: fragmented consciousness; dream; memory; explosion
    Example: a panel shaped like a thought balloon within a thought sequence

  Circular / oval:
    Often: dream; memory; flashback; idealized moment
    Old convention: "iris" shots borrowed from film (1920s influence)

  Angled/diagonal borders:
    Energy; movement; instability
    Jim Steranko (1960s-70s Nick Fury) pioneered this in American comics
```

## The Gutter

The gutter is the most theoretically rich element of comics. McCloud called it
where "the magic of comics happens."

```
WHAT THE GUTTER IS:
  The white (or colored) space between panels.
  In physical comics: sometimes actual margin between panels.
  In digital/borderless: the conceptual gap even without visible space.

GUTTER WIDTH AND EFFECT
  Wide gutter: more time has passed; more cognitive gap to fill
  Narrow gutter: less time; continuous action
  No gutter (borderless panels touching): simultaneous or continuous

  Example:
  [Panel: car moving] [wide gutter] [Panel: car stopped, door open]
  Reader infers: car slowed, turned, parked, door opened
  All that action: created by the reader in the gutter

COLORED GUTTERS
  White: standard; neutral
  Black: heavy, oppressive, dark narrative; death; unconsciousness
  Colored: contextual (a red gutter in a fire scene = everything in fire)

THE GUTTER AND TRAUMA
  Trauma studies and comics:
  What cannot be depicted directly can be approached through the gutter.
  Maus: the horror of Auschwitz is approached obliquely.
  The gutter allows representation of the unrepresentable --
  the reader's imagination fills in what the author cannot show.
  Hillary Chute's work on "Graphic Women" develops this.
```

## Speech and Thought Balloons

```
THE BALLOON SYSTEM
  Balloon: The container; oval or rounded rectangle
  Tail: The pointer; indicates speaker/thinker
  Lettering: uppercase typically; handwritten or digital font

TYPES:
  Speech balloon (solid outline, solid tail):
    Spoken word; present tense
    "I'm going to get you for this."

  Thought balloon (cloud shape, bubble tail):
    Inner monologue; never audible
    Classic format (golden age): multi-bubble tail
    Modern: increasingly replaced by caption boxes for interiority

  Whisper balloon (dashed outline):
    Quiet speech
    "Come closer..."

  Electronic transmission (jagged outline, lightning tail):
    Radio, telephone, intercom speech
    Signal: the voice is mediated, not present

  Scream (jagged outline, large balloon):
    Very loud; violent emotion

CAPTION BOXES
  Rectangular; usually at top or bottom of panel
  Narration by: third person narrator, or first person character
  "Three months earlier..." (scene-setting)
  "I had never seen her before." (character interiority)
  Caption vs balloon: caption = narrative frame; balloon = present action

THE TEMPORAL IMPLICATION:
  Speech balloons: happening NOW, as the panel depicts
  Caption boxes: the story being told FROM OUTSIDE (or after the fact)
  This is the same as first-person prose narration vs.
  dramatic present tense dialogue
```

## Reading Direction and Its Effects

```
WESTERN READING (left to right, top to bottom)
  Natural reading direction of Western languages
  Information flows from left (cause/setup) to right (effect/punchline)
  In a 3-panel sequence:
    [Setup] -> [Development] -> [Punchline]
  The reader reaches the punchline by moving rightward

  STRUCTURAL IMPLICATION:
  Left = before; Right = after
  Left = familiar; Right = new
  Left = question; Right = answer

MANGA READING (right to left)
  Japanese text reads right to left
  Manga panels read right to left
  Western manga translations: flipped (mirror image) OR
  unflipped with reader instruction to "start from the other end"
  Unflipped is now standard (Viz, Tokyopop switched post-2000)

  STRUCTURAL IMPLICATION IN MANGA:
  Right = before; Left = after
  (reversed from Western convention)
  This affects: staging of action, page turns for reveals,
               where dominant figure stands in a 2-character scene

WEBTOON (top to bottom)
  Korean manhwa for smartphone: vertical scroll
  No page turns; infinite canvas; episodes designed for scroll
  Different pacing: can use very long establishing sequences
  without "wasting" a page turn
  Page turn as narrative device: irrelevant in webtoon format
```

## Page Layout: The Page as Unit

The layout of a full page is itself a composition.

```
PAGE LAYOUT TYPES

GRID (fixed panel structure)
  Most common: 3x2 (6 panels) or 3x3 (9 panels)
  Regular grid = controlled, rigid, structured narration
  Example: Dave Gibbons in Watchmen uses strict 9-panel grid throughout
           The regularity = psychological clockwork; control; formalism
           Disrupting the grid (double page splash) = shattering the control

FREE LAYOUT (varied panel sizes)
  Expressive; panel size signals importance
  Large panels = important moments; climaxes
  Action pages often use free layout to convey energy

SPLASH PAGE
  Full page = single panel
  Used for: first appearance of hero; epic landscape; dramatic reveal
  The page is giving the reader a single sustained moment

DOUBLE-PAGE SPREAD
  Two pages = one image
  Most striking possible visual statement
  Page turn is the reveal
  PROBLEM: When the book is open, the spread reads across the spine;
           the physical binding interrupts the image

WIDESCREEN (popular 2000s era)
  Horizontal panels spanning full page width
  Inspired by cinema; Bryan Hitch's "The Authority" pioneered
  Each page like a single film frame from a different scene
  Creates cinematic pacing; criticized as decompressed (fewer ideas/page)
```

## Motion, Speed, and Sound

```
MOTION LINES (speed lines, action lines)
  Lines radiating from or trailing a moving object
  Faster motion: more lines, closer together, longer
  Direction: in direction of opposite motion (trailing behind)
  Japanese manga: more systematic; hatching patterns for different speeds

  ALTERNATIVES TO LINES:
  Blurred image (more photorealistic comics)
  Distorted form (stretched figure in flight)
  Multiple silhouettes (ghosting: same figure in 3 positions)

IMPACT / EFFECTS
  "POW" "CRASH" "BANG" -- onomatopoeia as graphic element
  Golden Age: lettered in panel; designed as visual element
  Jack Kirby's "WHAM": the letters are visual impact design
  This is typography serving narrative directly

FREEZING THE ACTION
  Comics cannot literally show motion.
  Comics shows: one frozen instant.
  The reader infers motion from:
    - Pre-impact and post-impact panels
    - Motion lines
    - Figure's pose and expression
    - Panel sequence
  This is McCloud's closure again: the reader creates motion
  in the gutter between frozen frames.
```

## Time Control: Comics vs Film

This comparison shows what makes panel grammar distinctly different from film
grammar.

```
FILM (director controls time)
  You see 24 frames per second; you cannot control pace
  A director can: cut (instant transition), dissolve (slower)
                  slow motion, fast motion
  Time is GIVEN to you; you experience it at the filmmaker's pace

COMICS (reader controls time)
  You can linger on a single panel indefinitely
  You can read backwards; re-read
  You can skip ahead; read last panel first
  The same narrative takes different duration per reader

  IMPLICATION FOR STORYTELLING:
  Comics: every element is permanent on the page
  You can design for: simultaneous reading (page as image)
                       OR sequential reading (panel by panel)
  A reader who looks at the full page before reading it
  and a reader who covers subsequent panels with their hand
  have different experiences of the same comic.

  FILM: no equivalent; frame appears and disappears

PACING IN COMICS
  Fast pacing: fewer panels; action-to-action; minimal text
  Slow pacing: many panels; moment-to-moment; more text; large panels
  Dense: lots of small panels; complex layouts; text-heavy
  Loose: few panels; large; spare; white space
```

## Panel Grammar in Practice: Key Examples

```
WATCHMEN (Moore/Gibbons, 1986)
  9-panel grid: enforced throughout
  The grid becomes the formal symbol of Dr. Manhattan's fragmented time
  When the grid breaks (splash): the world breaks with it
  Parallel reading: each chapter contains two stories
  running simultaneously through specific panel patterns

BATMAN: THE DARK KNIGHT RETURNS (Frank Miller, 1986)
  TV screens as panels (news commentators interrupt the main narrative)
  Grid variation within a single page for television inserts
  The formal choice = TV as the new narrator of public events

JIMMY CORRIGAN: THE SMARTEST KID ON EARTH (Chris Ware, 2000)
  Extremely small panels; tiny figures; massive negative space
  The loneliness is formal: figures isolated in enormous white fields
  Wordless sequences of remarkable complexity
  Temporal folding: pages that encode multiple generations simultaneously

AKIRA (Katsuhiro Otomo, 1982-1990)
  Aspect-to-aspect transitions for environment establishment
  Violence depicted across many small panels (detailed; not heroic)
  Cinematic page compositions; wide establishing shots

MAUS (Spiegelman, 1980-1991)
  Story within a story: present (Spiegelman interviewing father) and
  past (Vladek's Holocaust narrative)
  Mouse/cat/pig as ethnic markers (not naturalistic; symbolic)
  The frame disrupts comfort; enables approach to trauma
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Comics panels are like film stills"** -- Fundamentally different. Film frames
appear and vanish; the viewer has no control. Comics panels are permanent and
simultaneous on the page. The reader creates motion; the director does not.

**"More panels = more information"** -- More panels can mean: more time, more
detail, faster cutting, or a deliberately dense texture. The number of panels
is a formal choice with multiple possible meanings, not a simple density metric.

**"Speech balloons go wherever there's space"** -- Professional lettering follows
a reading order: left to right, top to bottom within the panel. The balloon
tails indicate speaker; balloons are placed so the eye reaches them in the
correct sequence. Misplaced lettering = confusing dialogue order.

**"The gutter is just blank space"** -- The gutter is the most cognitively active
part of the comic. It is where closure happens; where time passes; where the
reader participates. "Blank" is a material description; functionally it is full.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want to create...                               | Panel grammar technique           |
|---------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| A sense of overwhelming scale or emotion          | Full bleed or double spread       |
| A very rapid passage of time                      | Scene-to-scene transition; few panels|
| A slow, contemplative mood                        | Aspect-to-aspect; wide panels     |
| Tension before a reveal                           | Page turn placement (reveal at top|
|                                                   | of new page)                      |
| Interiority / inner monologue                     | Caption boxes (narrated); or      |
|                                                   | thought balloons                  |
| A claustrophobic, controlled feel                 | Strict grid; small panels         |
| Explosive action                                  | Free layout; large panels; bleed  |
| Signal that panels are simultaneous               | No gutter or very narrow gutter   |
