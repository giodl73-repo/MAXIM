## History of Webcomics

### The Early Web (1993-2000)

```
WHEN THE WEB BEGAN
  1993: Mosaic browser; images in web pages became possible
  1994: First webcomics appear (Eric Millikin's "Witches and Stitches" c. 1985
        as BBS, web version 1994)
  1996: Dr. Fun (David Farley): daily single-panel gag
        One of the first regularly updated webcomics

  THE INFRASTRUCTURE PROBLEM (early web):
  No RSS feeds; no social media; no mobile
  Readers had to remember to visit URLs daily/weekly
  Distribution: link lists; Keenspot network (2000); Web Comics Nation

  EARLY NOTABLE STRIPS:
  User Friendly (J.D. "Illiad" Frazer, 1997): sysadmin humor; first popular geek webcomic
  Sluggy Freelance (Pete Abrams, 1997): geeky adventure; long-running
  Penny Arcade (Gabe/Tycho, 1998): video game humor; became a media company
  PvP (Scott Kurtz, 1998): gaming pop culture; early crossover with print
```

### The 2000s Explosion

```
WEBCOMICS COME OF AGE (2000-2010)
  Broadband: readers can see images easily
  Blog platforms: easy self-publishing
  PayPal: monetization becomes possible (donations, merchandise)

  XKCD (Randall Munroe, 2005-present)
  Stick-figure webcomic; math, physics, computing, romance, language
  Audience: technically educated; link-sharing culture
  No buffer; Munroe writes and publishes; wildly influential
  Some strips: the most-shared image content in science/tech communities
  Books: "What If?"; "Thing Explainer"; XKCD extended into publishing

  QUESTIONABLE CONTENT (Jeph Jacques, 2003-present)
  Indie music culture; long-running character drama
  The "slice-of-life" webcomic that demonstrated:
  sustained character drama works on the web

  PENNY ARCADE (1998-present; media company)
  Gaming culture; PAX conventions; Child's Play charity
  Two guys drawing a webcomic -> $20M+ company with conventions
  First webcomic to clearly demonstrate: webcomic as business

  ACHEWOOD (Chris Onstad, 2001-2010, sporadic after)
  Bears and cats; profound absurdity; emotional depth
  Cult following; considered by many critics best-written webcomic

  DINOSAUR COMICS (Ryan North, 2003-present)
  Same six panels; same poses; different dialogue every day
  Pure verbal humor; conceptual constraint
  North: also writes mainstream comics (Squirrel Girl, etc.)
```

## McCloud's "Infinite Canvas" (2000)

```
SCOTT McCLOUD'S "REINVENTING COMICS" (2000)
  Written 7 years after "Understanding Comics"
  Central claim: the web enables the "infinite canvas"

  PRINT COMICS: constrained by page size; pages must turn
  Web canvas: no page; horizontal or vertical scroll infinitely
  Implication: comics can use spatial relationships
               impossible in print
               - one long horizontal strip
               - panels connected by size relationship (zoom in/out)
               - panels above/below each other semantically

  EXAMPLES McCLOUD demonstrated in his own webcomics:
  - Scrolling right to move through time
  - Zooming in to enter a character's thoughts
  - Panels spatially arranged to show causality

  DID IT HAPPEN?
  Partially. Very few webcomics exploited the infinite canvas extensively.
  What DID happen: webtoons (Korean vertical scroll) are the mass adoption
  of one infinite canvas direction (downward scroll on mobile).
  The horizontal infinite canvas: remains experimental.

  WHY INFINITE CANVAS DIDN'T DOMINATE:
  Reading on screen is different from reading on paper
  Scrolling infinitely is disorienting without structural cues
  Mobile phone changed the canvas question (vertical scroll natural)
  The page turn (print constraint) is also a useful narrative device
```

## Webcomic Business Models

```
THE ECONOMICS OF WEBCOMICS
  Traditional model:
  Publisher advances $ -> creator makes comic -> publisher sells -> creator gets royalties
  Risk: publisher's

  WEBCOMIC MODEL (evolved 2000s-2010s):
  Creator publishes free -> builds audience -> monetizes via:

  1. ADVERTISING
     Banner ads; Google AdSense
     Works when: very high traffic (100K+ daily)
     Declined: ad blockers; reduced CPM rates
     Current status: low revenue for most; viable only at top scale

  2. MERCHANDISE
     T-shirts, prints, plushies, mugs, books
     Penny Arcade: first major example
     CafePress, then Topatoco (webcomics-focused merch company)
     Current: Threadless, Redbubble, Etsy; creator control

  3. BOOKS (PRINT COLLECTIONS)
     Free online + paid print collection
     "Web to print" pipeline
     Many webcomics became New York Times bestsellers via this model:
     xkcd, Hyperbole and a Half, The Oatmeal

  4. PATREON (2013+)
     Monthly subscription; supporters pay for exclusive content
     Most impactful change in webcomic economics since Paypal
     Many full-time webcomic creators: 500-5,000 patrons at $3-10/month
     Generates: $18,000-600,000/year for the range
     Replaced: advertising as primary revenue for smaller creators

  5. CROWDFUNDING (Kickstarter, Indiegogo)
     Print editions, special projects
     Major webcomic collections raised $100K+ repeatedly
```

## Notable Webcomics

```
XKCD (Randall Munroe, 2005-)
  Stick figures; STEM humor; relationship observation; language
  Monday/Wednesday/Friday update schedule
  "1190: Time": one of the longest webcomic story experiments
  (3,099 panels published over 2 years; real-time narrative)
  Science books derived from Q&A sections

HOMESTUCK (Andrew Hussie, 2009-2016)
  Flash animations, GIFs, music, games, text walls, comics panels
  Wildly experimental; mixed media
  Cult following; massive fan community; influenced a generation of creators
  Plot: incomprehensibly complex
  Length: ~800,000 words equivalent; the most ambitious single webcomic work

HYPERBOLE AND A HALF (Allie Brosh, 2009-2013 active; 2021 return)
  Personal essay + crude MS Paint illustration
  "This is Why I'll Never Be an Adult": millions of shares
  "Adventures in Depression" (two parts): widely credited with helping
  people understand and communicate about depression
  Became a New York Times bestseller as a printed book

THE OATMEAL (Matthew Inman, 2009-)
  Single-topic posts; illustrated essays; humor
  Commercial model: print sales + licensing
  Raised $1.3M on Indiegogo for Tesla museum (2012 -- not comics; still)

ORDER OF THE STICK (Rich Burlew, 2003-)
  Stick figure D&D parody; 1,200+ strips
  Extended storyline; raised $1.25M Kickstarter for print editions
  Demonstrated: webcomic audience willing to pay for finished print

PENNY ARCADE (Gabe/Tycho, 1998-)
  Gaming; PAX conventions; Child's Play charity
  Revenue: multi-million; staff; actual company
  The most commercially successful webcomic-origin company
```

## Webtoons: Korean Format and Global Expansion

```
WEBTOON ORIGIN: South Korea (2004)
  Naver (Korean tech giant; like Google) launched "Naver Webtoon" 2004
  Kakao Webtoon: competing platform

  FORMAT: vertical infinite scroll
  Optimized for: mobile phone reading
  Episodes: ~50-80 panels per episode; free; ad-supported
  Color: unlike manga; full color standard (cheaper digital production)
  Update: weekly (some daily)

  NAVER WEBTOON (now Webtoon Entertainment):
  100M+ monthly active users globally
  Available in: Korean, English, Chinese, Thai, Indonesian, and more
  IPO: Nasdaq (2024)

  US EXPANSION:
  LINE Webtoon (North American): launched 2014
  Tapas: competing US platform
  Many English-language creators with massive followings:
  LORE OLYMPUS (Rachel Smythe): Greek mythology; pink/blue aesthetic
    One of the most read comics in the world
    Print editions: #1 New York Times bestseller
  I LOVE YOOOO (MyKindOfStory): romance; millions of subscribers
  UNORDINARY (uru-chan): superhero school; political allegory

  TOWER OF GOD (SIU, 2010-): manhwa; billions of views
  Anime adaptation (2020); one of the most-read manhwa globally

WEBTOON ECONOMICS:
  Creator monetization: Webtoon pays established creators
  "Fast Pass": readers pay to access new episodes early
  Merchandise, licensing, print
  IP licensing: NAVER funds webtoon-to-anime adaptations
```

## Digital Comics Distribution

### The comiXology Story

```
COMIXOLOGY (2007-present; Amazon-owned 2014)
  US digital comics storefront
  Initially: independent; partnered with all major publishers
  2014: Amazon acquired for $200M
  Integration: Kindle; digital comics on same platform as ebooks

  GUIDED VIEW:
  comiXology patented "Guided View" -- panel-by-panel zoom
  Adapts print pages (designed for print spread) to screen reading
  Each panel presented fullscreen; swipe to next panel
  Criticism: removes page composition; destroys designer intent
  Defense: more accessible for reading on phone

  2022: comiXology MERGED INTO KINDLE
  No more standalone comiXology app
  Reading experience degraded (users complained)
  Publishers with own apps (Marvel Unlimited, DC Universe Infinite)
  gained users

PUBLISHER DIGITAL PLATFORMS:
  Marvel Unlimited: subscription ($9.99/month); 30,000+ comics
  DC Universe Infinite: subscription; DC back catalog
  Viz Manga: Viz/Shueisha digital manga
  Manga Plus: free, legal, simultaneous with Japan release

PIRACY:
  Still significant; scanlations for manga ahead of official release
  Full comic scans (CBR/CBZ format) on various sites
  Legal platforms + simultaneous global release: best piracy counter
```

## Motion Comics and Animated Comics

```
MOTION COMICS
  Comics panels with added: slight animation, narration, sound
  Marvel: "Astonishing X-Men" (2009); "Spider-Woman" motion comics
  DC: various

  CRITICAL RECEPTION: mostly negative
  Neither: pure comics (loses reader control; loses silence)
  Nor: proper animation (too few keyframes; mechanical)
  The hybrid fails to use either medium's strengths

  WHY IT DOESN'T WORK:
  Comics' power (reader-controlled time; reader closure)
  is destroyed when the panels animate and advance on their own.
  Animation's power (fluid motion; temporal expression)
  requires full animation, not limited motion on still panels.
  Motion comics are a compromise that satisfies neither.

  EXCEPTION: some experimental works use limited animation well
  (certain webcomics; Homestuck's Flash animations as chapters)
  When animation is additive (choice of creator; not necessary for story):
  it can work. When it replaces reader agency: it fails.
```

## The Creator-to-Reader Pipeline

```
WHAT CHANGED WITH WEBCOMICS AND DIGITAL
  Traditional: creator has no direct relationship with audience
               publisher intermediates; mail in; no feedback loop
  Web/digital: creator has direct feedback (comments; Twitter; Discord)
               Creator knows immediately what is resonating
               Audience can fund creators directly (Patreon)
               Creator can self-publish without permission or gatekeeping

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Genre diversity: creators serve specific niches not viable in print
  Audience size: a "small" successful webcomic (50K readers) = commercial
                 book deal impossible; Patreon viable
  Geographic diversity: creator in Philippines; reader in Norway; no friction
  Speed: creator publishes Tuesday; reader reads Tuesday

  THE LONG TAIL APPLIED TO COMICS:
  Amazon's long-tail thesis: the internet enables profitable niches
  Webcomics demonstrate: the long tail of comics readership
  Niche interests (vet school; knitting; specific fandoms) have audiences
  too small for traditional publishing; perfect for webcomics
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Webcomics are amateur"** -- Some are. Many professional careers began as
webcomics (Randall Munroe has no comics training; his work is read by millions).
The distribution channel (web) says nothing about production quality or
professional status.

**"Webtoon is a type of comic"** -- Webtoon is a platform (Naver Webtoon /
Webtoon Entertainment). "Webtoon" is also used generically to mean the vertical-
scroll format. These are two different senses of the word. A "webtoon" (format)
can be on any platform; the Webtoon platform hosts webtoons.

**"Digital comics are just scanned print comics"** -- Some are. But digital-first
comics (Webtoon native content; webcomics) are designed for digital reading:
vertical scroll, full color, panel sizing optimized for phone screens. These are
not print-first works; they are native digital comics.

**"Infinite canvas changed everything"** -- McCloud predicted it would;
it mostly didn't. The mobile phone changed comics reading (vertical scroll
is natural on phone) but the horizontal infinite canvas McCloud imagined
remains experimental. Practical constraints (phone orientation, scrolling
direction) shaped digital comics differently than McCloud expected.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Approach | Why |
|-----------|----------|-----|
| Primary audience is mobile-first | Vertical-scroll Webtoon format | Natural phone orientation; one panel per screen width; Webtoon/Tapas platforms |
| Niche audience that values creator relationship | Patreon direct model + merchandise | Parasocial monetization; creator retains IP and pricing control |
| Audience demonstrates purchase willingness | Print-on-demand backend (Kickstarter / TopatoCo) | Physical product = premium revenue; validates demand before inventory risk |
| Maximizing reach on existing platform | Webtoon Originals / Tapas contract | Platform provides audience but takes revenue share and may own adaptation rights |
| Experimental form / non-linear narrative | Infinite canvas or interactive format | McCloud's vision; limited commercial viability but high artistic potential |
| Building audience from zero | Free weekly schedule + social media + archive | Consistency and backlog are the growth engine; monetize after audience exists |
| The dominant digital comics format today         | Webtoon vertical scroll          |
| Largest webtoon platform                         | Naver Webtoon (100M+ users)      |
| What happened to comiXology                      | Amazon acquired; merged to Kindle|
| Why motion comics don't work                     | Neither comics nor animation     |
