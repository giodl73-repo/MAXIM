## Cassin's Dictionary of Untranslatables

Barbara Cassin (ed.), *Vocabulaire européen des philosophies: Dictionnaire des intraduisibles* (2004; English: *Dictionary of Untranslatables*, trans. ed. Apter et al., 2014):

**The project**: A dictionary of philosophical terms that resist translation — not because they are obscure, but because they do conceptual work in their original language that no single word in another language replicates.

**Key concept**: The untranslatable is not something that *cannot* be translated (everything can be explained in any language); it is something that *one keeps on translating* — a symptom of the untranslatable is that it requires re-translation, because no single rendering is adequate.

```
CASSIN — SELECTED ENTRIES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  GERMAN: Stimmung
  Standard English rendering: "mood," "atmosphere," "attunement"
  What it means: The tonal quality of a space or experience;
  Heidegger's use: our primordial openness to Being; the way
  we are always "attuned" to the world before deliberate thought.
  Why "mood" fails: Stimmung in German derives from Stimme (voice)
  and Stimmen (to tune an instrument). The harmony/tuning
  metaphor is present and meaningful. "Mood" is purely
  psychological; Stimmung is ontological.

  GERMAN: Dasein
  Standard rendering: "Being-there," "existence," "human reality"
  (Sartre used the French: "réalité humaine" — Heidegger objected)
  What it means: For Heidegger: the kind of being that humans are —
  characterized by thrown, temporal, relational existence.
  Not "a subject" or "a consciousness"; the very mode of existing.
  Why untranslatable: Anglophone philosophy has adopted "Dasein"
  as a loan word — the most common resolution.

  GREEK: Logos
  Renderings: "word," "reason," "discourse," "speech," "argument"
  What it means: In different contexts — rational discourse, the
  divine creative word (John 1:1), logical argument, the rationality
  of the cosmos. No English word covers all these senses.
  Consequence: John 1:1 "In the beginning was the Logos" becomes
  "In the beginning was the Word" (KJV) — losing the philosophical
  resonance with Greek rational tradition.

  FRENCH: Esprit
  Renderings: "spirit," "mind," "wit," "intelligence"
  What it means: Covers what English distributes between "spirit"
  (spiritual/religious), "mind" (cognitive), and "wit" (clever).
  Esprit de corps: rendered "esprit de corps" in English —
  English borrowed the whole phrase.

  ARABIC: Ta'aruf (تعارف)
  "The mutual self-disclosure between strangers"
  Cross-cultural social-epistemic concept
  No English equivalent; untranslated in Sufi contexts.
```

## Sapir-Whorf: Language Shapes Thought

The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis (Benjamin Lee Whorf, Edward Sapir, 1930s): the structure of a language influences or determines the thoughts its speakers can think.

```
SAPIR-WHORF — TWO VERSIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STRONG VERSION (Linguistic Determinism):
  You cannot think thoughts that your language cannot
  express. People who speak languages without tense
  cannot conceptualize time.
  Status: REJECTED by modern linguistics and psychology.
  Evidence against: Bilingual speakers can think in
  both frameworks; language acquisition shows children
  have prelinguistic concepts.

  WEAK VERSION (Linguistic Relativity):
  Language influences the ease, habitualness, and
  salience of certain cognitive operations — without
  making others impossible.
  Status: SUPPORTED by experimental evidence.
  Examples:
  • Color perception: speakers with more color terms
    distinguish colors faster (but can still distinguish
    colors they have no name for)
  • Spatial reasoning: languages that use absolute
    direction (north/south) vs. relative direction
    (left/right) affect navigation habits
  • Time perception: Mandarin speakers think of time
    vertically more often than English speakers

  TRANSLATION IMPLICATION:
  If weak Sapir-Whorf is right: translation across
  languages with different grammatical/lexical structures
  is not impossible but involves shifts in cognitive
  salience. The target-language reader genuinely
  experiences the translated text differently from
  a source-language reader — not just due to culture
  but due to cognitive structure.
```

## Color Terms: A Case Study in Linguistic Relativity

Color term research is the most studied example of linguistic relativity. The learner will find this connects to formal semantics (bounded regions in color space).

```
COLOR TERM STRUCTURE — RUSSIAN vs. ENGLISH
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  COLOR SPACE (blue region):
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ENGLISH: [ ─────────────── blue ──────────────── ]
           light blue            dark blue
           (all one category)

  RUSSIAN: [ goluboy (голубой) ] [ siniy (синий) ]
            light blue            dark blue
            (two distinct basic color terms)

  EXPERIMENTAL FINDING (Winawer et al. 2007):
  Russian speakers are faster at discriminating
  colors that cross the goluboy/siniy boundary
  than English speakers.
  For colors within one Russian category:
  no advantage over English speakers.

  The language makes the category boundary cognitively
  more salient — but doesn't make cross-boundary
  discrimination impossible for English speakers.
  (Weak Sapir-Whorf confirmed.)

  TRANSLATION IMPLICATION:
  Translating Nabokov's use of "siniy" as "blue" loses
  the precision. "Dark blue" may be the best English
  option, but it's analytical (two words, one is a
  modifier) where the Russian is lexical (one word,
  basic-level category).

  OTHER LANGUAGES WITH UNUSUAL COLOR SYSTEMS:
  Japanese: ao (青) = both blue and green (context-dependent)
  Vietnamese: xanh = blue/green (same word)
  Pirahã (Amazonia): only two color terms (light and dark)
  Japanese translating "traffic light is green" must
  specify which ao.
```

## Grammatical Untranslatability

Some languages grammaticalize distinctions that others leave implicit:

```
GRAMMATICAL DISTINCTIONS ACROSS LANGUAGES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  EVIDENTIALITY (Quechua, Turkish, Bulgarian):
  Grammar forces specification of how you know something.
  Quechua -mi = direct evidence (I saw it myself)
  Quechua -si = indirect evidence (I heard it)
  Quechua -chá = inference/speculation

  "It rained yesterday" (direct: I saw it)
  vs. "Apparently it rained yesterday" (reported)
  vs. "It must have rained yesterday" (inference)

  English must add words ("apparently," "I heard that")
  to mark these distinctions. Quechua has no choice
  but to mark them grammatically.

  TRANSLATION: English text into Quechua *forces*
  the translator to add epistemological commitments
  (direct vs. reported evidence) that may not be
  determinable from the English source.

  ASPECT (Russian):
  Russian verbs obligatorily distinguish perfective
  (action completed) from imperfective (ongoing/habitual).
  "He wrote a letter" — once? or was in the process of writing?
  Russian must choose. English can be ambiguous.

  HONORIFICS (Japanese, Korean):
  Multiple levels of politeness grammatically required
  in verb forms. "Eat" → taberu / tabemasu / meshiagaru
  depending on relative social status of speaker, hearer,
  subject. Translating into Japanese requires making
  social relationship explicit; translating out of Japanese
  loses that information.
```

## Translating Humor: The Hardest Case

Humor is probably the most translation-resistant linguistic phenomenon.

```
WHY HUMOR RESISTS TRANSLATION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PUNS:
  Use: a single phonological form with two meanings.
  These are language-specific — the form/meaning mapping
  differs between languages.
  English "Time flies like an arrow; fruit flies like a banana"
  is untranslatable as a pun because other languages don't
  have "flies" as both noun and verb with the same phonology.

  Solution: find an equivalent pun in the target language —
  but this changes the content. The translation is a new joke.

  CULTURAL REFERENCE:
  Comedy often relies on shared knowledge of cultural
  institutions, celebrities, events that non-members
  don't possess.
  British comedy (The Office, Fawlty Towers) loses texture
  in US translation — the specific class registers, the
  particular schools and accents that signal status.
  → US versions remake entirely rather than translate.

  PROSODY AND TIMING:
  Comedy is rhythm. "The delivery" — timing, pause,
  stress — is part of the joke. Written jokes translated
  word-for-word lose the cadence that made them funny.
  Standup comedy is nearly untranslatable.

  PRAGMATIC IMPLICATURE:
  Much humor works by implication — saying X to mean
  not-X (irony), or violating Gricean maxims deliberately.
  Irony markers vary cross-linguistically; what's
  self-evidently ironic in one culture requires explicit
  framing in another.
  → Risk: flat-footed literal translation of irony
    reads as sincere.

  WHAT WORKS:
  Slapstick, physical comedy: highly cross-linguistic
  (Chaplin still works globally).
  Universal situation comedy (embarrassment, bodily
  functions, status anxiety): more cross-linguistic.
  Wordplay, allusion, irony: least translatable.
```

## Legal Translation: EU as Case Study

The European Union produces legislation in 24 official languages simultaneously. All versions are equally authentic legal texts. This is one of the largest translation enterprises in the world and illustrates the problem of legal equivalence.

```
EU TRANSLATION INFRASTRUCTURE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SCALE:
  European Commission alone: ~1.75 million pages translated
  per year (pre-Brexit; now slightly fewer)
  ~24 languages × ~1,800 pages/language/day (order of magnitude)

  INSTITUTIONAL STRUCTURE:
  DGT (Directorate-General for Translation): ~2,500 linguists
  plus freelancers
  EURAMIS: translation memory system (shared across institutions)
  IATE: Inter-Agency Terminology (shared glossary)

  THE LEGAL EQUIVALENCE PROBLEM:
  When a Directive says "unfair terms" in English,
  and "Koopmansovereenkomst" in Dutch, and "clauses
  abusives" in French — are these legally equivalent?

  CASE C-191/99 (CIBA-GEIGY):
  Court of Justice ruled on divergent interpretations
  of "any other person" in English vs. equivalent
  in German. Court: "authentic in all official languages"
  means comparison of all versions required.

  TRANSLATION STRATEGY:
  EU translators use functional equivalence for legal terms:
  the translation must have the same legal effect in the
  target legal system, not just the same literal meaning.
  "Unfair terms" → German "missbräuchliche Klauseln" —
  not word-for-word ("unfair" = "unbillig" in German)
  but the legal category that performs the same function.
```

## Machine Translation and the Untranslatable

This is directly relevant to the learner's MT/AI background:

```
WHAT MT HANDLES WELL vs. POORLY
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NMT HANDLES WELL:
  • Literal content transfer (facts, events, descriptions)
  • Common idioms (trained on millions of translated examples)
  • Technical text with stable terminology
  • News-style prose (clear, explicit, limited cultural depth)

  NMT HANDLES POORLY:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ PRAGMATIC FAILURE:                                       │
  │ Irony, sarcasm, understatement often lost.               │
  │ "That's just great" (sarcastic) often renders as        │
  │ straightforwardly positive.                              │
  │                                                          │
  │ CULTURE-SPECIFIC CONTENT:                                │
  │ Jokes, allusions, culture-specific register              │
  │ smoothed over into generic fluency.                      │
  │                                                          │
  │ GRAMMATICAL FORCED CHOICES:                              │
  │ English "you" → Spanish "tu/Ud./vosotros/Uds."           │
  │ NMT must choose a politeness/number form.                │
  │ If the English is ambiguous, the Spanish cannot be —     │
  │ NMT makes a guess that may be wrong.                     │
  │                                                          │
  │ LEXICAL GAPS:                                            │
  │ "Schadenfreude" in German source → ?                     │
  │ MT either loans the word or gives a clumsy paraphrase.   │
  │                                                          │
  │ LOW-RESOURCE LANGUAGES:                                  │
  │ Quechua, Yoruba, Pirahã — little parallel training data  │
  │ → MT quality drops dramatically.                         │
  │                                                          │
  │ BLEU SCORE BLINDNESS:                                    │
  │ BLEU measures n-gram overlap with reference.             │
  │ It cannot detect pragmatic failure, cultural inadequacy, │
  │ register mismatch, or gendered defaults.                 │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Phenomenon | Translatable? | Best approach |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Lexical gaps (Schadenfreude, saudade) | Yes, with loss | Borrowing + footnote; or long gloss |
| Color terms (Russian siniy vs. goluboy) | Partial loss | "Dark blue"/"light blue" — loses basic-level status |
| Grammatical evidentiality | Addition required | Translator must add epistemic markers not in original |
| Puns | Usually no | Find equivalent pun in target language (changes content) |
| Irony | Often lost by NMT | Human translators use cultural knowledge; NMT often fails |
| Legal terms | Functional equivalence | Match legal function in target legal system, not words |
| Philosophical terms (Dasein, Logos) | Borrowing common | Adopt as loan word + explanation |

## Common Confusion Points

**Untranslatability is not impossibility**: Nothing is truly untranslatable in the strong sense — everything can be explained in any language with enough words. The untranslatable is what requires more words, or different words, or words that lose something in the process. Cassin's framing: it's what you *keep on translating*.

**Sapir-Whorf ≠ "Germans can't think like English speakers"**: The weak version of linguistic relativity (which has experimental support) is about cognitive ease and salience, not about conceptual impossibility. Russian speakers are faster at certain color discriminations because their grammar makes the category salient. This is real, measurable, and modest.

**BLEU doesn't measure untranslatability**: BLEU measures n-gram overlap with reference translations. A translation that handles a cultural untranslatable brilliantly (by finding an equivalent in the target culture) might score low BLEU because it diverges from the reference translation's approach to the same problem. BLEU is systematically blind to pragmatic and cultural translation quality.

**Legal translation is not special — it's just high-stakes**: Legal translation faces the same equivalence problem as all translation, but the consequences of pragmatic failure are enforceable court judgments rather than aesthetic displeasure. The EU's "all versions equally authentic" rule is a legal fiction that forces courts to do comparative interpretation across languages.
