## The Retranslation Phenomenon

Great works of literature get retranslated repeatedly. Why?

**The standard view**: Each era retranslates for itself. Language changes; the original stays fixed; what was natural translation in 1900 sounds archaic in 2000. Also: better scholarship, new manuscript discoveries, evolving theoretical approaches.

**The deeper view (Benjamin)**: The original has an "afterlife" — it changes as history changes. New translations don't replace old ones; they add to the original's life.

### Proust: Multiple Translations as Demonstration

Marcel Proust, *À la recherche du temps perdu* (1913-27):

- **C.K. Scott Moncrieff** (1922-30): The canonical first translation. Titled *Remembrance of Things Past* (after Shakespeare, not Proust's title — and wrong in sense). Highly literary, period-appropriate. Criticized for being too Victorian, too decorative.
- **Terence Kilmartin** revision (1981): Updated Scott Moncrieff; corrected errors; slightly modernized.
- **Andreas Mayor and D.J. Enright** revision (1992): Further revised; used the correct title *In Search of Lost Time*.
- **Various new translations (2002-)**: Penguin project assigned different volumes to different translators (Lydia Davis on *Swann's Way*, etc.); aiming for accuracy over elegance.

```
PROUST TRANSLATION EXAMPLE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ORIGINAL (opening of Swann's Way):
  "Longtemps, je me suis couché de bonne heure."

  LITERAL: "For a long time, I went to bed early."

  SCOTT MONCRIEFF (1922):
  "For a long time I used to go to bed early."

  LYDIA DAVIS (2002):
  "For a long time, I went to bed early."

  THE DIFFERENCE:
  "Used to" (habitual past, English) vs. passé composé
  (French; technically completed action, but idiomatically
   habitual in this context).
  Davis's version is more literal but "early" at the end
  gives it a different cadence from Scott Moncrieff's.
  Neither is clearly right — Davis prioritizes accuracy
  over rhythm; Scott Moncrieff vice versa.
```

### Homer: The Impossibility of the Definitive

Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* have been translated into English more than any other text. Each translation reflects its era's priorities:

```
HOMER TRANSLATIONS — COMPARATIVE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  CHAPMAN (1611) — George Chapman
  Iambic pentameter; Elizabethan richness
  Keats: "Then felt I like some watcher of the skies /
  When a new planet swims into his ken" (upon first reading)
  Quality: poetic; archaic; not very accurate to Greek

  POPE (1715-1720) — Alexander Pope
  Heroic couplets; Augustan elegance
  Not accurate to Greek (Pope barely read Greek)
  Brilliant poem; not Homer

  RICHMOND LATTIMORE (1951) — literal, scholarly
  Long hexameter-like lines; deliberately Homeric
  Very accurate; less readable as poetry
  Academic standard for 50 years

  ROBERT FAGLES (1990) — balance of accuracy and accessibility
  Free verse; contemporary idiom; dramatic
  Most-read; criticized for over-dramatizing

  EMILY WILSON (2017) — first woman to translate Homer
  Deliberately plain style; 12,110 lines (same count as Greek)
  Attention to gender: rendered woman words more precisely
  "Tell me about a complicated man" (first line) —
  vs. Fagles: "Sing to me of the man, Muse" —
  Wilson removes the invocation; Fagles preserves it
  Critical: is "complicated man" Odysseus or modern projections?
```

## Translating Poetry

Robert Frost: "Poetry is what is lost in translation."

The challenge:
- Meter and rhyme schemes are language-specific
- Sound effects (alliteration, assonance) cannot typically be replicated
- The meaning of a poem includes its formal properties — the way the form enacts the content

```
STRATEGIES FOR TRANSLATING POETRY
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  OPTION 1: Reproduce the form in target language
  → Often produces awkward or forced verse
  → German tends to succeed more than English
  (German more inflected; can maintain Latin-like case)

  OPTION 2: Use equivalent target-language form
  → Horace's odes → English odes in Horatian tradition
  → Loses the specific formal identity
  → Gains naturalness in target language

  OPTION 3: Free verse
  → Most common in contemporary literary translation
  → Captures meaning and rhythmic intention loosely
  → Pope's solution: Homeric meter → English heroic couplet
    (not equivalent, but analogous)

  OPTION 4: Prose translation
  → Often the most accurate
  → Clearly signals that the translation is not poetry
  → Nabokov for Onegin (eventually)

  OPTION 5: Imitation (Lowell)
  → Use the foreign poem as material; write a new poem
  → Labeled as "imitation," not "translation"
  → Lowell's *Imitations* (1961): Rimbaud, Baudelaire,
    Pasternak, Montale — brilliant English poems;
    loose relationship to source
```

## Cultural Untranslatables in Literature

Literary texts are dense with culturally specific references:

```
UNTRANSLATABLES IN LITERARY CONTEXT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Japanese: mono no aware (物の哀れ)
  "Pathos of things" — sensitivity to impermanence;
  the bittersweet feeling of fleeting beauty.
  Core to Japanese aesthetics (The Tale of Genji).
  Translation options:
  • Transliterate: mono no aware
  • Gloss: "the pathos of things"
  • Expand: "sensitivity to ephemera and their passing"
  Each loses something.

  Russian: toska (тоска)
  Nabokov's definition: "a longing with nothing to long for";
  deep melancholy, anguish, spiritual restlessness.
  Used extensively in Chekhov and Tolstoy.
  Translation options:
  • "Longing," "anguish," "melancholy" — all partial
  • Transliterate: toska (Nabokov's choice sometimes)

  Portuguese: saudade (saudade)
  Longing for something loved and lost or perhaps
  never possessed; nostalgia + longing + melancholy.
  Central to fado (Portuguese music).
  Translation: "longing," "nostalgia" — neither captures the
  precise quality; "saudade" is often untranslated in English

  German: Weltschmerz (world-pain)
  The pain caused by comparing the world as it is
  with the world as it ought to be.
  Romantic literature: Werther, Novalis.
  English borrowing: Weltschmerz.

  Yiddish: kvetch, schmaltz, chutzpah
  All borrowed into English — but the borrowings
  carry cultural freight that dilutes in transit.
```

## Canonical Translator Figures

### Constance Garnett (1861-1946): Russia in English

Garnett translated virtually all of the major Russian authors: Tolstoy (17 volumes), Dostoevsky (13 volumes), Turgenev (15 volumes), Chekhov (13 volumes), Gogol (6 volumes), Herzen (6 volumes).

She learned Russian in adulthood while recovering from illness. She translated at extraordinary speed (some say she smoothed out difficulties she couldn't parse by simply translating over them with English idiom).

**Nabokov's critique**: Garnett domesticated Russian, lost the "Russian-ness." Russian syntax is different from English; Garnett ironed it flat into English smoothness.

**The counterfactual defense**: Without Garnett, the English-speaking world would have had far less Russian literature. Her translations made Chekhov and Dostoevsky central to English-language modernism (they influenced Joseph Conrad, Virginia Woolf, D.H. Lawrence). Good-enough translations that open a literature have value that perfect translations that arrive late don't.

### Gregory Rabassa (1922-2016): Latin American Boom in English

Rabassa translated the major Latin American novels: García Márquez (*One Hundred Years of Solitude*, 1970), Julio Cortázar (*Hopscotch*, 1966), Mario Vargas Llosa (*Conversation in the Cathedral*, 1975).

García Márquez famously said Rabassa's English translation of *One Hundred Years of Solitude* was better than the original Spanish — a comment that scandalized purists but made Rabassa's reputation. What García Márquez meant: Rabassa found English equivalents that were as musical and rhythmically powerful as the Spanish, not merely accurate.

### Anthea Bell (1936-2018): Asterix and Beyond

Bell translated the Asterix comics from French (with Derek Hockridge), adapting untranslatable puns, Roman name jokes, and French cultural references into equivalent English humor. This is arguably the most technically demanding translation problem: how do you translate a pun?

Asterix character names: In French, Astérix, Obélix, Getafix (Panoramix in French). Bell created English names that both fit the Roman-era comic names pattern (-ix suffix) and were punny in English. *Getafix* (the druid who makes the magic potion) = "get a fix" (drug reference that's funny but doesn't interfere with the story). *Cacofonix* (the bard) = cacophony + -ix. These are creative solutions, not translations in the standard sense.

## The Gender Politics of Translation

The history of literary translation includes a structural gender problem:

```
WOMEN AS INVISIBLE TRANSLATORS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  HISTORICAL PATTERN:
  Translation has historically been coded as feminine,
  derivative work — reproduction vs. original creation.
  Women were considered suited for it because it was
  "secondary" — receiving and conveying, not originating.

  CONSEQUENCE:
  Many important translations were done by women who
  received less pay, less credit, and less recognition
  than their male contemporaries who were writing
  "original" works.

  EXAMPLES:
  Mary Shelley translated works from French and Italian.
  Constance Garnett (see above) — male critics dismissed
    her translations while depending on them.
  Edith Grossman translated Cervantes and García Márquez
    but the latter's name dominates.

  RECENT CORRECTION:
  Emily Wilson's Homer (2017) explicitly highlighted:
  Previous translators of Homer included no women.
  Wilson's translation brought attention to gender
  assumptions embedded in previous renderings:
  • *polytropos* (Odyssey first line): "many-turning"
  • Fagles: "of twists and turns"
  • Wilson: "complicated" — is the man cunning/devious
    or complex? Wilson claims previous translations
    romanticized Odysseus; her "complicated" is more
    neutral and accurate.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Lost in translation" vs. "gained in translation"**: The discourse around literary translation is dominated by the loss frame (Frost). The gain frame is less discussed but real: a skilled translator can make explicit what the original left implicit, find equivalents that work better in the target language, and occasionally surpass the original's effect. García Márquez's assessment of Rabassa is not false modesty.

**Retranslation ≠ saying earlier translations are wrong**: New translations are usually not corrections of errors in previous ones (though they do correct some errors). They are new readings of the same text — new productions of the original's "afterlife." Scott Moncrieff and Lydia Davis are not in competition; they offer different readings of Proust.

**Emily Wilson's "complicated"**: Wilson's choice to render *polytropos* as "complicated" rather than "many-turnings/cunning" is not just feminist politics — it's a defensible translation that keeps the ambiguity of the Greek. The Greek can mean clever, devious, resourceful, or experienced. "Complicated" preserves this ambiguity in English where the others foreclose it.

**Anthea Bell's Asterix is harder than Proust**: This counterintuitive claim has scholarly support. Translating Proust requires accuracy, rhythm, and literary sensitivity. Translating Asterix requires all of that plus the invention of puns, name jokes, and visual-verbal humor that has no source equivalents. Bell and Hockridge had to create English wit that didn't exist in the French, not simply find English equivalents for existing French wit.
