## Eugene Onegin: The Text

Alexander Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin* (1833) is the foundational work of Russian literature:
- Novel in verse: 14-line stanzas (the "Onegin stanza") with a fixed rhyme scheme (AbAbCCddEffEgg)
- 389 stanzas; ~5,500 lines
- Combines Byronic hero with Russian social criticism, proto-realism, and lyrical intensity
- Pushkin's Russian is considered the standard of the language — his relationship to Russian is comparable to Shakespeare's to English

The translation problem is extreme: the Onegin stanza's rhyme scheme is only sustainable in Russian because Russian is highly inflected (the grammar provides natural rhymes). English, with its limited case endings and smaller rhyme vocabulary, cannot replicate the scheme without grotesque distortions.

## Nabokov's Principle

Nabokov stated his principle explicitly in the introduction and in polemical essays:

> "The clumsiest literal translation is a thousand times more useful than the prettiest paraphrase." (*Problems of Translation: Onegin in English*, 1955)

> "To reproduce the rhyme scheme and preserve the fire of the poem, the translator would have to sacrifice faithfulness; but nobody really cares about fire, and those who want beauty had better seek it in Pushkin's Russian."

```
NABOKOV'S TRANSLATION HIERARCHY
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  WHAT MUST BE PRESERVED:
  1. Exact meaning of every word (semantic fidelity)
  2. Syntactic structure (as much as English allows)
  3. Every image, every association
  4. Footnotes for anything that requires cultural context

  WHAT CAN BE SACRIFICED:
  1. Rhyme (entirely — no rhymes in Nabokov's Onegin)
  2. Meter (prose rhythms acceptable; strict meter no)
  3. Poetic "beauty" in English (irrelevant — serves the
     translator's vanity, not the reader's understanding)
  4. Readability (if the reader wants to understand
     Pushkin, they should learn Russian)

  THE ARGUMENT:
  Any rhymed or metered English Onegin requires
  filling lines with padding, replacing Pushkin's
  words with approximate synonyms for rhyme or meter,
  inverting syntax for scansion.
  These are all lies — false representations of what
  Pushkin wrote.
  The honest translation says: here is what Pushkin
  actually wrote, word by word, with footnotes.
  The reader must do the aesthetic work.
```

## Sample: Nabokov's Rendering

The opening of Chapter 1, Stanza 1:

```
PUSHKIN'S RUSSIAN (transliterated):
My dyadya samykh chesnykh pravil,
Kogda ne v shutku zanemog,
On uvazhat' sebya zastavil,
I luchshe vydumat' ne mog.

NABOKOV'S TRANSLATION:
"My uncle has most honest principles:
when he was taken seriously ill,
he forced one to respect him
and nothing better could invent.

His example is others' science;
but, good God, what a bore it is
to sit by a sick man by day and night,
without ever stepping beyond the house!"

NABOKOV'S FOOTNOTES (abbreviated):
"most honest principles": Pushkin alludes to
La Fontaine's fable opening ("un animal qu'on nomme
Asne...") and possibly to Krylov. The phrase is
immediately ambiguous — is the uncle praiseworthy
or ironically described?

"forced one to respect him": Russian "zastavil"
forces the sense of compulsion; the uncle's illness
compels respect against the narrator's will.
```

Compare what a verse translation might do:

```
A VERSE TRANSLATION (hypothetical, in the style
of Charles Johnston or others):
"My uncle, man of firm convictions,
By falling sick has proved his worth;
His illness, meriting inflictions
Of honour upon younger birth..."
```

Nabokov's point: the verse version has added "younger birth" to fill the meter, changed the tone from ironic to mock-heroic, and lost the specific Pushkinian joke.

## The Wilson Polemic

Edmund Wilson, Nabokov's friend and prominent American literary critic, reviewed the Onegin translation in the *New York Review of Books* (July 15, 1965) and demolished it:

**Wilson's charges**:
1. Nabokov's English is sometimes incorrect — wrong idioms, archaic usages
2. His "literal" translation is often not as literal as he claims
3. He misunderstood certain Russian idioms (Wilson challenged specific translations)
4. The commentary is sometimes brilliant but often pedantic
5. The resulting text is unreadable and defeats its own purpose

**Nabokov's response** (*NYRB*, February 1966) was characteristic:
- Wilson's Russian is inadequate to judge; he makes elementary errors in his critique
- The specific counterexamples Wilson cites are wrong
- The charge that certain translations are incorrect is itself incorrect
- Wilson's aesthetics are "journalistic"

## Nabokov vs. Lowell's "Imitations"

Nabokov's position was not just about Onegin — it was a general attack on "imitation" as a mode.

Robert Lowell's *Imitations* (1961) — acknowledged creative reimaginings of Sappho, Rimbaud, Baudelaire, Pasternak, Montale — appeared a few years before Nabokov's Onegin. Lowell was praised for the English poems he produced.

Nabokov's position: Lowell's "imitations" are not translations and should not be marketed as translations. They are Lowell's poems, using foreign poems as raw material. This is not dishonest if labeled correctly; it is dishonest if labeled as "based on" or "translated from" in a way that implies fidelity to the source.

## What Nabokov's Translation Reveals

Nabokov's interlinear Onegin is not primarily a gift to readers — it is a theoretical argument about the nature of translation, made through practice.

**What it reveals about Pushkin**: The footnotes (1,000+ pages) constitute Nabokov's attempt to reconstruct the entire cultural world that Pushkin's original reader inhabited. Allusions to French literature, Russian history, German philosophy, 19th-century fashion — all explained. The translation + commentary is a machine for recovering Pushkin's context.

**What it reveals about translation**: Any translation that reads well as poetry in English has substituted the translator's aesthetic judgment for Pushkin's. The reader who enjoys a smooth English Onegin is not enjoying Pushkin; they are enjoying the translator's performance of Pushkin. Nabokov's interlinear strips this away: here is what Pushkin actually wrote, with all the footnotes needed to understand it. Your aesthetic pleasure must come from your imagination of what the Russian sounds like, guided by the scholarly apparatus.

**The paradox of the great stylist**: Nabokov in English was a supreme prose artist (Lolita, Pale Fire). His choice to produce an ugly, literal translation is not a failure of his skills but a demonstration of his principles: the translator has no right to impose their own aesthetic on the original.

## Brodsky's Response

Joseph Brodsky (Russian-American poet, Nobel 1987) engaged with the Nabokov approach from a different angle. He translated his own poems from Russian to English, working with co-translators.

Brodsky's counter-argument to Nabokov: **Poetic form is part of the meaning**, not separable from it. A poem that rhymes and scans is a different object from a prose approximation of the same words. To give a reader prose in place of verse is to give them something structurally false.

Brodsky's own translations were criticized for English that was sometimes awkward in pursuit of formal equivalence — choosing rhyme over idiomatic English. His response: better awkward rhyme than fluent prose. He was trying to do the opposite of Garnett.

## Implications for Machine Translation

Nabokov's position has a direct application to MT evaluation that the learner can appreciate:

If Nabokov is right:
- BLEU score (which measures n-gram overlap with reference translations) is measuring the wrong thing — it would penalize Nabokov's Onegin for differing from smoother reference translations
- A "fluent" NMT output that reads well in English but has substituted the system's "aesthetic judgment" (statistical optimization) for the source is exactly the kind of translation Nabokov condemned
- The best MT system by Nabokov's standards would be one that was maximally literal — and therefore maximally low-BLEU against human reference translations

If Nida / the dynamic equivalence camp is right:
- Fluent NMT that produces the right effect on the reader is exactly right
- BLEU against human dynamic-equivalence translations is a reasonable metric

The theoretical debate between Nabokov and Nida maps directly onto a debate about what MT should optimize for.

## Common Confusion Points

**Nabokov's translation is not a failure to achieve style**: It is a deliberate theoretical commitment. Nabokov knew perfectly well how to write elegant English; he chose not to in this case. This is a consistent position, however wrong it may be aesthetically.

**The commentary matters as much as the translation**: The four-volume Onegin is not primarily a translation — it is the first comprehensive scholarly edition of Onegin in English. The 1,000+ pages of commentary is where Nabokov's interpretive genius is displayed. The translation proper is the framework; the commentary is the substance.

**Wilson was not simply wrong**: While Nabokov won the reputation battle (he was the better writer, more publicly aggressive, and his literary reputation was at a peak while Wilson's was declining), Wilson identified real weaknesses. Some of Nabokov's "archaisms" are pedantic rather than precise. The debate about "rememorating" is not as clear-cut as Nabokov claimed.

**Pushkin is the test case because it's hardest**: Nabokov chose Pushkin (and Russian) for his argument because Pushkin's verse is maximally untranslatable by the standards of any English verse translator. The Onegin stanza can't be reproduced in English without grotesque distortion. This makes Nabokov's point more compelling — the very best case for verse translation fails.
