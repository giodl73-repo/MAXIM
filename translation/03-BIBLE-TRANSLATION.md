## The Septuagint (LXX)

The Septuagint (from Latin *septuaginta* — seventy; tradition says 72 Jewish scholars translated it in 72 days, all producing identical results) is the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, produced for the Jewish diaspora in Alexandria, Egypt, roughly 3rd-2nd centuries BCE.

### Why It Matters

1. **The NT was written in Greek**: NT authors quoting the OT usually quoted the LXX, not the Hebrew. This means NT theology is partly built on LXX translation choices.

2. **Theological consequences of Greek choices**:

```
SEPTUAGINT TRANSLATION CHOICES WITH CONSEQUENCES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  HEBREW almah (Isaiah 7:14)
  → LXX Greek parthenos ("virgin")
  Hebrew: almah = "young woman" (not specifically a virgin)
  Other Hebrew word for virgin: betulah
  LXX chose the stronger term.
  → Matthew 1:22-23 quotes LXX: "a virgin shall conceive"
  → Virginal conception of Jesus is partly derived from
     LXX's translation choice.
  → Jerome's Vulgate follows LXX: virgo.
  → Catholic Mariology depends partly on this translation.

  HEBREW nephesh (Genesis 2:7)
  → LXX Greek psyche ("soul")
  Hebrew: nephesh = "living being" (breath of life; the whole person)
  Greek psyche has Platonic connotations: immortal soul
  → Christianity absorbed Platonic soul/body dualism partly
     through LXX's translation of nephesh as psyche.
  → The "immortal soul" concept is not clearly in the Hebrew.

  HEBREW YHWH (the divine name)
  → LXX Greek Kyrios ("Lord")
  Hebrew: YHWH is the personal name of God (the Tetragrammaton)
  LXX replaced it throughout with Kyrios.
  → NT uses Kyrios for Jesus in contexts quoting OT YHWH texts.
  → High Christology ("Jesus is Lord") depends partly on
     this substitution.

  HEBREW avodah (Exodus 20:5)
  → LXX Greek zelos ("jealousy")
  Hebrew: avodah = "strong devotion" → translated as divine jealousy
  → "Jealous God" becomes a theological proposition
     partly from translation choice.
```

## Jerome's Vulgate (382-405 CE)

Jerome (347-420 CE) translated the entire Bible into Latin over roughly 25 years. His method was controversial from the start.

### The Textual Problem

Before Jerome, multiple Latin translations (*Vetus Latina* — "Old Latin") circulated, all slightly different, often poorly done. Pope Damasus commissioned Jerome to produce a reliable standard text.

Jerome went back to the Hebrew for the OT — a controversial choice, since the LXX was what the church had been using. Jewish scholars helped him with the Hebrew.

```
JEROME'S APPROACH vs. ALTERNATIVES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  JEROME'S CHOICE: Hebrew text for OT (Hebraica veritas)
  • Learned Hebrew from Jewish teachers
  • Believed the Hebrew was the original
  • Produced: more accurate OT than LXX-based translations

  CRITICS AT THE TIME (Augustine):
  • Christian church uses LXX; that's what the
    Apostles knew; why abandon it?
  • Disturbing the faithful with new text
  • Augustine: LXX was divinely guided — its
    departures from Hebrew are the Holy Spirit's
    corrections

  LONG-TERM CONSEQUENCE:
  Jerome's Vulgate was declared the official Catholic
  Bible at the Council of Trent (1546) — in response to
  the Reformation challenge. Protestantism insisted on
  original Hebrew/Greek; Catholic Church defended Vulgate.
  → The text itself became a doctrinal battleground.
```

### Deuterocanonical Books

Jerome included books present in the LXX but not in the Hebrew canon (Tobit, Judith, 1-2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, additions to Daniel and Esther). He called them *deuterocanonical* (second canon) — accepted but of lesser authority.

Protestants followed Jerome's instinct and removed them (*Apocrypha*). Catholics, at Trent, declared them fully canonical. This difference still structures the Catholic/Protestant Bible today: Catholic Bibles have 73 books; Protestant 66.

## Luther's German Bible

Covered in 02-HISTORY-TRANSLATION in political context. Here: the translation choices in detail.

### The "Alone" Controversy

Luther's translation of Romans 3:28:

- Greek: *pistei* (by faith)
- Vulgate: *per fidem* (through faith)
- Luther's German: *durch den Glauben allein* (through faith **alone**)

Luther's defense:
1. German grammar: "Man is justified by faith" requires the particle *allein* to be idiomatic in German — otherwise the German reads as if faith is one among several means of justification.
2. The *sense* of Paul's argument throughout Romans is that faith alone justifies; Luther's addition makes explicit what Paul intends.
3. Augustine and Ambrose can be read as supporting *sola fide*.

Catholic response: Adding a word to scripture is equivalent to falsifying it; this is precisely the theological controversy Luther was adjudicating by translation.

Modern assessment: Luther's *allein* is a translation into a specific theological tradition, not a neutral rendering. The Greek text does not say "alone." This is dynamic equivalence serving a theological program.

## The King James Version (1611)

The King James Bible (commissioned 1604, published 1611) is the most influential English text ever produced.

### The Political Context

James I wanted a Bible that would serve his political purposes:
- The Geneva Bible (1560) had marginal notes that challenged royal authority (notably: notes on Exodus supporting resistance to tyrants)
- The Bishops' Bible (1568) was the authorized version but poorly executed
- James convened the Hampton Court Conference (1604) and authorized a new translation

### The Method

47 scholars organized into six companies (Westminster, Cambridge, Oxford) each handling different books. Each company translated, reviewed within the company, then passed to the other companies for review.

```
KJV PRODUCTION PROCESS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SIX COMPANIES OF TRANSLATORS:
  1. Westminster Company I: Genesis - 2 Kings
  2. Cambridge Company I: 1 Chronicles - Ecclesiastes
  3. Oxford Company I: Isaiah - Malachi
  4. Cambridge Company II: Apocrypha
  5. Oxford Company II: Gospels, Acts, Revelation
  6. Westminster Company II: Epistles

  PROCESS:
  Each company → draft translation
  → circulated to all other companies
  → final review committee of 12
  → printer

  SOURCES:
  • Hebrew MT for OT
  • Erasmus's Greek (Textus Receptus) for NT
    (not the best Greek text — later manuscripts
    used; TR is rejected by modern text criticism)
  • Heavy reliance on Tyndale for much of the NT
    (estimates: 83-84% of Tyndale's phrasing retained)
  • Previous English translations: Coverdale, Matthew,
    Great Bible, Geneva, Bishops' Bible all consulted
```

### "The Noblest Monument of English Prose"

H.L. Mencken's characterization is the standard one. Why?

The KJV translators worked at a moment when English literary prose was at a formative stage. They brought:
- **Cadence**: The KJV reads aloud with rhythmic power. "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want." The stress patterns are not accidental — the translators were attentive to spoken rhythm.
- **Saxon vocabulary**: They preferred Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate synonyms, making the text feel immediate. "Love" not "affection"; "sorrow" not "lamentation."
- **Dignified simplicity**: Short sentences; declarative structure; no rhetoric of philosophical complexity.
- **Memorability**: The KJV has supplied English with idioms: "the skin of my teeth," "how are the mighty fallen," "a thorn in the flesh," "the writing on the wall," "the powers that be," "fight the good fight."

### The Textual Basis Problem

The KJV NT is based on the Textus Receptus — the Greek text published by Erasmus and later by Stephanus and Beza. The TR is based on late medieval manuscripts, not the best available texts.

```
TEXTUAL CRITICISM AND BIBLE TRANSLATION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  19TH-20TH CENTURY MANUSCRIPT DISCOVERIES:
  Codex Sinaiticus (1844): 4th century Greek Bible;
  oldest nearly complete NT; many variants from TR
  Codex Vaticanus: 4th century; older than TR manuscripts
  Dead Sea Scrolls (1947): Hebrew OT; older than Masoretic Text;
  confirmed MT's accuracy for most books

  RESULT: Modern translations use critical eclectic text
  (Nestle-Aland Greek NT; Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia OT)
  These omit or bracket passages in KJV that don't appear
  in oldest manuscripts:
  • Mark 16:9-20 (the long ending — not in oldest MSS)
  • John 7:53-8:11 (woman taken in adultery — not in oldest MSS)
  • 1 John 5:7-8 (the Comma Johanneum — clearly a later addition)

  This creates controversy: King James Only movement insists
  on KJV's TR basis; mainstream scholarship uses critical text.
  The argument is simultaneously textual and theological.
```

## The Politics of Every Translation Choice

The seemingly technical decision of which word to use can have political, theological, and social consequences:

```
TRANSLATION AS POLITICAL CHOICE — EXAMPLES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  GENDER-INCLUSIVE LANGUAGE:
  Greek adelphoi = "brothers" (or "brothers and sisters"?)
  NRSV (1989): "brothers and sisters" (inclusive)
  ESV (2001): "brothers" (formal; masculine)
  Controversy: Is the Greek generic masculine (typical in
  ancient languages, meaning all people) or specifically male?

  "YOUNG MAN" vs. "SERVANT":
  Hebrew eved = "slave/servant/worker"
  Some translations: "servant" (softer)
  Accurate: "slave" (in Philemon, Paul asks Philemon
  to receive Onesimus back — as slave or freed?)
  Translation choice affects whether the NT endorses or
  challenges slavery.

  "HOMOSEXUALITY":
  Greek arsenokoitai (1 Corinthians 6:9) — disputed
  Some: "sexual perverts" (broad)
  Others: "sodomites" (specific)
  Others: "those who practice homosexuality" (modern concept)
  The Greek word is rare; its meaning disputed;
  the translation choice is the theology.

  "RICH" vs. "WEALTHY":
  Hebrew/Greek words for wealth vary in moral valence.
  Translation choice affects how passages about wealth
  read as moral prescriptions.
```

|-------------|---------|----------|-------------|

## Common Confusion Points

**The KJV's "thee" and "thou" are not archaic — they were precise**: Modern English has lost the *thou/you* distinction (singular/plural familiar/formal). In Elizabethan English, *thou* was singular intimate; *you* was plural or formal. The KJV uses *thou* for God (intimate, singular) consistently. Modern translations lose this precision because English no longer has the distinction.

**LXX and Masoretic Text are often different**: The Masoretic Text (MT) — the Hebrew standard text — was finalized by Jewish scholars (Masoretes) 7th-10th centuries CE. The LXX was translated from Hebrew texts in the 3rd-2nd centuries BCE — texts that sometimes differ significantly from the MT. The Dead Sea Scrolls (100 BCE-70 CE) show that both text types existed in antiquity; neither is simply "the original."

**"Authorized Version" = KJV**: The KJV is often called the "Authorized Version" (AV) in British usage, but there is no record of it actually being formally authorized by royal proclamation. It says "Appointed to be read in churches" on the title page. The "authorized" designation is customary, not legal.

**Luther's 95 Theses were not primarily about indulgences as commerce**: The scholarly history shows that Thesis 1 reads: "When our Lord and Master Jesus Christ says 'Repent' [*poenitentiam agite*], he wills the entire life of believers to be one of repentance." Luther's quarrel was with the translation (and the theology derived from it) of *metanoeite* as "do penance" (exterior acts) rather than "repent" (interior change). The indulgence controversy was downstream of this textual/theological point.
