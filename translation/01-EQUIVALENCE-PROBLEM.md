# The Equivalence Problem: What Is Translation?

## The Big Picture

```
THE EQUIVALENCE PROBLEM — THEORETICAL MAP
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  WHAT IS TRANSLATION?

  TRIVIAL ANSWER: Replacing words in one language with
  words in another language.
  WHY WRONG: Ignores that languages have different
  grammatical categories, conceptual structures,
  rhetorical traditions, and cultural contexts.

  THE REAL QUESTION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ What, exactly, should be "equivalent"?                 │
  │                                                        │
  │  A. The words (formal equivalence)                     │
  │  B. The meaning (semantic equivalence)                 │
  │  C. The effect on the reader (dynamic equivalence)     │
  │  D. The function performed (functional equivalence)    │
  │  E. The style, register, tone (aesthetic equivalence)  │
  │                                                        │
  │ These goals are often mutually exclusive:              │
  │ maximizing A often sacrifices C and E.                 │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CAMPS:
  Formal         Dynamic         Critical
  equivalence    equivalence     approaches
  (Nabokov)      (Nida)          (Benjamin, Venuti,
                                 Derrida)
      │              │               │
      └──────────────┼───────────────┘
                     ▼
         All agree translation is
         more than word substitution.
         All disagree about what
         it should be.
```

## Jakobson: Three Types of Translation

Roman Jakobson ("On Linguistic Aspects of Translation," 1959) provides the foundational taxonomy:

```
JAKOBSON'S THREE TRANSLATION TYPES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  1. INTRALINGUAL TRANSLATION
     (rewording within one language)
     Original: "The dog bit the man"
     Rewording: "The man was bitten by the dog"
     Or: "A canine attacked a male human"
     This is translation within English.
     Paraphrase, summary, simplification are all
     intralingual translation.

  2. INTERLINGUAL TRANSLATION
     (proper translation between languages)
     "Le chien a mordu l'homme" → "The dog bit the man"
     The focus of most translation theory.

  3. INTERSEMIOTIC TRANSLATION
     (from one sign system to another)
     Novel → film adaptation
     Musical score → performance
     Traffic sign → rule of law
     "Transmutation" — not just between languages
     but between sign systems.

  WHY THIS MATTERS:
  Jakobson's taxonomy shows translation is not uniquely
  a linguistic phenomenon — it's a semiotic phenomenon.
  Film adaptation "translates" a novel; a conductor
  "translates" a score. The problems (fidelity, loss,
  transformation) appear across all three types.
```

## Formal vs. Dynamic Equivalence (Nida)

Eugene Nida (*Toward a Science of Translating*, 1964) developed the most influential modern equivalence theory, primarily in the context of Bible translation.

**Formal equivalence**: Match source structure as closely as possible.
- Word-for-word where possible
- Preserve grammatical structure
- Preserve order of information
- Result: often awkward, opaque to target reader

**Dynamic equivalence**: Produce on the target reader the same effect the original produced on the source reader.
- "Closest natural equivalent"
- Reader should not feel they are reading a translation
- Idioms replaced with idioms; not "he lifted his eyes" but "he looked up"
- Result: fluent text; may lose foreignness

```
NIDA'S DYNAMIC EQUIVALENCE — EXAMPLE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SOURCE (Greek NT): "saluting one another with a holy kiss"
                     (Romans 16:16)

  Formal equivalent: "Greet one another with a holy kiss"
  (awkward in modern English — what is a holy kiss?)

  Dynamic equivalent: "Greet one another warmly in the
                       Christian spirit"
  (or in some languages: "Give each other a warm hug
   as fellow Christians")

  Nida's reasoning: In Middle Eastern culture, the kiss
  was the greeting. The *function* was to perform
  warm Christian community. The equivalent in a
  non-kissing culture achieves the same function.

  CRITIQUE: But the kiss has theological history —
  early church liturgy, Kiss of Peace. The "dynamic"
  rendering loses the cultural texture that is itself
  historically significant.
```

## Schleiermacher's Binary

Friedrich Schleiermacher ("On the Different Methods of Translation," 1813) framed the basic choice that structures all subsequent debate:

**The translator has only two real options:**

1. **"The translator leaves the author in peace as much as possible and moves the reader towards the author."** (Foreignization — Verfremdung)
   - The reader should feel the strangeness of encountering a foreign text
   - Translation preserves the foreignness, the distance, the otherness of the original
   - The reader must work to encounter the author's world

2. **"The translator leaves the reader in peace as much as possible and moves the author towards the reader."** (Domestication)
   - The text is made to feel natural in the target language
   - The author "speaks" in the reader's language and idiom
   - No foreignness; fully assimilated to target culture

```
SCHLEIERMACHER'S BINARY (with descendants)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FOREIGNIZATION                    DOMESTICATION
  (Verfremdung)                     (Einbürgerung)

  Schleiermacher → Venuti           Cicero → Nida → most
  Nabokov (extreme)                 commercial translation

  "Make strange"                    "Make natural"

  Preserves:                        Preserves:
  • Cultural difference              • Fluency
  • Historical distance              • Reader experience
  • Translator visibility            • Accessibility

  Loses:                            Loses:
  • Fluency                         • Cultural otherness
  • Accessibility                   • Historical texture
  • Reader comfort                  • Translator visibility

  Examples:                         Examples:
  Nabokov's Onegin                  King James Version (1611)
  (footnotes > text)                Pope's Homer (18th c.)
  Hölderlin's Greek                 Most mass-market
  Brecht's alienation               translations
  effect (theater)
```

## Venuti: Domestication, Foreignization, and the Invisible Translator

Lawrence Venuti (*The Translator's Invisibility*, 1995) radicalized Schleiermacher's binary with a political argument:

**The invisible translator**: Anglo-American translation culture prizes fluency above all. A good translation reads as if it were originally written in English. This means the translator's labor becomes invisible — the translation seems effortless, natural, given.

**The political problem**: Domestication is cultural imperialism at the level of the sentence. It assimilates foreign texts to Anglo-American norms, erasing difference, making all texts feel familiar. English-language cultures translate very little; when they do, they domesticate completely. Other cultures translate more and often foreignize more.

**Foreignization as resistance**: Retain foreignness — strange syntax, untranslated terms, cultural specificity — to resist the assimilation machine. Make the reader aware they are encountering a foreign text; make the translator visible.

```
VENUTI'S STATISTICS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Translation in publishing:
  US: ~3% of books published are translations
  Germany: ~12-15%
  France: ~25-30%
  Italy: ~25%

  Venuti's interpretation: The US domesticates and
  absorbs foreign culture minimally; other cultures
  must translate to participate in global discourse.
  This is a power asymmetry expressed in translation
  statistics.

  Critique of Venuti's critique:
  • Foreignization can be as aesthetically dominant
    as domestication (German Academic English is
    often foreignized in oppressive ways)
  • "Visibility" can be self-congratulatory —
    the translated text becomes about the translator
  • Reader access matters — foreignization for
    political reasons can be elitist
```

## Benjamin: Pure Language and the Afterlife of the Original

Walter Benjamin ("The Task of the Translator," 1923) — the most philosophically challenging translation essay:

**"Translatability"**: Not all texts are equally translatable. Great literary works have a particular kind of translatability — they demand translation, call for it. Translation is not a service performed for readers; it is something the original text *needs* to fulfill its life.

**"Afterlife"** (*Fortleben, Überleben*): Translation is not a copy of the original but its continuation — its *Überleben* (survival, but also "over-living"). The original changes over time; the translation captures a moment in that living process. Translation shows what the original's life *is*.

**"Pure Language"**: The ultimate goal of translation is not to communicate meaning but to reveal "pure language" — the language that underlies all languages, in which meaning is not transmitted but expressed. Each translation brings this pure language closer to expression.

```
BENJAMIN — KEY CONCEPTS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ORIGINAL TEXT             TRANSLATION
  ┌──────────────────┐      ┌──────────────────┐
  │ Not a            │      │ Not a copy or    │
  │ communication of │      │ imitation.       │
  │ information.     │      │                  │
  │                  │      │ The original's   │
  │ Has a "life" that│      │ afterlife —      │
  │ continues beyond │      │ its survival in  │
  │ the moment of    │      │ another language │
  │ production.      │      │ and time.        │
  └──────────────────┘      └──────────────────┘
           │                        │
           └───────────┬────────────┘
                       ▼
              "PURE LANGUAGE"
              The harmony behind all languages —
              revealed in the reciprocal illumination
              of original and translation.
              Not achieved but approached.

  "No translation would be possible if in its
  ultimate essence it strove for likeness to
  the original. For in its afterlife — which
  could not be called that if it were not a
  transformation and a renewal of something
  living — the original undergoes a change."
```

Benjamin's essay is notoriously difficult and was not intended as practical translation guidance. It has been enormously influential in translation theory and in deconstruction (Derrida wrote about it extensively).

## Derrida: The Tower of Babel

Derrida ("Des Tours de Babel," 1985) reads the Babel story through Benjamin:

**God's punishment**: God "confuses" human languages, preventing the completion of the tower. But this confusion is also a gift — the multiplicity of languages makes translation both necessary and impossible. Translation is the original supplement: the original text is always already in need of translation (it cannot contain itself).

The proper name "Babel" means both the city and "confusion" (in Hebrew). Translate it? You lose the proper name. Don't translate it? You lose the meaning. The impossible demand of translation is performed in the name itself.

**Derrida's claim**: Translation is not secondary to the original; the original is always already a translation — always already incomplete, demanding supplement. There is no pure original; there is no pure translation. Both are in a relation of mutual debt.

## The Impossibility Thesis

Humboldt: "No word in one language is exactly equivalent to a word in another. Different words do not designate the same thing with different sounds; they designate different concepts that are related to the same object only roughly."

```
THE IMPOSSIBILITY THESIS — STRONGEST FORM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  CLAIM: Languages are not codes for the same
  underlying meanings. Each language divides
  the world differently — different grammatical
  categories, different conceptual distinctions.

  EXAMPLES:
  Russian siniy/goluboy: two distinct color categories
  where English has one (blue)
  → Russian has no word meaning exactly "blue"
  → translation requires choosing, losing discrimination

  German Schadenfreude: pleasure at another's misfortune
  → English has no single-word equivalent
  → translate as concept, lose lexical compactness

  Japanese mono no aware: pathos of impermanence
  → concept exists in English but requires 4 words
  → something is always lost or added

  Pirahã (Amazonia): no recursive grammar, no numbers,
  no color terms, no tense beyond "now"
  → translating "I will see you tomorrow" requires
  embedding a culturally alien concept

  COUNTERARGUMENT (Jakobson):
  "Languages differ essentially in what they must convey
  and not in what they may convey."
  English doesn't have to specify gender in "I hired
  a worker" — but it can. Russian must. This is a
  grammatical constraint, not a conceptual one.
  Translation works through circumlocution
  even when direct lexical equivalents don't exist.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Theory | Core claim | Best application |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| Formal equivalence (Nabokov) | Preserve source form; translator should be "ugly, literal, accurate" | Sacred texts; scholarly editions; historical documentation |
| Dynamic equivalence (Nida) | Match effect on reader; closest natural equivalent | Religious texts for non-specialists; children's literature; mass communication |
| Foreignization (Venuti/Schleiermacher) | Keep strangeness; resist assimilation; make translator visible | Literary translation with ethical intent; postcolonial contexts |
| Domestication | Make natural; reader should not feel they're reading a translation | Commercial fiction; user documentation; technical manuals |
| Benjamin | Translation is the original's afterlife; seeks pure language | Not a practical method — theoretical framing of translation's purpose |
| Jakobson's taxonomy | Intralingual / interlingual / intersemiotic | Taxonomizing translation-related activities |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Faithful" is ambiguous**: When translators say a translation is "faithful," they mean faithful to *something* — but what? Faithful to the words? The meaning? The effect? The style? The culture? Naming what is being preserved (and thus what is being sacrificed) is more useful than claiming generic fidelity.

**Dynamic equivalence ≠ paraphrase**: Nida was not advocating loose paraphrase. Dynamic equivalence requires close reading of the source and precise calibration of effect. The goal is matching effect precisely, not approximating content loosely.

**Venuti's foreignization is not the same as awkward translation**: Foreignization is a principled aesthetic and political choice to preserve difference. Awkward translation is just bad translation. The difference is intentionality and craft.

**Jakobson's examples are usually gender**: His canonical example of what translation must do — "I hired a worker" in Russian requires choosing a gender — is useful precisely because it shows that grammatical constraints (not conceptual impossibility) drive most translation difficulties. The Russian translator doesn't face a conceptual impossibility; they face an information-addition requirement.

**The equivalence debate connects to the formal semantics the learner knows**: Compositional semantics, model-theoretic semantics, formal pragmatics — all ask versions of the equivalence question. Two sentences are synonymous iff they have the same truth conditions in all possible worlds. Translation equivalence is a pragmatic notion that exceeds truth-conditional equivalence. This is a precise connection to the MIT TCS/logic background.
