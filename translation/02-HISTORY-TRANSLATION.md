## Antiquity: Cicero's Foundational Debate

Cicero (*De Optimo Genere Oratorum*, 46 BCE) is the first theorist of translation in the Western tradition:

**The problem**: Cicero was translating Greek orators (Aeschines and Demosthenes) into Latin. He faced a choice.

**His solution**: Translate not *verbum pro verbo* (word for word) but *sensum exprimere* (express the meaning). The goal is to preserve the force and quality of the argument, not the linguistic form.

```
CICERO'S WORD-FOR-WORD vs. SENSE-FOR-SENSE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  WORD-FOR-WORD (verbum pro verbo):
  • Translate each source word with a target word
  • Preserve grammatical structure
  • Follow source order
  Result: "an interpreter, not an orator" — mechanical,
  loses argumentative power and rhetorical force

  SENSE-FOR-SENSE (sensum exprimere):
  • Capture the meaning, not the words
  • Use native Latin rhetoric
  • Add or subtract as Latin style demands
  Result: the Greek speeches "speak Latin" —
  readers encounter the argumentative force
  in their own rhetorical tradition

  Cicero's self-description: "I did not think I ought
  to count them [the words] out to the reader like
  coins, but to pay them by weight, as it were."
```

**Why this matters**: Cicero established the dominant Western translation method for 1,500 years. He also set up the tension that has never been resolved — the formal/dynamic equivalence debate is Cicero's debate, rediscovered and elaborated in every century.

## Jerome: The Vulgate (382 CE)

Pope Damasus commissioned Jerome to produce a reliable Latin Bible — the Greek and Latin texts in circulation had too many variant readings and contradictions.

Jerome's work (382-405 CE) resulted in the Vulgate (*vulgata editio* = "common edition"), which became the official Catholic Bible and was translated from for another thousand years.

### Jerome's Translation Principles

Jerome essentially applied Cicero's sense-for-sense method — *except* for scripture:

> "In translating [secular texts] I render not word-for-word, but sense-for-sense. But in the case of the Holy Scriptures, even the order of the words is a mystery." (*Letter 57 to Pammachius*)

This exception for scripture had enormous consequences. It made word-for-word translation seem more reverent — closer to God's intended word — and fueled the demand for literal translations of scripture across centuries.

```
JEROME'S CONSEQUENTIAL TRANSLATION CHOICES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  HEBREW almah → LATIN virgo
  Hebrew: almah = "young woman" (not necessarily virgin)
  Greek LXX: parthenos = "virgin" (stronger)
  Jerome follows LXX: virgo
  → Isaiah 7:14: "a young woman shall conceive"
     becomes "a virgin shall conceive"
  → Christological interpretation now embedded
     in the Latin text
  → Catholic theology built on Vulgate's "virgo"

  "HAIL, FULL OF GRACE" (Luke 1:28)
  Greek: kecharitomene = "one who has been favored"
  Jerome: gratia plena = "full of grace"
  → Stronger than Greek; elevated Mary's status
  → Immaculate Conception doctrine partly grounded in
     Jerome's amplified translation

  PENITENTIAM AGITE vs. METANOEITE
  Greek: metanoeite = "repent, change your mind"
  Jerome: paenitentiam agite = "do penance"
  → Greek suggests interior change
  → Latin suggests exterior acts of penance
  → This distinction fueled the Reformation debate
     over the sacrament of penance
  → Luther's 95 Theses begin from Erasmus's
     correct translation of metanoeite as "repent"
     (interior), not "do penance" (exterior + institutional)
```

## The Toledo School: Arabic to Latin (1085-1280)

The most consequential translation project in medieval intellectual history was largely invisible to its beneficiaries for centuries.

**The background**: When the Western Roman Empire collapsed (5th century CE), Western Europe lost most of Greek philosophy and science. Greek texts survived in Byzantium (Greek East) and were translated into Arabic by the Abbasid caliphate (particularly at the House of Wisdom in Baghdad, 8th-9th centuries). Islamic scholars (al-Kindi, al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes) then extensively commented on and extended these texts.

**Toledo**: When Toledo was reconquered from Islamic rule (1085), Christian scholars encountered one of the great libraries of Islamic scholarship. The Toledo school of translators (Archbishop Raymond's patronage, ~1130-1280) translated Arabic texts into Latin — conveying Aristotle, Euclid, al-Khwarizmi, Galen, and Islamic science back to Western Europe.

```
KNOWLEDGE TRANSMISSION CHAIN
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  GREEK ORIGINALS
  (Aristotle, Euclid, Hippocrates, Galen)
         │
         ▼ (5th-6th c. CE)
  SYRIAC TRANSLATIONS
  (Christian scholars in Syria and Iraq)
         │
         ▼ (8th-10th c.)
  ARABIC TRANSLATIONS + EXTENSIONS
  (House of Wisdom, Baghdad;
   al-Kindi, al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes;
   al-Khwarizmi adds algebra)
         │
         ▼ (11th-12th c., Toledo)
  LATIN TRANSLATIONS
  (Gerard of Cremona, Adelard of Bath,
   Michael Scot, Herman the German)
         │
         ▼
  EUROPEAN UNIVERSITIES
  (Paris, Bologna, Oxford)
  Aristotle becomes "the Philosopher"
  Aquinas reconciles Aristotle with Christianity
  (Scholasticism)
  → Foundation of European medieval science
    and philosophy

  WITHOUT TOLEDO: The Scientific Revolution is
  delayed by generations. Algebra (al-Khwarizmi)
  reaches Europe via this chain. "Algorithm" is
  Latinization of "al-Khwarizmi."
```

Key translators:
- **Gerard of Cremona** (1114-1187): Translated ~80 works from Arabic to Latin, including Ptolemy's *Almagest*, Avicenna's *Canon of Medicine*, al-Khwarizmi's *Algebra*
- **Adelard of Bath**: Translated Euclid's *Elements* from Arabic, introducing rigorous geometric proof to Western Europe
- **Herman the German**: Translated Aristotle's *Poetics*, *Rhetoric*, *Nicomachean Ethics*

## Luther's German Bible (1522)

Martin Luther's translation of the New Testament (September 1522) is one of the most consequential acts of translation in history — politically, linguistically, and religiously.

### The Erasmian Correction

The immediate trigger: Erasmus published a new critical edition of the Greek New Testament (1516), correcting the Vulgate and restoring original Greek. This demonstrated that Jerome's Latin had translation errors with theological consequences.

The metanoeite/paenitentiam agite example: Erasmus showed that the Greek word means "repent" (change your inner disposition) not "do penance" (perform sacramental acts). Luther's 95 Theses begin from this insight.

### Luther's Translation Method

Luther's famous statement from the *Sendbrief vom Dolmetschen* (Letter on Translation, 1530):

> "I must ask the mother in the house, the children in the street, the common man in the marketplace, and listen to what they say and translate accordingly, so that they can understand and see that one is speaking to them in German."

```
LUTHER'S TRANSLATION PRINCIPLES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  1. VERNACULAR NATURALIZATION:
     Use the German that common people actually speak.
     Not Latinate theological German but market German,
     kitchen German, children's German.

  2. SENSE-FOR-SENSE (Ciceronian):
     "In the German tongue one must not ask the Latin
     letters how to speak German... one must ask the
     mother at home, the children on the street,
     the common man in the market."

  3. THEOLOGICAL COMMITMENTS EMBEDDED:
     Controversial addition: "alone" to Romans 3:28
     "A man is justified by faith [alone], apart from works"
     Greek has no "alone" (monon)
     Latin has no "alone"
     Luther: "alone" is grammatically natural in German;
     also theologically necessary (sola fide)
     Catholic critics: he added a word to scripture!
     Luther: the sense demands it

  4. LINGUISTIC STANDARDIZATION:
     Luther chose East Middle German (the dialect
     of the Saxon Chancery) as his base.
     Because his Bible was read throughout Germany,
     this dialect became the basis of standard German
     (Hochdeutsch). Luther's Bible is to German
     what the KJV is to English — the linguistic
     standard-setter.
```

### Political Consequences

The Protestant Reformation spread faster because the Bible was in German and could be printed. Gutenberg's press (1450s) made mass distribution possible. Luther's German Bible was a bestseller — ~200,000 copies sold between 1534 and 1574. Common people could now read scripture and evaluate priests' claims against the text.

## Early Modern Translation and Nation-Building

The 16th-18th centuries saw translation become explicitly tied to national language formation:

```
TRANSLATION AND NATIONAL LANGUAGES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ENGLAND:
  Tyndale's NT (1526): first English NT from Greek;
  burned by Catholic authorities; Tyndale executed (1536)
  → Much of KJV comes from Tyndale

  Coverdale Bible (1535): first complete English Bible
  Great Bible (1539): authorized version under Henry VIII
  Geneva Bible (1560): Protestant; scholarly; popular
  King James Version (1611): definitive; see next guide

  FRANCE:
  Lefèvre d'Étaples: French NT (1523)
  Olivétan: French Bible (1535); revised by Calvin
  Translation central to French Reform

  ITALY:
  Diodati Bible (1607): Protestant Italian
  Catholic censorship blocked vernacular translation longer

  PATTERN: Every Reformation required a vernacular
  Bible. The Bible was the vernacular's first and
  most influential text. Translation = nation-building
  = religious authority.
```

## 18th-19th Century: Domestication Dominant

The 18th century was the era of classical domestication. Pope's Homer is the paradigm:

**Alexander Pope's *Iliad* (1715-1720)**: Pope did not know Greek (he used cribs). He produced a celebrated English poem in heroic couplets that reads magnificently as 18th-century English verse. Homer "speaks" in Augustan English.

Matthew Arnold's criticism: Pope's Homer has nothing of the "plainness and directness" or "rapid fire" of the original. It is a great poem but not a translation of Homer; it is Pope.

```
THE DOMESTICATION SPECTRUM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "TRANSLATORS" WHO BARELY KNEW THE SOURCE LANGUAGE:
  • Fitzgerald's Omar Khayyam (Rubaiyat, 1859):
    Fitzgerald had modest Persian; produced beloved
    English poem. "A Pot of Wine, a Loaf of Bread--
    and Thou." Persian original: harder-edged,
    more ambiguous.

  "FAITHFUL" TRANSLATORS WHO KNEW THE LANGUAGE:
  • Constance Garnett (1861-1946):
    Translated almost all of Russian literature
    (Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, Chekhov, Turgenev)
    into English 1890-1920s.
    Made Russian literature accessible to English readers.
    Criticized by Nabokov for over-domesticating,
    normalizing Russian syntax into English smoothness,
    losing the "Russian-ness."
    Counter: Without Garnett, English-speakers would
    have no Dostoevsky at all. Good enough translation
    that opens a literature is better than no translation.
```

## 20th Century: Translation Theory Becomes Academic

The 20th century saw translation theory professionalized:

- **Nida** (1964): Linguistic-based equivalence theory (see 01-EQUIVALENCE-PROBLEM)
- **Polysystem theory** (Even-Zohar, 1978): Translated literature occupies a position in the target culture's literary system. When a culture's literature is "young" or "peripheral," translations are central (Hebrew literature in the early 20th century, when translated classics shaped the emerging Hebrew literary tradition). When literature is "established," translations are peripheral.
- **Descriptive Translation Studies** (Toury, 1980s): Describe what translators actually do (norms), not what they should do (rules). Translation norms include initial norm (source-orientation vs. target-orientation) and operational norms (specific choices made).
- **Skopos theory** (Vermeer, 1984): Translation is determined by its *Skopos* (purpose). A legal contract needs a different translation than a novel. The Skopos, not faithfulness to the original, determines the appropriate translation strategy.

## Localization Industry (1980s-present)

Localization (*l10n* — L + 10 letters + N; the learner will recognize this as the standard abbreviation pattern) is the industrialization of translation for software, product, and content:

```
LOCALIZATION PIPELINE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SOURCE TEXT
  (English master)
       │
       ▼
  TRANSLATION MEMORY (TM)
  Previous translations stored; new text matched against TM
  100% match: reuse previous translation (no cost)
  Fuzzy match: edit existing translation
  New text: human translation + MT post-editing
       │
       ▼
  GLOSSARY / TERM MANAGEMENT
  Ensure consistent terminology across products
  ("click," "tap," "select" consistently translated
   to same terms)
       │
       ▼
  LOCALIZATION TESTING
  UI strings fit in their containers?
  Date/number formats correct?
  Right-to-left text (Arabic, Hebrew) rendering?
  Cultural appropriateness review?
       │
       ▼
  LOCALIZED PRODUCT
  (in up to 100+ languages for major tech products)

  MICROSOFT WINDOWS: ~110 languages
  ADOBE: 26+ languages
  iOS/Android: 35-40 languages

  The learner's Azure/Windows background: every UI
  string in Windows goes through exactly this pipeline.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**The Toledo School was translating from Arabic, not Greek**: The scholars who recovered Aristotle for medieval Europe were usually translating from Arabic translations (and sometimes Arabic commentaries), not from original Greek. The chain of transmission matters — what was actually available in 12th-century Spain was mostly Arabic.

**Luther "added" *alone* — was that dishonest?**: This is a Protestant-Catholic debate with a 500-year history. Defenders: Luther's *allein* follows naturally from German grammar and the sense of the passage. Attackers: he added a word that changed theological meaning. Modern translators face versions of this choice constantly — the question is whether the addition is interpretive or distorting.

**Pope didn't know Greek**: The most celebrated English translation of Homer was made by someone who didn't read Greek. This was not unusual in the 18th century. Chapman (the translator Keats wrote about) knew Greek; Pope used cribs from others who did. The question this raises: is it better to have a great poem from someone who doesn't know the source, or a more accurate text from someone who does?

**Algorithm came from al-Khwarizmi via translation**: The word "algorithm" is a medieval Latin corruption of "al-Khwarizmi" (the mathematician's name). The word "algebra" comes from the title of his book *al-Kitab al-mukhtasar fi hisab al-jabr wal-muqabala*. These are direct traces of the Toledo transmission in modern mathematical vocabulary.
