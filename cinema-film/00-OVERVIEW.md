# Cinema and Film — Overview

## The Big Picture

Cinema is applied optics + photochemistry + signal processing + psychophysics + narrative grammar + industrial economics. Each layer built on and required the previous. The technology tree converges on a display system that fools the visual cortex into perceiving continuous motion from discrete still frames.

## Technology-to-Art Pipeline

Cinema's artistic vocabulary emerged directly from technical constraints. Understanding the constraints explains the conventions:

```
TECHNICAL CONSTRAINT -> ARTISTIC CONVENTION

Constraint                              Convention that emerged
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
35mm frame: fixed aspect ratio (1.33:1)| "Academy ratio" as visual grammar
                                       | Compositions designed for this box
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
16 frames/second needed for motion     | "Slow motion" = shoot faster (48fps)
illusion; 24fps became standard        | "Fast motion" = shoot slower (8fps)
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
No sync sound recording                | Intertitles, pantomime acting style
(silent era)                           | Visual storytelling priority
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
Panchromatic film insensitive to red   | Different makeup (orthochromatic era)
colors (orthochromatic stock)          | High-contrast lighting
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
Deep focus (small aperture) requires  | Deep focus staging: multiple planes
very bright light                      | Orson Welles: Citizen Kane (1941)
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
Short rolls (1,000 ft = ~10 min)       | Scene length constrained
                                       | Magazine change = necessary cut
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
Optical printer: compositing by        | Matte shots as standard FX tool
re-photographing multiple strips       | (practical limitation -> technique)
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
CRT scan lines / interlacing           | 30fps NTSC vs 24fps film = "film look"
(video vs film)                        | vs "video look" aesthetic difference
--------------------------------------+--------------------------------------
Digital intermediate (2000s)           | Color grading as expressive tool
                                       | Deliberate "look" applied in post
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why 24 frames/second? | Compromise: enough for smooth motion illusion, minimum film stock consumption; 1927 sound sync standard |
| What is the 180° rule? | Cameras must stay on one side of action axis; crossing = spatial disorientation for viewer |
| What ended the studio system? | Paramount Decree 1948 (antitrust) forced studios to sell theaters; TV competition followed |
| What is a digital intermediate? | Scan film to digital, grade/manipulate, output back to film or direct digital; enables modern color grading |
| What is the franchise model? | Pre-sold IP, sequel potential, merchandise revenue, worldwide simultaneous release; lower creative risk |
| What is a distribution window? | Sequential release: theaters -> physical media -> streaming -> broadcast; each window monetizes residual audience |
| What killed the 3-strip Technicolor? | Eastmancolor (1950): single-strip color film; much cheaper, good enough quality |
| What is the "film look"? | 24fps + motion blur + color grade characteristics; distinct from video's 30fps + interlace + direct color |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Persistence of vision explains cinema."** This is the most common misconception in film theory. Persistence of vision (retinal afterimages persisting after stimulus removed) is not what makes cinema work. The actual mechanism is the phi phenomenon (apparent motion between discrete stimuli) and beta movement — psychological perceptual phenomena, not optical afterimages. The "persistence of vision" explanation was proposed by Peter Mark Roget in 1824 and has been disproven; it persists in film textbooks through inertia.

**"The Lumières invented cinema."** They invented the Cinématographe (combined camera/projector) and gave the first commercial public projection (December 28, 1895). But Dickson/Edison had the Kinetoscope (1891), Marey had chronophotography (1882), and Le Prince had a working motion picture camera (1888). The Lumières are first in the specific combination of public projection + film transport + commercial exhibition.

**"CGI replaced practical effects."** High-end productions use both extensively. Practical effects (physical models, pyrotechnics, prosthetics) are still preferred for many situations: they're cheaper, they interact with real light naturally, and they give actors something to react to. CGI is essential for elements that can't practically exist: space battles, scale miniature destruction, digital crowd duplication. Modern blockbusters are a hybrid.

**"The auteur theory means the director makes all the decisions."** Auteur theory (Cahiers du Cinéma critics, 1950s) is a critical interpretive framework claiming a consistent personal vision across a director's body of work. It's a theory of criticism, not of production. Directors work within studio constraints, with strong creative contributors (DPs, editors, composers). The auteur theory correctly identifies directorial vision as often the through-line, but overstates the director's singular control.
