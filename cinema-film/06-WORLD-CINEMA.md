# World Cinema: Italian Neorealism, French New Wave, German Expressionism, Japanese Masters, New Hollywood

## The Big Picture

"World cinema" is a convenient label for the various movements that challenged Hollywood's industrial model — using different production methods, aesthetic priorities, or narrative values to produce work that Hollywood's system couldn't or wouldn't make. Each movement responded to specific historical conditions and produced lasting technical innovations.

```
WORLD CINEMA MOVEMENTS: OVERVIEW

GERMAN EXPRESSIONISM (1919-1933)
  Context: WWI defeat, Weimar inflation, political instability
  Aesthetic: distorted sets, extreme shadows, psychological horror
  Production: UFA Studio, Berlin
  Key films: Cabinet of Dr. Caligari (1920), Nosferatu (1922),
             Metropolis (1927), M (1931)
  Legacy: horror genre aesthetics, film noir lighting, shadow as meaning

SOVIET MONTAGE (1920s)
  Context: Russian Revolution; cinema as propaganda + art
  Aesthetic: intellectual montage, non-continuity editing
  Key filmmakers: Eisenstein, Pudovkin, Dziga Vertov
  Key films: Battleship Potemkin (1925), Man with a Movie Camera (1929)
  Legacy: advertising, music video, documentary editing theory

FRENCH POETIC REALISM (1930s)
  Context: Depression era, existentialist literature
  Aesthetic: working-class protagonists, fatalism, atmosphere
  Key filmmakers: Jean Renoir, Marcel Carné
  Key films: The Rules of the Game (1939), Children of Paradise (1945)
  Legacy: noir, European art cinema sensibility

ITALIAN NEOREALISM (1945-1952)
  Context: WWII devastation, limited studio resources
  Aesthetic: on-location, non-actors, social injustice themes
  Key filmmakers: Rossellini, De Sica, Visconti
  Key films: Rome Open City (1945), Bicycle Thieves (1948),
             La Terra Trema (1948)
  Legacy: documentary-influenced fiction, dogma 95, mumblecore

JAPANESE GOLDEN AGE (1950s)
  Context: occupation ends, rediscovering national culture
  Aesthetic: traditional + modern synthesis, visual formalism
  Key filmmakers: Kurosawa, Ozu, Mizoguchi
  Key films: Rashomon (1950), Tokyo Story (1953),
             Seven Samurai (1954)
  Legacy: international art cinema, genre film (Kurosawa -> Westerns -> action)

FRENCH NEW WAVE (1958-1968)
  Context: Cahiers du Cinéma critics become filmmakers; cheap camera tech
  Aesthetic: handheld, jump cuts, self-reflexive, low budget
  Key filmmakers: Godard, Truffaut, Chabrol, Rivette, Rohmer
  Key films: The 400 Blows (1959), Breathless (1960),
             Last Year at Marienbad (1961), 8½ (1963)
  Legacy: independent cinema globally, auteur theory in practice

NEW HOLLYWOOD (1967-1982)
  Context: old studio system failing; counterculture + Paramount Decree
  Aesthetic: character ambiguity, downbeat endings, adult themes
  Key filmmakers: Coppola, Scorsese, Altman, Friedkin, Kubrick, Cassavetes
  Key films: Bonnie and Clyde (1967), The Graduate (1967),
             Easy Rider (1969), The Godfather (1972),
             Chinatown (1974), Taxi Driver (1976)
  Legacy: American art cinema, stars directing, anti-hero protagonists
```

## German Expressionism (1919-1933)

```
GERMAN EXPRESSIONISM: AESTHETICS AND CONTEXT

CULTURAL CONTEXT:
  Germany post-WWI: economic collapse, political chaos
  Weimar Republic (1919-1933): unstable democracy
  Inflation: 1923 hyperinflation (prices doubled every 4 days)
  Cultural anxiety: defeat, shame, uncertain identity
  Expressionist art movement: already strong in painting (Munch, Kirchner)

  Cinema as psychological expression rather than representation
  Expressionism: inner psychological reality > outer material reality
  The world as distorted by the mind of the protagonist (or society)

CABINET OF DR. CALIGARI (Robert Wiene, 1920):
  Frame story: madman tells story of Dr. Caligari, murderous somnambulist
  SETS: painted, distorted geometry
    Streets: walls lean at impossible angles
    Stairs: different-sized steps
    Shadows: PAINTED onto sets (not created by lights)
    This is key: German Expressionism often used painted shadows
    vs Hollywood: shadows created by lighting
  Reveal: storyteller is patient in asylum; story = his delusion
  Psychological reading: entire film = distorted perspective of madman

NOSFERATU (F.W. Murnau, 1922):
  Unauthorized Dracula adaptation (Stoker estate sued, won)
  Count Orlok: not glamorous vampire; terrifyingly alien
  Real locations + expressionist lighting
  Shadow compositions: Orlok's shadow climbs stairs separately
  Visual horror without words

METROPOLIS (Fritz Lang, 1927):
  98-minute running time (original ~150 minutes, lost)
  Budget: 5M Reichsmarks (most expensive German film)
  Workers underground / wealthy above: class allegory
  Special effects: Schüfftan process (mirror effects)
  Art direction: constructivism + expressionism
  Influence: every science fiction film after it (Star Wars, Blade Runner)

FILM NOIR LEGACY:
  German directors emigrated to Hollywood (1933-1939, Nazi rise)
  Fritz Lang, Billy Wilder, Otto Preminger, Edgar Ulmer
  Brought: expressionist lighting (chiaroscuro, shadow)
  Applied to: Hollywood crime films = Film Noir
  Film Noir is literally German Expressionism in Los Angeles
```

## Italian Neorealism (1945-1952)

```
ITALIAN NEOREALISM

HISTORICAL CONDITIONS:
  Italy 1945: bombed-out, occupied, poor
  Cinecittà studios (Rome): used as refugee camp
  No money for studio sets, no film stock, limited equipment
  BUT: postwar authenticity demand (document reality)
  AND: Roberto Rossellini started making films immediately

AESTHETIC PROGRAM:
  Shoot on location (no sets): real streets, real buildings
  Non-professional actors: real workers, real faces
  Long takes: observe life rather than construct it
  Natural lighting: what's there, not studio lighting
  Social realist themes: poverty, survival, injustice
  Dialect and regional speech (not standard Italian)
  Minimal score: sometimes none at all

ROME OPEN CITY (Rossellini, 1945):
  Shot during/immediately after German occupation
  Partially improvised: scripted scenes + documentary material
  Some professional actors (Anna Magnani), many non-professionals
  Film stock: obtained on black market
  Processing: improvised
  Result: rough, grainy, immediate quality
  IMPACT: internationally recognized as new kind of cinema

BICYCLE THIEVES (De Sica, 1948):
  Father and son search for stolen bicycle in Rome
  ENTIRELY non-professional cast (De Sica's choice)
  Real locations: working-class Rome
  Simple story: economic desperation, moral compromise
  Ending: downbeat, unresolved, "life doesn't resolve"
  Voted #1 Greatest Film of All Time (Sight & Sound, 1952)
  Still on the Sight & Sound top 10 (2022: #4)

LA TERRA TREMA (Visconti, 1948):
  3-hour film about Sicilian fishermen
  Entirely non-professional cast speaking Sicilian dialect
  (Required subtitles in Italian)
  Neo-Marxist analysis of economic exploitation
  Most extreme neorealist experiment

DECLINE (~1952-1955):
  Italian Communist Party supported neorealism
  Christian Democrat government: concerned about negative image of Italy
  Andreotti Law (1949): banned export of "defamatory" Italian films
  Commercial pressure: audiences wanted entertainment
  Neorealism: slow death from government pressure + commercial competition
  End marker: Federico Fellini begins departing from it (La Strada, 1954)
```

## Japanese Cinema: Kurosawa, Ozu, Mizoguchi

```
JAPANESE CINEMA GOLDEN AGE (1950s)

CONTEXT:
  US occupation ends 1952
  Japan reasserting cultural identity
  New freedom to address wartime and social themes
  Economic recovery enabling film investment

AKIRA KUROSAWA (1910-1998):
  Western / Japanese synthesis:
  Studied: Shakespeare, Dostoevsky, Ford, Wyler, Hawks
  Combined: Japanese aesthetics + Western narrative momentum

  RASHOMON (1950):
  Same event told from four contradictory perspectives
  No objective truth revealed
  Philosophical: subjectivity of all narrative
  At Venice Film Festival 1951: Golden Lion (first Japanese film)
  IMPACT: Western discovery of Japanese cinema
  "Rashomon structure": multiple perspectives = now standard narrative device

  SEVEN SAMURAI (1954):
  207 minutes, three-month shoot, enormous production
  Seven samurai defend village against bandits
  Action sequences: telephoto lenses, multiple cameras, slow motion
  Character ensemble development
  INFLUENCE:
  -> The Magnificent Seven (1960, US western remake)
  -> Star Wars: Episode IV (ensemble structure)
  -> Every "team assembles" narrative
  Kurosawa's films remade as Westerns 7 times

YASUJIRO OZU (1903-1963):
  OPPOSITE approach from Kurosawa
  Minimalism: no pans, no tracking shots (mostly)
  "Tatami shot": camera at floor level (~45cm high)
  Narrative: domestic, quotidian, family relationships
  Tokyo Story (1953): elderly parents visit adult children
                      who are too busy; they return to die
  Voted #1 Greatest Film (Sight & Sound 2012 directors' poll)
  Technical precision: every frame composed with extraordinary care
  "Nothing happens": narrative tension from absence of dramatic events

KENJI MIZOGUCHI (1898-1956):
  Long-take style: scenes play out in uninterrupted shots
  Women's perspective: female protagonists, suffering under patriarchy
  Ugetsu (1953), Sansho the Bailiff (1954): masterpieces of long-take cinema
  Ugetsu: ghost story / anti-war allegory in Edo period Japan
  Visual style: extraordinary (scroll-painting compositions)
```

## French New Wave (Nouvelle Vague, 1958-1968)

```
FRENCH NEW WAVE

ORIGINS: CAHIERS DU CINÉMA
  Film journal founded 1951 by André Bazin
  Critics: Jean-Luc Godard, François Truffaut, Claude Chabrol,
           Jacques Rivette, Éric Rohmer
  Their argument: cinema is an art form; directors are its authors
  American genres (Hawks, Ford, Hitchcock): actually art
  French commercial cinema: boring, literary adaptation ("Tradition of Quality")
  AUTEUR THEORY: director as primary creative voice

  These critics decided to make films themselves
  Why the film theory criticism? To justify what they'd then do.

ENABLING TECHNOLOGY:
  Arriflex 35mm camera: shoulder-mounted, lightweight
  Fast film stock (higher ASA): shoot in available light
  Portable sync sound (Nagra recorder, 1958): location dialogue
  16mm for some (blown up to 35mm)

  The technology enabled the aesthetic:
  Without lightweight camera: no handheld
  Without fast film: no shooting in natural light
  Without location sync sound: no improvised dialogue

THE 400 BLOWS (Truffaut, 1959):
  Autobiographical: Truffaut's own delinquent childhood
  Non-professional child lead (Jean-Pierre Léaud)
  Location shooting: Paris streets
  Ending: unresolved freeze frame (Antoine runs to beach)
  This ending: famous precisely because it REFUSES resolution

BREATHLESS (Godard, 1960):
  Jump cuts: within scenes (continuous action, discontinuous editing)
  Used deliberately to break immersion
  Actors address camera directly (break 4th wall)
  Improvised dialogue
  Crime story: French New Wave filtered through Hollywood B-movies
  Quotes: Humphrey Bogart, newspaper headlines, visual jokes
  Jump cuts: Godard couldn't get timing right in editing room,
             decided to cut out boring footage and not hide it

8½ (Fellini, 1963):
  Italian, but New Wave spirit
  Film about making a film
  Memory, fantasy, guilt, creativity, failure
  Self-reflexive form: film as subject matter
  Most imitated film structure in "art cinema" from 1963 onward

LEGACY:
  Independent cinema worldwide: New Wave model
  Low budget + auteur vision = viable alternative to studio system
  Cassavetes: American parallel (Shadows, 1959)
  American New Hollywood: absorbed New Wave influences through film school
```

## New Hollywood (1967-1982)

```
NEW HOLLYWOOD

CONTEXT:
  Studio system: collapsing post-Paramount Decree + TV
  1966: Production Code abandoned (ratings system instead)
  1967-1968: youth market, counterculture
  Film schools: USC, UCLA, AFI producing technically trained directors
  Baby Boomers: first generation raised on films + TV as high culture

KEY ENABLERS:
  MPAA ratings (1968): adult content now possible without Code
  Easy Rider budget: $400K, earned $60M (1969)
    -> proved: cheap films can make fortunes
    -> gave young directors leverage
  European art cinema: via film festival circuit
    -> directors Americanized Godard, Fellini, Bergman

KEY DIRECTORS AND THEIR INNOVATIONS:
  Robert Altman: overlapping dialogue, ensemble, anti-narrative
    M*A*S*H (1970), Nashville (1975), The Player (1992)
  Martin Scorsese: street-level violence, voice-over, music
    Mean Streets (1973), Taxi Driver (1976), GoodFellas (1990)
  Francis Ford Coppola: literary ambition in genre film
    The Godfather (1972), Apocalypse Now (1979)
  Roman Polanski: European noir sensibility
    Chinatown (1974): anti-detective film (no justice, no resolution)
  William Friedkin: documentary realism in genre
    The French Connection (1971), The Exorcist (1973)

NEW HOLLYWOOD END:
  Jaws (Spielberg, 1975): wide release + marketing = modern blockbuster
  Star Wars (Lucas, 1977): franchise model + merchandise
  These films: enormous commercial success proved SAFE films work better
  Studios preferred: bankable stars + marketable concepts
  New Hollywood directors: too expensive, too difficult
  Heaven's Gate (Cimino, 1980): $44M budget, $3M domestic gross
    -> ended UA, scared all studios back to control

LEGACY:
  Created: American art cinema tradition (Scorsese still practicing)
  Set: template for prestige filmmaking within studio system
  Established: director-star relationship as power center
  Influence: entire prestige TV boom (Breaking Bad, The Wire) =
             New Hollywood storytelling values applied to television
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Neorealism means documentary."** Neorealist films are fiction films. They use documentary-influenced aesthetics (location shooting, non-professional actors, available light) but tell scripted stories with characters. Bicycle Thieves is a tightly plotted dramatic narrative; it just feels documentary due to its look and casting. The crucial distinction: fiction with documentary aesthetics, not documentation.

**"The French New Wave rejected Hollywood."** They were obsessed with Hollywood — particularly American genre films. Godard, Truffaut, and their colleagues were devoted to Ford, Hawks, Hitchcock. The Cahiers critics explicitly rehabilitated Hollywood directors as auteurs. The New Wave's critique was of the stiff French "literary" cinema, not of Hollywood. Breathless is structured around American noir and Humphrey Bogart worship.

**"Kurosawa 'influenced' Star Wars."** Lucas acknowledges very specific and direct influences: The Hidden Fortress (1958) for the two peasants who become R2-D2 and C-3PO (two low-status characters as audience entry points), the samurai robes for Jedi costumes, the wipe transitions. George Lucas was a USC film school student; Kurosawa's films were curriculum. The influence is specific and acknowledged, not general "inspiration."

**"New Hollywood ended when Spielberg and Lucas started making blockbusters."** It's more nuanced: New Hollywood's aesthetic and commercial values coexisted for years. Spielberg and Lucas were themselves New Hollywood directors (Jaws replaced a small-scale Altmanesque film; Star Wars was passion project). The issue was that their success made studio executives prefer their model over the messier, less predictable auteur model. The money shifted, not the aesthetic instantly.
