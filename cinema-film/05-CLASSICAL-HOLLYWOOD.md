# Classical Hollywood: Studio System, Production Code, Star System, Vertical Integration, Paramount 1948

## The Big Picture

Classical Hollywood (1930-1960) was a vertically integrated industrial oligopoly that controlled film production, distribution, and exhibition. It operated as a factory system for cultural goods — standardizing production, controlling talent, and managing risk through genre, star power, and economies of scale. It was broken by antitrust enforcement (1948) and undermined by television, but its industrial logic persists in modified form today.

## The Production Code (Hays Code)

```
THE HAYS CODE (Motion Picture Production Code, 1930-1968)

BACKGROUND:
  1920s scandals: Fatty Arbuckle (murder trial), drug use,
  morality concerns about Hollywood
  1922: Will Hays hired as first "czar" of MPPDA
       (Motion Picture Producers and Distributors of America)
  1930: Production Code written (drafted by Catholic priest Daniel Lord
        and publisher Martin Quigley)
  1930-1934: Code exists but unenforced
  1934: Joseph Breen appointed to enforce Code
        "Breen Office" = Production Code Administration (PCA)
        ALL scripts must be submitted; ALL films must receive certificate
        Theaters agreeing to show MPPDA films agreed not to show uncertified films
        Economic coercion: virtually complete compliance

WHAT THE CODE PROHIBITED:
  Sex and sexuality:
  - No "scenes of passion" that might stimulate lower emotions
  - No adultery portrayed sympathetically
  - No homosexuality (the "sex perversion" clause)
  - No nudity
  - Married couples: separate beds only
  - Seduction: implied, never explicit

  Crime:
  - No crime may go unpunished (enforced moral order)
  - Murder: victim cannot seem to deserve it
  - Gangsters: cannot be portrayed sympathetically as heroes
  - (But: gangsters can be protagonists if punished at end)
  - Illegal drugs: no depiction of use

  Politics and religion:
  - No attacks on religion
  - No criticism of foreign governments (trade considerations)

  Race:
  - No interracial relationships (miscegenation clause)
  - (This was particularly enforced; film could not show
     Black-white romance)

EFFECTS ON FILM NARRATIVE:
  Enforced "classic" Hollywood moral universe:
  Good wins, evil punished, marriage sacred
  "Good" characters must suffer for their sins even if sympathetic
  "Double coding": innuendo, subtext, coded language
    -> Rich tradition of subtext in classic Hollywood
    -> "Pre-Code" films (1929-1934): much more explicit
       Baby Face (1933, Barbara Stanwyck), Red Headed Woman (1932)

CIRCUMVENTION:
  Billy Wilder: Sunset Boulevard (1950) — dark, bitter, Code pushed
  Hitchcock: Psycho (1960) — production without Breen approval
             (Hitchcock was his own producer; released anyway)
<!-- @editor[content/P1]: Typo — "Otto Premature" should be "Otto Preminger" -->
  Otto Premature: The Moon Is Blue (1953), The Man with the Golden Arm (1955)
    Released without Code certificate; theater chains accepted anyway
    Proved: Code enforcement was weakening

END OF CODE:
  1966: first Code rating system (G, M, R, X) introduced
  1968: MPAA ratings system replaces Code entirely
  Code dies: external pressure (foreign films competing with no code),
             legal challenges, changing audience
```

## The Studio Factory System

```
THE STUDIO FACTORY MODEL

PHYSICAL INFRASTRUCTURE:
  Studio lot: 50-200+ acres in Hollywood/Burbank/Culver City
  Permanent sets: standing city streets, western towns, period interiors
  Technical departments: cinematography, sound, special effects
  Support: wardrobe, makeup, props, transportation
  All employees: under weekly contract

CONTRACT SYSTEM:
  Stars: 7-year exclusive contracts (standard)
  Director, writers, cinematographers: same
  Studio controls: roles assigned, loans between studios
    (Stars "loaned" to other studios for fee)
  Star had no approval over assigned roles
  Star had no approval over script, director, co-stars
  Suspension clause: refuse a role -> lose weeks of contract
    without pay (weeks added to end of contract)

GENRE PRODUCTION:
  Studios specialized by genre (generally):
  MGM: prestige musicals, glamour ("More stars than in the heavens")
  Warner Bros: social realism, crime, musicals (Busby Berkeley)
  Paramount: sophisticated comedy, European-influenced drama
  RKO: Astaire-Rogers musicals, Hitchcock
  Universal: horror (Frankenstein, Dracula, Wolfman)
  Columbia: screwball comedy, Frank Capra
  Republic: B-westerns, serials

PRODUCTION SCHEDULING:
  A-films: $500K-$3M+, 60-90 day shoot, major stars
  B-films: $100K-$300K, 20-30 days, contract players
  Serials: very low budget, weekly episodes
  Films per year: major studios 40-60/year (A+B+serials)

THE B-FILM SYSTEM:
  Double feature: A-film + B-film for one ticket price
  B-film: budget filler, trained new talent
  B-westerns: Roy Rogers, Gene Autry, Hopalong Cassidy
  B-horror: low budget genre films
  Training ground: many major directors started in B-films
    (Roger Corman, Don Siegel, Robert Aldrich)

RISK MANAGEMENT:
  Genre formulas reduce creative risk
  Star vehicles: pre-sold to audience (star following)
  Sequels: proven concept reduces risk (not new in studio era)
  The "package" (post-Paramount): star + director + script
    as financing unit (transitional to modern era)
```

## The Star System

```
THE STAR SYSTEM

FUNCTION OF THE STAR:
  Pre-sold commodity: audience buys ticket for star, not film
  Brand: studio can calculate revenue based on star's recent performance
  Risk management: star with track record = bankable insurance
  Product differentiation: Star A's Westerns vs Star B's Westerns

CONTRACT STARS (Studio Era):
  Joan Crawford (MGM/Warner Bros): female stardom as construction
    Studio rebuilt her identity: name change, appearance, persona
    Crawford: born Lucille LeSueur, renamed by MGM contest
  Clark Gable (MGM): "King of Hollywood"
    Under contract: had no choice in roles (Selznick loaned to MGM
    for Gone With the Wind in exchange for distribution rights)
  Bette Davis (Warner Bros): fought against system, usually lost
    Refused roles: suspended 3 months (contract extended)
    Failed lawsuit (Davis v Warner Bros, 1937): contract upheld

STAR CONSTRUCTION:
  Publicity department: controlled all press coverage
  Photography: approved photos only (no unflattering images)
  Personal life: manufactured or suppressed
  Names changed: arcane, ethnic, "wrong" names replaced
  (Spangler Arlington Brugh -> Robert Taylor)
  (Issur Danielovitch Demsky -> Kirk Douglas)
  (Marion Morrison -> John Wayne)
  (Norma Jeane Mortenson -> Marilyn Monroe)

  Studio as platform/publisher:
  Analogous to: record labels building pop stars in 1990s
  Or: TikTok/YouTube: platform promotes creators it controls

STAR ECONOMICS (classical):
  Star salary: modest vs today (contract, not per-film)
  Crawford: $250K/year at MGM (1930s)
  Gable: $350K/year (late 1930s)
  Average US worker: ~$1,000-1,500/year
  But: no percentage of profits (studio gets all upside)

POST-PARAMOUNT STAR SYSTEM:
  Stars become independent (form their own production companies)
  James Stewart: first major star percentage deal (Westerns, 1950s)
  Got % of profits -> made enormous amounts on Winchester '73 (1950)
  Marlon Brando, Burt Lancaster: independent production companies
  Stars now: $20-30M per picture (2020s) or % of profits or both
  Back-end: studios claim "net profits" never exist
    (Hollywood accounting: famous for creative bookkeeping)
    Forrest Gump (1994): $678M gross, "never made a profit" (Paramount)
```

## Paramount Decree (1948)

```
UNITED STATES v. PARAMOUNT PICTURES (1948)

CASE HISTORY:
  1938: DOJ files antitrust suit against Big Five + Little Three
  Charge: conspiracy to restrain trade in film production,
          distribution, and exhibition
  1940: consent decree (block booking limited; other practices)
  WWII: proceedings suspended
  1946: resumed
  1948: Supreme Court ruling

VIOLATIONS FOUND:
  Block booking: force exhibitors to take whole slate of films
                 to get any films (especially hits)
  Blind bidding: exhibitors must bid on films before seeing them
  Clearances: exclusive time periods for first-run theaters
  Formula deals: fixed percentage terms for theater groups
  Pool arrangements: theaters divided among studios

SUPREME COURT HOLDING:
  Per se antitrust violation:
  Vertical integration of production + distribution + exhibition
  itself constitutes illegal restraint of trade in film industry
  Structural remedy required: studios divest theaters

CONSENT DECREES (1949-1952):
  Studios: signed decrees to divest theater holdings
  RKO: first to comply (1948, before formal decree)
  Paramount: divested by 1950
  Warner Bros, 20th Century Fox, Loews (MGM): by 1952

EFFECTS:
  Studios: sold theater chains (became exhibition chains like
           AMC, which grew from acquisitions of studio theaters)
  Block booking: ended
  Blind bidding: eventually ended in most states
  Independent theaters: could now book films from any distributor

  Production: studios reduced committed production
  Contract system: began unraveling (no guaranteed studio-owned theater
                   outlets = uncertainty in distribution = less commitment)
  Independent production: grew (studios became financiers/distributors
                           as much as producers)

  TV competition: simultaneously, TV taking audience
  1946: 4 billion theater admissions/year (US peak)
  1950: 3 billion
  1955: 2 billion
  Combined effect: studio system unsustainable

LONG-TERM IMPACT:
  Today's studios are primarily distribution + financing entities
  Production: mix of studio and independent
  Exhibition: independent chains (largest: AMC, Regal, Cinemark)
  Current: streamers (Netflix, Disney+) = vertical integration attempt
           but production (owned) + distribution (owned platform)
           without exhibition (they don't own theaters)
  DOJ watching: 2022 Paramount Decree formally vacated
                (studios CAN now own theaters again; haven't yet)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"The Production Code was government censorship."** The Code was industry self-regulation. Government censorship (state/local censor boards) existed simultaneously and was what the industry was trying to preempt. The Code was private, contractual, and economically enforced — studios agreed not to distribute uncertified films to theaters that agreed to the MPPDA system. First Amendment protections didn't apply to film until 1952 (Burstyn v Wilson).

**"The Big Five lost all power after 1948."** Distribution remained with the studios and became more powerful in some ways after the Paramount Decree. Studio distribution arms controlled access to major theatrical release. The studios became vertically integrated in production and distribution without exhibition — still oligopoly, just differently structured. The "New Hollywood" era required a studio distribution deal as much as the classical era.

**"Stars were victims of the contract system."** Some were. Others built extraordinary careers and wealth within it. The system provided enormous resources, professional training, and market access that independent production couldn't match. The exploitation and the opportunity coexisted. The stars who successfully broke out (Davis, Gable eventually, then 1950s stars) often used the studio system as a platform before leveraging independence.

**"Hollywood Accounting is a myth."** It is real, well-documented, and systematic. Studios structure film accounting with overhead charges, distribution fees charged back to the film, interest on production loans, and marketing costs that can make a $678M-grossing film ($200M production cost) show no net profit. David Prowse (Darth Vader) sued for Phantom Menace profit share and was told the film never made net profits. Winston Groom (Forrest Gump novelist) was told the same. The practice is not hidden; it's contractual. Sophisticated talent insist on "adjusted gross" rather than "net profit" participation.
