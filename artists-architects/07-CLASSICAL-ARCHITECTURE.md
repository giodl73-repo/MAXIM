# Classical Architecture — Vitruvius, Palladio, Wren, Vanbrugh, Soane

## The Classical System

Classical architecture is not one style — it is a system of related conventions derived from Greek and Roman building and codified by Vitruvius (c. 25 BCE) and later theorists.

```
THE CLASSICAL ORDERS

  DORIC (simplest)          IONIC (refined)          CORINTHIAN (most ornate)
  ┌────────────────┐        ┌──────────────────┐      ┌──────────────────────┐
  │  ENTABLATURE   │        │   ENTABLATURE    │      │    ENTABLATURE       │
  │  (cornice,     │        │   with frieze    │      │    with frieze       │
  │   frieze,      │        │   of continuous  │      │    of continuous     │
  │   architrave)  │        │   scroll         │      │    scroll            │
  ├────────────────┤        ├──────────────────┤      ├──────────────────────┤
  │  CAPITAL       │        │  CAPITAL with    │      │  CAPITAL with        │
  │  plain abacus  │        │  volutes (spirals)│     │  acanthus leaves     │
  ├────────────────┤        ├──────────────────┤      ├──────────────────────┤
  │  SHAFT         │        │  SHAFT (fluted)  │      │  SHAFT (fluted)      │
  │  (plain or     │        │                  │      │  more slender        │
  │   fluted)      │        │                  │      │                      │
  ├────────────────┤        ├──────────────────┤      ├──────────────────────┤
  │  BASE: none    │        │  BASE: torus,    │      │  BASE: similar to    │
  │                │        │  trochilus,torus │      │  Ionic               │
  └────────────────┘        └──────────────────┘      └──────────────────────┘

  TUSCAN: Simplified Doric (Roman invention)
  COMPOSITE: Ionic + Corinthian capital (Roman invention)

  VITRUVIAN CHARACTER:
  Doric = masculine, strong, austere → military temples (Mars, Minerva)
  Ionic = matron-like, moderate → matronly deities (Juno, Diana)
  Corinthian = slender, virginal → Venus, Flora
```

The orders are not decorative choices — they carry meaning. They are a language system where forms have conventional associations that trained viewers read automatically.

## Vitruvius (c. 80–15 BCE)

Marcus Vitruvius Pollio wrote De Architectura (On Architecture) — the only complete architectural treatise to survive from antiquity. Ten books covering: city planning, building materials, temple design, the orders, private dwellings, hydraulics, clocks, and military machines.

### The Three Principles

The most cited passage in architectural theory:

```
  FIRMITAS (strength/durability) — it must stand up
  UTILITAS (utility/function) — it must serve its purpose
  VENUSTAS (beauty/delight) — it must be beautiful

  Vitruvius: these three are inseparable. A building that
  is beautiful but not sturdy is not good architecture.
  A building that is sturdy and functional but ugly is not
  good architecture. All three are necessary.

  Le Corbusier's phrase "a machine for living in" is sometimes
  cited as an abuse of Vitruvius' utilitas at the expense of
  venustas. But Le Corbusier also cared deeply about beauty —
  he just redefined what it was.
```

### Symmetry and Proportion

Vitruvius' theory of proportion is based on the human body:

```
  "For without symmetry and proportion no temple can have
  a regular plan; that is, it must have an exact proportion
  worked out after the fashion of the members of a
  finely-shaped human body."

  The Vitruvian Man (Leonardo's drawing c.1490 illustrates this):
  ─ Navel at the center of a circle (arms and legs extended)
  ─ Genitals at the center of a square (arms horizontal,
    feet together)
  ─ These are Vitruvius' proportions: body defines the geometry.

  COLUMN PROPORTIONS:
  Doric: height = 6–7 × base diameter
  Ionic: height = 8–9 × base diameter (more slender)
  Corinthian: height = 9–10 × base diameter (most slender)

  This ratio maps to the Vitruvian "character" of each order.
```

## Andrea Palladio (1508–1580)

Palladio is the most influential architect who ever lived — not by direct buildings (he built mainly in Vicenza and the Veneto) but through his Quattro Libri dell'Architettura (1570), which was the standard architectural reference in England and America for 200 years.

### The Four Books

The Quattro Libri is a practical manual: drawings of the classical orders with exact measurements; plans and elevations of Palladio's own buildings (with corrections and improvements); reconstructions of Roman buildings; and theory.

Its practical value: unlike most treatises, it gives exact measurements. Any architect with the book could build a Palladian building anywhere.

### Villa Rotonda (c. 1570)

```
  PLAN:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                      │
  │          PORTICO      PORTICO                        │
  │             ┌──────────────────┐                     │
  │  PORTICO    │                  │   PORTICO           │
  │    ──────── │   CENTRAL DOME   │ ────────            │
  │             │     (round)      │                    │
  │             │                  │                    │
  │             └──────────────────┘                    │
  │          PORTICO      PORTICO                        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  4 IDENTICAL FACADES (one for each cardinal direction)
  Each with a Roman temple front (6 Ionic columns + pediment)
  Central dome like the Pantheon
  Perfect bilateral symmetry on all four axes

  NOT A CHURCH OR TEMPLE: a private country villa.
  First domestic building to use a full temple front.
  This colonnade-on-a-villa becomes the standard for
  English country houses, American civic buildings (Monticello,
  the Capitol, thousands of plantation houses), and bank buildings
  worldwide.
```

### Palladianism in England and America

The Quattro Libri was translated into English (1715–20, by Giacomo Leoni; later editions by William Kent). Inigo Jones had already introduced Palladio to England around 1620. The Georgian architecture that defines 18th-century England and colonial America is almost entirely Palladian:

- Country houses: Chiswick House (Burlington, 1729), Stourhead
- American civic: Monticello (Jefferson, directly from Palladio)
- The U.S. Capitol (multiple architects citing Palladio)
- Thomas Jefferson called Palladio "the Bible"

This is a remarkable example of one 16th-century Italian architect's drawings shaping most of the architecture seen by English-speaking people for 200 years.

## Christopher Wren (1632–1723)

Wren is England's greatest architect. His career as an architect began after the Great Fire of London (1666) — which destroyed 13,000 houses, 87 churches, and St. Paul's Cathedral — and he designed 51 of the rebuilding churches, including the new St. Paul's.

### St. Paul's Cathedral (1675–1711)

```
  THE DESIGN PROBLEM:
  Wren's first approved design (the "Great Model") was a Greek
  cross plan with a large dome — closer to Michelangelo's St. Peter's.
  The Anglican clergy rejected it: it didn't suit Anglican liturgy
  (which requires a nave for processions and a chancel for choir).

  THE COMPROMISE:
  Wren got a "warrant design" approved (Latin cross plan),
  but with a clause allowing him to make "ornamental changes."
  He then made substantial structural changes he considered
  "ornamental."

  THE DOME SOLUTION (a genuine structural innovation):
  ─ OUTER DOME: Light lead-covered timber dome — visually
    correct from outside, correct height for London skyline.
  ─ INNER DOME: Lower, shallower dome — visually correct
    from inside, proper height for interior.
  ─ HIDDEN BRICK CONE: A structural masonry cone between them,
    carrying the weight of the lantern (850 tons) and concealed
    within the space between inner and outer domes.
  ─ Three domes in one structure — each serves different purposes.

  The engineering solution to the triple-dome problem:
  Wren separated structural function (the cone), inner visual
  effect (inner dome), and outer visual effect (outer dome).
  This is modular design applied to architecture.

  Compare:
  Brunelleschi: single double-shell dome
  Michelangelo: double-shell dome (St. Peter's)
  Wren: three independent shells, each optimized separately
```

### Scientific Background

Wren was a scientist before he was an architect — he was Savilian Professor of Astronomy at Oxford (1661) and a founding member of the Royal Society. He knew Newton, Hooke (who was his structural consultant for St. Paul's), and the leading scientists of his era. His approach to structural problems was analytical rather than traditional.

## John Vanbrugh (1664–1726)

Vanbrugh is the most theatrical of the English Baroque architects. His background was in the theater (he wrote popular Restoration comedies) before pivoting to architecture with no formal training.

### Blenheim Palace and Castle Howard

His two great works demonstrate the Baroque conception of architecture as theatrical scenography:

```
  CASTLE HOWARD (1699–1726):
  ─ First building in England with a central dome (preceded St. Paul's
    dome by several years in practical completion).
  ─ Asymmetrical arrangement of wings that must be understood
    in sequence — you "read" the building while moving through it.
  ─ The approach road is designed to give progressive views.

  BLENHEIM PALACE (1705–1722):
  ─ Given by Parliament to the Duke of Marlborough for defeating
    Louis XIV at Blenheim.
  ─ Massive — 320 rooms, 187-foot entrance facade.
  ─ The Great Court (entrance) is not domestic in scale —
    it is civic/martial.
  ─ Sarah Churchill (the Duchess) hated it. She wanted
    a livable house; Vanbrugh gave her a national monument.
    "No house," she said, "but a town."

  THE PICTURESQUE QUALITY: Vanbrugh designed landscape and
  building together — the bridge over a dry valley at Blenheim,
  the fictional ruined tower at Claremont. The building is
  meant to be experienced across landscape, not just from
  close. This anticipates the Picturesque movement.
```

## John Soane (1753–1837)

Soane is the most original English architect and the hardest to place — a classical architect who subverted classicism, stripping it down to light and space and shadow in ways that anticipate modern architecture.

### The Bank of England

```
  SOANE'S BANKING HALLS (1788–1833, largely demolished 1930s):
  ─ Large spaces covered by shallow brick domes with "lune"
    openings (curved cutouts) that let in light from above.
  ─ The architecture dematerializes: walls dissolve into
    incised lines rather than applied moldings.
  ─ Light arrives from unexpected sources, animating surfaces.
  ─ No heavy ornament — classical motifs stripped to thin incisions.

  "PRIMITIVE HUTS" AND INCISED ORNAMENT:
  Soane was influenced by the French rationalist theorist
  Laugier (Essay on Architecture, 1753), who argued that
  architecture's origin and ideal was the primitive hut —
  and that all the orders derive from structural necessities
  (columns from tree trunks, architraves from beams).
  If so, ornament should not be applied but incised —
  suggesting structure rather than decorating it.
```

### Sir John Soane's Museum

Soane's house in Lincoln's Inn Fields (London) is itself his masterpiece — a collection of art, architecture, and antiquities arranged in complex, layered, light-filled rooms. He manipulated mirrors, skylights, and colored glass to produce perpetually shifting light. The house has no conventional sequence; you are always discovering another room, another corridor, another view.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Classical" = ancient Greek** — In architectural usage, "Classical" includes Greek, Roman, and any subsequent revival. Roman architecture added the arch, vault, and dome to the Greek post-and-lintel system. The Pantheon's rotunda (118–125 CE) and the Roman basilica (secular hall) are classical. The orders are Greek; the spatial typology is often Roman.

**Palladio's buildings are in Italy** — Most are. But Palladio's architectural legacy in English-speaking countries comes from his book, not his buildings. Very few English architects visited the Veneto; millions lived in Palladian houses built by architects following his drawings.

**Wren was always an architect** — No. He was one of the leading scientists of the 17th century (Savilian Professor of Astronomy) and switched to architecture after the Great Fire. There are no other architects of his stature who came to it from science. His analytical approach to the dome problem — separating structural function from visual effect — reflects scientific training.

**Classical architecture is "conservative"** — Classical architecture has been used for radical programs (revolutionary French Neoclassicism), fascism (Albert Speer's Nazi buildings), liberal democracy (the Capitol, Lincoln Memorial), and commercial banking (virtually every 19th-century bank facade). The style is politically neutral; the uses are not.
