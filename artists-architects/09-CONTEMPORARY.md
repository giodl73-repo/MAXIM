# Contemporary Art and Architecture — Koolhaas, Hadid, Piano, Foster, Gehry, Warhol, Rothko, Basquiat

## The Postwar Break

After 1945, the twin certainties of modernism — that form follows function, that less is more — began to unravel. The confident programs of Sullivan, Corbusier, and Mies had produced brilliant buildings and disastrous urban plans. By the 1960s, both art and architecture were questioning their own foundations.

```
ART (POSTWAR):
  Abstract Expressionism (NY, 1945–60)
  ─ Pollock, de Kooning, Rothko, Newman, Kline
  ─ The gesture, the field of color, the scale of experience

  Pop Art (1960s)
  ─ Warhol, Lichtenstein, Johns, Rauschenberg
  ─ Consumer culture, media imagery, the boundary of art/commodity

  Conceptual Art (1965–)
  ─ Sol LeWitt, Joseph Kosuth, Lawrence Weiner
  ─ The concept is the work; execution is secondary

  Neo-Expressionism (1980s)
  ─ Basquiat, Kiefer, Baselitz
  ─ Return of figuration; raw paint; political history

ARCHITECTURE (POSTWAR):
  Late Modernism (1960s–80s)
  ─ Continuing modernist principles with more complexity
  ─ Piano, Foster (high-tech); Ando (critical regionalism)

  Postmodernism (1960s–90s)
  ─ Venturi, Jencks, Graves, Moore
  ─ "Both/and" instead of "either/or"; historical reference;
    decoration as meaning; irony

  Deconstructivism (1988–)
  ─ Gehry, Hadid, Libeskind, Tschumi, Koolhaas
  ─ Formal instability; fractured geometry; questioning function

  Digital Architecture (2000–)
  ─ Parametric design; algorithms generating form
  ─ Hadid late work; Bjarke Ingels Group (BIG)
```

## Abstract Expressionism — Rothko

**Mark Rothko (1903–1970)** is the central figure of Color Field painting — the quieter, more contemplative wing of Abstract Expressionism.

### Color Field

```
  ROTHKO'S PAINTINGS:
  ─ Large canvases (typically 6–10 feet tall).
  ─ Two or three soft-edged rectangular bands of color,
    stacked vertically.
  ─ The colors are related — warm to warm, cool to cool,
    or carefully balanced complements.
  ─ The edges blur — there is no hard boundary.
  ─ The color appears to glow from within.

  HOW THIS WORKS:
  Rothko stained the canvas with thin oil and pigment —
  the paint soaks in rather than sitting on top.
  This produces the "inner glow" — color is part of
  the canvas surface, not a coating on it.

  Multiple thin layers (glazes) of different hues produce
  the warm/cool oscillation within a single color area.

  ROTHKO'S STATEMENT:
  "I'm not an abstractionist. I'm not interested in the
  relationship of color or form or anything else. I'm
  interested only in expressing basic human emotions —
  tragedy, ecstasy, doom, and so on."

  He wanted viewers to stand close — 18 inches from the
  canvas — and be surrounded by the color field.
  When a woman cried in front of one of his paintings,
  he said he had achieved a religious experience.

  THE ROTHKO CHAPEL (Houston, 1971):
  14 large paintings in an octagonal non-denominational chapel.
  All dark — deep plum, near-black maroon.
  The most concentrated statement of color field as spiritual space.
```

## Pop Art — Andy Warhol

**Andy Warhol (1928–1987)** is the most important art-historical figure of the second half of the 20th century — not because his work is beautiful but because it definitively answered the question Duchamp opened: what is art?

### The Silkscreens

```
  CAMPBELL'S SOUP CANS (1962):
  32 paintings of Campbell's soup can labels — identical size,
  identical technique, differing only by soup flavor.
  Exhibited at the Ferus Gallery, LA. A nearby gallery sold
  real soup cans for 33 cents each.

  WHAT WARHOL FORCED:
  ─ Consumer culture is not "low" — it is the dominant visual
    culture of the era. Why should art ignore it?
  ─ Repetition and seriality (like factory production) as
    aesthetic choice, not failure of originality.
  ─ The "aura" of the unique original (Walter Benjamin's
    concept) is already gone from consumer society.
    Warhol makes this visible in art itself.

  MARILYN DIPTYCH (1962):
  50 silkscreens of Marilyn Monroe (from a single publicity photo),
  left half in color, right half in black and white that fades
  to near-invisible.
  ─ Mass reproduction flattens a person to an image.
  ─ The image multiplies while the person (who just died) fades.
  ─ The silkscreen technique allows intentional variation
    (off-register, too much ink, wrong color) — which becomes
    the aesthetic.

  THE FACTORY AND ASSISTANTS:
  Warhol's studio was called "The Factory." He had assistants.
  He said "I want to be a machine."
  This is the logical extension of Duchamp's readymade:
  if the concept defines art, execution can be delegated.
  This became the model for the large-scale conceptual art
  studios of the late 20th century (Jeff Koons, Damien Hirst).
```

## Jean-Michel Basquiat (1960–1988)

Basquiat is the most important American painter of the 1980s — the figure who reconnected painting to political content, personal biography, and the Black urban experience that mainstream art had ignored.

### The Paintings

```
  BACKGROUND:
  ─ Grew up in Brooklyn; Haitian-Puerto Rican heritage.
  ─ Began as a street artist (SAMO graffiti, 1977–1979).
  ─ Moved to painting on canvas around 1980.
  ─ By 1982, at 22, he was shown at Documenta (Kassel)
    and selling for significant sums.
  ─ Dead at 27 from heroin overdose.

  VISUAL SYSTEM:
  ─ Raw, seemingly unfinished surfaces — multiple layers
    of paint, scratched through, text written and crossed out.
  ─ Figures drawn with energetic, unstable lines (influence
    of street art, Cy Twombly, and Jean Dubuffet).
  ─ Text as visual element: words, phrases, names
    crossed out, repeated, branded.
  ─ Recurrent symbols: crowns, copyright symbols (©),
    crosses, skulls, teeth, skeletons.

  CONTENT:
  ─ Black heroes and victims: athletes, jazz musicians,
    historical figures (Charles the First, 1982: Charlie Parker
    + Crown = royalty denied).
  ─ The anatomy of the Black body: both heroic (the athlete)
    and vulnerable (the dissected figure, the corpse).
  ─ The commodity system: copyright symbols applied to everything
    as critique of art market that profits from Black culture
    while marginalizing Black artists.
  ─ Police violence, economic inequality, historical erasure —
    embedded in images that also celebrate vitality and creativity.
```

## Renzo Piano (b. 1937)

Piano is the pre-eminent "civic architect" of the late 20th and early 21st centuries — associated with major cultural institutions built for public access.

### Centre Pompidou (with Richard Rogers, 1977)

```
  THE HIGH-TECH STRATEGY:
  ─ All mechanical systems (ducts, pipes, structural members,
    elevators) placed on the exterior.
  ─ The interior is a single column-free space, 160m × 60m,
    with no fixed walls.
  ─ Color-coding: blue (air), green (water), yellow (electrical),
    red (escalators and circulation), white (structure).

  THE RESULT:
  ─ Maximum flexible interior space (the stated program).
  ─ The "machines" of the building — typically hidden —
    become the aesthetic.
  ─ The facade is infrastructure.

  THE REVERSAL:
  Le Corbusier: the building is a machine (metaphor).
  Piano+Rogers: the building shows its machinery (literal).
  This is honesty about industrialization taken to an extreme
  — architecture as structural expressionism.

  IMPACT: Initially controversial in Paris. Now the most visited
  building in France.
```

## Norman Foster (b. 1935)

Foster represents the mature "high-tech" tradition — structural expressionism with more refinement and urban integration than the Centre Pompidou.

### The Gherkin (30 St. Mary Axe, 2003)

```
  FORM DRIVEN BY FUNCTION AND PHYSICS:
  ─ Circular plan reduces wind load (no corners to catch wind).
  ─ Double-skin facade with spiraling air shafts:
    air enters at low pressure zones, rises through the building,
    exits at high pressure zones — natural ventilation without
    mechanical air conditioning for most of the year.
  ─ The diagonal structural grid (diagrid) is efficient
    for a circular tower — the diagonals carry both axial
    and lateral loads with less steel than a conventional frame.
  ─ The swelling middle and tapered top are optimal forms
    for wind performance, not arbitrary aesthetics.

  This is engineering aesthetics: the form is shaped by
  physical performance requirements, and the physical
  optimization produces beauty.
  The same principle as Sullivan's "form follows function,"
  but now computed rather than intuited.
```

## Frank Gehry (b. 1929)

Gehry is the most recognizable living architect — the Bilbao Effect (the claim that a great building can transform a city's economy and identity) is named after his Guggenheim Museum Bilbao.

### Guggenheim Museum Bilbao (1997)

```
  FORMAL SYSTEM:
  ─ Complex curved titanium surfaces that cannot be described
    by simple geometric forms (no circles, no flat planes).
  ─ Designed using CATIA software (originally for aerospace —
    Dassault Aviation) adapted by Gehry's firm.
  ─ Each titanium panel is unique — computer-cut to precise
    specification from the 3D digital model.

  THE DIGITAL-TO-PHYSICAL PIPELINE:
  Design in 3D CAD → CNC-cut steel structure → CNC-cut
  titanium cladding → assembly.

  Without computers, this building couldn't exist.
  The forms are not hand-drawable; the manufacturing is not
  possible without CNC. This is the first major building
  whose form was enabled entirely by digital design/fabrication.

  THE "BILBAO EFFECT":
  Bilbao was a post-industrial Basque city in economic decline.
  The museum opened 1997; tourist arrivals tripled within 3 years.
  GDP growth and urban regeneration attributed (partly) to the museum.

  This claim has been contested and replicated worldwide —
  every post-industrial city now wants a "Bilbao moment."
  The success rate of similar attempts is lower than advocates admit.
```

## Zaha Hadid (1950–2016)

Hadid was the first woman to win the Pritzker Prize (2004). Her work pioneered parametric design — using algorithmic systems to generate architectural form.

```
  PARAMETRIC DESIGN:
  Traditional design: architect draws forms.
  Parametric: architect defines rules, parameters, and constraints;
  the computer generates form families from these rules;
  the architect selects and refines.

  HADID'S FORMS:
  ─ Flowing, continuous surfaces with no sharp joints.
  ─ Floors, walls, and ceilings merge into a continuous shell.
  ─ Furniture and architecture are continuous (one material, one form).
  ─ The form appears "liquid" or "frozen in motion."

  MAXXI Museum (Rome, 2009):
  Overlapping curved galleries that intersect at multiple angles.
  No single orientation — the visitor is always turning, angling,
  looking up at unusual angles. Disorientation is deliberate:
  it breaks the conventional museum as neutral container.

  CRITICISM:
  ─ The flowing forms are visually distinctive but often difficult
    to occupy — furniture is hard to place; hanging art on curved
    walls requires custom solutions.
  ─ The parametric aesthetic has been widely copied, often
    without the structural or spatial logic.
```

## Rem Koolhaas (b. 1944)

Koolhaas is the most intellectually influential architect of the late 20th century. His firm (OMA) designs buildings; his writing (Delirious New York, S,M,L,XL) shapes how architects think.

```
  DELIRIOUS NEW YORK (1978):
  ─ A "retroactive manifesto" for Manhattan.
  ─ Argues that New York's skyscrapers, grids, and density
    produced a culture of "Manhattanism" — the celebration of
    congestion, program mixing, and the culture of the elevator.
  ─ The skyscraper as an instrument of "social condenser":
    mixing programs (office, apartment, restaurant, hotel)
    produces urban vitality.

  GENERIC CITY (1994):
  ─ Most cities are converging toward a generic template
    (airports, malls, office parks) — the historical city
    is an exception that resists globalization.
  ─ The generic city may be the honest form of contemporary life.
  ─ Provocative: Koolhaas doesn't condemn it but describes it
    with analytical sympathy.

  CCTV HEADQUARTERS (Beijing, 2012):
  ─ Two leaning towers connected at top and bottom by a bridge.
  ─ The form makes the entire structural system visible as a loop.
  ─ No conventional setback from ground; the building wraps
    space at ground level.
  ─ Structurally unprecedented: the asymmetric cantilever
    at the top required highly sophisticated structural analysis.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Abstract Expressionism = uncontrolled spontaneity** — Pollock's drip paintings are systematic (specific paint viscosity, height of pour, walking speed, canvas on floor). Rothko's color fields are the result of years of research into color relationships. The "spontaneous" appearance is constructed.

**The Bilbao Effect can be replicated** — The Bilbao Guggenheim was successful partly due to unique circumstances: timing (1990s economic boom), location (genuine post-industrial need), the quality of the building. Many subsequent "iconic building" commissions by cities seeking transformation have not achieved the same result. The effect is real but not reliably reproducible.

**Basquiat was "discovered" by Warhol** — Basquiat was already exhibiting and selling before he met Warhol. The relationship (they became close friends and collaborators) changed both careers. The "discovery" narrative erases Basquiat's independent artistic development and positions Warhol as the gatekeeper of legitimacy — which is exactly the kind of erasure Basquiat's paintings critique.

**Parametric architecture = the Hadid style** — Parametric design is a method, not a style. BIG (Bjarke Ingels Group) uses parametric methods to produce functional, contextual buildings that don't look like Hadid's work. The flowing-liquid aesthetic is Hadid's specific aesthetic application of the method, not the method itself.
