# Post-Impressionism — Cezanne, Van Gogh, Gauguin, Seurat

## What Post-Impressionism Is

"Post-Impressionism" is not a movement — it's a critical label (coined by Roger Fry, 1910) for four painters who each took Impressionism as their starting point and pushed it in fundamentally different directions. They are united only by what they came after.

```
  IMPRESSIONISM'S ACHIEVEMENT:
  ─ Color as light, not local color.
  ─ Immediate perceptual experience.
  ─ The visible world, honestly observed.

  WHAT IMPRESSIONISM LEFT UNSOLVED:
  ─ Structure (the world dissolves into shimmering light).
  ─ Emotion (pure observation doesn't carry personal feeling).
  ─ Meaning (what is painting FOR beyond visual pleasure?).
  ─ Scientific color theory not fully realized.

  THE FOUR RESPONSES:

  CEZANNE  → Structure. Rebuild the classical solidity that
              Impressionism dissolved. "Do Poussin over again
              from nature."

  VAN GOGH → Emotion. Use color and mark as expression of
              inner psychological state. Intensity over accuracy.

  GAUGUIN  → Meaning/Symbolism. Escape European modernity.
              Art as spiritual expression, not observation.

  SEURAT   → Science. Apply color theory rigorously.
              Divisionism: pure color dots, optical mixing.
```

## Paul Cezanne (1839–1906)

Cezanne is the hinge figure of modern art — he occupies the same position in painting that Kant occupies in philosophy. Everything before is preparation; everything after is response.

### The Problem Cezanne Solved

Impressionism showed that you paint light, not objects. But in painting light, the objects dissolved — the haystack became a vibrating surface, the cathedral became a wash of color. Where is the structure?

```
  CEZANNE'S PROGRAM (his letters state it directly):
  "I want to do Poussin over again from nature."
  "To treat nature by the cylinder, the sphere, the cone."

  Poussin = classical structure, geometry, composition.
  "From nature" = Impressionist observation, not studio convention.

  WHAT HE ACTUALLY DID:
  ─ Multiple viewpoints within a single painting.
    A tabletop tilts at one angle in one area and another in another.
    A jar is seen from slightly above in one place and slightly below
    elsewhere. This is not error — it is Cezanne painting what he
    knows about an object's three-dimensional structure while
    remaining true to the surface of the canvas.

  ─ Passage (passage): Cezanne dissolves the edge between two
    neighboring planes, allowing color to pass from one object
    to the next without a hard boundary.
    This makes the canvas surface cohere as a flat system of
    color patches, not as a window onto space.

  ─ "Modulation" not "modelling":
    Traditional painting: model form by adding white (for light)
    and black (for shadow) to a base color.
    Cezanne: modulate form by shifting hue — cooler (blue-green)
    for shadow; warmer (red-yellow) for light.
    Color does the work that value (light/dark) used to do.
```

### Why Cezanne Leads to Cubism

```
  CEZANNE'S MULTIPLE VIEWPOINTS:
  The Still Life paintings show a table seen from slightly
  different positions simultaneously. The fruit bowl may be
  seen from above; the cloth from the side; the bottle from
  eye level.

  PICASSO AND BRAQUE (1907–1912) saw this and asked:
  "What if we made the multiple viewpoints explicit?"
  → Cubism: analyze the object from multiple viewpoints
    and reassemble them on the flat canvas.

  Cezanne didn't intend Cubism. He intended to remain true
  to the complexity of perception — that when you look at a
  table, your eye moves, your head shifts slightly, and you
  synthesize the view. Cubism made this process explicit.

  Cezanne is the reason Picasso said:
  "Cezanne is the father of us all."
```

### Mont Sainte-Victoire

Cezanne painted this mountain near his home in Aix-en-Provence over 60 times in the last 20 years of his life. Like Monet's series, it is both subject and laboratory:

```
  LATE MONT SAINTE-VICTOIRE (1902–1906):
  ─ The mountain dissolves into faceted planes of blue-green,
    orange, and grey.
  ─ The distinction between mountain, sky, and foreground
    flattens as color passages move across the canvas.
  ─ Yet the mountain is more present, more itself, than in any
    conventional landscape.

  THIS IS THE PARADOX OF CEZANNE:
  By being more honest about how painting works (flat,
  colored surface) he achieves a stronger sense of presence
  than illusionism.
```

## Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890)

Van Gogh is the most misread figure in art history: the myth of the mad genius obscures the fact that he was a systematic, theoretically sophisticated, deliberately intentional painter.

### The Self-Education

Van Gogh was largely self-taught. He studied Japanese woodblock prints obsessively; he copied Rembrandt and Millet; he read widely on color theory (Delacroix, Blanc's Grammar of the Arts of Drawing). His letters to his brother Theo (more than 800 survive) are among the most sustained records of an artist's thinking in any century.

### Color as Emotion

```
  VAN GOGH'S COLOR THEORY:

  He read Charles Blanc (1867): "In seeking the combination of
  two complementaries, one kills both." Meaning: juxtaposed
  complementaries vibrate against each other, destabilizing both.

  Van Gogh's use: put complementaries in direct contact not to
  harmonize but to generate visual tension.

  Example: Night Café (1888)
  "I have tried to express the terrible passions of humanity
  by means of red and green."
  The red walls and green billiard table are simultaneously
  complementary (optically intense) and psychologically disturbing
  (red and green clash rather than harmonize here).

  THIS IS NOT "painting how I feel" in a naive sense.
  It is a specific, theorized technique: color as emotional carrier.
```

### The Brushstroke as Structure

```
  IMPRESSIONISM: Brushstroke records optical impression.
  VAN GOGH: Brushstroke is itself the structural element.

  In The Starry Night (1889):
  ─ The night sky is not an amorphous dark field.
  ─ It is made of discrete, rhythmic, curving strokes.
  ─ Each stroke has direction, length, color.
  ─ The strokes create a visual flow (the swirls of the sky)
    that is simultaneously atmospheric and structural.

  In the Wheat Fields:
  ─ Different marks for wheat, sky, and earth.
  ─ The wheat is short horizontal dashes; the sky long curves;
    the ground slower, heavier marks.
  ─ The world is differentiated by mark type, not just color.

  This is what Expressionism will later formalize:
  the mark itself (gesture) as the primary carrier of meaning.
```

### The Production Rate

Van Gogh painted approximately 900 paintings in 10 years of serious work. In his last 70 days (Saint-Rémy and Auvers-sur-Oise), he produced over 150 paintings. This is not prolific sloppiness — he was painting with enormous deliberate concentration. Many of the late paintings are the most technically developed of his career.

## Paul Gauguin (1848–1903)

Gauguin is the most morally complicated Post-Impressionist. His art made genuine aesthetic achievements; his life was characterized by abandonment of his family and exploitation of colonial subjects in Tahiti and the Marquesas.

### The Symbolic Color Program

```
  GAUGUIN'S STATED PROGRAM ("Avant et Après," "Noa Noa"):

  Impressionism: Nature as seen.
  Gauguin: Nature as felt, as spiritually significant.

  "I am a great artist and I know it. It is because I am
   that I have endured such suffering."

  SYNTHETISM: (with Émile Bernard, Pont-Aven group)
  ─ Simplified, bold forms.
  ─ Flat areas of non-naturalistic color.
  ─ Black outlines enclosing colored areas (cloisonnism —
    like medieval cloisonné enamelwork or stained glass).
  ─ Color carries symbolic, spiritual meaning — not optical truth.

  Vision After the Sermon (1888):
  ─ Breton women after hearing a sermon.
  ─ Their vision (Jacob wrestling the angel) appears in a
    field of pure red — not red as light, but red as the
    color of the spiritual/visionary experience.
  ─ The flat red defines the visionary space differently from
    the observed world.
```

### Tahiti and Colonial Art

Gauguin moved to Tahiti in 1891, Marquesas in 1901, seeking what he called "primitive" art and life uncorrupted by European civilization. His Tahitian paintings use Polynesian figures, imagery, and symbolic content alongside Western pictorial structure.

This is profoundly complicated: Gauguin's paintings use the Tahitian subjects partly as formal material (the flat color works well with Polynesian physiognomy and dress) and partly as an exoticizing fantasy that misrepresents actual Tahitian culture. The late 20th-century critique of this work (as colonial exploitation) is substantive. His paintings are simultaneously among the most beautiful of the period and deeply compromised.

## Georges Seurat (1859–1891)

Seurat is the most scientific of the Post-Impressionists — he applied color theory with the rigor of an engineer.

### Divisionism / Pointillism

```
  THEORETICAL BASIS:
  ─ Chevreul (simultaneous contrast of complementaries, 1839)
  ─ Ogden Rood (Modern Chromatics, 1879): optical mixture
    of color is purer and more luminous than physical mixture.

  Physical mixture: blue + yellow paint = muddy green.
  Optical mixture: blue dot + yellow dot (close together, seen
    from distance) = more luminous green in the eye.

  SEURAT'S TECHNIQUE:
  ─ Apply pure color in small, uniform dots.
  ─ Dots of complementary colors placed adjacent → vibrate with intensity.
  ─ The eye mixes them at viewing distance → more luminous than
    mixed paint could achieve.
  ─ Every dot is the same size and shape (unlike Impressionist
    varied marks).

  A SUNDAY ON LA GRANDE JATTE (1884–1886):
  ─ 6.9 × 10.2 feet.
  ─ Painted from studies made outdoors; the final work painted
    in the studio.
  ─ Contains millions of dots.
  ─ It took 2 years.

  THE IRONY:
  Impressionism aimed to capture the instantaneous moment.
  Seurat's most famous painting of leisure required 2 years
  of systematic labor.
  The figures are stiff, hieratic, frozen — the opposite of
  Impressionist flux.
  The scientific method produced a monumental stillness.
```

### Mathematical Proportion

Seurat was also interested in Charles Henry's "psychophysics" — the idea that certain lines and angles produce specific emotional responses. Horizontal = calm; upward diagonal = joy; downward diagonal = sadness. His late paintings (La Parade, Le Chahut, Le Cirque) follow these principles systematically.

This is the application of scientific psychology to aesthetics — the same project that Mondrian and the De Stijl movement will pursue more abstractly.

## Comparison Table

| Figure | Post-Impressionist Direction | Core Technical Innovation | What It Led To |
|--------|------------------------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Cezanne** | Structure / classical solidity | Multiple viewpoints; modulation over modelling | Cubism (Picasso, Braque); modern abstraction generally |
| **Van Gogh** | Emotion / psychological intensity | Brushstroke as structure; color as emotion | Expressionism (Die Brücke, Der Blaue Reiter) |
| **Gauguin** | Spiritual / anti-naturalist | Flat color; Synthetism; symbolic color | Fauvism (Matisse); Primitivism in modern art |
| **Seurat** | Science / optical theory | Divisionism/Pointillism; systematic dot application | Neo-Impressionism; Op Art (Albers, Riley) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Van Gogh was mentally ill, therefore his art is disturbed** — The causality is wrong. Van Gogh's painting technique is theoretically grounded and deliberately executed. His mental illness (now thought to possibly be temporal lobe epilepsy) produced episodes when he couldn't paint, not episodes that produced the paintings. The Starry Night was painted during a stable period, not during a breakdown.

**Cezanne's "distorted" tabletops were errors** — Cezanne was one of the most technically sophisticated painters of his era. The tilting tabletops are deliberate — he was trying to show what his visual system synthesized across multiple viewpoints of a complex three-dimensional scene. When he paints a bowl of fruit seen partly from above and partly from the side simultaneously, this is correct to his perceptual experience, not a failure of classical perspective.

**Gauguin "escaped" to authentic primitive paradise** — Tahiti in 1891 was already a French colony with Western-style commerce, disease, and missionary Christianity. Gauguin's vision of untouched Polynesian paradise was largely a fantasy he constructed. The paintings depict an imagined pre-colonial Tahiti that no longer existed and arguably never existed as he depicted it.

**Pointillism is just Impressionism with smaller dots** — Seurat explicitly rejected Impressionist spontaneity and intuition. His technique was systematic and laborious; his compositions were geometric and planned; his color application was rule-governed by optical theory. The look is similar but the method and intention are opposite.
