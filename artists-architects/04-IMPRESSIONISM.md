# Impressionism — Monet, Manet, Degas, Renoir, Cassatt, Pissarro

## What Impressionism Actually Was

Impressionism is regularly described as "painting what the eye sees." This is correct but incomplete. It was also:
- A rejection of academic painting's hierarchy of subjects
- An application of Chevreul's color theory and Helmholtz's optics
- A response to photography (what can painting do that photos can't?)
- A social phenomenon centered on Paris in the 1860s–80s

```
ACADEMIC PAINTING (before Impressionism)
  ─ Required training at the École des Beaux-Arts.
  ─ Exhibition via the Salon — the single official gatekeeping institution.
  ─ Subject hierarchy: history/mythology > portraiture > genre > landscape > still life.
  ─ Smooth, invisible brushwork (no visible paint texture).
  ─ Local color (objects have their "real" color, modified by light/shadow).
  ─ Finished surfaces; no paint showing.

IMPRESSIONISM'S REVOLT (1860s–1880s)
  ─ Subject: ordinary modern Parisian life, landscape outdoors.
  ─ Exhibition: Salon des Refusés (1863), then independent shows.
  ─ Brushwork: visible, rapid, directional marks.
  ─ Color: influenced by Chevreul (simultaneous contrast of complementaries).
  ─ Light: replaces local color; objects have the color of the light that illuminates them.
  ─ Finish: deliberately incomplete-looking by academic standards.
```

**The name "Impressionism"** came from a hostile critic's review of Monet's Impression, Sunrise (1872), exhibited 1874. He used it as mockery. The painters adopted it.

## Édouard Manet (1832–1883)

Manet is chronologically the bridge between Realism and Impressionism. He rejected academic conventions but also rejected the rural plein-air painting of the Impressionists — he mostly painted in the studio, from urban subjects.

### Le Déjeuner sur l'herbe (1863)

```
  THE PAINTING: A naked woman sits with two fully dressed men
  at a picnic. Another woman bathes in the background.

  THE SCANDAL:
  ─ Naked women in art = mythology or allegory (Venus, Diana).
  ─ Manet's women are modern Parisian women.
  ─ She looks directly at the viewer — not modestly averted.
  ─ The setting is a contemporary leisure scene, not classical past.
  ─ The directness breaks the code: this is not Venus, this is
    a real woman, the viewer is present, and nobody is pretending otherwise.

  WHAT'S TECHNICALLY INTERESTING:
  ─ Flat modeling — Manet suppresses half-tones, moving directly
    from light to shadow. The figure looks "cut out."
  ─ Japanese prints (which were arriving in Paris in the 1850s)
    influenced this flattening.
  ─ This flatness would become central to Modern art — the acknowledgment
    that painting is on a flat surface, not a window.
```

### Olympia (1865)

A nude lying on a bed, attended by a Black servant with flowers (from a man, presumably). She looks at the viewer directly. This is clearly a Parisian courtesan, not a goddess.

Titian's Venus of Urbino (1534) and Giorgione's Sleeping Venus use the same pose — but the women are mythological or asleep. Manet's woman is awake, real, and looking at you. The outrage was precisely this: the same conventional motif stripped of its mythological alibi.

## Claude Monet (1840–1926)

Monet is the central figure of Impressionism and the one who pushed its principles furthest — eventually producing abstract paintings of light before anyone used that term.

### Series Painting

Monet's systematic innovation: paint the same subject under different light conditions, multiple times, to show how light transforms the subject:

```
  HAYSTACKS SERIES (1890–1891): 25 paintings of haystacks
  in different seasons, times of day, weather conditions.

  ROUEN CATHEDRAL SERIES (1892–1894): 30+ paintings of the
  cathedral facade at different times of day.

  MORNING → AFTERNOON → DUSK:
  The same stone facade shifts from lavender-grey to
  blinding gold to deep orange-brown.

  THE ARGUMENT: The real subject of the painting is not the
  haystack or the cathedral — it is the light at a specific
  moment. The motif is just the carrier.

  This is a radical epistemological claim:
  what we see is always conditioned by the light,
  which is always changing.
  There is no "true" appearance — only appearances.
```

### Water Lilies (Nymphéas, 1896–1926)

Monet's garden at Giverny became his late-career obsession. The large Water Lily paintings (some up to 42 feet wide) eliminate horizon, sky, and land. The surface of the water IS the surface of the canvas:

```
  STRUCTURE:
  ─ No horizon line. No stable ground.
  ─ Sky reflected in water, lily pads floating, reflections of trees.
  ─ You cannot locate yourself in space.
  ─ The painting is about immersion in perceptual experience, not
    representation of a scene.

  THESE ARE ABSTRACT PAINTINGS before abstract painting.
  Kandinsky, Rothko, and Abstract Expressionism trace lines
  through these late Monets — color as the whole content.

  Monet was nearly blind (cataracts) while painting some of the
  late Nymphéas. Some of the most abstract panels were repainted
  after surgery restored partial sight — but the ones from the
  blind period remained.
```

## Edgar Degas (1834–1917)

Degas was associated with Impressionism but differs from it in key ways: he mostly painted indoors, under artificial light; he was deeply interested in academic tradition (he copied Italian masters); his subjects were ballet, horse racing, café scenes, and bathing women.

### The Camera as Compositional Model

Degas was one of the first artists to seriously study photography (his friend Nadar was a pioneering photographer). His compositions reflect photographic accident:

```
  TRADITIONAL COMPOSITION: Balanced, centered, static.
  ─ Subject positioned in center/foreground.
  ─ No cropping; everything relevant visible.

  DEGAS' COMPOSITIONS: Cropped, asymmetric, off-center.
  ─ A ballerina is cut by the frame at the shoulder.
  ─ A key figure is in the margin; a casual figure fills center.
  ─ Space intrudes unexpectedly (negative space in foreground).

  THIS LOOKS LIKE PHOTOGRAPHY: A photograph "doesn't know"
  what's important — it captures everything in the frame
  without hierarchical weighting. Degas simulated this
  "accidental" vision in carefully constructed paintings.

  MOVEMENT:
  Degas was interested in Eadweard Muybridge's chronophotography
  (horses in motion, 1878). His paintings of horses and dancers
  capture specific arrested moments of motion — the transition
  between stable positions, not poses held for the painter.
```

## Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841–1919)

Renoir is the most sensuously pleasurable of the Impressionists — his paintings are warm, colorful, social, and overtly beautiful. His subject: leisure, pleasure, the bourgeois happiness of Sunday afternoons.

**Le Moulin de la Galette** (1876): An outdoor dance at a Montmartre café. Dappled sunlight through trees falls on dozens of figures. The figures are friends and neighbors — the painting depicts a specific social world, a specific class, a specific moment of Parisian life.

Renoir's technique: broken color applied rapidly; warm flesh tones with almost no shadow; backgrounds dematerialized in color and light.

## Mary Cassatt (1844–1926)

Cassatt is the only American artist fully integrated into the Impressionist circle. She was invited to exhibit with the Independents by Degas and became one of the most technically accomplished of the group.

Her subject: women in domestic life — nursing, bathing children, at the theater. She depicted women as active subjects, not passive objects:

```
  MODERN WOMAN (1893, for Chicago World's Fair, now lost):
  A major mural depicting modern women picking fruit — an allegory
  of women's productive agency, not domestic service.

  JAPANESE PRINT INFLUENCE:
  After seeing the Japanese woodblock print exhibition in Paris (1890),
  Cassatt made a series of colored aquatints directly influenced by
  Hiroshige and Utamaro: flat color, bold outline, unusual viewpoints.
  These are among the technically most innovative prints of the century.
```

## Camille Pissarro (1830–1903)

Pissarro is the organizing figure of Impressionism — he exhibited in all eight Impressionist shows (the only one to do so), was a mentor to Cézanne, Gauguin, and others, and is the most explicitly political (anarchist) of the group.

His subjects: rural French landscapes, market towns, the Seine — observed with Constable-like meteorological care but with looser, more broken brushwork.

## The Color Theory Foundation

Impressionism is not just about painting outdoors. It has a scientific theoretical basis:

```
  CHEVREUL (1839), "On the Law of the Simultaneous Contrast of Colors":
  ─ Colors influence each other when placed adjacent.
  ─ Complementary colors (red/green, blue/orange, yellow/violet)
    placed next to each other appear more intense.
  ─ A shadow cast on a red surface will appear to have a
    greenish cast (the eye supplies the complementary).

  HELMHOLTZ (1850s): Scientific optics of color perception.
  ─ Color is in the eye, not in the object.
  ─ The same surface appears different colors in different light.

  IMPRESSIONIST APPLICATION:
  ─ Shadows are not grey or black — they are colored
    (complementary to the illuminating light).
  ─ A yellow sunlit surface has violet-blue shadows.
  ─ Colors are mixed optically (on the retina) not physically
    (on the palette).
  ─ White is full light; use pure colors at full saturation.

  This is color theory as physics and perception science,
  applied as painting technique.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Impressionism is just quick sketchy painting** — Impressionism's visible brushwork is deliberate, not careless. Monet's Haystacks series was laboriously planned, re-executed, and finished in the studio. The "spontaneous" appearance is the result of systematic technical refinement.

**Manet = Monet** — These are different people. Monet: landscape, light, Giverny, water lilies. Manet: urban Paris, figures, scandal, Olympia. Manet was older (1832 vs. 1840), didn't exhibit in the Impressionist shows (he preferred the Salon), and was more concerned with art-historical dialogue than plein-air painting.

**Impressionism was immediately rejected** — The Impressionist group was commercially successful within about 15 years. By the 1880s, Renoir and Monet were selling well. By 1900, Impressionism was the mainstream. The "struggle against rejection" narrative is mostly about the period 1863–1875.

**They painted "what they saw"** — They painted what their theory of vision said they should see. The application of Chevreul's color theory meant painting shadows as violets and blues even when "looking" at the shadow would produce a grey perception. The theory shaped the seeing.
