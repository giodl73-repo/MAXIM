# Artists & Architects — Overview

## The Big Picture

The history of art and architecture is not primarily a history of taste — it is a history of technical innovation, intellectual programs, and responses to material and social conditions. Every major shift corresponds to something: a new engineering method, a new theory of vision, a political commission, a response to industrialization, a collapse of shared assumptions.

```
TIMELINE: MAJOR MOVEMENTS IN WESTERN ART AND ARCHITECTURE

  ANCIENT (3000 BCE – 400 CE)
  Egypt, Greece, Rome — civic/religious function; proportion as ideal

  MEDIEVAL (400 – 1400 CE)
  Byzantine, Romanesque, Gothic
  Architecture as theology; light as metaphysics; structure as symbol

  RENAISSANCE (1400 – 1600)
  Brunelleschi → Alberti → Leonardo → Michelangelo → Raphael
  Perspective, anatomy, humanism; art as intellectual discipline

  BAROQUE (1600 – 1750)
  Caravaggio → Rubens → Rembrandt → Vermeer → Velazquez
  Chiaroscuro; drama; direct emotional address; Protestant/Counter-Reformation

  18TH–19TH CENTURY
  Goya → David → Turner → Constable → Delacroix → Courbet
  From neoclassicism (reason, order) to Romanticism (feeling, sublime)
  to Realism (ordinary life, political art)

  IMPRESSIONISM (1860s–1880s)
  Monet → Renoir → Degas → Manet → Cassatt → Pissarro
  Optical truth over academic convention; plein-air; broken color

  POST-IMPRESSIONISM (1880s–1900s)
  Cezanne → Van Gogh → Gauguin → Seurat
  Each pushing in different directions: structure, expression, symbol, optics

  MODERN ART (1905–1945)
  Picasso → Matisse → Duchamp → Kandinsky → Mondrian
  Multiple simultaneous revolutions: Cubism, Fauvism, Dada,
  abstraction, conceptual art — all attacking the same assumptions

  CLASSICAL ARCHITECTURE
  Vitruvius → Palladio → Wren → Soane
  Orders, proportion, civic expression, theory of architecture

  MODERN ARCHITECTURE (1880s–1960s)
  Sullivan → Wright → Le Corbusier → Mies
  Form follows function; machine aesthetic; total design; less is more

  CONTEMPORARY (1960s–present)
  Postmodernism, deconstructivism, critical regionalism
  Gehry → Hadid → Piano → Foster → Koolhaas
  Warhol → Rothko → Basquiat → conceptual/installation art
```

## Two Traditions: Artists and Architects

Art and architecture are separate disciplines but deeply interwoven:

```
ARTIST                          ARCHITECT
────────                        ──────────
Primary medium: paint, stone,   Primary medium: building materials,
  metal, print, performance       space, structure
Product: portable object or     Product: built environment; permanent,
  fixed surface                   functional, public
Training: workshop/atelier      Training: mathematical, engineering
  apprenticeship (historically)   + design (historically)
Patronage: church, nobility,    Patronage: church, state, wealthy
  then market                     patrons, then corporate/public
Theory: beauty, expression,     Theory: proportion, function, structure,
  representation                  light, urban context

Many Renaissance figures worked in both:
  Brunelleschi: architect and sculptor
  Michelangelo: sculptor, painter, architect
  Leonardo: painter, architect, engineer, scientist
```

## What Makes Art/Architecture Historically Important

Not aesthetic preference but technical, intellectual, or cultural innovation:

```
  TECHNICAL INNOVATION: Brunelleschi's dome (structural engineering
  breakthrough); Vermeer's optical techniques; Pointillism's
  color theory; reinforced concrete in modern architecture.

  INTELLECTUAL PROGRAM: Alberti's codification of perspective;
  Le Corbusier's Modulor system; Mondrian's reduction to primary
  elements; Duchamp's concept that the artist decides what is art.

  PROBLEM ARTICULATION: Cezanne didn't just paint differently —
  he identified the problem that Cubism would solve.
  Manet didn't just shock — he revealed the conventions everyone
  else was following. The "scandal" is always that something
  implicit has been made explicit.

  HISTORICAL POSITIONING: Turner's skies anticipate Impressionism;
  Impressionism enables Post-Impressionism which enables Cubism
  which enables abstraction. The logic is not inevitable but
  each step creates the problem the next step addresses.
```

| Term | Domain | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Chiaroscuro** | Painting | Dramatic contrast of light and dark |
| **Sfumato** | Painting | Leonardo's soft-edge technique; tonal blending |
| **Tenebrism** | Painting | Extreme Caravaggio-style light from darkness |
| **Impasto** | Painting | Thick paint applied with palette knife; Van Gogh |
| **Plein-air** | Impressionism | Painting outdoors, from observation |
| **Divisionism/Pointillism** | Post-Imp. | Color laid as separate dots; optical mixing |
| **Classical orders** | Architecture | Doric, Ionic, Corinthian, Tuscan, Composite |
| **Entasis** | Architecture | Slight convex curve in column to correct optical illusion |
| **Rustication** | Architecture | Rough-textured stonework at building base |
| **Fenestration** | Architecture | Pattern and arrangement of windows |
| **Cantilever** | Structure | Structural element extending beyond its support |
| **Curtain wall** | Architecture | Non-load-bearing exterior wall (glass in modern arch.) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Art history is about style periods** — Style labels (Baroque, Impressionism) are convenient fictions that obscure more than they reveal. Caravaggio and Vermeer are both "Baroque" but have almost nothing in common technically or temperamentally. The labels are teaching tools, not natural kinds.

**Architecture is applied engineering** — Architecture is engineering plus meaning. The Parthenon could have been built more cheaply with less precision; the entasis, the slight inward tilt of the columns, the optical corrections are not structural requirements — they are philosophical commitments about how a building should be perceived. Modern architecture debates whether a building that works functionally but doesn't mean anything has succeeded.

**"Abstract" art abandoned skill** — Abstract painting typically required more deliberate theoretical commitment than representational art. Mondrian spent years reducing his painting systematically. Rothko spent decades understanding how color fields function psychologically. The skill required is different from classical academic training, not absent.

**Modern architecture is ugly because it's cheap** — Some modern architecture is cheap and ugly. But Le Corbusier, Mies, and Wright working at full intention produced buildings of remarkable beauty on their own terms. The problem is that their vocabulary — flat roof, curtain wall, open plan — was copied without understanding and built cheaply. The same thing happened with the Classical orders: copied without understanding produces kitsch, not classicism.
