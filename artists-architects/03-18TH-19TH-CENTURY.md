# 18th and 19th Century — Goya, David, Turner, Constable, Delacroix, Courbet

## The Political Context

Art in this period is inseparable from the convulsive political history:

```
MOVEMENTS AND THEIR HISTORICAL MOMENTS

  NEOCLASSICISM (1760–1820)
  ─ Reaction against Baroque excess and Rococo frivolity.
  ─ Ancient Rome and Greece as models for civic virtue.
  ─ Directly tied to Enlightenment rationalism and revolutionary politics.
  ─ Jacques-Louis David: official painter of the French Revolution and Napoleon.

  ROMANTICISM (1780–1850)
  ─ Reaction against Enlightenment rationalism and industrialization.
  ─ Sublime nature, individual feeling, imagination, exotic cultures.
  ─ Goya (Spain), Turner (England), Delacroix (France),
    Friedrich (Germany), Cole (America).
  ─ In parallel: the Gothic novel, Beethoven, Byron.

  REALISM (1840–1880)
  ─ Reaction against Romantic idealization.
  ─ Ordinary working people as worthy subjects; depicting poverty,
    labor, and social conditions without idealization.
  ─ Courbet (France): the defining figure.
  ─ Directly tied to 1848 revolutions and socialist politics.
  ─ Technical predecessor to Impressionism.
```

## Francisco Goya (1746–1828)

Goya spans Neoclassicism and Romanticism and transcends both. He is simultaneously the last court painter of the Spanish monarchy and the first modern artist — someone who painted private nightmares with no patron to please.

### Court Painter and Witness

Goya was appointed First Court Painter to Charles IV in 1799. His royal portraits are remarkable for their unflattering precision:

```
  CHARLES IV AND HIS FAMILY (1800):
  ─ The royal family is arranged in a formal group portrait.
  ─ No idealization: the king looks fat and weak; the queen
    (Maria Luisa, who actually ran the court) dominates;
    the faces are ordinary, even dim.
  ─ Goya includes himself, like Velazquez in Las Meninas,
    at his canvas in the left background.

  WHAT MAKES IT RADICAL:
  Any court painter who painted the king as Goya painted Charles IV
  would normally have been dismissed. Why wasn't Goya?
  Two theories: (1) the royal family liked it (they saw what they wanted),
  (2) the court recognized excellence even when unflattering.
  Neither is fully satisfying. It remains a mystery.
```

### Disasters of War

The Peninsular War (Napoleon's invasion of Spain, 1808–1814) produced Goya's most important work. The Disasters of War is a series of 82 etchings documenting the brutality of the French occupation and Spanish resistance — and the atrocities committed by both sides.

```
  WHAT DISTINGUISHES THE DISASTERS OF WAR:

  Earlier war art: heroism, cavalry charges, noble deaths.
  Goya: rape, execution, dismemberment, starvation, corpses.
  No heroism. No glory. Just atrocity.

  The Third of May, 1808 (painted 1814):
  ─ The Napoleonic execution of Madrid resisters.
  ─ The central figure: a man in white, arms thrown wide
    (Christ-like composition), facing a firing squad.
  ─ The soldiers are identical, faceless, mechanical.
  ─ The victims are faces, individuals, terrified.
  ─ This is the inverse of all previous war painting.

  THIS INVERTS THE TRADITION:
  Goya doesn't show the heroic moment before death —
  he shows the seconds of waiting, the terror, the waste.
  Manet's Execution of Maximilian (1867) directly quotes it.
  Picasso's Guernica (1937) is its 20th-century equivalent.
```

### The Black Paintings (1820–1823)

At 74, deaf, increasingly disturbed, Goya painted 14 murals directly on the walls of his house (La Quinta del Sordo — the "House of the Deaf Man"). No patron. No commission. Never intended for public display.

The Black Paintings include Saturn Devouring His Son — a gigantic screaming figure eating a human body — and the Witches' Sabbath, the Pilgrimage of San Isidro, and others. They were transferred to canvas after his death.

```
  WHY THEY MATTER:
  ─ First major works of art explicitly NOT made for an audience.
  ─ Personal psychological expression with no decorative purpose.
  ─ Anticipate Expressionism (Munch, Ensor) by 80 years.
  ─ The direct ancestor of the 20th-century idea of art as
    private psychological statement.
```

## Jacques-Louis David (1748–1825)

David is the paradigm case of the politically engaged artist — his painting changed as his politics changed, from revolutionary Jacobin to Napoleonic propagandist to exiled royalist.

### The Neoclassical Program

```
  OATH OF THE HORATII (1784):

  Roman story: three brothers swear to fight for Rome.
  Painted the year before the Revolution.
  Exhibited at the Salon to enormous acclaim.

  THE VISUAL SYSTEM:
  ─ Strict geometry: three arches in background; three groups
    of figures; the swords at the exact center.
  ─ Figures are sculptural — hard edges, smooth surfaces,
    no atmospheric blurring.
  ─ Men: upright, active, directed outward (toward duty).
  ─ Women: collapsed, emotionally devastated, directed inward.
  ─ The contrast is gendered and political: Roman civic virtue
    vs. private feeling.

  WHAT IT SAID IN 1784:
  Civic duty over private happiness. Sacrifice for the republic.
  This read as anti-monarchist, pro-republican in 1784 France.
  David probably intended it; the court bought it anyway.
```

### Revolutionary and Imperial Service

David became the visual director of the French Revolution — organizing revolutionary festivals, designing republican iconography, painting The Death of Marat (1793):

```
  DEATH OF MARAT (1793):

  Marat (Jacobin journalist and politician) was murdered in his
  medicinal bath by Charlotte Corday.

  David's painting:
  ─ Marat is depicted as a secular pietà — the composition
    echoes Christ's deposition.
  ─ The bath, the green cloth, the letter are rendered with
    still-life precision.
  ─ The wounds are minimal; the face is peaceful.
  ─ The inscription: "To Marat, David."

  This is political propaganda of the highest artistic order.
  It transforms a murdered politician into a secular martyr
  of the Republic.
```

After Robespierre's fall, David was imprisoned. Under Napoleon, he returned as official court painter: Napoleon Crossing the Alps (1801), the Coronation of Napoleon (1807). After Napoleon's defeat, he fled to Brussels and died in exile.

## J.M.W. Turner (1775–1851)

Turner is the most technically radical British painter and the direct predecessor of Impressionism. By his late period, he was painting effects of light and atmosphere so dissolved that Constable, seeing one of Turner's exhibited works, said "He seems to paint with tinted steam."

### The Sublime

The Romantic concept of the sublime (Burke, Kant): the experience of overwhelming natural force — storms, vast mountains, the open sea — that exceeds our capacity to comprehend or represent it, producing a mixture of terror and exaltation.

Turner's career tracks the progressive dissolution of representational content in favor of this experience:

```
  EARLY TURNER (1800s): Traditional landscape with Claude-inspired
  composition. Careful topography. Shows he could do it.

  MIDDLE TURNER (1810s–20s): Dramatic weather, historical subjects.
  The Fighting Temeraire (1839): a great warship towed to the breaker's
  yard by a steam tug. Turner's typical sunset — orange, gold, haze.
  The sublime as elegy.

  LATE TURNER (1840s): Snow Storm: Steam-Boat off a Harbour's Mouth
  (1842). Turner claimed to have been lashed to a mast to experience
  the storm directly. The painting is a vortex of white, grey, and
  dark. No stable horizon. No clear subject. The storm IS the painting.

  RAIN, STEAM AND SPEED (1844): A locomotive crossing a bridge in rain.
  The subject is legible but barely — the modernity (steam technology)
  dissolves into the same atmospheric vortex as the sailing ship.
```

Turner is the first painter to make atmospheric dissolution itself the subject — not what is seen, but how it is seen through disrupted conditions. Impressionism begins here.

## John Constable (1776–1837)

Where Turner pursued the sublime, Constable pursued truth to ordinary English nature — the Stour Valley where he grew up, specific weather conditions, actual sky at a specific moment.

```
  CONSTABLE'S INNOVATIONS:

  CLOUD STUDIES:
  ─ Constable made systematic studies of cloud formations,
    noting the date, time, direction of wind.
  ─ "The sky is the chief organ of sentiment" in landscape.
  ─ Before Constable: skies were conventional blue gradients.
  ─ After: skies have actual meteorological conditions.

  BROKEN COLOR:
  ─ Constable's large exhibition paintings were made with
    "Constable's snow" — small dabs of white and pure color
    mixed optically rather than blended on the palette.
  ─ Delacroix saw the Hay Wain (1821) in Paris, repainted
    his Massacre at Chios to use this technique.

  PLEIN-AIR PRACTICE:
  ─ Constable made extensive oil sketches outdoors.
  ─ These spontaneous studies were often more vibrant than
    his finished exhibition pieces.
  ─ The Barbizon School in France (Corot, Daubigny) adopted
    this practice → Impressionism adopts it permanently.
```

## Eugène Delacroix (1798–1863)

Delacroix is the champion of Romanticism in French painting — in direct opposition to Ingres (David's most distinguished pupil), who championed Neoclassical line and contour.

### Color vs. Line

The battle between Colorists (Rubens, Delacroix) and Draughtsmen (Poussin, David, Ingres) is one of the structuring debates of French art:

```
  INGRES (DRAUGHTSMEN TRADITION):
  ─ Drawing is the foundation of all art.
  ─ Form must be established through precise contour.
  ─ Color is secondary — it fills in established forms.
  ─ The ideal: classical Greek sculpture.

  DELACROIX (COLORIST TRADITION):
  ─ Color is the primary vehicle of expression.
  ─ Rubens and Venetian painters (Titian) as models.
  ─ Form emerges from colored light, not from contour.
  ─ The painterly surface itself expresses emotion.

  THE LIBERTY LEADING THE PEOPLE (1830):
  ─ Celebrates the July Revolution of 1830.
  ─ Liberty as a real woman (and allegorical figure)
    leading the barricades.
  ─ Compositional triangle; violent diagonal energy.
  ─ The French flag as compositional spine.
  ─ Combines allegorical and documentary realism.
  ─ Has been the most politically reproduced painting in history.
```

## Gustave Courbet (1819–1877)

Courbet is the founding figure of Realism — a political as well as aesthetic position.

```
  REALISM AS PROGRAM:

  "I have never seen an angel. Show me an angel and I will
   paint one." — Courbet

  WHAT HE REJECTED:
  ─ Neoclassicism: history painting, noble subjects, Greek heroes.
  ─ Romanticism: exotic subjects, emotional extremes, the sublime.

  WHAT HE PROPOSED:
  ─ Paint what exists, as it exists, on the scale it warrants.
  ─ Ordinary people, ordinary landscapes, ordinary labor —
    treated with the scale and seriousness previously reserved
    for historical and religious subjects.

  A BURIAL AT ORNANS (1849–1850):
  ─ 21.9 × 10.3 feet — monumental scale for a provincial funeral.
  ─ No heroes. The mourners are specific individuals (Courbet's
    neighbors and family from Ornans).
  ─ No sentiment: faces are blank, individual, unheroic.
  ─ The effect was shocking — this scale demanded importance;
    the subject refused to deliver it.

  THE POLITICAL CLAIM:
  Courbet was a socialist. Painting ordinary people at historical
  scale was a political statement: they matter as much as history's
  heroes and saints. The scale is the argument.
```

Courbet participated in the Paris Commune (1871) and was held responsible for the demolition of the Vendôme Column. He fled to Switzerland and died in exile — a radical to the end.

## Common Confusion Points

**Neoclassicism = conservative** — Not straightforwardly. David was a radical Jacobin; the Oath of the Horatii was read as a call to republican revolution. "Classical" form was co-opted for revolutionary politics before it became Napoleon's imperial style.

**Turner is just about beautiful fog** — Turner's "fog" is technically precise: he understood atmospheric perspective (Leonardo), the physics of light scattering, and the difference between fog, steam, rain, and haze. Rain, Steam and Speed depicts specific modern phenomena — railroad technology in weather — not just prettiness.

**Goya's Black Paintings were finished works** — They were murals painted on the plaster walls of his private dining room and living room. They were never shown publicly in his lifetime. The degree to which they were "finished" is unknown — we have them only as transferred canvas copies.

**Courbet's realism = photography** — Courbet insisted on the painter's material relationship to the world. He said paint (specifically, palette-knife painting building up surface texture) should look like paint. His surfaces are thick, physical, material. This is the opposite of photography's pretension to transparency. Realism as Courbet practiced it was specifically about the materiality of paint, not its invisibility.
