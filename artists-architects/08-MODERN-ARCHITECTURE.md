# Modern Architecture — Sullivan, Wright, Le Corbusier, Mies van der Rohe

## The Break with Classicism

Modern architecture emerged from a specific historical problem: industrialization produced new building types (factories, railway stations, office towers, apartment blocks) and new materials (cast iron, steel, reinforced concrete, plate glass) that had no classical precedent.

```
THE CRISIS (1880–1920):

  NEW PROGRAMS:         NEW MATERIALS:
  ─ Skyscraper          ─ Structural steel (Bessemer, 1856)
  ─ Factory             ─ Reinforced concrete (Monier patents, 1867)
  ─ Railway station     ─ Plate glass (industrial, 1870s)
  ─ Department store    ─ Elevators (Otis, 1852)

  THE WRONG ANSWER: Put classical facades on everything.
  The Beaux-Arts response: the Flatiron Building has a
  cornice (as if there were a classical building behind it).
  The Pennsylvania Station is a Roman bath house wrapped
  around a train shed.

  THE RIGHT QUESTION (Sullivan, Wright, Corbusier, Mies asked):
  What does steel mean architecturally?
  What does a building that is primarily glass look like?
  What is the honest expression of these new materials and programs?
```

## Louis Sullivan (1856–1924)

Sullivan is the father of American modernism and the inventor of the skyscraper as an architectural type.

### "Form Ever Follows Function"

Sullivan's phrase (from "The Tall Office Building Artistically Considered," 1896) is the most quoted in architecture:

```
  THE FULL QUOTE (usually truncated):
  "It is the pervading law of all things organic and inorganic,
  of all things physical and metaphysical, of all things human
  and all things superhuman, of all things true and false, that
  the life is recognizable in its expression, that form ever
  follows function. This is the law."

  WHAT HE MEANT:
  Not: form should be stripped of ornament (he was extremely ornate).
  Not: function determines appearance mechanically.
  Rather: a building's external form should express what it is —
  just as a tree's form expresses its life, its structural
  organization, and its relation to light and gravity.

  A skyscraper should LOOK like a skyscraper.
  It should express:
  ─ Its verticality (it is tall by its nature)
  ─ Its structure (the skeleton is its life)
  ─ Its cellular repetition (floors of offices stacked)
  ─ Its base (different from office floors — entrances, shops)
  ─ Its top (different from both — mechanical, capping)
```

### The Skyscraper Solution

Sullivan's tripartite division for the tall office building:

```
  ┌─────────────────────────┐
  │         ATTIC            │ ← Capping: different from shaft;
  │   (different character)  │   ornamental expression of termination
  ├─────────────────────────┤
  │                          │
  │      OFFICE FLOORS       │ ← Shaft: repetitive cells; express
  │   (identical repetition) │   the stacking of equal floors
  │                          │   through identical treatment
  │                          │
  ├─────────────────────────┤
  │      GROUND FLOOR        │ ← Base: entrance, lobby, street
  │   (different character)  │   relationship — different program,
  └─────────────────────────┘   different architectural treatment

  Sullivan's Wainwright Building (1891, St. Louis):
  This tripartite logic realized — the prototypical skyscraper type.
```

## Frank Lloyd Wright (1867–1959)

Wright is the greatest American architect and the most idiosyncratic. He worked from the 1880s to his death in 1959 — an 70-year career that produced about 1000 designs (500 built). No two buildings are alike.

### Organic Architecture

Wright's "organic architecture" is not decorative naturalism — it is a set of structural principles:

```
  PRINCIPLES OF ORGANIC ARCHITECTURE (Wright):

  1. DESTRUCTION OF THE BOX:
     Traditional room = enclosed box with apertures.
     Wright opens the box: walls extend past corners; spaces
     flow into each other; the interior connects to the exterior.
     "I was trying to kill the box and let the imprisoned
     air out into freedom."

  2. SPACE AS MATERIAL:
     "Space is the breath of art." The room is not defined
     by its walls — it is the space itself that is designed.
     The architect manipulates movement through space,
     compression and release, light and shadow.

  3. HORIZONTAL EXTENSION:
     Wright's Prairie Houses (1900–1910) cling to the ground —
     horizontal, low, sheltered under broad overhanging eaves.
     This responds to the flat Midwestern landscape.

  4. MATERIALS TRUTH:
     Wood should look like wood; stone should look like stone;
     brick should look like brick. No paint, no plaster covering.
     Materials are used to express their own character.

  5. INTEGRATION:
     Every element — furniture, windows, lighting, landscaping —
     designed as part of the whole. Early clients found it difficult
     to rearrange their furniture; Wright had designed the rooms
     for specific furniture in specific positions.
```

### Fallingwater (1935)

```
  SITE: A waterfall on Bear Run Creek, western Pennsylvania.
  PROGRAM: Weekend house for Edgar Kaufmann.

  STRUCTURAL SYSTEM:
  ─ Reinforced concrete cantilevers extend over the waterfall.
  ─ The house is not beside the waterfall — it is above it.
  ─ The falls can be heard from every room; a stairway descends
    directly into the stream.
  ─ The structural engineer (Mendel Glickman) calculated that
    the cantilevers as Wright designed them would fail.
    Wright overruled him. The cantilevers did sag significantly
    (10 inches deflection). Reinforcement was added.

  COMPOSITIONAL PRINCIPLE:
  ─ Horizontal planes (cantilevered floors and trays) float
    over vertical stone piers (quarried on site, laid irregularly).
  ─ Horizontal vs. vertical; man-made vs. natural; floating
    vs. anchored. The tension between these is the design.

  This is the best-known private house in the world.
  It was named the "best work of American architecture of
  all time" by the AIA in 1991.
```

### Usonian Houses

Wright believed good architecture should be available to the middle class, not just the wealthy:

```
  USONIAN PRINCIPLES (1930s–1950s):
  ─ One story (no basement, no attic).
  ─ Carport instead of garage.
  ─ Radiant floor heating (pipes in the concrete slab).
  ─ Natural materials (brick, wood, stone).
  ─ Built-in furniture, eliminating conventional rooms' clutter.
  ─ Cost: originally ~$5,000–15,000 (middle-class affordable, 1940s).

  About 100 Usonian houses were built.
  The open plan, the carport, the radiant heating:
  all became standard American residential features.
```

## Le Corbusier (1887–1965)

Le Corbusier (Charles-Édouard Jeanneret-Gris) is the most influential architect of the 20th century and the most controversial. His ideas shaped cities worldwide, often catastrophically.

### Five Points of a New Architecture (1927)

```
  1. PILOTIS (stilts): Raise the building off the ground on columns.
     ─ Free the ground plane (garden flows under the building).
     ─ Structural columns are separated from partition walls.

  2. FREE FAÇADE: The exterior wall is no longer load-bearing.
     ─ The structural skeleton (columns) is inside.
     ─ The facade can be arranged freely — large windows,
       bands of glass, any configuration.

  3. FREE PLAN: Interior walls are not load-bearing.
     ─ Can be placed anywhere.
     ─ The floor plan of each level is independent.

  4. RIBBON WINDOWS: Horizontal bands of glass the full width.
     ─ Possible because the facade is free.
     ─ Equal light distribution across the space.

  5. ROOF GARDEN: The flat roof becomes usable outdoor space.
     ─ Compensates for the ground taken by the building.
     ─ Insulates the top floor; provides outdoor living.

  Villa Savoye (1929–31) is the canonical realization of all five.
```

### The Machine Aesthetic

```
  "A house is a machine for living in."

  This does NOT mean houses should be industrial or cold.
  Le Corbusier meant: machines are beautiful when perfectly
  designed for their function (he wrote rapturously about
  ocean liners, airplanes, and cars).
  A house should be as precisely designed for living as a
  Hispano-Suiza car is designed for driving.

  THE PURISM:
  Le Corbusier and Ozenfant (Purism, 1918):
  ─ Art should use "pure" forms — forms that have evolved
    to their most efficient and universal expression.
  ─ The objects of industrial design (bottles, glasses, plates)
    achieve a kind of classical perfection through functional selection.
  ─ Architecture should do the same.
```

### The Modulor

```
  THE MODULOR (1945):
  Le Corbusier's proportional system based on the human body.

  Starting from a 6-foot (183cm) standing man:
  ─ Navel: 113 cm
  ─ Top of head: 183 cm
  ─ Raised arm: 226 cm
  ─ These establish two Fibonacci series:
    Red series (base 113): 113, 70, 43, 27...
    Blue series (base 226): 226, 140, 86, 53...

  The two series relate by the Golden Section (φ = 1.618).

  Le Corbusier applied these dimensions to everything: room
  heights, window sizes, facade proportions, furniture.

  THIS IS APPLIED MATHEMATICS:
  The Modulor is essentially a Fibonacci-based proportional
  system derived from human scale. Whether or not it succeeds
  as a universal system (critics dispute this), the project —
  finding a mathematical order that unifies human scale with
  architectural proportion — is the same project as Vitruvius
  and Palladio, expressed in 20th-century mathematical terms.
```

### Urban Projects — The Problem

Le Corbusier's urban plans proposed demolishing large areas of historic cities and replacing them with widely spaced towers in parks:

```
  PLAN VOISIN (1925): Proposed demolishing most of central Paris
  north of the Seine (except the historic monuments) and replacing
  it with 18 identical cruciform glass towers in a park.

  THE REASONING: The medieval/Haussmann city is unhealthy
  (poor light, poor air, traffic chaos). Towers in park provide
  sun, fresh air, green space for all. The street is for cars, not
  pedestrians.

  THE PROBLEM: This logic was applied (without Le Corbusier directly)
  to postwar public housing projects worldwide — Pruitt-Igoe (St. Louis),
  Cabrini-Green (Chicago), Bijlmermeer (Amsterdam).
  These were social disasters.

  WHY: Le Corbusier's theory eliminated the street as social
  infrastructure. Streets are not just traffic channels — they
  are economic, social, and surveillance systems. Without them,
  the towers became isolated and ungovernable.
  Jane Jacobs demolished this theory in The Death and Life of
  Great American Cities (1961).
```

## Mies van der Rohe (1886–1969)

Mies is the most influential architect of the mid-20th century. His two phrases — "Less is more" and "God is in the details" — define the aesthetic of corporate modernism.

### German Pavilion, Barcelona (1929)

```
  THE BARCELONA PAVILION:
  Built for the 1929 International Exposition; demolished 1930;
  reconstructed 1986 from original drawings.

  WHAT IT IS: Not a building in any normal sense.
  No program, no required function. A structure for the King
  of Spain to sign papers during the Exposition opening.

  WHAT IT DOES:
  ─ Eight cruciform steel columns support a flat roof slab.
  ─ Wall planes of marble, glass, and stone slide past each
    other at right angles — but they don't touch the columns.
  ─ The structure and the space-defining walls are separated.
  ─ You can't tell where outside stops and inside begins.
  ─ The horizontal water in two pools (one interior, one
    exterior) ties everything to a single horizontal datum.

  THIS IS THE UNIVERSAL SPACE:
  Space flows continuously, defined by independent planes,
  not enclosed by walls. This will become the model for:
  ─ The open-plan office
  ─ The open-plan house
  ─ The museum as flexible white cube
  ─ The glass corporate tower lobby
```

### Farnsworth House (1951)

```
  A SINGLE-ROOM WEEKEND HOUSE:
  ─ 8 white steel columns support two horizontal planes
    (floor and roof).
  ─ All walls are glass.
  ─ The entire house is enclosed by a perfect glass rectangle.
  ─ The structure is expressed with absolute precision and purity.

  TECHNICALLY:
  All steel members are exactly the same dimension.
  The floor plate is elevated above the flood plain on the columns.
  The glass is full-height and full-width on every face.
  The only opaque elements: the central utility core (bathrooms,
  kitchen, mechanical).

  THE PROBLEM:
  The client (Dr. Edith Farnsworth) sued Mies over cost overruns.
  She found the house difficult to live in — impossible to clean
  (every mark showed on glass and steel), no privacy, overheating
  in summer.

  "Less is more" is an aesthetic principle, not a prescription
  for comfortable living. The tension between architectural
  concept and lived experience is the Mies problem.
```

### The Glass Tower — Seagram Building (1958)

```
  WITH PHILIP JOHNSON:
  ─ A bronze and dark glass tower set back from Park Avenue
    on a raised plaza.
  ─ The bronze I-beam mullions on the facade are not structural
    (the structure is internal concrete core) — they are applied
    to express structure visually (solve the problem that actual
    steel structure is encased in fireproofing).
  ─ The plaza is an urban gift — the first major privately owned
    public space in New York.

  "I BEAMS" ON THE FACADE — APPLIED ORNAMENT?
  Mies said no. He argued that expressing structure, even with
  non-structural members, is more honest than hiding it.
  The applied mullion communicates the structural logic of the
  building even though it's not the actual structure.
  Critics called this contradiction; admirers called it expression.

  THE CORPORATE GLASS TOWER:
  The Seagram Building was the standard for corporate architecture
  for 30 years. Every glass-box office tower from 1960–1990 traces
  back to it — usually as a cost-cut copy without the proportional
  care.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Form follows function" = no ornament** — Sullivan's buildings have profuse ornament (art nouveau-influenced vine and flower patterns on the surfaces). His argument is that the overall form of the building should express its function — not that decoration should be eliminated. Ornament should grow organically from the building's nature, not be applied from pattern books.

**Le Corbusier ruined cities** — Le Corbusier didn't implement Plan Voisin. The "towers in park" housing disasters were built by municipal housing authorities following a bastardized reading of Corbusian principles — eliminating the elements that cost money (diversity of uses, community facilities, the street grid) while keeping only the formal element (the tower). Le Corbusier's unrealized Chandigarh and his Unité d'Habitation (Marseille) work more successfully than the American public housing projects.

**Mies' buildings are cold and inhuman** — This is a legitimate criticism of many buildings inspired by Mies. The original buildings (Barcelona Pavilion, Farnsworth House, Seagram Building) are exercises in restraint that achieve a specific kind of beauty through precision. The copies are often cold because they lack the original's proportional care, material quality, and structural honesty.

**Wright was difficult to work with** — Yes. He was egocentric, frequently dishonest about costs, had affairs with clients' wives, and left multiple wives and families. Taliesin (his studio commune) had cult-like characteristics. His architecture is outstanding; his character was not. These are separable.
