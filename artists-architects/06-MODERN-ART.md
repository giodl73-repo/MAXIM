# Modern Art — Picasso, Matisse, Duchamp, Kandinsky, Mondrian

## The Modern Art Revolution (1905–1940)

Modern art was not a single movement but a series of overlapping revolutions that dismantled every assumption of Western painting:

```
  WHAT WAS DISMANTLED:

  ─ THE PICTURE AS WINDOW: Painting represents a scene.
    Cezanne began it; Cubism, abstraction, and Dada finished it.

  ─ COLOR AS DESCRIPTION: Color tells you what color things are.
    Fauvism (Matisse) made color autonomous — carrier of pure emotion.

  ─ SUBJECT MATTER AS GIVEN: Art depicts recognizable things.
    Kandinsky and Mondrian showed painting without subjects.

  ─ CRAFT AS STANDARD: Skill in representation is the measure.
    Duchamp showed that the artist's idea, not their hand, defines art.

  ─ ART AS UNIQUE OBJECT: The painting is a unique, irreproducible thing.
    Dada and later Conceptual art challenged this.

  TIMELINE:
  1905 — Fauvism (Matisse, Derain): Salon d'Automne
  1907 — Proto-Cubism: Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (Picasso)
  1908–12 — Analytic Cubism (Picasso, Braque)
  1910 — First abstract watercolor (Kandinsky, disputed)
  1913 — Armory Show (NY): Modern art arrives in America
  1913 — Duchamp's first readymade (Bicycle Wheel)
  1913 — Mondrian begins abstract development
  1916 — Dada founded in Zurich (Cabaret Voltaire)
  1917 — Fountain (Duchamp) — the readymade as art object
  1920 — De Stijl movement (Mondrian, van Doesburg)
  1936 — Abstract Expressionism beginning to form
```

## Pablo Picasso (1881–1973)

Picasso is simultaneously the most famous artist of the 20th century and the most technically transformed. His career spans from academic realism at age 12 to Cubism to classicism to Surrealism to late expressionism — he never settled.

### Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907)

```
  WHAT IT IS: Five nude women in a brothel setting.

  WHY IT MATTERS:
  ─ Started under Cézanne's influence (multiple viewpoints,
    planar faceting).
  ─ Transformed during painting by African and Iberian masks.
  ─ The result is formally fractured: the left two figures look
    like Iberian sculpture; the right two have African mask-like faces.
  ─ No single viewpoint; no unified space; no classical beauty.

  THE DISRUPTION:
  This painting breaks the convention that the nude in painting
  is an aesthetic object, harmoniously beautiful, arranged for
  the viewer's pleasure. These figures are aggressive,
  confrontational, formally violent. They look at you.

  Braque, who Picasso showed it to privately, reportedly said
  it was like "drinking petrol and spitting fire."
  Even the Modernist avant-garde was shocked.

  FROM HERE TO CUBISM: Picasso kept working on the problem
  Les Demoiselles opened — how to represent a body that exists
  in three dimensions on a flat surface without the conventions
  of perspective that had been the answer since 1420.
```

### Analytic Cubism (1908–1912)

Working with Georges Braque in such close collaboration that their paintings from this period are often indistinguishable:

```
  PROBLEM: How to paint a three-dimensional object honestly
  on a two-dimensional surface?

  PERSPECTIVE (traditional answer): project from a single
  viewpoint. But this gives you only one aspect of a thing —
  the accident of where you stood.

  CUBIST ANSWER: Show multiple aspects simultaneously.
  Walk around the object; analyze its structure from all sides;
  reassemble the facets on the canvas.

  THE LOOK:
  ─ Monochromatic (brown, grey, ochre) — removes the distraction
    of color to focus on structure.
  ─ Fragmented planes of the same object overlap and interpenetrate.
  ─ The viewer reconstructs the object from the fragments.
  ─ The space is shallow — objects can't recede into depth,
    they are held near the surface.

  WHAT IT CORRESPONDS TO:
  ─ Einstein's special relativity (1905): no privileged
    frame of reference; observation depends on the observer's
    position and motion. The art world liked this parallel.
    It's too simple, but it captures something real: both
    challenge the single privileged viewpoint.
```

### Guernica (1937)

Picasso's response to the Nazi bombing of the Basque town of Guernica (April 26, 1937):

```
  FORMAL CHOICES:
  ─ Monochromatic: black, white, grey — like newspaper photographs,
    like death, like moral clarity.
  ─ Cubist fragmentation: the violence fragments the bodies.
  ─ 11.5 × 25.6 feet — large enough that you are inside it.
  ─ No swastikas, no German uniforms — not documentary but
    mythological: the bull (Spain?), the horse (the people?),
    the screaming woman, the dead baby, the broken soldier.

  IMPACT: Became the visual symbol of civilian suffering in
  modern warfare. Was kept in New York (at Picasso's request)
  during the Franco dictatorship; returned to Spain in 1981
  after democracy was restored.
```

## Henri Matisse (1869–1954)

Matisse is Picasso's great contemporary rival — the two defined the poles of 20th-century painting. Where Picasso fragmented and analyzed, Matisse harmonized and simplified.

### Fauvism (1905–1907)

At the 1905 Salon d'Automne, Matisse and his circle (Derain, Vlaminck) exhibited paintings of wild color. A critic called them "les fauves" (wild beasts) and the name stuck.

```
  FAUVIST PRINCIPLE:
  Color is autonomous — it carries emotional and expressive
  force independent of description.

  Woman with a Hat (1905):
  ─ His wife's face: green stripe down the nose, violet forehead,
    orange jaw.
  ─ These are not the colors of a human face. They are the colors
    that Matisse felt expressed the presence and vitality of the figure.
  ─ A collector (Leo Stein) bought it with deep reluctance; a young man
    at the opening called it the ugliest painting he'd ever seen.
    The young man was Ernest Hemingway.
```

### The Great Decoration

Matisse spent his career seeking a kind of art that was at once decorative and profound:

```
  "I want my art to be an art of balance, of purity and
  serenity devoid of troubling or depressing subject matter...
  a soothing, calming influence on the mind."

  THE DANCE (1909–1910, for Shchukin):
  ─ Five figures in a circle, dancing on a vivid blue-green ground.
  ─ Pure, flat color (no shading): intense pink-red figures,
    vivid green ground, cerulean blue sky.
  ─ The forms simplified to the essential movement.

  ODALISQUES (1920s):
  ─ Women in patterned interiors — elaborate rugs, fabrics, screens.
  ─ Pattern on pattern; figure becomes pattern becomes background.
  ─ The flatness of the space is deliberate — everything on
    the same visual plane, undifferentiated by depth.
```

### The Cut-Outs

In his 80s, unable to paint, Matisse began cutting painted paper into shapes and assembling them:

```
  JAZZ (1947): A book of cut-out plates with Matisse's written
  commentary — described as "crystallized memories" of circus,
  folk tales, travel.

  THE PINK NUDE (1935), THE SNAIL (1953):
  ─ Pure form reduced to its essential action.
  ─ The snail is not a representation — it is an evocation.
  ─ Color as shape, shape as color.

  This becomes the reference point for later Color Field painting
  (Ellsworth Kelly, etc.) and abstract printmaking.
```

## Marcel Duchamp (1887–1968)

Duchamp is the most conceptually consequential artist of the 20th century. He produced relatively few works but changed the definition of art itself.

### The Readymades

```
  BICYCLE WHEEL (1913): A bicycle wheel mounted on a stool.
  IN ADVANCE OF THE BROKEN ARM (1915): A snow shovel.
  BOTTLE RACK (1914): A commercial iron bottle rack.
  FOUNTAIN (1917): A urinal, submitted to the Society of
  Independent Artists exhibition, signed "R. Mutt."

  THE QUESTION DUCHAMP FORCED:
  The Society of Independent Artists had no jury — anyone who
  paid the entry fee could exhibit.
  Duchamp submitted Fountain under a pseudonym.
  It was rejected (the committee said it wasn't art).

  Duchamp (secretly on the organizing committee) resigned.
  He argued: if any member can exhibit, and R. Mutt paid the
  fee, then the rejection was based not on rules but on the
  committee's decision that this is not art.
  That decision is the aesthetic judgment. The urinal itself
  is irrelevant.

  WHAT THIS ESTABLISHED:
  ─ Art is whatever an artist says is art (within a context).
  ─ The institution (gallery, museum) that frames the object
    determines whether it is art.
  ─ The artist's concept, not their craft, defines the work.
  ─ The "original" is meaningless — any urinal of the same model
    is as valid as Duchamp's.

  This is Duchamp's program: "I wanted to put painting once again
  at the service of the mind." Retinal art (pleasing the eye)
  was exhausted; conceptual art was the future.
```

## Wassily Kandinsky (1866–1944)

Kandinsky is generally credited with the first intentionally abstract paintings.

```
  THE ORIGIN STORY (disputed):
  Kandinsky returned to his studio one evening and saw a painting
  of extraordinary beauty leaning against the wall — so beautiful
  he didn't immediately recognize it. When he did, he realized
  it was his own landscape painting, turned upside down.
  Without the subject, it was just color and form — and more
  beautiful for it.

  THE MUSIC PARALLEL:
  Music doesn't represent anything — it works through pure
  form (rhythm, melody, harmony) to produce emotional response.
  Why can't painting do the same?

  Kandinsky's early abstractions have musical titles:
  Composition, Improvisation, Impression — named by analogy.

  THE THEORY (Concerning the Spiritual in Art, 1911):
  ─ Colors have spiritual/psychological properties.
  ─ Yellow: earthly, aggressive.
  ─ Blue: spiritual, heavenly, introverted.
  ─ Form and color in combination = spiritual vibration.
  ─ This is not purely subjective — Kandinsky believed in
    objective spiritual properties of color.

  His theoretical framework is Theosophical spiritualism
  (Madame Blavatsky). The science is questionable; the paintings
  are remarkable.
```

## Piet Mondrian (1872–1944)

Mondrian's trajectory is one of the most systematic in art history: from realistic Dutch landscape painting to complete abstraction in under 15 years.

```
  THE REDUCTION (1908–1920):

  1908: Realistic landscapes, trees, dunes.
  1912: Trees with Cubist fragmentation (saw Cézanne and Cubism in Paris).
  1913: "Plus" and "Minus" works — trees abstracted to horizontal
        and vertical lines.
        "The tree becomes the field of forces which it evokes."
  1917: First Neoplasticist paintings — grid of black lines on white,
        with rectangles of primary colors.

  THE PRINCIPLE (De Stijl manifesto, 1917):
  ─ Art must reach universal plastic expression.
  ─ Eliminate the particular: no curves (too naturalistic),
    no secondary colors (too mixed/impure), no diagonal.
  ─ Only: horizontal and vertical lines (the primary directions
    of existence); primary colors (red, yellow, blue) + black, white, grey.
  ─ The tension between these is "dynamic equilibrium."

  THIS IS NOT DECORATIVE MINIMALISM:
  Mondrian believed he was expressing the fundamental structure
  of reality — the balance of opposing forces (horizontal/vertical,
  male/female, static/dynamic) that underlies all phenomena.
  It is a metaphysical program, not aesthetic preference.

  THE ARCHITECTURE CONNECTION:
  Mondrian's grid directly influenced Bauhaus typography and
  design, Swiss International Style graphic design, and the
  grid-based design systems used in web layout (Bootstrap,
  CSS grid, the 12-column system — all echo Mondrian's
  horizontal/vertical + proportion-by-ratio approach).
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Cubism shows things "from all sides at once"** — This is the standard explanation but it's too simple. Analytic Cubism doesn't systematically show the front, back, left, and right simultaneously. It shows the visual residue of having analyzed an object — the overlapping planes that result from synthesizing the experience of moving around and examining something. The result is not a diagram of viewpoints but a record of looking.

**Duchamp's Fountain is a joke** — It is both a joke and a serious philosophical argument. The joke is on the art world; the argument is that the art world's classifications are revealed as social decisions, not natural kinds. Philosophers of art (Arthur Danto, George Dickie's institutional theory of art) took Duchamp entirely seriously and built major theories around the Fountain.

**Mondrian just made grids** — Mondrian's grids are not decorative. Each painting involves carefully calibrated decisions about line thickness, proportion of rectangles, placement of primary colors. He believed these choices had metaphysical significance — expressing the underlying structure of dynamic equilibrium. Whether or not this is correct, the resulting paintings are visually sophisticated and not interchangeable.

**Abstract Expressionism begins with Kandinsky** — Kandinsky's work is the most important precedent for American Abstract Expressionism, but the movement (Pollock, de Kooning, Rothko, Newman) grew independently in New York in the 1940s, absorbing Surrealism (automatic drawing), the European émigrés who fled the Nazis, and the specific postwar American cultural context.
