## Cardiovascular Disease

```
CVD CAUSAL CHAIN:

  Risk factors                Pathophysiology          Outcomes
  ──────────────────────→    ─────────────────    →  ─────────────────
  Hypertension                                         MI (heart attack)
  High LDL-C             →   Atherosclerosis      →   Stroke
  Smoking                     (plaque buildup          Heart failure
  Diabetes                    in coronary/              Peripheral artery
  Obesity                     cerebral arteries)        disease
  Physical inactivity
  Family history/genetics

FRAMINGHAM HEART STUDY (1948-ongoing):
  Landmark cohort study that identified major CVD risk factors.
  Framingham Risk Score: 10-year CVD risk from age, sex, TC, HDL, SBP,
    diabetes, smoking status.
  Key finding: cardiovascular risk is continuous, not threshold-based.
  No "safe" blood pressure — lower is generally better within physiological range.

BLOOD PRESSURE THRESHOLDS (ACC/AHA 2017 guidelines):
  Normal:       <120/80
  Elevated:     120-129/<80
  Stage 1 HTN:  130-139 / 80-89
  Stage 2 HTN:  ≥140/≥90
  Hypertensive crisis: >180/>120

  Lowering SBP by 10 mmHg: ~20% reduction in major CVD events
  At population level: modest shifts in the entire distribution have larger
  impact than treating only the extreme tail (Rose theorem — see below)

PHARMACOLOGICAL PREVENTION:
  Statins: LDL lowering → 25% relative reduction in CVD per 1 mmol/L LDL drop
  Aspirin: antiplatelet; secondary prevention (after MI/stroke); primary
    prevention now controversial (benefits < bleeding risk in low-risk patients)
  Antihypertensives: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers
```

## The Rose Theorem — Population Approach to Prevention

Geoffrey Rose's fundamental insight (1985): The majority of disease burden comes from the large number of people at moderate risk, not the small number at extreme risk.

```
CASE DISTRIBUTION OF CVD EVENTS:

  Risk group          % of population   % of CVD events
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  High risk (top 10%)       10%              ~25%
  Moderate risk (next 50%)  50%              ~50%
  Low risk (bottom 40%)     40%              ~25%

  IMPLICATION: Treating only high-risk patients misses MOST of the disease.
  Population-wide interventions (reduce average population salt intake,
  reduce average smoking rate) have larger absolute impact than
  targeted high-risk interventions.

  But: population-wide interventions impose small inconvenience on
  many healthy people to benefit a few. The "prevention paradox."

TWO COMPLEMENTARY STRATEGIES:
  HIGH-RISK STRATEGY: Screen and treat individuals above threshold
    Pros: efficient, targets those who benefit most
    Cons: misses most cases, requires screening infrastructure

  POPULATION STRATEGY: Shift entire distribution (lower mean)
    Pros: larger absolute benefit, no screening required
    Cons: most individuals get no individual benefit
    Examples: salt reduction in food supply, alcohol taxation,
              tobacco taxes, trans-fat bans
```

```
  Prostate:     ~34,000     Liver:       ~28,000

KEY MODIFIABLE RISK FACTORS:
  Tobacco:      ~30% of cancer deaths (lung, bladder, head/neck, etc.)
  Obesity:      ~7-8% (endometrial, breast post-menopause, colon, etc.)
  Alcohol:      ~6% (oral, pharynx, esophagus, liver, breast)
  Infections:   HPV (cervical, oropharyngeal), H. pylori (gastric),
                HBV/HCV (liver), HIV (Kaposi, lymphomas)

  Ionizing radiation: Leukemia, thyroid, breast
  Occupational: Asbestos (mesothelioma), benzene (leukemia)

CANCER SCREENING PRINCIPLES:
  Screening = testing asymptomatic people to find early disease
  Requirements:
  1. Disease has significant burden (prevalence/severity)
  2. Detectable preclinical phase (screen in this window)
  3. Effective treatment available at early stage
  4. Screening test: adequate sensitivity/specificity + acceptable
  5. Benefits outweigh harms (false positives, overdiagnosis, anxiety)
```

## Screening Biases

```
LEAD TIME BIAS:
  Screening detects disease earlier in its natural history.
  Survival from diagnosis appears longer — but death occurs at same time.
  Patient lives just as long — just "knows" about cancer longer.

  Before screening: |──symptom──────────────death──|
  After screening:  |──screen──────────────────────death──|
                         ↑ lead time ↑

  FIX: Use disease-specific mortality rates, not survival from diagnosis.

LENGTH-BIASED SAMPLING:
  Screening selectively detects slower-growing, less aggressive tumors
  (they have longer preclinical window → more likely caught by screen).
  Fast-growing tumors appear between screens (interval cancers).
  Result: screened-detected tumors systematically less aggressive.
  Makes screening appear more beneficial than it is.

OVERDIAGNOSIS:
  Detecting disease that would never have caused symptoms or death
  during the patient's natural lifespan.
  Example: Prostate cancer detected by PSA screening that grows so
  slowly it would never manifest — but once detected, patient receives
  treatment (surgery, radiation) with serious side effects.
  PSA screening debate: substantial overdiagnosis; NNT to prevent
  one death from prostate cancer ≈ 750 men screened for 10 years.

RECOMMENDED SCREENING (US Preventive Services Task Force — USPSTF):
  Colorectal:  Colonoscopy every 10yr (age 45-75) or FOBT annually
  Breast:      Mammography every 2yr (women 40+, per 2024 guidelines)
  Cervical:    Pap + HPV test every 5yr (age 21-65)
  Lung:        Low-dose CT annually (50-80yr, ≥20 pack-year history)
  AAA:         Ultrasound once (men 65-75 who ever smoked)
```

## Diabetes — Epidemiology and Prevention

```
DIABETES BURDEN:
  Global: ~537M adults with diabetes (2021); projected 643M by 2030
  US: ~37M (11.3% of adults); prediabetes: ~96M (38% of adults)
  Type 2: ~90-95% of cases; driven by obesity + physical inactivity
  Type 1: autoimmune; ~5-10% of cases

TYPE 2 DIABETES PREVENTION:
  Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) RCT:
  - 3,234 high-risk adults (prediabetes), randomized
  - Lifestyle intervention: 7% weight loss + ≥150 min/wk moderate activity
  - Result: 58% relative reduction in T2D incidence over 3 years
  - Metformin arm: 31% reduction
  - Lifestyle > metformin; lifestyle effect maintained 10+ years

DIAGNOSIS CRITERIA:
  HbA1c:    ≥6.5% (diabetes), 5.7-6.4% (prediabetes)
  FPG:      ≥126 mg/dL (diabetes), 100-125 (prediabetes)
  2-hr OGTT: ≥200 mg/dL (diabetes), 140-199 (prediabetes)
  Random:   ≥200 mg/dL + symptoms

COMPLICATIONS (CHRONICALLY ELEVATED BLOOD GLUCOSE):
  Macrovascular: CVD, stroke, peripheral vascular disease
  Microvascular: Retinopathy (leading cause of blindness), nephropathy
                 (leading cause of kidney failure), neuropathy
  Cost: ~$327B/year in US (2017) — direct medical + indirect productivity
```

## COPD

```
COPD:
  Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease — preventable progressive airflow
  limitation. Includes emphysema (alveolar destruction) and chronic bronchitis.

  Global burden: 3rd leading cause of death worldwide
  US: ~16M diagnosed; leading cause of disability

  PRIMARY CAUSE: Tobacco smoking (~90% of cases in high-income countries)
  Other causes: biomass fuel smoke (cooking fires → global burden in LMICs),
                occupational dusts, outdoor air pollution, alpha-1 antitrypsin def.

SPIROMETRY STAGING (GOLD criteria):
  FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.70 (post-bronchodilator) = obstructive pattern
  GOLD 1 (mild):    FEV1 ≥80% predicted
  GOLD 2 (moderate): FEV1 50-79%
  GOLD 3 (severe):   FEV1 30-49%
  GOLD 4 (very severe): FEV1 <30%

PREVENTION: Smoking cessation is the only proven intervention that slows
  FEV1 decline. All other treatments are symptomatic.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| NCD prevention question | Approach |
|---|---|
| Reduce population CVD at lowest cost | Population strategy: salt reduction, tobacco tax, trans-fat ban |
| Identify high-risk individuals | Screen with validated risk score (Framingham, PCE) |
| Prevent colorectal cancer in average-risk adult | Colonoscopy every 10yr from age 45 |
| Prevent type 2 diabetes in prediabetes | DPP-style lifestyle program (weight loss + activity) |
| Reduce lung cancer mortality | Low-dose CT screening for heavy smokers age 50-80 |
| Prevent HPV-related cancers | HPV vaccine at age 11-12 (before exposure) |
| Why PSA screening controversial | Overdiagnosis, overtreatment outweigh benefit except targeted use |
| Reduce COPD burden | Tobacco control policy (primary prevention at population level) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Survival improvement vs. mortality reduction in cancer screening**: Always look at disease-specific mortality rates, not 5-year survival rates. Better 5-year survival can reflect lead-time bias with no actual mortality reduction. Lung cancer screening (NLST): reduced lung cancer mortality 20% — this is mortality reduction, the correct endpoint.

**Relative risk reduction vs. absolute**: Statins reduce CVD event risk by ~25% relative. In a person with 2% 10-year absolute risk, 25% relative reduction = 0.5% absolute reduction = NNT 200. In a person with 20% 10-year absolute risk, 25% relative reduction = 5% absolute reduction = NNT 20. High-risk patients benefit far more in absolute terms from the same drug.

**Rose's prevention paradox in NCD**: A large population-wide salt reduction saves thousands of lives per year but no individual can point to their own saved life. A high-risk treatment saves one identified patient. Politics and media favor visible, identifiable beneficiaries. This creates systematic underinvestment in population-level prevention relative to its cost-effectiveness.

**NCDs in LMICs**: Common assumption that NCDs are "diseases of affluence." Wrong. 80% of NCD deaths occur in LMICs. Double burden: infectious disease + NCDs simultaneously. CVD and diabetes have increased dramatically in countries undergoing nutritional transition (ultra-processed food penetration, urbanization, reduced physical activity).
