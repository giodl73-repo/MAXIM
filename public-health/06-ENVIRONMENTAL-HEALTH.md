## Air Quality — PM2.5 and Health

```
PARTICULATE MATTER SIZE FRACTIONS:
  PM10:  Coarse particles (≤10 μm diameter) — deposited in upper airways
  PM2.5: Fine particles (≤2.5 μm) — penetrate deep into alveoli
  PM0.1: Ultrafine (<0.1 μm) — may enter bloodstream directly

  PM2.5 is the most health-relevant fraction.

SOURCES:
  Combustion: vehicles, power plants, industry, residential heating
  Secondary formation: SO₂ and NOₓ react in atmosphere → sulfate/nitrate PM2.5
  Wildfire smoke: increasingly significant source in western US
  Biomass burning: dominant source in many LMICs (cookstoves)

HEALTH EFFECTS OF PM2.5:
  Cardiovascular: atherosclerosis acceleration, MI, stroke, heart failure
  Respiratory: COPD exacerbation, asthma, reduced lung function development
  Cancer: lung cancer (IARC Group 1 carcinogen — highest certainty level)
  Neurological: emerging evidence for dementia risk
  Metabolic: insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes

DOSE-RESPONSE:
  No established safe threshold — linear no-threshold (LNT) assumed
  Each 10 μg/m³ increase in long-term PM2.5 ≈ 6-8% increase in CVD mortality
  WHO Air Quality Guidelines (2021): PM2.5 annual mean ≤5 μg/m³
  US NAAQS (2024): PM2.5 annual mean ≤9 μg/m³ (tightened from 12)
  Global median PM2.5: ~32 μg/m³ (huge proportion over WHO guideline)

GLOBAL BURDEN:
  PM2.5 air pollution: ~7M deaths/year attributable
  ~4.2M from ambient (outdoor) air pollution
  ~2.8M from household air pollution (cooking fires)
  Attributable fraction is computed using PAR methodology from epidemiology
```

## Ozone

```
OZONE (O₃):
  Ground-level: pollutant formed from NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight (photochemical smog)
  Stratospheric: beneficial — shields UV-B

  HEALTH EFFECTS:
    Lung inflammation, chest pain, cough
    Aggravates asthma and COPD
    Repeated high exposures → permanent lung function damage
    Limited evidence for CVD effects

  US NAAQS: 70 ppb (8-hour average)

  CLIMATE INTERACTION: Ozone precursor emissions increase with temperature.
    Climate change → more ozone formation days.
    Wildfire smoke contributes to both PM2.5 and ozone.
```

## Lead — Historical and Contemporary Exposure

```
LEAD TOXICITY:
  No established safe blood lead level (BLL) for children
  Primary effect: neurodevelopmental — IQ points, behavior, attention
  Mechanism: displaces Ca²⁺ in synaptic transmission, disrupts
    NMDA receptor function, impairs myelin formation

  Blood Lead Reference Value (CDC): 3.5 μg/dL (action trigger)
  Action: environmental investigation, nutritional counseling, abatement

HISTORICAL SOURCES:
  Leaded gasoline (phased out US 1986): atmospheric → soil → dust
  Lead paint (banned US residential use 1978): still in 35-50% of US homes built before 1978
  Lead pipes (plumbing): Flint, Michigan crisis (2014-2019) — corrosive water leached lead from pipes
  Lead solder in food cans (phased out)

FLINT, MICHIGAN CASE STUDY:
  April 2014: City switched water supply from Detroit (Lake Huron) to
    Flint River to cut costs. River water more corrosive.
  Failure to add corrosion inhibitor (orthophosphate): violated EPA LCR (Lead and Copper Rule)
  Pipes began leaching lead: BLL ≥5 μg/dL in children rose from 2.4% to 4.9%
  Denial by state/local officials delayed response 18+ months
  Epidemiological evidence: pediatrician Mona Hanna-Attisha published
    retrospective cohort showing BLL elevation; initially disputed by officials
  Lessons: environmental justice (majority Black, low-income city);
    whistleblower suppression; regulatory failure; infrastructure underinvestment

LEAD ABATEMENT:
  Paint: encapsulation or removal (EPA-certified contractors)
  Soil: cover with clean soil, plants; remove if very high
  Water: filter (NSF-53 certified), flush before use, replace pipes
  Nutrition: calcium, iron, vitamin C reduce lead absorption
```

## Water Contamination

```
WATERBORNE DISEASE CATEGORIES:

  Microbial (most important globally):
    Cholera (V. cholerae): fecal-oral via contaminated water/food
    Typhoid (S. typhi): fecal-oral
    Cryptosporidiosis: protozoan, chlorine-resistant — filter required
    Hepatitis A, E: fecal-oral, waterborne outbreaks
    Giardia: protozoan, rivers/lakes

  Chemical:
    Arsenic: naturally occurring in groundwater (Bangladesh, India, SW US)
      WHO guideline: 10 μg/L; millions exposed to >50 μg/L
      Health: skin lesions, lung/bladder/liver cancer, cardiovascular
    Nitrates: fertilizer runoff
      Health: methemoglobinemia in infants ("blue baby syndrome")
      EPA MCL: 10 mg/L nitrate-N
    PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances): industrial/firefighting foam
      Health: immune effects, cancer, thyroid; bioaccumulates; "forever chemicals"
      EPA MCL (2024): 4 ng/L for PFOA and PFOS individually
    Heavy metals: lead (see above), mercury (methylmercury via fish)

WATER TREATMENT CHAIN:
  Source → Coagulation/flocculation → Sedimentation → Filtration
  → Disinfection (chlorination, UV, ozone) → Distribution

  Chlorination byproducts (trihalomethanes, haloacetic acids): potential
  carcinogens at high levels; regulated by EPA. Tradeoff: chlorinate more →
  more THMs; chlorinate less → more microbial risk.
```

## Climate Change and Health

```
CLIMATE CHANGE HEALTH PATHWAYS:

  DIRECT:
    Heat: excess mortality from extreme heat events
          Heat index = feels-like temperature accounting for humidity
          Wet bulb temperature: ≥35°C = physiological survivability limit
          Urban heat island: cities 1-3°C warmer than surroundings
    Extreme weather: floods (injury, displacement, cholera), wildfires (PM2.5)
    Cold-related mortality: decreases (smaller effect than heat increase)

  INDIRECT (via ecosystem changes):
    Infectious disease range expansion:
      Malaria: geographic range shifts poleward and to higher altitude
      Dengue: Aedes aegypti expansion (now in southern Europe, US south)
      Lyme: Ixodes scapularis tick expansion northward into Canada
      West Nile: longer transmission seasons
    Water security: drought, glacial melt, sea-level freshwater intrusion
    Food security: crop yield changes, extreme weather disruption
    Air quality: more ozone formation days, longer wildfire seasons

  SOCIAL/DOWNSTREAM:
    Displacement: climate refugees → overcrowding → infectious disease
    Conflict: resource scarcity → violence → injury, mental health
    Mental health: climate grief, PTSD from disasters, solastalgia

ATTRIBUTABLE BURDEN:
  WHO estimate: 250,000 additional deaths/year 2030-2050 from climate change
    (malaria, diarrhea, malnutrition, heat stress)
  Lancet Countdown annual tracking: monitors 43 health-climate indicators
```

## Environmental Justice

```
DEFINITION:
  The fair treatment and meaningful involvement of all people,
  regardless of race, color, national origin, income, with respect
  to environmental regulations, programs, policies.

EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE:
  In the US, communities of color and low-income communities:
  - Live closer to hazardous waste sites (CERCLA Superfund)
  - Experience higher PM2.5 and NOₓ exposure from traffic
  - Had higher lead exposure historically (older housing stock)
  - Face more extreme heat (less green space, more pavement)
  - Suffer more flooding (development in flood plains)

  Causal mechanisms:
  - Siting decisions: industrial facilities placed in low-political-power areas
  - Housing: racial covenants → concentrated poverty → industrial proximity
  - Zoning: industrial-residential colocations in lower-income areas
  - Enforcement: EPA enforcement less vigorous in minority communities

EPA ENVIRONMENTAL JUSTICE PROGRAMS:
  Title VI of Civil Rights Act: prohibits disparate impacts in federally
    funded programs
  EJScreen: EPA mapping tool showing cumulative EJ burden by tract
  Justice40: Biden initiative — 40% of federal climate/clean energy investment
    benefits to disadvantaged communities

CUMULATIVE RISK:
  Multiple simultaneous exposures + social vulnerability + susceptibility
  Total health burden > sum of individual exposures analyzed separately
  EJ populations: PM2.5 + lead + noise + stress + food desert + heat
```

## Systems Thinking Bridges

| Environmental health concept | Systems / CS analogue |
|---|---|
| Exposure assessment chain (source → transport → dose → effect) | Fault propagation analysis: source emits a defect, transport medium carries it (network, atmosphere, water), dose is what reaches the receptor, effect is the downstream outcome — the chain is a causal DAG for tracing harm from origin to endpoint |
| Linear no-threshold (LNT) dose-response | Conservative safety margin design: LNT assumes no safe threshold below which risk is zero; same as assuming a security vulnerability exists at all exposure levels with no safe operating region — the regulatory choice reflects risk aversion when evidence is insufficient to identify a threshold |
| NOAEL → uncertainty factors → reference dose (RfD) | Safety factor engineering: NOAEL from animal studies ÷ 10 (animal-to-human extrapolation) ÷ 10 (population variability) ÷ additional factors = RfD; the cascaded safety factors mirror design safety margins in structural or electrical engineering |
| Cumulative risk (EJ populations: multiple simultaneous exposures) | Composability of failure modes: independent stressors (PM2.5 + lead + noise + heat + food insecurity) interact non-linearly; total health burden exceeds sum of individual exposures analyzed separately — analogous to how independent service degradations compound in a distributed system under correlated failures |
| PAR (population attributable risk) | Fraction of failures attributable to a cause: PAR = (risk_exposed − risk_unexposed) × prevalence / total risk; the fraction of cases that would disappear if exposure were eliminated — the same counterfactual reasoning as dependency attribution in root cause analysis |
| Ecological fallacy in environmental epidemiology | Multi-level modeling error: county-level pollution correlates with health outcomes, but individuals within a county have heterogeneous exposures; aggregate-level correlation ≠ individual-level causation — the same reason you cannot infer user experience from server-side metrics without joining to user-level data |
| Dose-response threshold (non-carcinogens) | Fault tolerance with graceful degradation: below a threshold, biological repair mechanisms keep pace with damage; above it, cumulative harm exceeds repair — analogous to a circuit breaker that tolerates low error rates but trips above a threshold to prevent cascade failure |

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Environmental health question | Tool/approach |
|---|---|
| Assess air pollution health risk | PM2.5 concentration × dose-response function → attributable mortality |
| Investigate suspected contamination cluster | Exposure assessment → case-control or cohort study |
| Set regulatory limit for a toxin | NOAEL/LOAEL from animal studies → uncertainty factors → RfD |
| Identify areas of highest EJ burden | EPA EJScreen, CDC SVI, CalEnviroScreen |
| Prioritize pollutant for reduction | Compare attributable disease burden (DALYs) across pollutants |
| Investigate lead exposure in children | BLL testing + environmental investigation of home/soil/water |
| Evaluate water system contamination | Source characterization + treatment chain + distribution sampling |

## Common Confusion Points

**Linear no-threshold (LNT) vs. threshold models**: For carcinogens (radiation, certain chemicals), EPA typically assumes no safe threshold — any dose carries proportional risk. For non-carcinogens, a threshold is assumed below which no adverse effects occur. PM2.5 uses LNT (no safe threshold demonstrated epidemiologically). The choice of model affects regulation stringency enormously.

**Correlation vs. causation in environmental epidemiology**: Ecological studies often used (county-level pollution vs. health outcomes). Ecological fallacy applies: individual exposures may not track county means. Best evidence comes from natural experiments (plant closures, fuel standard changes) or individual-level exposure assessment.

**IARC classification ≠ dose matters**: IARC Group 1 = sufficient evidence of carcinogenicity, not "extremely dangerous at any dose." Red meat is Group 2A (probably carcinogenic); processed meat is Group 1. This does not mean processed meat is as dangerous as asbestos — classification reflects evidence quality, not magnitude of risk.

**Hormesis**: Some toxins show J-curve dose-response: low doses protective, high doses harmful. Low-dose radiation hormesis is actively debated but not widely accepted as regulatory basis. Don't conflate the possibility of hormesis with evidence sufficient to abandon protective precautionary standards.
