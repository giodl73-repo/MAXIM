## The Four Health System Functions (WHO Framework)

```
STEWARDSHIP:      Policy setting, regulation, oversight
                  "Setting the rules of the game"
                  Government / ministry of health primary role

FINANCING:        Revenue collection, pooling, purchasing
                  Pre-payment + risk pooling = insurance logic
                  Higher pooling → greater risk protection

RESOURCE          Workforce, infrastructure, medicines, equipment
GENERATION:       Medical education, hospital construction, supply chain

SERVICE           Preventive, curative, rehabilitative, palliative
DELIVERY:         Primary care, specialist, hospital, community

GOALS:
  Health improvement (population health status)
  Responsiveness (patient experience, dignity, choice)
  Financial protection (catastrophic expenditure avoidance)
  Fairness in financing (progressive vs. regressive)
  Efficiency (cost per health unit)
```

## Bismarck Model — Social Health Insurance

```
BISMARCK MODEL MECHANICS:

  Employers + Employees → mandatory payroll contributions →
  → Insurance funds (non-profit "sickness funds") →
  → Negotiate rates with independent providers → Care delivered

  KEY FEATURES:
  - Multi-payer (many competing/regulated funds)
  - Risk equalization required: funds covering sicker populations get transfers
  - Employment-based enrollment: coverage tied to work status
  - Weakness: What about unemployed, elderly, self-employed?
    → Government subsidizes: Germany has ~90% of population via SHI;
      Beihilfe (civil servants), PKV (private) cover rest

  EXAMPLES:
  Germany:    ~100 statutory health insurance (GKV) funds; premium ~14.6% of wage
  France:     Assurance Maladie; co-payments + mutuelles (supplemental insurance)
  Netherlands: Regulated competition among insurers; individual mandate
  Belgium:    Mutualities (sickness funds) with tight regulation
  Japan:      Employment-based insurance + National Health Insurance for non-employed

  GERMANY SPENDING: ~12.8% GDP on health; high by EU standards
  QUALITY: Generally very good; some concerns about prevention/primary care
```

## Beveridge Model — National Health Service

```
BEVERIDGE MODEL MECHANICS:

  Government taxes → single payer (government) →
  → Government-employed clinicians in government-owned facilities → Care

  KEY FEATURES:
  - Single-payer, single-provider
  - Universal coverage by citizenship/residence
  - Global budgets for hospitals (block funding)
  - GP gatekeeping to control specialist/hospital access
  - Strength: universal, low administrative cost, strong equity
  - Weakness: waiting times, underfunding → "rationing" debates,
    limited patient choice, political vulnerability (budget cuts)

  UK NHS:
    Founded 1948 (Aneurin Bevan); "free at point of use" principle
    Spending: ~10.2% GDP; GP contracts with NHS (independent contractor model)
    NICE (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence):
      Conducts cost-effectiveness analysis; publishes technology appraisals
      £20,000-30,000/QALY threshold for routine coverage decisions
      Very influential model — adopted globally for HTA
    Waiting time issues: long waits for elective surgery, GP access

  CANADA:
    Medicare: universal hospital + physician coverage; provincial administration
    Physicians: private independent; hospitals: non-profit
    No coverage: drugs, dentistry, long-term care (political failure)
    Spending: ~11.5% GDP; wait times major issue

  SCANDINAVIA:
    Decentralized NHS models; regional/municipal administration
    High spending (Sweden: ~11%), high outcomes, low inequality
    Strong primary care; tight integration of health and social care
```

## US Health System — Mixed/Hybrid

```
US HEALTH SYSTEM STRUCTURE:

  EMPLOYER-SPONSORED INSURANCE (ESI):
    ~159M people. Employers purchase insurance; employees share premium.
    Tax-exempt: largest federal health subsidy (~$300B/year foregone tax).
    Fragmented: ~900 commercial insurers.

  MEDICARE:
    Age 65+, disabled, ESRD. ~65M enrollees.
    Part A: Hospital (financed by payroll tax)
    Part B: Physician/outpatient (premiums + general revenue)
    Part C: Medicare Advantage (private plans under Medicare contract)
    Part D: Prescription drugs (2003 addition, subsidized)

  MEDICAID:
    Low-income. Joint federal-state. ~90M enrollees.
    ACA expanded eligibility to 138% FPL → 38 states expanded
    12 states have not expanded (primarily South) → coverage gap

  ACA (AFFORDABLE CARE ACT, 2010):
    Individual mandate, insurance exchanges, Medicaid expansion
    Coverage: ~20M newly insured; uninsured rate fell from ~16% to ~8%
    Exchanges (Marketplace): subsidized premiums for 100-400% FPL
    Prohibition on pre-existing condition exclusions

  UNINSURED: ~25-27M people (8%)

US SPENDING:
  ~17.3% GDP (2021) — far exceeding other wealthy nations
  Peer-country average: ~10-12% GDP
  Per capita: ~$12,000/year vs. ~$6,000 UK, ~$5,000 Germany
  Outcomes: life expectancy below OECD average (78.9 yr vs. 81.1 yr)
  "High cost, average quality, poor equity"

REASONS FOR HIGH US SPENDING:
  Administrative complexity: 25-30% of hospital revenue on billing/admin
  Provider market power: consolidation → price increases not offset by quality
  Drug prices: no government negotiation (until IRA 2022, partial)
  Fee-for-service incentives: volume > value
  Litigation costs: defensive medicine
  Fragmentation: care coordination failures → expensive readmissions
```

## Universal Health Coverage (UHC)

```
UHC CUBE (WHO three dimensions):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │           ↑ WHO IS COVERED?                              │
  │           │  (population coverage)                       │
  │           │                                              │
  │   ────────┼──────────────→ WHICH SERVICES?              │
  │           │                (service coverage)            │
  │           │                                              │
  │           └──── WHAT COSTS? (financial protection)       │
  │                 (out-of-pocket cap)                       │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SDG Target 3.8: Achieve UHC including financial risk protection,
  access to quality essential services, access to medicines and vaccines

UHC SERVICE INDEX (WHO/World Bank):
  Scale 0-100 measuring 14 tracer indicators
  (family planning, ANC, immunization, HIV treatment, TB treatment,
   sanitation access, IHD treatment, diabetes treatment, etc.)
  Global average: 68 (2019)
  Range: 26 (South Sudan) to 90+ (Japan, Canada, Singapore)

FINANCIAL PROTECTION:
  Catastrophic health expenditure: OOP > 10% of total household consumption
    Currently ~12% of global population faces catastrophic expenditure
  Impoverishment: OOP costs push household below poverty line
    ~100M people pushed into extreme poverty by health costs annually

ESSENTIAL MEDICINES:
  WHO Essential Medicines List: ~479 medicines; updated every 2 years
  Access gap: ~2B people lack access to essential medicines
  Primary reason: cost, not availability (generics + financing)
```

## Health Financing Concepts

```
REVENUE COLLECTION METHODS:
  General taxation:     Progressive; pooled; political competition
  Payroll tax:          Flat % on wages; employment-contingent
  Premiums:             Regressive if flat; risk-rated is worst for equity
  Out-of-pocket (OOP):  Most regressive; creates utilization barriers
  External aid:         Unsustainable; donor-controlled

  Best for equity: general tax > payroll > premiums > OOP

RISK POOLING:
  Principle: pool large number of individuals → statistical averaging
  → no single person faces catastrophic individual risk
  Larger pool + more diverse risk profile → more effective insurance

  Community rating: everyone pays same premium regardless of health status
  Risk adjustment: adjust payments to insurers based on enrollee risk
  Without risk adjustment: "cream skimming" — insurers select healthy patients

GLOBAL HEALTH FINANCING TRENDS:
  Domestic resources: rising in MICs (income growth → fiscal space)
  Development assistance for health (DAH): ~$44B/year (2019 pre-COVID)
  Out-of-pocket: still >40% of health spending in low-income countries
  Social health insurance: expanding in many LMICs (Rwanda, Ghana, Indonesia)
  Ethiopia, Rwanda, Bangladesh: expanded coverage through community-based SHI
```

## Health Technology Assessment (HTA)

```
HTA: systematic evaluation of technologies for coverage decisions
  Technology = drugs, devices, procedures, programs

COST-EFFECTIVENESS ANALYSIS:
  ICER = (Cost_new − Cost_old) / (Effect_new − Effect_old)
  Units: $/QALY (or $/DALY averted)
  Threshold: if ICER < willingness-to-pay threshold → adopt

  NICE (UK) threshold: £20,000-30,000/QALY (~$25,000-40,000)
  WHO guidance: use 1-3× GDP per capita as threshold
  US: no official threshold; cost-effectiveness rarely used in formulary decisions

  EXAMPLE:
    New cancer drug: cost +$120,000/year, gains 0.5 QALYs
    ICER = $120,000 / 0.5 = $240,000/QALY → above most thresholds

  BUDGET IMPACT vs. COST-EFFECTIVENESS:
    A drug can be cost-effective but have large budget impact
    (e.g., effective drug for common disease). Both matter for coverage.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Health policy question | Key framework |
|---|---|
| Expand to universal coverage | UHC cube: who, what, how much coverage |
| Choose financing mechanism | Tax > payroll > premiums > OOP for equity |
| Evaluate if a new drug should be covered | HTA/ICER vs. willingness-to-pay threshold |
| Compare US to other systems | % GDP, outcomes, equity — US outlier on cost |
| Why US spends so much | Admin overhead, provider consolidation, no drug price negotiation |
| Address catastrophic health spending | Financial protection via pre-payment and pooling |
| Fund health in low-income country | DAH + domestic mobilization + SHI development |

## Common Confusion Points

**Canada is not Beveridge**: Canada has Beveridge financing (public single-payer insurance) but delivery is mostly private — physicians are independent contractors, hospitals are mostly non-profit but not government-owned. The UK's NHS is more purely Beveridge (government employs most clinicians, owns most hospitals).

**Medicare ≠ Medicaid**: Medicare = age/disability-based federal program. Medicaid = income-based state-federal program. Both created in 1965 (Great Society). Confusion is common in US public discourse.

**UHC ≠ socialized medicine**: UHC is the goal (all people receive needed health services without financial hardship). How to achieve it is a policy choice: could be Bismarck (multi-payer insurance), Beveridge (NHS), or regulated mixed system. Singapore achieves near-UHC through mandatory savings accounts (Medisave) + catastrophic insurance — a different model entirely.

**Efficiency vs. equity in health policy**: Cost-effectiveness analysis maximizes aggregate health per dollar — which can conflict with equity if optimal programs favor lower-risk/lower-cost populations. A $20,000/QALY program for young adults > $20,000/QALY program for elderly on pure ICER grounds but this may violate equity principles. NICE uses severity weighting; some analysts add equity weights to DALYs.
