# 05 — Settings

## Prong, Bezel, Pavé, Channel, Tension — Securing Stone to Metal

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **The fundamental problem**: a gemstone must be held securely in metal while maximizing light entry (especially for transparent/translucent stones) and protecting the stone from impact; setting style is a design choice with real tradeoffs between security, visibility, light transmission, and stone protection
- **Prong setting (claw setting)**: most common for solitaire diamonds; 4 or 6 metal prongs (claws) hold stone at girdle; maximum light entry around all sides; stone very visible; prongs can catch on fabric; prong tips can wear and require retipping (maintenance issue); 4-prong makes stone appear squarer, 6-prong rounder; claw tips: round, square, V-shaped (for pointed corners of princess/marquise), pointed; Tiffany setting (1886) = 6-prong raised solitaire on knife-edge band — iconic, was revolutionary in exposing the diamond to light from all sides vs earlier closed-back "collet" settings
- **Bezel setting**: thin strip of metal folded/burnished over the stone's girdle all around; most secure setting; protects stone edges from chipping; suits lower-domed stones and cabochons; obscures part of stone's circumference; can look heavy; full bezel vs partial bezel (open on sides); flush bezel; tube setting (cylindrical bezel in a drilled hole); popular for solitaires in contemporary/minimalist designs and for active-wear jewelry
- **Pavé and micro-pavé**: small stones set very close together, metal barely visible; multiple stones set in drilled holes with tiny beads/grains of metal raised between them (bead setting) or with small prongs; creates "paved" effect of continuous sparkle; micro-pavé = very small stones (0.01 ct melee), requires magnification to set; labor-intensive; stones can fall out over time (maintenance issue); hand pavé vs machine/CAD-set pavé
- **Channel setting**: stones set in a groove (channel) cut into the metal, held by two parallel rails; no metal between stones; clean, streamlined appearance; suits baguettes, rounds, princess cuts in bands; stones relatively secure; difficult/expensive to resize (ring shank must be cut); cathedral channel settings raise the stone area above the band
- **Tension setting**: stone held by compression from the metal band itself; no prongs or bezels; dramatic floating appearance; requires very precise fitting (metal must exert exactly right springback force); hardened alloy (often platinum or 18K white gold); only certain stones safe (diamond, sapphire, ruby — 9-10 Mohs; softer stones can be damaged by the pressure); not all sizes/shapes suit tension; difficult to repair if band is damaged
- **Gypsy / flush setting**: stone set flush with the surrounding metal surface; drilled hole slightly smaller than stone, metal spread over girdle; very protective; low profile; popular in men's rings; also called "burnish setting"; clean modern look; stone relatively difficult to replace if damaged
- **Illusion setting**: small diamond surrounded by shaped white metal plate that visually extends the appearance of the stone; makes a 0.25 ct diamond appear larger; common in engagement rings of mid-20th century; can appear dated; also helps hide chips at girdle
- **Cluster setting**: multiple smaller stones arranged to create appearance of one larger stone; large Victorian diamond clusters; flower cluster (central stone surrounded by ring of same-size stones); calibré setting (precisely cut, matched colored stones in an outlined pattern — Art Deco characteristic)
- **Star setting**: small stone set within a star of engraved lines radiating from the stone; enhances apparent size; decorative technique from Indian and Middle Eastern tradition
- **Stone setting process** (brief): select setting matched to stone size; set stone with tweezers; use setting tool (pusher, bezel rocker, burnisher for bezel; setting bur + graver for prongs) to move metal over stone girdle; check stone is secure (no movement, no light leaking under girdle); bright-cut finish around stone enhances sparkle
- **CAD/CAM in settings**: modern fine jewelry increasingly designed in CAD (MatrixGold, Rhino + plugins, JewelCAD) → 3D printed wax or resin → cast; allows precise stone setting calculations, matched seat depth, ensures stone drops in correctly before investing; parametric ring sizing; CAD-generated models for presentation before fabrication
