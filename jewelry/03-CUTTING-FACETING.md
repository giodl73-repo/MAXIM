# Cutting & Faceting — Lapidary, Brilliant Cut, Cabochon

## The Big Picture

Cutting does not add sparkle — it reveals it. The physics is total internal reflection: light entering a faceted stone bounces off pavilion facets at angles exceeding the critical angle, then exits through the crown toward the viewer. The mathematics of the brilliant cut is precisely tuned to this optic.


## The Optics: Why Cutting Matters

```
TOTAL INTERNAL REFLECTION (TIR)
================================

n₁ = refractive index of stone
n₂ = RI of air (1.0)
Critical angle θc = arcsin(n₂/n₁)

For diamond:  n = 2.417  →  θc = arcsin(1/2.417) = 24.4°
For corundum: n ≈ 1.766  →  θc = arcsin(1/1.766) = 34.6°
For glass:    n ≈ 1.50   →  θc = arcsin(1/1.50)  = 41.8°

Diamond's TIR kicks in at 24.4°, which is why pavilion facets
at ~40.75° easily exceed the critical angle and reflect virtually
all light back up through the crown.

Glass at 41.8° critical angle — pavilion angles that work for
diamond (40.75°) let light leak through glass. This is why
glass jewelry lacks the depth and brilliance of diamond.

WHAT MAKES BRILLIANT DIAMONDS BRILLIANT:
- High RI → low critical angle → efficient light trapping
- Precise facet geometry → light exits through crown toward viewer
- High dispersion (0.044) → white light splits into spectral fire
```

## Cabochon (Cab)

The oldest cutting form. No mathematical precision required — aesthetic judgment.

```
WHEN TO USE CABOCHON
======================

Optical phenomena requiring dome surface:
├── Asterism (star effect)
│   Stars from reflected light off parallel needle inclusions (rutile)
│   Star ruby: rutile in 3 orientations (120°) → 6-rayed star
│   Star sapphire: same; best quality = sharp, centered star
│   Cut with dome axis perpendicular to c-axis of crystal
│
├── Chatoyancy (cat's eye)
│   Single band of reflected light from parallel fibrous inclusions
│   Cat's-eye chrysoberyl: finest; also occurs in tourmaline, quartz
│   Cut with dome axis perpendicular to fibers
│
├── Adularescence (moonstone)
│   Blue shimmer from alternating feldspar layers (albite/orthoclase)
│   Scattering effect, not reflection; blue body color most valued
│
├── Labradorescence (labradorite)
│   Structural color from twinning planes in feldspar
│
└── Play of color (opal)
    Diffraction array of silica spheres (200–300 nm spacing)
    Produces spectral colors depending on sphere size
    Must be cabochon — faceting destroys the play

Also used for: translucent stones, heavily included stones,
              opaque ornamental materials (turquoise, malachite, jade)
```

## The Round Brilliant — Geometry

The most analyzed cut in jewelry history. Marcel Tolkowsky's 1919 thesis established the proportions still used today.

```
STANDARD ROUND BRILLIANT
=========================
                   Table
              ┌─────────────┐
             /│             │\
            / │   CROWN     │ \
           /  │   Height    │  \
Crown     /   │  ~16.2%     │   \  Crown
angle  ──╱    │             │    ╲── angle
  34.5°  \────┼─────────────┼────/   34.5°
          |   │   GIRDLE    │   |
           \  │   Thin–     │  /
            \ │   thick     │ /
             \│  PAVILION   │/
              \             /
               \           /
                \  ~43%    /  Pavilion
                 \  depth /   angle
                  \      /    40.75°
                   \    /
                    \  /
                     \/  Culet (small facet or pointed)

PROPORTIONS (GIA Excellent range):
Table size:      53–57% of girdle diameter
Total depth:     59–62.5%
Crown angle:     34–35°
Pavilion angle:  40.75°± (~40.6–41.0° range for top grades)
Girdle:          thin to slightly thick

FACET COUNT: 58 total
 Crown: 1 table + 8 bezels + 8 upper half-facets + 8 star facets = 33
 Pavilion: 8 main facets + 16 lower half-facets + 1 culet = 25
```

### Brilliance vs Fire

```
BRILLIANCE: white light returned to viewer's eye
            Maximized by good pavilion angle (light doesn't leak through)
            and large table (wide aperture for light entry)

FIRE:       dispersion — splitting white light into spectral colors
            Diamond dispersion 0.044 → significant fire
            Controlled by crown angle (higher crown = more fire)

TRADEOFF:   Smaller table + higher crown → more fire, less brilliance
            Larger table + shallower crown → more brilliance, less fire
            Ideal cut balances both

GIA cut grades:
Excellent → Very Good → Good → Fair → Poor
Based on: brightness, fire, scintillation (pattern of light/dark),
          weight ratio, durability, polish, symmetry
```

## The Rose Cut

```
ROSE CUT
=========
                    Apex
                     /\
                    /  \
                   / 24 \   facets rise to single point (no table)
                  / trian-\
                 /  gular  \
                /  facets   \
               /─────────────\  Girdle
               |             |
               |    FLAT     |  No pavilion — sits flat on metal
               |    BACK     |
               └─────────────┘

History: Dutch/Indian origins, 16th-17th century
Uses: material conservation (flat diamonds), antique reproductions,
      Georgian-era jewelry, contemporary revival
Properties: face-up appears larger than round brilliant at same weight
            (spread over table); less brilliance (no pavilion reflection);
            intimate, warm light quality
```

## Colored Stone Cutting: Different Goals

Diamond cutting maximizes brilliance (white light return) for a near-colorless stone. Colored stone cutting maximizes saturation and body color.

```
COLORED STONE CONSIDERATIONS
==============================

1. ORIENTATION
   Pleochroic stones must be oriented to show best color face-up
   Tanzanite: blue-violet face-up (not brown or colorless axis)
   Alexandrite: orient for strongest color change
   Tourmaline: cut to minimize unattractive color zones

2. WINDOWING vs EXTINCTION
   Windowing: too shallow pavilion → stone transparent (see-through),
              body color absent when face-down; poor quality cut
   Extinction: too deep pavilion → stone appears dark, lifeless
   Balance: pavilion depth calibrated to stone's RI and desired tone

<!-- @editor[content/P3]: This angle-by-RI list would be stronger as a table with species examples per RI band — easier to look up at the bench -->
3. STANDARD ANGLES BY RI
   Low RI (1.55–1.65): steeper pavilion needed (43–45°)
   Medium RI (1.70–1.80): 41–43°
   High RI (1.90+): shallower (38–40°) — tourmaline, zircon

4. WEIGHT vs BEAUTY
   Commercial cutting: maximize carat weight (native cutting in
   source countries often prioritizes weight retention)
   Custom cutting: sacrifice weight for beauty (precision faceting
   in Germany, US, Thailand for collector market)
```

## The Diamond Cutting Process

```
STEP 1: PLANNING
  3D laser scan of rough
  Computerized yield optimization (Sarin, OGI Systems software)
  Identifies optimal orientation: minimize inclusions face-up,
  maximize weight, respect grain direction

STEP 2: MARKING
  Scribe orientation lines on rough

STEP 3: CLEAVING OR SAWING
  Cleaving (historical): split along octahedral cleavage plane;
                          single mallet blow — irreversible
  Laser sawing (modern): more control; creates "kerf" (loss of material)
  Bruting/girdle shaping: two stones rotated against each other
                          (traditional) or laser bruting (modern)
  Creates circular girdle outline

STEP 4: BLOCKING
  Polishing wheel (scaife) charged with diamond grit in oil
  Place 8 main crown facets + 8 main pavilion facets (17 total with table)
  Forms the basic shape

STEP 5: BRILLIANTEERING
  Add all remaining 41 facets
  Exact angles critical — measured with Sarin/OGI scope
  Each facet polished individually

STEP 6: FINISHING
  Final polish; quality check under microscope
  Laser inscription of GIA report number on girdle
  Grading submission to GIA/AGS
```

## Gem Carving (Glyptics)

Ancient carved gems served as seals, amulets, and portraits.

```
TYPES
 Intaglio: design carved into the stone (recessed)
           Used as seal rings — pressed into wax
           Greek/Roman tradition; Babylonian cylinder seals precede

 Cameo:    design carved in relief from upper layer
           Typically banded agate, shell, sardonyx, or onyx
           Upper layer: figure; lower layer: background (contrast)
           Roman portraits, mythological scenes most common

TOOLS: rotating copper or iron wheel + diamond/emery powder
       Later: diamond-tipped burins under magnification
Modern: pneumatic engravers (GRS systems), CNC

Key collections: British Museum (Gemma Augustea), Vienna
                 Kunsthistorisches Museum, Hermitage
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Ideal cut" isn't a universal standard**:
GIA uses Excellent/Very Good/Good/Fair/Poor. AGS uses 0–10. "Hearts and arrows" = informal descriptor for precision-cut round brilliants showing 8 hearts + 8 arrows in special viewer. "Triple Excellent" = GIA Ex cut + Ex polish + Ex symmetry. These are marketing terms layered on top of the grading standards.

**Same carat weight ≠ same size**:
A 1 ct oval cut appears larger face-up than a 1 ct round brilliant of the same weight — ovals have less depth. A "spready" round with shallow cut appears large but leaks light (windowing). Size appearance is about table diameter and depth ratio, not just carat.

**Lab-grown diamonds: same cut grades**:
GIA grades lab-grown diamonds exactly as natural diamonds. A lab-grown GIA Excellent triple-X cut is optically identical to a natural GIA Excellent triple-X.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Cut Choice |
|------|------------|
| Maximum brilliance (diamond) | Round brilliant, GIA Excellent |
| Finger-elongating effect | Oval, marquise, pear |
| Vintage appearance | Cushion, rose cut, old European cut |
| Clean lines / geometric | Emerald cut, asscher |
| Maximum face-up size for budget | Oval or cushion (less depth = more spread) |
| Best for inclusions visible stone | Avoid emerald cut; use brilliant cut |
| Opal/star ruby/chatoyant gem | Cabochon required |
| Colored stone — best color | Optimize for orientation, not brilliance formula |
