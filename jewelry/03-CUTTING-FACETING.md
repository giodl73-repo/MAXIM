# 03 — Cutting & Faceting

## Lapidary, Brilliant Cut, Cabochon — Geometry of Light

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Why cutting matters**: a rough diamond has no sparkle; cutting creates flat reflective surfaces (facets) at precise angles that maximize internal reflection and light return to the eye; the optics of a cut stone depend on total internal reflection (light hitting a facet at greater than the critical angle is reflected rather than transmitted); diamond's critical angle ~24.4° (very low — due to high refractive index 2.417) allows very efficient light trapping; this is why diamond is so brilliant: wide range of angles internally reflect
- **Cabochon (cab)**: the oldest cutting form; smooth, dome-shaped top, flat bottom; no facets; used for opaque, translucent, or inclusion-rich stones that show optical phenomena: star rubies/sapphires (asterism), cat's-eye chrysoberyl (chatoyancy — parallel needle inclusions reflect as single band of light), labradorite (labradorescence), moonstone (adularescence — blue shimmer), opals; also traditional for lower-quality stones; shape options: round, oval, pear, rectangular (cushion); dome height is "calibration" — low dome vs high dome
- **Faceted cuts — the brilliant cut**: developed progressively from the mid-17th century (early brilliant cuts by Vincenzo Peruzzi, Venice, ~1700); the **round brilliant** is the most analyzed cut in history; standard modern brilliant = 58 facets (33 crown + 25 pavilion, sometimes including culet); crown angle ~34-35°, pavilion angle ~40.75°, table 53-57%, depth 59-62.5% — these produce maximum brilliance; GIA cut grades Excellent → Very Good → Good → Fair → Poor; AGS 0-10 scale
  - **Fire vs brilliance**: brilliance = white light return; fire = dispersion of white light into spectral colors (diamond has dispersion 0.044 — one of the highest for transparent minerals); cut that maximizes brilliance can slightly reduce fire and vice versa; ideal cut balances both
  - **Fancy shapes**: princess (square modified brilliant — 57-76 facets); oval (modified brilliant — similar optical performance); cushion (antique brilliant, pillow shape); emerald cut (step cut, rectangular, truncated corners — emphasizes clarity over brilliance; shows inclusions more readily); asscher (square emerald cut); marquise (navette — elongated oval points); pear; heart; radiant (mix of brilliant and step cut)
- **Step cuts**: facets run parallel to girdle in concentric rectangular rows; emerald cut, asscher, baguette (small rectangular step cut used as side stones); less brilliance than brilliant cut; "hall of mirrors" effect; highlights color and clarity rather than dispersion; preferred for colored stones and clean diamonds
- **Rose cut**: dome-shaped with flat base and facets that rise to a single apex; 3 to 24 triangular facets; Dutch and Indian origins (16th-17th century); no pavilion (no depth below girdle); used for smaller diamonds when material conservation important; fashionable in Georgian and Victorian jewelry; contemporary revival in antique reproductions; face-up appearance larger than round brilliant of same carat weight
- **The lapidary's process**:
  1. **Grading and planning**: evaluating rough for yield; major inclusions mapped; orientation chosen to minimize visible flaws and maximize weight; for diamonds, fluorescence, grain, and inclusion types inform direction
  2. **Cleaving or sawing**: along cleavage planes (diamonds) or with diamond saw; splitting rough into suitable pieces
  3. **Bruting (girdle shaping)**: two diamonds rotated against each other (traditional) or laser bruting (modern) to create circular girdle
  4. **Blocking**: placing main facets; 17 facets (table + 8 bezel + 8 pavilion main facets) creating the basic form
  5. **Brillianteering**: adding the remaining small facets (upper and lower girdle facets, star facets) using polishing wheel charged with diamond powder; exact angles critical
  6. **Polishing**: final polish on lapping wheel; quality of polish affects surface reflectivity
- **Diamond cutting centers**: historically Amsterdam and Antwerp (since 15th century); Surat, India (now dominant — 90%+ of world's diamonds cut in India, primarily small stones); New York; Tel Aviv (high-end, large stones); cutting skill hierarchy: small melee = India, large/fancy = Belgium/Israel/NYC
- **Colored stone cutting**: different from diamond cutting — colored stones cut to maximize color, not brilliance; refractive index and birefringence considerations; tourmaline cut against certain orientations to reduce color zoning visibility; pleochroic stones (tanzanite, alexandrite) cut to show the most desired color face-up; windowing (see-through table with no reflection — poor cut) vs extinction (too dark) balance
- **Gem carving (glyptics)**: engraving gems into intaglios (recessed) or cameos (raised relief); ancient art from Mesopotamia and Egypt; gem engravers (glypticians) used rotating copper wheels and diamond points; Greek and Roman intaglio seals used for wax seals; cameo = typically banded agate, shell, or sardonyx — image carved in relief from upper layer, contrasting lower layer shows as background
