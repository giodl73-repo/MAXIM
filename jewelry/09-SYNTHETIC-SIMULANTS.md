# 09 — Synthetic & Simulants

## Lab-Grown Diamonds, CVD, HPHT, Cubic Zirconia, Moissanite

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Simulants vs synthetics — the critical distinction**: simulant = looks similar to the natural gem but has different chemical composition and properties (e.g., CZ simulates diamond but is ZrO₂, not carbon); synthetic (lab-grown) = same chemical composition and crystal structure as the natural gem, produced in a laboratory (e.g., lab-grown diamond IS diamond — chemically, crystallographically, physically identical); simulants are always disclosed as imitations; synthetics are legally real gems but must be disclosed as lab-grown per FTC regulations
- **Lab-grown diamonds — HPHT (High Pressure, High Temperature)**: replicates the conditions under which natural diamond forms in the mantle; graphite or diamond seed + metal catalyst (usually iron/cobalt/nickel alloy) at 5-6 GPa and 1,300-1,600°C; used since 1954 (GE first synthesis — initially only industrial grit); gem-quality HPHT production perfected in 1990s-2000s; characteristic inclusions: metallic inclusions (iron/nickel — magnetic!), "flux" inclusions; common colors from HPHT: yellow (nitrogen), near-colorless, blue; large crystals (up to 10 ct+); cuboctahedral or cubic crystal habit (vs natural diamond's octahedral)
- **Lab-grown diamonds — CVD (Chemical Vapor Deposition)**: hydrocarbon gas (methane + hydrogen) ionized in plasma chamber at low pressure; carbon atoms deposit on a seed diamond substrate layer by layer; lower pressure/temperature than HPHT; thin plates grown; may show graining (columnar growth pattern visible under magnification); characteristic: brown/gray color as-grown (from vacancy clusters) → post-growth HPHT treatment to achieve colorlessness or fancy pink/blue; "Type IIa" characteristics common (minimal nitrogen); largest commercial producers: Element Six (De Beers), IIa Technologies, Fenix, Pure Grown Diamonds
- **Detection of lab-grown diamonds**: gem labs (GIA, GCAL, IGI) have developed sophisticated detection tools; HPHT: growth sectors visible in UV fluorescence (cuboctahedral pattern); metallic inclusions; strain pattern under polarized light; CVD: strain pattern; graining; phosphorescence (long afterglow — unusual in natural diamond); spectroscopic signatures (FTIR — nitrogen aggregation patterns differ; photoluminescence at 737nm Si-vacancy center common in CVD); De Beers' Synth­ia and Gem-A equipment; GIA's iD100 automated screening tool; DiamondView (UV imaging) used by labs
- **Economic disruption of lab-grown diamonds**: mined diamond polished prices dropped 50-60% 2022-2024 partially attributed to lab-grown supply; lab-grown now ~$50-80/ct vs $5,000-15,000/ct for equivalent mined stones; De Beers launched its own lab-grown brand (Lightbox, 2018) — deliberately pricing at $800/ct fixed to position as "fashion jewelry, not heirloom"; significant market debate: will lab-grown eliminate diamond as investment/heirloom object?; mined diamond companies (De Beers, Alrosa) emphasize natural rarity; lab-grown proponents emphasize identical physical/chemical properties
- **Lab-grown colored stones**: corundum (ruby and sapphire) grown by: Verneuil process (flame fusion, 1902 — Auguste Verneuil; molten alumina powder drops onto seed — fast, cheap; produces "boules"; characteristic curved growth striae visible under microscope at 10× — key detection feature); Czochralski (crystal pulling from melt); hydrothermal (high-pressure water + nutrients, slow growth, best quality — resembles natural growth); flux growth (dissolved in molten salt); lab-grown ruby/sapphire widely used in watchmaking (synthetic corundum bearings), industrial applications, and jewelry at much lower cost
- **Lab-grown emerald**: hydrothermal synthesis (Tairus, Russia — "hydrothermal emeralds" with natural-looking inclusions) and flux synthesis (Chatham, Gilson); distinguishable by characteristic inclusions: phenakite or flux feathers vs natural jardin; two-phase inclusions (liquid + vapor in natural) vs one-phase (liquid only) in some synthetics; RI and specific gravity identical; iron content (natural emeralds have Fe from host rock; some synthetics lack Fe → Chelsea filter difference)
- **Simulants — cubic zirconia (CZ)**: ZrO₂ stabilized with Y₂O₃; hardness 8-8.5; RI 2.15-2.18 (close to diamond 2.417 but lower dispersion 0.058 vs diamond 0.044); high dispersion creates more fire than diamond; flawless, colorless; gives away as non-diamond by: over-sparkly fire, lower heft (density 5.6-6.0 g/cm³ vs diamond 3.51), thermal conductivity (diamond thermal conductor — diamond testers use thermal conductivity; CZ fails thermal test); produced industrially in tons; current price essentially zero; Swarovski = premium brand CZ with proprietary cutting
- **Moissanite (SiC)**: silicon carbide; originally discovered in 1893 by Henri Moissan in meteorite crater (Canyon Diablo, Arizona); natural moissanite extremely rare; synthetic moissanite (Charles & Colvard, 1990s) marketed as diamond alternative; hardness 9.25; RI 2.65-2.69 (HIGHER than diamond); dispersion 0.104 (more fire than diamond — rainbow effect at certain angles); thermal conductivity similar to diamond → FAILS thermal diamond tester (marketed as advantage — "passes" the thermal test but detectable by RI or dedicated moissanite tester); deliberately positioned as ethical alternative by sustainability-focused consumers; much cheaper than diamond but significantly more expensive than CZ
- **Other simulants historically**: rock crystal (quartz) for diamond (10× harder than quartz, but quartz used for centuries as simulant); paste (lead crystal glass — Georgian era paste jewelry was not considered inferior, worn by aristocrats; high lead content → high RI and dispersion); white topaz; white sapphire (lab-grown); synthetic spinel (colorless); glass (rhinestone — Herkimer NY quartz originally, now glass; Swarovski rhinestones)
