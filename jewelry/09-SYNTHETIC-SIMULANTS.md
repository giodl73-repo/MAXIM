# Synthetic & Simulants — Lab-Grown Diamonds, CVD, HPHT, CZ, Moissanite

## The Big Picture

The critical distinction: a **synthetic** has the same composition as the natural gem (a lab-grown diamond IS carbon in cubic crystal form). A **simulant** looks similar but has different chemistry (CZ is ZrO₂, not carbon). The disruption is mostly in synthetics — where the physical object is identical to the natural but the production method is industrial.

## Lab-Grown Diamonds: HPHT

Replicates conditions under which natural diamond forms in the earth's mantle (~150–200km deep, ~5 GPa, ~1300°C).

```
HPHT PROCESS
=============

Components:
 - Small diamond seed crystal
 - Metal catalyst (iron/nickel/cobalt alloy — the "flux")
 - Graphite or diamond powder (carbon source)

Conditions:
 Pressure: 5–6 GPa (50,000–60,000 atmospheres)
 Temperature: 1,300–1,600°C
 Duration: days to weeks depending on target size

Press types:
 Belt press: GE's original design (1954 first synthesis)
 Cubic press: 6 anvils from 6 directions; used in China/Russia
 BARS press (Russia): more precise pressure control

Crystal habit:
 Cuboctahedral or cubic (octahedral + cube faces)
 vs natural diamond's octahedral or macle shapes
 → growth sectors visible under DiamondView UV imaging

Characteristic inclusions:
 Metallic inclusions (Fe, Ni, Co) from the flux catalyst
 These inclusions are MAGNETIC — HPHT lab diamonds can be
 weakly attracted to a strong neodymium magnet (natural and CVD
 diamonds are not magnetic)

Color:
 Yellow (type Ib nitrogen) — earliest HPHT; still common
 Near-colorless: difficult; requires N scavenging (titanium or aluminum)
 Blue: boron addition → type IIb (identical to Hope Diamond chemistry)
 Post-growth HPHT of CVD: removes brown → colorless or pink/blue
```

## Lab-Grown Diamonds: CVD

Chemical Vapor Deposition grows diamond at low pressure from a hydrocarbon gas plasma.

```
CVD PROCESS
===========

Chamber: microwave plasma reactor or hot-filament reactor
Gas:     methane (CH₄) + hydrogen (H₂) at low pressure (~100 torr)
Plasma:  microwave ionizes gas → carbon radicals form
Substrate: diamond seed plate (IIa quality, usually laser-cut slab)

Growth:
 Carbon atoms deposit layer by layer on seed surface
 H₂ etches away graphite preferentially → only diamond remains
 Growth rate: ~0.5–2 carats per day per reactor
 Shape: flat plates (slab growth) — not octahedra
 Orientation: [100] growth common

Post-growth challenges:
 Color: as-grown CVD is often brown/gray (due to vacancy clusters,
        interstitial carbon, and strain from columnar growth)
 Treatment: HPHT treatment (~2000°C, ~5GPa) of as-grown CVD diamond
            eliminates brown → colorless or produces:
            - Colorless (high demand for engagement rings)
            - Pink (very valuable fancy; Fe-vacancy centers)
            - Blue (boron addition during growth)

Notable characteristic:
 Si-vacancy centers: silicon contamination from chamber walls →
                     characteristic photoluminescence at 737nm
                     Very rare in natural diamond
                     Detectable by photoluminescence spectroscopy
```

## Detection of Lab-Grown Diamonds

Detection is an arms race. Detection tools advance; growth techniques advance to avoid detection signatures.

```
DETECTION METHODS
==================

VISUAL/OPTICAL
 Strain pattern under crossed polarizers:
  CVD: columnar strain (vertical pattern)
  HPHT: cubic/octahedral growth sectors
  Natural: irregular/absent

 UV fluorescence (DiamondView):
  HPHT: cuboctahedral UV pattern (multiple growth sectors visible)
  CVD: tabular growth sectors; strong phosphorescence (afterglow)
  Natural: no phosphorescence; irregular fluorescence

 Phosphorescence:
  CVD lab-grown: long afterglow after UV exposure (seconds to minutes)
  Natural diamond: almost never phosphoresces (rare exception)
  HPHT: generally no phosphorescence

SPECTROSCOPIC
 FTIR spectroscopy:
  Natural: Type Ia (N aggregates as A and B centers) = most natural diamonds
  CVD: Type IIa (no N) or single N (Ib) — unusual for natural
  HPHT yellow: Type Ib (isolated N) = common in HPHT, rare in natural

 Photoluminescence (PL) at 737nm:
  Si-vacancy (SiV) center → characteristic of CVD; absent in most naturals
  Low concentration doesn't prevent visible detection by lab equipment

 Raman spectroscopy: both show diamond peak at 1332 cm⁻¹
                      Does NOT distinguish; confirms it's diamond

PHYSICAL
 Metallic inclusions: Fe/Ni/Co in HPHT (magnetic test works for many)
 Graining in CVD: visible under high magnification (columnar growth)

INSTRUMENTS
 GIA iD100: automated device; 30-second screening; ~99% accurate
 De Beers Synthia: professional screening
 DiamondView: UV fluorescence imaging; gold standard for lab use
 Gemological labs (GIA, GCAL, IGI, HRD): definitive grading reports
              that state "Laboratory-Grown" or "Natural"
```

## Economic Disruption

```
PRICE COMPARISON (per carat, polished, 2024)
============================================

Quality point        Natural         Lab-grown
============         =======         =========
1ct G/VS1 (round)   ~$5,000–8,000   ~$500–800
2ct F/VS2           ~$18,000–25,000 ~$1,500–2,500
0.5ct E/VVS2        ~$2,000–3,000   ~$200–300
5ct fancy pink       ~$1M+           ~$20,000–50,000

Price decline rate (lab-grown): ~15–20% per year
 2020: ~$3,000–5,000/ct polished lab round
 2022: ~$1,000–2,000/ct
 2024: ~$200–800/ct
 Forecast: converging toward production cost floor ~$50–100/ct

KEY MARKET DYNAMICS
 De Beers launched Lightbox (2018): lab-grown at fixed $800/ct
  Strategy: signal lab = fashion, not investment/heirloom
  Cut the price floor deliberately to devalue the lab-grown category

 De Beers then collapsed their own lab-grown pricing further →
  Lightbox pivot: stopped emphasizing lab; refocused on natural rarity

 Mined diamond prices: down 20–40% 2022–2024
  Industry debate: is lab-grown causing this or other factors?
  (China demand slowdown, macro economic, both)

 Lab-grown resale: effectively zero
  A lab diamond purchased for $500/ct resells for essentially nothing
  Natural diamond resale: 30–60% of purchase price (declining market)
```

## Simulants

### Cubic Zirconia (CZ)

```
CUBIC ZIRCONIA
==============
Composition: ZrO₂ stabilized with Y₂O₃ (yttria) or CaO
Crystal:     Cubic (isometric) — like diamond
Hardness:    8–8.5 Mohs (harder than many gemstones, not diamond)
Density:     5.6–6.0 g/cm³ (denser than diamond 3.52 — heavier feel)
RI:          2.15–2.18 (lower than diamond 2.417)
Dispersion:  0.058–0.066 (higher than diamond 0.044 → more "fire")

HOW IT'S DISTINGUISHED FROM DIAMOND:
1. Thermal conductivity: diamond = best conductor; CZ = poor conductor
   Diamond tester (Presidium) detects by thermal conductivity
   CZ fails → reads as non-diamond

2. Density: CZ heavier than diamond; 1ct CZ is smaller than 1ct diamond
            (same carat weight = smaller physical stone if CZ)

3. Dispersion: CZ shows more rainbow fire than diamond under direct light
               Looks "too sparkly" to trained eye

4. Cost: essentially zero — commodity production, unlimited supply
         Swarovski = premium branded CZ with proprietary precision cutting

5. Under microscope: no inclusions (too perfect); flat facet junctions
                      (diamond facet junctions show slight wear polish)
```

### Moissanite

```
MOISSANITE
===========
Composition: Silicon carbide (SiC)
Discovery:   Henri Moissan, 1893, Canyon Diablo meteor crater (Arizona)
             Initially thought to be diamond; later identified as SiC
             Natural moissanite: extremely rare (only in meteorites,
             kimberlite, silicon carbide mineral inclusions)
Synthesis:   Charles & Colvard, 1990s (Lely process then modified)
             Now multiple manufacturers

Properties:
 Hardness:    9.25 Mohs (harder than any mineral except diamond)
 Density:     3.21 g/cm³ (slightly less dense than diamond 3.52)
 RI:          2.65–2.69 (HIGHER than diamond 2.417)
 Dispersion:  0.104 (over 2× diamond's 0.044 → VERY high fire/rainbow)
 Thermal:     High thermal conductivity (similar to diamond)

THE THERMAL TEST ISSUE:
 Diamond testers use thermal conductivity — moissanite thermal conductivity
 is similar to diamond → moissanite PASSES the standard diamond tester
 (reads as diamond)
 Requires: dedicated moissanite tester (measures electrical conductivity
           — moissanite is a semiconductor; diamond is an insulator)
           OR RI measurement (RI 2.65+ = not diamond)
           OR birefringence (moissanite shows double refraction; diamond cubic = none)

MARKET POSITIONING:
 Deliberately marketed as ethical/sustainable alternative
 Price: ~$400–600/ct vs lab diamond ~$200–500/ct vs natural ~$5,000+/ct
 Some consumers actively choose moissanite over both
 Strong "moissanite community" online

DETECTION:
 Double refraction (birefringence): cubic diamond = single refraction;
                                    hexagonal SiC = double refraction
                                    Visible under 10× loupe as "doubling"
                                    of back facet edges
 RI: RI > 2.417 = not diamond (moissanite ~2.65–2.69)
 Dedicated tester: electrical conductivity distinguishes from diamond
```

### Historical Simulants

```
GLASS / PASTE
=============
Lead crystal (flint glass): PbO added to glass → RI ~1.7–1.9+
                             High dispersion → significant fire
                             Strass paste: patented by Georg Friedrich Strass
                             ~1730; finest paste; high Pb content
Georgian-era paste: NOT considered inferior; aristocracy wore paste
                    alongside real gems deliberately (safety while traveling,
                    fashion experimentation)
Modern rhinestone: glass; cut; low-end fashion
Swarovski crystal: precision-cut glass; controlled RI; proprietary coating

WHITE TOPAZ (lab-grown)
 Al₂SiO₄(F,OH)₂; colorless; RI 1.619–1.627; hardness 8
 Common simulant in silver jewelry; obvious with loupe (lower brilliance)
 Perfect basal cleavage — wears and chips

YAG / GGG (1960s–70s)
 Yttrium aluminum garnet: RI 1.83; dispersion poor; now obsolete
 Replaced by CZ in the 1970s–80s due to CZ's much better optical properties

ROCK CRYSTAL (natural quartz)
 Used as diamond simulant for centuries; Greek/Roman period
 RI ~1.544; much lower than diamond; obvious under loupe
 Historical jewelry: "rock crystal" pendants common through 19th c.
```

## Synthetic Colored Gemstones

```
SYNTHETIC RUBY/SAPPHIRE (Corundum Al₂O₃)
==========================================

Verneuil process (1902 — Auguste Verneuil):
 Flame fusion: Al₂O₃ powder fed through oxyhydrogen flame
               Drips onto rotating seed → "boule" grows
 Fast (1 boule/hour); cheap; produces large quantities
 Characteristic: curved growth striae (curved lines under microscope
                 at 10×) — KEY detection feature; natural has straight
 Used in: watchmaking (synthetic ruby bearings since 1900s),
          industrial abrasives, cheap jewelry

Czochralski (crystal pulling):
 Seed crystal dipped in melt; slowly pulled while rotating
 Good quality; used for larger stones; still curved striae somewhat

Hydrothermal:
 High-pressure water + dissolved nutrients + seed
 Slow; expensive; best quality
 Hydrothermal ruby/sapphire (Tairus, Russia) has natural-looking
 inclusions; harder to detect

Flux growth:
 Dissolved in molten flux salt at high temperature
 Flux inclusions visible under microscope (feathers, fingerprints)

DETECTION:
 Curved striae (Verneuil) — definitive
 Inclusion type: gas bubbles, flux feathers, phenakite, irregular
                 (different from natural silk/fingerprint inclusions)
 RI same; SG same → only microscope and spectroscopy distinguish

SYNTHETIC EMERALD
 Chatham (US): flux grown; "Chatham emeralds" known since 1940s
 Gilson (France): flux and hydrothermal
 Tairus (Russia): hydrothermal; natural-like inclusions
 Detection: iron content (natural emeralds contain Fe from host rock;
            some synthetics lack Fe → Chelsea filter behavior differs);
            inclusion types; RI matches; growth patterns
```

## Common Confusion Points

**CVD and HPHT both produce "real" diamonds**:
Both produce genuine diamond (C, cubic crystal, sp³ bonds). The FTC changed its regulations in 2018 to remove "natural" from the definition of diamond — diamond means the material, regardless of origin. "Natural diamond" and "laboratory-grown diamond" are the correct terms.

**Lab-grown diamond resale value**:
Lab-grown diamond has effectively zero resale value because supply is unlimited and price is declining. A natural diamond also has poor resale (30–60% of retail) but historically better than lab-grown. This is critical for engagement ring decisions.

**Moissanite fools diamond testers**:
Standard thermal conductivity diamond testers (Presidium) will register moissanite as diamond. Dedicated moissanite testers or gemological instruments are needed. The 10× loupe test (doubling of facet edges from birefringence) is the simplest manual check.

**"Conflict-free" lab diamonds**:
Lab diamonds avoid Kimberley Process issues but have their own supply chain concerns (energy use, labor in cutting centers, etc.). "Conflict-free" is not automatically synonymous with "ethical."

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Choice |
|------|--------|
| Budget diamond alternative | CZ (zero cost, looks fine) |
| Diamond alternative that passes thermal tester | Moissanite (but birefringent) |
| Ethical/sustainable choice, diamond look | Lab-grown diamond |
| Identical physical properties to natural diamond | Lab-grown diamond (CVD or HPHT) |
| Heirloom/investment value | Natural mined diamond |
| Maximum fire/rainbow effect | Moissanite (dispersion 0.104) |
| Colored gem on a budget | Lab-grown corundum (ruby/sapphire) |
| Vintage "paste" jewelry look | Lead crystal / Strass rhinestone |
| Independent of mined supply chain | Lab-grown any species |
| Largest stone per dollar | CZ (effectively free) or moissanite |
