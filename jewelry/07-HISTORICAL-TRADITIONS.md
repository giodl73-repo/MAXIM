# 07 — Historical Traditions

## Egyptian, Mughal, Byzantine, Art Nouveau/Deco — Jewelry Through Civilization

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Ancient Egypt** (~3000 BCE – 30 BCE): the foundational fine jewelry tradition; gold = flesh of the gods (Ra's skin); lapis lazuli (imported from Badakhshan, Afghanistan), carnelian, turquoise (Sinai Peninsula) = sacred stones; faience (glazed composition material — silica + copper or cobalt colorant; not technically ceramic) = inexpensive substitute for precious stones in amulets and funerary jewelry; shabtis and amulets more common than jewelry as deposits; techniques: cloisonné inlay (gold cells filled with cut stone slabs or glass), granulation, repousse, wire working; Tutankhamun's tomb (1323 BCE) — finest preserved collection: pectoral collars, usekh collars (wide beaded collar), diadem, pectorals (breastplate ornaments with falcon/scarab motifs); lapis lazuli availability = primary reason for trade relationships between Egypt and Mesopotamia
- **Ancient Greece and Rome**: Greek jewelry (600-300 BCE): gold filigree and granulation at highest mastery; wreath crowns (gold leaves); Hellenistic period (323-31 BCE) — more elaborate, colored stones added; Roman jewelry: cameos and intaglios (glyptic tradition); gold snake bracelets (ubiquitous Roman motif — ouroboros); pompeii finds preserve domestic jewelry of middle classes; Roman signet rings for seal impressions; Roman taste ran to colored stones: emerald, sapphire, garnet, amethyst, pearl
- **Byzantine** (~300-1453 CE): Constantinople as synthesis of Roman/Greek/Eastern traditions; characterized by polychrome cloisonné enamel on gold (the definitive Byzantine jewelry style); Justinian and Theodora mosaics (Ravenna) depict elaborate imperial jewelry; gold collars, pendants with hanging chains and pendilia; repoussé and opus interrasile (pierced goldwork with punched or drilled patterns — lace-like gold); religious iconography central (crosses, saints); Byzantine tradition passed to Orthodox Christian world (Russian, Serbian, Georgian goldsmithing)
- **Islamic world** (~700-1800 CE): Moorish Spain (Al-Andalus) tradition of filigree and gold work; Fatimid Cairo gold jewelry; Ottoman Empire jewelry — diamonds, rubies, emeralds, turquoise in elaborate settings; Mughal Empire India (1526-1857): arguably the greatest synthesis of jewelry traditions — Mughal emperors were obsessive gem collectors (Babur, Jahangir, Shah Jahan — who had gems collected from all over the world); Mughal jadau technique (uncut/foil-backed stones set in gold without solder — stone seats, not prong-set); Kundan setting (highly refined Indian setting of uncut gemstones in 24K gold foil); meenakari (enamel painting on reverse of kundan pieces — invisible when worn but revealed on inspection); Jaipur the center of Mughal/Rajput jewelry tradition; the Koh-i-Noor diamond's history traces Mughal → Persian → Afghan → Sikh → British history
- **European Renaissance and Baroque** (1400-1700): portraiture as primary evidence; Benvenuto Cellini (1500-1571) — greatest goldsmith of the Renaissance, author of influential *Treatise on Goldsmithing*; pendants with enameled figurative scenes (Italian/German); Spanish colonial wealth → influx of emeralds and pearls (Baroque pearl = baroque = irregularly shaped); Dutch Golden Age jewelry (Vermeer portraits show contemporary jewelry)
- **Georgian period** (1714-1830): closed-back settings (metal behind stone to reflect light from behind in candlelight — diamonds look brighter); foil-backed colored stones (silver-backed for diamonds, gold-backed for colored); Pinchbeck (zinc-copper alloy resembling gold, invented by Christopher Pinchbeck c.1720); hairwork jewelry (sentimental — hair of deceased loved one woven into brooches/lockets); paste jewelry (glass gem simulants — high-quality examples were not considered inferior, worn by aristocracy)
- **Victorian** (1837-1901): mourning jewelry (jet — fossilized wood from Whitby, Yorkshire; vulcanite; black glass; hair); sentimental jewelry (acrostic bracelets: first letter of each stone spells word — e.g., Ruby, Emerald, Garnet, Amethyst, Ruby, Diamond = REGARD); cameos; archaeological revival (discovery of Pompeii, Egyptian obelisks → Egyptomania); naturalistic motifs (flowers, insects, serpents); Albert chains (watch fobs named after Prince Albert); seed pearls; carbuncles (star rubies/garnets in cabochon)
- **Art Nouveau** (~1890-1910): reaction against industrial mass production; nature as subject (insects, female figures, flowers, flowing lines); René Lalique — master of the style; plique-à-jour enamel, glass, horn, ivory incorporated (non-precious materials elevated); feminine flowing lines; dragonflies, orchids, women with flowing hair; American equivalent: Louis Comfort Tiffany; center: Paris
- **Art Deco** (~1920-1935): reaction to Art Nouveau's curvilinear excess; geometric abstraction; influence of Cubism, Jazz, Egyptian revival (Tutankhamun 1922), Japanese art; platinum as dominant metal (allowed thinner, lighter settings); calibré-cut colored stones in geometric outlines; black and white (onyx + diamond), vivid color combinations; Cartier, Van Cleef & Arpels, Boucheron as defining makers; jabot pins, brooches, sautoir (long necklace), clips; the period where diamond's modern cultural dominance was cemented
- **Post-WWII to contemporary**: Danish modernism (Georg Jensen — clean silver forms); Italian design tradition (Bulgari — colored stones, bold gold); American studio jewelry movement (Art Smith, Sam Kramer — avant-garde); Scandinavian functionalism; contemporary: 3D printing enabling new forms; titanium and alternative metals for men; sustainable/ethical sourcing movements
