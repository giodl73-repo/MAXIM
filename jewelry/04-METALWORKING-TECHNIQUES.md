# 04 — Metalworking Techniques

## Lost Wax Casting, Forging, Filigree, Granulation — Bench Craft

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Lost wax casting (cire perdue)**: oldest and most versatile casting technique for jewelry; 5,000+ year history (earliest: Harappan civilization dancing girl, ~2500 BCE; also ancient Egypt, pre-Columbian Americas independently); modern process: carve/model in wax → invest in plaster/ceramic → burn out wax in kiln (wax lost → cavity remains) → centrifugally or vacuum cast molten metal into cavity → quench → break investment → clean; allows very complex 3D forms; standard in commercial jewelry production (rubber molds from master model → multiple wax injections → tree of wax sprues → bulk casting)
  - Wax types: hard wax (carving wax — Ferris file-a-wax, Freeman wax; dense, holds detail); soft wax (injection wax — for filling rubber molds); wax sheet and wire (for building up forms)
  - Investment: plaster-based (traditional — calcium sulfate hemihydrate + silica for heat resistance); vacuum mixing to remove air bubbles; burnout kiln schedule: ramp to 1350°F, hold 2+ hours; flask size and orientation affect fill
  - Casting methods: centrifugal (flask on rotating arm — momentum drives metal); vacuum (vacuum chamber below flask — atmospheric pressure pushes metal); steam casting (simple, low-tech — steam pressure); gravity (simple pour, for large pieces)
- **Fabrication (constructed/fabricated jewelry)**: building a piece from sheet, wire, tube, and findings through cutting, forming, and soldering; the alternative to casting; different aesthetic (crisper lines, thinner walls possible); traditional fine jewelry fabrication vs cast
  - Sheet metal work: rolling mill (reduces thickness, also creates texture with pattern rollers); annealing; cutting with jeweler's saw (piercing saw + flexible blade, 80 teeth/inch for fine work); filing; sanding
  - Wire and tube drawing: pulling metal through progressively smaller draw plate holes; produces round, square, half-round, twisted wire; tube formed from sheet strip soldered and drawn
  - Forming: dapping (dome punches + dapping block to form domes); stakes and mandrels; chasing and repoussé (see below)
- **Soldering**: joining metal with lower-melting-point filler metal (solder); jewelry soldering distinct from plumbing — all precious metal soldering uses alloy-matched solder; gold solder: plumb solder = same karat as piece; solder grades by flow temperature: hard, medium, easy, extra-easy (use hard first, successive operations use progressively lower-flowing solders to avoid re-flowing earlier joins); flux (borax + water paste, or liquid fluoride flux) prevents oxidation; torch types: acetylene/air (jeweler's torch), acetylene/oxygen (higher heat for platinum, also propane/oxygen)
  - Platinum soldering: much higher temperatures (1,400-1,700°C range); requires oxygen/hydrogen or acetylene/oxygen; clean technique — platinum picks up carbon contamination (discolors, embrittles); all adjacent stones must be removed
- **Chasing and repoussé**: working metal from both sides; repoussé = pushing metal up from reverse with punches (creates raised design); chasing = refining and detailing from front; pitch bowl (mixture of pitch/plaster/tallow) holds work while malleable; ancient Greek toreutics (elaborately chased silver vessels); Benvenuto Cellini's saltcellar and Perseus among finest historical examples; West African lost-wax + repoussé bronze tradition (Benin bronzes)
- **Filigree**: fine wire (twisted or plain) bent and soldered into lacy openwork patterns; wire as thin as 0.2mm; historical centers: Malta (classic source), Genoa, Catalan Spain, Portugal, Russia (Novgorod), India (Karimnagar), China; wires either applied on a metal base or joined entirely in air (openwork); skill in even soldering without melting the fine wires; historical dating of filigree by wire gauge, twist pattern, and joining method
- **Granulation**: surface decoration with tiny spheres (granules) of metal; Etruscan granulation (600-300 BCE) = finest historical example — granules down to 0.14mm diameter bonded to surface with no visible solder; the technique was lost in antiquity and rediscovered by German archaeologist H.A.P. Littledale in 1930s; mechanism: copper enrichment at contact point (depletion gilding or colloidal hard soldering) → fusion bonding at granule contact point only; modern revival jewelers (John Paul Miller, Castellani)
- **Engraving**: incising a design into metal surface with a graver (burin); hand engraving (push or hammer) vs pneumatic engraving tools; bright-cut engraving (angled cuts that reflect light — Georgian silver decoration); engine turning / guilloche (mechanical lathe patterns under enamel — Fabergé eggs, watch dials)
- **Enameling**: fusing glass powder to metal surface by firing at 750-900°C; several techniques: cloisonné (wire cells define color areas), champlevé (metal gouged/cast to create cells), plique-à-jour (translucent enamel in open cells without backing — "stained glass" effect), limoges / painted enamel (painted overglaze enamel on flat surface), basse-taille (transparent enamel over textured/engraved metal — texture visible through enamel); colorants: copper → green/blue, cobalt → deep blue, manganese → purple, iron → red/brown; lead-based enamels historically, lead-free modern; temperature management — different enamel colors require different firing temperatures and order
