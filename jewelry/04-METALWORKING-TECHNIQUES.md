# Metalworking Techniques — Lost Wax, Forging, Filigree, Granulation

## The Big Picture

Two fundamental approaches: **casting** (pour molten metal into a mold) and **fabrication** (build from sheet, wire, and tube by cutting, forming, and joining). Everything else — filigree, granulation, enameling — is a surface treatment or decorative variation on these two foundations.

```
JEWELRY METALWORKING MAP
==========================

===============                     ===============
                                     Soldering (hard solder — filler metal)
CASTING             FABRICATION      Fusing (no solder — surface diffusion)
=======             ===========      Riveting (mechanical)
                                     Tabs and bezels (mechanical fold)

perdue):           working:          SURFACE DECORATION
 - Traditional      - Rolling mill   ==================
 - Modern centri-   - Sawing         Engraving (incised lines)
   fugal/vacuum     - Filing         Chasing/repoussé (hammered relief)
 - Rubber mold      - Forming        Granulation (fused spheres)
   production                        Filigree (wire openwork)
                   Wire work:        Enameling (fused glass)
Sand casting        - Drawing        Texturing (hammers, rollers)
Cuttlebone          - Twisting       Patina / oxidation
centrifugal         - Winding        Plating (electrochemical)
casting
```

## Lost Wax Casting (Cire Perdue)

5,000+ years of continuous use. The Harappan "Dancing Girl" (~2500 BCE) is the oldest identified example. Pre-Columbian Americas (Mixtec, Inca) arrived at the same process independently.

### Process Flow

```
1. MASTER MODEL
   +-----------+
   | Wax carve |  or  3D print → resin model
   | or sculpt |       (SLA/DLP printers standard now)
   +-----------+
         |
         v
2. MOLD MAKING
   +-----------+
   | Vulcanize |  Surround master in rubber → heat cure
   | rubber    |  Cut mold in half to extract master
   | mold      |  Mold reusable thousands of times
   +-----------+
         |
         v
3. WAX INJECTION
   +-----------+
   | Inject    |  Heated wax injected under pressure
   | wax into  |  into rubber mold
   | mold      |  Multiple wax copies produced
   +-----------+
         |
         v
4. SPRUE TREE
   +-----------+
   | Attach    |  Wax gates (sprues) connect wax pieces
   | wax pieces|  to central wax sprue/button
   | to tree   |  "Christmas tree" of multiple wax pieces
   +-----------+
         |
         v
5. INVESTING
   +-----------+
   | Place in  |  Metal flask; pour investment plaster
   | flask;    |  (calcium sulfate hemihydrate + silica)
   | pour      |  Vacuum to remove air bubbles
   | investment|  Set at room temperature 2–4 hours
   +-----------+
         |
         v
6. BURNOUT (WAX ELIMINATION)
   +-----------+
   | Kiln      |  Ramp: room temp → 300°F → 700°F → 1350°F
   | burnout   |  Wax melts and burns out → cavity remains
   | schedule  |  Hold at 1350°F for 2+ hours
   | 4–12 hrs  |  "Lost wax" = the wax is gone
   +-----------+
         |
         v
7. CASTING
   +-----------+
   | Centrifugal:  Molten metal in crucible on rotating arm
   | or Vacuum:    Flask inverted over vacuum chamber;
   | cast metal    atmospheric pressure pushes metal in
   | into hot      Gravity: simple pour (large pieces)
   | investment    Steam: steam pressure (simple, low-tech)
   +-----------+
         |
         v
8. QUENCH + DEVEST
   +-----------+
   | Quench hot|  Plunge hot flask into water →
   | flask in  |  investment disintegrates (steam explosion)
   | water     |  Retrieve casting on tree
   +-----------+
         |
         v
9. FINISHING
   +-----------+
   | Cut sprues|  Snip/saw off gates; file sprue marks
   | clean,    |  Pickle to remove oxides
   | file,     |  File, sand through grits
   | polish    |  Polishing compounds (tripoli then rouge)
   +-----------+
```

### Wax Types

| Wax | Properties | Use |
|-----|-----------|-----|
| Carving wax (Ferris, Freeman) | Dense, hard, holds crisp detail | Hand carving; files/burs like metal |
| Injection wax | Low-viscosity melt, flows into molds | Rubber mold production |
| Wax sheet | Rolled sheets, various gauges | Building flat components |
| Wax wire | Round/half-round/square profiles | Wire elements, borders |
| Build-up wax | Tacky; bonds to other wax | Adding to existing models |

## Fabricated Jewelry (Sheet, Wire, Tube)

Alternative to casting. Produces crisper, thinner-walled forms impossible to cast reliably.

### Sheet Metal Work

```
Rolling mill:       Two hardened steel rollers, adjustable gap
                    Reduce sheet thickness incrementally
                    Pattern rolling: textured rollers create surface textures
                    Wire rolling: round-oval-flat progressively

Jeweler's saw:      Frame saw + flexible blade
                    "Piercing saw" for internal cutouts
                    Blade grades: 6/0 (finest) → 8 (coarsest)
                    80 teeth/inch = standard silver work; finer = cleaner cut

Filing system:      Needle files (round, half-round, square, triangular,
                    barrette) for detail; hand files for larger areas
                    Grits: coarse → medium → smooth → polishing papers

Dapping:            Dome punches + dapping block
                    Creates hemispherical domes from flat discs
                    Combines two domes → hollow sphere (solder join at equator)
```

### Wire Drawing

```
DRAW PLATE
==========
  Hardened steel plate with progressively smaller holes
  Wire pulled through holes in decreasing size
  Each pass: wire stretches, diameter decreases, work-hardens
  Anneal every 2–3 passes to restore ductility

  Round wire → square hole in draw plate = square wire
  Round wire → half-round channel = half-round wire

  Tube: form strip into U shape → solder seam → draw through round holes
        to close and size; or start with seamless drawn tube stock
```

### Annealing

```
WORK HARDENING AND ANNEALING CYCLE
====================================

Metal cold-worked      Crystal dislocations      Becomes hard,
(bent, rolled,   →     accumulate, slip     →    springy, brittle
hammered)              planes fill
                              ↓
                       ANNEAL (heat + quench)
                              ↓
              Dislocations migrate to grain boundaries,
              annihilate; grain structure relaxes
                              ↓
                       Metal soft and ductile again

Gold: heat to 650–700°C (dull cherry red), quench in water or air cool
Silver: same temperature range; air cool (quench can cause stress)
Copper: 650°C
Platinum: 800–1000°C (bench torch insufficient — needs oxy-acetylene)
```

## Soldering

Joining metals with a lower-melting-point filler alloy (solder). Fundamentally different from plumbing/electrical soldering — all precious metal soldering uses alloy-matched solder (no lead, no tin).

```
SOLDER GRADES BY FLOW TEMPERATURE (gold solder example)
=========================================================

Grade        Flow Temp    When to Use
=====        =========    ===========
IT (extra-hard)  ~820°C  First joins on a complex piece
Hard             ~780°C  Second set of joins
Medium           ~750°C  Third joins; won't re-flow earlier
Easy             ~720°C  Final joins; lowest risk of re-flow
Extra-easy       ~695°C  Repairs only; weakest alloy

Rule: use harder solders first; progressively softer solder grades
      for each subsequent join operation on the same piece.
      Earlier joins won't re-flow when doing later ones.

GOLD SOLDER PURITY ("plumb solder")
  Must match karat of the piece: 18k gold piece → 18k solder
  Otherwise hallmarking is compromised (lower purity alloy in the join)
```

### Flux and Pickle

```
FLUX (prevents oxidation during heating)
  Borax cone + water paste: traditional; buff paste on join
  Liquid flux (fluoride-based): flows further, clean
  Paste flux: stays in place; pre-packaged
  Applied to all surfaces before torch heating

PICKLE (removes oxides after soldering)
  Traditional: 10% sulfuric acid (h₂SO₄) solution (hazardous)
  Modern: sodium bisulfate (Sparex, Rio Grande's Pickle)
           or citric acid (safer; slower)
  Never introduce steel tools or steel binding wire into pickle —
  causes copper plating on silver/gold (electrochemical reaction)
  Use copper, brass, or wooden tongs in pickle only

TORCH TYPES
  Propane/air: jeweler's torch (Blazer, Little Torch)
               Sufficient for silver and gold up to 18k
  Propane/oxygen: higher heat; needed for platinum
  Acetylene/air: Smith little torch or similar — versatile
  Natural gas/air: fixed bench setup in professional shops
```

## Chasing and Repoussé

Two sides of the same technique: working metal in three dimensions from both front and back.

```
REPOUSSÉ (French: pushed back)
  Metal pushed forward (raised) from the reverse side
  Tools: mushroom-shaped pushers, ball punches
  Work surface: pitch bowl (pine pitch + whiting + tallow)
                pitch supports metal, absorbs hammer force,
                releases for repositioning

CHASING (from reverse: engraving from front)
  Metal detailed and refined from the front
  Same tools (chasing punches) but used to define edges,
  add texture, crisp up raised areas

PROCESS:
 Anneal metal (needs to be soft)
       ↓
 Embed in warm pitch face-down (pitch holds it)
       ↓
 Repoussé: hammer punches from back to raise design
       ↓
 Reheat to remove from pitch; clean; re-anneal
       ↓
 Turn over; embed face-up in pitch
       ↓
 Chasing: refine and detail from front
       ↓
 Repeat until complete

Historical masters: Greek toreutics (Pheidias' shields), Benvenuto
Cellini, West African Benin bronzes (lost wax + chasing combined)
```

## Filigree

Fine wire bent into lacy openwork patterns. Not structural metal — decorative surface treatment.

```
FILIGREE ANATOMY
=================

Base wire (twisted):  Two wires twisted together → more texture
Flat wire:            Roller-flattened round wire
Settings wire:        Short lengths for decorative cells
Filled cells:         Wire coiled tightly to fill an enclosed area (grain)

TWO TYPES:
1. Applied filigree: wire patterns soldered onto a solid metal base
2. Open filigree: wire patterns joined only at intersections — no base metal
   (suspended in air, maximum delicacy — harder to make)

REGIONAL TRADITIONS:
Malta:        Open filigree, silver; trade characteristic souvenir
Portugal:     Filigrée de Viana; gold and silver; heart forms
Russia:       Novgorod and Moscow schools; gold/silver; dense geometric
India:        Karimnagar (Andhra Pradesh); silver; traditional craft
China:        Jingdezhen; gold filigree on enamel objects
Spain:        Catalan and Salamanca schools

Wire gauges: 26–32 gauge (0.2–0.4mm diameter); delicate to near-invisible
```

## Granulation

Surface decoration with tiny metal spheres (granules). The Etruscan mastery (~600–300 BCE) was so refined that the bonding technique was literally lost until the 1930s.

```
ETRUSCAN GRANULATION
=====================

Granule sizes: 0.14–1.5mm diameter (smallest barely visible to naked eye)
Appearance: granules appear to float on the surface with no solder visible

HOW IT WORKS (rediscovered by H.A.P. Littledale, 1936):
  Method: Colloidal hard soldering (Littledale's hypothesis)
          or Copper enrichment at contact (modern metallurgical analysis)

  Copper enrichment process:
  1. Apply copper salt (copper carbonate or copper hydroxide) in organic
     binder (glue, gum tragacanth) to contact points
  2. Heat below melting point of main metal
  3. Copper salt reduces to Cu metal at contact point
  4. Cu-Au eutectic (melting point lower than pure Au) forms at
     tiny contact zone → local fusion at contact point only
  5. Granule bonds to surface with no visible solder; no fill between granules

  Compare: solder-fused granulation (modern, less refined —
           visible solder fills between granules; detectably different)

Modern practitioners: John Paul Miller (Cleveland), Castellani (Rome,
revivalists 1850s), Elizabeth Treskow (German, 20th c.)
```

## Engraving

```
HAND ENGRAVING TOOLS
=====================

Graver (burin): hardened tool steel with cutting face
 - Square graver: V-groove lines; lettering
 - Flat graver: broad flat cuts; bright-cut
 - Round (ball) graver: curved cuts; scroll work
 - Lozenge: fine lines, cross-hatching
 - Spit sticker: pointed; detail work
 - Knife: V-cuts for inlaying

Bright-cut engraving: angled cuts that create mirror-like reflective
                       facets; characteristic of Georgian silver decoration

Engine turning (guilloche): mechanical lathe cuts precise geometric
                             patterns in metal; Fabergé eggs; watch dials;
                             transparent enamel applied over engraving (basse-taille)

Pneumatic engravers: GRS Graversmith, Sherline; air-powered hammer
                     replaces manual push; reduces carpal tunnel risk;
                     modern professional standard

Laser engraving: computer-controlled; precise for inscriptions;
                 not artistic — leaves different surface character than hand
```

## Enameling

Fusing ground glass powder to metal surface at 750–900°C. A specialized sub-craft with its own temperature control demands.

```
ENAMEL TYPES
=============

Cloisonné:    Wire cells (cloisons) soldered to metal base;
              each cell filled with different enamel color;
              fired; ground flush; polished
              Traditional: Japanese, Chinese, Byzantine

Champlevé:    Metal carved/stamped/etched to create depressions;
              filled with enamel; fired; ground flush
              Celts (Merovingian), medieval Rhenish goldwork

Plique-à-jour: Enamel fills open-backed wire cells (like stained glass)
               No metal backing — light transmits through translucent enamel
               Most technically demanding; Art Nouveau (Lalique)
               Requires either acid etching away backing or fire-stitch method

Limoges / painted enamel:
               White opaque enamel as base; painted with enamel pigments;
               each color layer fired separately; miniaturist work
               Grisaille (monochrome white on black) = specific technique

Basse-taille:  Transparent colored enamel over engraved/engine-turned metal;
               metal texture visible through translucent enamel
               Fabergé used extensively

COLORANTS IN ENAMEL GLASS
 Cobalt (Co):    deep blue
 Copper (Cu):    green, turquoise
 Manganese (Mn): purple, violet
 Iron (Fe):      yellow, red-brown
 Gold (Au):      ruby red (gold colloid — historically expensive)
 Uranium (U):    uranium glass yellow-green (historical, radioactive)
 Tin (Sn):       opacifier (makes translucent → opaque)

Firing temperature: 750–870°C; different colors have different optimal ranges;
must fire in right order (high-temp colors first)
Lead-based enamels: traditional (lower melting point, more brilliant);
Lead-free modern: required in many commercial applications
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Soldering silver vs plumbing soldering**:
Plumbing uses tin-lead solder (low melting, not alloy-matched). Silversmithing uses silver solder (55–80% Ag alloy content, much higher melting). Never use plumbing solder on jewelry — it contains lead and corrupts the metal.

**Casting gives thin walls?**
No — casting tends toward thicker cross-sections than fabrication. Thin-walled hollow forms are better achieved by fabrication (sheet forming) than casting.

**Granulation vs pavé**:
Granulation = tiny spheres of metal bonded to the surface (decoration only, no stones). Pavé = tiny gemstones set in drilled holes with tiny metal beads raised between them. Both produce a similarly textured visual effect.

**Filigree wire gauge**:
26–32 AWG for traditional filigree (0.2–0.4mm diameter). By comparison, standard round ring shank wire is 4–6 AWG (~5mm). Filigree wire is nearly 100× thinner in cross-sectional area.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Goal | Technique |
|------|-----------|
| Complex 3D organic form | Lost wax casting |
| Precise geometric form | Fabrication (sheet + solder) |
| Surface decoration with texture | Chasing/repoussé; engine turning |
| Lacy openwork | Filigree (wire) or piercing saw (sheet) |
| Raised dots on surface | Granulation |
| Glass-like color panels | Enameling (cloisonné, champlevé) |
| Transparent glass effect | Plique-à-jour |
| Fine illustrated surface | Limoges painted enamel |
| Production multiples from one master | Lost wax with rubber mold |
| One-of-a-kind wearable art | Fabrication or direct wax carving |
