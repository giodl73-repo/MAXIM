# Hallmarks & Standards — Karat, Fineness, Assay Offices

## The Big Picture

Hallmarking is the world's oldest consumer protection system. English gold and silver hallmarking dates to 1300 CE — predating virtually every modern consumer protection framework by 600 years. The problem it solves is fundamental: precious metals are invisible in alloy form — you cannot see that "gold" is 58.5% Au without assaying it.

```
HALLMARKING SYSTEM LAYERS
==========================

MARKS ON A PIECE (UK example — most comprehensive)
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Sponsor's  │  Standard  │  Assay Office  │  Date      │
│  Mark       │  Mark      │  Mark          │  Letter    │
│  (maker)    │  (purity)  │  (which office)│  (year)    │
│  ABC▲       │  ♦750♦     │  anchor (Birm.)│  'f' (2024)│
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

OPTIONAL / ADDITIONAL MARKS
 Traditional fineness mark: lion passant (sterling silver)
 Import mark: f-shaped mark on imported goods
 Convention mark (Vienna): balance scales in pentagon (international)
 Commemorative marks: jubilees, millennium, etc.

The US system: largely voluntary, self-declaration, no assay office
EU: mandatory for most countries but systems vary
UK: legally mandatory above minimum weight thresholds
```

## Gold Purity: Karat vs Fineness

```
KARAT     FINENESS     AU %       LEGAL STATUS
======    ========     ====       ============
24k       999–990      99.9–99%   Investment, coins; rarely hallmarked
22k       916          91.6%      Hallmarked as 916; Indian/Middle East
                                   bridal jewelry; UK/EU legal
18k       750          75%        EU fine jewelry standard; highly liquid
14k       585          58.5%      US market standard; hallmarked
10k       417          41.7%      US legal minimum for "gold" label
 9k       375          37.5%      UK/Australia entry-level fine jewelry
 8k       333          33.3%      Germany/Austria historical; declining

Below 10k (417): CANNOT legally be called "gold" in the United States.
UK: 9k is the legal minimum for "gold" (375 fineness).
EU: 8k is the absolute minimum in some member states; 9k in most.

The fineness numbers ARE the stamp you see on European jewelry:
 "750" = 18k.  "585" = 14k.  "375" = 9k.
American jewelers stamp "18k," "14k," "585," "750" — all legal.
```

## UK Hallmarking (The Gold Standard System)

UK hallmarking is mandatory for items sold as precious metal above minimum weights. The four required marks:

```
UK HALLMARK COMPONENTS
=======================

1. SPONSOR'S MARK (Maker's Mark)
   ┌─────┐
   │ ABC │  Registered maker's initials in distinctive cartouche
   └─────┘  Each cartouche shape unique to maker category
            Must be registered at a UK assay office
            Same manufacturer using different metals = different cartouche

2. STANDARD MARK (Purity)
   Gold:     ♦ or crown shape containing millesimal fineness
             999, 990, 916, 750, 585, 375
   Silver:   ♦ with lion passant (sterling 925) or millesimal 999/958/925
   Platinum: ♦ with millesimal 999/950/900/850
   Palladium: ♦ with millesimal 999/950/500 (added 2010)

3. ASSAY OFFICE MARK
   London:     Leopard's head (head, no body — since 1300)
   Birmingham: Anchor (1773)
   Edinburgh:  Castle (1457)
   Sheffield:  York rose (1773)
   Dublin:     Hibernia (historically, though Ireland independent)
   Others closed: Chester (1962), Glasgow, Newcastle, Exeter

   The four active offices: London, Birmingham, Edinburgh, Sheffield

4. DATE LETTER
   Each office uses a cycling letter style (letter changes annually)
   Different letter styles per cycle per office
   Allows precise dating: e.g., London "A" in Gothic script = different
   year than London "A" in Roman capitals
   Date letters allow authentication of antiques, estate jewelry
```

### Minimum Weight Thresholds (UK)

| Metal | Threshold | Below threshold |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Gold | 1 gram | Exempt from mandatory hallmark |
| Silver | 7.78 grams | Exempt |
| Platinum | 0.5 grams | Exempt |
| Palladium | 1 gram | Exempt |

Items below threshold may voluntarily be hallmarked but are not required.

## US Marking System

```
US HALLMARKING: VOLUNTARY, SELF-DECLARED
=========================================

There are NO mandatory US assay offices for jewelry.
Maker applies marks to their own product.
FTC rules govern accuracy:

GOLD (FTC 16 CFR Part 23):
 Quality mark must be accurate within ±0.5 karat
 14k stamp → actual content must be ≥13.5k (562 fineness)
 Any quality mark MUST be accompanied by a registered trademark
 (maker's mark) — prevents anonymous misrepresentation

SILVER:
 "Sterling" or "Sterling silver" → must be ≥92.5% Ag
 "Silver" alone → content must be stated

PLATINUM:
 "Platinum" or "Plat" alone → must be ≥95% PGMs (Pt/Pd/Ir/Ru/Rh/Os)
  AND ≥85% platinum
 If less pure: requires numerical statement ("585Pt")
 "Pt950": 95% platinum (most common fine jewelry stamp)

CONSEQUENCE: US consumers rely on maker/retailer reputation.
             No third-party verification.
             Creates more risk of misrepresentation than European systems.
             Import goods entering US must still comply with FTC standards.
```

## European and International Systems

| Country/Region | System | Notes |
|----------------|--------|-------|
| **Italy** | Mandatory state hallmarks + maker's mark | Gold mostly 750 (18k); fineness in geometric frame; provincial assay marks historically |
| **France** | Mandatory; eagle's head (18k Au), owl (imported); French tradition extensive | Different marks for domestic vs import |
| **Germany** | No state hallmarks (voluntary); Lotmarke for 18k+ | Self-declaration with maker's mark |
| **Switzerland** | Complex; federal and cantonal marks; watch industry extensive | Swan = 18k gold; key historical watch marks |
| **Russia / USSR** | 5-pointed star (Soviet) or kokoshnik (woman's headdress) after 1994 | Numbers after mark indicate fineness (585, 750, 925) |
| **Turkey** | Mandatory; crescent and star marks | Active international exporter |
| **India** | BIS Hallmarking (Bureau of Indian Standards); mandatory since 2021 for major cities | Three-mark system: BIS logo + purity + assay office |
| **China** | Not mandatory in same sense; State stamps on official items | "G18K" = 750 fineness gold |

### Vienna Convention on Precious Metals (1972)

```
CONVENTION ON THE CONTROL AND MARKING OF ARTICLES
OF PRECIOUS METALS (CCM — Common Control Mark)

Members (22 states, 2024): UK, Austria, Czech Republic, Denmark,
Finland, Hungary, Ireland, Israel, Latvia, Lithuania, Netherlands,
Norway, Poland, Portugal, Sweden, Switzerland, Ukraine, and others.
NOT members: US, Canada, China, Japan, India.

Common Control Mark (CCM):
 Gold:     Balance scales in pentagon, + fineness in hexagon
 Silver:   Balance scales + fineness
 Platinum: Balance scales + fineness

Member states' national marks are mutually recognized.
CCM mark on a piece means it was independently tested and
is legally accepted in all member countries without re-assay.
```

## Gemological Certification (Not Hallmarks, but Adjacent)

GIA grading reports are not hallmarks (they don't certify metal) but they are the de facto consumer protection standard for gemstones.

```
GIA DIAMOND GRADING REPORT
===========================
Contains:
 Shape and cutting style
 Measurements (mm, to 0.01mm)
 Carat weight (to 0.00 ct)
 Color grade (D–Z)
 Clarity grade (FL–I3)
 Cut grade (Excellent–Poor) [round brilliants only]
 Polish grade
 Symmetry grade
 Fluorescence (None/Faint/Medium/Strong/Very Strong + color)
 Clarity characteristics plot (inclusion map)
 Proportion diagram (crown angle, pavilion angle, table %, depth %)
 Comments (if any treatments)
 Security features (hologram, UV fluorescent ink, microprint)
 GIA report number LASER INSCRIBED on girdle (verifiable)

GIA COLORED STONE REPORT:
 Species and variety
 Weight
 Shape, cut, measurements
 Transparency
 Color description
 Geographic origin (if determinable — separate higher-fee service)
 Treatment disclosure (heat treatment, fracture filling, etc.)

REPORT NUMBER DATABASE: gia.edu/report-check — verify any GIA report online
```

## Kimberley Process Certification Scheme

```
KPCS (2003): International response to "conflict diamonds"
              (diamonds funding rebel forces in Sierra Leone,
               Angola, DRC, Liberia civil wars)

MECHANISM:
 Governments certify rough diamond shipments
 Government-issued certificate accompanies each parcel
 Importing countries verify certificate authenticity
 Diamonds without certificate cannot be legally traded
 85 countries representing ~99.8% of global production

CRITICISM:
 Does not cover polished diamonds (only rough)
 Does not cover state violence (Zimbabwe Marange massacre 2008 —
   diamonds from that region were eventually allowed back in)
 Minimal enforcement; corruption in some member states
 Does not apply to lab-grown diamonds
 Human rights organizations argue definition of "conflict" too narrow

PRACTICAL EFFECT:
 Diamond retailers commonly offer "Kimberley Process certified" stones
 Cannot guarantee supply chain purity beyond rough stage
 Lab-grown diamonds sidestep the question entirely
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Why US hallmarking is weak**:
The FTC requires accuracy of stamps but does not require third-party verification. A manufacturer can stamp "18k" on gold-filled items if they misread the standard — there's no assay office catching it before it reaches the consumer. Reputable retailers self-police; grey market can misrepresent.

**"Plat" vs "Pt950"**:
FTC allows "Plat" for ≥95% platinum, ≥85% Pt content. "Pt950" is a specific alloy. Without numerical designation, "Plat" could be Pt950Co or Pt950Ru. For documentation purposes, always request the specific alloy designation.

**GIA report vs appraisal**:
GIA report = objective grading of physical stone properties. Appraisal = professional estimate of monetary value for insurance. These are different documents serving different purposes. A GIA-graded stone still needs an appraiser to set insurance value.

**Vintage pieces without hallmarks**:
Many antique and vintage pieces were made before hallmarking was common (US) or fall below weight thresholds (small items everywhere). Absence of hallmark ≠ fake. Test with touchstone/acid or XRF spectrometer.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | What to Look For |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Buying in UK | Look for full 4-mark hallmark from assay office |
| Buying in US | FTC quality stamps + maker's trademark; buy from reputable retailers |
| Importing to EU | Check CCM or national assay marks |
| Diamond purchase | GIA Grading Report (verify report number at gia.edu) |
| Ruby/sapphire purchase | AGL, GIA, or Gübelin colored stone report |
| Emerald purchase | Report stating oil treatment grade |
| High-value antique | Specialist appraisal + hallmark identification guide |
| Sustainability concern | RJC certification or Fairtrade/Fairmined gold |
| Conflict diamond concern | Kimberley Process (limited); or choose lab-grown |
