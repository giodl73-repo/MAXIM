# 08 — Pearls

## Natural vs Cultured, Nacre Formation, Mikimoto, Grading

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Pearl formation**: natural pearl forms when a foreign irritant (parasite, shell fragment — NOT a grain of sand as folklore has it) gets into a mollusk's mantle tissue; the mollusk deposits concentric layers of nacre around it; nacre = aragonite (calcium carbonate polymorph) crystals + conchiolin (protein matrix); tablets of aragonite oriented parallel to surface → thin-film interference → iridescent orient/luster; natural pearl is solid nacre throughout; entirely random, rare — hence historically among the most precious gems; pre-Mikimoto, a natural pearl necklace could cost more than a yacht
- **Cultured pearls (Mikimoto)**: Kokichi Mikimoto (1858-1954, Japan) first successfully cultured round pearls ~1893 (U.S. patent 1896); process: bead nucleus (polished bead from Mississippi River mussel shell — North American freshwater mussel shells specifically sought) surgically implanted into oyster's gonad along with small piece of donor mantle tissue (the tissue grafts and the gonad's nacre-secreting epithelium grows over the bead); oyster farmed for 2-5 years; nacre layer 0.3-0.8mm thick over solid bead nucleus; cultured pearl = 90-99% bead + thin nacre coating; natural pearl = 100% nacre; both physically identical nacre — the trade controversy is the nacre thickness
  - X-ray to distinguish: bead nucleus visible as distinct sphere; natural pearl shows growth rings throughout on X-ray
- **Saltwater pearl varieties**:
  - **Akoya**: Pinctada fucata martensii; Japanese/Chinese farmed; 2-11mm; white/cream with rose overtone; the classic round pearl necklace; nacre 0.35-0.6mm typical; Japanese akoya = premium (stricter quality control); Chinese akoya cheaper, thinner nacre
  - **Tahitian / black pearl**: Pinctada margaritifera (black-lipped oyster); French Polynesia; 8-18mm; not truly black — dark gray/charcoal with green, blue, purple, bronze peacock overtones; "peacock" = most valued overtone; unbleached, untreated (color is natural); large oyster → larger nuclei → larger pearls
  - **South Sea pearl**: Pinctada maxima (silver/gold-lipped oyster); Australia, Indonesia, Philippines; 10-20mm; white, silver, golden; gold color (naturally occurring in gold-lipped variety); largest cultured pearls commercially; thin-nacre problem less common (long growth period); Australian South Sea = premium white/silver; Indonesian = golden
  - **Sea of Cortez**: Pteria sterna; Mexico; rare, small production; spectral iridescence
- **Freshwater pearls**: Hyriopsis schlegelii or H. cumingii (triangle shell mussel); Chinese freshwater pearl production = 99% of world's freshwater pearls; no bead nucleus — tissue only; result = all-nacre pearl (more similar to natural pearl in structure); shapes vary (rounds now achievable with modern techniques); less perfectly round historically; flooding market (prices collapsed 1990s-2000s); Edison pearls (large, near-round freshwater with bead nucleus — modern development, branded by Chinese producer)
- **Pearl grading systems**: no universal standard (unlike diamond); multiple commercial systems (Mikimoto's A-AAA, GIA ABCDE scale, proprietary retailer scales); key factors:
  - **Luster**: the most critical — sharpness and brightness of reflections; high luster = mirror-like reflections; low luster = chalky, milky
  - **Orient**: iridescent rainbow play of color (secondary optical phenomenon); highly prized in fine pearls
  - **Surface**: freedom from blemishes (spots, scratches, circles, bumps); AAAA = clean; commercial grade = slight surface characteristics
  - **Shape**: round > near-round > oval > drop > button > baroque; symmetry valued for strands; unique baroque shapes valued for pendants/artistic use
  - **Size**: larger = more valuable (exponentially, since larger sizes rare); measured in mm diameter (8mm, 9mm, 10mm strands)
  - **Nacre thickness**: critical for durability and luster; thin nacre (< 0.35mm) = bead may show through, peeling possible; GIA recommends rejecting nacre under 0.4mm for akoya
- **Knotting and stringing**: cultured pearl strands knotted between each pearl (prevents all pearls falling off if string breaks, prevents rubbing between pearls); silk thread most traditional (some polyester/nylon now); restringing recommended every 2-3 years as silk stretches; French wire (tiny spring coil) at clasp ends protects silk at wear point
- **Pearl treatments**: bleaching (whitens/evens color — standard for akoya); polishing (enhances luster through light buffing); dyeing (chocolate, black freshwater — not always disclosed); Mabe pearls (hemispheres grown against shell, flat back, assembled); irradiation (darkens freshwater pearls — gray/blue tones); coating (wax or lacquer — peels, not desirable)
