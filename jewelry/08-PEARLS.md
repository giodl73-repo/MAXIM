# Pearls — Natural vs Cultured, Nacre Formation, Mikimoto, Grading

## The Big Picture

Pearl is the only gemstone produced by a living organism. The physics that makes it beautiful — thin-film interference from concentric aragonite platelets — is the same physics as an oil slick on water, but controlled at nanometer scale over months of biological secretion.

```
PEARL FORMATION SPECTRUM
=========================

NATURAL PEARL          CULTURED PEARL         FRESHWATER CULTURED
=============          ==============         ====================
Parasite or            Bead nucleus +         Tissue only,
shell fragment         mantle tissue graft    no bead nucleus
(NOT sand)             implanted surgically
     |                        |                      |
     v                        v                      v
  100% NACRE             Bead 85-97%             100% NACRE
  (all the way)          + nacre 3-15%           (like natural)
                         (thin coating)

QUALITY INDICATOR: nacre thickness
 Natural: maximum — entire pearl is nacre
 Cultured saltwater (akoya): 0.35–0.8mm nacre over bead
 Cultured freshwater: 0.5mm+ nacre (all nacre, shapes vary)

DETECTION: X-ray distinguishes
 Natural: growth rings throughout on X-ray
 Bead-nucleated: large spherical bead visible as distinct structure
```

## Nacre: The Physics of Pearl Luster

```
NACRE STRUCTURE
===============

Top view: tablets of aragonite (CaCO₃) laid in layers,
          offset like brick coursework, bound by conchiolin protein

Cross-section (microscopic):
          ════════════════  aragonite platelet (0.5µm thick)
          ________________  conchiolin matrix
          ════════════════  aragonite platelet
          ________________  conchiolin
          ════════════════
          ...repeated 1000+ times (each layer ~0.5µm)

OPTICAL EFFECT: Thin-film interference
 Light partially reflects from each aragonite surface
 Waves interfere constructively/destructively based on layer spacing
 Creates iridescent luster (orient) — characteristic pearly glow
 Same physics as: soap bubbles, butterfly wings, peacock feathers

ARAGONITE vs CALCITE:
 Both CaCO₃ polymorphs; nacre = aragonite (orthorhombic crystal)
 Calcite: less attractive; chalky luster; used by some bivalves
 Aragonite: higher refractive index (1.53–1.69); better optical properties
```

### Aragonite Decomposition

Pearls are organic. They degrade if:
- Stored dry and hot → aragonite → calcite conversion → luster loss
- Exposed to acids (including skin acids, perfume) → dissolve
- Rubbed against each other → surface abrasion

**Pearl first, perfume last** — the standard care instruction has a real chemical basis.

## Kokichi Mikimoto and Cultured Pearls

```
TIMELINE
========
1858: Born in Toba, Japan; noodle and rice cake vendor's son
1878: Begins oyster farming (natural pearl fishing was overharvested)
1893: First successful hemispherical (mabe) cultured pearl
1896: First round cultured pearl; Japanese patent
1908: Mass production begins on Mikimoto's oyster farms
1920s: Natural pearl market collapses; Basra/Persian Gulf merchants
        lose fortunes as cultured pearls flood market
1954: Mikimoto dies; "King of Pearls"

THE PROCESS (still used today):
 1. Select donor oyster; extract small piece of mantle tissue
 2. Select recipient oyster; surgical implant into gonad:
    - Polished bead (from Mississippi River mussel shell)
    - Small piece of donor mantle tissue alongside
 3. Return oyster to water; tissue grafts; grows epithelium over bead
 4. Nacre secretion begins from epithelial cells
 5. Farm 2-5 years for akoya; 3-7 years for South Sea/Tahitian
 6. Harvest; sort; process
```

### Why Mississippi River Mussel Shell?

```
BEAD NUCLEUS MATERIAL
======================
North American freshwater mussel shell (Megalonaias nervosa and
others — Unionidae family) is the global standard because:
 - Calcium carbonate composition similar to nacre → better adhesion
 - Dense, uniform crystal structure → resists dissolution
 - Concentric growth layers in shell mimic nacre growth → oyster
   "accepts" the nucleus more readily (less rejection)
 - Can be cut to precise spheres; takes high polish

Alternative nuclei tested: plastic (rejected by oysters),
                            other shells (less stable),
                            glass (rejection rates high)

Modern innovation: Chinese freshwater pearls with shell nuclei
                   "Edison pearls" — large round freshwater
```

### Akoya in Detail

```
AKOYA: the classic round white pearl
====================================
 Oyster: Pinctada fucata martensii (small — 7–10cm shell)
 Growth period: 8 months – 2 years
 Typical nacre: 0.35–0.6mm (Japanese premium); 0.2–0.4mm (Chinese)
 Size: 2–11mm; strands usually 6–7mm to 8–8.5mm
 Shape: near-perfect round (most consistent of all types)
 Color: white body with pink/silver overtone most prized
         cream body color = lower value
         Blue akoya = treated (irradiated)

 Japanese vs Chinese akoya:
 Japan: Mie Prefecture (Toba area); stricter quality control;
         higher nacre thickness standards; fewer harvested per farm
 China: higher volume; thinner nacre common; lower price point
         but quality improved dramatically since 2000s
```

### Tahitian (Black) Pearls

```
TAHITIAN PEARL
==============
 "Black" is a misnomer — range of dark colors
 NATURAL colors (no treatment needed):
  - Charcoal gray (most common)
  - Green peacock (most prized — greenish gray)
  - Peacock (green + rose overtone — top quality)
  - Aubergine (purple-gray)
  - Blue/gray
  - Bronze (warm brown-gray)
  - Yellow-green (rarer)

 "Peacock" = trade term; CIBJO definition requires green + rose overtone
 Grading labs (GIA) resist "peacock" as grade — subjective

 Overtone: secondary iridescent color visible over body color
 Most valued overtone: green/rose (peacock); bronze second
```

### South Sea Pearls

```
SOUTH SEA PEARL
================
 Oyster: Pinctada maxima — largest commercially farmed oyster
         Silver-lip (white/silver pearls) — Australia
         Gold-lip (golden pearls) — Indonesia, Philippines
         Shell up to 30cm; produces large nuclei → large pearls

 Golden South Sea: rare; natural golden color from golden-lip oyster;
                   no treatment; "24k" of pearls; Indonesia/Philippines
                   Premium: deeper, even gold color without brown tinge

 Australian South Sea: white to silver; strict grading; consistently
                        highest nacre standards of any cultured pearl
                        Nacre: 2–6mm (vs akoya's 0.35–0.6mm) — extraordinary
```

## Freshwater Pearls

```
FRESHWATER PEARL OVERVIEW
==========================
Species: Hyriopsis schlegelii, H. cumingii (triangle shell mussel)
         Unionidae family mussels; farmed in lakes and rivers
Location: China dominates 99%+ of world production
          (Zhuji City, Zhejiang Province = center)
          Some US production (declining)

NUCLEATION: tissue only (no bead) in traditional freshwater
             = ALL-NACRE structure (structurally similar to natural)
             Modern "Edison" pearls: bead-nucleated, round, 12–14mm+

SHAPES (traditional freshwater):
 Baroque: irregular; "popcorn" shapes historical
 Near-round: improved farming techniques; increasing production
 Round: achievable now with improved technique; increasing
 Ringed: circular ridges (concentric circles visible on surface)
 Keshi: accident pearls — all nacre, various shapes

FRESHWATER PEARL TREATMENT:
 Bleaching: standard; evens color
 Heat treatment: increases luster of freshwater
 Dyeing: common for chocolate, black, purple freshwater
 → GIA recommends disclosure of dyed freshwater

PRICING COLLAPSE: freshwater flooded market 1990s–2000s;
                  1mm freshwater rounds now essentially commodity
                  Fine round near-baroque freshwater still value
```

## Pearl Grading: No Universal Standard

Unlike diamonds (GIA 4Cs), pearls have no single global grading standard. Multiple systems coexist:

```
GRADING FACTORS (universal agreement on these, not grades)
===========================================================

1. LUSTER (most critical)
   High luster:  Mirror-like reflections; sharp, bright
   Low luster:   Chalky; milky; dull
   Measurement: subjective (AAA/AA/A commercial) or
                descriptive (GIA: Excellent/Very Good/Good/Fair/Poor)

2. ORIENT (secondary)
   Iridescent rainbow effect over body color
   (different from luster — luster is white light reflection;
    orient is spectral iridescence)
   Present in finest natural and cultured pearls
   Absent in low-quality pearls

3. NACRE THICKNESS
   Akoya minimum (GIA): ≥0.4mm recommended
   Thin nacre: bead may show through (chalky spots visible when rotated)
               peeling possible in years; early luster loss
   Measuring: microscopic cross-section or X-ray estimation

4. SURFACE QUALITY
   AAAA/AAA: essentially clean (< 5% surface blemished)
   AA:       slight blemishes (< 25%)
   A:        obvious blemishes (< 75%)
   Commercial: heavy blemishing
   Blemish types: spots, scratches, circles, bumps, welts, pits

5. SHAPE
   Round > Near-round > Oval > Drop > Button > Baroque > Keshi
   Symmetry prized in strands; unique baroque valued in pendants

6. SIZE
   Larger = more valuable (exponential pricing at thresholds)
   Akoya: 6.5mm = standard; 9mm = fine; 11mm = exceptional
   South Sea: 12mm = fine; 16mm+ = exceptional; 20mm = rare

7. COLOR
   Body color: white, cream, rose, silver, gold, gray, black
   Overtone: additional iridescent layer (pink, silver, green, bronze)
   Together determine market category and value
```

### Matching in Strands

```
STRAND MATCHING CRITERIA
========================
All pearls in a strand should match on:
 Color (body + overtone)
 Luster level
 Surface grade
 Size (or graduated pattern — larger at center, smaller at clasp)
 Shape (all round, or all baroque — mixed quality confuses valuation)

A strand of 7mm Japanese akoya (high luster, well-matched, AAA):
  ~$2,000–8,000+ (2024 market) depending on nacre thickness and quality
```

## Pearl Care and Stringing

```
CARE RULES
==========
Pearl first, perfume last:
 Cosmetics, perfume, hairspray all dissolve nacre
 Put on pearls after applying all products

Storage:
 Not in dry sealed container — needs slight humidity
 Avoid rubber bands (sulfur) and direct sunlight
 Separate from harder gems (nothing scratches pearls — every gem does)
 Soft pouch or cloth-lined box

Cleaning:
 Soft damp cloth; mild soap; no ultrasonic cleaners
 Restring before putting in ultrasonic (ultrasonic breaks silk thread)

STRINGING
==========
Silk thread: traditional; strongest; slight stretch over time
Nylon: some modern strands; doesn't stretch; less traditional feel
Knots between each pearl:
 1. Prevents all pearls rolling away if string breaks
 2. Prevents pearl-on-pearl abrasion
 3. Required for strand insurance valuation in many cases

French wire (coil): tiny spring coil at clasp end; protects silk
                     where it meets metal clasp (highest wear point)

Restring schedule: every 2–3 years for frequently worn strands;
                   silk absorbs skin oils and weakens; loosens over time
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"A grain of sand causes natural pearls"**:
False. Natural pearls form around parasites or shell fragments that penetrate the mantle tissue. Sand is rarely the irritant — and actually, contact with sand normally causes the mollusk to expel it.

**Cultured pearls are "fake"**:
Cultured pearls are real pearls. The nacre is biologically identical to natural pearl nacre. The distinction is that a human inserted the nucleus — not that the nacre is artificial. "Imitation pearl" = glass or plastic beads coated with pearlescent paint (those are fake).

**All freshwater pearls are cheap**:
Traditional freshwater baroque pearls are commodity. Premium round freshwater (top luster, clean surface, 9-11mm) and Edison pearls command significant prices. Fine all-nacre freshwater can rival akoya in quality.

**Tahitian pearls are dyed**:
Standard Tahitian pearls are NOT dyed — the dark colors are entirely natural from the black-lipped oyster. However, some freshwater pearls sold as "black" ARE dyed. The way to tell: check the drill hole — dyed pearls show color concentrated at the drill hole; natural Tahitian pearls show the same color throughout.
