# 02 — Gemstones

## Classification, Crystal Systems, the 4Cs — Diamond to Tanzanite

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Gemstone classification**: minerals (crystalline inorganic solids — most gemstones), rocks (lapis lazuli = metamorphic rock), and organic materials (pearl, coral, amber, jet); gems valued for beauty (color, clarity, brilliance), durability (hardness, toughness), and rarity; the "precious vs semi-precious" distinction is commercial, not scientific — legally meaningless in many jurisdictions but widely used in trade
- **Crystal systems and why they matter**: gem minerals crystallize in one of seven systems; crystal system determines cleavage planes (directions of easy splitting), optical properties (single vs double refraction), and available cutting forms; diamond = cubic (isometric) → no birefringence, cleavage in four octahedral directions (why brilliant cut works); calcite = trigonal → strong birefringence; tourmaline = trigonal → dichroism (different colors viewed along different axes)
- **Mohs hardness scale**: 1 (talc) to 10 (diamond); not linear — diamond is ~4× harder than corundum (9); hardness determines abrasion resistance and wearability for jewelry; diamonds (10) scratch everything including each other; corundum (9) — ruby/sapphire; topaz (8); quartz (7) — minimum recommended for rings, since airborne dust is ~quartz hardness; feldspar (6) — moonstone, Labradorite; apatite (5) — some ornamental use; opal (5.5-6.5) — fragile, needs protective setting
- **The four precious gemstones**: diamond, ruby, sapphire, emerald — historically "precious"; all others "semi-precious" in traditional trade usage
  - **Diamond**: pure carbon in cubic crystal (sp³ bonds — hardest material); color graded D (colorless) to Z (light yellow/brown); fancy colors (pink, blue, green, red) separate scale and can be more valuable than colorless; clarity grades FL → IF → VVS1/VVS2 → VS1/VS2 → SI1/SI2 → I1/I2/I3 (GIA scale); Type Ia (most common — N aggregates), Type IIa (nitrogen-free — rarest, highest clarity), Type IIb (boron impurity → blue color + semiconductor)
  - **Ruby**: corundum (Al₂O₃) + Cr³⁺ (1-3% chromium) → red; trigonal crystal; hardness 9; Burmese (Mogok) rubies — most prized "pigeon blood" red; Thai rubies (slightly darker); "star ruby" (asterism — needle-like rutile inclusions, cabochon cut reveals 6-ray star); unheated vs heat-treated distinction (heat treatment to improve clarity/color = industry standard, disclosed in certificates); historically "carbuncle" = any red stone
  - **Sapphire**: corundum + Fe²⁺/Ti⁴⁺ (blue); also in pink, yellow, orange, purple, green, colorless — "fancy sapphires"; padparadscha = rare orange-pink sapphire (Sri Lankan origin; name from Sinhalese for lotus blossom); Kashmir sapphires (cornflower blue, historically most prized — mine exhausted ~1930s); Ceylon (Sri Lanka) sapphires; Montana sapphires (US); padparadscha certification controversially applied
  - **Emerald**: beryl (Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈) + Cr³⁺ or V³⁺ → green; hardness 7.5-8; almost always heavily included (internal fractures called "jardin" = garden — accepted in emerald where unacceptable in diamond); Colombian emeralds (Muzo, Chivor mines) = gold standard; Zambian emeralds (darker, more blue-green); "oiling" (filling fractures with cedar oil or resin = standard practice, must be disclosed); Sandawana (Zimbabwe) small but intensely colored
- **Semi-precious gems** (selected): tanzanite (zoisite + V — blue-violet, found only near Mt Kilimanjaro, discovered 1967 by Maasai herder Ali Juuyawatu; all from Tanzania; pleochroic — three different colors in three axes); alexandrite (chrysoberyl + Cr — shows green in daylight, red in incandescent — finest color-change mineral; original Russian stones from Ural mountains, discovered 1830 during Tsar Alexander II's birthday); opal (amorphous silica — hydrated SiO₂·nH₂O; play of color from diffraction in regular arrays of silica spheres; Australian Lightning Ridge = black opal; Mexican = fire opal; Ethiopian = Welo opal); aquamarine (beryl + Fe² → blue-green); amethyst (quartz + Fe + irradiation → purple); tourmaline (complex borosilicate — widest color range of any gem); garnet group (multiple species — pyrope, almandine, spessartine, grossular tsavorite, demantoid)
- **Gemological testing and certification**: GIA (Gemological Institute of America), AGS, IGI, HRD as major labs; grading reports for diamonds (4Cs + fluorescence + proportions diagram + clarity map); colored stone reports (origin determination, treatment disclosure); spectroscopy (FTIR, UV-Vis, Raman) to identify treatments and synthetic; Chelsea color filter (distinguishes chromium-colored stones — emerald vs green glass under Chelsea filter); dichroscope (identifies birefringent gems and dichroism); refractometer (refractive index — each mineral has characteristic RI range)
