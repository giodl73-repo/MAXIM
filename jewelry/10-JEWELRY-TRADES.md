## Goldsmith

The master precious metal trade. In medieval Europe, goldsmith guilds were among the most powerful in any city.

```
HISTORICAL CONTEXT
==================
London Goldsmiths' Company: Royal Charter 1327 (still active)
 - Originally controlled testing and marking → origin of hallmarking
 - Prime Warden: historically second only to Mayor of London in guild hierarchy
 - The assay function grew from guild oversight of members

GOLDSMITHS AS BANKERS:
 Florentine goldsmiths (Medici): held deposits, extended credit
 English goldsmiths (17th c.): issued receipts for gold deposited
                               → those receipts circulated as currency
                               → origin of paper money and banking
 The goldsmith's strong room → vault → bank is a direct lineage

FAMOUS HISTORICAL GOLDSMITHS:
 Benvenuto Cellini (1500–1571): wrote "Treatise on Goldsmithing"
                                 (still read); made Saltcellar of Francis I,
                                 Perseus with Medusa
 Fabergé (Peter Carl, 1846–1920): court goldsmith to Tsar Nicholas II
                                   Easter eggs; world's finest modern goldwork
 Castellani (Fortunato Pio, Rome 19th c.): revived Etruscan granulation
 René Lalique: goldsmith → Art Nouveau pioneer → glass artist

MODERN GOLDSMITH SKILLS:
 - Fabrication (sheet, wire, tube work)
 - Lost wax casting / rubber mold production
 - Stone setting (advanced prong, bezel, pavé)
 - Soldering, annealing, pickling
 - Filigree, granulation (traditional crafts)
 - Polishing, plating
 - Platinum working (specialist subset)
 - CAD/CAM (increasingly standard)
```

## Silversmith

Distinct trade from goldsmith historically, though overlapping in practice. Silversmithing extends to hollow ware (flatware, tea services, presentation pieces) beyond jewelry scale.

```
SILVERSMITH SCOPE
==================
Jewelry: rings, bracelets, necklaces in silver (925 sterling)
Hollow ware: tea services, pitchers, bowls, trays (different scale)
Flatware: forks, knives, spoons (different tools and forming)
Presentation pieces: sports trophies, corporate gifts, civic regalia

SHEFFIELD PLATE (1742–1840s):
 Thomas Boulsover's discovery: fused copper + silver layers
 Roll together → silver on copper sheet
 Precursor to electroplating
 Sheffield became silversmith capital; Sheffield assay office followed

ELECTROPLATING (1840s):
 Elkington & Co, Birmingham: patented silver electroplating 1840
 EPNS = Electroplated Nickel Silver (base metal + silver plate)
 Destroyed Sheffield plate industry; transformed silversmithing

NOTABLE SILVERSMITHS:
 Paul de Lamerie (1688–1751): French Huguenot immigrant; finest
                               Georgian English silversmith; rococo
 Hester Bateman (1709–1794): rare woman silversmith; ran workshop
                               after husband's death; prolific output
 Paul Storr (1771–1844): Regency period; large-scale commissions
 Georg Jensen (1866–1935): Danish; defined 20th century silver aesthetic;
                            simple sculptural forms; hammered surfaces
                            Jensen company still operating (Copenhagen)
```

## Bench Jeweler

The generalist craftsperson who assembles, repairs, and maintains jewelry at the bench. Most daily work is repair, not making.

```
THE JEWELER'S BENCH
====================
Physical setup:
 Wooden bench with curved front cutout
 Leather "bench skin" (apron) below catches filings, dropped stones
 Pin (bench pin) extending from bench — work rest
 Suction cup or bench vise for holding work
 Flex-shaft machine (rotary tool — Foredom) for grinding/polishing/drilling
 Polishing motor with buff wheels
 Soldering area: firebrick, torch, pickle pot

DAILY WORK BREAKDOWN (typical retail/repair bench):
 Ring sizing (40%): cutting shank, adding/removing metal, soldering, finish
 Prong retipping (20%): add solder to worn prong tips
 Clasp repair (10%): lobster clasps, box clasps, spring rings
 Stone replacement (15%): find matching stone, re-set
 Cleaning / refinishing (10%): polish, rhodium replate
 Custom work (5%): new fabrication; commission pieces

RING SIZING IN DETAIL:
 Size up (too small):
  Method 1: stretching mandrel (works for plain shanks ≤1 full size)
  Method 2: cut shank, add metal piece (jump ring / strip), solder, finish
 Size down (too large):
  Cut metal out, close gap, solder, finish
  Pavé/channel bands: complex — stones at cut point may need
                      removal and resetting (expensive)
 Marks left by sizing: visible if same solder not used; color mismatch
                        in rose gold; size stamp disturbed
```

## Diamond Cutter (Diamantaire)

One of the most specialized and high-stakes trades in jewelry. One planning decision can add or subtract $50,000+ from a single stone's value.

```
DIAMOND CUTTING CENTERS
========================
Historical origin: Amsterdam (1400s, Jewish merchant community)
                   Antwerp (1400s, concurrent development)
                   Both cities had combination of: capital, expertise,
                   trade connections, and gem-cutting traditions

Modern distribution by volume:
 India (Surat, Gujarat): ~90% of world's diamonds cut by number
                          Most small melee (under 0.30 ct)
                          ~800,000 people in Surat's diamond industry
                          Cost efficiency; excellent for calibrated melee

 Belgium (Antwerp): Antwerp World Diamond Centre (AWDC)
                     High-end and large stones; training programs;
                     transit of 50%+ of world's rough diamond supply

 Israel (Tel Aviv/Ramat Gan): large and fancy shapes; high-value
                               specialized cutting; diamond exchange

 United States (New York): custom cutting; repair; dealer community;
                            smaller volume

CUTTING HIERARCHY:
 Small melee (<0.10 ct): India (automated + semi-automated)
 Standard commercial (0.10–1.0 ct): India + China
 Fine make (1.0 ct+): Antwerp + Israel + NY
 Large stones (10 ct+): Only Antwerp/Israel/NY; extremely specialized
 Fancy shapes: Specialized firms in each center

PLANNING — the highest-skill decision:
 "Sawing plan": maximize value, not carat weight
 Large rough stone: dozens of possible outcomes; computer modeling
 Sarin software (Israel): 3D scan; automated planning recommendation
 Human expert review: final decision; experience can override software
 One planning error → loss of millions (wrong grain orientation → cleave;
                       missed inclusion → lower clarity after cutting)

INDUSTRY PRICING:
 Rapaport Price List: weekly benchmark for polished diamond prices
                       (Martin Rapaport, NY); industry standard since 1978
                       Published Thursday night; used globally
                       Discounts/premiums from Rapaport = market price
                       Heavy price drops in 2022–2024 Rap
```

## Gemologist

The scientist of the gem world. Distinct from jeweler — a gemologist evaluates and identifies; a jeweler makes and repairs.

```
GIA (GEMOLOGICAL INSTITUTE OF AMERICA)
========================================
Founded: 1931, Los Angeles, by Robert Shipley
Current HQ: Carlsbad, California (+ NYC + Antwerp + 10 global campuses)
Mission: Set industry standards for gem grading; train gemologists
Primary credential: GG (Graduate Gemologist) — 1–2 years distance learning
                                                + on-site lab component

GG CURRICULUM:
 Diamond grading: 4Cs, proportion measurement, plotting inclusions,
                   fluorescence, polish, symmetry
 Colored stone identification: 30+ species; inclusion-type recognition
 Pearl identification: nacre testing, cultured vs natural
 Gem instruments: polariscope, refractometer, spectroscope,
                   Chelsea filter, dichroscope, thermal probe
                   UV lamp, loupe (10×), microscope (10–60×)
 Gem markets: buying, selling, valuation context

OTHER CREDENTIALS:
 FGA: Fellow of the Gemmological Association of Great Britain (Gem-A)
       UK equivalent; older (founded 1908); well-respected internationally
 DGA: Diamond Grading certificate (Gem-A lower-level)
 GG + Appraisal cert (GIA): adds valuation methodology to grading
 AGS (American Gem Society): Certified Gemologist credential; also cut
                              grading laboratory (AGS 0-10 scale)

GIA GRADING LABORATORY:
 Separate from education; operates independently
 GIA Diamond Grading Reports: primary market standard
 GIA Colored Stone Reports: identification + treatment disclosure
 GIA Origin Reports: geographic origin determination (high-value stones)
 Report numbers: laser inscribed on diamond girdle (viewable at 10×)
                  verifiable at gia.edu/report-check

WHERE GEMOLOGISTS WORK:
 GIA / AGS / IGI grading labs
 Retail jewelry appraisal (insurance, estate, divorce proceedings)
 Auction houses (Sotheby's, Christie's, Bonhams — jewelry specialists)
 Wholesale/import-export certification
 Estate jewelry dealers
 Museum collections
 Insurance companies (loss assessment)
```

## Lapidary

Gemstone cutter and polisher — any gem other than diamond (diamond cutter = separate specialty).

```
LAPIDARY EQUIPMENT
==================
Trim saw:          Diamond blade; initial rough cuts
Slab saw:          Larger machine; 4–16 inch blades; slabs from large rough
Arbor:             Vertical or horizontal rotating shaft; mounts lap discs
Lap discs:         Metal discs (silicon carbide, diamond) in progressive
                    grits: 60, 120, 220, 600, 1200 grit; then polish
Cabbing machine:   Combination unit: grinding wheels + polishing pads
Faceting machine:  Precision: mast height controls depth; protractor
                   angle setting; index gear (divides circle into
                   96 equal parts = 96-tooth index gear standard;
                   allows all standard facet arrangements)
Polish laps:       Tin, lead, wood, etc.; charged with:
                    Cerium oxide (glass, quartz)
                    Diamond paste (corundum, topaz)
                    Alumina (mixed use)
                    Chrome oxide (green for jade)

FACETING TECHNIQUE:
 Stone dopped to aluminum or wood dop stick with dopping wax
 Stick held at precise angle against rotating lap
 Angle determined by species' RI (each mineral has optimal pavilion angle)
 Index gear position determines which facet is being cut
 Repeat for each facet position

CUSTOM CUTTING (precision faceting / fantasy cutting):
 German school (Idar-Oberstein): world center; intricate free-form designs
 Fantasy cuts: concave facets, curved surfaces (not possible on flat laps —
               requires special equipment)
 Precision faceting for collectors: meet points exact to 0.01mm;
                                      commercially non-viable for retail
```

## Engraver

```
TOOLS AND TECHNIQUES
=====================
Graver (burin): hardened tool steel rod; various tip geometries
 Square graver: V-channels for lines and letters
 Flat graver: broad cuts; bright-cut work
 Round (ball): curved decorative cuts
 Lozenge: cross-hatching; fine lines
 Spit sticker: detail work in tight spaces

Held: in palm; pushed with controlled pressure along the metal
Supporting: pitch bowl, ring clamp, engraving block (held in vise)

GRS Graversmith: air-powered handpiece; pneumatic hammer action;
                  reduces hand fatigue; modern professional standard
                  GRS is a brand (Lindsay gravers another)

Bright-cut engraving: specific technique: graver held at angle
                       creates mirror-facet cuts that catch light
                       Characteristic of Georgian/Regency silver borders

Engine turning (guilloche): mechanical lathe; rotating chuck;
                             geometric patterns; underneath transparent enamel
                             Fabergé eggs; watch dials

Laser engraving: computer-controlled; fast; leaves different surface quality
                  (micro-pitted, not polished) — distinct from hand

Wax/clay: not cutting metal; modeling in wax; different process entirely
```

## Enamellist

```
A SPECIALIST TRADE
==================
Distinct from bench jeweler skills — enamel requires:
 Understanding of glass chemistry and kiln behavior
 Patience for multi-firing (each layer fired separately)
 Control of temperature for different enamel colors

Training centers historically:
 Limoges, France: painted limoges enamel; 12th century tradition
 Geneva, Switzerland: watch dial enamel; haute horlogerie
 Byzantine tradition workshops (religious objects)
 Fabergé workshop: combined enamel + goldwork at highest level

Key challenge: thermal expansion mismatch
 Metal and enamel must have compatible coefficients of thermal expansion
 If not matched: enamel cracks or pops off on cooling
 Standard: fine silver and 18k gold are best enamel bases
           Copper also works (well-matched expansion)
           Sterling silver: slightly problematic (Cu content affects)
           Platinum: poor base for enamel

Contemporary enamellist organizations:
 The Enamel Guild (US); Limoges Enamel Society
 Many contemporary studio artists crossing into enamel from ceramics
```

## Pearl Stringer

A declining specialist trade as pearl jewelry becomes less common as fine jewelry category.

```
PEARL STRINGING SKILLS
=======================
Thread selection: silk (traditional, stretches over time) or nylon
Knotting: individual overhand knot between each pearl
          Requires: knot tool or fine tweezers
          Knot must be: tight against pearl, not too large
French wire: small coiled spring at clasp end;
              prevents thread abrasion at metal connection point

Strand matching before stringing:
 Align pearls for color graduation (darkest to lightest)
 Or size graduation (largest at center to smallest at ends)
 Match luster level throughout

Restringing frequency: every 2–3 years for worn strands
 Silk stretches; knots loosen; spaces appear between pearls
 Visual sign: visible thread between pearls when held horizontally
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Bench jeweler ≠ designer ≠ gemologist**:
These are three distinct skill sets that rarely overlap completely. A bench jeweler can make and repair without any gemological training. A designer can create without bench skills. A gemologist can evaluate without making anything.

**GIA report ≠ appraisal**:
A GIA Diamond Grading Report objectively describes the physical stone. It does not assign a dollar value. An insurance appraisal by a GIA-credentialed appraiser assigns replacement value — a separate document, separate service.

**The Antwerp diamond connection**:
Antwerp remains the world's most important diamond transit hub — ~50%+ of the world's rough and polished diamonds pass through Antwerp Bourse even if not cut there. "Antwerp-cut" is sometimes used as shorthand for high-quality precision cutting, not necessarily meaning cut in Belgium.

**Silversmith vs goldsmith in practice**:
The trade distinction is blurring. Most contemporary metalsmiths work in all precious metals. The historical guild separation (silversmiths in Silversmiths' Company, goldsmiths in Goldsmiths' Company) still exists in UK institutional structure but has no practical production relevance.

| Need | Who to Consult |
|------|---------------|
| Resize a gold ring | Bench jeweler |
| Evaluate a diamond purchase | GIA Graduate Gemologist |
| Commission bespoke fine jewelry | Goldsmith or CAD jewelry designer |
| Get GIA report for a diamond | GIA Laboratory directly (mail or walk-in) |
| Identify a colored stone | Gemologist (with instruments) or gem lab |
| Restring pearl necklace | Pearl stringer or bench jeweler (with stringing skill) |
| Custom cut a special stone | Precision lapidary (Idar-Oberstein or equivalent) |

| Insurance appraisal | Certified appraiser (GIA GG + appraiser credential) |
| Estate jewelry valuation | Auction house specialist (Sotheby's, Christie's) |
