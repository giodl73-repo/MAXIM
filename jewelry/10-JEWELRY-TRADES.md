# 10 — Jewelry Trades

## Goldsmith, Silversmith, Lapidary, Gemologist — The Trade Hierarchy

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Goldsmith**: the master trade in jewelry making; worked exclusively with gold (and platinum in modern practice); historically separate from silversmith; goldsmith guilds extremely powerful — London Goldsmiths' Company (1327, Royal Charter) one of the most powerful livery companies; historically controlled testing and marking (hallmarking); goldsmiths often doubled as bankers (held deposits, extended credit) — roots of modern banking (Florentine goldsmiths → Medici banking); "gold" in many Germanic languages: "goldsmith" = Goldschmied; the assay office function (testing metal purity) grew from guild goldsmith oversight
- **Silversmith**: works silver into hollow ware (flatware, bowls, tea services, presentation pieces) as well as jewelry; technically distinct from jeweler but overlapping in practice; English silversmith tradition: Paul de Lamerie (Huguenot immigrant, finest Georgian silversmith); Paul Storr; Hester Bateman (18th c. — rare woman silversmith who ran successful workshop after husband's death); Georg Jensen (Danish, 20th c. — defined modernist silver aesthetic); Sheffield plate (18th c.) = fused silver on copper before electroplating; EPNS = electroplated nickel silver (base metal + silver plate)
- **Bench jeweler**: assembles, repairs, and sets stones in jewelry; works at the jeweler's bench (wooden bench with leather apron "bench skin" below to catch metal filings and dropped stones); tools: flexible shaft machine (pendant drill), hand gravers, files, pliers (flat nose, round nose, chain nose, bent), tweezers, stone-setting tools (pusher, burnisher, bezel rocker), soldering setup (torch, pickle, flux); jeweler's loupe (10×); benchwork = the skilled physical craft separate from design; repair work (ring sizing, prong retipping, clasp repair, stone replacement) = most daily bench jeweler activity; manufacturing (bench fabrication of new pieces); sizing rings: cutting shank, adding or removing metal, soldering, finishing
- **Diamond cutter (diamantaire)**: one of the most specialized trades; Antwerp and Amsterdam as historical centers (Jewish diamond merchant community, 15th century forward); Indian cutting centers now dominate volume (Surat); training: 5+ years apprenticeship for master cutter; tools historically: diamond-charged cast iron lapping wheels (scaife); modern: CNC laser sawing, bruting machines, computerized faceting; planning the rough = most skilled decision (maximize yield vs maximize quality, find optimal orientation to avoid inclusions); Antwerp World Diamond Centre; Rapaport price list (weekly diamond pricing benchmark used globally by industry)
- **Gemologist (GIA Graduate Gemologist)**: credential from GIA (Gemological Institute of America, founded 1931 by Robert Shipley, Carlsbad CA); the industry standard for gem identification and grading; curriculum: diamond grading (4Cs, proportions, fluorescence), colored stone identification (30+ species), pearl identification, gem instruments; instrument skills: polariscope, refractometer, spectroscope, Chelsea filter, dichroscope, thermal probe; GIA Graduate Gemologist (GG) = primary credential; FGA (Fellow of the Gemmological Association of Great Britain) = UK equivalent; gemologist job: work in labs, retail appraisal, estate auction houses (Christie's/Sotheby's), import/export certification
- **Lapidary**: gemstone cutter and polisher (non-diamond); distinct from diamond cutter; facets colored stones on lapidary machine (rotating lap wheel); cab cutter for cabochons; trim saw, arbor, lap discs in different grits; gem carver (glyptician for engraved gems); faceting machine (precision indexed, protractor angle setting, mast height = depth, lap spinning); standard cutting angles for refractive index (each gem species has optimal facet angles for its RI); custom cutting (precision faceted gems for collectors, not always commercially viable shapes)
- **Engraver**: incises patterns, inscriptions, heraldry into metal or gemstones; hand engraving (push graver for goldwork, hammer-and-chisel for harder metals); pneumatic engraving tools (GRS Graversmith); bright-cut (angled cuts that create bright mirror facets); wax engraving (for personalization, family crests on signet rings); intaglio seal cutting; requires both artistic ability and extremely fine motor control
- **Enamellist**: applies and fires enamel to metal; separate specialty from benchwork; must understand kiln temperatures for different enamel colors; plique-à-jour requires extraordinary patience (open cells, no backing); limoges painters work like miniaturists; historical training in Limoges, France (center of limoges enamel), Geneva (watch dial enamel), and workshops associated with major houses
- **Pearl stringer**: specialist trade; knots silk thread between each pearl; must select and match pearls for strand (color, size, luster gradation); knowledge of clasp mechanics; increasingly rare as specialized pearl work declines
- **Key schools and institutions**: GIA (Carlsbad + NYC + Antwerp + 10 global campuses); British Academy of Jewellery; École de Bijouterie et Joaillerie (Paris); Florence Academy of Art jewelry program; Revere Academy (San Francisco); Gemological Association of Great Britain (Gem-A); Antwerp Diamond High Council; traditional apprenticeship model declining, formal school model increasing
