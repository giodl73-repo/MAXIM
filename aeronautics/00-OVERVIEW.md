## 1. The Four Forces of Flight

```
LIFT (L): aerodynamic force perpendicular to freestream velocity
  Generated by pressure difference between upper (lower pressure) and lower (higher pressure)
  wing surfaces; and/or by circulation of airflow around wing
  L = q∞ × S × CL = ½ρV² × S × CL
  q∞ = dynamic pressure; S = wing reference area; CL = lift coefficient

WEIGHT (W): gravitational force; acts through aircraft center of gravity
  W = mg; always toward Earth
  For level flight: L = W
  For climb: L < W (not correct exactly; L = W cos γ where γ = climb angle)

THRUST (T): force in direction of flight from propulsion system
  Reaction to mass flow accelerated rearward (Newton's 3rd law)
  For level unaccelerated flight: T = D

DRAG (D): aerodynamic force parallel and opposite to freestream velocity
  D = q∞ × S × CD = ½ρV² × S × CD
  Parasite drag: profile + pressure + interference drag (increases with V²)
  Induced drag (drag due to lift): Di = L²/(q∞ × π × b²) = proportional to L²; decreases with V²
  Total drag minimum at best L/D speed

FLIGHT ENVELOPE:
  Altitude vs. airspeed operating boundaries
  Stall boundary: minimum speed (CL max reached)
  Structural boundary: never-exceed speed VNE (dynamic pressure limit)
  Compressibility effects: Mcrit (Mach number where shock waves first form on wing)
  Temperature, altitude, and Mach define the standard atmosphere operating envelope
```

## 2. Standard Atmosphere

```
ICAO STANDARD ATMOSPHERE:
  SL: T₀ = 288.15 K, p₀ = 101,325 Pa, ρ₀ = 1.225 kg/m³
  Troposphere (0-11 km): T = 288.15 - 6.5 × h (K; h in km); lapse rate 6.5 K/km
  Tropopause (11 km): T = 216.65 K (constant to 20 km)
  Stratosphere (20-32 km): T increases again

PRESSURE-ALTITUDE:
  p/p₀ = (T/T₀)^5.2561  (troposphere)
  Density ratio: σ = ρ/ρ₀
  Speed of sound: a = √(γRT) where γ = 1.4 (diatomic ideal gas), R = 287 J/(kg·K)

MACH NUMBER: M = V/a
  Subsonic: M < 1
  Transonic: M ≈ 0.8-1.2 (mixed subsonic/supersonic)
  Supersonic: M > 1; Hypersonic: M > 5

DYNAMIC PRESSURE: q = ½ρV² (EAS) or q = ½ρ₀V_EAS²
  Equivalent Airspeed (EAS): indicated airspeed corrected for compressibility at SL density
  True Airspeed (TAS): actual speed through air mass
  Groundspeed: TAS corrected for wind
  Calibrated Airspeed (CAS): IAS corrected for instrument error and position error
```

## 3. Airworthiness and Certification

### FAR/CS Structure

```
US FEDERAL AVIATION REGULATIONS (FAR) / EU Certification Specifications (CS):
  FAR 21: certification procedures for products and articles
  FAR 23: airworthiness standards — normal, utility, aerobatic, commuter (≤19 pax, ≤8,618 kg)
    Reorganized in 2017: performance-based rather than prescriptive
  FAR 25: airworthiness standards — transport category aircraft (airliners, large aircraft)
  FAR 27/29: rotorcraft (small/large)
  FAR 33: aircraft engines
  FAR 35: propellers
  FAR 121: airline operations (AOC holders)
  FAR 135: commuter and on-demand operations

EUROPEAN: EASA CS-25 (transport category) — largely harmonized with FAR 25
  DO-178C: Software Considerations in Airborne Systems (DAL levels A-E)
    DAL-A: catastrophic failure condition; must show no errors
    Software verification through coverage analysis (MC/DC for safety-critical)
  DO-254: hardware design assurance (equivalent for hardware)
  ARP4754A: guidelines for development of civil aircraft systems

TYPE CERTIFICATE (TC): the fundamental certification of an aircraft design
  Supplemental TC (STC): modification of an existing TC
  Production approval (PA): manufacturer can produce to TC specifications
  Airworthiness Certificate: individual aircraft airworthy → allowed to fly

DESIGN PHILOSOPHY:
  Fail-safe: critical structural elements designed so that failure does not lead to
    catastrophic failure (multiple load paths; crack stoppers)
  Damage tolerance: structure must tolerate assumed damage and survive for inspection interval
  Safe-life: component retirement before expected failure (fatigue life tracking)
```

## 4. Aircraft Categories and Performance Metrics

```
AIRCRAFT TYPES:
  General aviation (GA): FAR 23; piston/turboprop single/multi-engine
  Regional turboprop: ATR-72, Dash-8; 50-80 seats
  Regional jet: Embraer E-jets, Bombardier CRJ; 50-120 seats
  Narrow-body airliner: A320neo, B737 MAX; 150-220 seats; Mach 0.78-0.82
  Wide-body airliner: A350, B787, A330; 250-400 seats; long-range
  Ultra-long-range: A350-900ULR; 19,000 km; Mach 0.85

PERFORMANCE METRICS:
  Specific fuel consumption (SFC): fuel flow / thrust (kg/N·s); lower is better
  Specific impulse (Isp = thrust / weight flow): seconds; rockets use this
  Lift-to-drag ratio (L/D): aerodynamic efficiency
    GA aircraft: L/D ≈ 10-15
    Airliners: L/D ≈ 17-20 at cruise
    Gliders: L/D ≈ 40-70
    Concorde (supersonic): L/D ≈ 7 (why it's so inefficient)
  Range equation (Breguet): R = (V/c) × (L/D) × ln(W₁/W₂)
    V = cruise speed, c = SFC, L/D = aerodynamic efficiency, W₁/W₂ = fuel ratio
    → Maximize: speed, L/D, and fuel fraction; minimize SFC
  Payload-Range trade: structural weight limits total takeoff weight;
    carry more fuel = less payload and vice versa
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Parameter | What it determines | Key relation |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Wing area (S) | Stall speed, maximum lift | L = ½ρV²·S·CL |
| Aspect ratio (AR) | Induced drag | Di ∝ 1/AR |

## Common Confusion Points

**Lift is not solely due to Bernoulli:** The popular Bernoulli explanation (air travels faster over upper surface because of longer path) is incomplete and partially wrong (equal transit fallacy: air does NOT have to arrive simultaneously at trailing edge). Lift comes from circulation (angular momentum imparted to fluid) and the associated pressure difference. Kutta-Joukowski theorem: L = ρVΓ.

**Indicated vs True vs Equivalent Airspeed:** These are different. IAS → CAS (instrument correction) → EAS (compressibility correction) → TAS (density correction). Stall speed in IAS is constant with altitude; in TAS it increases with altitude. Airspeed indicators read IAS (directly measures dynamic pressure). Cruise control is in Mach number and IAS.

**Aspect ratio matters enormously for induced drag:** A wing with AR=12 (modern airliner) has half the induced drag of AR=6 at the same lift. Winglets increase effective AR without increasing span (structurally limited). Low-AR wings (delta) are needed at high Mach (reduce compressibility drag despite high induced drag).

**Engine bypass ratio: turbofan vs turbojet:** Pure turbojet: all air through core. Turbofan with BPR=8: 8× as much air bypasses core as goes through. Most of the thrust from the fan, not the core. Much lower exhaust velocity → much better propulsive efficiency. Modern airliner engines: BPR 10-18.
