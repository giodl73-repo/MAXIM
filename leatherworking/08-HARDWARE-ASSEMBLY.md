# Hardware and Assembly: Rivets, Snaps, Buckles, D-Rings, Gussets

## The Big Picture

Hardware serves both mechanical and aesthetic functions. The choice of hardware material (brass vs. steel vs. nickel vs. antique brass) establishes the piece's visual register. The setting method determines whether the hardware holds under load. Most hardware failures in leatherwork are setting errors — wrong tool, wrong force, wrong anvil — rather than hardware defects.

```
HARDWARE SELECTION MATRIX
===========================

FUNCTION                HARDWARE OPTIONS           TYPICAL MATERIAL
--------------------    ----------------------     ----------------
Strap to strap join     Rivet (solid/tubular)      Copper, brass, steel
                        Chicago screw (decorative) Brass, nickel, steel

Adjustable length       Roller buckle              Brass, nickel, antique
(belt, strap)           Center bar buckle

Bag closure             Turn lock / twist lock     Brass, antique brass
                        Clasp / hook-and-bar       Brass, nickel
                        Magnetic snap              Nickel (hidden)

Attachment point        D-ring + rivet             Brass, steel
(bag handle)            O-ring + strap             Brass
                        Swivel hook                Nickel

Closure (snaps)         Line 24 snap (4-part)      Antique brass, nickel
                        Line 20 snap (smaller)     Antique brass, nickel
                        Sam Browne stud            Brass

Strap end               Chicago screw or rivet     Brass

MATERIAL MATCHING RULE: All hardware on a piece should be same finish
  (don't mix antique brass and bright nickel)
  Exception: intentional contrast choice (must be deliberate)
```

## Rivets

```
RIVET TYPES AND SETTING
========================

SOLID BRASS RIVETS:
  Construction: two pieces — head + post (tubular or solid)
  Tubular: post has hollow channel; peened to rivet
  Solid: harder; stronger; requires more force
  Setting:
    1. Punch hole: same diameter as rivet post
    2. Insert rivet through stacked pieces
    3. Place on anvil (domed or flat depending on desired finish)
    4. Strike post with peening tool or ball-peen hammer
    5. Post flares to larger diameter → locks permanently

COPPER RIVETS (lace rivets):
  Soft copper post + burr (washer-shaped keeper)
  Process: insert post; slip burr over post end
           cut post ~3mm above burr; peen with peening tool
  Traditional on denim (Levi Strauss patent 1873); also leather
  Strong; traditional look; copper patinas with age

DOUBLE-CAP RIVETS (steel or brass):
  Two decorative caps; no visible post
  Process: punch holes in both pieces;
           male cap (post) through from one side
           female cap snaps over post from other side
           Set with rivet setter tool + mallet
  Note: steel rivet caps are structural; brass may decorative-only
  Check pull-out strength before using on load-bearing application

LINE 20 vs. LINE 24 (decorative rivets):
  Smaller (line 20) vs. larger (line 24) diameter caps
  Not structural; purely decorative

CHICAGO SCREWS:
  Male + female threaded posts that screw together through leather
  Advantages: REMOVABLE (unlike rivets); can be disassembled
  Use for: watchband attachment; optional removable straps; prototyping
  Stack height: must match total leather thickness being joined
               Too short: can't close; Too long: loose, rattles
               Rule: stack height = total leather thickness + 1/16"
  Brass vs. steel: brass more traditional; steel stronger
```

## Snap Fasteners

```
SNAP TYPES AND SETTING
=======================

4-PART SNAP SYSTEM (most common):
  Part A: Socket (female) cap — smooth round face
  Part B: Socket — ball cavity
  Part C: Stud (male) cap — smooth round face
  Part D: Post (stud post)

  Installation:
    TOP PIECE: A (cap) on outside; B (socket) on inside
               Hole size: match post diameter
               Set with setter (hole in center) + base + mallet

    BOTTOM PIECE: C (cap) on outside; D (post) on inside
               Set same way

  The post (D) snaps into socket (B) when pressed; releases when pulled

LINE 24 vs. LINE 20:
  Line 24: larger (15mm diameter); heavier duty; jackets, bags, holsters
  Line 20: smaller (12mm); lighter goods, wallets
  Setting tools: specific to snap size (not interchangeable)

MAGNETIC SNAPS:
  Two magnets in decorative caps; no mechanical snap
  Use: bag closures; hidden closure (no visible snap)
  Concern: magnets can damage electronics (credit cards, phones)
            Place away from items the bag will carry
  Setting: punch hole; fold cap prongs through leather; bend prongs flat

SAM BROWNE STUD:
  Decorative solid brass post + loop
  Strap loops over post; does not snap
  Traditional military/officer use; luggage straps
  Setting: Chicago screw or rivet from back of leather
```

## Buckles

```
BUCKLE TYPES AND USE
====================

ROLLER BUCKLE (standard):
  Frame + roller + tongue
  Roller rotates to reduce strap wear; preferred for frequently-adjusted items
  Used for: belts, bag straps, watch bands
  Center bar vs. roller: roller = less friction; center bar = simpler

  ATTACHING BUCKLE TO STRAP:
    Slot punch at strap end: slot = buckle bar width + 2mm clearance
    Fold strap over buckle bar: ~3" folded section
    Punch matching holes in both layers (for rivet or Chicago screw)
    Rivet or screw through: permanent (rivet) or removable (Chicago screw)
    Skive the fold area: thin the leather that folds over the buckle bar

D-RING:
  D-shaped ring with flat bar
  Used for: handle attachment; adjustment; joining two straps
  Setting: same as buckle — strap loops through flat bar, folds back, riveted

O-RING:
  Circular ring
  Pass strap through O-ring; stitch or rivet back
  Less directional than D-ring; used where rotation acceptable

LOBSTER CLASP / SWIVEL HOOK:
  Spring-loaded clip; clips to D-ring
  Used for: detachable straps; key fobs; crossbody bag straps
  Sold in various sizes; width must match strap width

SIZING BUCKLES TO STRAPS:
  Buckle frame inner width must equal strap width exactly
    (or +0.5mm for easy movement; not more or strap flops)
  Belt buckle: same width as belt strap
  Bag strap buckle: same width as bag strap (1" strap → 1" buckle)
```

## Gusset Construction

```
GUSSET DESIGN AND CONSTRUCTION
================================

WHAT IS A GUSSET:
  Expansion panel that adds depth to a flat bag or pocket
  Without gusset: flat wallet, clutch, envelope
  With gusset: depth for contents; bag can hold volume

GUSSET TYPES:
  STRAIGHT GUSSET:
    Rectangular strip; connects two face panels
    Sewn around perimeter of both faces
    Used for: flat bags, pouches
    Challenge: corners must be mitered or darted

  ACCORDION GUSSET:
    Gusset folds inward when empty; expands when filled
    Allows bag to collapse flat when not in use
    Fold lines at regular intervals; must be scored to fold cleanly

GUSSET SIZING:
  Gusset width = how wide the bag opens / depth of contents
  Common: 1"–4" for bags; 1/4"–1/2" for wallets
  Gusset length = perimeter of bag face minus corners

CORNER SOLUTIONS:
  MITERED CORNER (stitched):
    Gusset notched at corner; two sides sewn together at 45°
    Result: crisp square corner; more complex stitching
    Use for: structured bags; professional finish

  PINCHED/DARTED CORNER:
    Gusset gathered/darted at corner; stitched flat
    Result: slightly soft corner
    Faster than miter; less precise

  ROUNDED CORNER:
    Pattern includes rounded corner on both face and gusset
    Gusset simply bends around the curve; no notching required
    Easiest; design must plan for rounded corners
    Use for: soft bags, pouches, toiletry bags

HARDWARE INTEGRATION:
  D-ring riveted to gusset top: strap attachment point
  Zipper: run along top of gusset (or face); must account for zipper tape thickness
  Magnetic snap: through face panel; gusset clear of snap position
```

## Turn Locks and Closures

```
TURN LOCK / TWIST LOCK HARDWARE
=================================

TURN LOCK (Tuck Lock):
  Two parts: base (riveted to bag face) + tongue (on flap)
  Flap closes over base; turn 90° → locked; turn back → open
  Used on: briefcases, structured bags, purses
  Setting: punch rectangular slot for base post; rivet or screw
  Notes: base must align with tongue precisely; use template

MAGNETIC CLASP:
  Two magnets; one in each half of closure
  Click together when aligned; pull apart when opened
  Setting: prong-based (most common):
    Punch two holes through leather; insert prong-post; fold prongs outward
    Reinforce leather with backing leather or Bag-E-Wash on inside
    (prongs apply stress concentration; reinforcement prevents tearing)

HOOK AND BAR CLOSURE:
  Traditional for purses, saddlebags
  Hook (bowed metal bar) passes through ring/bar
  Can be subtle or decorative (cast brass)
  Setting: rivet or Chicago screw through strap/flap

ZIPPER INSTALLATION:
  Zipper tape width + clearance = space needed in pattern
  Top-sew zipper: sew through face and zipper tape; tape visible
  Recessed zipper: zipper tape behind face; edge sewn over tape
  Tools required: zipper foot (machine); leather needle; slow speed
  Leather preparation: skive the edges that fold over zipper tape
  Critical: test zipper pull clearance before final assembly
```

## Assembly Order — Critical Sequence

```
LEATHER ASSEMBLY SEQUENCING
=============================

RULE: Install hardware that requires access from both sides BEFORE
      assembling pieces that close that access.

Example: Installing turn lock in bag face:
  1. Mark hole position on face BEFORE any other assembly
  2. Punch holes; install turn lock
  3. THEN attach face to gusset/body

If you assemble first and THEN try to install the turn lock:
  You may not be able to reach inside to fold the prongs
  Or: the backing reinforcement can't be accessed
  → Must disassemble; waste of rivets and effort

GENERAL SEQUENCE:
  1. Cut all pieces
  2. Skive all edges requiring thinning
  3. Install hardware requiring through-access (turn locks, magnetic snaps)
     Add interior reinforcement before closing
  4. Install any interior pockets / linings
  5. Assemble main structure (gusset to faces)
  6. Add handles/straps (rivet/screw attachment points)
  7. Install buckles and adjustable hardware last
  8. Final edge finishing (burnish edges after all glue and stitching)
  9. Conditioning and final topcoat

FOR PROTOTYPING:
  Build one complete mockup from inexpensive leather or even cardboard
  Check all hardware placement, stitch line accessibility, construction logic
  Then cut the good leather
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the correct Chicago screw stack height? | Total leather thickness + 1/16" |
| What is the difference between solid and tubular rivets? | Solid = peened (stronger); tubular = hollow post flared outward |
| What is a 4-part snap system? | Cap + socket (top piece) + cap + post (bottom piece) — 4 separate parts |
| Line 24 vs. Line 20 snaps? | Line 24 = larger (15mm); Line 20 = smaller (12mm) |
| What buckle width for a 1.5" strap? | 1.5" buckle (inner frame width must match strap width exactly) |
| What is a gusset? | Expansion panel between bag faces that adds depth |
| What is a mitered corner on a gusset? | Gusset notched at corners; two sides joined at 45° → crisp square corner |
| Why install hardware before assembly? | Many hardware settings require access from both sides, inaccessible after assembly |
| What reinforces magnetic snap installation? | Backing leather or stabilizing fabric on leather inside; prongs distribute force |
| What is a Sam Browne stud? | Decorative brass post + loop; strap loops over without snapping; military/officer tradition |

## Common Confusion Points

**Chicago screws are not interchangeable by diameter alone.** The thread pitch, diameter, and stack height must all be correct for the specific screw size. Using a slightly longer Chicago screw than the leather thickness produces a loose joint that rattles. Using too short produces a joint that doesn't close. Buy in the exact stack height for your leather combination.

**Line 24 and Line 20 snaps use different setting tools.** This seems obvious but is a constant source of frustration. The setter has a centered post; the size of that post is specific to the snap size. Using the wrong setter deforms the cap without properly setting the snap. Label your snap setters.

**Gussets must accommodate zipper tape if used together.** A zipper added to a gusset design increases the total height needed at the attachment point by the zipper tape width (typically 5–8mm). This changes the pattern dimensions for both the zipper strip and how it attaches to the face panels. Always add the zipper in the pattern design phase, not as an afterthought.

**Magnetic clasps weaken leather over time.** The prong-fold installation method applies stress to a small area of leather at each prong. Without a backing reinforcement piece (another layer of leather or stiffener behind the clasp), the stress concentration at the prongs tears through the leather. This is one of the most common failure points in bags with magnetic closures. The fix is simple: always back magnetic clasps with a second layer of leather.

**Assembly sequence errors are often irreversible.** Unlike woodworking where you can sometimes disassemble a glued joint, riveted leather is typically permanent. Chicago screws can be removed, but rivets cannot. Planning the assembly sequence carefully — particularly knowing which hardware requires access from both sides — prevents the frustrating experience of finishing most of a bag and then realizing you can't install the turn lock.
