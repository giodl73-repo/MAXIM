# Tooling and Carving: Casing, Swivel Knife, Stamps, Sheridan Style

## The Big Picture

Leather tooling is the art of pressing and cutting designs into the surface of dampened vegetable-tanned leather. The moisture temporarily softens the fibers, allowing them to be displaced by stamps and swivel knife cuts, and they retain the impression as the leather dries. This process only works on veg-tan leather — chrome-tan lacks the firm structure and fiber organization to hold impressions.

```
TOOLING WORKFLOW
=================

SELECT VEG-TAN LEATHER (4–7 oz typical for carving)
     |
     v
TRANSFER DESIGN (stylus or swivel knife directly)
  Tape tracing paper pattern to leather
  Use stylus to transfer design lines
  Alternatively: draw freehand with stylus
     |
     v
CASE THE LEATHER (dampen uniformly)
  Sponge or spray water on grain side until:
  "Toothpick test" — press toothpick; leaves mark; snaps back = ready
     |
     v
SWIVEL KNIFE WORK
  Cut design lines with swivel knife
  Creates beveling guide and design outline
     |
     v
STAMPING / BEVELING
  Bevel (B200): press fibers down beside swivel knife cuts
  Background stamps: texture the area around design
  Modeling tools: smooth and shape raised areas
     |
     v
DRY COMPLETELY before dyeing
     |
     v
DYES (antique finish; base color; highlights)
     |
     v
FINISH COAT
```

## Casing Leather — The Critical Step

The moisture content of the leather during tooling determines everything. Too wet: impressions don't hold (fibers too mobile). Too dry: fibers resist displacement; won't take clean impressions.

```
CASING TECHNIQUE
=================

METHOD:
  Wet grain side with sponge or spray bottle
  Allow to absorb until surface is uniformly damp
  Two indicators:
  1. COLOR: Leather appears darker overall (uniform moisture absorption)
     AND has re-lightened somewhat (first wet darkens; as moisture distributes
     evenly, it lightens back toward natural color)
  2. TOOTHPICK TEST:
     Press toothpick firmly into surface
     Result: leaves clear impression; SNAPS BACK immediately = correct
     Too wet: impression stays compressed; no spring-back = wait
     Too dry: barely marks; stiff = re-wet and wait

TIMING:
  After wetting: wait 5–15 minutes (thin leather: less; thick leather: more)
  Working window: 20–60 minutes before leather dries too much
  Re-casing: if leather dries during work, re-wet from the back (flesh) side
             (Dampening from back distributes moisture more slowly;
              surface doesn't go too wet and lose impressions already made)

TEMPERATURE EFFECTS:
  Hot shop: leather cases faster; dries faster; shorter working window
  Cool shop: slower casing; longer working window
  Humidity: high humidity extends working window

WHAT CASING DOES TO FIBER:
  Water temporarily disrupts hydrogen bonds in collagen
  Fibers become mobile and displace under pressure
  When leather dries: fibers lock in displaced position
  Result: permanent 3D impression retained
```

## Swivel Knife — The Primary Tooling Instrument

```
SWIVEL KNIFE ANATOMY AND TECHNIQUE
=====================================

ANATOMY:
  Barrel (rotates freely in hand)
  Blade: specific profile — wide bevel, thin edge
  Yoke (frame)
  Finger saddle (top ring; index finger guides)

  ← rotate barrel with fingers →
  ↑ index finger in saddle (guides direction)
  ↓ lean blade toward design inside of curves

GRIP:
  Index finger rests in top saddle ring (steers)
  Thumb and middle finger rotate barrel (swivel function)
  Third and fourth fingers provide balance and support

THREE MOTIONS:
  1. FORWARD (straight lines): push forward; no rotation
  2. ROTATION: rotate barrel to change direction (pivot on tip)
  3. LEAN: blade leans into inside of curves (1–15° tilt)
            Leaning = deeper on outside of curve; thins beveling result

BLADE ANGLES:
  Heel of blade contacts leather → too deep = cuts through
  Toe contacts → too shallow = doesn't cut fiber enough
  Optimal: blade tip + slightly toward heel; smooth sliding feel

CUTTING DEPTH:
  ~1/2 leather thickness (never through)
  Cut too shallow: bevel won't stand up; looks flat
  Cut too deep: structural weakness; tool catches

SHARPENING:
  Strop on fine leather charged with jeweler's rouge or green compound
  Both sides of the bevel; maintain profile
  Stroke of the strop: only in the cutting direction
  A dull swivel knife tears instead of cuts; visible under magnification
```

## Beveler and Stamp Families

```
BEVELING TOOLS AND FAMILIES
=============================

BEVELER (B-series):
  B200: Most common "workhorse" beveler
        Texture matches most design styles
        Use: press and rock into swivel knife cut (angled 45°)
        Result: pressed fibers on one side of cut stand up;
                other side is angled down (creates relief depth)

  B197: Narrower; for tight curves
  B202, B205: Heavier texture; more decorative impression

  TECHNIQUE:
    Hold beveler at ~45° to leather surface
    Strike with mallet; move forward; strike again
    Each strike overlaps previous slightly
    Keep cut line on the LEFT side of the beveler (bevel presses right side down)
    On curves: rotate beveler to follow curve

BACKGROUND STAMPS (BG-series):
  Texture the background around the design
  Lowers the background visually; raises the design by contrast
  Common: Matting stamp (BG-series) or pear shader
  Application: uniform texture across entire background area
  Time-consuming: requires many overlapping strikes

PEAR SHADER (P-series):
  Smooth rounded stamp
  Creates convex bulge in petals, leaves, rounded design elements
  Strikes center of element; edges: less pressure
  Effect: 3D curvature within a design element

VEINER / SEEDER:
  V-shaped tools, teardrop shapes, small seeds
  Add decorative details to flowers, leaves, borders

CAMOUFLAGE / BASKET WEAVE:
  Geometric repeating pattern; no swivel knife needed
  Basket weave: single stamp size; press in grid with offset
  Self-indexing with notch on each stamp face
  Common beginner project; impressive visual effect
```

## Sheridan Floral Style

The dominant tradition in Western decorative leather carving:

```
SHERIDAN STYLE FUNDAMENTALS
=============================

HISTORY:
  Developed in Sheridan, Wyoming (hence the name)
  Master craftsmen: Don King, Bill Güerther (1940s–1970s)
  Still the dominant style for Western saddlery and accessories

VISUAL ELEMENTS:
  Flowing stems and vines (curved swivel knife work)
  Five-petal flowers (layered petals with pear shader)
  Leaves with center vein and veiner detail
  Acanthus scrolls (tight spiral stems)
  Seeds and small filler elements

DESIGN PRINCIPLES:
  Asymmetry within symmetry (no exact mirrors; slightly varied)
  Elements flow from a main stem
  Negative space balanced with positive design
  "Seed" elements fill any empty spaces

CARVING SEQUENCE FOR A FLORAL ELEMENT:
  1. Transfer or draw design on cased leather
  2. Swivel knife: cut all stems, petal outlines, leaf veins
  3. Bevel: all stem edges (inside of curves); leaf edges
  4. Pear shader: each petal (center strike; lighter on edges)
  5. Background: mat stamp entire background
  6. Seeder: add seeds in open spaces
  7. Veiner: detail leaf veins, petal details
  8. Camouflage: optional; fills remaining background

SCALE:
  Beginners: 4" × 6" panel; single flower with two leaves
  Intermediate: 6" × 9"; full design with multiple flowers and vine
  Advanced: saddle cantle; skirting; full belt design

LEARNING RESOURCES:
  Ron Ross (Sheridan master); Bob Beard; Al Stohlman (Al Stohlman books
  = still standard reference after 50+ years)
```

## Dyeing Before vs. After Tooling

```
DYE TIMING DECISION
====================

DYE BEFORE TOOLING:
  Apply base color to leather; allow to dry completely
  Then case and tool
  Advantages:
    Tooling impressions visible immediately in contrast
    Easier to see where you've been
  Disadvantages:
    Water from casing can reactivate alcohol dyes → migrate
    Must re-wet carefully to avoid washing out dye
    Not common in Sheridan-style tooling

DYE AFTER TOOLING (standard approach):
  Tool on natural veg-tan
  Allow leather to dry completely after tooling
  Apply dye (usually antique finish over base)
  Advantages:
    Tooled surface is the reference state (undyed = clean canvas)
    Antique finish process works best on natural leather
    No dye migration issues during casing
    Most professional Sheridan-style work done this way

ANTIQUE FINISH PROCESS:
  1. Apply base dye (or leave natural)
  2. Apply antique paste (Fiebing's Antique Finish, Leather Craft)
     Work into recesses (swivel knife cuts, background stamps)
  3. Wipe back with soft cloth (circular motion)
     The antique pigment remains in the recesses (cuts, stamp texture)
     The high relief areas (raised design, smooth leather) wipes clean
  4. Result: dark recesses + lighter raised design = dramatic 3D contrast
  5. Protect with finish coat (resolene, acrylic finisher)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Casing too wet vs. casing correctly.** Beginners often skip the wait time after wetting. Freshly wet leather is too wet — the fibers are fully mobile and won't hold impressions. The leather must absorb the water and the surface must return from uniformly dark to a slightly lighter, more uniform tone. This waiting step is not optional; it's the entire mechanism.

**Swivel knife technique is a skill that takes months.** The barrel rotation, lean, and forward motion must be smooth and continuous. Most beginners produce scratchy, hesitant cuts because they are thinking too much about each motion. The correct approach: practice flowing continuous strokes on scrap; the muscle memory is what matters, not the intellectual understanding.

**Beveling direction is a choice, not an accident.** The bevel presses fibers down on one side of the cut. You choose which side based on the design — typically the inside of curves (so the design element stands up above the background). Incorrect beveling direction produces a muddy, flat result even with good swivel knife work.

**Antique finish requires a very dry leather to work correctly.** Applying antique finish to leather that still has any moisture from casing traps moisture under the finish coat and produces patchy, uneven results. Tooled leather must be completely dry (overnight minimum; 24+ hours in humid conditions) before dyeing and finishing.

**Background stamping takes as long as all the rest of the work combined.** New craftspeople dramatically underestimate the time required to stamp the background. The background must be uniformly textured (no gaps, no double-struck overlaps too visible) across the entire area around the design. For a 6" × 9" panel, this can take an hour. The result is worth it — without properly stamped background, the design elements don't "pop."
