# Cutting and Skiving: Knives, Punches, Edge Thinning

## The Big Picture

Cutting and skiving are the foundational operations before any assembly. Cutting defines the shape; skiving (edge thinning) defines the thickness at edges, folds, and seam areas. Bad cutting and skiving compound into every subsequent step — it is far easier to produce clean edges at the cutting stage than to compensate later.

```
CUTTING TOOL LANDSCAPE
========================

STRAIGHT CUTS:
  Straight knife / head knife     → Long straight lines; leather paring
  Round knife / half-moon knife   → Curved and straight; versatile workhorse
  Strap cutter                    → Consistent-width straps (uses blades)
  Rotary cutter                   → Fast straight lines; requires cutting mat

CURVED CUTS:
  Round knife (curved use)        → Any curve; controlled by rotation
  Swivel knife (for cutting)      → Sharp curves (usually used for tooling,
                                    but also cuts intricate patterns)
  Scissors (heavy duty)           → Garment work; thin leather

HOLES AND PUNCHES:
  Drive punch (round)             → Single hole; belt holes; snap settings
  Oblong punch (oval)             → Buckle slot; strap end holes
  Chicago screw punch             → Specific hole size for hardware
  Rotary punch                    → Multi-size punch wheel; less precise
  Hole punch plier                → Quick single holes in thin leather

EDGE TOOLS:
  Edge beveler (#1–#3)            → Chamfer 45° on edge; removes square corner
  Wing divider / stitch groover   → Marks parallel line (stitch guide)
  Skiver / draw gauge             → Controlled thickness reduction at edge
```

## Cutting Surfaces

The cutting surface determines the quality of cuts and tool life:

```
CUTTING SURFACE OPTIONS
========================

MARBLE SLAB / GRANITE PLATE:
  Traditional and still best for head knife / round knife work
  Hard, smooth, non-porous surface
  Knife edge glides cleanly; no resistance or drag
  Does not absorb cutting blows (vs. wood which has give)
  Result: cleanest cuts possible
  Limitation: heavy; expensive; fixed location
  Size: minimum 12" × 12"; 18" × 24" preferred

TEMPERED GLASS:
  Similar to marble for cutting purposes
  Lighter; less expensive
  Breaks if dropped; thermal shock risk
  Adequate substitute for marble if careful

CUTTING MAT (self-healing):
  Flexible; portable; cheap
  "Self-healing" = compressed fibers re-close after cut
  NOT ideal for heavy leather and round knife work
  OK for: thin leather, rotary cutter, punches
  The slight cushion reduces precision on straight knife cuts
  Will show tracks over time

LEATHER (bench cover):
  Some craftspeople use a thick scrap of leather over a hard surface
  The grip + cushion suits some operations (hand punching)
  But degrades straight cuts

RECOMMENDATION:
  Primary surface: marble or granite (investments pay long-term)
  Secondary: self-healing mat for rough layout and portable work
```

## Round Knife vs. Head Knife — Technique

```
ROUND KNIFE vs. HEAD KNIFE
============================

ROUND KNIFE (half-moon knife):
  Blade: curved half-circle; both sides can cut
  Use: rocking motion across leather
  ↻ rock forward  ← blade rotates on its curve
  Long cuts: series of overlapping rocking cuts
  Advantage: intuitive; one tool for many operations;
             can cut along curves by changing rock orientation
  Disadvantage: less precision on very long straight cuts than head knife

HEAD KNIFE (rectangular blade):
  Blade: rectangular; sharpened on one long edge
  Use: pull cut (draw toward body) along a ruler or freehand
  Long straight cuts: ruler + single pull stroke
  Advantage: precision straight cuts; controlled pressure on thin leather
  Disadvantage: awkward on curves

JAPANESE vs. WESTERN TECHNIQUE:
  Western: push cut (away from body); knife angled forward
  Japanese: pull cut (toward body); knife pulled along leather
  Both work; pull cut gives more control for many craftspeople
  Safety: both require "off-path" placement of non-cutting hand

CUTTING TECHNIQUE FUNDAMENTALS:
  1. Sharp knife is mandatory — a dull knife requires more force and skids
  2. Cut in one clean stroke if possible (multiple short strokes leave ridges)
  3. Cut against a metal ruler for straight lines (wood can be cut into)
  4. Keep knife vertical to surface (angled knife = angled edge)
  5. Don't drag — rock, pull, or push deliberately
```

## Strap Cutters and Consistent-Width Cutting

```
STRAP CUTTER SYSTEMS
=====================

ADJUSTABLE STRAP CUTTER (steel or wood):
  Movable fence at fixed distance from blade
  Draw knife along edge → consistent parallel strip
  Width setting: loosen, set fence, tighten
  Limitation: requires perfectly straight starting edge

  +---FENCE---+
  |           |   blade at right angle to fence
  |  →→→→→→  |   leather passes beneath fence
  +-----blade-+

APPLICATIONS:
  Belts: typically 1.25", 1.5", 2" widths
  Bag handles: 0.75"–1.5"
  Lacing: 1/8"–1/4" (requires very sharp knife)

RULER + WING DIVIDERS:
  Set wing dividers to desired width
  Scribe both edges from the straight reference edge
  Cut along scribed lines
  More work but no strap cutter required

ROLLING CUTTER + RULER:
  Rotary cutter with metal ruler: very fast on thin leather
  45° angle produces straight cut with controlled pressure
  Useful for: lining leather, thin straps, garment work
```

## Skiving — Edge Thinning

Skiving is the process of reducing leather thickness at edges, folds, and seam areas. Critical for producing clean folds, reducing bulk at stitched edges, and creating professional-looking folded handles and bag edges.

```
SKIVING CONCEPTS
=================

WHY SKIVE:
  1. FOLDED EDGES: Leather folded over a core (handle, bag edge)
     Must skive the fold edge to ~1/3 original thickness
     → Fold lays flat without bunching at the fold line

  2. OVERLAPPED JOINTS: Two layers of full-thickness leather = excessive bulk
     Skive one or both layers at the overlap zone
     → Overlap is same total thickness as single layer

  3. STITCH LINE THINNING: Stitching through two layers of thick leather
     Skive one layer at the stitch zone → cleaner stitching

  4. LINING APPLICATION: Thin lining leather glued to back
     Skive the outer leather's edges where lining will meet → seamless transition

SKIVE TYPES:
  +---+----------+----+
  EDGE SKIVE:         Wedge from full thickness to near-zero at edge
  (most common)       +─────────────────┐
                      │ full thickness   \  ← skive removes this wedge
                      +──────────────────┘
                                         edge (near zero thickness)

  HORIZONTAL SKIVE:   Reduce to half thickness uniformly across a zone
                      Used for overlap areas; entire zone thinned equally

  FEATHER EDGE:       Taper to absolute minimum at the edge (nearly nothing)
                      Used for very visible folds; maximum cleanliness
```

## Skiving Tools and Technique

```
SKIVING TOOLS
==============

DRAW SKIVER:
  Round or straight blade in a wooden or plastic handle
  Pull toward body; angle controls how much is removed
  Technique: angle = angle of finished skive
  Best for: longer straight skives; consistent angle
  Limitation: requires practice; easy to cut through if not careful

PUSH SKIVER (Japanese):
  Long, flat blade; push away from body
  Better control for some; preference is personal
  Common in Japanese leather craft (Leathercraft Japan style)

BENCH SKIVER (HEAD KNIFE USED AS SKIVER):
  Head knife or round knife held very flat against marble
  Leather pulled through at low angle while knife holds position
  Very accurate for consistent-depth skive across a width
  Requires marble (flat, hard surface critical)

ELECTRIC SKIVER:
  Motorized blade spins; leather fed under guard
  Very fast; very consistent
  Required for production work (manual skiving at scale = fatigue)
  Risk: easy to remove too much material if feeding too fast

TESTING THICKNESS AFTER SKIVING:
  Thumb + forefinger squeeze: feel for consistent thin area
  Bend test: skived edge should fold without ridging
  Look from edge: should see smooth taper from full to thin
```

## Edge Beveling

```
EDGE BEVELER TECHNIQUE
========================

PURPOSE: Remove the sharp 90° corner from a cut leather edge
         Creates a 45° chamfer → professional appearance
         Required before burnishing (sharp corner won't burnish)

TOOL: Edge beveler (#1, #2, #3)
  #1: narrowest cut (light/thin leather)
  #2: medium (most common; 5–7 oz leather)
  #3: wide cut (heavy leather, 8+ oz)

TECHNIQUE:
  Hold beveler at consistent angle (~45°)
  Push forward along edge in smooth strokes
  Maintain consistent speed and pressure
  Both corners of each edge (top face AND bottom face)
  Corners (radius points): rotate beveler through curve

COMMON ERROR:
  Removing too much material (using too large a beveler or too much pressure)
  Result: rounded edge instead of crisp chamfer
  Prevention: test on scrap first; consistent pressure

AFTER BEVELING:
  The edge is ready for:
    Burnishing (naked edge finishing)
    Dyeing (edge paint/dye application)
    Edge paint / Tokonole finishing
```

## Punches and Hole Patterns

```
PUNCH SELECTION GUIDE
======================

DRIVE PUNCH (round):
  Sizes: 1mm to 25mm (common belt hole: 3mm–4mm)
  Use: mallet on marble (or lead punch block)
  Belt hole spacing: 1" apart; first hole at 4" from strap end;
                     provide 5 holes minimum for adjustment
  Setting: back face of punch must be flat → rings if struck squarely

OBLONG PUNCH:
  For buckle slots; strap end holes
  Size: must accommodate buckle pin + clearance
  Single strike (vs. multiple overlapping round punches → cleaner)

PRICKING IRON vs. STITCHING CHISEL:
  Pricking iron: diamond-shaped tines; marks hole positions; does not fully punch
                  Spacing: 3mm, 4mm, 5mm between holes (common choices)
  Stitching chisel: chisel-profile tines; actually cuts slits
  Both drive with mallet on marble
  Use pricking iron to mark holes along pre-grooved stitch line;
  then punch through with stitching chisel

CONSISTENT HOLE SPACING:
  Maintain constant mallet force
  Overhang: one tine from previous set seats in last hole of previous set
            → automatically maintains consistent spacing
```

## Common Confusion Points

**A dull knife is more dangerous than a sharp one.** A sharp knife cuts leather with controlled light pressure. A dull knife requires force, which means the knife skids if the leather moves — toward whatever hand is holding the leather. Sharpen frequently; the difference in control is dramatic.

**Skiving through the leather is a common beginner error.** The leather's thickness is often only 3–4mm. The skive removes material from one face; if the angle is wrong or the motion too aggressive, you cut through. Practice on scrap leather identical to your project leather until you can consistently skive to 50%, 25%, and 10% of original thickness without cutting through.

**Pricking irons vs. stitching chisels are both needed.** Using only a stitching chisel directly: accurate but slower. Using only a pricking iron: marks without fully opening. The standard workflow is pricking iron (mark all holes at once along a stitch line, checking spacing) then stitching awl or chisel to fully open each hole. Some craftspeople use only the pricking iron then open each hole with a stitching awl individually.

**Edge beveling must be done before any edge finishing.** You cannot burnish a sharp 90° corner — the fibers won't consolidate properly. The bevel chamfers the corner, creating the surface that burnishes. Beveling also removes the sharpest part of the edge that would catch on things and cause premature wear.

**Marble matters more for heavy leather.** On thin garment leather (2–3 oz), a self-healing mat is adequate. On 7–9 oz saddle or belt leather, you need the hardness and flatness of marble — the leather is stiff enough that a soft surface causes the cut to deflect slightly, and the heavy knife requires firm support to cut cleanly in one stroke.
