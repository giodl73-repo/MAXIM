# Pattern Making: Templates, Seam Allowance, Layout on Hide

## The Big Picture

Leatherworking patterns differ from fabric sewing patterns in one critical way: leather does not have grain in the textile sense, but it does have a structural hierarchy across the hide (back = stiff and uniform; belly = stretchy and loose), and you must plan layout on the hide accordingly. Patterns are made from stiff cardstock or template plastic — leather is too expensive to work out design directly on the material.

```
PATTERN DEVELOPMENT WORKFLOW
================================

DESIGN INTENT
     |
     v
PAPER SKETCH → dimensions, proportions, construction logic
     |
     v
PAPER MOCKUP (use newsprint or cheap paper)
  Full-scale; cut and assemble with tape
  Verify: dimensions work; construction logic is sound
  Adjust here — not on leather
     |
     v
CARDSTOCK / TEMPLATE PLASTIC PATTERN
  Transfer from paper mockup to stiff cardstock
  Label: piece name; grain direction; quantity needed; any notes
  Mark: stitch lines; fold lines; hardware positions; slot/hole positions
     |
     v
LAYOUT ON HIDE (see below)
     |
     v
TRACE WITH SILVER PEN or scratch awl
CUT
```

## Seam Allowance in Leather vs. Fabric

```
SEAM ALLOWANCE COMPARISON
===========================

FABRIC SEWING:
  Seam allowance: 5/8" (15mm) standard; sometimes 1/2" or 3/8"
  Purpose: allow for sewing machine foot width + edge finishing
  Design: patterns include seam allowance; finished size = pattern - 2× SA

SADDLE STITCH / HAND STITCH:
  Standard practice: NO seam allowance
  Reason: leather edges are FINISHED (beveled, burnished) as part of the piece
  The stitch line IS the edge reference
  Pattern = finished size
  Stitch placement: 3–5mm from edge (measured with wing divider or groover)
  Edge: beveled, burnished, or painted to finish

MACHINE STITCH:
  Small seam allowance if making a lapped/hidden seam: 5–8mm
  For visible saddle stitch on machine: still minimal seam allowance
  Garment leather: standard fabric seam allowance may apply if lined
  and seam will be hidden inside

CONSTRUCTION JOINT TYPES:
  Butt seam: edges meet; stitched; no overlap
  Lap seam: one piece overlaps other; stitched through both
  Folded edge: edge folded over; stitched (requires skiving the fold edge)
  Turned and stitched: edge wrapped over a core; stitched (bags, straps)
```

## Geometric Construction for Leather Pieces

Common patterns require specific geometric skills:

```
GEOMETRIC CONSTRUCTION REFERENCE
===================================

STRAP / BELT PATTERN:
  Rectangle only — most basic
  But: taper straps (wider at attachment; narrower at end)
  Width taper: divide into equal sections; mark width at each end;
               connect with straight line

GUSSET / BOX EXPANSION PANEL:
  Gusset = expansion panel between two faces of a bag
  Geometry: gusset width = how far bag opens
  Length: perimeter of bag opening minus corner radius × 2
  Must turn corners: either mitered (sewn) or darted (folded/sewn)

ROUND SHAPES:
  Circle for clutch, card holder disc: compass or trammel
  Radius from center; cut cleanly with round knife
  Oval: two radii construction; use compass at two focal points
        or: trace an ellipse jig

HANDLES:
  Simple strap: rectangle; fold around core or use as-is
  Rolled handle: trapezoid shape; wider at center; tapered to ends;
                 wrapped around a rope core; skived edges fold over

PATTERN FOR ZIPPER POUCH:
  Front + Back: equal rectangles (or shaped)
  Gusset or none (flat pouch vs. box)
  Zipper length = width of opening - 1" (for pull access)
  Lining: mirror of exterior (all panels)

HOLSTER PATTERN:
  Trace outline of firearm directly
  Add: clearance (1/8" all around for movement)
  Add: retention strap location
  Test fit on unloaded firearm before cutting good leather
```

## Using a Hide — Layout Efficiency

```
HIDE LAYOUT PRIORITIES
========================

PREMIUM AREAS (use for show pieces and critical panels):

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │          NECK (avoid for large flat pieces)    │
  │          (wrinkles; uneven; use for straps)    │
  │   ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐     │
  │   │  BACK / BEND: Priority 1              │    │
  │   │  → wallet panels, card holder faces  │    │
  │   │  → holster faces, belt blanks        │    │
  │   │  → anything requiring flatness       │    │
  │   │    and uniform thickness             │    │
  │   └──────────────────────────────────────┘     │
  │   SHOULDER   SHOULDER                          │
  │   (Priority 2: straps, secondary panels)       │
  │   BELLY          BELLY                         │
  │   (Priority 3: linings, gussets, lacing)       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

LAYOUT RULES:
  1. Place largest pieces first (they constrain everything else)
  2. Align long pieces (belts, straps) along the back spine direction
  3. Avoid belly for structural pieces (stretches; inconsistent)
  4. Nest pieces to minimize waste (like cutting a board foot of wood)
  5. Account for scars, brand marks, insect bites → work around or use
     in hidden areas

MEASURING HIDE AREA:
  Hides sold by sq ft (irregular shape)
  Place on grid paper; count squares
  Or: string perimeter; area via formula (approximate)
  Actual usable area: ~70–80% of total sold (waste, defects, belly)
```

## Templates — Making and Using

```
TEMPLATE MATERIALS
==================

CARDSTOCK / MANILA FOLDER:
  Most common; cheap; available
  Stiff enough for small patterns
  Limitation: wears with repeated use; warps with humidity
  Use for: test patterns; one-off pieces

CLEAR TEMPLATE PLASTIC (acetate / mylar):
  Drafting film; ~0.01" thick
  Can see leather grain/defects through template
  More durable than cardstock; good for repeated use
  Mark with permanent marker

THIN PLYWOOD OR HARDBOARD (1/8"):
  Best for patterns used many times (production work)
  Trace around edge with silver/white pen; very consistent
  Make: rout or jigsaw; sand edges smooth
  Use for: anything cut more than 10× (belts, wallet panels)

TEMPLATE REGISTRATION MARKS:
  Mark on template: stitch line, edge, hardware positions
  Transfer to leather: silver pen (visible on dark leather) or
                       light scratch with round-point awl
  Stitch line positions: use wing dividers (set to stitch distance from edge)
                         more accurate than template marks

LABELING TEMPLATES:
  Write on template: piece name, quantity, version, date, scale
  Store flat (cardboard warps; roll plastic)
  Mistake-proofing: mark "GRAIN SIDE UP" or indicate any directional cut
```

## Workshop Planning: Layout from a Full Side

A practical example: planning a wallet and belt from a single shoulder:

```
LAYOUT EXERCISE: WALLET + BELT FROM SHOULDER
=============================================

AVAILABLE: One 8 oz natural veg-tan shoulder (~8–10 sq ft)

PIECES NEEDED:
  Bifold wallet exterior: 4.25" × 9.5" (opens to 8.5" tall × 4.25" wide)
  Card pockets (4): 3.5" × 3.75" each
  Bill divider: 3.75" × 8.25"
  Belt blank: 1.25" × 46" (for 34–38" waist; add 8" for buckle + tip)

PLACEMENT PLAN:
  1. Belt blank: run along the straight-grain direction of back
     Shoulder may not yield 46" in one piece → join at buckle end (hidden)
     Or: cut 48" from the back portion if shoulder extends that far
  2. Wallet exterior: place in flattest area of shoulder (near back junction)
     Avoid wrinkled neck area
  3. Card pockets: fill remaining flat area
  4. Bill divider: more flexible placement; any flat area
  5. Scraps: punch lace, small straps, test pieces

YIELD CALCULATION:
  Wallet: 1 × (4.25 × 9.5) = 40 sq in
  Card pockets: 4 × (3.5 × 3.75) = 52.5 sq in
  Bill divider: 1 × (3.75 × 8.25) = 31 sq in
  Belt: 1.25 × 46 = 57.5 sq in
  Total: ~181 sq in ÷ 144 = ~1.26 sq ft needed
  Shoulder at 8 sq ft: plenty of yield; nesting for waste reduction
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the primary seam allowance for hand-stitched leather? | None (zero) — pattern = finished size; edge is finished as-is |
| What material for durable repeated-use patterns? | Thin plywood or hardboard (1/8"); acetate/mylar for medium use |
| What hide region for critical flat panels? | Back/bend (tightest, most uniform, least stretch) |
| What hide region to avoid for structural pieces? | Belly (stretchy, loose fiber, inconsistent thickness) |
| Where to align belt/strap blanks on hide? | Along the back spine direction (parallel to the backbone) |
| What is usable yield from a purchased hide? | ~70–80% (waste from defects, edges, belly, cutting) |
| How do you transfer stitch line position? | Wing dividers (set to stitch-to-edge distance); more accurate than templates |
| What is a gusset? | Expansion panel between two faces of a bag; defines how far it opens |
| What material for a test mockup? | Newsprint or craft paper; tape together to verify construction logic |
| How do you mark on dark leather? | Silver metallic pen or light scratch with awl |

## Common Confusion Points

**Leather patterns don't scale the same way fabric patterns do.** Fabric patterns often have "ease" built in for garment fit. Leather items (wallets, holsters, bags) typically have zero ease for structural pieces — the fit is exact. If anything, tight items (card pockets) should be 1–2mm smaller to provide the gripping friction that holds the item in place.

**Belly leather is not waste — it has specific uses.** The stretchy, looser belly leather is excellent for wide gussets in bags (where some yield is actually desirable), for lacing material, and for small items that don't require structural rigidity. The error is using it for items requiring dimensional stability (wallets, card holders, holster shells).

**Test fit before cutting good leather.** This seems obvious but is systematically violated by beginners. The paper mockup step is not optional — it's where 90% of design errors are caught at paper cost rather than leather cost. A $50 side of leather is not where you discover the card pocket is 5mm too narrow.

**Silver pen vs. pencil vs. awl for marking.** Pencil: lead can transfer to the leather surface (especially on light leather); hard to see. Silver/white pen (like a Staedtler silver pen): visible; doesn't transfer; recommended. Light awl scratch: leaves a permanent groove (fine if it's exactly on a cut line; not if it's an exploratory mark). Use silver pen for all layout except the final cutting line (awl for that if precision needed).

**Layout accounts for stretch direction.** Even in veg-tan, leather has a slight directional stretch — typically more stretch across the back (perpendicular to spine) than along it. For straps and belts, this matters: align the long axis of the strap along the spine direction (minimum stretch direction) so the strap stays at its intended length under load.
