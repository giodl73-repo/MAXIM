# Marine Biology — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Ocean covers 71% of Earth's surface. Average depth: 3,688 m. Volume: ~1.335 billion km³.
The ocean is not just a habitat — it is the largest, most structurally complex biosphere on
Earth, and the one we understand least. Most of it has never been directly observed.

## Primary Production: Where Energy Enters the Ocean

```
PHOTOSYNTHETIC vs CHEMOSYNTHETIC PRIMARY PRODUCTION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SUNLIT OCEAN (>99% of total marine primary production)
  6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  Producers: diatoms, dinoflagellates, coccolithophores, cyanobacteria
  Global rate: ~50 Pg C/year
  Key constraints: NITROGEN and IRON (not CO₂ or light in open ocean)

CHEMOSYNTHETIC ECOSYSTEMS (<1% of total, locally significant)
  H₂S + CO₂ + O₂ → CH₂O + H₂SO₄    (sulfur-oxidizing bacteria)
  CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O              (methanotrophs at cold seeps)
  Producers: Gammaproteobacteria, ANME archaea
  Key constraint: flux of H₂S or CH₄ from geological sources
  Significance: life without sunlight — primary model for life elsewhere
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Ocean = fish" — wrong frame.** By mass and metabolic activity, the ocean is dominated by
microorganisms — bacteria, archaea, viruses, phytoplankton. Fish are a small fraction of total
ocean biomass. The microbial loop processes more carbon than fish ever touch.

**Phytoplankton vs zooplankton.** "Phyto" = plant-like = photosynthetic (energy producers).
"Zoo" = animal-like = heterotrophic (energy consumers). Both are plankton = passive drifters,
not active swimmers. The distinction is trophic, not taxonomic.

**Chemosynthesis is not rare or exotic.** Every hydrothermal vent, cold seep, and whale fall
hosts a chemosynthetic ecosystem. Scattered geographically but biologically significant — and
they prove that the photosynthesis-solar energy link is not universal to life.

**Dead zones ≠ deep sea oxygen minimum.** Coastal hypoxic dead zones (Gulf of Mexico, Baltic
Sea) are shallow zones depleted of O₂ by agricultural runoff driving bacterial blooms. The
deep-sea OMZ depletes O₂ differently: sinking organic matter from surface decomposing at depth.

**Coral reefs are not tropical deserts with a lucky fish assemblage.** The Connell biodiversity
paradox: nutrient-poor tropical water supports extraordinary reef diversity because of tight
internal nutrient cycling within the reef matrix — nutrients entering from outside are minimal.
