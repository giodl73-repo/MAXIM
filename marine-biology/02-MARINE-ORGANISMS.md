# Major Marine Organism Groups

## The Big Picture

Marine biodiversity spans all domains of life. The ocean is not a single biome — it is
dozens of overlapping ecological niches connected by water. Understanding it means
understanding which organisms do what, at what scale, and why.

## Phytoplankton: Engines of Ocean Carbon

Phytoplankton are unicellular photosynthetic organisms that drift in the photic zone.
Collectively they fix ~50 Pg C/year — ~50% of global primary production.

### Diatoms

```
DIATOM STRUCTURE AND ECOLOGY
==============================

     ┌─────────────────────────────┐
     │   SILICA FRUSTULE (shell)   │
     │   ┌───────────────────────┐ │
     │   │  ●  Cell contents  ●  │ │   Two overlapping halves
     │   │                       │ │   (epitheca + hypotheca)
     │   └───────────────────────┘ │   like a petri dish
     └─────────────────────────────┘
     Intricate nanoscale geometry per species
     (glass engineering analog — billions of years
      of evolutionary optimization)

ECOLOGY:
  Cold, nutrient-rich water → diatoms dominate
  Spring bloom species: Thalassiosira, Chaetoceros, Skeletonema
  Require: dissolved silicic acid (H₄SiO₄) for frustule synthesis
  Silica limitation → bloom crashes → mass sinking (carbon export)
  Dominant in: polar oceans, coastal upwelling zones, spring blooms

FOSSIL RECORD:
  Diatomite (diatomaceous earth): industrial filtration medium
  Entire sedimentary layers composed of diatom frustules
  Record of paleoclimate (species assemblage → past temperature)
```

### Dinoflagellates

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Motility | Two flagella: one around cell (spin), one trailing (propulsion) |
| Cell armor | Cellulose thecal plates (armored) or naked |
| Mixotrophy | Many can photosynthesize AND ingest prey — osmotrophy |
| HABs | Harmful algal blooms: *Alexandrium* (saxitoxin), *Karenia brevis* (brevetoxin) |
| Bioluminescence | *Noctiluca scintillans*, *Pyrocystis* — ocean bioluminescence in waves |
| Coral symbiont | *Symbiodinium* (zooxanthellae) — provides >90% of coral energy |
| Dinoflagellate toxins | Paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP), ciguatera fish poisoning |

### Coccolithophores

```
COCCOLITHOPHORE — ECOLOGY AND BIOGEOCHEMISTRY
===============================================

Structure: unicellular alga covered in CaCO₃ (calcite) scales = coccoliths
Size: 5-100 μm
Distribution: warm, stratified, nutrient-poor tropical/subtropical ocean

BIOLOGICAL CARBONATE PUMP:
  Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ → CaCO₃ + H₂O + CO₂
  Forms calcite scales → cells sink → CaCO₃ reaches ocean floor
  → Chalk: compressed coccoliths (White Cliffs of Dover = Cretaceous coccoliths)
  → Long-term carbon sequestration mechanism

CLIMATE FEEDBACK (complex):
  Calcification RELEASES CO₂ (counterintuitive)
  But also exports alkalinity to deep ocean
  Net effect depends on ratio of organic to inorganic carbon export
  Most abundant species: Emiliania huxleyi — visible from space in satellite imagery
  (turquoise bloom signatures in North Atlantic: >100,000 km²)
```

### Cyanobacteria

- **Prochlorococcus:** smallest photosynthetic organism (~0.6 μm). Most abundant
  photosynthetic organism on Earth — 2.9×10²⁷ cells globally. Dominates oligotrophic
  (nutrient-poor) gyres. Discovered 1988 by Chisholm lab at MIT.
- **Synechococcus:** larger (~1 μm), faster-growing, tolerates higher nutrients.
- **Trichodesmium:** colonial, nitrogen-fixer. Critical in tropical oceans where N is limiting.
  Forms visible surface "sea sawdust" rafts. Fixes N₂ → ammonium → fuels other plankton.

## Zooplankton: Primary Consumers

### Copepods

The most abundant animal on Earth. ~13,000 species. Dominate marine zooplankton biomass.

```
COPEPOD ECOLOGY
================

  Typical size: 0.5–5 mm
  Feeding: capture individual phytoplankton cells with thoracic limbs
  Life cycle: nauplius larvae (6 stages) → copepodite (5 stages) → adult
  Diapause: some species sink to deep water and enter dormancy for
            months (Calanus hyperboreus in Arctic — critical oil sac storage)
  Abundance: 10–1000 individuals per m³ at surface during blooms

ECOLOGICAL ROLE:
  Transfer phytoplankton carbon to fish
  Produce large fecal pellets → sink rapidly → active biological pump
  Antarctic krill (Euphausia superba) shares this role in southern ocean
  Krill biomass ~500 million tonnes — more than all humans combined
```

### Molluscs

| Class | Examples | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Cephalopoda | Octopus, squid, nautilus | Largest invertebrate brain; 600+ million neurons in octopus; Humboldt squid at 2 m; Architeuthis (giant squid) at 13 m |
| Bivalvia | Clams, oysters, mussels, scallops | Filter feeders; bioindicators; bivalve reefs as fish nurseries |
| Gastropoda | Sea snails, nudibranchs, sea hares | Nudibranchs: aposematic; kleptoplasty (sea slugs steal chloroplasts) |
| Polyplacophora | Chitons | 8 articulating plates; intertidal grazer on algae |

**Cephalopod intelligence:** Octopuses solve puzzles, use tools (coconut shell shelter),
show individual personality. Distributed nervous system: 2/3 of neurons in arms (not brain).
Short lifespan (~1-2 years) limits learned behavior accumulation. Convergent intelligence —
no common ancestor with vertebrate intelligence.

## Fish: Vertebrate Diversity in the Sea

~33,000 fish species — more than all other vertebrates combined.

```
FISH PHYLOGENY (simplified)
=========================

Vertebrates
├── Agnatha (jawless)
│   ├── Lampreys         — parasitic; 360+ million years unchanged
│   └── Hagfish          — deep-sea detritivores; knot-tying to escape
│
└── Gnathostomata (jawed)
    ├── Chondrichthyes (cartilaginous)  ← 450 mya divergence
    │   ├── Elasmobranchii
    │   │   ├── Sharks (>500 spp)       — apex predators; cartilage, no
    │   │   │   White shark: 6 m        swim bladder (must swim to breathe)
    │   │   └── Rays, skates            — flattened; electroreception
    │   └── Chimaeras (ratfish)
    │
    └── Osteichthyes (bony)             ← 420 mya divergence
        ├── Sarcopterygii (lobe-finned)
        │   └── Coelacanth (Latimeria)  — "living fossil"; fin homolog
        │                                 of tetrapod limb
        └── Actinopterygii (ray-finned) ← 96% of all fish
            ├── Teleosts (~30,000 spp)
            │   ├── Swim bladder for neutral buoyancy
            │   ├── Highly diverse feeding: filter (whale shark),
            │   │   suction, biting, parasitic (anglerfish)
            │   └── Examples: tuna, salmon, cod, herring, eels,
            │                 seahorses, clownfish
            └── Non-teleost actinopterygians: sturgeons, gars
```

**Shark sensory systems:**
- Ampullae of Lorenzini: electroreceptors detecting <0.005 μV/cm fields
- Lateral line: mechanoreception of water pressure changes
- Olfaction: directional; detect blood at 1 part per billion
- No swim bladder: large, oily liver for buoyancy; must swim to pass water over gills

## Marine Mammals: Return to the Sea

Five independent lineages of mammals returned to marine life. Convergent evolution
of streamlined body, flippers, and osmoregulation at work.

```
MARINE MAMMAL LINEAGES
========================

ORDER / CLADE        EXAMPLES           WHEN RETURNED TO SEA
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cetacea              Whales, dolphins,  ~53–48 mya from even-toed
                     porpoises          ungulate ancestor (Pakicetus)

Carnivora
├── Pinnipedia       Seals, sea lions,  ~28 mya (multiple origins)
│                    walrus
├── Mustelidae       Sea otters         ~5 mya (recent)
└── Ursidae          Polar bears        ~150,000–350,000 ya (ongoing)

Sirenia              Dugongs,           ~55 mya from same ancestor
                     manatees           as elephants (Afrotheria)
```

**Cetacean evolution — key transitions:**
- *Pakicetus* (~53 mya): terrestrial, wolf-sized; ankle bones shared with even-toed ungulates
- *Ambulocetus* (~49 mya): amphibious; swam with undulating spine (like otter) not tail flukes
- *Rodhocetus* (~47 mya): mostly aquatic; hind limbs reduced; tail flukes appear
- *Basilosaurus* (~37 mya): fully aquatic; elongated body; vestigial hind limbs
- Modern cetaceans: hind limbs gone (internal vestigial pelvis remains as evidence)

**Cetacean echolocation (odontocetes — toothed whales):**
- Sperm whale can produce 230 dB clicks — loudest biological sound
- Melon (fatty forehead) focuses clicks into directional beam
- Clicks used for navigation AND prey capture (stunning prey)
- Bottlenose dolphin resolution: can distinguish 5 cm vs 5.5 cm spheres at 10 m

**Whale diving physiology:**
- Sperm whale: dives to 2,250 m for 90 minutes
- Myoglobin concentration 10× higher than land mammals (dark muscle = oxygen store)
- Collapsible ribcage: lungs compress to prevent nitrogen narcosis
- Splenic contraction at dive: releases extra oxygenated red blood cells

## Seabirds: Flight at the Ocean Interface

| Group | Example | Adaptation |
|-------|---------|------------|
| Procellariiformes | Wandering albatross (3.5 m wingspan) | Dynamic soaring: extract energy from wind gradient over waves; can fly 1,000 km/day |
| Sphenisciformes | Emperor penguin | Flightless; counter-current heat exchange in flippers; dives to 565 m |

## Common Confusion Points

**Whale shark ≠ whale.** *Rhincodon typus* is the largest fish (up to 12.6 m) — it is a
shark (cartilaginous), not a cetacean. Filter feeds like a whale but breathes through gills.

**Jellyfish are not fish and not simple.** No brain, no blood, ~95% water — yet capable of
forming meter-scale gelatinous aggregations (blooms) that clog power plant intakes and
fishery nets. *Turritopsis dohrnii* — "immortal jellyfish" — can revert to polyp stage after
sexual maturation. Not immortal in practice (predation, disease) but theoretically capable.

**Krill ≠ shrimp.** Krill (Euphausiids) are zooplankton; shrimp (Decapods) are larger benthic
or pelagic. Krill lack the decapod leg number. Antarctic krill total biomass is estimated at
~500 million tonnes — comparable to all humans. Blue whales eat almost exclusively krill.

**Sea urchins and sea stars are not related to fish.** Echinoderms (spiny-skinned) — pentaradial
symmetry, no head, water vascular system. Deuterostomes like vertebrates but diverged ~550 mya.
The evolutionary relationship is ancient: echinoderms are our distant cousins.

**Dolphins are not fish.** Cetaceans are mammals: breathe air, bear live young, nurse with milk,
warm-blooded, have hair (vestigial whiskers at birth in some species). The fish-like streamlined
form is convergent evolution — not homology.
