# Chemosynthesis and Alternative Energy Pathways

## The Big Picture

For ~3.8 billion years of Earth's history, biology assumed sunlight was the non-negotiable
energy source. The 1977 discovery of hydrothermal vents and their dense, thriving
communities shattered that assumption. Chemosynthesis — primary production using chemical
oxidation reactions rather than photosynthesis — provides the energy base for entire
ecosystems in permanent darkness.

```
PHOTOSYNTHESIS vs CHEMOSYNTHESIS — PARALLEL PRIMARY PRODUCTION
================================================================

PHOTOSYNTHESIS                    CHEMOSYNTHESIS
  6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light              CO₂ + H₂S + O₂
       → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂                 → CH₂O + H₂SO₄
                                        (sulfur oxidation)
  Energy source: SUNLIGHT           Energy source: CHEMICAL
  Location: photic zone (0-200 m)   Location: any where geology
  Producers: phytoplankton,           delivers reduced compounds
    plants, cyanobacteria             (deep-sea vents, cold seeps,
  Global rate: ~120 Pg C/yr          shallow springs, caves)
  Requires: light                   Requires: reduced compound
                                      (H₂S, CH₄, NH₃, H₂, Fe²⁺)
                                      AND oxidant (O₂ or SO₄²⁻)

CHEMOSYNTHETIC REACTIONS (examples):
  Sulfur oxidation:   H₂S + CO₂ + O₂ → [CH₂O] + H₂SO₄
  Methane oxidation:  CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O  (aerobic)
  Anaerobic methane:  CH₄ + SO₄²⁻ → HCO₃⁻ + HS⁻ + H₂O
  Iron oxidation:     4Fe²⁺ + O₂ + 8H⁺ → 4Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
  Nitrification:      NH₃ + O₂ → NO₂⁻ + NO₃⁻ (Thaumarchaeota)
```

  FeS, ZnS, CuFeS₂ precipitate       calcium, silica minerals)

  Focused: black/white smokers — high T, chemistry intense

## Riftia pachyptila: The Iconic Vent Animal

*Riftia pachyptila* — giant tube worm — is the textbook example of chemosymbiosis.
It has no mouth, no gut, no anus. Entirely dependent on endosymbiotic bacteria.

```
RIFTIA PACHYPTILA — ANATOMY AND SYMBIOSIS
==========================================

  PLUME (red, feathery)  ← hemoglobin-rich; bright red
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Absorbs: O₂ and H₂S simultaneously from water   │
  │  Transport: simultaneous in blood (no mixture)   │
  │  Hemoglobin binds H₂S at separate site from O₂   │
  │  (unique adaptation — H₂S is lethal to other     │
  │   hemoglobin-carrying animals including humans)  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  WHITE TUBE (up to 2.4 m long)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Chitin/protein tube — structural support        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  VESTIMENTUM (trunk)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Anchors to tube; receives blood supply          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  TROPHOSOME
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Specialized organ: up to 50% of body mass       │
  │  Packed with ENDOSYMBIOTIC BACTERIA              │
  │  ~10⁹ bacteria per gram of trophosome tissue     │
  │  Bacteria: Candidatus Endoriftia persephone      │
  │            (Gammaproteobacteria)                 │
  │  Reaction: H₂S + CO₂ + O₂ → organic C            │
  │            (thioautotrophy)                      │
  │  Host receives: organic carbon compounds         │
  │  Bacteria receive: H₂S, O₂, CO₂ delivered        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

FASTEST GROWTH RATE OF ANY MARINE INVERTEBRATE:
  Up to 85 cm/year under good vent conditions
  Rationale: vent activity is episodic; must grow fast
  to exploit productive phase before vent shifts/dies
```

**How Riftia carries H₂S without poisoning itself:**
Normal hemoglobin: H₂S competes with O₂ at heme iron binding site → blocks O₂ transport → death.
Riftia hemoglobin: extracellular (in blood, not in cells); has a separate H₂S-binding cysteine
residue. H₂S and O₂ are transported simultaneously to trophosome bacteria without competing.
Engineered solution evolution arrived at over millions of years.

## Cold Seeps: Methane-Based Communities

Cold seeps are where methane and sulfide-rich fluids seep through seafloor sediments —
no volcanic heat required. Found on continental margins worldwide.

```
COLD SEEP SYSTEM
================

CONTINENTAL MARGIN CROSS-SECTION
  ↑ Sea surface
  ↓
  WATER COLUMN
  ↓
  SEAFLOOR SEDIMENT (hundreds of meters thick on continental margins)
        │
        │  CH₄ generated by:
        │  1. Microbial methanogenesis (buried organic matter)
        │  2. Thermogenic breakdown of organic matter below
        │  3. Methane hydrate dissociation
        │
        ▼ Fluid seeps upward
  SEEP COMMUNITY:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Surface mats: sulfide-oxidizing bacteria             │
  │    (Beggiatoa, Thioploca) — white bacterial mats      │
  │    visible from ROV — first sign of seep activity     │
  │                                                       │
  │  Carbonate rocks: precipitated from DIC produced      │
  │    during methane oxidation — hard substrate for      │
  │    chemosymbiotic bivalves (Bathymodiolus mussels,    │
  │    Calyptogena clams, vesicomyid clams)               │
  │                                                       │
  │  In sediment: ANME archaea consortia                  │
  │    (Anaerobic Methane Oxidizers)                      │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**ANME — Anaerobic Methane Oxidation:**

One of the most important but invisible reactions in the ocean. ANME archaea (ANME-1,
ANME-2, ANME-3) perform the "reverse methanogenesis":

```
ANME ARCHAEA — REVERSE METHANOGENESIS
=======================================

Normal methanogenesis (methanogens):
  CO₂ + 4H₂ → CH₄ + 2H₂O   (or acetate → CH₄ + CO₂)

ANME reverse it:
  CH₄ + SO₄²⁻ → HCO₃⁻ + HS⁻ + H₂O   (AOM: anaerobic oxidation of methane)

  Energy yield: ΔG° = -17 to -40 kJ/mol (barely positive under these conditions)
  Living on this tiny energy margin requires: close syntrophic partnership

ANME-SRB (sulfate-reducing bacteria) CONSORTIUM:
  ANME archaea and SRB (Desulfosarcina, Desulfococcus) form
  tightly coupled consortia (physical aggregates, ~10-100 μm)
  ANME oxidize CH₄ → electrons/reducing equivalents transferred to SRB
  SRB reduce SO₄²⁻ using those electrons → releases HS⁻

  Net reaction eliminates both CH₄ and SO₄²⁻
  Global significance: ANME consume ~90% of methane rising from
  seafloor before it reaches the water column
  Without ANME: far more CH₄ would reach the atmosphere
  (CH₄ is 80× more potent GHG than CO₂ over 20 years)
```

## Vent Biology Across the Globe

Different vent systems have different geology → different chemistry → different biology:

| Location | Spreading Rate | Key Species | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| East Pacific Rise | Fast (~10 cm/yr) | *Riftia*, Calyptogena clams, Bathymodiolus mussels | Most studied; type site |
| Mid-Atlantic Ridge | Slow (~2.5 cm/yr) | Rimicaris shrimp (bacterial mats on shrimp backs) | No tube worms; different community |
| Juan de Fuca (Pacific NW) | Intermediate | Similar to EPR | Axial Seamount — active volcano under study |
| Lau Basin (Pacific) | Back-arc spreading | Iron-plated snail (*Chrysomallon squamiferum*) | Iron sulfide scales on foot — scales unique |
| Indian Ocean | Active | Similar Rimicaris | Less studied |
| Arctic (Loki's Castle) | Very slow | Novel archaea | LUCA candidate (*Asgard archaea* from here) |

**Scaly-foot snail (*Chrysomallon squamiferum*):** only animal known to use iron sulfide (greigite)
as structural material. The foot is covered in scales of iron pyrite (FeS₂) and greigite (Fe₃S₄).
Effectively a biological iron-plated mail coat. Chemoautotrophic endosymbionts in esophageal gland.

## Common Confusion Points

**Vents ≠ all hot.** Black smokers (350-400°C) are only part of vent systems. Most vent
biology concentrates in the diffuse flow zones (5-30°C above ambient = 7-32°C) at the
margins of vent fields. The extreme black smoker is a chimney; the biology is mostly
in the warm fringes.

**Chemosynthesis ≠ anaerobic.** Many chemosynthetic bacteria use O₂ as their electron
acceptor (aerobic thioautotrophs, *Riftia*'s symbionts). Anaerobic chemosynthesis (ANME
using SO₄²⁻) is a different pathway. Both are chemosynthesis but not the same reaction.

**ANME archaea are not cultured.** ANME consortia have never been successfully grown in
the lab (as of 2025). Everything known about their metabolism comes from environmental
metagenomics, isotope tracing, and CARD-FISH in situ imaging. This makes them one of
the most metabolically important uncultured organisms known.

**Vent communities are not permanent.** Individual vents can shut off within years to
decades. Species on any given vent are in a successional race — colonize, exploit, disperse
before the vent dies. This creates vent biogeography: how do larvae from one vent reach
the next (km to thousands of km away)? Mid-ocean ridge current systems carry larvae
along ridge axis.
