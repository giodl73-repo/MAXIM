# Ocean Zones and Physical Environment

## The Big Picture

The ocean is structured vertically by light penetration, pressure, and temperature.
These physical gradients create distinct biological zones — each with its own physics,
chemistry, and life strategies. Horizontal zonation (coastal vs open ocean) adds another
dimension.

```
OCEAN ZONE STRATIFICATION
==========================

                EPIPELAGIC ZONE (0–200 m)

                │ P: 20–100 atm                                 │
```

## Zone 1: Epipelagic (0–200 m)

The engine of ocean biology. 100% of ocean primary production starts here.

```
EPIPELAGIC PHYSICAL STRUCTURE
==============================

  0 m ── Sea surface ────────────────────────────────────────────
         Gas exchange: CO₂ in, O₂ out (or vice versa with bloom)
         T: tropical 28-30°C, polar 0-2°C
         Mixed layer: 20-200 m (wind-driven turbulence)

  ─────── THERMOCLINE ───────────────────────────────────────────
         T drops sharply: 20°C → 5°C over 100-300 m
         Acts as density barrier: stratifies the water column
         Prevents deep nutrient replenishment in tropics
         (absent at poles — allows mixing that drives productivity)

 100 m ── 1% light level (euphotic zone base) ──────────────────
         Below this: insufficient light for net photosynthesis
         Deep chlorophyll maximum (DCM): ~80-120 m
         (optimum light+nutrient intersection)

 200 m ── Zone boundary ────────────────────────────────────────
```

**Why the thermocline matters:** In the tropics, warm low-density surface water sits on top
of cold dense deep water. The thermocline is the density gradient that separates them. It
blocks upward nutrient flux from the deep ocean — which is why tropical open ocean is
nutrient-poor (oligotrophic) despite abundant sunlight. Polar oceans lack a permanent
thermocline → wind mixes surface and deep water → nutrients always available → high
productivity despite low light.

## Zone 2: Mesopelagic (200–1000 m)

The twilight zone. Partially illuminated but insufficient for photosynthesis.
Biologically defined by **diel vertical migration (DVM)** — the largest daily animal
movement on Earth.

```
DIEL VERTICAL MIGRATION
========================

  NIGHT                              DAY
  ┌────────────────────┐           ┌────────────────────┐
  │   0-200 m          │           │   0-200 m          │
  │   ████ krill       │           │   (prey remain)    │
  │   ████ copepods    │           │                    │
  │   ████ lanternfish │           │                    │
  │   (feeding on      │           │                    │
  │    phytoplankton)  │           │                    │
  └────────────────────┘           └────────────────────┘
  ┌────────────────────┐           ┌────────────────────┐
  │  200-1000 m        │           │  200-1000 m        │
  │  (resting, cold)   │           │   ████ krill       │
  │                    │           │   ████ copepods    │
  │                    │           │   ████ lanternfish │
  │                    │           │   (hiding from     │
  │                    │           │    visual predators│
  │                    │           │    in dim light)   │
  └────────────────────┘           └────────────────────┘

Carbon transport: animals eat at surface, defecate in deep water
→ Active carbon pump supplement to passive sinking
```

**Oxygen Minimum Zone (OMZ):** In the mesopelagic, O₂ is depleted by bacteria decomposing
sinking organic particles, while supply from photosynthesis and gas exchange is cut off.
Some organisms exploit OMZs (jumbo squid, Humboldt squid can tolerate ~0.5 mL O₂/L).

## Zone 4: Abyssopelagic (4000–6000 m)

Abyssal plains. The most extensive biome on Earth but least studied.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Temperature | 0–3°C — uniform |
| Pressure | 400–600 atm |
| Salinity | ~34.7 ppt — stable |
| Light | Absolute zero |
| Food source | Marine snow settling from above |
| Flux of organic carbon | ~0.5–2% of surface production reaches here |
| Sediment composition | Foraminiferan ooze, siliceous ooze, red clay |

**Abyssal nodule fields:** Polymetallic nodules (manganese, cobalt, nickel, copper)
precipitate from seawater over millions of years. Nodule communities are distinct,
slow-growing, and cannot recover on human timescales. Subject of deep-sea mining debate.

## Zone 5: Hadal (>6000 m)

Ocean trenches form where tectonic plates subduct. The V-shape funnels organic material,
making trenches relatively food-rich compared to abyssal plains.

```
MARIANA TRENCH PROFILE
=======================

Sea surface ─────────────────────────────────── 0 m
                       │
    ~7 km descent      │
    (water column)     │
                       │
Abyssal plain ─────────────────────────────── ~6,000 m
                      ╱╲
                     ╱  ╲   TRENCH WALLS (steep)
                    ╱    ╲  nutrient funneling effect
                   ╱      ╲
Challenger Deep ──╱────────╲──────────────── 10,935 m

Pressure at Challenger Deep: ~1,086 atm = 15,900 psi
= equivalent to 50 jumbo jets stacked on your palm

FIRST VISIT: USS Challenger 1875 (sounding line, no sample)
FIRST CREWED DESCENT: 1960, Trieste (Piccard + Walsh)
JAMES CAMERON: 2012, solo dive in Deepsea Challenger
```

**Key hadal organisms:**
- *Hirondellea gigas* — amphipod (shrimp-like crustacean) dominating Challenger Deep
- Holothurians (sea cucumbers) — often >50% of visible biomass in trenches
- Piezophilic bacteria — require high pressure for growth; enzymes evolved for function at >600 atm

## Pressure: The Master Constraint

Pressure increases 1 atm per 10 m depth. At 10,000 m: 1,000 atm.

```
EFFECTS OF HIGH PRESSURE ON BIOCHEMISTRY
=========================================

MEMBRANE LIPIDS
  Problem: pressure compresses membranes → reduces fluidity → impairs function
  Solution: piezophiles increase proportion of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs)
            PUFAs resist packing → maintain membrane fluidity at high pressure
            (same principle as cold-adapted organisms using PUFAs for low-temp fluidity)

ENZYMES
  Problem: pressure alters protein conformation → active site geometry changes
  Solution: piezophilic enzymes have more flexible active sites
            achieved by replacing amino acids that pack tightly
            (e.g., Ile → Val substitutions reduce steric bulk)
  Example: lactate dehydrogenase in Mariana Trench fish has higher
           catalytic efficiency at 400 atm than surface homologs

SMALL MOLECULE STABILIZERS
  Trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) — accumulates in deep-sea fish
  Counteracts pressure-induced protein destabilization
  TMAO concentration scales with depth: ~40 mmol/kg per 1000 m
  → Mariana snailfish (~8000 m) contains ~250 mmol/kg TMAO
  Upper depth limit of vertebrates (~8000-8200 m) may be set by
  physiological TMAO tolerance
```

## Physical-Biological Linkage Summary

```
PHYSICAL CONDITION → BIOLOGICAL CONSEQUENCE
=============================================

Strong thermocline → Nutrient trap in deep water → Oligotrophic surface
                   → Low surface productivity (tropical gyres)
                   → BUT: coral reefs exploit recycling to succeed anyway

No thermocline    → Deep mixing → nutrient upwelling → High productivity
(polar oceans)    → Massive phytoplankton blooms → Rich fisheries
                  → (Antarctic krill biomass: ~500 million tonnes)

High pressure     → Enzyme and membrane adaptation
(deep zones)      → Piezophilic specialization
                  → TMAO accumulation as stabilizer
                  → Upper vertebrate depth limit ~8200 m

OMZ               → Most organisms avoid it
(200-1000 m)      → Some exploit low O₂ habitat (reduced predation)
                  → Humboldt squid diel migration through OMZ

Hadal V-shape     → Organic matter concentrates in trench
                  → Relatively high food availability vs flat abyss
                  → Supports diverse communities at 10,000 m
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Aphotic ≠ abiotic.** The bathypelagic, abyssopelagic, and hadal zones receive zero light
but support communities that can be surprisingly dense — especially in nutrient-funnel
environments like trenches. Life does not require light; it requires energy.

**Pressure units.** 1 atm = 101,325 Pa = 14.7 psi. Deepest point (10,935 m) ≈ 1,086 atm.
Each 10 m adds 1 atm. At 100 m scuba diving limit: 11 atm. Submersibles like Alvin are rated
to 4,500 m (450 atm); Nereid Under Ice to 11,000 m (1,100 atm).

**Thermocline is not a membrane.** It is a gradient of density/temperature that resists
vertical mixing. Nutrients exist below it in abundance — the thermocline just prevents
them from mixing upward without physical forcing (upwelling, polar mixing, El Niño disruption).

**Zones are ecological constructs, not hard walls.** Fish swim between zones; particles sink
through them; DVM organisms cross multiple zones daily. The zone names describe where animals
spend most of their time, not hard boundaries they cannot cross.
