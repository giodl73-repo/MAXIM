# Marine Conservation and Anthropogenic Impacts

## The Big Picture

Human exploitation of the ocean operates across multiple stressors simultaneously:
overfishing, habitat destruction, pollution, climate change, and deep-sea mining.
No ocean ecosystem is unaffected. The compounding effect of multiple stressors —
warming + acidification + deoxygenation — creates synergistic damage that models based
on single stressors systematically underestimate.

```
MAJOR ANTHROPOGENIC STRESSORS ON MARINE SYSTEMS
=================================================

                     MARINE ECOSYSTEM
                            │
        ┌───────────────────┼──────────────────┐
        │                   │                  │
        ▼                   ▼                  ▼
  OVERFISHING          POLLUTION           CLIMATE CHANGE
  ~90% of stocks       Plastics            Warming
  fully or             Nutrient runoff     Acidification
  over-exploited       Chemical            Deoxygenation
  IUU fishing          contaminants        Sea level rise
  Bycatch             Dead zones           Species range shift
  Ghost gear           Oil spills          Phenological mismatch
        │                   │                  │
        └───────────────────┴──────────────────┘
                            │
              CUMULATIVE IMPACT (synergistic)
              Greater than sum of parts:
              Warming + acidification → coral bleaching
              Bleaching + overfishing → reef collapse
              Deoxygenation + warming → fish range compression
```

## Overfishing: The Tragedy of the Commons

```
FISHERIES ECONOMICS — OPEN-ACCESS TRAGEDY
==========================================

HARDIN'S COMMONS (1968) applied to fisheries:
  Individual fisherman's logic:
    Each fish I catch = full economic benefit to me
    Each fish I catch = small fraction of cost shared by all fishermen
    Rational individual strategy: fish as hard as possible
    Collective result: extinction of the resource

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  OPEN ACCESS FISHERY:                               │
  │  Private benefit > Private cost until stock crashes │
  │  No individual incentive to conserve                │
  │  Classic public goods problem (non-excludable,      │
  │   rival resource)                                   │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

GLOBAL FISHERIES STATUS (FAO 2022):
  Stocks within biologically sustainable levels:  64.6%
  Overfished:                                     35.4%
  Underfished:                                     ~6%

  Top 10 species = ~27% of global catch
  Wild capture: ~80-82 million tonnes/year (plateau since 1990s)
  Aquaculture: now exceeds wild capture (~90+ million tonnes/year)

HISTORICAL BIOMASS LOSSES:
  Large predatory fish biomass: ~90% decline since industrialization
  (Myers & Worm, Nature 2003)
  Sharks: many species >70% decline in past 50 years
  Bluefin tuna: Atlantic Northern stock ~70% decline
  Whales: many species 90-99% reduced by whaling
```

### Illegal, Unreported, and Unregulated (IUU) Fishing

IUU fishing accounts for an estimated 11-26 million tonnes/year — up to 26% of
total global catch. It primarily affects developing nations with limited enforcement.
High-seas IUU enables fishing of stocks with no regulatory oversight.

**Global Fishing Watch (GFW):** Satellite AIS (Automatic Identification System) +
machine learning detects fishing vessel behavior globally. As of 2024, tracks ~65,000
vessels. IUU vessels often disable AIS — but SAR (synthetic aperture radar) satellites
can detect vessel presence regardless of AIS.

## Coral Reef Decline

```
GLOBAL CORAL REEF STATUS TRAJECTORY
=====================================

  YEAR        LIVE CORAL COVER (global estimate)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1950        ~50% (pre-industrial baseline)
  1980        ~40%
  2000        ~30-35%
  2016        ~25% (after third global bleaching)
  2024        ~14-16% (after fourth global bleaching)

  Rate of loss: ~2% per year average
  Regional variation extreme:
    Caribbean: 50% → 8-10% (most degraded)
    Great Barrier Reef: 50% bleached in 2016 alone
    Coral Triangle: still relatively intact (nearest benchmark)

  COMPOUNDING STRESSORS ON REEFS:
  Thermal bleaching (climate)
  + Ocean acidification (weakens skeleton)
  + Crown-of-thorns starfish (Acanthaster) outbreaks
  + Sedimentation from land clearing
  + Nutrient pollution (algae overgrowth)
  + Destructive fishing (dynamite, cyanide)
  + Tourism damage
  = Synergistic collapse

  RESTORATION ATTEMPTS:
  Coral gardening: growing coral fragments in nurseries → transplant
  Assisted evolution: selectively breeding heat-resistant corals
    (some zooxanthellae clade D corals more tolerant but slower-growing)
  CRISPR approaches: editing coral thermal tolerance genes (experimental)
  Translocation: moving heat-tolerant genotypes to vulnerable reefs
  Sunshield trials: fine calcium carbonate spray to reflect sunlight
    (controversial — regional effect, secondary impacts unclear)
```

## Plastic Pollution

```
PLASTIC IN THE OCEAN
======================

SOURCES:
  ~8-12 million tonnes/year enters ocean (primary source: Asia rivers)
  Yangtze, Ganges, Mekong, Indus among top plastic-polluting rivers
  Cumulative: ~150-200 million tonnes already in ocean (2024)

PLASTIC BREAKDOWN PATHWAY:
  Macroplastic (>5 mm) → UV/wave action → microplastic (<5 mm)
  → nanoplastic (<1 μm) — may not break down further on human timescales

OCEAN DISTRIBUTION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SURFACE GYRES ("garbage patches"):                    │
  │  Ekman convergence accumulates buoyant debris          │
  │  Great Pacific Garbage Patch: ~1.6 million km²         │
  │  (NOT solid — low-density microplastic soup)           │
  │                                                        │
  │  SEAFLOOR:                                             │
  │  70%+ of plastic sinks or settles eventually           │
  │  Mariana Trench sediment: plastic bags, wrappers       │
  │  found at 10,928 m — deepest confirmed plastic         │
  │                                                        │
  │  ARCTIC/ANTARCTIC:                                     │
  │  Ice concentrates microplastics                        │
  │  Arctic sea ice: 12,000 microplastic particles/liter   │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

BIOLOGICAL IMPACTS:
  Entanglement: sea turtles, seals, whales, seabirds (ghost gear)
  Ingestion of macroplastics: sea turtles (mistake bags for jellyfish)
                               seabirds: feed to chicks
  Microplastic ingestion: documented in 914+ marine species
    → Physical damage to gut
    → Chemical leaching (plasticizers, flame retardants adsorb to plastic)
    → Plastic as vector for invasive species (biofilm colonization = "plastisphere")
  Nanoplastic: crosses cell membranes; detected in blood, brain tissue
    of fish; in human blood (2022 study); significance still being assessed

GHOST FISHING:
  Lost/abandoned fishing gear continues to fish indefinitely
  ~640,000 tonnes of fishing gear lost/abandoned per year
  Ghost nets: drift for years, trap fish, whales, turtles
  Derelict crab pots: ~2 million in Chesapeake Bay alone
```

## Deep-Sea Mining: Emerging Threat

```
DEEP-SEA MINING — TARGETS AND THREATS
=======================================

TARGET: POLYMETALLIC NODULES
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Formation: ~2-15 cm diameter; grow at ~1 cm per       │
  │    million years (geological timescale)                │
  │  Composition: manganese, cobalt, nickel, copper,       │
  │    rare earth elements (REE)                           │
  │  Location: abyssal plains (4000-5500 m)                │
  │  Key areas: Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) — Pacific    │
  │             Penrhyn Basin — Pacific                    │
  │             Indian Ocean basin                         │
  │  Estimate: 21+ billion tonnes in CCZ alone             │
  │  Market: Ni, Co, Cu critical for EV batteries          │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

TARGET: SEAFLOOR MASSIVE SULFIDES (SMS)
  At hydrothermal vents: high-grade Cu, Zn, Au, Ag deposits
  More localized than nodules
  Would directly destroy vent ecosystems (already rare globally)

TARGET: COBALT-RICH CRUSTS
  Seamount surfaces: Co, Ni, Mn, REE
  Forms on seamount rock over millions of years

MINING IMPACT CONCERNS:
  Nodule removal:
    Nodules ARE the substrate — hard surface in otherwise soft sediment
    Unique community lives ON and under nodules (sponges, deep-sea corals,
    Mn-oxidizing bacteria, polychaetes, holothurians)
    Recovery timescale: minimum 50-100 years for sediment settlement
    Nodules: ZERO recovery (1 cm/million years growth rate)
    Sediment plumes: kilometers of water column; smothering of
    filter feeders far from mining site

  The Disturbance and Recolonization (DISCOL) experiment:
    1989: plowed ~11 km² of Peru Basin abyssal plain
    Re-surveyed 2015 (26 years later): minimal recolonization
    Sediment still disturbed; nodule community still ~70% lower
    than reference areas
    Conclusion: recovery takes centuries to millennia, not decades

GOVERNANCE:
  International Seabed Authority (ISA): UN body managing deep-sea mining
  High Seas: not national jurisdiction — ISA controls
  Status (2024): 31 exploration contracts issued; no commercial mining yet
  Moratorium calls: France, Chile, New Zealand, Pacific Island nations
```

## The Triple Threat: Warming + Acidification + Deoxygenation

```
CLIMATE CHANGE TRIPLE THREAT TO OCEAN
========================================

WARMING:
  Surface ocean warmed ~0.6-0.7°C since pre-industrial
  Rate: ~0.1°C per decade (accelerating)
  Effects:
    Coral bleaching threshold crossed more frequently
    Species range shifts poleward
    Breeding phenology: mismatch with food availability
    Sea ice loss: krill and ice-dependent species
    Ocean stratification increases → biological pump weakens
    Harmful algal bloom frequency increases

ACIDIFICATION:
  pH dropped 0.1 unit (26% more H⁺) since pre-industrial
  Aragonite saturation state decreasing in polar oceans
  Effects:
    Reduced calcification in corals, molluscs, echinoderms
    Pteropod shell dissolution in Southern Ocean (now)
    Fish behavior disruption at pH <8.0
    Synergy with warming: dual stress = exponential mortality

DEOXYGENATION:
  Ocean oxygen declining ~2% per decade in some regions
  Mechanism: warming reduces O₂ solubility AND increases stratification
             → less deep-water ventilation → expanding OMZs
  Effects:
    Habitat compression: fish squeezed between surface (hot)
    and OMZ (O₂-poor) — reduced thermal refuge
    Habitat lost: ~5 million km² ocean uninhabitable for
    sensitive species by 2100 (RCP8.5)
    Coastal dead zones expand seasonally

TRIPLE THREAT SYNERGY:
  Warming + acidification → coral bleaching + weakened skeleton
  → reduced resistance to disease + storm damage
  Deoxygenation + warming → range compression for fish
  → increased competition in shrinking habitat
  Acidification + deoxygenation → compounded stress for
  calcifying organisms in deep-water upwelling zones
```

## Marine Protected Areas: Current State

```
GLOBAL MPA COVERAGE (2024)
============================

  PROTECTION LEVEL          AREA        % OF OCEAN
  ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
  All MPAs (any designation) 30M km²    ~8%
  Strongly protected         ~10M km²   ~2.7%
  Fully protected (no-take)  ~2M km²    ~0.5-1%

  HIGH SEAS (international waters):
  Cover 64% of ocean surface
  Only ~1.2% has any protection
  High Seas Treaty (2023 — BBNJ Agreement):
    Signed but not yet in force (requires 60 ratifications)
    Framework for establishing high seas MPAs
    Significant step toward 30x30 target

  EFFECTIVENESS DISTRIBUTION:
  Many "paper parks" — designated but not enforced
  True effective protection: 3-5% of ocean (with enforcement)

  CRITICAL GAPS:
  Deep sea: <1% protected
  High seas: <2% protected
  Migratory species corridors: rarely protected
  Seamounts: largely unprotected (concentrated biodiversity)
```

## Sustainable Seafood and Aquaculture

```
SUSTAINABLE SEAFOOD — FRAMEWORKS AND TRADEOFFS
================================================

MSC CERTIFICATION (Marine Stewardship Council):
  Third-party certification for wild-capture fisheries
  Criteria: sustainable stock levels, minimal bycatch,
            effective management
  Issues: certification process industry-funded;
          some controversial certifications
  Coverage: ~15% of global wild marine catch certified

AQUACULTURE — THE PROMISE AND THE REALITY:
  Global aquaculture production: ~90 million tonnes/year (2022)
  Now > wild capture for human consumption
  Types:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  FINFISH (salmon, tilapia, carp):                      │
  │    Atlantic salmon: dominant, Norway/Chile             │
  │    Feed conversion: 1.2 kg feed → 1 kg salmon          │
  │    Problems: sea lice outbreaks (treat with pesticides)│
  │              escapees hybridize with wild fish         │
  │              waste accumulation under net pens         │
  │                                                        │
  │  BIVALVES (oysters, mussels, clams):                   │
  │    Positive: filter feeders improve water quality      │
  │    No feed needed: extract food from seawater          │
  │    Low environmental footprint                         │
  │    Carbon-sequestering (calcification stores carbon)   │
  │    → Potentially BENEFICIAL aquaculture                │
  │                                                        │
  │  SHRIMP (Penaeus vannamei dominant):                   │
  │    High value, high demand                             │
  │    Problems: mangrove clearing for ponds               │
  │              antibiotics use → resistance              │
  │              effluent → coastal dead zones             │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

FORAGE FISH PROBLEM:
  ~20 million tonnes/year of small fish (anchovy, herring, menhaden)
  ground into fishmeal for aquaculture and livestock feed
  1 kg salmon requires ~2 kg wild fish (as fishmeal)
  Net protein: aquaculture can be LESS efficient than eating forage fish
  Alternatives: insect meal, soy, algae — all being trialed
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Sustainable" fisheries are not necessarily healthy.** MSC-certified fisheries fish at
or near MSY — the fish population is in a degraded state (K/2 = half historic biomass).
"Sustainable" means not currently collapsing, not that the ecosystem is intact.

**Dead zones and ocean deoxygenation are different.** Coastal dead zones are from nutrient
pollution (eutrophication). Open-ocean deoxygenation is from climate change reducing O₂
solubility and increasing stratification. Both result in low-O₂ water but through completely
different mechanisms and in different places.

**Deep-sea mining has not started commercially.** As of 2025, all mining is exploratory.
The ISA continues developing regulations under the "Mining Code." The key governance question
is whether a precautionary approach (no mining until we understand impacts) or a permissive
approach prevails. The DISCOL experiment strongly suggests impacts are generational.

**Aquaculture does not reduce pressure on wild fisheries straightforwardly.** Carnivorous
species aquaculture (salmon, shrimp) consumes wild fish (as fishmeal). The net effect on wild
fish stocks depends on species mix. Bivalve and seaweed aquaculture genuinely reduce pressure.
Inland tilapia and carp farming (herbivores, grain-fed) have different tradeoffs than
marine cage salmon farming.

**30×30 targets are not uniformly effective.** A "protected area" designation can be a paper
park with no enforcement. The quality and enforceability of protection matters more than the
nominal percentage. 30% of ocean under paper designation is ecologically meaningless; 10%
with strong enforcement and community support is transformative.
