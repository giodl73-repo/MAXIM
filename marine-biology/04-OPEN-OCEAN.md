# Pelagic Zone and Open Ocean Ecology

## The Big Picture

The open ocean (pelagic zone) covers ~65% of Earth's surface and constitutes ~99% of
Earth's inhabitable volume by space. Most of it appears blue and empty but is actually a
complex three-tier system: primary producers at the surface, a global carbon export pump
driven by sinking particles, and a microbial loop recycling most of the carbon before it
leaves the photic zone.

```
OPEN OCEAN CARBON AND ENERGY FLOW
===================================

ATMOSPHERE
    │ CO₂ in (via gas exchange)
    ▼
════════════════════════════════════════════════════  sea surface
PHOTIC ZONE (0-200 m) — PRIMARY PRODUCTION
    │
    │  Phytoplankton: CO₂ + H₂O + light → organic C
    │  ~50 Pg C/year fixed globally
    │
    ├── Grazed by zooplankton → fish → apex predators
    │   (Efficiency: ~10% per trophic level)
    │
    ├── MICROBIAL LOOP (90% of carbon recycled here)
    │   DOC (dissolved organic carbon)
    │    ↓  bacterial uptake
    │   Bacteria (1 μm) → grazed by flagellates
    │    ↓                  → grazed by ciliates
    │   CO₂ released by respiration (remineralized)
    │   → never reaches deep ocean
    │
    └── BIOLOGICAL PUMP (10% exported to deep)
        Fecal pellets (fast-sinking, ~100 m/day)
        Marine snow aggregates (slow-sinking)
        Vertical migrant carcasses + defecation
         ↓
════════════════════════════════════════════════════  200 m
TWILIGHT ZONE (200-1000 m)
    │ Much of sinking carbon remineralized here by bacteria
    │ Active transport by DVM migrants
    │
    ↓ ~1-3% of surface production escapes to deep
════════════════════════════════════════════════════  1000 m+
DEEP OCEAN — long-term carbon storage
    Abyssal sediment: carbon locked for 1000-100,000 years
```

## Primary Production: Rates and Limiting Factors

```
GLOBAL OCEAN PRIMARY PRODUCTION
=================================

Total marine net primary production: ~50 Pg C/year
 (~50% of global total including terrestrial systems)

DISTRIBUTION:
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  High productivity                Low productivity             │
│  ┌─────────────────────┐         ┌──────────────────────────┐  │
│  │ Coastal upwelling   │         │ Subtropical gyres        │ │
│  │ zones: 1-5 g C/m²/d│         │ (Pacific, Atlantic)      │ │
│  │ Polar spring blooms │         │ <0.1 g C/m²/day          │ │
│  │ Estuaries           │         │ = "ocean deserts"        │ │
│  │ Continental shelves │         │ ~40% of ocean surface    │ │
│  └─────────────────────┘         └──────────────────────────┘ │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

LIMITING FACTORS BY REGION:
  Polar oceans:      Light-limited (seasonal darkness)
  Tropical open:     Nutrient-limited (N, Fe — not CO₂ or light)
  Coastal zones:     P-limited (runoff dominates; N plentiful)
  Southern Ocean:    Iron-limited (HNLC: high-nutrient, low-chlorophyll)
```

**Iron limitation and HNLC zones:**
Southern Ocean, North Pacific, and equatorial Pacific are HNLC (High Nutrient, Low
Chlorophyll). Nitrate and phosphate are abundant; phytoplankton remain sparse. Why?
Iron. Phytoplankton need Fe as cofactor in photosynthetic electron transport chains
(ferredoxin, cytochromes). Iron is insoluble at seawater pH — limiting in remote ocean
far from terrestrial dust inputs.

**Iron fertilization experiments (SOIREE, IRONEX, LOHAFEX):** Adding Fe to HNLC zones
triggers phytoplankton blooms within days. Scale: 1 kg Fe → ~100,000 kg C fixed. Whether
this carbon reaches deep sediment (vs being recycled in the microbial loop) is the
unresolved question for geoengineering proposals.

## The Biological Pump: Carbon Export to the Deep

```
BIOLOGICAL PUMP — MECHANISMS AND EFFICIENCY
=============================================

SURFACE PRODUCTION
  100 units C fixed by phytoplankton
          │
          │ Grazed by zooplankton
          │ → Fecal pellets: rapid sinking (50-200 m/day)
          │ → Key because large, dense, sink fast
          │
          │ Cell aggregation → marine snow
          │ → Slower sinking (1-10 m/day)
          │ → Heavily colonized by bacteria (decompose en route)
          │
          │ Vertical migrant active transport:
          │ → Animals feed at surface, descend, defecate/die at depth
          │ → ~15-40% of passive flux in some regions
          │
          ▼
AT 100 m: ~20 units remaining (80% lost to microbial loop in top 100 m)

AT 200 m: ~10 units (mesopelagic bacterial decomposition)

AT 1000 m: ~1-3 units (entering the deep)

AT 4000 m: ~0.5-1 unit (reaches abyssal sediment)

OF 100 UNITS FIXED:
  ~90 remineralized in photic zone (microbial loop)
  ~9 remineralized in mesopelagic/bathypelagic
  ~1 reaches sediment → long-term storage (centuries-millennia)
```

**Martin Curve:** John Martin (1987) parameterized flux attenuation with depth as:
F(z) = F₁₀₀ × (z/100)^(-0.858) — the "Martin curve." The exponent varies by region
and community structure. High diatom flux (fast-sinking silica frustules) flattens the
curve — more carbon escapes to deep. This is the quantitative foundation of biological
pump geoengineering debate.

## The Microbial Loop: The Hidden Carbon Cycle

The microbial loop is not well-known outside marine biology but it processes more
carbon than all visible marine life combined.

```
MICROBIAL LOOP — PATHWAY
=========================

  Phytoplankton
       │
       │ Release: 10-50% of fixed carbon as dissolved organic
       │          carbon (DOC) via exudation, viral lysis,
       │          sloppy feeding by grazers
       ▼
  DISSOLVED ORGANIC CARBON (DOC) POOL
  ~662 Pg C in ocean (comparable to atmospheric CO₂!)
       │
       │ Bacterial uptake: heterotrophic bacteria (SAR11 clade
       │ dominant) consume DOC → bacterial biomass
       ▼
  HETEROTROPHIC BACTERIA (~10²⁸ cells in ocean)
       │
       │ Grazed by: heterotrophic nanoflagellates (HNF)
       ▼
  HETEROTROPHIC FLAGELLATES (2-20 μm)
       │
       │ Grazed by: ciliates, microzooplankton
       ▼
  CILIATES / MICROZOOPLANKTON
       │
       │ Grazed by: mesozooplankton (copepods, krill)
       │ → re-enters main food web
       │
       │ Also: CO₂ respired at each step
       ▼
  CO₂ REMINERALIZED → atmosphere/surface water
  (carbon retained in photic zone, never exported)

VIRAL SHUNT (parallel pathway):
  Viruses infect bacteria → cell lysis → DOM released
  ~10²⁹ viruses in ocean (most abundant biological entities)
  Kill 20-40% of bacteria daily — prevent bacterial biomass
  from reaching flagellates; instead releases DOC back to pool
  Accelerates microbial loop cycling vs export
```

**Why this matters quantitatively:**
- Phytoplankton fix ~50 Pg C/year
- ~45 Pg C is remineralized in the photic zone (microbial loop + grazers)
- Only ~5 Pg C exported below 200 m (biological pump)
- Of that, only ~0.5-1 Pg C reaches deep sediment long-term
- The microbial loop is the reason the biological pump is inefficient

## Upwelling Systems: Where the Ocean is Highly Productive

Upwelling = cold, nutrient-rich deep water brought to surface by wind-driven circulation.
The world's major fisheries are built on upwelling zones that cover <1% of ocean area
but produce ~20% of global wild fish catch.

```
COASTAL UPWELLING MECHANISM
=============================

WIND direction: along shore (e.g., northward along California coast)
           ↓
EKMAN TRANSPORT: surface water moves 90° to right of wind (NH)
                 = offshore, away from coast
           ↓
UPWELLING: deep water rises to replace offshore-moving surface water
           Cold (10-15°C), nutrient-rich (high NO₃⁻, PO₄³⁻, Si)
           ↓
PHYTOPLANKTON BLOOM: within days-weeks of upwelling onset
           ↓
ZOOPLANKTON BLOOM: weeks later
           ↓
FISH BIOMASS: anchovy, sardine, mackerel (forage fish)
           ↓
COMMERCIAL FISHERIES + SEABIRDS + MARINE MAMMALS

FOUR MAJOR EASTERN BOUNDARY UPWELLING SYSTEMS (EBUS):
  California Current (NE Pacific coast)
  Peru/Humboldt Current (SE Pacific coast)    ← most productive
  Benguela Current (SW Africa)
  Canary Current (NW Africa)
```

**Peru/Humboldt Current System:**
- Extends from Chile to Ecuador (~5,000 km of coastline)
- Produces ~10-15% of global marine fish catch despite <0.1% of ocean area
- Anchovy (*Engraulis ringens*) — historically up to 20 million tonnes/year catch
- Supports world's largest seabird colonies (Peruvian guano islands)
- Cold SST: 14-18°C (anomalous for tropical latitude — due to upwelling)

## ENSO Effects on Marine Productivity

El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) disrupts the temperature stratification that drives
upwelling, causing dramatic crashes in marine productivity.

```
ENSO AND THE PERU CURRENT
==========================

NORMAL CONDITIONS (La Niña)        EL NIÑO CONDITIONS
  Trade winds push warm water        Trade winds weaken
  westward                           ↓
  ↓                                  Warm water flows back east
  Cold, nutrient-rich upwelling      ↓
  along Peru coast                   Sea surface WARMS (+3-6°C)
  ↓                                  ↓
  Massive phytoplankton blooms        Thermocline deepens
  ↓                                   (below 200 m)
  Anchovy thrive                      ↓
  ↓                                   Upwelling brings warm water,
  Guano birds thrive (millions)       NOT deep cold nutrients
  ↓                                   ↓
  Fisheries boom                      PRODUCTIVITY COLLAPSE
                                      ↓
                                      Anchovy crash
                                      ↓
                                      Mass seabird mortality
                                      ↓
                                      Global fishery impacts
                                      (Peru anchovy = fishmeal
                                       for global agriculture)

1982-83 El Niño: global temperature anomaly +0.5°C
  Peruvian guano bird population: 20 million → 300,000
  Anchovy catch: from 8 Mt → near zero
```

## Oligotrophic Gyres: The Ocean Deserts

The subtropical gyres (North Pacific, South Pacific, North Atlantic, South Atlantic,
Indian Ocean) occupy ~40% of Earth's surface. Low productivity but not zero — and
important for global carbon cycling via the microbial loop.

```
SUBTROPICAL GYRE STRUCTURE
============================

         ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
         │         SUBTROPICAL HIGH PRESSURE            │
         │         (Hadley cell descending air)         │
         └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              │
              Winds spiral → Ekman convergence
              (water piles up in center, NOT upwelling)
                              │
                              ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │   GYRE CENTER        │
                  │   Deep thermocline   │
                  │   (200-300 m)        │
                  │   Extremely low N, P │
                  │   Prochlorococcus    │
                  │   dominates          │
                  │   (tiny, efficient   │
                  │    at low nutrients) │
                  └──────────────────────┘

North Pacific Subtropical Gyre:
  Largest ecosystem on Earth by area (~20 million km²)
  Prochlorococcus: 2.9×10²⁷ cells total globally
  Chlorophyll: ~0.03 mg/m³ (blue water, clear, nutrient-starved)
  BUT: collectively processes significant DOC via microbial loop
```

**Great Pacific Garbage Patch** — not a floating island of plastic.
It is a region where Ekman convergence accumulates surface flotsam (plastic debris)
by the same physics that creates the gyre. Microplastics (~1 mm to micrometers) are
distributed throughout the water column. The Eastern Garbage Patch is ~1.6 million km²
but plastic concentration is still very low (g/m²). See 09-CONSERVATION.md.

## Ocean Color as Productivity Proxy

Satellite ocean color (SeaWiFS, MODIS, Sentinel) measures chlorophyll-a via light
reflectance. Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue → reflects green. High chl = green ocean.

```
OCEAN COLOR → PRIMARY PRODUCTION
==================================

  FALSE-COLOR SATELLITE IMAGE:
  PURPLE/BLUE  → Very low chlorophyll (<0.1 mg/m³) — subtropical gyres
  GREEN        → Moderate chlorophyll (0.1-1 mg/m³) — coastal, productive
  YELLOW/RED   → High chlorophyll (>1 mg/m³) — bloom conditions, upwelling

  Time series from MODIS (2000-present):
  → Can calculate global net primary production
  → Monitor bloom timing (phenology shifts with warming)
  → Detect harmful algal blooms (HABs) from space
  → Track upwelling intensity interannually
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What fraction of fixed carbon reaches sediment? | ~1-2% (of 100% fixed at surface) |
| What is the microbial loop? | DOC → bacteria → flagellates → ciliates; remineralizes most carbon in photic zone |
| What limits production in the Southern Ocean? | Iron (HNLC: high nutrient, low chlorophyll) |
| What are EBUS? | Eastern boundary upwelling systems — four coastal zones producing ~20% of fish catch |
| What does El Niño do to marine productivity? | Warms and deepens thermocline → shuts off upwelling → productivity collapse |
| Why are subtropical gyres blue? | Ultra-low chlorophyll — dominated by tiny Prochlorococcus; nutrients absent |
| What is the biological pump? | Export of organic carbon from surface to deep via sinking particles and migrants |
| How much of ocean is "high productivity"? | <5% of ocean area (coastal, upwelling, polar zones) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Biological pump ≠ efficient.** Only ~1-2% of surface-fixed carbon reaches deep sediment.
The other 98-99% is remineralized — either in the photic zone (microbial loop) or in the
mesopelagic by bacteria. The pump "works" for climate purposes not because it is efficient
but because 1% of 50 Pg C/year is ~0.5 Pg C/year locked away for millennia.

**Upwelling ≠ just coastal.** The equatorial Pacific has major equatorial upwelling where
the ITCZ causes trade winds to diverge, drawing deep water up. This equatorial upwelling
supports the tuna fisheries of the central Pacific.

**ENSO ≠ just climate.** El Niño has direct, immediate biological consequences — seabird
and fish population collapses are a primary signal. The 1982-83 and 1997-98 events reduced
Peruvian anchovy to near-zero within one season.

**DOC ≠ nutrients.** The ocean DOC pool (~662 Pg C) is vast but most of it is recalcitrant
(resistant to bacterial degradation). Only the labile fraction cycles rapidly. This is why
the ocean stores carbon: much of the DOC is "locked" in forms bacteria cannot easily use.

**Marine snow ≠ just dead plankton.** Marine snow is a complex aggregate of organic material,
bacteria, transparent exopolymers (TEP), fecal material, and clay minerals. Its sinking rate
depends on aggregate size and density — which is why large diatom blooms export more carbon
(diatom frustules are dense) than Prochlorococcus-dominated oligotrophic systems.
