# Marine Food Webs and Trophic Dynamics

## The Big Picture

Marine ecology organizes the ocean's biomass into a hierarchy of who eats whom,
how efficiently energy moves between levels, which species hold entire systems together
(keystone species), and where human extraction has broken the system (fisheries collapse).

```
MARINE FOOD WEB — PELAGIC SYSTEM
==================================

         SOLAR ENERGY / CHEMICAL ENERGY
                    │
                    ▼
         ┌──────────────────────────────┐
LEVEL 1  │  PRIMARY PRODUCERS           │  ~50 Pg C/yr
         │  Phytoplankton, macroalgae,  │
         │  chemosynthetic bacteria     │
         └──────────────────┬───────────┘
                            │ ~10% transferred
                            ▼
         ┌──────────────────────────────┐
LEVEL 2  │  PRIMARY CONSUMERS           │  ~5 Pg C/yr
         │  Zooplankton (copepods, krill)│
         │  Herbivorous fish            │
         └──────────────────┬───────────┘
                            │ ~10%
                            ▼
         ┌──────────────────────────────┐
LEVEL 3  │  SECONDARY CONSUMERS         │  ~0.5 Pg C/yr
         │  Small predatory fish        │
         │  Squid                       │
         └──────────────────┬───────────┘
                            │ ~10%
                            ▼
         ┌──────────────────────────────┐
LEVEL 4  │  TERTIARY CONSUMERS          │  ~0.05 Pg C/yr
         │  Tuna, sharks, dolphins      │
         │  Large predatory fish        │
         └──────────────────┬───────────┘
                            │ ~10%
                            ▼
         ┌──────────────────────────────┐
LEVEL 5  │  APEX PREDATORS              │  ~0.005 Pg C/yr
         │  Orcas, great white sharks   │
         │  Bluefin tuna, sperm whales  │
         └──────────────────────────────┘

TROPHIC EFFICIENCY: ~10% rule (Lindeman, 1942)
  Each level: ~90% of energy lost to respiration, egestion, decay
  Consequence: 1 kg tuna requires ~10,000 kg phytoplankton
  (via ~1000 kg copepods → ~100 kg small fish → ~10 kg larger fish → 1 kg tuna)
```

## Trophic Efficiency and the 10% Rule

The 10% rule is an approximation. Actual values range from 3-20% depending on:

```
TROPHIC EFFICIENCY VARIATION
==============================

  MORE EFFICIENT (~15-20%):        LESS EFFICIENT (~3-5%):
  ─────────────────────────────    ─────────────────────────────
  Direct grazing chains            Long food chains (more steps)
  Cold water (less respiration)    Warm water (high respiration)
  Short chain (phyto → anchovy)    Microbial loop dominated systems
  Diatom-based systems             Picoplankton-based systems
  (large cells, fast sinking)      (small cells, recycled in
                                    microbial loop)

REAL NUMBERS:
  Phytoplankton → copepods:    12-15% (good grazers)
  Copepods → herring:          10-15%
  Herring → tuna:              5-10%
  Overall (5 levels):          0.1% of phytoplankton C to apex predators

FISHERIES IMPLICATION:
  Catching anchovies (level 2) requires 10× less phytoplankton production
  than catching tuna (level 3-4)
  → Forage fish fisheries are energetically efficient
  → Top predator fisheries require enormous primary production base
```

## Keystone Species: Trophic Cascades

A keystone species has disproportionate ecological impact relative to its biomass.
Remove it and the ecosystem restructures fundamentally.

### Sea Otter Trophic Cascade

```
SEA OTTER → SEA URCHIN → KELP FOREST — THE CLASSIC CASCADE
=============================================================

INTACT SYSTEM:
  Sea otters present
       │ prey on
       ▼
  Sea urchins (controlled, ~few per m²)
       │ graze on
       ▼
  Kelp (Macrocystis) — 30-60 m height, old-growth kelp forest
       │ provides habitat for
       ▼
  250+ fish species, invertebrates, seabirds, marine mammals

OTTER REMOVAL (fur trade — 18th-19th century):
  Sea otters eliminated
       │ releases predation pressure
       ▼
  Urchin population EXPLODES (100s per m²)
       │ overgrazes kelp
       ▼
  URCHIN BARREN — bare rock, no kelp, no fish habitat
  Biodiversity collapses to fraction of kelp forest

RECOVERY (reintroduction):
  Sea otter reintroduction (Alaska: 1970s, California: ongoing)
       │
       ▼
  Urchin populations suppressed
       │
       ▼
  Kelp forest returns within 5-10 years

CARBON STORAGE IMPLICATION (2012 study, Wilmers et al.):
  Kelp sequesters ~12-fold more carbon where otters are present
  Otter presence ↔ ~8.7 million tonnes additional CO₂ stored
  (indirect effect of keystone predator on carbon cycle)
```

### Other Marine Keystone Species

| Keystone Species | Where | Effect of Removal |
|------------------|-------|-------------------|
| Sea urchins (as prey) | Kelp forests | Barren when urchin predators removed |
| Sharks (reef) | Coral reefs | Mesopredator release → trophic cascade |
| Sea stars (*Pisaster*) | Pacific intertidal | Mussels monopolize rock → biodiversity loss |
| Wolves of the sea (orcas) | All oceans | Control seals, sea lions, sometimes whales |
| Parrotfish | Coral reefs | Graze algae off coral — essential for coral recruitment |
| Dugongs | Seagrass beds | Graze seagrass → nutrient cycling; remove → seagrass monoculture |

**Pisaster ochraceus (sea star) — Paine 1966:**
Robert Paine's original keystone species experiment. Removed all *Pisaster* from intertidal
plots in Mukkaw Bay, Washington. Result: mussels (*Mytilus*) overran the entire plot. From
15 species to 1 dominant. Coined the term "keystone species" from this experiment.

## Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)

```
MPA EFFECTIVENESS — WHAT THE DATA SHOW
========================================

TYPICAL OUTCOMES INSIDE NO-TAKE MPAS
(meta-analysis, Lester et al. 2009, ~100 MPAs studied):
  Density of organisms:    +166% vs outside
  Biomass:                 +446% vs outside
  Average organism size:   +28% vs outside
  Species diversity:       +21% vs outside

WHY DENSITY INCREASES SO MUCH:
  Fish move more freely in non-fished areas
  → Age structure recovers (older, larger, more fecund females)
  → Reproductive output increases dramatically
  → Spills over: fish emigrate from MPA into surrounding area

SPILLOVER EFFECT:
  Evidence for spillover: higher catch adjacent to MPAs
  Stronger for sedentary species (groupers, snappers)
  Weaker for mobile species (tuna, marlin — range > MPA size)

CURRENT COVERAGE (2024):
  ~8% of global ocean has some form of protection
  ~3% is no-take (fully protected)
  Target: 30% protection by 2030 ("30x30" — UN biodiversity framework)
  Gap: current rate of MPA creation too slow to reach 30x30 on schedule

DESIGN PRINCIPLES:
  Size matters: large MPAs more effective
  No-take vs regulated: no-take substantially more effective
  Enforcement: poorly enforced MPAs show minimal benefit
  Connectivity: MPAs linked by larval dispersal more resilient
  Representative: must cover full range of habitat types
```

## Fisheries Ecology: Maximum Sustainable Yield and Its Failures

```
MAXIMUM SUSTAINABLE YIELD (MSY) — CONCEPT AND PROBLEMS
========================================================

CONCEPT (Schaefer, 1954):
  Fish population follows logistic growth:
  dN/dt = rN(1 - N/K)
  where r = intrinsic growth rate, K = carrying capacity

  Population growth is fastest at N = K/2
  → Remove population to K/2 → harvest maximum sustainable yield
  → MSY = rK/4 (theoretical maximum harvest rate)

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Population   0   K/2    K                         │
  │  Growth rate  0   MAX    0                         │
  │  MSY harvest at N = K/2                            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

PROBLEMS WITH MSY IN PRACTICE:
  1. Unknown parameters: r and K require data we rarely have
  2. Ignores age structure: removing large females
     disproportionately reduces reproduction
  3. Assumes equilibrium: ignores environmental variability
     (ENSO, climate shifts move K and r interannually)
  4. Single-species model: ignores ecosystem interactions
     (removing prey species affects predators)
  5. Political pressure: MSY estimates systematically biased
     upward by fishing interests in management processes
  6. Ignores Allee effects: at very low N, finding mates
     becomes harder → reproduction crashes → collapse faster
     than logistic model predicts
```

### Grand Banks Cod Collapse

```
GRAND BANKS COD COLLAPSE — CASE STUDY
========================================

HISTORY:
  Grand Banks (off Newfoundland): one of world's richest fisheries
  500 years of cod fishing, supporting communities since 1500s
  Peak biomass estimates: 2-3 million tonnes
  Catches sustainable at ~300,000 t/yr through mid-20th century

  1970s: factory trawlers from EU, Soviet Union, Canada
  1977: Canada declares 200-mile EEZ → Canadian fleet expands
  1980s: catch maintained artificially via technology (more efficient boats)
         → catch holding up = evidence of OVERFISHING, not stability
  1990: Scientific assessment: stock near collapse
         Industry denies → DFO (Fisheries & Oceans Canada) under pressure
  1992: COLLAPSE
         Stock assessment: spawning biomass < 1% of historic levels
         July 1992: cod moratorium declared — 35,000 jobs lost overnight

  2024: Stock still has not recovered
         Some signs of recovery but well below 5% of historic biomass
         REASON FOR NON-RECOVERY: 30 years of no fishing NOT sufficient
         because: capelin (cod prey) also collapsed, ecosystem restructured
         → "ecosystem regime shift" — system locked in alternate state

LESSONS:
  1. Catch data alone is not population data
     (catch can stay high as fish work harder with fewer fish)
  2. "Shifting baseline syndrome": each generation normalizes
     to depleted state as reference point
  3. Ecosystem effects: cod removal → skate/crab/shrimp explosion
     → ecosystem will not automatically return even if cod return
```

## Ocean Acidification

```
OCEAN ACIDIFICATION CHEMISTRY
===============================

ATMOSPHERIC CO₂ INCREASE:
  Pre-industrial: ~280 ppm CO₂
  2024:           ~424 ppm CO₂
  ~30% absorbed by ocean (ocean as CO₂ sink)

DISSOLUTION AND EQUILIBRIA:
  CO₂(atm) ⇌ CO₂(aq)          [gas exchange]
  CO₂(aq) + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃      [carbonic acid formation]
  H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻         [bicarbonate — fast]
  HCO₃⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + CO₃²⁻         [carbonate — slower]

NET EFFECT:
  More CO₂ absorbed → more H⁺ produced → pH decreases
  More H⁺ reacts with CO₃²⁻ → HCO₃⁻ (reducing CO₃²⁻)
  Less CO₃²⁻ → lower aragonite saturation state (Ω_arag)

NUMBERS:
  Pre-industrial surface pH: ~8.2
  Current (2024) surface pH: ~8.1
  Projected 2100 (RCP8.5): ~7.8
  Change: 0.1 unit = 26% more H⁺ (logarithmic scale)
           projected 0.3-0.4 unit total = 100-150% more H⁺

BIOLOGICAL IMPACTS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Calcifying organisms (corals, molluscs, echinoderms,  │
  │  pteropods, coccolithophores):                         │
  │    Slower calcification rates                          │
  │    Weaker shells (dissolve faster than deposited)      │
  │    Dissolution of aragonite (less stable than calcite) │
  │    begins at Ω_arag < 1                                │
  │                                                        │
  │  Fish: navigation, olfaction disrupted at pH 7.8-7.9   │
  │    Larvae of some species fail to avoid predators      │
  │    (CO₂ disrupts GABA receptor signaling)              │
  │                                                        │
  │  Pteropods (free-swimming snails):                     │
  │    Already dissolving in Southern Ocean (Ω_arag < 1    │
  │    in some areas in winter — now)                      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Dead Zones: Coastal Hypoxia

```
DEAD ZONE FORMATION — GULF OF MEXICO EXAMPLE
=============================================

  MISSISSIPPI RIVER WATERSHED
  (agricultural fertilizer runoff: N, P)
          │
          ▼
  NUTRIENT-RICH FRESHWATER PLUME
  (Mississippi delta, Louisiana coast)
          │
          ▼
  PHYTOPLANKTON BLOOM
  (excess nutrients → massive algal growth)
          │
          ▼
  ALGAE DIE, SINK
  (decomposed by bacteria at depth)
          │
          ▼
  BACTERIAL RESPIRATION CONSUMES O₂
  (water column stratified in summer → no mixing)
          │
          ▼
  OXYGEN MINIMUM ZONE (<2 mg/L O₂)
  = "DEAD ZONE" (fish and shrimp flee; invertebrates die)
          │
  EXTENT: ~22,000 km² in summer 2017 (record)
  ANNUAL: forms spring, dissipates fall when storms mix water
  GLOBAL: >400 dead zones worldwide
  Notable: Baltic Sea (permanent), Black Sea, Chesapeake Bay

GLOBAL DEAD ZONE TREND:
  1950: ~50 known hypoxic zones
  2008: ~400
  2024: ~700+
  Trend: increasing with nitrogen fertilizer use
  Nitrogen fixed industrially: now > all natural N-fixation
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is trophic efficiency? | ~10% of energy transfers to next trophic level |
| What is a keystone species? | Species with outsized ecological impact relative to biomass |
| What happens when sea otters are removed? | Urchin explosion → kelp forest collapse → urchin barrens |
| What is MSY? | Maximum Sustainable Yield: harvest at K/2 population for logistic growth |
| Why did Grand Banks cod not recover after 30 years? | Ecosystem restructured; prey species (capelin) also collapsed; regime shift |
| What pH has ocean surface water dropped to? | From ~8.2 (pre-industrial) to ~8.1 (2024) — 26% more H⁺ |
| What is ocean dead zone? | Hypoxic coastal zone (<2 mg/L O₂) from nutrient runoff + algal bloom + decomposition |
| What % of ocean is fully protected? | ~3% (no-take) as of 2024 |

## Common Confusion Points

**Trophic level is not fixed per species.** Bluefin tuna feed on squid (TL 3) and anchovies
(TL 2) — their trophic position varies with diet. "Omnivory" (feeding across multiple levels)
is common in the ocean. The 10% rule applies on average, not for every step in every chain.

**Keystone ≠ dominant.** Sea otters are rare in most systems. Keystone species are defined by
disproportionate effect relative to biomass, not by their abundance. A species can be rare but
still functionally essential.

**MSY is still used despite its problems.** No better simple model replaces it in fisheries
management. The alternative — ecosystem-based fisheries management (EBM) — is more accurate
but requires much more data and is harder to apply politically.

**Ocean acidification ≠ acid ocean.** The ocean is still alkaline (pH >8 currently) and will
remain alkaline even under worst-case scenarios (pH ~7.7-7.8 by 2100). "Acidification" means
"becoming more acid" — moving from pH 8.2 toward 7.8, never actually crossing pH 7.0. But pH 7.8
is catastrophically different for carbonate chemistry: aragonite saturation drops below critical
thresholds for many calcifying organisms.

**Dead zones can be seasonal.** The Gulf of Mexico dead zone forms every spring-summer and
partially recovers each fall when storms mix the water column. Baltic and Black Sea dead zones
are more permanent due to longer stratification. "Dead zone" implies no fish, but the fauna
return when O₂ recovers — unless the zone persists and sediment becomes permanently anoxic.
