# Food Science — Maillard, Emulsification, Sous Vide, and Molecular Gastronomy

## The Big Picture

Food science is the application of chemistry, physics, and microbiology to understand and control what happens to food during preparation, processing, and storage. Molecular gastronomy (Adrià, Blumenthal, This, McGee) brought this to restaurant cooking in the 1990s-2000s, but the underlying principles are the same ones that explain why bread browns, why mayo doesn't separate, and why gelatin sets.

```
FOOD SCIENCE CORE DOMAINS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HEAT CHEMISTRY:
  Maillard reaction    → browning, flavor (meat, bread, coffee)
  Caramelization       → browning, sweetness (sugar)
  Denaturation         → protein structure change (eggs, meat)
  Gelatinization       → starch swelling/gelling (thickening)

COLLOID CHEMISTRY:
  Emulsification       → oil-water interface (mayo, dressings, sauces)
  Foams                → gas-liquid interface (whipped cream, meringue)
  Gels                 → liquid trapped in polymer network (gelatin, agar, pectin)
  Sols                 → particles suspended in liquid (milk)

FLAVOR CHEMISTRY:
  Volatile compounds   → aroma (aldehydes, esters, terpenes, thiols)
  Taste compounds      → salt, acid, bitter, sweet, umami
  Texture              → mouthfeel (viscosity, fat globule size)
  Flavor interactions  → umami synergy, salt + acid amplification

PRECISION COOKING:
  Sous vide            → controlled time × temperature
  Molecular techniques → hydrocolloids, spherification, transglutaminase
```

## The Maillard Reaction

### Discovery and Mechanism

Louis-Camille Maillard (French physician, 1878–1936) reported the reaction in 1912 while studying amino acid-sugar interactions in biological chemistry. He had no idea it was relevant to cooking — that application came decades later.

```
MAILLARD REACTION MECHANISM (simplified)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
REACTANTS: Amino acids + reducing sugars (glucose, fructose, lactose, etc.)
CONDITION: Temperature 140-165°C; low water activity favors reaction
           (wet surfaces → steam → temperature stuck at 100°C → no Maillard)

REACTION CASCADE:
  Amino group (-NH₂) + carbonyl (C=O of reducing sugar)
       │ Amadori rearrangement
       ▼
  Amadori product (rearranged intermediate)
       │ multiple pathways
       ▼
  100-150°C: Colorless precursors form
  150-165°C: Yellow/orange pigments form
  165-180°C: Brown pigments form (melanoidins)
  >180°C:    Darker brown + acrolein/acrylamide formation (harmful)

PRODUCTS:
  ~1,000+ distinct volatile compounds formed (aroma complexity)
  Key aroma compounds by food:
    Bread crust: furfuryl mercaptan, 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (rice-popcorn note)
    Roasted coffee: 2-furfurylthiol, pyrazines, furanones
    Seared beef: 2-methyl-3-furanthiol, bis(2-methyl-3-furyl) disulfide
    Chocolate: pyrazines, furans, aldehydes
    French fries: acrolein, methylglyoxal (after ~180°C, some acrylamide)
```

### Maillard vs Caramelization

```
COMPARISON
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Feature          Maillard Reaction       Caramelization
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reactants        Amino acids + sugars    Sucrose/other sugars only
Temperature      ~140-165°C (onset)      ~160°C (sucrose) / ~110°C (fructose)
pH effect        Faster in alkaline      Faster in acid or alkali
Examples         Meat, bread, coffee     Caramel candy, creme brûlée top
Flavors          Meaty, nutty, roasted   Sweet, buttery, slightly bitter
Products         Melanoidins + 1000+     Diacetyl, furaneol, hydroxymethylfurfural
                  volatiles
```

**Practical:** Adding baking soda (alkaline) to chocolate chip cookies → faster Maillard browning → darker cookie with more browning flavors. Traditional Chinese red-braised pork: sugar + alkaline soy sauce → Maillard dominates over caramelization.

## Emulsification

### Emulsion Types

An emulsion is a thermodynamically unstable dispersion of one liquid in another immiscible liquid. Without an emulsifier, oil and water separate immediately.

```
EMULSION TYPES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Oil-in-Water (O/W):   Oil droplets suspended in water continuous phase
                      Examples: milk, cream, vinaigrette, mayonnaise
                      Mouthfeel: lighter, thinner

Water-in-Oil (W/O):   Water droplets suspended in oil continuous phase
                      Examples: butter, margarine, some spreads
                      Mouthfeel: richer, heavier, "greasy"

Emulsifier mechanism:
  Amphiphilic molecule (hydrophilic head + hydrophobic tail)
  Head faces water phase
  Tail faces oil phase
  Creates stable interface → prevents coalescence
```

### Key Emulsifiers in Food

| Emulsifier | Source | Application |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Lecithin** | Egg yolk, soy | Mayo, chocolate, bread |
| **Mono/diglycerides** | Partial glycerol esters | Commercial bread, ice cream |
| **Protein** | Egg white, milk casein | Whipped cream, sauce |
| **Soy lecithin** | Extracted from soy | Chocolate (prevents bloom), salad dressing |
| **Mustard** | Allyl isothiocyanates + mucilage | Classic vinaigrette |

### Mayonnaise: The Canonical Emulsion

```
MAYONNAISE PHYSICS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Composition: 70-80% oil, 10-15% water (vinegar + lemon), egg yolk, salt

Process:
  Acid (vinegar/lemon): lowers pH → denatures some proteins → better emulsification
  Egg yolk: lecithin (phosphatidylcholine) as emulsifier
            Adds LDL (lipoproteins) as secondary emulsifiers
  Mechanical energy (whisking): breaks oil into tiny droplets

Why it's stable:
  Lecithin head → water phase; lecithin tail → oil phase
  Adsorbs to oil-water interface immediately
  Steric repulsion between coated droplets → prevents coalescence
  Viscosity increase: packed droplets slow Brownian motion

Why it fails:
  Adding oil too fast → droplets too large → not enough emulsifier per droplet
  Too little emulsifier → some droplets un-coated → coalesce
  Temperature: too cold → lecithin crystallizes → fails
               too hot → protein denatures differently → fails
  Freeze → ice crystals destroy emulsion structure

Fix broken mayo: add to fresh egg yolk + acid drop by drop
```

## Sous Vide

Sous vide (French: "under vacuum") is precision temperature cooking in a water bath. The technique was developed commercially by Bruno Goussault (food scientist) in 1974 and Georges Pralus (chef) independently for foie gras; it became mainstream in high-end kitchens in the 2000s.

```
SOUS VIDE PRINCIPLES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional cooking problem:
  Oven at 200°C → surface of steak → 200°C surface, 55°C center
  To reach 55°C center → surface overcooks to 80°C
  Result: gradient of doneness → grey ring around rare center

Sous vide solution:
  Water bath at exact target temperature (e.g., 55°C)
  Food in vacuum-sealed bag in bath
  Time (hours) → entire piece equilibrates to 55°C
  No gradient — uniform doneness throughout
  Then: high-heat sear (Maillard) immediately before serving

PASTEURIZATION WITHOUT OVERCOOKING:
  Pasteurization = time × temperature (not just temperature)
  Traditional: 72°C for 15 seconds (high-temp, very short)
  Sous vide: 55°C for 60-90 min = equivalent pasteurization
  Allows "pasteurized medium-rare" steak
  FDA tables: time-temperature equivalents for pathogen reduction

SPECIFIC APPLICATIONS:
  Chicken breast: 63°C / 1-2 hours → perfectly moist (denatures salmonella)
  Salmon: 40-45°C / 30-40 min → translucent, custard-like texture
  Egg: 63°C / 1 hour → silky runny yolk (coagulation begins ~62°C)
       65°C / 1 hour → soft-cooked yolk
  Short ribs: 55-60°C / 48-72 hours → collagen → gelatin; fork-tender
  Carrots: 84°C / 1-2 hours → perfectly cooked but intact
```

## Molecular Gastronomy

Ferran Adrià (elBulli, 1987-2011) and Heston Blumenthal (The Fat Duck) drove the "molecular gastronomy" movement — applying food science principles to create novel textures, presentations, and sensory experiences.

```
MOLECULAR GASTRONOMY TECHNIQUES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SPHERIFICATION (Adrià, 2003):
  Basic: Alginate solution + calcium chloride bath
    Alginate dripped into CaCl₂ bath → calcium cross-links membrane → sphere
    Liquid interior; thin gel membrane; pops in mouth
    Example: "Olive oil caviar," mango spheres
  Reverse: Calcium in food, alginate in bath (for dairy/alcoholic spheres)

GEL NOODLES / GEL SHEETS:
  Methylcellulose noodles (hot broth → gels, dissolves in mouth)
  Agar pasta (sets at room temp, melts hot)
  Gelatin "glass" sheets

FOAMS:
  Liquid + soy lecithin → immersion blender → persistent foam
  Warm foam: use gelatin or methylcellulose in hot liquid
  Applications: beetroot foam, parmesan foam, truffle foam
  Overused in late 2000s → became parody of the style

TRANSGLUTAMINASE ("meat glue"):
  Enzyme cross-links glutamine + lysine residues in proteins
  Bonds meat pieces together (cold-set, irreversible)
  Applications: fish terrines, vegetable-bound proteins

LN₂ / CRYOGENIC:
  Liquid nitrogen: -196°C instantly freezes
  Powder (from fats/liquids): "parmesan snow," chocolate powder
  Ice cream with no ice crystals (very fast freezing → small crystals)
  Tableside drama + legitimate technique

CONTROLLED CRYSTALLIZATION:
  Tempered chocolate: specific crystal polymorph (Form V)
  Isomalt sugar sculpture: non-hygroscopic, works at lower temperature
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the Maillard reaction? | Amino acids + reducing sugars at ~140-165°C → brown, flavorful compounds |
| Maillard vs caramelization? | Maillard needs protein; caramelization is pure sugar; different flavors, different temperatures |
| Why does mayo fail when oil is added too fast? | Not enough lecithin to coat all droplets → uncoated droplets coalesce |
| What sets gelatin vs agar? | Both set on cooling; gelatin melts at mouth temperature (~35°C); agar melts at ~85°C |
| What is sous vide's key advantage? | Uniform doneness throughout; time × temperature allows pasteurization without overcooking |
| What is spherification? | Sodium alginate + calcium chloride → thin gel membrane around liquid sphere |
| What is transglutaminase? | Enzyme that cross-links proteins → "meat glue" |

## Common Confusion Points

**Maillard reaction needs DRY surface.** Wet surface → steam → temperature stuck at 100°C → no Maillard. This is why you pat meat dry before searing; why adding sugar to a sauce before reducing prevents browning.

**Sous vide is not the same as poaching.** Poaching = uncontrolled temperature (simmering water = ~85-90°C). Sous vide = precisely controlled bath to ±0.1°C. The precision is the innovation.

**"Molecular gastronomy" flavors are not artificial.** The food itself is real; the techniques are new delivery mechanisms. Olive oil caviar contains olive oil. Parmesan foam tastes like parmesan. The aesthetic is about texture/presentation surprise, not artificial flavor.

**Food science predates molecular gastronomy by decades.** Industrial food science (starch, emulsifiers, gelling agents) has been in commercial food processing since the 1940s-1960s. Adrià et al. brought these industrial techniques into restaurant kitchens with artistic intent.
