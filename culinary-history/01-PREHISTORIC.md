# Prehistoric Cooking — Fire, Hearths, and the First Technology

## The Big Picture

Cooking is the oldest technology. Before tools, before language (probably), before clothing — there was fire and what fire did to food.

```
TIMELINE: FIRE AND FOOD
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5 Mya    Oldowan tools → access to animal bone marrow (raw)
           Australopithecus/early Homo: larger teeth, small brain

2.0 Mya    Homo erectus appears
           Dramatic reduction in gut size
           Dramatic increase in brain size
           ↑ Body size
           No direct fire evidence yet, but anatomical changes suggest
           dietary shift (Wrangham: must have been cooked food)

1.0 Mya    Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa: ash, burnt bone
           Earliest convincing hearth evidence
           Still debated: controlled use or natural fire?

400 kya    Qesem Cave, Israel: repeated hearth use
           Central hearth in living space
           Elephant bones, deer bones, processed seeds

300 kya    Widespread consistent fire use across Eurasia
           Neanderthal cooking confirmed (dental calculus evidence)
           Neanderthal: big brain, small gut — cooked food confirmed

12 kya     Natufian culture (Levant): mortars, pestles, ground grains
           Pre-agricultural food processing at scale

10.5 kya   First cereal domestication (Fertile Crescent)
           Cooking transforms wild seeds into edible food
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Wrangham's Cooking Hypothesis

Richard Wrangham (*Catching Fire: How Cooking Made Us Human*, 2009) made the strongest case that cooking was biologically transformative, not merely culturally useful.

### The Energetics Argument

```
RAW FOOD                           COOKED FOOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starch: resistant granules         Starch: gelatinized, digestible
Protein: compact, folded           Protein: denatured, more accessible
Cellulose: intact, indigestible    Cellulose: softened (some breakdown)
Chewing required: hours            Chewing required: minutes
Gut extraction efficiency: ~50%    Gut extraction efficiency: ~80-95%
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net result: same food → ~30% more usable calories when cooked
```

### The Anatomical Correlates

| Feature | Raw-food apes | Homo erectus | Modern humans |
|---------|---------------|--------------|---------------|
| Gut size | Large (chimp: 2× human relative) | Reduced | Small |
| Teeth | Large molars | Smaller | Smaller |
| Jaw muscles | Large (sagittal crest) | Reduced | Further reduced |
| Brain:body ratio | 0.9% (chimp) | 2%+ | 2.5% |

**The energy budget argument:** Large gut is energetically expensive (20% of basal metabolic rate). Shrinking gut freed energy. Reducing chewing freed time (chimps spend 4-5 hr/day chewing). Both redirected to brain.

### Criticisms

- Direct evidence for controlled fire at 1 Mya is debated (vs. natural fire opportunism)
- Wrangham dates cooking influence to 1.9 Mya; fire evidence only to 1 Mya → 900kyr gap
- Alternative: shift to raw meat + marrow access (rather than cooking) explains some anatomy
- Most paleoanthropologists accept cooking mattered; dispute the timeline

## Hearth Archaeology

### What Survives

```
DIRECT EVIDENCE               INDIRECT EVIDENCE
────────────────────          ────────────────────────────────
Ash deposits                  Burnt bone fragments
Charred bone                  Heat-altered lithics (flint changes color/structure)
Carbon-14 dateable plant      Phytoliths (silica plant cells survive fire)
  material                    Magnetic field distortion (heated hearth sediments)
Polycyclic aromatic           Zooarchaeological patterns (butchery + burning)
  hydrocarbons in sediment
```

### Key Sites

| Site | Location | Age | Evidence |
|------|----------|-----|----------|
| Wonderwerk Cave | South Africa | ~1 Mya | Ash, burned bone, plant ash |
| Gesher Benot Ya'aqov | Israel | ~790 kya | Burned seeds, flint |
| Qesem Cave | Israel | ~400 kya | Central hearth, cutmarked burnt bone |
| Kebara Cave | Israel | ~60 kya | Neanderthal hearths with ash mounds |
| Pinnacle Point | South Africa | ~164 kya | Shellfish processing, ochre |

### The Hearth as Social Technology

A hearth is not just a stove — it is a **spatial anchor for group coordination**:

```
HEARTH EFFECTS ON SOCIAL ORGANIZATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Predator deterrence    → Safe sleeping, extended nighttime activity
Light extension        → Social interaction after dark → language development?
Warmth sharing         → Group cohesion, pair bonding
Food sharing           → Division of labor (gather/hunt + cook)
Communal meal          → Social hierarchy, ritual, reciprocity norms
Fixed location         → Site fidelity, base camp organization
```

## Early Food Processing Technologies

### Grinding Stones (Mortars, Pestles, Querns)

Grinding predates agriculture. The Natufian culture (15,000-11,500 BP, Levant) had elaborate grinding assemblages for processing wild grains, nuts, acorns.

```
GRINDING FUNCTION
Wild grain (hard seed coat, phytic acid, bitter)
    │ grinding + water
    ▼
Flour/paste (starch accessible, reduced anti-nutrients)
    │ cooking
    ▼
Flatbread/porridge (fully digestible, shelf-stable dried)
```

Acorns are a key case: oak trees were abundant but acorns contain bitter tannins. Grinding + leaching + cooking makes them an excellent caloric source. Evidence for acorn processing appears ~30,000 BP. Indigenous California peoples built entire food systems around processed acorns.

### Underground Ovens (Earth Ovens)

Widespread globally — Australian *mumu*, Polynesian *hāngi*, American *pibil*, Pacific Northwest pit cooking. Technology: dig pit, line with stones, heat stones with fire, add food wrapped in leaves/wet material, cover, slow-cook via retained heat. Allows cooking of tough plant materials (camas bulbs, agaves) that are otherwise unpalatable.

## Early Fermentation

Fermentation may predate controlled fire use. Overripe fruit with wild yeast creates ethanol — hominids were encountering this millions of years ago (ADH4 gene for ethanol metabolism is 10 Mya old, predating our genus).

### Evidence

```
FERMENTATION EVIDENCE TIMELINE
~13,000 BP  Raqefet Cave (Israel): stone mortars with wheat/barley starch residues
             Grain processed for fermented beverage before bread
~9,000 BP   Jiahu, China: pottery vessel residues — rice/honey/hawthorn wine
~7,000 BP   Georgia (Caucasus): grape wine residue in clay vessels
~5,000 BP   Mesopotamian written records of beer production
            "Hymn to Ninkasi" (1800 BCE) is both a hymn and a beer recipe
```

**The beer-before-bread hypothesis** (Patrick McGovern, others): Fermented beverages may have motivated grain cultivation before bread-making. Evidence is contested but the grain processing in Raqefet predates standard cereal agriculture.

### Why Fermentation Emerged Naturally

- Wild yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) is on fruit surfaces, in the air
- Grain + water + warmth + time = spontaneous fermentation
- Result: lower pH (kills pathogens), ethanol (preservative), CO₂ (bread leavening)
- All this happens without understanding the mechanism

## Salt and Early Preservation

Salt is the first industrial commodity. Its value was caloric/preservation before it was culinary.

```
SALT'S ROLES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Biological: sodium required for nerve function (~1.5-2.3g Na/day minimum)
Preservation: dehydrates bacteria (water activity reduction)
             draws water from meat/fish via osmosis
             creates bacteriostatic environment
Economic:   non-perishable, portable, universally needed
            "salary" derives from Latin salarium (salt payment to soldiers)
Cultural:   covenant salt (Bible), sacred status in many traditions
```

### Early Salt Sources

| Source | Technology | Era |
|--------|-----------|-----|
| Sea evaporation pans | Sun-drying tidal pools | ~6,000 BP, coastal |
| Salt springs/brine | Boiling brine | ~8,000 BP, Neugablonz, Germany |
| Rock salt mining | Underground shafts | ~3,500 BP, Hallstatt, Austria |
| Animal blood/organs | Diet supplement (pastoralists) | Prehistoric |

**Hallstatt, Austria** (3,500 BP): the world's oldest known salt mine, continuously operated, preserved miners' tools and food in the salt. Celtic "salt lord" economy: Hallstatt = "salt settlement" in Celtic.

## Common Confusion Points

**Wrangham's hypothesis is contested but mainstream-adjacent.** Most paleoanthropologists accept that cooking was important; the specific claim that anatomy proves cooking at 1.9 Mya (without direct fire evidence) is the contested part.

**Fermentation ≠ intentional technology (at first).** It happened naturally to stored grain and fruit. Humans learned to control and optimize it, but they didn't "invent" it — they learned to guide a natural process.

**Salt's historical value was not about taste.** In pre-refrigeration societies, salt was life-or-death for meat/fish preservation. A salt monopoly was power. The Roman *via Salaria* (Salt Road) shows salt's economic centrality.

**Neanderthals cooked.** This used to be controversial. Dental calculus and hearth evidence is now clear. Neanderthals had fire, cooked meat and plant foods, and consumed a surprisingly diverse diet including plants, shellfish, and pigeons.
