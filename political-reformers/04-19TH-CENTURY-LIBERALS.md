## Simón Bolívar (1783–1830)

### Context

Spain's Latin American colonies had been restive since the 1790s. Napoleon's invasion of Spain (1808) and the captivity of Ferdinand VII created a power vacuum: if the legitimate king was absent, to whom did loyalty flow? The Creole elite — Spanish-descended landowners born in the Americas — saw their opportunity.

Bolívar was born in Caracas to an aristocratic family, was educated in Europe, and absorbed Enlightenment political theory (Rousseau, Montesquieu, Locke). He returned to Venezuela in 1807 committed to independence.

### The Campaigns (1811–1826)

```
  BOLÍVAR'S LIBERATION OF SOUTH AMERICA
  ========================================

  1810-12  First Venezuelan Republic — failed
  1812     Exiled after Spanish reconquest
  1813     "Admirable Campaign" — reconquers Venezuela;
            "Decree of War to the Death" (harsh)
  1814     Exiled again after defeat
  1815     "Letter from Jamaica" — defines vision:
            republican federation; constitutional democracy
  1817-19  Campaign from east Venezuela + Angostura
            Congress of Angostura (1819): Venezuela constitution
  1819     Battle of Boyacá — defeats Spanish in New Granada
            Gran Colombia formed (Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador)
  1821     Battle of Carabobo — definitively frees Venezuela
  1822     Battle of Pichincha — Ecuador
  1824     Battles of Junín and Ayacucho — Peru
            (with Gen. Sucre) — Spanish power in S. America ends
  1825     Bolivia created (named after him)
```

### Bolívar's Vision vs. His Reality

| What He Proposed | What He Built | What Survived |
|-----------------|--------------|---------------|
| Pan-American union | Gran Colombia (1819–1831) | Dissolved; became Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador |
| Republican constitutions | Bolivian Constitution (1826): president for life | Rejected; never implemented |
| Slavery abolished | Gradual abolition + military service for freedom | Slavery ended more slowly in practice |
| Democratic republic | Ruled by executive decree; dictatorship 1828–30 | Constitutional systems eventually emerged |

**The contradiction**: Bolívar said "Only democracy can bring true freedom" and then assumed dictatorial powers when he concluded his compatriots weren't ready for democracy. He died in 1830 en route to exile, disillusioned: "Those who served the revolution have ploughed the sea."

### Legacy

The legacy is the *act* of liberation, not the institutions Bolívar built — which mostly failed. Six countries' independence (Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, Panama) is his work. The institutional failures reflect a genuine problem: colonial societies with no tradition of self-governance cannot instantly produce stable republics. Spain had spent 300 years preventing any such tradition from developing.

## Giuseppe Garibaldi (1807–1882)

### What He Did

Garibaldi is the Italian unification's military engine — a genuinely charismatic guerrilla commander who fought in South America (Brazilian Empire, Uruguay) before returning to Europe for the 1848 revolutions and the final unification campaigns.

**The Expedition of the Thousand (1860)**: The pivotal act. With 1,089 volunteers (the *Camicie Rosse* — red shirts), Garibaldi invaded Sicily in May 1860, defeated the Neapolitan Bourbon army against all expectations, crossed to the mainland, and marched north. By September, he had conquered the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies — a territory of 9 million people — with roughly 20,000 volunteers. He then handed it over to King Victor Emmanuel II of Piedmont-Sardinia.

```
  THE INSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
  ============================

  Garibaldi was a republican (wanted a republic, not a monarchy)
  Cavour (Prime Minister of Piedmont) was a constitutional monarchist
  Victor Emmanuel II was the king who would unify Italy

  The political genius:
  Garibaldi conquered the south militarily
  Then handed it to a king he didn't fully support
  Because he concluded that unity under a monarch was better
  than continued division

  "Italy is born" — his reported words to Victor Emmanuel II
  at their meeting in Teano, October 26, 1860
```

**Why Garibaldi matters beyond Italy**: He demonstrated that a volunteer force with ideological commitment can defeat a professional army. He was a model for guerrilla nationalists across the 19th and 20th centuries — and explicitly admired by Mazzini, Lincoln (who offered him a Union command), and later by nationalist movements globally.

## Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865)

### What He Did

Lincoln's historical significance has two distinct components: **preservation of the Union** and **abolition of slavery**. These are related but not identical.

**The Constitutional Argument**: Lincoln's position on secession was constitutional, not merely sentimental:

```
  LINCOLN'S CONSTITUTIONAL LOGIC
  =================================

  The Union created the states (Articles of Confederation, then
  Constitution) → therefore states cannot unilaterally dissolve
  what created them.

  Secession = insurrection, not independence.
  The federal government has a duty to suppress insurrection.

  Compare to:
  Jefferson Davis's position: states are sovereign; they entered
  the union voluntarily and can leave voluntarily.

  This is a genuine constitutional dispute, not just about slavery.
  Lincoln chose the nationalist interpretation; Jefferson Davis the
  states' rights interpretation. The Civil War settled it by force.
```

**The Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863)**: Military and diplomatic instrument disguised as moral document. It:
- Freed enslaved people in Confederate states only (where Lincoln had no authority)
- Did NOT free enslaved people in border states (Delaware, Maryland, Kentucky, Missouri) that stayed in the Union
- Was justified as a war measure under commander-in-chief powers
- Prevented Britain and France from recognizing the Confederacy (European opinion turned against slavery in 1830s; recognizing a slave-states' revolt became politically untenable)

**The 13th Amendment (December 6, 1865)**: The actual legal work. "Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime... shall exist within the United States." Ratified eight months after Lincoln's assassination.

### Lincoln's Coalition Management

The specific skill that produced the 13th Amendment: managing a fractious coalition of radical Republicans (want immediate equality), moderate Republicans (want union but unsure about full equality), border-state unionists (want union; do not want abolition), and war Democrats (support war; skeptical of abolition).

Lincoln's method:
```
  HOW LINCOLN PASSED THE 13TH AMENDMENT
  ========================================

  January 1865: Needed Democratic votes for 2/3 majority.
  Lincoln authorized patronage offers to lame-duck Democratic
  congressmen (government jobs in exchange for votes).
  This was bare-knuckle politics.

  "I am President of the United States, clothed with immense
  power, and I expect you to procure those votes."
  — Lincoln to his agents

  16 Democrats voted yes. Amendment passed 119-56.
  Four months later, Lincoln was dead.
```

### The Counterfactual

If Lincoln loses the 1864 election to George McClellan (peace Democrat): McClellan had pledged to negotiate with the Confederacy. The likely outcome is a negotiated peace recognizing Confederate independence, with slavery intact in the Confederate states indefinitely. Abolition does not happen until much later — if at all — through constitutional means.

The 1864 election was genuinely close until Sherman took Atlanta in September 1864. Sherman's military victory enabled Lincoln's political victory, which enabled abolition.

## Otto von Bismarck (1815–1898)

### What He Did

Bismarck unified the German states under Prussian leadership through *Realpolitik* — politics based on power and practical considerations rather than ethical or ideological principles. He served as Minister-President of Prussia (1862–90) and Chancellor of the German Empire (1871–90).

**The Three Wars of Unification:**

| War | Year | Result |
|-----|------|--------|
| Danish War (with Austria) | 1864 | Schleswig-Holstein transferred from Denmark |
| Austro-Prussian War | 1866 | Austria expelled from German affairs; North German Confederation created |
| Franco-Prussian War | 1870–71 | France defeated; German Empire proclaimed in the Hall of Mirrors, Versailles |

**The institutional architecture:**

```
  BISMARCK'S GERMANY (1871)
  ==========================

  +---------------------+
  | Kaiser (Wilhelm I)  | ← ceremonial but real authority
  +---------------------+
           |
  +---------------------+
  | Chancellor (Bismarck)| ← actual governing power
  +---------------------+
           |
  +---------------------+        +-------------------+
  | Reichstag (Parliament)        | Bundesrat        |
  | Elected by universal          | (State governments)|
  | male suffrage                 | Prussian-dominated |
  +---------------------+        +-------------------+

  KEY FEATURE: Chancellor not responsible to Reichstag.
  Reichstag could reject legislation but couldn't remove
  chancellor. Power concentrated in chancellor + kaiser.
  This was Bismarck's design to govern democratically
  while retaining autocratic authority.
```

**The Welfare State (1883–89)**: Bismarck's counter-intuitive move — he introduced the first national social insurance programs:
- Health insurance (1883)
- Accident insurance (1884)
- Old-age and disability pensions (1889)

**Motive**: Explicitly anti-socialist. "I will forestall the socialists if the government will only have the courage to promote the welfare of workers." He used state welfare to undercut the Social Democratic Party's appeal. The welfare state was *conservative* in purpose — preserving existing hierarchies by removing the economic grievances that fueled socialist organizing.

**Consequences**: This framing is fundamental to understanding the welfare state debate. Bismarck's model directly influenced Attlee, FDR, and every subsequent welfare-state builder — and the conservative critique that welfare undermines rather than builds independence traces to the same analysis that Bismarck himself used, just from the opposite political direction.

### Bismarck's Failure Mode

His system was personally dependent. He had no mechanism for passing power to a successor. When Wilhelm II became Kaiser in 1888 and forced Bismarck's resignation in 1890, the system broke down. The German political structure Bismarck built — strong chancellor, weak parliament, aggressive nationalist identity — created the conditions for WWI.

## William Ewart Gladstone (1809–1898)

### What He Did

Four times Prime Minister of the United Kingdom (1868–74, 1880–85, 1886, 1892–94), Gladstone is the model of incremental liberal reform within a constitutional system.

**Major reforms:**

| Reform | Year | What Changed |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Irish Church Disestablishment | 1869 | Church of Ireland disestablished; removed state subsidy |
| Land Act | 1870 | First statutory protection for Irish tenant farmers |
| Elementary Education Act | 1870 | Established public schools in England |
| Army reform (Cardwell) | 1871 | Abolished purchase of commissions; merit-based officer corps |
| Ballot Act | 1872 | Secret ballot for elections — ended landlord coercion of tenants |
| Judicature Act | 1873 | Consolidated English court system |
| Corrupt Practices Act | 1883 | Limited election spending; reduced bribery |
| Third Reform Act | 1884–85 | Extended franchise to agricultural laborers; 3x electorate |
| Irish Home Rule bills | 1886, 1893 | Failed; split Liberal Party; became consuming mission |

### Gladstone's Method: "Irish business"

Gladstone became Prime Minister in 1868 saying he had "a mission to pacify Ireland." The Irish Question — the governance of a colonial territory with a hostile Catholic majority and a Protestant settler minority — consumed the second half of his career. His conversion to Irish Home Rule in 1885 split his party (Joseph Chamberlain and the Liberal Unionists broke away) and cost him two governments.

```
  THE GLADSTONE-BISMARCK COMPARISON
  ===================================

  GLADSTONE                       BISMARCK
  ---------                       --------
  Parliamentary system works      Parliament = necessary but managed
  Reform through persuasion       Reform through power
  Liberalism as principle         Conservatism as goal; welfare as tool
  Moral language of politics      Amoral Realpolitik
  Ireland: self-government        Poland: Germanization
  Reduced empire's worst parts    Built empire through force
  Lost to his principles          Maintained power until forced out

  Both were highly effective. Both left incomplete legacies.
  Gladstone's failures came from overextending moral
  commitments. Bismarck's failures came from building
  a system that required him personally to function.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Lincoln was always an abolitionist."**
He was an anti-slavery politician who wanted to prevent slavery's *expansion* to new territories, not a radical abolitionist. His 1858 position: slavery is a moral wrong but the Constitution protects it where it exists. His evolution from "contain slavery" to "abolish it" happened during the war, driven by military necessity, coalition management, and genuine moral development.

**"Bismarck was a reactionary."**
He was a conservative — he wanted to preserve Prussian and monarchical authority. He introduced universal male suffrage (!) to undercut liberal middle-class constitutional demands, and introduced the welfare state to undercut socialist working-class organizing. He used radical instruments for conservative ends. The welfare state he built is not conservative by modern classification; it was by the standards of 1880.

**"Garibaldi failed because Italy became a monarchy instead of a republic."**
Garibaldi made a conscious strategic choice: republican principles were less important than actual unification. He handed Sicily and Naples to Victor Emmanuel because he judged that Piedmont under Cavour could deliver unification and Mazzini's republican movement could not. This was correct. The subsequent Italian constitutional monarchy, while imperfect, was a functioning state.

**"Bolivar's failure was personal."**
It was structural. The societies he liberated had no tradition of self-governance; colonial administration had actively prevented its development. The Creole elite who inherited power had the education and wealth to govern but no institution-building experience. The "ploughing the sea" epigram is real but it obscures the genuine structural difficulty.
