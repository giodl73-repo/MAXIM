## Magna Carta and the Barons (1215)

### What Happened

King John of England (reigned 1199–1216) was a uniquely bad king in specific ways that matter: he lost Normandy (1204), exhausted the treasury, levied arbitrary taxes, imprisoned barons without trial, and defied the Pope (provoking an interdict that closed all churches in England). By 1215, the barons were in open revolt. They took London. John had no choice.

**Runnymede, June 15, 1215**: John sealed Magna Carta — 63 clauses covering everything from feudal obligations to fish weirs on the Thames.

### What the Clauses Actually Said

Most of Magna Carta is mundane feudal administration. The historically significant clauses:

| Clause | Content | Long-term significance |
|--------|---------|----------------------|
| 39 | "No free man shall be seized, imprisoned... except by the lawful judgment of his equals or by the law of the land" | Habeas corpus; due process; foundation of rule of law |
| 40 | "To no one will we sell, to no one deny or delay right or justice" | Independence of courts from executive |
| 12 | Extraordinary taxes require consent of the kingdom | Parliamentary control of taxation (developed much later) |
| 61 | 25-baron committee empowered to enforce the charter | First constitutional enforcement mechanism (failed immediately) |

**Clause 39 is the load-bearing pillar.** Everything built on the idea that the executive cannot imprison people arbitrarily traces to this clause — through Coke, Locke, the English Bill of Rights (1689), the 5th Amendment (US), and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948).

### What Happened Immediately After

John appealed to the Pope, who annulled the charter as "shameful, demeaning, illegal, and unjust." John died in 1216 (dysentery). His nine-year-old son became Henry III. The regency government reissued Magna Carta — slightly revised, stripped of the enforcement clause — to win baronial loyalty during a succession crisis. It became standard practice to reissue it.

```
  MAGNA CARTA'S LIFECYCLE:
  ========================

  1215: Sealed under duress → annulled in 9 weeks
  1216: Reissued to stabilize regency (Henry III)
  1217: Reissued again; Forest Charter separated out
  1225: Definitive version — Henry III's own free gift
  1297: Entered on the Statute Roll (statute law)

  1600s: Sir Edward Coke reinterprets it as fundamental law
         constraining both king AND parliament
         → "Magna Carta will have no sovereign" (Coke)
         → American colonists deploy this argument against Parliament
```

### The "Great Charter" Myth

Magna Carta as a document protecting individual rights against tyranny is largely a 17th-century invention. In 1215, it was a feudal settlement protecting barons' property rights against royal expropriation. The document says "free men" (*liberi homines*) — in 1215, perhaps 10% of the English population; serfs had no protection under it.

What makes it historically important: (1) the principle that the king is *under* the law, not above it — which was genuinely novel as enforceable doctrine; (2) Edward Coke's early-17th-century reinterpretation, which read it as protecting all English subjects; and (3) the American colonists' deployment of that reinterpretation as a weapon against Parliament.

## Simon de Montfort and Parliament (1265)

### Context

Henry III (1216–1272) governed badly, spent money lavishly on foreign wars and relatives, and repeatedly violated the principles barons thought Magna Carta established. Simon de Montfort, Earl of Leicester, led the baronial reform movement. After his military victory over Henry at the Battle of Lewes (1264), de Montfort controlled the kingdom for 14 months.

### The Parliament of January 1265

De Montfort summoned a parliament that included, for the first time:
- Two knights from each county
- Two burgesses from each town

This was the innovation. Previous councils had been baronial assemblies. De Montfort included the *commons* — knights and townspeople — in part to build a broader coalition for his regime, in part because he apparently believed representation should extend beyond the nobility.

```
  DE MONTFORT'S PARLIAMENT vs. EARLIER COUNCILS
  ===============================================

  EARLIER:                      DE MONTFORT'S (1265):
  +------------------+          +------------------+
  | King             |          | King             |
  | Great lords      |          | Great lords      |
  | Bishops          |          | Bishops          |
  | (Invited who     |          | 2 knights/county |
  |  king wanted)    |          | 2 burgesses/town |
  +------------------+          +------------------+
                                 ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
                                 THESE ARE NEW
                                 Commons = representative assembly
```

### De Montfort's Death (1265)

Henry III's son, the future Edward I, defeated de Montfort at the Battle of Evesham. De Montfort was killed; his body was mutilated. Henry III restored his prerogatives.

But Edward I, crowned 1272, kept the parliament model. His "Model Parliament" of 1295 included representatives from counties, cities, and boroughs alongside lords and clergy — essentially the Edwardian formalization of de Montfort's improvisation.

### Counterfactual

Without de Montfort's improvisation in 1265: representative assemblies in England might have developed differently, or more slowly. The specific form — two chambers, commons separate from lords — is traceable to this moment. The American colonial assemblies and Congress are downstream of this model.

### Why de Montfort Matters More Than He's Remembered

De Montfort is not well-known in American or world history, but his specific innovation — adding non-noble representatives to a royal council and calling it a parliament — is the institutional DNA of every legislature that includes elected representatives from geographic constituencies. Madison and Jefferson did not invent representative government; they inherited a 500-year-old English practice.

## Martin Luther (1483–1546)

### What He Did

On October 31, 1517, Luther nailed (or sent by letter — historians dispute) 95 theses to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg. The theses attacked the sale of *indulgences* — certificates sold by the Church purporting to reduce time in purgatory. Within weeks, printed copies spread across Europe.

This was not the first critique of Church corruption. What made it different: the printing press.

```
  LUTHER + PRINTING PRESS AS SYSTEM
  ===================================

  Wycliffe (1380s): Similar ideas → handwritten copies → limited spread
  Hus (1415): Similar ideas → council convened → burned at stake
  Luther (1517): 95 theses → Gutenberg press (1440) = 50 years old
                           → Thousands of copies within weeks
                           → Pope cannot control information distribution
                           → Frederick III (Elector of Saxony) protects Luther
                              (political interest in weakening papal authority)
                           → Diet of Worms (1521): Luther refuses to recant
                           → Excommunicated; translates Bible into German
                           → Vernacular scripture = individual reading =
                              priests not required as intermediaries
```

### Luther's Political Consequences

Luther did not intend a political revolution. His intent was theological reform. The political consequences were seismic:

| Political Effect | Mechanism |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Princes adopted Lutheranism to seize Church properties | *Cuius regio* logic: prince's religion = territory's religion |
| Weakened papal authority over secular rulers | Undermined the Pope's ability to excommunicate and sanction kings |
| German nationalism proto-form | Luther's German Bible created a common written standard — linguistic unification predating political unification by 350 years |
| Peasants' War (1524–25) | Peasants misread Luther's spiritual freedom as social freedom; Luther repudiated them viciously |
| Fragmentation of Christendom | No single religious authority → competing denominations → eventually, tolerance as necessity |
| Thirty Years' War (1618–48) | Protestant vs. Catholic conflict → 8 million dead → Peace of Westphalia |

### Luther and Tolerance

Luther is *not* a figure of religious tolerance. He was virulently anti-Semitic (his 1543 tract "On the Jews and Their Lies" called for burning synagogues and expelling Jews; it was cited by Nazi propagandists). He supported brutal suppression of the Peasants' War. His contribution was fracturing religious authority, not advocating pluralism.

### Legacy

The Peace of Westphalia (1648), which ended the Thirty Years' War, established the principle of state sovereignty — that rulers govern within their borders free from external religious authority. This is the foundation of modern international law. It is downstream of the Reformation, which is downstream of Luther. The path runs: Luther's challenge to papal authority → religious fragmentation → wars of religion → sovereignty doctrine as the only stable settlement → Westphalian international order → UN Charter (1945).

## John Calvin (1509–1564)

### What He Did

Calvin arrived in Geneva in 1536, was expelled, returned in 1541, and governed it as a theocratic republic until his death. His contribution is less institutional reform than intellectual architecture.

**Calvin's Geneva:**
```
  GENEVAN THEOCRACY (Calvin's model, 1541–1564)
  ===============================================

  City Council  <────────────────────> Consistory
  (secular govt)   mutual oversight    (Church discipline)
       |                                    |
       v                                    v
  Laws, order,                        Moral conduct,
  commerce,                           excommunication,
  defense                             theological orthodoxy

  Calvin controlled through persuasion + moral authority,
  not formal office (he was not a citizen until 1559).

  Executed Michael Servetus (1553) for theological heresy.
  Banished people for dancing, playing cards, irreverent behavior.
```

**Calvinist theological innovations with political consequences:**

| Doctrine | Political implication |
|----------|----------------------|
| Predestination | "Elect" status unknowable → visible success = possible sign of grace → Protestant work ethic (Weber's thesis) |
| Covenant theology | Government rests on covenant between ruler, people, and God → if ruler violates covenant, resistance justified |
| Consistory model | Church independent of state, with its own authority → church-state tension as constitutional framework |
| Vocation (calling) | Every occupation has religious dignity → no sacred/secular hierarchy → bourgeois culture |

### Calvin's Influence on Politics

Calvinist covenant theology directly influenced the theoretical justification for resistance to tyranny:

```
  CALVINIST POLITICAL THEOLOGY → CONSTITUTIONAL THEORY
  ======================================================

  Calvinist resistance theory (Huguenots, Scots):
  → If ruler violates covenant, resistance is justified
  → Vindiciae contra Tyrannos (1579): anonymous Huguenot tract
    "Against tyrants" — systematic theory of resistance

  John Knox (Scotland): → Presbyterian church government
                           (elders elected, not appointed)
                           → democratic structure within church
                           → model for civil representation

  Puritans (England, then New England):
  → Calvinist covenant theology
  → Mayflower Compact (1620): covenant = founding document
  → Town meeting = congregational church meeting applied to
     civic governance
  → John Winthrop's "city on a hill" = theocratic ideal that
     secularizes into American exceptionalism
```

Max Weber's thesis (*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905) that Calvinist predestination created the psychological preconditions for capitalist accumulation remains contested but influential. The mechanism: if success signals election, then worldly success becomes a spiritual obligation, and accumulation (rather than spending) becomes a virtue.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Magna Carta was a democratic document."**
It was a feudal settlement protecting noble property rights. The interpretation as a universal rights document was invented 400 years later by Edward Coke and adopted by American colonists for their own political purposes. This does not make the document unimportant — it makes the history of its interpretation important.

**"Luther was a religious tolerant who freed Christians from papal tyranny."**
Luther suppressed the Peasants' War, was virulently anti-Semitic, and anathematized the Swiss Reformed tradition (Zwingli, later Calvin). He fragmented religious authority, which eventually led to tolerance as a necessary settlement — but that was an unintended consequence, not his intention.

**"Calvin's theocracy was just religious tyranny."**
Calvin's model was more constitutionally sophisticated than that framing allows. The Consistory exercised discipline independent of civil authority; the Council governed independently of the church. The mutual-oversight model is not identical to a simple fusion of church and state. The model's influence on Presbyterian church governance (elected elders) fed directly into representative government theory.

**"De Montfort was a democrat ahead of his time."**
He included the commons for strategic reasons — coalition building for his regime. Whether he had principled reasons for representative inclusion is unclear. What matters is the institutional innovation, not his intention.
