# Democratic Reformers: FDR, Attlee, Adenauer, De Gaulle, Lula

## The Big Picture

```
  POST-WWII DEMOCRATIC STATE BUILDING (1933–2003)
  =================================================

  The central question: Can democracy survive economic crisis,
  war, fascism, and the Cold War — while expanding its own
  basis and welfare guarantees?

  FDR (US, 1933–45):
  - Depression + fascism threat → New Deal + WWII coalition
  - State responsibility for welfare; Labor rights; Banking regulation
  - Internment of Japanese-Americans (dark side)
  - Extended executive power permanently

  ATTLEE (UK, 1945–51):
  - WWII aftermath → welfare state built from scratch
  - NHS, National Insurance, nationalization
  - Decolonization beginning (India 1947)
  - In one parliament, against resistance, from rubble

  ADENAUER (West Germany, 1949–63):
  - From Nazi defeat to functional democracy
  - Basic Law (constitution) with strong rights protections
  - Reconciliation with France (Schuman Plan → EU)
  - Market economy + welfare state ("social market economy")
  - NATO integration; Western alignment

  DE GAULLE (France, 1944–46; 1958–69):
  - Liberation; provisional government
  - Returned in crisis (Algeria War) to write new constitution
  - 5th Republic: strong president; stable executive
  - "France" as political entity above party

  LULA (Brazil, 2003–2010):
  - First working-class president of Brazil
  - Bolsa Família: conditional cash transfers → 30M out of poverty
  - Growth + equity simultaneously (contested achievement)
  - Later corruption scandal; jailed 2018; returned 2023
```

## Franklin Delano Roosevelt (1882–1945)

### The Crisis

By 1932, US unemployment stood at 25%. Banks had failed by the thousands. Hoover's response — laissez-faire, balanced budgets, no federal relief — had made things worse. In Europe, economic crisis was producing fascism (Mussolini in power since 1922; Hitler chancellor January 1933). The structural question: could liberal democracy respond to mass unemployment without becoming either communist or fascist?

### What the New Deal Did

```
  THE NEW DEAL: STRUCTURE (1933–38)
  ===================================

  FIRST 100 DAYS (1933):
  - Emergency Banking Act: closed banks; federal review before reopening
  - FDIC: deposit insurance (prevents bank runs)
  - CCC: Civilian Conservation Corps (2.5M workers, forests/parks)
  - TVA: Tennessee Valley Authority (hydroelectric; rural electrification)
  - FERA: Federal Emergency Relief (cash to states for relief)
  - AAA: Agricultural Adjustment Act (price supports)
  - NIRA: National Industrial Recovery Act (labor rights; later struck down)

  SECOND NEW DEAL (1935–38):
  - Wagner Act: right to organize; collective bargaining; NLRB
  - Social Security Act: old-age pensions + unemployment insurance
  - SEC: Securities and Exchange Commission (market regulation)
  - Works Progress Administration: 8M jobs; art, theater, roads
  - Rural Electrification Act: 90% of farms had electricity by 1950
    (12% in 1935)

  KEY INSTITUTIONAL INNOVATIONS:
  FDIC         — bank deposit insurance; prevents bank run spirals
  SEC          — market transparency requirements; investor protection
  NLRB         — labor relations board; collective bargaining enforcement
  Social Security — permanent entitlement; not temporary relief
  TVA          — federal infrastructure investment as development model
```

**What the New Deal Did Not Do**: The economy did not fully recover until WWII mobilization. The New Deal reduced unemployment from 25% to ~15%; it did not end the Depression. The "WWII ended the Depression" analysis is largely correct. The New Deal's significance is institutional, not purely economic: it established the principle that the federal government bears responsibility for citizens' economic welfare.

**FDR's Political Method**:

FDR governed through coalition management and pragmatic experimentation. He tried things, kept what worked, discarded what didn't. "Above all, try something." His coalition: urban ethnic workers, Southern segregationists, Black voters (who had been Republican since Lincoln), labor unions, farmers, and intellectuals. This coalition was internally incoherent on race — the Southern segregationists refused to include Black workers in the Social Security Act's original coverage (agriculture and domestic service excluded). FDR accepted this to preserve the coalition.

**The Court-Packing Scheme (1937)**: After the Supreme Court struck down several New Deal programs, FDR proposed expanding the Court to 15 justices (adding 6 Roosevelt-friendly judges). Congress rejected it — even Democratic allies. One of the few times FDR overplayed his hand. The Court subsequently upheld New Deal legislation anyway ("the switch in time that saved nine").

**Japanese American Internment (Executive Order 9066, 1942)**: 120,000 Japanese Americans (70,000 of whom were US citizens) interned in camps without due process. Authorized by FDR; upheld by Supreme Court in Korematsu v. United States (1944); formally repudiated by Congress in 1988 (Civil Liberties Act) with reparations. The Korematsu case was overturned by implication in Trump v. Hawaii (2018). This was a genuine constitutional atrocity by a wartime president.

## Clement Attlee (1883–1967)

### The Post-War Moment

The 1945 UK election took place while WWII was still technically ongoing (Germany surrendered May 8; the election was July 5, counted July 26). Churchill expected to win on the strength of his wartime leadership. Labour won by a landslide: 393 seats to 213. The returning soldiers and civilian workforce voted for what they'd been promised — a country worth fighting for.

### What Attlee Built in Five Years (1945–51)

```
  THE ATTLEE WELFARE STATE: BUILT FROM RUBBLE
  =============================================

  CONTEXT: Britain in 1945
  - National debt: 200%+ of GDP
  - Physical destruction: bombed cities, depleted industry
  - Dollar shortage: dependent on Marshall Plan
  - Colonial unrest (India about to explode)
  - Rationing continued until 1954 (longer than during the war)

  WHAT WAS BUILT ANYWAY:
  National Health Service (July 5, 1948):
  - Universal healthcare; free at point of use
  - Funded through taxation; not insurance
  - Nationalized 2,688 hospitals
  - Opposed by British Medical Association until implementation
    (Aneurin Bevan: "stuffed their mouths with gold" — gave
     consultants high pay to get cooperation)

  National Insurance Act (1946):
  - Unemployment, sickness, maternity, widow's, death benefits
  - Beveridge Report (1942) blueprint
  - Contributory system: everyone pays in; everyone benefits

  National Assistance Act (1948):
  - Safety net for those not covered by National Insurance
  - Officially ended the Victorian Poor Law system

  Nationalizations:
  - Bank of England (1946)
  - Coal mines (1947): 1,000 mines → National Coal Board
  - Railways (1948)
  - Electricity (1948)
  - Gas (1949)
  - Iron and Steel (1951) — reversed by Churchill 1952

  Indian Independence (1947):
  - Attlee moved quickly; recognized British India was ungovernable
  - Partition of India and Pakistan: 1–2M dead in communal violence
  - Not Attlee's fault, but he owned the timeline
```

**Why Attlee Succeeded Where Bolívar, Wilson, and Others Failed**:

Attlee was the opposite of a charismatic leader. He was famously unassuming — Churchill: "An empty taxi arrived at 10 Downing Street, and when the door was opened, Attlee got out." His management style: delegate to competent ministers (Bevan on Health; Morrison on nationalization; Bevin on foreign affairs); set clear goals; let people work.

The Beveridge Report (1942) had been a public sensation — it sold 700,000 copies, more than almost any British government document. Attlee was implementing a plan that the British public already wanted.

## Konrad Adenauer (1876–1967)

### The Problem He Faced

Adenauer became first Chancellor of West Germany in 1949 at age 73. Germany had:
- Just committed the Holocaust and aggressive war
- Been partitioned into four occupation zones
- Had the Federal Republic (West Germany) under effective Allied control
- No democratic tradition (Weimar Republic had lasted 14 years)
- Enormous displaced persons crisis
- Physical destruction: most cities bombed

### What He Built

**The Basic Law (Grundgesetz, 1949)**:

```
  BASIC LAW INNOVATIONS vs. WEIMAR CONSTITUTION
  ===============================================

  WEIMAR (1919) FAILURES:
  - Article 48: emergency presidential powers → Hitler used to govern
    without Reichstag
  - Easy dissolution of Reichstag
  - Proportional representation → fragmented parties
    → coalitions collapse → instability → crisis

  BASIC LAW (1949) SOLUTIONS:
  - Constructive vote of no confidence: cannot remove Chancellor
    without electing a replacement (prevents crisis of no
    government)
  - 5% threshold: parties must get 5% to enter Bundestag
    → reduces fragmentation
  - Federal Constitutional Court: independent; can strike down
    unconstitutional laws
  - Article 1: human dignity inviolable; binding on all state action
  - "Eternity clauses": certain provisions cannot be amended
    (federalism, human dignity, democratic principles)
  - No presidential emergency powers equivalent to Article 48
```

**The Social Market Economy (Soziale Marktwirtschaft)**:

Adenauer + Economics Minister Ludwig Erhard:
- Currency reform (1948, before Adenauer): replaced worthless Reichsmark with Deutschmark; price controls lifted immediately
- Market economy: private ownership; competition; no central planning
- Social: strong labor protections; codetermination (workers on supervisory boards); generous welfare benefits
- Result: *Wirtschaftswunder* (economic miracle): 8% annual growth through the 1950s

**Reconciliation with France**:
- Schuman Declaration (1950): pooled coal and steel resources (France + West Germany + Benelux + Italy)
- European Coal and Steel Community → Treaty of Rome (1957) → European Economic Community → EU
- Adenauer + De Gaulle: Élysée Treaty (1963): Franco-German friendship as European anchor
- NATO membership: West Germany rearmed (controversial) but within collective security structure

**Israel reconciliation**: Adenauer negotiated reparations with Israel (Luxemburg Agreement, 1952) — paying DM 3 billion in goods over 12 years. Opposed within Germany; supported by Adenauer as moral obligation. Established that West Germany accepted responsibility for the Holocaust.

## Charles de Gaulle (1890–1970)

### Two Phases

**Phase 1 (1940–46)**: De Gaulle refused French armistice with Germany. Escaped to London; organized Free French; maintained France as a belligerent power through the darkest years. His legitimacy was self-asserted — he had no democratic mandate, no army to speak of initially, only the claim that France continued to exist and he represented it. The claim worked.

**Phase 2 (1958–69)**: The Fourth Republic was collapsing under the Algerian War. France had had 22 governments in 13 years. A military coup was imminent. De Gaulle returned from retirement with a constitutional settlement as his condition.

**The Fifth Republic Constitution (1958)**:

```
  4TH REPUBLIC (1946–58):           5TH REPUBLIC (1958–):
  ========================           ====================
  Parliamentary supremacy           Presidential dominance
  PM responsible to Assembly        President appoints PM; dissolves
  President ceremonial              Assembly; direct election (1962)

  22 governments in 13 years        Stable executive leadership

  Fragmented parties                PM governs if presidential majority
  Constant coalition crisis         "Cohabitation" when they diverge

  Algeria War: army threatening      Constitution written to solve
  coup; government paralyzed        the specific crisis
```

De Gaulle ended the Algerian War (1962) — against the wishes of the French military and *pieds-noirs* (French settlers). He survived two assassination attempts. The Évian Accords gave Algeria independence; 800,000 French settlers returned to France.

**Gaullism**: A specific ideology — France as a great power, independent of both the US and the USSR; European integration on French terms; nuclear deterrent (*force de frappe*); withdrawal from NATO command structure. The specific content is less important than the model: national identity as political resource; the state as expression of national will.

**1968 and the Limits**: The May 1968 student-worker uprising — the largest general strike in French history — briefly seemed to threaten his government. De Gaulle disappeared for 24 hours (flew to Baden-Baden to confirm military loyalty), returned, dissolved the National Assembly, won a landslide. A year later he resigned after losing a referendum on regional reform. He died 16 months later.

## Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (b. 1945)

### What He Did

Lula was born to a family of 7 children from rural poverty in the Brazilian northeast. He never finished elementary school. He worked as a metalworker, lost a finger in a factory accident, became a union leader, led the massive ABC Paulista strikes (1978–80) that challenged the military dictatorship, co-founded the Workers' Party (PT) in 1980, ran for president and lost three times, and won in 2002.

**Bolsa Família (2003)**:

```
  BOLSA FAMÍLIA: CONDITIONAL CASH TRANSFER
  ==========================================

  MECHANISM:
  - Cash transfers to poor families
  - CONDITIONS: children must attend school; get vaccinations
  - Scale: 11 million families (2003) → 14 million (2010)
  - Cost: 0.5% of GDP
  - Results:
    - 30+ million people lifted above poverty line
    - Child mortality reduced
    - School enrollment increased
    - Malnutrition reduced

  WHY IT WORKED (vs. direct food aid, etc.):
  - Cash transfers let families prioritize their own needs
  - Conditions aligned incentives with long-term development
    (education, health) rather than short-term consumption
  - Administrative efficiency: single program vs. multiple
    overlapping programs
  - Monitoring: Cadastro Único (unified registry) tracked
    beneficiaries

  LULA's growth + equity result:
  - GDP growth: 4% average 2003–2010
  - Inequality: Gini coefficient declined
  - Both at once — not the usual tradeoff
```

**The Worker's Party as institution**: The PT was structured with internal democracy — primaries, elected leadership, delegate system. It was the most democratically organized political party in Brazilian history. When Lula ran for president in 1989, 1994, 1998, and 2002, the party organization sustained the movement through three defeats.

**The Fall (2016–18)**: Lula's successor Dilma Rousseff was impeached (2016) on budgetary manipulation charges. Lula himself was convicted in 2018 of corruption in the Lava Jato (Car Wash) operation — accepting a beachfront apartment as a bribe from construction company OAS. He was sentenced to 9 years, imprisoned, jailed for 18 months. Supreme Court later ruled the judge (Sergio Moro) had shown bias; conviction annulled. Lula ran again in 2022, narrowly defeated Bolsonaro, returned to presidency in 2023.

## Common Confusion Points

**"The New Deal ended the Great Depression."**
WWII mobilization ended it. The New Deal reduced unemployment from 25% to ~15%. The institutional significance (FDIC, SEC, Social Security, NLRB, labor rights) is permanent and distinct from the macroeconomic question.

**"The NHS was always popular in the UK."**
The British Medical Association opposed it until implementation. Junior doctors threatened strike action. Aneurin Bevan (Attlee's Health Minister) "stuffed their mouths with gold" — gave consultants very high salaries to buy cooperation. The NHS's current near-sacred status in British culture is the product of 75 years of operation, not of enthusiasm at founding.

**"Adenauer was a democrat."**
He was a Christian Democrat who believed in constitutional democracy as the best available system for Germany's specific situation. He also used intelligence services against political opponents, retained Nazi-era bureaucrats in key positions, and had an authoritarian management style within his party. He built democracy while not being a liberal democrat in temperament.

**"Lula's poverty reduction was just good timing (commodity boom)."**
Brazil had commodity booms before that didn't reduce poverty. The Bolsa Família program design — conditional cash transfers at scale with a unified registry — explains a specific outcome that didn't happen automatically. The commodity boom expanded the fiscal space; Bolsa Família directed it toward the poor.
