# Circumnavigation — Magellan+Elcano, Drake, Anson, Bougainville

## Era Overview

```
PROVING THE GLOBE: 1519–1770
==============================

  KNOWN before 1519: Africa (coast), Americas (Atlantic coast), Asia (coastal).
  UNKNOWN: Does the Pacific connect Asia to the Americas? How wide?
           Is there a southern passage? Is Terra Australis real?

  1519–22 ─── MAGELLAN+ELCANO: first circumnavigation.
              5 ships out, 1 back. 18 survivors of 270.
              Pacific proved to be enormous and crossable.

  1577–80 ─── DRAKE: second circumnavigation.
              First to command the entire voyage.
              Proved the Drake Passage — no Terra Australis to 57°S.

  1740–44 ─── ANSON: circumnavigation of survival.
              1,900 men out. 188 return. Scurvy.
              Spurred reforms in naval provisioning.

  1766–69 ─── BOUGAINVILLE: first French circumnavigation.
              Scientific. First Polynesia described for European science.

  1768–71 ─── COOK: first voyage. Transit of Venus. New Zealand. East Australia.
              (Covered in 04-PACIFIC-EXPLORATION.md)
```

## Ferdinand Magellan (1480–1521) and Juan Sebastián Elcano (c. 1487–1526)

### Bio Snapshot

**Magellan**: Portuguese nobleman who served in India and Malacca. Lost favor with Portuguese king; switched allegiance to Spain (1518). Proposed to reach the Spice Islands (Moluccas) by sailing west.

**Elcano**: Basque navigator. Joined Magellan's fleet as master (ship's officer). Commanded the *Victoria* after Magellan died. Completed the circumnavigation.

### The Voyage (1519–1522)

```
MAGELLAN-ELCANO: THE VOYAGE
==============================

  FLEET: 5 ships (Trinidad, San Antonio, Concepción, Victoria, Santiago).
         265–270 men. September 20, 1519: departs Sanlúcar de Barrameda.

  ATLANTIC TO SOUTH AMERICA:
    Brazil coast, then south.
    Winter quarters at San Julián (Patagonia), 1520.
    Mutiny attempted by 3 Spanish captains. Magellan suppresses it.
    (One captain executed, two marooned. Magellan was Portuguese in a
    Spanish enterprise — ethnic tension throughout.)

  MAGELLAN STRAIT (October–November 1520):
    550 km of channels through Tierra del Fuego.
    Took 38 days to navigate.
    San Antonio deserts, returns to Spain.
    Santiago had already been wrecked earlier (surveying).
    Only 3 ships enter the Pacific.

  THE PACIFIC (November 1520 – March 1521):
    Magellan names it "Pacific" — calm seas during their crossing.
    (The Pacific is not reliably calm — he was lucky.)
    99 days. No island with food until Guam.
    Scurvy. Eating leather straps and sawdust mixed with water.
    Men dying.

  THE PHILIPPINES (March 1521):
    Magellan makes contact with Cebu Island.
    Chief Humabon converts to Christianity.
    Chief Lapu-Lapu on Mactan Island refuses.
    APRIL 27, 1521: MAGELLAN KILLED in Battle of Mactan.
    He led a small force of 60 against hundreds on the beach.
    He is killed. His men retreat.
    ~150 Spaniards remain. 2 ships (Concepción is burned — too few crew).

  TO THE SPICE ISLANDS (July–November 1521):
    Trinidad and Victoria reach Tidore (Moluccas).
    Cloves loaded.
    Trinidad needs repairs — stays behind (eventually captured by Portugal).
    Victoria: one ship, ~47 men.

  RETURN (Elcano commanding, November 1521 – September 1522):
    Back across Indian Ocean. Cape of Good Hope.
    Portuguese harass them — one ship stop at Cape Verde.
    Victoria arrives Sanlúcar: September 6, 1522.
    18 survivors. Cargo of cloves paid for the expedition.

  WHAT WAS PROVED:
    The Earth is a globe. You can sail west and return from the east.
    The Pacific is MUCH larger than anyone imagined.
    There is a southern passage around South America.
    The Spice Islands can be reached by the western route.
```

**The calendar problem**: The crew discovered on return that their log showed they were one day behind the calendar in Spain. They had sailed west — gaining a day relative to the sun. This confused them (violating their religious calendar). This is the first recorded demonstration that a circumnavigation causes a day offset — the same issue that Jules Verne's Phileas Fogg exploits in *Around the World in Eighty Days* (1872).

## Francis Drake (1540–1596)

### Bio Snapshot

English privateer and naval officer. Devon. Second circumnavigation (1577–1580). Also led the first English circumnavigation and the first circumnavigation commanded continuously by the same person. First naval commander to circumnavigate alive (Elcano had served under Magellan). Vice Admiral of the fleet that defeated the Spanish Armada (1588).

### The Circumnavigation (1577–1580)

```
DRAKE'S CIRCUMNAVIGATION — KEY EVENTS
========================================

  PURPOSE: Semi-official piracy in the Pacific, where Spanish ships
           were undefended (they expected attack only from the east).

  Departs Plymouth: December 13, 1577. 5 ships.

  SOUTH AMERICA COAST:
    Magellan Strait transit: August 1578.
    After the Strait: ships separated in a storm.
    2 ships lost or turned back.
    Discovery: after the Magellan Strait, the Pacific and Atlantic
    are open water — the "Drake Passage" (57–60°S).
    There is NO Terra Australis connecting South America to Antarctica.
    (Antarctica exists further south — not reached for 240 more years.)

  PACIFIC (Golden Hinde, sole remaining ship):
    Up the Pacific coast: Chile, Peru.
    Captures the Cacafuego: Spanish treasure galleon carrying silver.
    26 tons of silver, 80 lbs of gold, jewels.
    Drake continues north along California coast to ~48°N.

  CROSSING THE PACIFIC:
    Drake crosses via Moluccas (Spice Islands).
    Loads cloves. Alliance with Sultan of Ternate against Portuguese.
    Across Indian Ocean, Cape of Good Hope.

  RETURNS PLYMOUTH: September 26, 1580. 3 years in Pacific.

  Queen Elizabeth boards the Golden Hinde and knights Drake.
  The cargo was worth approximately £150,000 — more than twice the
  annual government revenue. Spain demanded return of stolen goods.
  Elizabeth knighted Drake instead.
```

## George Anson (1697–1762)

### Bio Snapshot

British admiral. His circumnavigation (1740–1744) was not a voyage of discovery but a military operation that became a survival exercise of historic scale.

### The Survival Circumnavigation (1740–1744)

```
ANSON'S VOYAGE — THE NUMBERS
================================

  DEPARTURE: September 1740. 6 warships. ~1,900 men.

  THE LOSSES:
    Most deaths: scurvy and tropical fever (dysentery, typhoid).
    By the time Anson rounded Cape Horn: 3 of 6 ships lost.
    Cape Horn crossing: July–August (southern winter).
    Storms. Many men swept overboard. Scurvy rampant.

    By the time they reached Juan Fernández Island (Pacific):
    ~300 men had died. Ships in desperate condition.

  THE PACIFIC CAMPAIGN:
    Took Centurion (his flagship) to the Philippines.
    1743: captured the Manila Galleon Nuestra Señora de Covadonga.
    Bullion: worth ~£400,000. Enormous.

  RETURN:
    Reached England June 15, 1744.
    188 men survivors. Out of 1,900.
    ~1,712 deaths — the highest death toll of any British circumnavigation.

  WHAT IT PROVED:
    Scurvy was destroying the Royal Navy's operational effectiveness.
    Anson was explicit: properly provisioned ships would have lost
    far fewer men.

  CONSEQUENCE:
    James Lind's scurvy experiment (1747): lemon/lime juice as treatment.
    Lind proved it, but the Navy was slow to adopt it.
    1795: Naval surgeon Gilbert Blane convinced the Navy to require
    lemon juice on all ships.
    Within a decade: scurvy essentially eliminated from the British Navy.
    Anson's catastrophic losses helped motivate the reform.
```

## Louis Antoine de Bougainville (1729–1811)

### Bio Snapshot

French naval officer, mathematician, and explorer. Paris. Served in the French and Indian War (Canada). First French circumnavigation (1766–1769). Later fought in the American Revolution on the French side. Retired as a senator. The bougainvillea plant is named for him.

### The First French Circumnavigation (1766–1769)

```
BOUGAINVILLE'S VOYAGE — SIGNIFICANCE
=======================================

  Purpose: Scientific and diplomatic. Not plunder.
  Fleet: 2 ships. 330 men.
  Route: Standard route — Atlantic, Cape Horn, Pacific, Indian Ocean, Cape.
  Time: 1766–1769.

  KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  TAHITI (April 1768):
    9-day stop.
    Described as an earthly paradise.
    The "noble savage" idea (Rousseau) seemed embodied.
    Bougainville described Tahitian society favorably, if somewhat
    through a romanticized lens.
    One crew member discovered to be a woman (Jeanne Baré —
    she was Philibert Commerson's assistant, disguised as a man
    to circumvent the ban on women on French naval vessels).
    Jeanne Baré is the first woman to complete a circumnavigation.

  CORAL REEFS (Pacific):
    Bougainville nearly wrecked on the Great Barrier Reef
    (1 month before Cook did the same — Cook wrecked; Bougainville
    turned back when depth soundings alarmed him).

  NATURALIST: Philibert Commerson
    Collected 3,000 plant specimens.
    Described bougainvillea (the plant).
    Described Pacific flora systematically.

  SCIENTIFIC CONTRIBUTION:
    First comprehensive French geographic account of the Pacific.
    Tahiti descriptions sparked Enlightenment debates about
    natural society vs. civilization.
    Diderot's "Supplement to Bougainville's Voyage" (1772):
    used Tahiti as a thought experiment about natural law.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Magellan circumnavigated the globe."**
Magellan died in the Philippines. Elcano commanded the return. Only 18 of the original ~270 men completed the circumnavigation. The voyage is usually called the "Magellan-Elcano expedition."

**"Drake's voyage was exploration."**
Primarily piracy with strategic goals. Drake looted Spanish ships and ports on the Pacific coast (which were undefended because the Spanish only expected attack from the east). Elizabeth knighted him; Spain called him "El Draque" (the Dragon). The geographic knowledge was a byproduct.

**"Anson's voyage was a failure."**
As a military operation, partially. As a catalyst for naval reform: highly significant. The systematic provisioning reforms that followed Anson's losses (and Lind's 1747 experiments) ultimately eliminated scurvy from the British Navy — which contributed to British naval dominance in the late 18th and 19th centuries.
