# Scientific Expeditions — Humboldt, Darwin, Wallace, Challenger

## Era Overview

```
SCIENCE AT THE FRONTIER: 1799–1876
=====================================

  CONTEXT:
    By 1800, most coastlines were mapped.
    The interiors of South America, Africa, Asia, the Pacific islands
    were still poorly known to European science.
    Natural history: Linnaeus's taxonomy (1735–1753) created a framework.
    What was needed: specimen collection, measurement, systematic observation.

  KEY FIGURE — JOSEPH BANKS (1743–1820):
    Sailed with Cook on first voyage.
    Collected 30,000 plant specimens, 1,000 new species.
    President of the Royal Society for 42 years.
    Institutionalized the naturalist-on-expedition model.

  1799–1804 ─── HUMBOLDT: Americas. 6,000+ species collected.
               Elevation-temperature-vegetation maps. Climate science begun.

  1831–36 ─── DARWIN: Beagle voyage. Galapagos. Origin of evolution by natural selection.

  1854–62 ─── WALLACE: Malay Archipelago. 126,000 specimens.
              Independently derived evolution by natural selection.
              The letter to Darwin that forced *On the Origin of Species*.

  1872–76 ─── HMS CHALLENGER: First systematic ocean survey.
              Ocean depth, chemistry, biology. Oceanography as a discipline.
```

## Alexander von Humboldt (1769–1859)

### Bio Snapshot

Prussian naturalist. Berlin, Freiberg (geology). Independently wealthy — spent his entire fortune on science. Latin American expedition (1799–1804): ~6,000+ species new to European science, 60,000 plant specimens. Wrote 30+ volumes of results (*Voyage to the Equinoctial Regions*, *Personal Narrative*, *Kosmos*). Died at 89 still working on *Kosmos*.

### The American Journey (1799–1804)

```
HUMBOLDT'S EXPEDITION — SCOPE
================================

  PARTNERS: Aimé Bonpland (botanist).
  ROUTE:
    Venezuela: Orinoco River. Discovered the Casiquiare Canal —
    a natural waterway connecting the Orinoco and Amazon systems.
    Cuba (twice).
    Colombia, Ecuador, Peru: Andes traversal.
    Mexico.
    Washington DC (met Jefferson).
    Returned to Europe via Philadelphia.

  MEASUREMENTS:
    Brought 42 scientific instruments.
    Barometric altitude measurements → first accurate elevations of Andes.
    Magnetic inclination measurements across the hemisphere.
    Temperature at altitude. Ocean temperature. Air chemistry.
    Earthquake activity. Volcanic phenomena.

  CHIMBORAZO (June 23, 1802):
    Attempted to climb Chimborazo (then believed highest mountain in world).
    Reached ~5,878m. Altitude record for ~30 years.
    Observed: vegetation changes with altitude.
    Drew the famous "Naturgemälde" (nature painting):
    a cross-section of Chimborazo showing how plants change with
    elevation — the same as they change with latitude.
    The same species at 4,000m near the equator as at sea level near the poles.
    This was the first map of ecological zones.
    The founding insight of biogeography and ecology.
```

**Humboldt's lasting contributions**:

```
HUMBOLDT'S INTELLECTUAL LEGACY
================================

  INTERCONNECTEDNESS:
    Humboldt insisted on seeing nature as a whole, not parts.
    Temperature, altitude, vegetation, animals, humans — all connected.
    Deforestation changes climate (he observed this in Venezuela — 1800!).
    The founder of ecology as a way of thinking.

  BIOGEOGRAPHY:
    Why do similar climates have similar plants, even on different continents?
    Temperature zones → vegetation zones. Altitude equivalent to latitude.
    This framework is the basis of modern biogeography.

  CLIMATE SCIENCE:
    First systematic temperature mapping across latitudes.
    Isotherm maps (lines of equal temperature).
    Recognized that human activities (deforestation, irrigation) alter climate.
    His 1843 essay: human-caused climate change as a concern.

  INFLUENCE:
    Darwin: "My admiration of his famous personal narrative partly
    determined my career." Darwin read Humboldt on the Beagle.
    Thoreau, Emerson, George Perkins Marsh — all read Humboldt.
    The US national park movement traces partly to Humboldt's ideas.
```

## Charles Darwin (1809–1882)

### Bio Snapshot

English naturalist. Cambridge (theology, but passionate about natural history). Joined the Beagle voyage aged 22 as companion to Captain Robert FitzRoy (technically an unpaid gentleman naturalist, not the official ship's naturalist). Five years at sea. Returned 1836. Published *On the Origin of Species* 1859, 23 years after returning. Died 1882.

### The Beagle Voyage (1831–1836)

```
THE BEAGLE VOYAGE — DARWIN'S KEY OBSERVATIONS
===============================================

  PURPOSE: Hydrographic survey of South America's coasts.
  Darwin's role: companion to FitzRoy; natural history observation.

  SOUTH AMERICA:
    Patagonia: fossil mammals — giant extinct relatives of living animals.
    (Giant ground sloth, glyptodont — extinct but related to living armadillos.)
    Why would extinct animals in one region be related to living ones there?

    Brazil: tropical rainforest richness. More species than Darwin had imagined.

    Chile: felt an earthquake. Observed marine fossils in the Andes at elevation.
    (Geological evidence of uplift — the land had risen.)

  GALAPAGOS ISLANDS (September–October 1835):
    5 weeks. 13+ islands.
    Darwin collected finches, mockingbirds, tortoises.
    Not the eureka moment of legend.
    (He didn't even label which island each finch was from initially.)

    In retrospect (after he got home):
    The mockingbirds differed by island. The tortoises: different islands,
    different shell shapes.
    John Gould (ornithologist) identified Darwin's "wrens" as 13 distinct
    finch species, all seemingly derived from one South American ancestor.

  THE INSIGHT (developed 1837–1838):
    Species are not fixed. They change.
    Animals that are separated geographically diverge over time.
    The mechanism: differential survival ("natural selection").
    Individuals that are better suited to their environment survive and reproduce.
    Traits that aid survival become more common.
    Over many generations: a new species.

  WHY DARWIN WAITED 20 YEARS:
    He wrote up the theory in 1844 but did not publish.
    The social and religious implications were explosive.
    He worked on barnacles for 8 years (1846–1854) — establishing his
    credibility as a naturalist before risking the theory.
    Wallace's 1858 letter: forced his hand.
```

**The Wallace catalyst**: In 1858, Darwin received a letter from Alfred Russel Wallace, working in the Malay Archipelago, that described — almost identically — the theory of natural selection. Darwin was devastated. His friends Charles Lyell and Joseph Hooker arranged a joint presentation at the Linnean Society (July 1, 1858). Darwin then wrote *On the Origin of Species* in 13 months.

## Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913)

### Bio Snapshot

Welsh naturalist. Self-educated. No university degree. Worked as a land surveyor and schoolteacher. First collecting expedition: Amazon (1848–52) — lost 4 years of collections when his ship caught fire. Second expedition: Malay Archipelago (1854–62) — 126,000 specimens including 5,000 beetles new to science, 212 new species of bird.

### The Malay Archipelago and Evolution

```
WALLACE'S CONTRIBUTIONS
=========================

  THE WALLACE LINE (1859):
    A biogeographical boundary running between Bali and Lombok,
    and between Borneo and Sulawesi.
    West of the line: Asian fauna (tigers, orangutans, elephants).
    East of the line: Australian fauna (marsupials, cockatoos, birds of paradise).
    The line coincides with a deep ocean trench — a former sea barrier
    that separated the Asian and Australian tectonic plates.
    Wallace noticed the abrupt faunal change before plate tectonics.
    The Wallace Line is still used as a biogeographic boundary.

  NATURAL SELECTION — PARALLEL DISCOVERY:
    February 1858: Wallace, ill with malaria on Ternate Island.
    In 2 hours: derives natural selection independently.
    "The idea came to me as it had to Darwin."
    Wrote it up. Mailed to Darwin (the letter took weeks to arrive).
    Darwin recognized his own theory — written by someone else.

  INDEPENDENT DERIVATION:
    Darwin and Wallace drew on the same source — Malthus's *Essay on the
    Principle of Population* (1798) — independently. Both recognized that
    food supply growth is arithmetic; population growth is geometric;
    therefore competition is inevitable; therefore differential survival.

  DIFFERENCES FROM DARWIN:
    Wallace did not apply natural selection to human intelligence — he
    believed some higher power must have guided human mental evolution.
    (Darwin disagreed.) Otherwise the theories are essentially identical.
    Wallace was also an early advocate for land nationalization
    and skeptical of capitalism — influenced by his working-class origins.
```

## HMS Challenger Expedition (1872–1876)

### Background and Scope

The Challenger expedition was the first systematic oceanographic survey — the founding voyage of oceanography as a science.

```
HMS CHALLENGER — THE MISSION
================================

  Ship: HMS Challenger (converted Royal Navy corvette).
       6 scientists, 23 officers, 216 sailors.
  Duration: December 21, 1872 – May 24, 1876.
  Distance: 68,890 nautical miles.
  Stations: 362 sampling stations.
  Deepest sounding: 8,185 m (Challenger Deep, Mariana Trench).
  Named: the deepest point on Earth is the "Challenger Deep."

  INSTRUMENTS AND METHODS:
    Deep-sea dredging: buckets lowered to collect sediment and organisms.
    Serial temperature measurements at depth.
    Chemical water samples at different depths.
    Sound-based depth sounding (piano wire lowered until it reached bottom).

  WHAT WAS FOUND:
    The deep ocean is not a sterile abyss.
    ~4,700 new species discovered.
    Deep sea floor covered in sediment (mostly ooze from dead organisms).
    Manganese nodules on the sea floor.
    Temperature: deep ocean water is cold (2–4°C) regardless of surface temperature.
    The major ocean basins have distinct depths and topography.
    The Atlantic has a central ridge (the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — though
    its volcanic significance was not understood until 1960s plate tectonics).

  REPORTS:
    50 volumes of scientific reports. Published 1880–1895.
    Took 20+ years to process all the data.
    The Challenger reports are the founding documents of oceanography.
```

**Key scientists on board**: Charles Wyville Thomson (expedition leader, died 1882 before reports were complete). John Murray took over, produced the full 50-volume report.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Darwin discovered evolution."**
Darwin (and Wallace) proposed natural selection as the mechanism. The concept that species change over time (evolution, descent with modification) had other proponents: Jean-Baptiste Lamarck (1809), Erasmus Darwin (Charles's grandfather), Robert Chambers (*Vestiges*, 1844). Darwin's contribution: the mechanism (natural selection) and the mountain of evidence.

**"Wallace was robbed of credit."**
The Linnean Society presentation gave both Darwin and Wallace joint credit. Wallace himself was satisfied with the arrangement and continued to call the theory "Darwinism." He outlived Darwin by 31 years and used that time to advance the theory further. The historical asymmetry is partly because Darwin published *On the Origin of Species* as a full book; Wallace published shorter papers.

**"Humboldt was primarily a traveler."**
He was a systematic scientist who happened to do his fieldwork by traveling. The 30+ volumes he produced were mathematical, chemical, and biological analyses of his data. *Kosmos* (5 volumes, 1845–1862) was an attempt to synthesize all of natural science into a unified vision of the universe. Andrea Wulf's *The Invention of Nature* (2015) makes the argument that Humboldt is the most important scientific figure that most people have never heard of.
