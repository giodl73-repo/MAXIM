# Polar Exploration — Franklin, Nansen, Amundsen, Scott, Shackleton

## Era Overview

```
POLAR EXPLORATION: 1845–1922
==============================

  THE QUESTIONS:
    Is there a Northwest Passage (Arctic route from Atlantic to Pacific)?
    Is there land at the North Pole or just ice?
    Is there land at the South Pole?
    Who gets there first?

  1818 ─── British Navy resumes Arctic exploration post-Napoleonic Wars.
  1845 ─── FRANKLIN: Best-equipped expedition ever sent to find NW Passage.
           Both ships lost. 129 men. Worst Arctic disaster.

  1850s–60s: Multiple rescue expeditions looking for Franklin.
            Map the Canadian Arctic as a byproduct.

  1875–76 ─ NARES: British Arctic expedition. Gets within 350 miles of North Pole.
  1893–96 ─ NANSEN: Fram — deliberately frozen in ice to drift across Arctic Ocean.
           Proven: no land at North Pole. Just ice sheet.
  1903–06 ─ AMUNDSEN: First transit of Northwest Passage.
  1907–09 ─ PEARY/COOK: Disputed North Pole claims.
  1910–12 ─ AMUNDSEN: First South Pole (December 14, 1911).
           SCOTT: South Pole second (January 17, 1912). All die returning.
  1914–16 ─ SHACKLETON: Endurance expedition. Ship crushed in ice.
           All 28 men survive. Greatest survival story in exploration.
  1922 ─── SHACKLETON dies of heart attack on Quest expedition.
```

## John Franklin (1786–1847)

### Bio Snapshot

British Royal Navy officer. Veteran of the Battle of Trafalgar. Two previous Arctic expeditions (1819–22, 1825–27) which were partly disastrous. Governor of Tasmania (Van Diemen's Land) 1836–43. Sent to find the Northwest Passage in 1845 at age 59.

### The Lost Expedition (1845)

```
FRANKLIN'S EXPEDITION — PREPARATION AND DISAPPEARANCE
=======================================================

  SHIPS: HMS Erebus and HMS Terror.
  MEN: 129.
  PROVISIONS: 3 years of food. Lead-soldered tinned food (a problem).
  EQUIPMENT: Steam engines for ice-breaking. First ships to have them.
             Library of 1,000+ books. Photographic equipment.
             Barrel organ. A hand organ. Daguerre apparatus.
             Heating system for crew comfort.

  WHY IT SHOULD HAVE WORKED:
    Best-equipped expedition ever sent to the Arctic.
    Modern ships. Experienced crew. Adequate provisions (or so it seemed).
    Franklin had Arctic experience.

  TIMELINE:
    May 1845: Departs Thames.
    July 1845: Last sighted by whalers in Baffin Bay.
    Summer 1845–Winter 1846: Wintered at Beechey Island.
               3 men buried there (found 1850, bodies exhumed 1984).
    Summer 1846: Moved south through Peel Sound.
    September 12, 1846: Ships frozen in ice north of King William Island.
    June 11, 1847: Franklin dies (cause unknown).
    April 22, 1848: Survivors abandon ships. 105 men attempt to walk south.
               Note in cairn: last survivors headed to mainland.
    All 105 die. From exposure, starvation, illness, scurvy.

  DISCOVERED:
    1850: Beechey Island graves (by one of 14 search expeditions).
    1859: Francis Leopold McClintock finds the note at Victory Point cairn.
    1984: Bodies at Beechey Island exhumed. Lead poisoning found.
          Lead from tinned food solder may have contributed to cognitive
          impairment (poor decision-making) during the ordeal.
    2014: HMS Erebus discovered by Canadian underwater archaeology.
    2016: HMS Terror discovered — in better condition, largely intact.

  WHY IT FAILED:
    Combination of: bad ice year (1846 was unusually icy),
    lead poisoning from tin cans, scurvy (lime juice inadequate),
    and decisions that led men away from sources of help.
    Local Inuit observed dying Europeans but were not sought for help.
```

## Fridtjof Nansen (1861–1930)

### Bio Snapshot

Norwegian scientist, explorer, and diplomat. Oslo. Zoologist by training. First person to ski across Greenland (1888). Designed the Fram — the most famous polar ship ever built. Won Nobel Peace Prize in 1922 for refugee work post-WWI.

### The Fram Expedition (1893–1896)

**Nansen's insight**: Previous attempts to reach the North Pole by ship or sledge failed because Arctic sea ice drifted against explorers. Nansen proposed going *with* the ice — deliberately freezing the ship into the pack ice and letting it drift across the Arctic Ocean.

```
THE FRAM DRIFT
================

  SHIP DESIGN:
    Specially designed to resist ice pressure.
    Hull: rounded shape (ice pushes the hull UP, not in).
    Timber: 70–80 cm thick in places.
    Interior bracing: an elaborate internal framework.

  PLAN:
    Freeze the Fram in the Siberian ice (northern Siberian coast).
    Let it drift naturally across the Arctic Ocean.
    Reach the North Pole region. Continue to Greenland.

  ACTUAL DRIFT (September 1893 – August 1896):
    Froze in ice north of Siberia: September 1893.
    Drifted: slowly westward across the Arctic Ocean.
    Maximum latitude of ship: 85°57'N — new Furthest North record.

  NANSEN'S SKI DASH (March–August 1896):
    The drift would not pass close enough to Pole.
    Nansen and Hjalmar Johansen left the Fram on skis and dogsleds.
    Reached 86°13.6'N — new Furthest North record (April 8, 1895).
    Could not go further. Turned back.
    Survived on walrus and bear meat. Wintered in makeshift stone hut.
    August 1896: encountered British expedition by chance in Franz Josef Land.

  WHAT THE DRIFT PROVED:
    No land at the North Pole — just drifting sea ice.
    Confirmed oceanic circulation patterns of the Arctic.
    The Siberian water does reach the Atlantic via Arctic.

  Fram returned safely: August 20, 1896. All hands well.
  First expedition to bring all crew back after 3 years in the Arctic.
```

## Roald Amundsen (1872–1928)

### Bio Snapshot

Norwegian explorer. Studied medicine briefly. Became first-mate on a Belgian Antarctic expedition (1897–99) — took command when captain became incapacitated. First Northwest Passage (1903–06). First South Pole (1911). Also first Northeast Passage (1918–20). Disappeared 1928 in the Arctic while searching for survivors of a dirigible crash.

### First Northwest Passage (1903–1906)

```
NORTHWEST PASSAGE TRANSIT
===========================

  Previous attempts: all failed or abandoned.
  Franklin: 129 men dead.

  Amundsen's approach:
    Small ship (Gjøa, 47 tons — tiny by exploration standards).
    Small crew (6 men).
    3 years of supplies.
    Wintered at King William Island (Gjøahavn): 22 months.
    Learned from local Netsilik Inuit: clothing, sleds, dogs.
    This is the key difference. Amundsen spent time learning from Arctic peoples.

  Completed the passage: August 1905. Reached Nome, Alaska.
  The passage took 3 years — too long for commercial use.
  Commercial transit became feasible only with nuclear icebreakers (1960s)
  and is becoming more accessible with Arctic sea ice decline.
```

### The Race to the South Pole (1911–1912)

```
AMUNDSEN vs. SCOTT — THE CONTRAST
=====================================

  AMUNDSEN (Norwegian):
    52 men, 19 sledges, 97 sled dogs.
    Skis. Dogs pull the loads. Men ski.
    Depot system: food caches pre-placed on the route.
    Dressed in skins (Inuit-style). Warm, flexible, breathable.
    Route: Ross Ice Shelf → Axel Heiberg Glacier (new route) → Plateau.

  SCOTT (British):
    65 men, motor sledges (broke down immediately), ponies, dogs.
    Motor sledges: failed in cold. Ponies: wrong animal for Antarctic.
    Dogs: used but not optimally. Men pulled sledges (man-hauling).
    Dressed in wool. Got wet. Lost insulation value.
    Route: Ross Ice Shelf → Beardmore Glacier (Shackleton's route).

  TIMELINE:
    Amundsen departs: October 19, 1911.
    Scott departs: November 1, 1911.

    Amundsen at South Pole: December 14, 1911.
    Team: Amundsen, Bjaaland, Hassel, Wisting, Hanssen.
    All 5 return safely.

    Scott at South Pole: January 17, 1912.
    Finds Amundsen's tent and Norwegian flag.
    "Great God! This is an awful place and terrible enough for us
    to have laboured to it without the reward of priority."
    5 men return north.
    Edgar Evans: died February 17 (collapse, probable brain hemorrhage).
    Lawrence Oates: walked out of the tent March 17 in a blizzard.
    "I am just going outside and may be some time."
    (He was dying of frostbite and gangrene; did not want to slow the others.)
    Scott, Wilson, Bowers: died in their tent ~March 29.
    Found by search party November 12, 1912. 11 miles from a depot.

  AMUNDSEN'S ADVANTAGES:
    Dogs. Skis. Inuit-style clothing.
    Lighter equipment choices.
    Better planning of depots.
    Earlier start.
    Frankly, better prepared.
```

## Robert Falcon Scott (1868–1912)

### Bio Snapshot

British Royal Navy officer. Two Antarctic expeditions (Discovery 1901–04, Terra Nova 1910–12). Second to the South Pole. Died with his entire returning party 11 miles from a food depot. His diaries were found with the bodies and published. He became a British hero; the framing of noble failure in the face of impossible odds shaped his legend for decades.

**The reassessment**: The modern view of Scott vs. Amundsen is that Amundsen was simply better prepared and more rational in his approach. Scott's man-hauling fetish, inadequate nutrition planning, and motor sledge dependence were genuine errors. Scott was a hero; he was also less well-prepared than Amundsen.

## Ernest Shackleton (1874–1922)

### Bio Snapshot

Anglo-Irish explorer. Served under Scott on the Discovery expedition (1901–04). Led three expeditions to Antarctica (Nimrod 1907–09, Endurance 1914–16, Quest 1921–22). The Endurance expedition is considered the greatest survival story in exploration. Died of a heart attack at 47 on the Quest expedition.

### The Endurance Expedition (1914–1916)

```
THE ENDURANCE — EVENTS
========================

  GOAL: First land crossing of Antarctica (Atlantic to Pacific).

  Ship: Endurance. December 1914: enters Weddell Sea.
  January 18, 1915: STUCK IN ICE.
  September 1915: Ice pressure crushing the hull.
  October 27, 1915: ABANDON SHIP. 28 men onto the ice.
  November 21, 1915: Endurance sinks.

  28 MEN ON THE ICE:
    They camp on the ice floe for months, drifting north.
    April 9, 1916: Ice breaks up. Load 3 small lifeboats.
    April 15: Land at Elephant Island. First land in 497 days.

  THE JAMES CAIRD VOYAGE:
    Elephant Island is uninhabited and unreachable by rescuers.
    Shackleton takes 5 men in the 22-foot James Caird.
    800 miles to South Georgia Island across the Drake Passage.
    Worst stretch of ocean in the world. Southern Ocean storms.
    16 days. Reached South Georgia: May 10, 1916.
    Wrong side of the island. Whalers on the other side.

  SOUTH GEORGIA CROSSING:
    Shackleton, Worsley, Crean: crossed the unmapped interior.
    Three men, 36 hours, no map, no shelter.
    Mountains and glaciers. No climbing equipment.
    Reached the whaling station: May 20, 1916.

  RESCUE:
    Four attempts to reach Elephant Island (sea ice blocked).
    August 30, 1916: Shackleton reaches Elephant Island.
    All 22 men still alive. 22 months since departure from England.
    NOT ONE MAN LOST.
```

**What made Shackleton's leadership exceptional**: The men were stranded for over 2 years with no certainty of rescue. There was ample reason for despair, mutiny, or violence. There was none. Shackleton:
- Kept routine and purpose (scheduled activities, regular meals)
- Maintained morale through humor and attention to individuals
- Made decisions that consistently prioritized the weakest members
- Led by example in physical hardship
- The James Caird voyage: he could have sent someone else.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Scott was unprepared because he was incompetent."**
Scott was competent as a naval officer. His Antarctic decisions reflect the British culture of the time — man-hauling was considered more noble than dog use. His nutrition planning was inferior to Amundsen's. He made genuine errors. He was not incompetent; he was outclassed by a better-prepared competitor.

**"Shackleton failed."**
His goal (first Antarctic crossing) was not achieved. His survival leadership was a success by any measure — 28 men, 22 months, 0 deaths. The distinction matters.

**"The Northwest Passage is now commercially viable."**
Annually navigable, without icebreaker, since roughly 2007 (first season possible). Commercial transit remains challenging (insurance, thin window, diversion from major shipping lanes). Arctic sea ice decline is making it more accessible each decade.
