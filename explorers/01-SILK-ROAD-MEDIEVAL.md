# Silk Road and Medieval Explorers — Marco Polo, Ibn Battuta, Zheng He, Rabban Sauma

## Era Overview

```
MEDIEVAL LONG-DISTANCE TRAVEL: 1200–1450
==========================================

  The world in 1200:
    Mongol Empire expanding across Eurasia.
    Islam stretches from Spain to Indonesia.
    China: Song dynasty, highest civilization level.
    Europe: fragmented kingdoms, limited geographic knowledge.
    Trade routes: Silk Road (overland) + Indian Ocean (maritime).

  Travel conditions:
    Overland: camels, horses, on foot. 30–50 km/day.
    Sea: monsoon-dependent. Indian Ocean: known and traveled.
    Safety: Mongol Empire, at its height, enabled relatively safe travel
    across Eurasia (Pax Mongolica, 1241–1350).

  MAJOR TRAVELERS:
  1254 ─── Marco Polo born, Venice.
  1271 ─── MARCO POLO: departs Venice with father and uncle.
  1275 ─── Arrives at Kublai Khan's court, Shangdu.
  1292 ─── Marco Polo departs China by sea.
  1295 ─── Returns to Venice. 24 years away.

  1304 ─── Ibn Battuta born, Tangier, Morocco.
  1325 ─── IBN BATTUTA departs Tangier for hajj.
           First step of a 29-year journey.
  1354 ─── Returns to Morocco. ~120,000 km traveled.

  1371 ─── ZHENG HE born, Yunnan Province, China.
  1405 ─── First of seven voyages. 317 ships. 28,000 men.
  1433 ─── Last voyage. Zheng He dies on return.
  1435 ─── China prohibits overseas voyages.

  1220 ─── RABBAN SAUMA born, near Beijing.
  1278 ─── Sauma and Markos depart China for Jerusalem.
  1287 ─── Sauma in Rome; meets Pope Nicholas IV.
  1294 ─── Sauma dies, Baghdad.
```

## Marco Polo (1254–1324)

### Bio Snapshot

Venetian merchant. His father Niccolò and uncle Maffeo had already traveled to China (1260–1269) and met Kublai Khan. Marco joined their return journey in 1271 at age 17. He served Kublai Khan in administrative roles for 17 years. Returned 1295. Dictated his account in prison in Genoa (captured during a war) to romance writer Rustichello of Pisa.

### The Journey

```
MARCO POLO'S ROUTE (1271–1295)
================================

  OUTWARD (1271–1275):
    Venice → Acre (Palestine)
    → Persia (Hormuz — briefly, then inland)
    → Afghanistan (Badakhshan — 1 year sick/recovering)
    → Pamirs (the "Roof of the World")
    → Tarim Basin (Kashgar, Khotan, Dunhuang)
    → Gobi Desert
    → Kublai Khan's summer court at Shangdu (Xanadu)
    Time: ~3.5 years. 15,000 km.

  CHINA YEARS (1275–1292):
    Marco in Kublai's service. Titles unclear.
    Possibly governor of Yangzhou (his claim is disputed).
    Traveled widely: Yunnan, Bengal, Cochin China, possibly Burma.
    Witnessed: paper money, coal, the Grand Canal, postal relay system,
               salt industry, silk production at scale.

  RETURN (1292–1295):
    By sea. Escorting a Mongol princess to Persia for marriage.
    14 ships, 600+ passengers. 2 years at sea.
    Indonesia, Malabar, Oman, Hormuz, then overland to Venice.
    Deaths: ~600 of 600+ passengers (not the nobles or Polos).
    Disease, shipwreck.

  TIME AWAY: ~24 years.
```

**What Polo described**: Paper money (unknown in Europe). Coal ("black stones that burn"). Enormous Chinese cities (Hangzhou — "City of Heaven" — larger than any European city). The postal relay system. Gunpowder used in warfare. The scale of Chinese civilization was simply incomprehensible to medieval Europeans.

**The reliability problem**: Many of Polo's claims cannot be verified. He doesn't mention: foot binding, the Great Wall, tea, Chinese writing, chopsticks. These omissions have led some historians to argue he may have compiled his account from other sources rather than personal observation. The weight of scholarly opinion is that he did travel to China, but his account mixes direct observation with hearsay.

**Impact**: Columbus carried a copy of Polo's book on his 1492 voyage. The book maintained European interest in direct trade routes to Asia — the motivation behind the Age of Exploration.

## Ibn Battuta (1304–1368/9)

### Bio Snapshot

Moroccan scholar and Berber. Born in Tangier. Made the hajj to Mecca in 1325 — and kept going. 29 years of travel. No single continuous journey — he returned to Mecca multiple times, had jobs and wives along the way. His account (*Rihla* — "Journey") was dictated to scholar Ibn Juzayy in 1355 at the order of the Sultan of Morocco.

### The Scale

```
IBN BATTUTA'S GEOGRAPHY (1325–1354)
======================================

  Regions visited:
    North Africa (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Libya, Egypt)
    Middle East (Arabia, Iraq, Persia, Syria, Palestine)
    East Africa (Mogadishu, Mombasa, Kilwa, Zanzibar — 1330s)
    Central Asia (through the Golden Horde to Crimea, Constantinople)
    South Asia (Afghanistan, India, Maldives, Sri Lanka, Bengal)
    Southeast Asia (Sumatra, Malaya, possibly Cambodia/Vietnam)
    China (Quanzhou, Hangzhou, Beijing — possibly; disputed)
    Anatolia (Turkey, multiple visits)
    West Africa (Mali Empire, 1352)

  Total: ~44 countries by modern boundaries.
         ~120,000 km (vs. Polo's ~40,000 km).

  WHY SO FAR:
    The Islamic world was connected.
    Arabic was a lingua franca from Morocco to Malaysia.
    Network of Muslim merchants, scholars, and rulers provided
    hospitality, employment, and letters of recommendation.
    Ibn Battuta used this network strategically.
    He served as a qadi (Islamic judge) in Delhi and the Maldives.
```

**Observations**:
- **Mali Empire** (1352–1353): Describes the wealth of Mansa Musa's successors. Mali's gold trade. The security and order of the empire. His account is among the primary sources for medieval West African history.
- **Black Death** (1347–1348): He was traveling through the Middle East during the Black Death pandemic. His account describes the mortality. He lost friends and associates to it.
- **Maldives**: Served as qadi (judge). Tried to impose Islamic strict legal standards; was expelled.
- **China**: His China account is disputed — he claims to have visited during a period when travel records don't confirm his presence, and his description is vague compared to Polo's detail.

**Comparison with Polo**:

```
IBN BATTUTA vs. MARCO POLO
============================

  Polo:      Italian merchant. Top-down view (served emperors).
             Left ~25 years after China, wrote from memory.
             Academic debate about veracity.

  Battuta:   Islamic scholar. Bottom-up view (used Islamic hospitality network).
             Dictated account in his 50s, relatively soon after travel.
             Rich detail on Islamic societies; China account thin.

  Both:      Single-source accounts without independent verification.
             Both shaped European/Islamic geographic understanding.
             Both described a world far more interconnected than Europeans assumed.
```

## Zheng He (1371–1433)

### Bio Snapshot

Chinese Muslim eunuch admiral. Born Ma He in Yunnan Province. Captured at 10 by Ming armies during conquest of Yunnan; castrated and made a court eunuch. Served Prince Zhu Di (later Emperor Yongle) loyally; promoted when Yongle seized the imperial throne. Given the name Zheng He and command of the treasure fleet.

### The Treasure Voyages

```
ZHENG HE'S SEVEN VOYAGES (1405–1433)
=======================================

  SCALE (first voyage, 1405–1407):
    317 ships. 28,000 men.
    "Treasure ships" (bao chuan): 120–130 meters long.
    (Comparison: Columbus's Santa María: ~18 meters. 90 men.)

  ROUTES:
    All seven voyages: Southeast Asia, Indian Ocean, Persian Gulf.
    Voyages 5–7: Arabian Peninsula, East Africa (Kenya/Tanzania coast).
    Reached: Java, Malacca, Ceylon, Calicut, Hormuz,
              Aden, Malindi, Mogadishu.
    DID NOT REACH: Europe, the Americas, circumnavigate Africa.

  PURPOSE:
    Tribute diplomacy: foreign rulers send tribute to acknowledge
    the supremacy of the Ming Emperor; China gives gifts in return.
    The gifts were often worth MORE than the tribute received.
    Economically irrational by mercantile logic; diplomatically valuable.

    Trade: Chinese silk, porcelain, copper cash.
    Return: spices, ivory, tropical woods, "exotic" animals
            (giraffes presented to the Emperor as "qilin" — unicorns).

  WHAT MADE THE SHIPS POSSIBLE:
    China's Grand Canal had developed the world's most advanced
    shipbuilding in the 14th–15th centuries.
    Watertight compartments (bulkheads) — not used in European ships
    until the 19th century.
    Stern-mounted rudders.
    Multiple masts with battened sails.
    Large enough to carry livestock, fresh water, and supplies for months.
```

**The sudden stop**: Emperor Yongle died (1424). Subsequent emperors were hostile to the voyages — Confucian officials argued they were wasteful, focused China outward when internal problems needed attention. The treasure ships were allowed to rot. In 1436 and 1449, overseas voyaging was explicitly prohibited. The shipbuilding expertise was lost within a generation.

**The counterfactual**: If China had continued the voyages, a Chinese admiral might have rounded Africa before da Gama (1498). The world's history would have been fundamentally different. Instead, the Ming dynasty turned inward precisely when Portugal was beginning systematic coastal exploration of Africa.

## Rabban Sauma (c. 1220–1294)

### Bio Snapshot

Nestorian Christian monk of Uyghur descent, born near Beijing. The reverse Marco Polo — traveled from China to Europe, not the other way. His account is less famous but equally significant as evidence of medieval transcontinental connectivity.

### The Journey West

```
RABBAN SAUMA'S JOURNEY (1278–1294)
=====================================

  DEPARTURE: Beijing (1278).
    Sauma and his disciple Markos set out to reach Jerusalem.
    Route: Central Asia → Persia.

  MARKOS: Elected Catholicos-Patriarch of the Church of the East
    (Pope-equivalent for Nestorian Christians) in 1281.
    Took the name Mar Yahballaha III. Stayed in Baghdad.

  DIPLOMATIC MISSION (1287):
    Sauma sent by Mongol Il-Khan Arghun to European courts.
    Purpose: propose a military alliance against the Mamluks.
    (Mongols wanted to retake Jerusalem; Christians wanted the same.)

  EUROPEAN VISITS:
    Constantinople → Rome → Genoa → Paris → Gascony
    Visited: Pope Nicholas IV, Philip IV of France,
             Edward I of England (Gascony).
    Met Pope Nicholas: communion, saw relics of St. Peter.
    Sauma held his own liturgy — the Pope was present.

  WHAT IT REVEALS:
    Nestorian Christianity was alive and well in Central Asia and China.
    The Mongol Empire was genuinely interested in Christian alliances.
    A man born near Beijing could travel to Rome and converse with the Pope.
    The medieval world was more globally connected than usually imagined.

  The alliance never materialized — different strategic timing.
  Sauma died in Baghdad (1294). His account survives in a Syriac manuscript.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Polo was the first European to visit China."**
Several European missionaries (Giovanni de Plano Carpini, 1245; William of Rubruck, 1253) preceded him to the Mongol court. Polo's account is the most detailed and widely read, but not the first contact.

**"Zheng He could have discovered America."**
Gavin Menzies' *1421* thesis claimed Chinese ships reached the Americas. This is not accepted by mainstream historians. The voyages are documented to East Africa and the Persian Gulf; no credible evidence of trans-Pacific voyaging has been found.

**"Ibn Battuta traveled continuously."**
He made multiple return trips to Mecca and spent extended periods in one place — serving as a qadi in Delhi for years, in the Maldives for 18 months. The 29-year journey was not a single loop but a series of journeys from Morocco as base.
