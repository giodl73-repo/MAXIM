# Age of Exploration — Columbus, da Gama, Cabot, Vespucci

## Era Overview

```
THE AGE OF EUROPEAN EXPLORATION: 1415–1522
============================================

  WHY NOW (1415)?
    Ottoman Turks control land routes to Asia (after 1453, Constant.).
    Portugal's position on the Atlantic coast — natural launch point.
    Advances in navigation: compass (from China via Arabs), astrolabe,
    portolan charts, lateen (triangular) sail (can sail closer to wind).
    Caravel: small, maneuverable, ocean-going. New ship design.
    Financial infrastructure: Genoa and Florentine banking.

  PORTUGAL LEADS (1415–1498):
    Prince Henry the Navigator (1394–1460) systematically funds
    expeditions down the African coast.
    Goal: reach India by sea. Bypass Islamic intermediaries.
    1488: DIAS rounds Cape of Good Hope.
    1498: DA GAMA reaches Calicut, India.

  SPAIN FOLLOWS (1492–1520):
    Columbus proposes western route to Asia (1492).
    Lands in Caribbean. Never realizes it's not Asia.
    1499: VESPUCCI explores South American coast.
    1507: "America" named after Vespucci (on Waldseemüller map).

  ENGLAND:
    1497: CABOT reaches Newfoundland. Claims for England.
    Foundation for later English colonization.

  PORTUGAL vs. SPAIN:
    Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): divides world between Portugal and Spain.
    Pope Alexander VI drew the line.
    East of line: Portugal (Africa, India, Brazil).
    West of line: Spain (Americas).
    All other European powers ignored this.
```

## Vasco da Gama (1460–1524)

### Bio Snapshot

Portuguese nobleman and navigator. Commissioned by King Manuel I to complete what Bartolomeu Dias had proven possible — round Africa and reach India. Three voyages (1497–99, 1502–03, 1524). Died in India on his third voyage. His route opened a maritime trade monopoly that made Portugal temporarily the wealthiest country in Europe.

### The Cape Route (1497–1499)

```
VASCO DA GAMA'S FIRST VOYAGE (1497–1499)
==========================================

  FLEET:
    4 ships. São Gabriel (flagship), São Rafael, Berrio, supply ship.
    170 men. Took 20 years of Portuguese maritime development with him.

  ROUTE:
    Lisbon → Canary Islands → Cape Verde
    Then: Dias had hugged the coast. Da Gama: CROSSED THE ATLANTIC.
    Made a massive arc southwest into the open Atlantic,
    then southeast to the Cape. This exploited trade winds and
    avoided the doldrums and contrary currents near Africa's equatorial coast.
    About 4 months at sea without sighting land.
    This was the longest open-ocean voyage yet attempted.

  ROUNDING THE CAPE:
    Bartolomeu Dias (1488) had rounded the Cape of Good Hope.
    Da Gama rounded it again, continuing northeast.

  EAST AFRICA:
    Mozambique, Mombasa, Malindi.
    At Malindi: hired Ahmed ibn Majid, the most famous Arab navigator
    of his time. Ibn Majid guided the fleet to Calicut.
    The crossing: 23 days. 2,800 km. Arrived May 20, 1498.

  CALICUT:
    The Zamorin (ruler) received them coolly.
    The trade goods brought (cloth, honey, olive oil) were considered
    insultingly low-quality by the sophisticated Calicut traders.
    Da Gama loaded some spices and left with poor relations.

  RETURN:
    Adverse monsoon. 3 months crossing the Indian Ocean.
    Scurvy. Lost ~30 men (including his brother Paulo).
    Arrived Portugal July 1499.

  FINANCIAL RETURN:
    Spices carried: worth 60x the cost of the expedition.
    Even a partial cargo at this markup financed the entire program.
```

**The strategic consequence**: Portugal established a chain of trading posts (*feitorias*) and fortresses around the Indian Ocean. Afonso de Albuquerque captured Goa (1510), Malacca (1511), and Hormuz (1515). Portugal controlled the spice trade for most of the 16th century — until the Dutch East India Company (VOC, 1602) broke the monopoly.

**The humanitarian cost**: Da Gama's 1502 voyage included the burning of a ship carrying Muslim pilgrims — hundreds of men, women, and children. He locked them in and burned the ship rather than honor their surrender. This is documented in multiple sources. The Cape route opened trade; it also opened systematic maritime violence.

## Christopher Columbus (1451–1506)

### Bio Snapshot

Genoese-born. Sailed for Spain. Disputed birth date and early biography. Married into Portuguese nobility (1479). Spent years in Portugal and Spain pitching his "Enterprise of the Indies" — a western route to Asia — before Ferdinand and Isabella agreed to fund it (1492).

### The Enterprise of the Indies (1492)

```
COLUMBUS'S GEOGRAPHICAL ERROR
================================

  Columbus believed (incorrectly) that:
    The Earth's circumference was about 25,000 km (correct: 40,075 km).
    Asia extended much further east than it actually does.
    Japan (Cipangu) was ~7,000 km west of Europe.
    (Actual distance: ~20,000 km westward.)

  Columbus's estimate was WRONG. Most educated Europeans knew it.
  The Portuguese rejected his proposal partly because their
  geographers calculated the distance correctly — too far.
  Spain funded it partly because the cost was low if he was right.

  What saved him: the Americas were there to run into.

  WHY HE THOUGHT HE WAS IN ASIA:
    He needed Asia to be near → it was near → therefore Asia.
    He never updated this belief. He died believing he'd reached Asia.
    His fourth voyage (1502) explored the Central American coast
    looking for the strait to the Indian Ocean.
    He found no strait because there was no strait — wrong continent.
```

```
THE FOUR VOYAGES
=================

  1st (1492–93): San Salvador (Bahamas), Cuba, Hispaniola.
                 Established colony on Hispaniola.
                 Returned as hero with "Indians" and gold.

  2nd (1493–96): 17 ships, 1,500 men. Established permanent colony.
                 Explored Puerto Rico, Jamaica, Hispaniola.
                 As governor: brutal — had hands cut off for theft.
                 Later arrested and removed by Spanish Crown for governance failures.

  3rd (1498–1500): Reached mainland South America (Venezuela, Trinidad).
                   "What I believe to be the mainland of Asia."
                   Returned to Spain in chains — Columbus arrested as colonial governor.

  4th (1502–04): Central America coast. Looking for strait to Indian Ocean.
                 Two ships abandoned due to worm damage. Stranded on Jamaica.
                 Rescued after year. Final return to Spain. Died 1506.
```

**Columbus's legacy**: He opened sustained contact between Europe and the Americas. The consequences included the Columbian Exchange (food plants, animals, diseases flowing both ways), the demographic collapse of indigenous populations (up to 90% mortality from disease within a century), the transatlantic slave trade, and the economic transformation of Europe from American silver. Columbus himself received little financial benefit — his share of profits was disputed by the Spanish crown.

## John Cabot (c. 1450–c. 1499)

### Bio Snapshot

Genoese-born, Venetian citizen, English service. Real name Giovanni Caboto. Similar background to Columbus. Convinced Bristol merchants and Henry VII to fund an expedition after Columbus's news arrived. His 1497 voyage is the English claim to North America.

### The 1497 Voyage

```
CABOT'S 1497 VOYAGE
======================

  SHIP: Matthew. ~50 tons. 18 men.
  ROUTE: Bristol → Atlantic → landfall June 24, 1497.
  LOCATION: Newfoundland (almost certainly). "New Found Land."

  What Cabot saw:
    Dense forests to the shore.
    Shallow seas with enormous fish schools (Grand Banks).
    Evidence of habitation (no people encountered).

  What Cabot thought he'd found:
    The "land of the Grand Khan" — Asia.
    (Same error as Columbus.)

  SIGNIFICANCE:
    Henry VII paid him £10 (remarkably little).
    Cabot's claim became the basis for English sovereignty
    over North America — argued in 16th–17th century colonization.

  SECOND VOYAGE (1498):
    5 ships. Cabot probably died at sea or reached North America
    and disappeared. His fate is unknown. One ship returned.
    The voyage was a commercial failure.

  LEGACY:
    The Grand Banks fishery Cabot described became one of the
    richest fishing grounds ever exploited — supporting Basque,
    Portuguese, English, and French fishing fleets for centuries.
    The fishery collapse in the 1990s (Atlantic cod) ended what
    Cabot first documented.
```

## Amerigo Vespucci (1454–1512)

### Bio Snapshot

Florentine merchant and explorer. Worked for the Medici bank. Sailed on at least two transatlantic voyages (1499 for Spain, 1501–02 for Portugal). His account of the South American coast was widely circulated and translated. German cartographer Martin Waldseemüller used Vespucci's first name "Amerigo" on a 1507 world map for the new continent.

### The Continental Recognition

```
WHY "AMERICA" IS NAMED FOR VESPUCCI (AND NOT COLUMBUS)
=========================================================

  Columbus: Never acknowledged the New World as new.
            Died insisting he'd reached Asia.
            His accounts described "the Indies."

  Vespucci (1501–1502 voyage along South American coast):
    Wrote that this was a "Mundus Novus" — a New World.
    Explicitly argued the lands were not Asia but an unknown continent.
    His letter "Mundus Novus" (1503) was widely printed across Europe.
    Waldseemüller's 1507 map: labeled the new continent "America" after
    Amerigo Vespucci, because Vespucci had described it as new.

  VESPUCCI'S ACCURACY:
    He described latitude-by-longitude positions that matched South America.
    The coasts he described are identifiable.
    Some claims (4 voyages — possibly 2) are disputed.
    His observations of indigenous customs are detailed and useful.
    He correctly identified the Amazon as a major river system.

  THE IRONY:
    Columbus opened the contact but got Cuba and Hispaniola named for his
    patron queens, not for himself.
    Vespucci recognized what it was and got two continents named after him.
    Columbus gets the credit; Vespucci gets the name.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Columbus was brave to sail the unknown ocean."**
European sailors had been sailing the Atlantic for decades (Canaries, Azores, Madeira). The North Atlantic crossing to Iceland was known. Columbus's route (trade winds south, westward, back north on westerlies) was the innovation. The conceptual boldness was sailing into open water without known stopping points — but it was a calculated risk, not a shot in the dark.

**"Da Gama was the first European to reach India."**
By the Cape route, yes. The overland route to India was well-known — Christian missionaries, Crusade-era travelers, and merchants had reached India overland for centuries. Da Gama's achievement was the maritime route, which made large-volume trade possible without traversing the Ottoman-controlled Middle East.

**"The Spanish/Portuguese Treaty of Tordesillas actually worked."**
Portugal got Brazil (east of the line) — that's why Brazilians speak Portuguese. Spain got the rest of the Americas. But England, France, and the Netherlands simply ignored the treaty. The Dutch and English East India Companies competed directly with Portugal in the Indian Ocean within a century.
