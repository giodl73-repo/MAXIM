# Pacific Exploration — Cook, Vancouver, Tasman, La Perouse

## Era Overview

```
MAPPING THE PACIFIC: 1642–1795
================================

  The Pacific is the world's largest ocean.
  It covers 30% of Earth's surface. More than all land combined.

  1642 ─── TASMAN: New Zealand, Tasmania, Tonga, Fiji. First European.
  1741 ─── VITUS BERING (Danish, Russian service): Alaska coast. Bering Sea.
  1766 ─── WALLIS: Tahiti. Claimants for Britain.
  1766–69 ─ BOUGAINVILLE: Tahiti, French account.
  1768–71 ─ COOK: First voyage. New Zealand circumnavigation. East Australia coast.
  1772–75 ─ COOK: Second voyage. Antarctic Circle crossed. No Terra Australis.
  1776–79 ─ COOK: Third voyage. Pacific Northwest. Death in Hawaii.
  1785–88 ─ LA PEROUSE: French equivalent of Cook. Lost without trace.
  1791–95 ─ VANCOUVER: Pacific Northwest coast survey. Precise mapping.
```

## James Cook (1728–1779)

### Bio Snapshot

English navigator. Yorkshire farm laborer's son. Merchant marine apprentice. Royal Navy enlisted (not commissioned — unusual). Self-taught in mathematics and navigation. His ability with celestial navigation was exceptional. Made all three Pacific voyages as commissioned officer; achieved Captain's rank. Killed by Hawaiians at Kealakekua Bay, February 14, 1779, in a dispute over a stolen boat.

### Why Cook Was Different

```
COOK'S APPROACH vs. PREVIOUS EXPLORERS
=========================================

  PREVIOUS PACIFIC EXPLORERS:
    Crossed the ocean to reach specific known destinations.
    Hugged known routes.
    Scientific observation: secondary or absent.

  COOK:
    Primary purpose of all three voyages: systematic observation.
    Took scientists on every voyage.
    Made longitude measurements with a marine chronometer (Harrison's H4).
    Produced charts accurate enough to be used 200 years later.
    Collected botanical, zoological, and anthropological data.
    Controlled scurvy on his ships (diet, cleanliness) — astonishing for era.
    Lost almost no men to scurvy. Killed some to disease at tropical ports.

  NAVIGATION INNOVATION:
    Harrison's marine chronometer (H4, tested 1761) solved the
    longitude problem.
    At sea, latitude is easy: measure sun's angle at noon.
    Longitude: requires knowing the exact time at the prime meridian.
    Without a clock accurate enough to survive sea voyage: impossible.
    Cook tested the K1 (copy of H4) on second voyage.
    Result: Cook's charts showed accurate longitudes for the first time.
```

### The Three Voyages

```
FIRST VOYAGE (1768–1771):
  Ship: HM Bark Endeavour (coal transport, refitted).
  Scientists: Joseph Banks, Daniel Solander.
  PRIMARY MISSION: Observe transit of Venus from Tahiti (June 3, 1769).
  SECONDARY (sealed orders): Search for Terra Australis.

  Tahiti → New Zealand (6-month circumnavigation; charts the entire coast)
  → Eastern Australia (April–August 1770)
    - Claims New South Wales for Britain
    - Wrecked on Great Barrier Reef (Cooktown repairs)
    - Botany Bay described (later: first penal colony)
  → Cape of Good Hope → England

  RESULT: New Zealand and eastern Australia on the map. No Terra Australis yet.

SECOND VOYAGE (1772–1775):
  Ships: Resolution and Adventure.
  MISSION: Settle Terra Australis question.

  Resolution crosses the ANTARCTIC CIRCLE: January 17, 1773.
  First ship to do so. Ice. No continent visible.
  Crosses again: December 1773. January 1774. Reaches 71°10'S.
  Turns back: solid ice. Could not proceed further.

  Also: Easter Island, Tonga, Marquesas, New Hebrides, New Caledonia.
  Tests K1 chronometer → longitude problem solved in practice.

  RESULT: Terra Australis does not exist (not as imagined in temperate latitudes).
          If a southern continent exists, it's under permanent ice.
          (Antarctica confirmed: Bellingshausen, Bransfield, 1820.)

THIRD VOYAGE (1776–1779):
  Ships: Resolution and Discovery.
  MISSION: Find Northwest Passage from Pacific side.

  Pacific Northwest coast survey (British Columbia, Alaska).
  Bering Strait: passed through to Arctic Ocean.
  Ice blocked further progress.
  Returned south: Hawaii.

  DEATH (February 14, 1779):
    Dispute at Kealakekua Bay, Hawaii.
    Hawaiians had stolen a small boat.
    Cook attempted to take Hawaiian chief hostage.
    Crowd surrounded him. Cook shot one man.
    Stabbed and clubbed in shallow water.
    4 marines also killed.
    His body was initially taken; bones returned as a mark of respect.
    His crew buried them at sea.

COOK'S LEGACY:
  The Pacific was mapped with modern accuracy.
  The myth of Terra Australis dispelled.
  Hawaii, New Zealand, and Australia placed on European charts.
  Scientific partnership model: navigator + naturalist.
  Crew health management: Cook's care for crew was unprecedented.
```

## Abel Tasman (1603–1659)

### Bio Snapshot

Dutch navigator. Worked for the Dutch East India Company (VOC). Two voyages (1642–43, 1644) commissioned by the VOC's Governor-General Anthony Van Diemen in Batavia (Jakarta).

### Tasman's Voyages

```
TASMAN'S FIRST VOYAGE (1642–43)
=================================

  Departed Batavia (Jakarta), August 1642.
  Route: south around Australia (far south, out of sight).
  First land: Tasmania (November 1642). Named Van Diemen's Land.
             (Renamed Tasmania 1856 to separate it from the brutal
             convict transportation era Van Diemen symbolized.)
  New Zealand: December 1642. Anchor in Golden Bay.
              4 sailors killed in first contact with Maori.
              Tasman named it "Murderers' Bay." Departed without landing.
  Tonga and Fiji: January 1643.
  Back to Batavia: June 1643.

  WHAT HE PROVED:
    A landmass south of Australia exists (Tasmania).
    Another landmass east of it (New Zealand).
    But: he did not determine if Australia was an island or
    connected to a southern continent.

SECOND VOYAGE (1644):
  VOC wanted to know if Australia was an island.
  Tasman sailed the north coast and western coast.
  Determined: Australia is (probably) an island.
  Could not confirm the south (which had been skirted in 1642).
  The VOC was disappointed — no gold, no silver, no useful trade goods.
  The VOC abandoned further exploration of Australia.

LEGACY:
  Tasmania (named for him), Tasman Sea (between Australia and New Zealand).
  New Zealand first mapped (crudely) by Tasman.
  His charts of northern Australia used for 130+ years.
```

## Jean-François de La Perouse (1741–1788?)

### Bio Snapshot

French naval officer. Served in the Seven Years' War and American Revolution. Commissioned by Louis XVI in 1785 for a scientific circumnavigation — France's answer to Cook. Disappeared in 1788 in the Vanuatu/Solomon Islands region. His fate was unknown for 38 years — one of the great mysteries of exploration.

### The Voyage (1785–1788)

```
LA PEROUSE'S VOYAGE — AMBITIONS AND END
==========================================

  PURPOSE:
    Finish what Cook left incomplete.
    Survey Pacific coasts Cook didn't map (Alaska, California, Asia).
    Collect scientific data (botany, anthropology, astronomy).
    Claim France's place in Pacific geopolitics.

  FLEET: Boussole and Astrolabe. ~220 men.

  ROUTE AND DISCOVERIES:
    Easter Island (April 1786).
    Hawaii.
    Alaska coast (July 1786). Mapped many features Cook had missed.
    Mishap: two longboats lost in Alaska surf → 21 men drowned.
    California (Monterey). First French contact with California.
    Macau. Korea (first European to chart Korean coast).
    Sakhalin Island. Confirmed it as an island (not peninsula).
    Kamchatka (September 1787): last known contact with Europe.
    Samoa (December 1787): 11 crew members killed in ambush.

  DISAPPEARANCE:
    Botany Bay, Australia: March 1788. Last definitive contact.
    (Arrives 6 days after First Fleet — British penal colony founding.)
    Departed for Pacific. Never seen again.

  FOUND:
    1827: Irish captain Peter Dillon finds artifacts at Vanikoro Island
    (Solomon Islands) — French-made tools, anchors, local stories of
    two ships that wrecked in a storm.
    1828: Dumont d'Urville visits: more wreckage confirmed.
    1964/1988/2005: underwater archaeology finds both ship wrecks.
    Site: storm wrecked them on reefs at Vanikoro, 1788. Survivors
    reached shore; built a small boat from wreckage; sailed away.
    Final fate of survivors: unknown.
```

## George Vancouver (1757–1798)

### Bio Snapshot

English navigator. Served under Cook on the second and third voyages. Commissioned 1791 for a survey of the Pacific Northwest coast — partly to resolve British-Spanish territorial disputes (Nootka Convention, 1790).

### The Pacific Northwest Survey (1791–1795)

```
VANCOUVER'S SURVEY — PRECISION MAPPING
=========================================

  PURPOSE:
    Accurately map the coast from California to Alaska.
    Determine if there is an interior passage connecting Pacific to Atlantic.
    (The "Northwest Passage" from the Pacific side.)

  METHOD:
    Systematic survey from the ships.
    Small boats carried into every inlet, fjord, and channel.
    Every arm of the sea followed to its end to determine if it connected
    to the interior.
    Latitude and longitude measured precisely with chronometers.

  KEY FINDINGS:
    Vancouver Island: circumnavigated, proved to be an island.
    Puget Sound: surveyed and named.
    Fraser River: identified.
    Inside Passage (BC): mapped.
    No interior passage: all inlets dead-ended. No NW Passage at these latitudes.

  ACCURACY:
    Vancouver's charts were used by mariners into the 20th century.
    His longitude measurements were accurate to within ~1 km.
    He named hundreds of features: Puget Sound, Mount Baker, Hood Canal,
    Admiralty Inlet — still in use.

  CULTURAL CONTACT:
    Met with Spanish authorities at Nootka Sound (resolving territorial claims).
    Traded with Northwest Coast indigenous peoples — extensive description.
    Complex societies; large plank houses; sophisticated art.
    Vancouver's account is an important early ethnographic record.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Cook discovered Australia."**
Dutch navigators (Dirk Hartog, 1616; Tasman, 1642–44) saw Australia before Cook. William Dampier (1688, 1699) explored parts of the Australian coast. Cook charted the *eastern* coast (1770) and claimed it for Britain — which led to colonization. The continent was known to Europeans for 150 years before Cook.

**"Cook was killed because Hawaiians thought he was a god."**
The story that Hawaiians believed Cook was the god Lono is a well-known but contested anthropological claim (Marshall Sahlins vs. Gananath Obeyesekere debate). What is clear: Cook was killed in a dispute over a stolen boat. The "god" theory was developed by 19th-century writers; contemporary Hawaiian oral tradition does not consistently support it.

**"La Perouse disappeared mysteriously."**
His ships wrecked on reefs at Vanikoro Island in a storm — a navigational accident, not mysterious. The mystery was not knowing *where* they wrecked for 38 years. The wreck sites were found by underwater archaeology (1964, 1988, 2005).
