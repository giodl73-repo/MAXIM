# Poetry Forms and Prosody

## The Big Picture

Prosody is the formal study of poetic meter, rhythm, and sound. Poetry is language organized by formal constraints on sound — constraints that may be quantitative (duration), accentual (stress), syllabic (count), or tonal (pitch). Different languages use different prosodic systems because their phonological structure differs.

## Layer 1: English Meter — Accentual-Syllabic System

### The Foot

English meter is organized by **feet** — units of stressed and unstressed syllables.

```
THE METRICAL FEET (English)
----------------------------
Symbol: /  = stressed syllable
         u  = unstressed syllable

IAMB       u /     "a-LONE"       "da-DUM"
TROCHEE    / u     "FA-ther"      "DUM-da"
ANAPEST    u u /   "un-der-STAND" "da-da-DUM"
DACTYL     / u u   "HAP-pi-ly"    "DUM-da-da"
SPONDEE    / /     "TRUE BLUE"    "DUM-DUM" (accent-to-accent)
PYRRHIC    u u     "of the"       "da-da" (no stress)

NAMING BY LINE LENGTH:
Monometer  = 1 foot
Dimeter    = 2 feet
Trimeter   = 3 feet
Tetrameter = 4 feet
Pentameter = 5 feet (THE most common in English)
Hexameter  = 6 feet (classical epic; rare in English)
Heptameter = 7 feet (rare; "fourteener")
```

### Iambic Pentameter

The dominant meter of English poetry for 400 years:

```
IAMBIC PENTAMETER
-----------------
5 iambic feet = 10 syllables: u / u / u / u / u /

"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"
 u /    u /    u  / u / u /
 Shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY

"To be or not to be, that is the question"
 u / u /  u /  u /   /  u  u /  u u
 — note that the last line breaks the pattern.
   Variation is normal; rigid regularity sounds mechanical.

Used by: Shakespeare, Marlowe, Milton, Dryden, Pope,
         Wordsworth, Keats, Browning, Yeats, Stevens

The blank verse (unrhymed iambic pentameter) of Shakespeare:
  "We are such stuff / as dreams are made on, and /
  our little life / is rounded with a sleep."

The heroic couplet (rhymed iambic pentameter in pairs):
  "Know then thyself, presume not God to scan,
  The proper study of mankind is Man." (Pope)
```

### Scansion (Reading Meter)

```
HOW TO SCAN A LINE
-------------------
1. Mark natural speech stresses (ignore the meter first)
2. Identify the dominant foot pattern
3. Scan with dominant pattern but allow variation
4. Mark variations (inversions, extra syllables, etc.)

COMMON VARIATIONS IN IAMBIC PENTAMETER:
Trochaic inversion at line start: common and natural
  "TIGER, tiger, burning bright" — trochee opening
Feminine ending: unstressed extra syllable at end
  "To BE or NOT to BE, that IS the QUES-tion" (11 syllables)
Caesura: mid-line pause (marked |)
  "Shall I compare thee | to a summer's day"
Enjambment: line runs on without pause to next line
  (opposite of end-stopped)
```

### European Fixed Forms

| Form | Lines | Rhyme Scheme | Meter | Key Feature | Example |
|------|-------|-------------|-------|-------------|---------|
| Italian Sonnet (Petrarchan) | 14 | ABBAABBA CDECDE (varied) | Iambic pentameter | Volta (turn) between octave and sestet; octave poses problem, sestet resolves | Petrarch, Donne, Hopkins |
| Shakespearean Sonnet | 14 | ABAB CDCD EFEF GG | Iambic pentameter | Three quatrains develop argument; couplet provides turn/resolution | Shakespeare's 154 sonnets |

| Villanelle | 19 | ABA ABA ABA ABA ABA ABAA | Iambic pentameter (flexible) | Two refrains (A1 = line 1, A2 = line 3) alternate as tercet closing lines; both close the final quatrain | "Do Not Go Gentle" (Thomas), "One Art" (Bishop) |

### The Sestina End-Word Rotation

The sestina's permutation of end-words is the most formally complex structure in Western fixed poetry:

```
SESTINA END-WORD ROTATION
---------------------------
6 end-words: let them be A B C D E F

Stanza 1: lines end A B C D E F
Stanza 2: lines end F A E B D C
Stanza 3: lines end C F D A B E
Stanza 4: lines end E C B F A D
Stanza 5: lines end D E A C F B
Stanza 6: lines end B D F E C A
Envoi (3 lines): each line contains 2 of the 6 words

The pattern is a specific mathematical permutation:
  Each stanza rotates the previous by the rule:
  last → first, then alternate from outside in.
  This is sometimes called "retrogradatio cruciata"
  (reverse cruciform movement).

The effect: by the final stanza, every possible
relationship between the 6 words has been expressed.
The end-words themselves become the poem's subject —
they accrete meaning through each new context.
```

## Layer 3: The Ode

### Pindaric Ode (see also 02-EPIC-AND-LYRIC.md)

The Greek choral ode for athletic victory. Structural unit: strophe + antistrophe (same meter, chorus turns one way then returns) + epode (different meter, chorus stands). Repeated in triads.

```
PINDARIC ODE — TRIADIC STRUCTURE

Each TRIAD:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ STROPHE      (στροφή = "turn")                               │
  │  Meter:  [x / u / u u / u u / u / ...]   [Pindar's own     │
  │          complex lyric meters; no English equivalent]        │
  │  Chorus physically turns left across the orchestra          │
  │  Content: opens the myth or narrative                       │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ ANTISTROPHE  (ἀντιστροφή = "counter-turn")                  │
  │  Meter:  IDENTICAL to strophe                               │
  │  Chorus turns right (reversing the strophe's movement)      │
  │  Content: continues/develops the myth                       │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ EPODE        (ἐπῳδός = "after-song")                        │
  │  Meter:  DIFFERENT from strophe/antistrophe (shorter)       │
  │  Chorus stands still                                        │
  │  Content: concludes the mythic passage; applies to athlete  │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  Repeated: 3–5 triads per ode.

PINDAR'S ODES — FORMAL CONTEXT:
  4 Panhellenic games → 4 victory ode collections:
    Olympian (14 odes), Pythian (12), Isthmian (8), Nemean (11)
  Structure within each ode:
    1. Address to the victor / city / patron deity
    2. Mythic narrative (often only tangentially related to victory)
    3. Maxims (gnomai) — the ode's argument about excellence (arete)
    4. Return to the victor; prayer or warning
  The myth is not ornamental — it provides the paradigm
  against which the athletic victory is measured and elevated.

KEY FORMAL FEATURES:
  - No enjambment across strophe/antistrophe boundary
  - Each strophe is a self-contained metrical unit
  - The metrical system is audible movement — the meter IS the dance
  - "Pindaric obscurity": dense allusion, abrupt transitions,
    suppressed logical connectives → the poem demands active assembly
    by an educated audience. Information density is high; surface
    fluency is not the goal.

FORMAL ANALOG:
  The triadic structure is a ternary formal pattern (ABA') where
  A and A' are identical in meter but different in content,
  and B provides resolution. Compare sonata form (exposition /
  development / recapitulation) — not coincidental; classical
  music theorists knew Pindar.
```

### Horatian Ode

Horace's Odes use regular stanzas with consistent meter throughout — more intimate and reflective, less grandly public than Pindar:

```
HORACE'S ALCAIC STROPHE (his most common)
-------------------------------------------
Two 11-syllable alcaic hendecasyllables:
  x x / u / / | u u / u /
  x x / u / / | u u / u /
One 9-syllable alcaic:
  x x / u / u / u /
One 10-syllable alcaic:
  / u u / u u / u / u

Used for moral reflection, carpe diem, friendship,
political commentary — more personal than Pindar.

"Odes" in English (Keats, Shelley, Wordsworth):
English "odes" do not follow Horace's or Pindar's
meters strictly — they take the elevated, meditative
mode and apply it to English stanzas.
Keats's "Ode to a Nightingale" uses 10-line stanzas:
  ABABCDECDE — a modified sonnet form expanded.
```

## Layer 4: Old English and Alliterative Verse

English poetry before the Norman Conquest (1066) used an entirely different system — purely accentual, based on alliteration rather than rhyme or syllable count:

```
OLD ENGLISH ALLITERATIVE METER
--------------------------------
4 stresses per line, divided by a caesura (|)
2 stresses in each half-line
At least 2 stressed syllables in first half
alliterate with 1st stressed syllable in second half
(3rd stress in 2nd half does NOT alliterate)

Beowulf:
"Hwat! We Gardena | in geardagum"
  /    u  /        u  /    u  /
"Listen! We have heard of the spear-Danes' glory
in days of yore" -- alliteration on G

The number of unstressed syllables is variable.
This is a STRESS-ONLY (accentual) system.

THE ALLITERATIVE REVIVAL (14th c.):
After Norman Conquest, this tradition submerged.
It revived in the 14th century in:
  - Pearl, Sir Gawain and the Green Knight (Gawain-poet)
  - Piers Plowman (Langland)
  These use alliterative verse but with medieval subject matter.
```

## Layer 5: Free Verse

Free verse is not formless — it uses line breaks, sound patterns, syntactic rhythm, and typographic spacing as its formal elements.

```
FREE VERSE — WHAT ORGANIZES IT
--------------------------------
NOT: strict meter or fixed form
YES: the line as the unit of composition

The line break is the fundamental formal decision:
  Where a line ends creates emphasis and pause.
  Where enjambment runs over creates speed and ambiguity.
  The white space on the page is a formal element.

WHITMAN'S LONG LINE (catalog style):
  "I celebrate myself, and sing myself,
  And what I assume you shall assume,
  For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you."
  — Anaphoric (repeated opening), enumerative, biblical
    in cadence (influenced by King James Bible's parallel
    structure). Not metered, but intensely rhythmic.

IMAGISM (Pound, H.D., early Eliot):
  "In a Station of the Metro"
  "The apparition of these faces in the crowd;
  Petals on a wet, black bough."
  — 2 lines; image juxtaposition as the formal principle.
    No development, argument, or narrative — only the image.

LANGUAGE POETRY (1970s-80s):
  Ron Silliman, Lyn Hejinian, Charles Bernstein.
  Form calls attention to language's materiality.
  Syntax disrupted; referential function disrupted.
  "Meaning is deferred, not delivered." — opaque to
  ordinary reading; requires active reconstruction.
```

## Layer 7: Slam Poetry and Performance Poetry

```
SLAM POETRY
-----------
Performance-based competitive poetry (first slam: 1986, Chicago,
Marc Smith at the Green Mill). Scored by randomly-selected audience judges.
Formal constraints: time limit (typically 3 minutes), no props, no costumes.
The form is deliberately anti-elitist: scoring is the crowd's, not critics'.

Slam poetry descends from oral traditions, spoken word,
Harlem Renaissance performance culture, and hip-hop.
The "hierarchy" (page > performance) has collapsed in criticism;
the critical apparatus has followed the form.

HIP-HOP PROSODY — FORMAL ANALYSIS

Rap is metered verse with a more complex formal system than most
academic poetry. The core parameters:

1. THE GRID: the underlying rhythmic grid of the beat
   Beat: 4/4 time, typically at 80-100 BPM (trap: 130-160 BPM half-time)
   Each bar = 4 beats. The rhyming unit is the bar.

2. FLOW: the rapper's rhythmic relationship to the grid
   On-beat flow: stressed syllables align with beat positions
   Behind-the-beat: syllables consistently lag the grid slightly
   Syncopated: rhythmic anticipation across beat positions

   Scansion example (Notorious B.I.G., "Juicy"):
     "It was all a DREAM / I used to READ Word Up mag-a-ZINE"
      u u u u  /       u u u u /    u u /  u   u u /
   Standard iambic scansion fails here — the stressed syllables
   are beat positions, not alternating stress. The "foot" is the
   quarter-note slot, not the stress-unstress pair.

3. RHYME DENSITY: rhymes in rap extend far beyond end-rhyme
   End-rhyme: terminal rhyme on the bar's final syllable (basic)
   Internal rhyme: rhymes within the line
   Multi-syllabic rhyme: rhyming stressed+unstressed syllable chains
   Assonance chains: vowel-matching across multiple words

   Eminem, "Lose Yourself" (multi-syllabic internal + end):
     "His palms are sweaty, knees weak, arms are heavy"
      A      B        C    D    E      F
   Sweaty / heavy: 2-syllable rhyme (STRESSED + unstressed)
   Knees weak / arms: internal assonance chain on /i:/ then /ɑː/

4. KENDRICK LAMAR — FORMAL COMPLEXITY
   DAMN. (2017): sustained formal variation across 14 tracks.
   "DNA.": aggressive triplet flow against a half-time beat.
   "HUMBLE.": deliberate rhythmic restraint, drop-back flow.
   "Alright": gospel-adjacent 6/8 feel over 4/4 grid — polyrhythm.
   The album's formal variety exceeds most contemporary print poetry
   in technical prosodic range.

   Transcription notation (adapted from Blumenfeld):
     / = primary stress (beat position)
     u = unstressed
     ^ = secondary stress (off-beat emphasis)
     | = bar line
     ~ = extended/melismatic syllable
```

Hip-hop as formal prosody:
  Rap is metered verse. The meter is often complex:
  - Polyrhythm (multiple simultaneous stress patterns)
  - Internal rhyme (rhymes within the line, not just end)
  - Assonance chains (vowel rhymes across lines)
  - "Flow" = the rap's rhythmic relationship to the beat
    (on-beat, slightly ahead, syncopated)
  Kendrick Lamar's DAMN. has more technical prosodic
  complexity than most contemporary print poetry.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Free verse is not easier to write than metered verse**
Free verse requires the same formal attention as metered verse — the choices just shift from prescribed patterns to self-imposed line-by-line decisions. A bad free verse poem is just prose with arbitrary line breaks.

**Blank verse is iambic pentameter without rhyme**
"Blank" means unrhymed. It is still strictly metered (iambic pentameter). Shakespeare's plays are written in blank verse (with some prose and some rhymed verse for particular effects). Milton's Paradise Lost is blank verse. This is not the same as free verse.

**Syllabic and accentual-syllabic meter are different systems**
In French, syllabic count is what matters — stress is not phonemically distinctive in French as it is in English. The alexandrine (12-syllable French line) does not have a stress pattern. When English poets write "syllabics" (Marianne Moore, Dylan Thomas occasionally), they count syllables regardless of stress — unusual for English, which hears stress strongly.

**The volta is not always at the same line**
In a Petrarchan sonnet, the volta (turn) typically falls between the octave and sestet (after line 8). But in practice, Petrarch himself moves the volta around. The "turn" is a cognitive or rhetorical shift — from problem to resolution, from thesis to antithesis, from description to reflection. Where it falls depends on the poem's logic, not a rule.
