## Oral-Formulaic Theory: The Generative Grammar of Epic

The most important contribution to the study of oral epic is **Milman Parry's** doctoral work (Paris, 1928) and **Albert Lord's** continuation in **The Singer of Tales** (1960). Their joint fieldwork in Yugoslavia (1930s–50s) recording South Slavic epic singers transformed understanding of Homer.

### The Core Discovery

```
PARRY'S OBSERVATION
-------------------
Homer's language is full of repeated phrases:
  "swift-footed Achilles"
  "rosy-fingered Dawn"
  "wine-dark sea"
  "Athena of the flashing eyes"

Previous scholarship: Homer used these for emphasis or style.

Parry's insight: these are FORMULAE — metrical units that fill
specific metrical slots in the dactylic hexameter line.

A FORMULA = a group of words regularly used under the same
            metrical conditions to express a given essential idea.

Not style. Not laziness. Not oral memory of fixed text.
These are generative units for composition in performance.
```

### The Composition-in-Performance Model

```
HOW ORAL POETS WORK (Parry/Lord model)
---------------------------------------

They do NOT memorize a fixed text.
They generate the poem in performance using a GRAMMAR:

  VOCABULARY (formulae):
  - Noun-epithet combinations for heroes, gods, places
  - Standard phrases for recurrent actions (arming, feasting,
    dying, sailing, arriving)
  - Type scenes (whole narrative units with standard structure)

  GRAMMAR (thematic patterns):
  - The arming scene: hero dresses in sequence (greaves,
    corselet, sword, shield, helm, spear) — always this order
  - The banquet scene: invitation, hand-washing, food, wine,
    conversation — always this structure
  - The council scene, the supplication scene, the duel, etc.

  Each performance is a new GENERATION of the poem from this grammar.
  Not variation from a fixed original. Generation from a system.

Compare:
  Each syntactically valid sentence in English is not memorized —
  it is generated by the grammar plus a lexicon.
  Oral epic works the same way, but the units are larger
  (formulae, type scenes) not words.
```

### The Homeric Question

The Homeric Question — did one poet write the Iliad and Odyssey, or were they compiled from many sources? — was the great philological controversy of the 19th century.

```
ANALYSTS (19th c.)
  The poems are compilations of shorter lays,
  identifiable by inconsistencies and interpolations.

UNITARIANS
  No — there is a single organizing artistic intelligence.
  The inconsistencies are in our reading, not the poem.

PARRY/LORD RESOLUTION
  Both are partly right. The oral tradition is the "composer."
  Individual performances have an author; the tradition is
  collective. The formulas are traditional (composed by nobody,
  used by everybody); the arrangement of a particular performance
  belongs to the singer.

  The Iliad and Odyssey represent a crystallization — probably
  written down or dictated at a transitional moment when oral
  tradition was encountering literacy. They bear the marks of
  both oral composition and literary organization.
```

## The Epic Tradition

### Greek Epic: Homer

```
ILIAD                              ODYSSEY
-----                              -------
War epic                           Homecoming epic
Achilles' wrath and its            Odysseus's 10-year return
consequences                       from Troy
Concentrated: ~51 days             Sprawling: 10 years + Telemachy
Tragic: Patroclus dies,            Comic structure: hero returns
Achilles kills Hector              in disguise, defeats suitors
Themes: glory, mortality,          Themes: cunning vs. strength,
        heroic code, pity          loyalty, identity, home
24 books, ~15,000 lines            24 books, ~12,000 lines

Both in dactylic hexameter:
  dum-da-da | dum-da-da | dum-da-da | dum-da-da | dum-da-da | dum-dum
  (longum, biceps, biceps pattern; substitute spondee for dactyl)
  A line is 6 dactylic feet; the 5th foot is almost always a dactyl.
```

### Latin Epic: Virgil

Virgil's **Aeneid** (c. 29–19 BCE) is a **literary epic** — composed in writing, consciously modeled on Homer, serving a political program (founding mythology of Rome under Augustus).

```
HOMER VS. VIRGIL
----------------
Homer:          Virgil:
Primary epic    Secondary/literary epic
Generated in    Composed over 10 years,
oral tradition  left unfinished (died 19 BCE)
Heroes pursue   Aeneas suppresses personal
personal glory  desire for Roman destiny
Achilles/        Aeneas carries the weight
Odysseus         of future civilization
Multiple gods   Fatum (Fate) is the engine
intervene        of the plot

VIRGIL'S STRUCTURAL INNOVATION
The Aeneid is 12 books split:
  Books 1-6 = Odyssey structure (wandering)
  Books 7-12 = Iliad structure (war in Latium)
A structural allusion to Homer embedded in the very architecture.
```

### Sanskrit Epic: Mahabharata and Ramayana

```
MAHABHARATA
  ~100,000 shlokas (couplets) = world's longest poem
  Core: the Kurukshetra War between Pandavas and Kauravas
  Embedded: Bhagavad Gita (philosophical dialogue)
  Developed over centuries, compiled ~400 BCE–400 CE
  Genre: itihasa ("thus indeed it was") — history + legend

RAMAYANA
  ~24,000 shlokas
  Rama's quest to rescue Sita from demon Ravana
  More unified, attributed to single poet Valmiki
  Became template for Southeast Asian literatures
  (Thai Ramakien, Indonesian Ramayana, etc.)

Both are orally transmitted and still performed
(Katha, Yakshagana, wayang kulit shadow puppetry).
Unlike Homer, they remain living performance traditions.
```

### African Oral Epic: Sundiata

```
SUNDIATA (Epic of Old Mali, c. 13th c., recorded 20th c.)
  Hero: Sundiata Keita, founder of the Mali Empire
  Tradition: griots (jalolu) — hereditary professional
             storytellers/musicians/historians of West Africa
  Formal features:
    - Praise songs (jaliya) embedded in narrative
    - The griot is a character in the tale
    - Audience participates (call and response)
    - Each performance is different — no fixed text
  Connect: Parry/Lord model applies exactly here.
           Griots compose in performance from stored formulae
           and thematic patterns. The "text" is the tradition.
```

## The Lyric Mode

The lyric is the hardest of the three major genres to define — it is not a structural form (like narrative) or a functional mode (like drama). The definition has shifted at every period.

```
WORKING DEFINITIONS
-------------------
Classical:  Sung poetry, accompanied by the lyre (hence "lyric").
            Sappho, Alcaeus, Pindar were called "lyric poets"
            because their poems were performed musically.

Modern:     The lyric is characterized by:
            1. A single speaking voice (not narrative, not dramatic)
            2. Present-tense orientation (even when recalling past)
            3. Compression and intensity
            4. Foregrounding of the language itself (poetic function)
            5. Some relation to private/subjective experience

Post-romantic: The lyric "I" is not necessarily the poet —
               it is a speaker constructed by the poem.
               The Romantic confessional lyric (Wordsworth, Keats,
               Sylvia Plath) encourages biographical reading;
               structuralists and New Critics resist this.
```

## Greek Lyric: Sappho and Pindar

### Sappho (c. 630–570 BCE)

```
SAPPHO
  Writing on Lesbos; leader of a thiasos (circle of women)
  Nine books in antiquity; survives in fragments
  The Sapphic strophe (meter she invented/popularized):
    Three 11-syllable lines + one 5-syllable adonic:
    dum-da-dum-dum | dum-da-da | dum-da-dum (x3)
    dum-da-da | dum-dum

  Content: erotic desire, longing, jealousy, celebration
  Fragment 31 ("He seems to me equal to the gods") —
    describes symptoms of desire: heart racing, speechless,
    sweat, trembling. First-person physical phenomenology
    of erotic experience.
  Fragment 2, 16, 31 are among the most analyzed lyrics
  in Western tradition.
```

### Pindar (c. 522–443 BCE)

```
PINDAR: THE EPINICIAN ODE
  Odes celebrating victors at the Panhellenic Games
  (Olympian, Pythian, Nemean, Isthmian odes)

  Structure: triadic — strophe + antistrophe + epode
    Strophe and antistrophe have identical meter (chorus moves
    one way, then returns); epode has different meter (chorus
    stands still). Repeated across the ode.

  MATHEMATICAL SYMMETRY: the triadic structure is
  architecturally regular. Each "triad" is a metrical unit.
  Choral performance made this physically embodied.

  Content: praise of victor + mythological narrative +
           gnomic wisdom + religious reflection
  Style: elaborate metaphor, compression, allusion,
         difficult syntax ("the dark Pindaric")

  Horace famously warned against imitating Pindar (Odes IV.2):
  "Whoever strives to rival Pindar / relies on waxen wings."
```

## Lyric Forms: A Reference Taxonomy

### Fixed Forms

| Form | Origin | Structure | Key Examples |
|------|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Sonnet (Italian) | Italy, 13th c. | 14 lines, octave (ABBAABBA) + sestet (CDECDE or variant); volta (turn) between them | Petrarch, Dante |
| Sonnet (Shakespearean) | England, 16th c. | 14 lines, 3 quatrains (ABAB CDCD EFEF) + couplet (GG) | Shakespeare, Sidney |
| Ode (Pindaric) | Greece, 5th c. BCE | Strophe/antistrophe/epode triads | Pindar |
| Ode (Horatian) | Rome, 1st c. BCE | Regular stanzas, same meter throughout | Horace; Keats's odes |
| Elegy | Greece | Alternating hexameter/pentameter (elegiac couplet) | Ovid, Tibullus; now: reflective poem on death |
| Epigram | Greece | Short, pointed, witty | Martial, Oscar Wilde (as form) |
| Villanelle | France, 16th c. | 19 lines: 5 tercets + quatrain; two refrains (A1 and A2) alternate; final stanza: A1/A2 | "Do not go gentle" (Thomas), "One Art" (Bishop) |
| Sestina | Occitan, 12th c. | 6 sestets + tercet; 6 end-words rotate through permutation pattern | Arnaut Daniel, Auden, Swinburne |
| Rondeau | France, 15th c. | 15 lines; refrain from first phrase; AABBA AABR AABBAR | Villon |

### Eastern Fixed Forms

| Form | Origin | Structure | Key Examples |
|------|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Haiku | Japan, 17th c. | 5-7-5 on (sound units); seasonal reference (kigo); cutting word (kireji) | Basho, Buson, Issa |
| Tanka/Waka | Japan, 7th c.+ | 5-7-5-7-7 = 31 on | Ono no Komachi, Man'yoshu |
| Ghazal | Arabic/Persian, 7th c.+ | Couplets (sher) all rhyming on same word (radif = refrain + qafia = rhyme word before it); poet's name in final couplet | Hafiz, Rumi, Ghalib |
| Qasida | Arabic, pre-Islamic | Long monorhyme ode; nasib (erotic prelude) + travel/desert + main praise section | Imru al-Qays |

## The Troubadour Tradition

The troubadours (Occitan poets, c. 1100–1300) are crucial: they invented or systematized many of the lyric forms and themes that define European poetry through to the present.

```
TROUBADOUR SYSTEM
-----------------
Location: Southern France (Provence), northern Spain, Italy
Language: Occitan (langue d'oc)
Social context: Courts, patronage, aristocratic culture

KEY GENRES:
  Canso     — love song; the prestige form. "Fin'amors"
              (courtly love): desire for an idealized,
              usually unattainable, married noblewoman.
  Sirventes — political/satirical poem (same melodies as
              canso, different content — commentary on
              current events, crusades, enemies)
  Tenso     — debate poem between two poets
  Pastorela — narrative of knight encountering shepherd
  Alba      — dawn song: lovers must part at dawn
  Planh     — lament (death, exile)

FIN'AMORS (courtly love) as a FORMAL SYSTEM:
  The lady is inaccessible (usually married to poet's lord)
  The poet suffers ennobling desire (not crude lust)
  Desire itself is the subject, not consummation
  The secrecy of the love is its condition
  The poet improves himself through longing

  This is a ROLE-PLAYING SYSTEM with typed positions:
  Poet (joglar/troubadour) → Lady (domna) → Rival/husband → Gossips (lauzengiers)
  Love is a feudal relationship: the lady is the lord

TROUBADOURS → TROUVERES (northern France) → MINNESANGER (Germany)
            → STILNOVISTI (Italy: Cavalcanti, Dante early work)
            → PETRARCHAN SONNET TRADITION
            → English sonneteers (Wyatt, Sidney, Spenser, Shakespeare)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Oral-formulaic theory does not mean the poems are inferior**
The formulaic structure is not a crutch — it is the medium. Evaluating Homer by the standard of literary originality (avoid cliches) is a category error. The formula is the substrate; skill lies in deploying it.

**The lyric "I" is not the author**
The "I" of a lyric poem is a constructed speaker. Sappho's "I" is a poetic persona, not a confessional autobiography (though biographical readings exist). The New Critical move was to separate them; the biographical/confessional tradition collapses them. Both tendencies exist in literary culture.

**"Epic" does not mean "long"**
Paradise Lost is long and is an epic; a short prose narrative can be epic in spirit. The term "epic" implies: heroic subject matter, elevated style, communal importance, the involvement of cosmic forces (gods, fate, destiny). Length is a consequence, not a definition.

**Ghazal structure is specific, not just "Middle Eastern poetry"**
The ghazal has precise formal requirements: monorhyme (all second lines of each couplet rhyme on the same word), the radif (a word or phrase repeated after the rhyme throughout), and the maqta (closing couplet containing the poet's name or pseudonym). Western poets who write "ghazals" often drop these constraints; traditionalists consider this a different form.

**Type scenes are not repetition from carelessness**
The repeated arming scene, feast scene, supplication scene in Homer are type scenes — deliberate formal units. Their repetition is compositionally functional. When Homer varies the type scene, the variation is meaningful against the standard pattern.
