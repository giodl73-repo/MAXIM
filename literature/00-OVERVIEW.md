## What Literature Is (and Is Not)

Literature distinguished from adjacent fields:

```
+-------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| LINGUISTICS       | LITERATURE       | RHETORIC           |
+-------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Language as system| Language as art  | Language as tool   |
| Phonology/syntax/ | Form/meaning/    | Persuasion/        |
| semantics/pragm.  | affect/culture   | argument/delivery  |
| Descriptive       | Interpretive     | Prescriptive       |
| Saussure/Chomsky  | Aristotle/Proust | Aristotle/Cicero   |
+-------------------+------------------+--------------------+

+-------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| PHILOSOPHY        | LITERATURE       | HISTORY            |
+-------------------+------------------+--------------------+
| Argument about    | Narrative about  | Evidence about     |
| truths            | possible worlds  | actual events      |
| Propositional     | Imaginative      | Archival           |
| Abstract          | Concrete/sensory | Documentary        |
+-------------------+------------------+--------------------+
```

The boundary is contested. Philosophy of literature asks: what makes a text literary? The answer involves **foregrounding** (making language conspicuous), **fictionality** (not asserting truth), and **institutional recognition** (what the canon treats as literary).

## Genre Taxonomy

### The Classical Three (Aristotle's Poetics, c. 335 BCE)

```
                    MIMESIS (imitation/representation)
                           |
          +----------------+----------------+
          |                |                |
       EPIC             LYRIC           DRAMA
       -----            -----           -----
   Narrator tells    Poet speaks      Characters act
   of events         directly         before audience
   Temporal          Present tense    Present tense
   Homer, Vergil     Sappho, Pindar   Aeschylus, Sophocles
```

Note: Aristotle's categories have been complicated by every century since. The lyric was not prominent in his account — his Poetics focused on epic and tragedy. The lyric canon was built largely by later critics.

## Oral vs. Written: Not a Simple Hierarchy

The standard assumption is that writing preserves oral composition. The corrective is Walter Ong's concept of **secondary orality** and Albert Lord's discovery of **oral-formulaic composition** (see 02-EPIC-AND-LYRIC.md). Key distinctions:

```
ORAL PERFORMANCE             WRITTEN TEXT
----------------             ------------
Ephemeral (unless recorded)  Persistent
Context-dependent            Context-independent
Formulaic (efficiency)       Novel phrasing (freshness prized)
Collaborative (audience)     Solitary reception
Multisensory                 Visual only
Memory + improvisation       Fixed inscription
No author in modern sense    Author concept central

ORAL-FORMULAIC THEORY (Parry/Lord):
  Oral poets compose in performance using pre-formed formulae
  (noun-epithet combinations, type scenes, story patterns)
  — not memorization of fixed text but generation from grammar.

  This is essentially a generative grammar of oral poetry.
  Connect: Chomsky's generative grammar emerged from the same
  structuralist milieu (1950s-60s) that formalized this insight.
```

## The Canon Debate — Compressed

The literary canon is not a neutral list. Its formation is a social process with identifiable mechanisms:

```
CANON FORMATION MECHANISMS
---------------------------
1. Institutional consecration
   Universities adopt texts → students read them → critics write
   about them → universities adopt them. Self-reinforcing loop.

   Analogy: network effects. A standard wins not only on merit
   but because everyone else uses it (TCP/IP, QWERTY).

2. Gatekeeping infrastructure
   Publishers, prizes (Nobel, Booker, Pulitzer), review organs
   (NYT Book Review, TLS) determine visibility.

   Analogy: platform monopolies — whoever controls distribution
   shapes what gets read.

3. Harold Bloom (1994) "Western Canon" — argues for aesthetic
   criteria: "strangeness" and "cognitive power."

4. John Guillory (1993) "Cultural Capital" — argues the canon
   is not about texts but about educational credentials. What
   counts as literacy is socially constructed.

5. The "opening" (1980s-present): multicultural curriculum,
   world literature courses, global novel prize attention.

   But: translation into English still gatekeeps; most world
   literature enters the anglophone canon through translation.
```

## Literary History — Periodization (Western)

```
ANCIENT        Greek and Latin. ~700 BCE–400 CE
               Epic, tragedy, lyric, comedy, satire, pastoral

MEDIEVAL       ~400–1400 CE
               Latin learning + vernacular emergence (Dante, Chaucer)
               French troubadours, German Minnesanger

RENAISSANCE    ~1400–1600
               Recovery of classical forms; printing press (1450s)
               Sonnet sequence, epic revival (Ariosto, Spenser)
               Elizabethan drama

BAROQUE        ~1600–1700
               Elaborate style, conceits (metaphysicals)
               Spanish Golden Age (Cervantes, Lope, Calderon)

ENLIGHTENMENT  ~1700–1780
               Prose ascendant; early novel (Defoe, Richardson)
               Satire (Swift, Pope, Voltaire)

ROMANTICISM    ~1780–1850
               Lyric at center; sublime, imagination, nature
               Blake, Wordsworth, Keats, Shelley, Goethe, Pushkin

REALISM        ~1850–1900
               Novel dominant; social documentation
               Balzac, Flaubert, Tolstoy, Dickens, George Eliot

MODERNISM      ~1900–1945
               Form as content; fragmentation, interiority
               Joyce, Woolf, Kafka, Eliot, Pound, Faulkner

POSTMODERNISM  ~1945–present
               Metafiction, pastiche, intertextuality
               Borges, Pynchon, DeLillo, Calvino, Morrison

CONTEMPORARY   ~1990–present
               World novel, autofiction, genre hybridity
               Post-postmodern realism, climate fiction, digital fiction
```

## The Formal System Lens (For the MIT TCS Reader)

Literary study has a structural dimension that maps cleanly onto concepts from formal language theory:

```
FORMAL SYSTEM ANALOGIES
-----------------------

Propp's 31 Morphological Functions (1928)
  A finite set of narrative moves from which all Russian
  folktales are generated. Essentially a context-free grammar
  for folk narrative. Characters are typed (villain, donor,
  helper, seeker) and functions are sequenced.
  Analogy: BNF grammar generating strings. Propp's morphology
  is the grammar; each tale is a derivation.

Greimas's Actantial Model (1966)
  Six actants (Subject/Object, Sender/Receiver, Helper/Opponent)
  define the relational deep structure of any narrative.
  Analogy: typed variables in a semantic frame. Every narrative
  is a projection of this fixed schema.

Genette's Narratology (1972, "Narrative Discourse")
  Formal taxonomy: story (histoire) vs. discourse (recit) vs.
  narrating (narration). Five dimensions: order, duration,
  frequency, mood, voice.
  Analogy: separation of specification from implementation.
  The story is the abstract structure; the discourse is the
  concrete rendering.

Jakobson's Communication Model (1960)
  Six factors (addresser/message/addressee/context/contact/code)
  → six functions (emotive/poetic/conative/referential/phatic/
  metalingual). The POETIC function foregrounds the message itself.
  Analogy: protocol stack, where the poetic layer is the one
  that reflects on its own encoding.

Sanskrit Rasa Theory
  Eight (later nine) permanent emotions (sthayi-bhava) that
  literary art evokes. Formal affect taxonomy: Shringara
  (love/beauty), Vira (heroism), Karuna (sorrow), etc.
  Analogy: a typed enum for affective states with compositional
  rules for how they combine.
```

## Translation Theory — The Fidelity Tradeoff

The core tension in translation theory maps onto a familiar engineering tradeoff:

```
FOREIGNIZATION                    DOMESTICATION
(Schleiermacher 1813,             (Nida 1964, "dynamic equivalence"
 Venuti 1995)                      Lawrence Venuti "fluency")

Keep the foreign.                 Make it fluent.
Move the reader to the text.      Move the text to the reader.

Preserves cultural specificity.   Maximizes accessibility.
"Feels like a translation."       "Reads like original English."

Examples:                         Examples:
Nabokov's Pushkin (hyper-         Fagles' Homer (contemporary
literal, extensive notes)         American idiom)

Engineering analogy:
  Lossless compression            Lossy compression
  (all source information         (optimized for target medium,
   preserved, may be harder       some fidelity sacrificed for
   to use downstream)             usability)

The "untranslatability" problem:
  German Weltanschauung, Japanese mono no aware,
  Sanskrit dharma, Arabic tarab — terms for which
  no English equivalent exists. Translators choose:
  borrow the word (foreignization) or approximate
  with a phrase (domestication).
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Literature" vs. "Fiction" vs. "Text"**
Literature: institutionally recognized verbal art. Fiction: narrative that asserts counterfactuals. Text: any written document. They overlap but are not synonymous — historical writing is not fiction; a lease is a text but not literature; a lyric poem is literature but not fiction.

**Genre labels are retrospective impositions**
Homer did not call the Iliad an "epic" — that category was built by later critics. Genre labels describe after the fact; they do not determine what a work is.

**Formalism in literary theory vs. formalism in mathematics**
"Formalism" in literary theory means attending to the form of the text (New Criticism, Russian Formalism, structuralism). It does not mean proof-based. It means text-focused rather than context-focused. The structural/linguistic formalism of Jakobson and Propp is genuinely formal in the mathematical sense; New Criticism's "formalism" is more loosely methodological.

**Structuralism in literature is Saussure applied**
Levi-Strauss applied Saussurean structural linguistics (sign as arbitrary relationship between signifier and signified, language as a system of differences) to myth. Jakobson applied it to poetry. The Chomsky connection: Harris (Chomsky's advisor) worked on distributional analysis; Chomsky's transformational grammar is both a continuation of and a departure from the structuralist program. Chomsky and the literary structuralists were working in the same intellectual milieu.

**The canon is not "the best books"**
It is the books that institutional infrastructure — universities, publishers, prizes — has made available and valorized. This does not mean the books are bad, only that selection is not neutral.
