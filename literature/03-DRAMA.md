# Drama

## The Big Picture

Drama is the literary mode in which characters speak and act before an audience (real or imagined). It exists at the intersection of literature and performance — the text is a script for enactment, not a work designed for private reading.

```

+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Layer 1: Aristotle's Poetics — The Formal Analysis

The **Poetics** (c. 335 BCE) is the foundational formal analysis of drama. It is not a prescriptive rulebook — Aristotle is analyzing existing practice (primarily Sophocles' Oedipus Rex). But it was read as prescriptive by Renaissance theorists, producing the "neoclassical rules."

### The Six Elements of Tragedy (in descending importance)

```
ARISTOTLE'S RANKING OF TRAGIC ELEMENTS
----------------------------------------
1. MYTHOS (plot/action) — "the soul of tragedy"
   The arrangement of events; not just what happens but
   HOW events are ordered and connected by necessity/probability.
   Plot > character because we can have plot without character
   but not character without plot (at the dramatic level).

2. ETHOS (character/moral disposition)
   Characters have moral qualities that drive action.
   Not psychological depth in the modern sense — ethical type.

3. DIANOIA (thought/reasoning)
   What characters think and argue; the intellectual content.
   Related to rhetoric: how characters make arguments.

4. LEXIS (diction/language)
   The actual words used. Style, register, meter (iambic trimeter
   for dialogue, various lyric meters for choral odes).

5. MELOPOEIA (song/music)
   The musical element. Choral odes were sung and danced.
   Greek tragedy was a total audiovisual spectacle.

6. OPSIS (spectacle/visual)
   Costumes, masks, stage machinery, visual effects.
   Aristotle ranks this LAST — "least connected to the art of
   poetry" — because it depends on production, not writing.

   Note: this ranking is literary-centric. From a performance
   studies perspective (Schechner, Turner) opsis is central.
```

### The Key Technical Terms

**Hamartia** — mistranslated for centuries as "tragic flaw" (moral failing). The Greek word means "error" or "missing the mark" (from archery). It is a mistake — of judgment, knowledge, or action — not a character defect. Oedipus's error is not pride; it is his ignorance of his own origins combined with actions that (necessarily, by the logic of fate) bring about what he feared.

```
HAMARTIA — CORRECT UNDERSTANDING
----------------------------------
Not: moral flaw that deserves punishment
Yes: an error in action or judgment, often from ignorance

Why this matters:
  The tragic hero is not being punished for vice.
  Tragedy requires a good person falling through error.
  A wicked person getting just deserts is not tragedy —
  it is just punishment narrative.

Aristotle's requirement: the hero must be "intermediate"
  — not completely good (fall would be unjust and shocking)
  — not completely evil (fall would be satisfying, not pitiful)
  → Someone good, who makes an error, whose fall is undeserved
```

**Anagnorisis** — recognition or discovery. The moment when the hero (or another character) passes from ignorance to knowledge.

```
ANAGNORISIS TYPES (Aristotle identifies several)
-------------------------------------------------
Simple: recognition of a person (Odysseus and his scar)
Complex: recognition combined with peripeteia (see below)
Best: hero recognizes own identity or the consequences
      of their own actions
      — Oedipus discovers he IS the murderer he sought
      — This is the model case: the recognition IS the reversal

The best anagnorisis is a structural device: it produces
both knowledge and reversal simultaneously.
```

**Peripeteia** — reversal of fortune. The moment when the action turns in the opposite direction from what was expected.

```
PERIPETEIA
----------
Not just any change in fortune.
Specifically: an action done in one expectation
              produces the OPPOSITE result.

The Messenger in Oedipus Rex comes to bring good news:
  He will tell Oedipus his father (Polybus) has died
  naturally, so Oedipus need not fear the prophecy.
  Instead, he reveals that Oedipus was adopted —
  and this information destroys him.

Best tragedy (Aristotle): anagnorisis and peripeteia
are simultaneous. One action produces recognition
and reversal at once. Maximum efficiency.
```

**Catharsis** — the famous contested term. Most likely: purgation or purification of the emotions of pity and fear that tragedy arouses.

```
CATHARSIS — THE DEBATE
-----------------------
1. Medical interpretation (purgation)
   Tragedy arouses pity and fear and then purges them.
   Aristotle's Politics uses "catharsis" medically for
   relief of pathological emotional excess.
   → Tragedy is therapeutic.

2. Intellectual interpretation (clarification)
   Catharsis as cognitive clarity — we come to understand
   the emotions of pity and fear through their artistic
   representation.
   → Tragedy is educational.

3. Social interpretation (civic function)
   Tragedy channels dangerous emotions into safe performance.
   The Athenian democracy needs its citizens to feel pity
   and appropriate fear — tragedy trains these emotions.
   → Tragedy is civic ritual.

All three interpretations have textual support.
The ambiguity is genuine — Aristotle's surviving Poetics
is incomplete (possibly lecture notes); the promised
treatment of comedy is lost.
```

### The Unities

The **three unities** (time, place, action) are a Renaissance interpretation of Aristotle, not exactly what he said:

```
WHAT ARISTOTLE ACTUALLY SAID
------------------------------
Unity of action: YES — Aristotle insists on this.
  The plot must be unified: one complete action with
  beginning, middle, end, connected by necessity/probability.
  Subplots should serve the main action.

Unity of time: SUGGESTED — he notes tragedy tries to fit
  within one sun's circuit (i.e., one day) where possible.
  This is observational, not prescriptive.

Unity of place: NOT MENTIONED by Aristotle at all.

WHAT NEOCLASSICISM DID (16th-18th c.)
  Italian and French critics (Castelvetro, Chapelain,
  Boileau) codified all three as mandatory rules.
  This is what Shakespeare was said to violate and
  why French neoclassical drama is set in a single room.
```

## Layer 2: Greek Tragedy — Structural Analysis

### The Five-Part Structure

```
STRUCTURAL ANATOMY OF A GREEK TRAGEDY
---------------------------------------

1. PROLOGOS (prologue)
   Spoken before chorus enters.
   Establishes context, setting, situation.
   Often a dialogue between characters.
   (In Oedipus Rex: Oedipus addresses the Thebans
   about the plague; Creon returns with oracle's answer)

2. PARODOS (entry ode)
   Chorus enters the orchestra (dancing floor) singing.
   Establishes the chorus's identity and emotional state.
   Sets the civic/communal context.

3. EPEISODION + STASIMON (alternating) — usually 3-5 cycles
   EPEISODION: a "dramatic episode" — actors speak, action advances
   STASIMON: choral ode sung and danced between episodes
             Chorus reflects on the action, provides moral context,
             invokes gods, comments on fate

4. EXODOS (exit ode)
   Final scene: resolution, lamentation, or departure.
   Chorus exits singing.
   Sometimes includes final proclamation of meaning.

ORCHESTRA: circular dancing floor where chorus performs
SKENE: stage building; actors perform in front of it
THEATRON: seating area (literally "viewing place")

The physical geometry:
  Chorus in orchestra = community perspective
  Actors on/near skene = individual action
  Audience in theatron = civic witness
```

### The Chorus

The chorus is the most alien element of Greek tragedy for modern audiences. It is not decoration:

```
CHORUS FUNCTIONS
-----------------
1. Civic identity: The chorus represents the community
   (citizens of Thebes, women of Argos, elders of Corinth).
   It grounds the individual action in collective stakes.

2. Commentary: The stasimon odes interpret the action,
   draw moral lessons, invoke relevant myths and precedents.
   They slow the drama for reflection.

3. Emotional regulation: The chorus performs the emotions
   that tragic action should arouse — models pity, fear,
   awe for the audience.

4. Structural rhythm: Alternating episodes/stasima creates
   a formal rhythm — action/reflection, action/reflection.

5. Historical residue: The chorus is a survival from
   the choral dithyramb, the pre-dramatic hymn to Dionysus
   from which tragedy developed. Aristotle says tragedy
   "evolved from those who led the dithyramb."
```

### The Three Tragedians

| Playwright | Dates | Surviving | Key Features |
|-----------|-------|-----------|--------------|
| Aeschylus | c. 525–456 BCE | 7 tragedies | Introduced 2nd actor; trilogy form (Oresteia); austere language; cosmic theology |
| Sophocles | c. 497–405 BCE | 7 tragedies | 3rd actor; reduced chorus from 50 to 15; psychological focus; Oedipus Rex as model tragedy |
| Euripides | c. 484–406 BCE | 18 tragedies | Realistic characters; psychological complexity; women as protagonists; challenged divine justice |

## Layer 3: Elizabethan Drama

### The Material Conditions

```
THE ELIZABETHAN THEATER (1560s-1640s)
---------------------------------------
Open-air amphitheaters (Globe, Fortune, Rose, Swan)
Thrust stage projecting into yard
Standing "groundlings" in yard (penny admission)
Seated gentry in three tiers of galleries
No scenery (verbal scene-setting in the text)
No artificial lighting (afternoon performances)
All-male company (boys played women's parts)
Repertory system (company performs many plays weekly)
No long runs — new plays constantly

CONSEQUENCES FOR THE DRAMA:
  Rich verbal scene-setting ("But soft, what light
  through yonder window breaks" — the actress is not
  in moonlight; Romeo describes it into existence)
  Soliloquy as direct audience address (possible
  because thrust stage = 270-degree contact)
  Multiple locations in quick succession (no scenery
  to change — cut to the next scene immediately)
  Verbal doubling of action: characters describe
  what they're doing as they do it
```

### Shakespeare: Structural Principles

```
SHAKESPEAREAN FIVE-ACT STRUCTURE (roughly)
-------------------------------------------
Act I:    Exposition; world established; inciting incident
Act II:   Rising action; complications develop
Act III:  Climax or pivot; major reversal or decision
Act IV:   Falling action; consequences unfold
Act V:    Resolution (death and order restored; or marriage)

This is not Aristotle's structure — it is a 5-act
version developed by Roman dramatists (Horace, Terence)
and codified by Renaissance editors.

MIXING GENRES
Shakespeare routinely mixes tragic and comic:
  Comedy: Falstaff scenes in Henry IV
  Tragedy: Ophelia's mad scene in Hamlet
  Romance: late plays (Winter's Tale, Tempest) blend all

The "problem plays" (Measure for Measure, All's Well,
Troilus) are uncomfortable in all categories.
```

## Layer 4: Modern Drama — The Naturalist Revolution and Its Rebellions

### Ibsen and Naturalism

Henrik Ibsen's mature plays (1879–1899) created modern drama by:

```
IBSEN'S INNOVATIONS
--------------------
1. The well-made play structure turned against itself
   The well-made play (Scribe, 1820s-50s) was a mechanical
   formula: careful exposition, building complication,
   obligatory scene, denouement. Ibsen used the form but
   loaded it with genuine psychological consequence.

2. Retrospective exposition
   Ibsen's plays are full of secrets — the action is
   about REVEALING the past, not building future action.
   "The past never stays past." (Ghosts, Hedda Gabler)

3. Dialogue as subtext
   Characters don't say what they mean. What's NOT said
   matters as much as what is said. Chekhov took this
   further (the famous "Chekhov gun" is a plot mechanism;
   his actual achievement is the opposite — loaded guns
   that don't fire because life doesn't resolve).

4. Social critique through domestic drama
   The family home as the site of oppression:
   A Doll's House (1879) — Nora's famous door-slam
   was the sound of bourgeois marriage's critique
```

### Brecht: Alienation and the Epic Theater

Brecht's theatrical theory is the most intellectually sophisticated and the most directly relevant to a reader with background in formal systems:

```
BRECHT'S VERFREMDUNGSEFFEKT (V-Effekt)
"Alienation effect" or "estrangement effect"

GOAL: prevent the audience from losing itself in the
      illusion of the performance. Keep them thinking.

Aristotelian theater:    Brechtian theater:
  Empathy                  Distance
  Identification           Observation
  Catharsis                Judgment
  Illusion maintained      Illusion disrupted
  "This is happening now"  "This is being shown to you"

DEVICES TO PRODUCE V-EFFEKT:
  Direct address to audience (break fourth wall)
  Songs that interrupt action and comment on it
  Placards announcing scenes before they happen
  Visible stage machinery (don't hide the apparatus)
  Actors stepping outside their characters
  Incongruous historical costumes in realistic settings
  Narration of what we are about to see, then seeing it

FORMAL ANALOGY (for the TCS reader):
  Brecht wants you to see the frame, not just the content.
  Like knowing you're reading a formal proof vs. being lost
  in the narrative of the proof.
  Or: seeing the HTTP headers, not just the webpage.
  The apparatus is foregrounded, not hidden.
  This is the opposite of "seamless UX" — it is a
  deliberate anti-immersive design philosophy.

WHY BRECHT DID THIS:
  Political purpose — he wanted audiences to think about
  the social conditions that produce the situations on stage,
  not to feel temporarily moved and then return home unchanged.
  "Don't weep; change the world."
```

### Theatre of the Absurd: Ionesco and Beckett

```
THEATRE OF THE ABSURD (1950s-60s)
----------------------------------
Not a movement with a manifesto — Martin Esslin's
retrospective label (The Theatre of the Absurd, 1961)
for plays sharing a set of formal and thematic features.

FORMAL FEATURES:
  Circular or non-progressive structure
  Language that fails to communicate
  Characters who cannot leave but don't know why
  Rituals repeated for no clear reason
  No psychological explanation for behavior
  No exposition in the conventional sense

THEMATIC FEATURES:
  Existence without purpose or justification (post-WWII)
  Language as inadequate to meaning
  Waiting, habit, and repetition as the human condition

WAITING FOR GODOT (Beckett, 1952):
  Two tramps wait for Godot, who never comes.
  Each act ends identically ("Shall we go?" "Yes, let's go."
  They do not move.)
  Nothing happens, twice (Vivian Mercier's famous review)
  The form IS the content: the circular non-structure
  embodies the meaninglessness it depicts.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Tragic flaw" is a mistranslation and distortion**
The "tragic flaw" interpretation of hamartia (via A.C. Bradley's Shakespearean Tragedy, 1904) made hamartia a moral defect that "deserves" the downfall. Aristotle says the opposite: the hero must be good — not exceptionally virtuous, but fundamentally decent — so that the fall produces pity, not satisfaction.

**Catharsis is not "emotional release for its own sake"**
The pop psychology version of catharsis — cry at a sad movie, feel better — is not what Aristotle meant. The Greek medical usage involves purification or proper functioning, not mere venting.

**The three unities are not Aristotle's rules**
Aristotle required only unity of action. Unity of time and unity of place were imposed by Renaissance critics (Castelvetro 1570, etc.). French neoclassical drama followed these strict unities; Shakespeare ignored them. This produced a centuries-long debate about whether Shakespeare was therefore "barbaric" (Voltaire, roughly) or superior (Goethe, Romantics).

**Brecht's V-Effekt is not just "anti-realism"**
Anti-realism includes symbolism, expressionism, surrealism — all kinds of non-literal theater. The V-Effekt is specifically about making the THEATRICAL APPARATUS visible, not just departing from realistic representation. A symbolist play may be non-realistic but is still designed to immerse you. Brecht wants you to remain outside, watching.
