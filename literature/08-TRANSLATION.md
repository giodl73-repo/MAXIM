# Translation Theory and Practice

## The Big Picture

Translation is the transfer of a text from one language (source) to another (target). Translation theory asks: what is preserved, what is lost, and by what principles should these decisions be made? The field has a genuine intellectual history, productive debates, and direct connections to linguistic theory.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
```

## Layer 1: The Core Dichotomy — Foreignization vs. Domestication

### The Historical Argument

Friedrich Schleiermacher laid out the fundamental choice in his 1813 lecture "On the Different Methods of Translation":

```
SCHLEIERMACHER'S FORMULATION
------------------------------
"There are only two methods of translation:
  Either the translator leaves the writer in peace as much
  as possible and moves the reader toward him;
  or he leaves the reader in peace as much as possible
  and moves the writer toward the reader."

FOREIGNIZATION:
  Leave the foreign in place.
  The reader feels they are reading a translation.
  The strangeness of the source culture is preserved.
  The target language is "bent" by the source.
  "The reader must be willing to make the journey."

DOMESTICATION:
  Adapt to the target culture.
  The reader forgets they are reading a translation.
  Source culture peculiarities are naturalized or removed.
  "The text speaks as if originally written in English."

Engineering analogy:
  Foreignization = lossless format (preserves all source
    information; may be harder to use in target context)
  Domestication = lossy format (optimized for target;
    some source information sacrificed for usability)

NEITHER IS "WRONG" — they serve different purposes:
  A scholarly text of Homer for classicists → foreignize
  A children's Homer for 10-year-olds → domesticate
  A verse translation for the poetry reader → complex trade
```

### Eugene Nida: Formal vs. Dynamic Equivalence

Nida (working on Bible translation) formalized domestication into a principled theory:

```
NIDA'S EQUIVALENCE THEORY (1964)
----------------------------------
Formal equivalence:
  Match the source structure — word for word, phrase for
  phrase, form for form.
  Preserves: syntax, idioms, proper names, even word order
  Problem: reads unnaturally in target language; may be
           opaque to target readers

Dynamic equivalence (later called "functional equivalence"):
  Match the EFFECT on the reader.
  "The closest natural equivalent to the source-language
  message, first in terms of meaning and secondly in
  terms of style."
  Preserves: the communication act, the reader's experience
  Changes: syntax, idioms, cultural references as needed

Example:
  Greek: "Greet one another with a holy kiss." (Romans 16:16)
  Formal: literal (may puzzle modern Western readers)
  Dynamic: "Welcome one another warmly in Christian love."
           (removes the culturally specific gesture; preserves the meaning)

  But critics of dynamic equivalence argue:
  The "holy kiss" is not an arbitrary vehicle for a
  universal meaning. Changing it loses the cultural
  specificity that is part of the message.
  → Foreignization argument re-enters.
```

### Lawrence Venuti: The Translator's Invisibility

Venuti's **The Translator's Invisibility** (1995) is the key contemporary argument:

```
VENUTI'S THESIS
---------------
In the anglophone world, the dominant norm for
translation is FLUENCY — translations should read
as if not translated at all. The translator should
be "invisible."

Why this is problematic:
1. The fluent translation conceals the translator's
   labor and the foreignness of the source.
2. It enforces the TARGET culture's values on the
   source text — a form of cultural imperialism.
3. It allows the target culture to consume the world's
   literature without having to encounter its foreignness.

Venuti advocates RESISTANT translation:
  Produce a translation that signals its foreign origin.
  Use archaism, neologism, heterogeneous registers.
  Make the translator visible.
  Force the reader to acknowledge they are reading
  a translation from another culture.

The political dimension:
  The global dominance of English means that
  English-language texts rarely get translated
  (why bother? everyone reads English), while
  everything gets translated INTO English.
  The translation economy is asymmetric.
  Domestication reinforces this asymmetry.
```

## Layer 2: Walter Benjamin on Translation

Benjamin's "The Task of the Translator" (1923, preface to his Baudelaire translation) is the most philosophically ambitious essay on translation:

```
BENJAMIN'S ARGUMENT
--------------------
Translation is not about transferring meaning.
It is about revealing the "pure language" (reine Sprache)
that underlies all languages but is fully present in none.

Individual languages are fragments of a whole:
  German "Brot" and French "pain" are not equivalent
  (they carry different cultural meanings), yet they
  "intend" the same thing — the object bread.
  Together they point toward the pure language where
  "bread" would be fully expressible.

The best translation:
  Not the one that sounds most natural in the target.
  Not even the one that best captures the source meaning.
  But the one that reveals the relationship between
  languages — the one that makes the original resonate
  with new meaning through the foreignizing light of
  the translation.

"A translation that sets out to transmit is doomed."
The translation must aspire to something the original
does not contain — the survival (Fortleben) of the
original's after-life in another tongue.

Benjamin is often read as advocating hyper-literal
translation (he praises interlinear translations).
But his point is philosophical: translation reveals
that the original is itself already a fragment of
a language no text fully expresses.
```

## Layer 3: The Problem of Untranslatability

Barbara Cassin's **Dictionary of Untranslatables** (2004, English translation 2014) catalogues terms that resist translation — not because translation is impossible but because the translation problem reveals something philosophically important.

```
UNTRANSLATABLE TERMS — SELECTED EXAMPLES
------------------------------------------
GERMAN
  Weltanschauung: "worldview" — but "world" + "viewing"
    implies a phenomenological relationship to the world
    that "worldview" (a transparent compound) misses.
  Bildung: cultivation/formation/education — but none
    captures the specific Hegelian process of self-formation
    through encountering the other.
  Geist: spirit/mind/ghost/intellect — Hegel's "Geist"
    requires all four and none alone is adequate.
  Stimmung: mood/atmosphere/attunement — used in music
    (tuning), in architecture (ambiance), in Heidegger
    (fundamental attunement). No English equivalent covers all.

JAPANESE
  Mono no aware: the pathos of things, bittersweet
    transience, the poignant beauty of impermanence.
    Has no English equivalent. The concept itself is
    culturally specific — a particular Japanese
    aesthetic/philosophical orientation.
  Ma: the pause, the space between, the interval.
    In music, architecture, conversation. The meaningful
    emptiness. "Negative space" is architectural but
    not the same.
  Amae: dependence/presumed benevolence — the reliance
    on another's goodwill that structures Japanese
    social relationships (Doi). Untranslatable because
    the concept describes a social pattern not foregrounded
    in Western psychology.

ARABIC
  Tarab: musical enchantment, the emotional transport
    produced by great music. More specific than "ecstasy";
    culturally embedded in Arab musical tradition.
  Wasta: connections/influence/who you know.
    Social capital exercised through personal networks.
    "Networking" is too neutral; "nepotism" is too negative.

PORTUGUESE
  Saudade: longing for something lost, absent, or perhaps
    never possessed. Richer than "nostalgia" (which is
    specifically about the past); saudade includes longing
    for the unattained and the present-but-absent.

WHAT UNTRANSLATABLES REVEAL:
  Concepts are not universal with different local labels.
  Languages carve up conceptual space differently.
  This is the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis in a mild form:
  available concepts shape (not determine) thought.
  Translation of untranslatables is ALWAYS interpretation.
```

## Layer 4: Poetry Translation

Poetry is the hardest translation problem because form and meaning are inseparable:

```
THE POETRY TRANSLATION PROBLEM
--------------------------------
What must be preserved? (You cannot preserve everything)

  MEANING (semantic content)
  SOUND (rhythm, rhyme, assonance, meter)
  FORM (fixed forms: sonnet, terza rima, haiku)
  TONE (register, irony, intimacy)
  CULTURAL CONTEXT (allusions, intertextual echoes)
  WORD ORDER AND SYNTAX

These often conflict:
  The word that rhymes may not have the right meaning.
  The meter may require a word that carries wrong connotations.
  The cultural allusion has no equivalent.

TRANSLATION STRATEGIES FOR POETRY
  1. Verse translation (aims to reproduce poetic form
     in the target language — may sacrifice meaning)
  2. Prose translation (sacrifices form, keeps meaning;
     scholarly standard for unfamiliar languages)
  3. Imitation (free recreation; admits departure)
  4. Adaptation (resets the poem in new cultural context)
  5. Interlinear (word-for-word, for study)

FAMOUS TRANSLATION DEBATES
  Nabokov vs. Arndt on Pushkin:
    Nabokov insisted on hyper-literal prose translation
    with extensive notes (foreignization to the extreme):
    "the transparency of a windowpane, not the opaque
    elegance of a painted glass."
    Edmund Wilson disagreed publicly. Their quarrel
    became famous in literary culture.

  Lattimore vs. Fagles on Homer:
    Lattimore (1950s): archaic, formal, close to Greek
    Fagles (1990s): contemporary idiom, more readable
    Both are excellent; they serve different readers.

  The Aeneid in English:
    Dryden's Aeneid (1697): heroic couplets, rhetorical
    Mandelbaum's (1972): blank verse, lyrical
    Fitzgerald's (1983): dignified blank verse
    Ferry's (2017): modern blank verse
    Each is a different translation philosophy.
```

## Layer 5: Machine Translation

```
MACHINE TRANSLATION — HISTORY AND LIMITS
------------------------------------------
Rule-based MT (1950s-1990s):
  Encode grammar rules and lexicons.
  Translate by applying rules.
  Worked for limited domains; failed on idiom,
  ambiguity, pragmatics.

Statistical MT (1990s-2010s):
  Train on parallel corpora (translated pairs).
  Find most probable translation given statistical
  patterns in the training data.
  IBM models, Moses system.

Neural MT / seq2seq (2014+):
  RNNs, then Transformers (2017).
  Much better at fluency; handles long-range dependencies.
  Google Translate, DeepL.

Current state:
  For languages with large parallel corpora and
  similar structure (French/Spanish/English): very good.
  For low-resource language pairs: degrades significantly.
  For literary text: adequate for sense; poor for nuance.

WHAT CURRENT NMT CANNOT DO:
  Translate untranslatables (it has to pick something)
  Preserve tone and register reliably
  Handle culture-specific allusions
  Maintain formal constraints of poetry
  Translate irony and ambiguity (it resolves them)
  Handle creative neologism
  Recognize that a text is intentionally strange
    (mis-translates deliberate obscurity as error)

The fundamental limit:
  NMT learns to predict fluent target-language output.
  Literary translation requires the opposite of fluency
  in some cases — it requires the "grain" of the source.
  A model trained to be fluent will systematically
  domesticate every translation.

Venuti's critique applies perfectly to NMT:
  The translator is maximally invisible.
  The foreignness is maximally erased.
  The model's training objective IS domestication.
```

## Layer 6: Translation and Canon Formation

```
TRANSLATION AND THE GLOBAL LITERARY SYSTEM
--------------------------------------------
Pascale Casanova "The World Republic of Letters" (2004):
  There is a global literary field with a center
  (Paris, then increasingly New York/London) and
  periphery. Prestige flows from the center.

  Translation into French or English = consecration.
  A text not translated = a text that does not exist
  for the global literary market.

The statistics:
  US/UK publish ~3% of books in translation.
  Many European countries publish 30-50%.
  The "translation gap" means the anglophone world
  consumes less of world literature than most.

WHAT GETS TRANSLATED:
  What international prizes certify (Nobel, Man Booker Int.)
  What English-language publishers think will sell
  What literary agents can negotiate rights to
  Often: what confirms Western expectations of the source culture

RELAY TRANSLATION:
  Many translations into English are made via an
  intermediate language, not the source.
  African languages → French → English
  Czech or Hungarian → German → English
  Each relay introduces distortion.

CONSEQUENCE FOR WORLD LITERATURE:
  The "world literature" in English translation is
  a curated selection, filtered by the economics and
  aesthetics of the anglophone market.
  Moretti's argument: the world novel that reaches
  the global market is already adapted (domesticated)
  to meet that market's expectations.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Translation is not transcoding**
In information theory, transcoding preserves information by changing format. Translation does not preserve information — it must make choices that change meaning, emphasis, and effect. The question is not "was information lost?" (it always is) but "which losses are acceptable and which principles guide the choices?"

**"Faithful" is ambiguous**
A translation can be faithful to: the word-for-word surface, the meaning, the effect on the reader, the cultural context, the sound, the form. These often conflict. Nabokov's "faithful" translation of Pushkin is word-for-word but loses all poetic effect — is that faithful?

**Untranslatability is a degree, not a binary**
Almost everything can be translated with enough explanation. The question is what the translation costs — how much explanation is needed, how much the feel of the original is lost. "Untranslatable" usually means "resistant to brief, idiomatic translation" not "impossible to convey in another language."

**Machine translation is not just "fast human translation"**
Human translation involves understanding and interpretation — what does this text mean, in this context, for this purpose? Neural MT produces probable sequences given training data patterns. For routine, formulaic, high-resource-language text, this works well. For literary text, it systematically fails in ways that are characteristic of its training objective (fluency maximization), not random.
