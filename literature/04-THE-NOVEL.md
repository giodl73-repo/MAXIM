# The Novel

## The Big Picture

The novel is the dominant literary form of modernity. It emerged in the 18th century, became the principal medium of serious literary culture in the 19th, and fragmented into multiple competing forms in the 20th. It is also the form most directly tied to the social, economic, and technological changes of modernity — print, literacy, the reading public, individualism, capitalism.

## Layer 1: What Is a Novel?

The question sounds trivial but has generated serious disagreement:

```
COMPETING DEFINITIONS
---------------------
Ian Watt (The Rise of the Novel, 1957):
  A novel is characterized by "formal realism" —
  commitment to representing particular individuals
  in particular times and places. This requires:
  - Individuated characters (not types)
  - Specific spatial setting (not "a forest" but this house)
  - Specific temporal setting (not "once upon a time" but 1720)
  - Ordinary language (not elevated poetic diction)

M.M. Bakhtin (The Dialogic Imagination, 1981):
  The novel is essentially POLYPHONIC — it incorporates
  multiple voices, languages, social registers, discourses.
  Unlike poetry (which strives for a single unified voice),
  the novel is inherently double-voiced, dialogic.
  Every utterance in a novel is aware of other utterances.

Georg Lukacs (The Theory of the Novel, 1920):
  The novel is the epic of a world abandoned by God.
  It narrates the search of a "problematic individual"
  for authentic values in a world that no longer provides them.
  The epic hero fits the world; the novel hero doesn't.

Practical working definition:
  Long prose fiction (50,000+ words) with individuated
  characters and a causal plot. Flexible enough to include
  epistolary novels, stream-of-consciousness, anti-novels.
```

## Layer 2: The Rise of the Novel (18th Century)

### The Material Preconditions

```
WHY THE NOVEL EMERGED IN 18TH-CENTURY ENGLAND
----------------------------------------------
1. Printing press (1450s) → cheap print by 1700s
2. Literacy rising, especially among women and middle classes
3. Leisure time + disposable income for the middle class
4. Circulating libraries (by 1740s) = access without purchase
5. Urban culture creates isolated individuals who need to
   imagine their way into other lives and social situations

The novel SERVES the new reading public:
  - Represents their world (middle-class domestic life)
  - Validates their values (moral seriousness, individual
    conscience, inner life as worthy of attention)
  - Provides entertainment in private, solitary reading

Compare: Epic serves aristocratic warrior culture.
         Novel serves bourgeois domestic culture.
```

### Foundational British Novels

| Novel | Author | Date | Innovation |
|-------|--------|------|-----------|
| Robinson Crusoe | Defoe | 1719 | First "formal realism" — specific details of island survival as documentation of psychological self-sufficiency |
| Pamela | Richardson | 1740 | Epistolary novel; female interiority as subject; virtue in conflict with power |
| Clarissa | Richardson | 1748 | Massive (1 million words); tragic epistolary; rape and psychological consequence |
| Tom Jones | Fielding | 1749 | Omniscient narrator; comic irony; "classic" structure with reflexive commentary |
| Tristram Shandy | Sterne | 1759–67 | Self-conscious metafiction before the term existed; time manipulation; digressions as structure |
| Pride and Prejudice | Austen | 1813 | Free indirect discourse perfected; social comedy as moral analysis |

### Free Indirect Discourse

One of the most important technical developments of the novel:

```
FREE INDIRECT DISCOURSE (FID)
------------------------------
The technique for representing inner thought without
using quotation marks or "she thought that..."

DIRECT THOUGHT:
  "She thought, 'He is so disagreeable.'"

INDIRECT THOUGHT:
  "She thought that he was disagreeable."

FREE INDIRECT DISCOURSE:
  "He was so disagreeable."

The third version borrows the character's language and
perspective without the grammatical subordination.
The narrator's voice merges temporarily with the
character's consciousness.

JANE AUSTEN's mastery:
  "It was a truth universally acknowledged that a single
  man in possession of a good fortune must be in want
  of a wife."
  — Is this a narrator's irony? The community's belief?
    Mrs. Bennet's worldview? All three simultaneously.
    FID makes all these coexist in one sentence.

FID is the technical precursor to stream of consciousness.
```

## Layer 3: The Realist Novel (19th Century)

### What Realism Means

```
REALISM — TECHNICAL DEFINITION
--------------------------------
Not: "looks like reality" (all art looks like something)
Yes: a program for representing social totality through
     the representation of particular individuals in
     particular socioeconomic circumstances.

Georg Lukacs (the most important theorist of realism):
  The great realist novel grasps "the type" — a character
  who is simultaneously an individual and a historically
  representative social figure. Not a sociological average
  but an individual who crystallizes historical forces.

  Balzac's Rastignac (Pere Goriot) is a particular young
  man AND the embodiment of social ambition in the new
  capitalist order.

  Tolstoy's Pierre and Andrei are particular people AND
  the collision of old and new values in Russia.

The realist novel IMPLIES a world: every character's
action assumes a social world dense enough to resist
or enable them. This is "social totality" — the sense
that the world is a structured, historically specific
whole, not just a backdrop.
```

### Key Realist Texts

| Novel | Author | Country | Achievement |
|-------|--------|---------|------------|
| Pere Goriot | Balzac | France | "Type" character; social totality; Vautrin as embodiment of criminal capitalism |
| Madame Bovary | Flaubert | France | Style as content; free indirect discourse; systematic irony; "Bovaryism" (romantic self-delusion) |
| War and Peace | Tolstoy | Russia | Largest canvas; historical novel; antiheroic realism; Napoleon seen from below |
| Anna Karenina | Tolstoy | Russia | Parallel plots; fatal logic of passion vs. duty |
| Middlemarch | Eliot | England | "The Web" — interconnected lives; moral intelligence; sympathy as epistemology |
| The Portrait of a Lady | James | USA | Limited perspective; consciousness as drama; the "central consciousness" technique |

### Henry James and the Point-of-View Revolution

```
HENRY JAMES'S CONTRIBUTION
---------------------------
James developed the "limited central consciousness" —
the story is filtered entirely through a single character's
perception and understanding.

Not just first-person narration (which has a narrator).
Not omniscient narration (which can go anywhere).
Third-person LIMITED: we see only what the central
character can observe or infer. The reader's ignorance
equals the character's ignorance.

"Showing" vs. "Telling" (James's doctrine):
  Don't tell us the character is proud.
  Show us an action that reveals pride.
  The reader infers the quality from the scene.

CONSEQUENCE FOR NARRATIVE THEORY:
  Genette's "internal focalization" (from 01-NARRATIVE-THEORY)
  is a formalization of what James practiced.
  "The center of consciousness" = the focalizer.
```

## Layer 4: Modernist Novel

The modernist novel (c. 1900–1940) broke with realism's formal conventions — not to deny reality but to find forms adequate to a new understanding of consciousness, time, and subjectivity.

```
MODERNIST FORMAL INNOVATIONS
------------------------------
1. STREAM OF CONSCIOUSNESS
   Representing the continuous flow of thought and
   sensation as it occurs, without narrative organization.
   William James coined the phrase (Principles of Psychology,
   1890); Bergson's "duration" provides philosophy.

   Woolf's Mrs. Dalloway: a single day; thoughts interrupt
   narration; past and present interpenetrate.
   Joyce's Ulysses: one day in Dublin; 18 chapters, each
   with different stylistic technique.

2. SPATIAL FORM (Joseph Frank, 1945)
   Rather than temporal sequence, modernist novels are
   organized spatially — juxtaposed images and motifs
   build meaning by relation, not by causal sequence.
   Like a poem, not a story.

3. MYTHIC METHOD (T.S. Eliot on Ulysses)
   Using ancient myth as structural scaffold for modern
   content. Ulysses maps Dublin onto the Odyssey.
   Eliot called this "the mythical method" — a way of
   giving "a shape and a significance to the immense
   panorama of futility and anarchy which is contemporary
   history."

4. UNRELIABLE NARRATION
   See 01-NARRATIVE-THEORY. The Sound and the Fury:
   four different narrators of the same events, each with
   different cognitive and emotional access.
```

## Layer 5: Postmodern Novel

```
POSTMODERN FICTION — KEY MOVES
--------------------------------
1. METAFICTION
   Fiction that is self-consciously aware of being fiction.
   "You are about to begin reading..." (Calvino)
   The author intrudes. The apparatus is visible.
   (Compare Brecht's V-Effekt — same move in theater)

2. INTERTEXTUALITY (Kristeva, Barthes)
   All texts are woven from other texts.
   Postmodern fiction foregrounds this weaving.
   Pynchon: Gravity's Rainbow is a palimpsest of references
   to WWII technology, film, Tarot, rocket science, German
   colonialism, paranoia theory.

3. HISTORIOGRAPHIC METAFICTION (Linda Hutcheon)
   Fiction that explicitly engages with and rewrites history.
   Questions the distinction between fiction and history.
   (Beloved, The French Lieutenant's Woman, Midnight's Children)

4. PASTICHE vs. PARODY (Jameson)
   Modernism used parody (imitation with critical intent).
   Postmodernism uses pastiche (imitation without critique)
   because there is no stable "original" to critique.

5. THE DEATH OF THE AUTHOR (Barthes, 1967)
   The author's intention does not determine meaning.
   The text is a space where multiple meanings coexist.
   The "author-god" who controls meaning is a fiction.
   → This liberates the reader as producer of meaning.
   → It also collapses the evaluative hierarchy: any
     reading is as valid as any other (contested).
```

## Layer 6: The World Novel

```
THE WORLD NOVEL — KEY CONCEPT
------------------------------
Franco Moretti "Conjectures on World Literature" (2000):
  The novel spread globally as a form with a history:
  a form developed in England, Germany, France, then
  adopted by writers elsewhere who had to negotiate
  between the imported form and local content.
  Result: a "compromise between a western formal
  influence... and local materials."

  This is not cultural imperialism in a simple sense —
  it is a creative negotiation. Achebe rewrites Conrad.
  Garcia Marquez fuses European modernism with Colombian
  oral tradition. Rushdie fuses Dickens with Bollywood
  with Islamic history.

MAGICAL REALISM
  Not just "magic in realistic settings."
  A response to a specific condition: Latin American
  and postcolonial societies where the "irrational"
  (indigenous cosmology, plantation history, political
  violence) is as real as the bourgeois-rational.
  Garcia Marquez's world where the dead walk is the world
  of Caribbean plantation history, where the systematic
  irrationality of colonialism is the normal condition.

KEY WORLD NOVEL WRITERS
  Chinua Achebe (Nigeria): Things Fall Apart — uses the novel
    form itself to contest the colonial narrative it was
    delivered in.
  Ngugi wa Thiong'o (Kenya): shifted to writing in Gikuyu —
    the question of which language the novel speaks is itself
    political.
  Orhan Pamuk (Turkey): My Name is Red — formal complexity
    (multiple narrators including murdered victims) exploring
    Ottoman art and Western influence.
  Mo Yan (China): Red Sorghum, Frog — magic realism, Chinese
    oral tradition, political history.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Realism" does not mean "accurate description"**
The realist novel does not just describe the world as it is. It produces a structured representation that claims to capture social totality through particular individuals. The "reality effect" (Roland Barthes) is achieved through the accumulation of apparently irrelevant details — the barometer on the wall in Flaubert — that signal "this is real." The details are chosen; the appearance of completeness is constructed.

**Modernism is not anti-realism for its own sake**
The stream of consciousness, fragmentation, and unreliable narration of modernist fiction are not arbitrary difficulty or elitism. They are attempts to find forms adequate to a changed understanding of consciousness (post-Freud), time (post-Bergson), and the nature of social reality (post-WWI). The form is the argument.

**"Stream of consciousness" is a term from psychology, not literature**
William James used it in his 1890 Principles of Psychology to describe the continuous flow of mental experience. Literary critics adopted it to describe the technique — which is actually several different techniques (interior monologue, free indirect discourse, and the unpunctuated monologue of Molly Bloom's soliloquy are distinct).

**The world novel is not "literature from around the world"**
It is a theoretical concept (associated with Moretti, Casanova, Damrosch) about how the novel as a form circulated globally and how writers in different national contexts negotiated the imported form with local traditions. It involves questions of translation, unequal cultural prestige, and what counts as "universal" in literary value.
