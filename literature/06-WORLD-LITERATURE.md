# World Literature

## The Big Picture

"World literature" is both a descriptive and a theoretical category. Descriptively, it refers to literary production across the world's traditions. Theoretically, it names a set of debates about how literary traditions relate to one another, how texts travel across languages and cultures, and whether there is a meaningful concept of global literary value.

## Sanskrit Kavya and Rasa Theory

Sanskrit literary tradition produced a formal theory of aesthetics — the **rasa theory** — that is one of the most sophisticated formal affect taxonomies in any literary tradition.

### Rasa: Formal Taxonomy of Aesthetic Emotion

Bharata Muni's **Natyashastra** (c. 200 BCE–200 CE) — the foundational treatise on drama and performance — introduced the concept of **rasa** ("juice" or "essence"):

```
RASA THEORY — FORMAL STRUCTURE
--------------------------------
A literary work does not simply depict emotions —
it transforms them into aesthetic experience.

The eight (later nine) rasas:

RASA (permanent emotion)    STHAYI-BHAVA (corresponding emotion)
Shringara (love/beauty)     Rati (love/desire)
Hasya (comic)               Hasa (mirth)
Karuna (compassion/sorrow)  Shoka (sorrow)
Raudra (fury)               Krodha (anger)
Vira (heroic)               Utsaha (heroic energy)
Bhayanaka (fearful/terror)  Bhaya (fear)
Bibhatsa (disgusting)       Jugupsa (revulsion)
Adbhuta (wonder)            Vismaya (astonishment)
Shanta (peace) [added later] Sama (calm/equanimity)

HOW RASA WORKS:
  vibhava (stimulus/determinant) →
  anubhava (consequent expression) +
  vyabhichari-bhava (transient emotions) →
  RASA (aesthetic emotion in the audience)

The theory is COMPOSITIONAL:
  The permanent emotion (sthayi-bhava) is actualized
  when a specific combination of stimuli, expressions,
  and transient emotions is properly arranged.
  This is a formal grammar of emotional effect.

Analogy:
  A typed function signature: the rasa is the return type,
  vibhava/anubhava are the argument types.
  The poet's craft is instantiating the function correctly.
  The audience experiences the return value.
```

### Dhvani Theory (Resonance/Suggestion)

Anandavardhana's **Dhvanyaloka** (c. 850 CE) added the concept of **dhvani** — the suggested or resonant meaning that is more powerful than the literal or secondary:

```
DHVANI — THREE LEVELS OF MEANING
----------------------------------
1. Vacyartha (primary/literal meaning) — what the words say
2. Laksyanartha (secondary/indicated meaning) — what is implied
3. Vyangya/dhvani (suggested/resonant meaning) — what reverberates

Example:
  "The banks of the Ganges are cool."
  Literal: a geographical fact
  Secondary: the Ganges is a place (go there)
  Dhvani: the religious and spiritual resonance of the Ganges,
          its association with purity, moksha, the sacred

The best poetry works primarily at the dhvani level.
The literal meaning is the vehicle; the suggestion is
the destination.

Compare: this is the aesthetic theory underlying all
poetry in which connotation > denotation.
In Western terms: the symbol (Symbolism, New Criticism's
"tension" and "irony") is a Western approach to a similar
phenomenon that dhvani theory formalizes more precisely.
```

### Kavya: The Formal Poetry

```
KAVYA (high literary art)
--------------------------
Not just "poetry" — a specific mode of high literary
composition. Characterized by:

Alamkara (figures of speech, ornamentation):
  Sanskrit poetics catalogued dozens of alamkara
  (similar to the Western catalog of tropes and schemes).
  The distinction between sabdalankara (figures of sound)
  and arthalankara (figures of meaning) parallels Western
  schemes vs. tropes.

Vakrokti (oblique expression):
  Kuntaka's term for the essential quality of poetic language:
  it "goes sideways" — avoids the direct path.
  Poetry is by definition not-literal.

Guna (qualities):
  Formal qualities of style — ojas (vigor), madhurya
  (sweetness/euphony), prasada (clarity). Different from
  Western style categories but structurally parallel.

Key texts:
  Kalidasa's Meghaduta ("Cloud Messenger") — lyric epic;
    a yaksha in exile asks a cloud to carry his message
    to his beloved, describing the journey across India.
    Masnavi structure, geographical catalog, rasa shringara.

  Kalidasa's Shakuntala — court drama; combines shringara
    and karuna; the king's loss of memory as the separation.
```

## Tamil Sangam Poetry

The Tamil Sangam anthologies (c. 300 BCE–300 CE) represent one of the world's oldest literary traditions, developed independently of Sanskrit influence:

```
SANGAM FORMAL SYSTEM
---------------------
The Sangam poems are organized by two main categories:

AKAM (interior/love poems)
  Subject: the stages of a love affair
  NOT narrative — the lovers are never named
  Setting determines meaning via TINAI SYSTEM:

TINAI — THE ECOLOGICAL CODE
  5 ecological zones, each associated with:
  - A landscape type
  - Dominant flora/fauna
  - A phase of the love relationship
  - A time of day/season

  Kurinji (mountain/jasmine): union of lovers
  Mullai (forest/jasmine shrub): patient waiting
  Marutam (river valley/marutam flower): unfaithfulness
  Neytal (seacoast/blue lily): longing/pining
  Palai (desert/harsh landscape): separation/hardship

  When a poem sets a scene in the mountains, this
  SIGNALS (not depicts) that the poem concerns union.
  The landscape is a code; the reader decodes.

PURAM (exterior/heroic poems)
  War, death, generosity, the heroic code
  Praise of kings, lamentation for fallen warriors
  Akananuru, Purananuru anthologies

FORMAL PRECISION:
  The tinai system is a mapping — {landscape} → {meaning}
  This is not metaphor (one thing compared to another).
  It is a code: a landscape image is a signifier whose
  signified is a defined emotional situation.
  The poem need not make the connection explicit; the
  trained reader supplies it.
```

## Chinese Classical Poetry

### The Shi Tradition

```
CHINESE SHI — REGULATED VERSE
-------------------------------
Classical Chinese poetry is tonal: each syllable has
one of four tones (level, rising, departing, entering).
The regulated verse form (lu shi) has strict tonal patterns:
  - 8 lines, 5 or 7 characters per line
  - Tonal pattern: alternating level/oblique tones in
    specified positions
  - Parallel couplets: lines 3-4 and 5-6 must be
    grammatically parallel (same structure, contrasting content)
  - End rhyme: lines 2, 4, 6, 8 rhyme on the same level tone

This is a multi-dimensional constraint system:
  - Tonal patterning (pitch contour)
  - Grammatical parallelism (structural symmetry)
  - Semantic contrast (within parallel couplets)
  - Rhyme scheme
  - Character count
  All simultaneously operative.

Du Fu's regulated verse is the standard of excellence.
His craft: maintain all formal constraints while achieving
maximum naturalness and emotional power — form is invisible.
```

### The Tang Poets

```
THE TANG DYNASTY (618–907): GOLDEN AGE OF CHINESE POETRY

Li Bai (701–762):
  Romantic, Daoist, wild. Celebrates wine, friendship,
  the moon. Irregular forms (not just regulated verse).
  "Quiet Night Thoughts" ("Jing Ye Si"):
    "Before my bed, the moonlight bright —
    Could it be frost upon the ground?
    I raise my head to see the moon,
    Then bow — and think of home."
  (This is a jueju — 4-line poem; 5 characters each.
   The simplest canonical form; maximum compression.)

Du Fu (712–770):
  The "Poet Sage." Historical conscience of the Tang.
  Witnessed the An Lushan Rebellion's devastation.
  The regulated verse master.
  "Spring View" written after the fall of Chang'an:
    "The country is broken; mountains and rivers remain.
    Spring comes to the city; grass and trees are deep.
    Feeling the times, flowers make us weep;
    Hating separation, birds frighten the heart."
    (Lines 1-2 and 3-4 are grammatically parallel couplets)

Wang Wei (699–759):
  Buddhist poet-painter. Landscape as spiritual condition.
  Jingshan poems: external scene as internal state.
  The school of "empty mountain" poetry — absence, stillness.
```

### The Ci Form

```
CI (SONG LYRIC)
---------------
Emerged in the late Tang, flourished in the Song dynasty.
Written to pre-existing musical tunes (pai) — the tune
name is the "title" of the ci pattern.
Each tune has a fixed metrical pattern (line lengths,
tonal requirements, rhyme positions).

Unlike regulated shi: irregular line lengths;
different tonal rules; often more personal/intimate.

Su Shi (1037–1101): most celebrated ci poet.
  Broadened the ci from love poetry to philosophical
  meditation. "Nian Nu Jiao — Chibi Huaigu":
  meditation on time, history, the Red Cliff battle,
  personal insignificance before history.

Li Qingzhao (c. 1084–1155): supreme woman poet.
  ci on loneliness, loss, late autumn.
  "Sheng Sheng Man": repetition, rhythm, sound
  performance enacting emptiness.
```

## Arabic Literary Tradition

### Pre-Islamic Poetry: The Qasida

```
THE MU'ALLAQAT ("Suspended Odes")
----------------------------------
Seven (or ten) pre-Islamic Arabic odes said to have been
"suspended" in the Kaaba at Mecca — the best poems of
the annual market and poetry contest.
Composed by tribal poets: Imru' al-Qays, Labid, Tarafa, etc.

THE QASIDA FORM:
  Long monorhyme poem (all lines rhyme on same final word)
  Three-part structure:
  1. Nasib: erotic/nostalgic prelude at abandoned campsite
             (atlal = ruins of the beloved's camp)
  2. Rahil: the journey, the horse or camel described
  3. Fakhr/Madih: self-praise or praise of patron/tribe

  The nasib is the most famous section — it establishes
  the poet at the ruins of the beloved's camp, weeping
  for the absent. This is the formal opening move of the
  qasida tradition.

INFLUENCE:
  The qasida became the prestige form of Islamic court
  poetry in Arabic, Persian (Firdausi, Rumi used it),
  Urdu, Turkish, and Ottoman literatures.
  The ghazal developed from the nasib section of the
  qasida, detached and expanded into an independent form.
```

### One Thousand and One Nights

```
ALF LAYLA WA-LAYLA (c. 8th–14th c., compiled gradually)
---------------------------------------------------------
Frame narrative: Shahryar/Scheherazade
  The sultan kills each wife after one night.
  Scheherazade survives by telling stories that never end.
  The narrative FRAME enacts the power of narrative:
  stories keep you alive; stopping the story = death.

STRUCTURE:
  Extradiegetic: Shahryar and Scheherazade (the frame)
  Intradiegetic: the tales she tells (level 2)
  Metadiegetic: characters within tales who tell tales (level 3)
  Metalepsis: Scheherazade sometimes appears in her own tales

The tales have varied origins: Persian, Indian (Panchatantra
elements), Arabic originals. They were compiled, not authored.
Antoine Galland's French translation (1704-17) was the first
European version — it added several tales not in any known Arabic
manuscript (including Sinbad, Aladdin, Ali Baba).

The One Thousand Nights is a world-system of narrative:
it influenced Borges directly (labyrinths, infinite libraries),
Calvino, García Márquez. Borges called Scheherazade
"the most beautiful figure in world literature."
```

## Hebrew Biblical Literature

```
HEBREW BIBLE AS FORMAL LITERARY SYSTEM

The Hebrew Bible is the most influential literary object in
Western literature — not as theology but as a formal system
that shaped every subsequent genre: the lyric, the elegy,
the love poem, the epic, the philosophical dialogue.

BIBLICAL PARALLELISM (Robert Lowth, "Lectures," 1753):
The defining formal feature of Hebrew poetry.
Not meter in the Greek/Latin sense — no syllable counting or
quantity. Instead: semantic/syntactic parallelism across lines.

Three types (Lowth's taxonomy, still foundational):

  Synonymous parallelism:
    Line A states X; Line B restates X in different words.
    "The heavens declare the glory of God;
     the firmament showeth his handiwork."  (Psalm 19:1)
    Formal structure: f(a) || f(a') where a ≈ a'

  Antithetical parallelism:
    Line A states X; Line B states not-X (the opposite).
    "A merry heart maketh a cheerful countenance:
     but by sorrow of the heart the spirit is broken." (Prov. 15:13)
    Formal structure: f(a) || f(¬a)

  Synthetic parallelism:
    Line B completes or extends Line A — not synonymous,
    not antithetical, but additive or causal.
    "I have set my king upon my holy hill of Zion.
     I will declare the decree." (Psalm 2:6-7)
    Formal structure: f(a) ∧ g(a,b)

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR WESTERN LITERARY FORM:
  The King James Bible (1611) rendered Hebrew parallelism
  into English prose. This gave English literary prose its
  characteristic rhythm of paired or tripled clauses.
  Lincoln's Second Inaugural ("With malice toward none, with
  charity for all, with firmness in the right") is biblical
  parallelism in political rhetoric.
  Martin Luther King's repetitive structure derives directly
  from the sermon form which derives from Psalm parallelism.

SONG OF SONGS (Shir HaShirim):
  The most formally unusual book in the Hebrew Bible.
  A sequence of erotic poems with no narrative frame,
  no theological content, no moral instruction.
  Inclusion in the canon attributed to Rabbi Akiva (c. 100 CE):
  "All the world is not worth the day on which the Song of
  Songs was given to Israel, for all the Writings are holy,
  but the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies."
  Formal features: alternating speakers (lover/beloved/chorus),
  dense natural imagery (apple trees, vineyards, mountains),
  the extended simile (wasf) comparing body parts to landscapes.
  It is the Western love poem's formal origin.

PSALMS — 150 POEMS, FULL GENRE RANGE:
  Lament (most common): address → complaint → appeal → trust → praise
  Praise (hallelujah psalms): imperative + reason structure
  Royal psalms: political theology in lyric form
  Wisdom psalms: gnomic, didactic, Proverbs-adjacent

LAMENTATIONS:
  5 poems on the destruction of Jerusalem (586 BCE).
  Chapters 1, 2, 4: acrostic — each verse begins with successive
  Hebrew letter (aleph to tav). Chapter 3: triple acrostic (3×22=66 verses).
  The formal constraint (acrostic) imposed on maximum grief content
  is a formal analog of the constraint-satisfaction tension
  identified in 02-EPIC-AND-LYRIC.md's lyric section.
```

## African Oral Traditions

### Sundiata: The Mande Epic

```
SUNDIATA: EPIC OF OLD MALI
----------------------------
Subject: Sundiata Keita, founder of the Mali Empire
         (c. 1217–1255). His rise from crippled childhood
         to king who defeated the Sosso Empire.

Tradition: Performed by griots (jalolu/jeli) —
  hereditary professional bards of West African societies.
  The griot is historian, genealogist, praise-singer,
  keeper of communal memory.

FORMAL FEATURES:
  - Prose narrative interspersed with praise songs
  - Direct address to the audience
  - Genealogical recitation (ancestry as heroic legitimation)
  - The griot is present as a character ("I, the griot,
    was there at the battle of Kirina")
  - Each performance differs — the text is the tradition,
    not any single performance

KEY STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS:
  The hero's disability (Sundiata cannot walk as a child)
  → divine intervention → sudden empowerment → exile →
  return → final battle. Compare Propp's morphology.

RELATION TO PARRY/LORD:
  The griot tradition confirms that oral-formulaic
  composition is not just a Greek or European phenomenon.
  Universal in preliterate societies with heroic traditions.
  The difference: African griots are a hereditary
  professional class, not amateur singers.
```

### Praise Poetry

```
IZIBONGO (Zulu praise poetry)
ORIKI (Yoruba praise poetry)
PRAISE POETRY AS FORM:
  Not praise in the simple sense — also challenge and blame.
  The praise poet (imbongi in Zulu; olorikiri in Yoruba)
  has social license to criticize power through the form
  of praise. "Praising" the lion for its violence = critique.

  Structure: accumulation of epithets, attributes, deeds.
  Not narrative but attributive — each line adds a quality.
  Like a formal specification: the subject's type signature
  is built line by line.

  Live performance, typically at public ceremonial occasions
  (installation of chiefs, military parades, funerals).
  The poet improvises on stored epithets and formulas.
```

## Japanese Literature

```
JAPANESE LITERARY TRADITION — KEY PERIODS AND FORMS

HEIAN PERIOD (794–1185): court literature
  The defining achievement: Heian court culture produced two
  of the world's most formally sophisticated texts.

MURASAKI SHIKIBU: Genji Monogatari (c. 1000-1010 CE)
  Often called "the world's first novel" — contested but useful.
  What makes it novel-like:
    - Sustained psychological interiority (not epic externality)
    - Character development over time (Prince Genji ages, declines)
    - Social world as the medium of the narrative
    - Free indirect discourse (the narrator slides into character POV)
  54 chapters; ~1,000 named characters.
  Mono no aware (物の哀れ): "the pathos of things" — the melancholy
  beauty of impermanence. The aesthetic is structurally Buddhist:
  beauty intensified by its transience.
  Formal innovation: the narrative has no clear climax and
  no resolution. The final chapters (Uji chapters) shift to
  a different protagonist. Western narrative teleology does
  not apply.

SEI SHONAGON: Makura no Soshi (The Pillow Book, c. 996-1002)
  A zuihitsu (miscellany): not narrative but accumulative.
  Lists, observations, anecdotes, judgments.
  "Things that make one's heart beat faster."
  "Awkward things." "Things that are near but seem far."
  The form is a pre-database database: categorical enumeration
  of the phenomenal world. Each list is a type instance.

HAIKU — THE FORMAL STRUCTURE
  Origin: haiku emerged from haikai no renga (comic linked verse).
  Hokku (opening verse of renga) → haiku (standalone).
  Matsuo Basho (1644–1694) elevated haiku to serious literary form.

  FORMAL CONSTRAINTS:
    5 / 7 / 5 on (Japanese sound units, ≠ English syllables)
    Kigo (季語): seasonal reference word (spring = cherry blossoms;
      autumn = harvest moon; winter = snow; etc.)
      Every haiku is indexed to a season.
    Kireji (切れ字): "cutting word" — a syntactic break that
      juxtaposes two images or perceptions.
      In English translation: often rendered as em-dash or line break.

  FORMAL ANALYSIS:
    The kireji is a discontinuity operator. The haiku is not
    a unified image but a juxtaposition: image A | image B.
    Meaning arises in the gap (ma, 間 — interval/pause).
    The reader performs the semantic connection.
    This is not ellipsis (missing content) but a formal feature:
    the poem IS the gap.

  BASHO'S FROG HAIKU (the canonical example):
    古池や (furuike ya)     — old pond / [kireji]
    蛙飛び込む (kawazu tobikomu) — frog jumps in
    水の音 (mizu no oto)   — sound of water
    The kireji after "old pond" creates the juxtaposition.
    The sound follows the silence of the old pond.
    The meaning is the relationship between stillness and
    disturbance — the poem does not state this; it enacts it.

  OTHER MASTERS:
    Yosa Buson (1716–1784): more painterly, imagistic
    Kobayashi Issa (1763–1828): personal, humane, often about
      small creatures; different emotional register from Basho

MEIJI AND MODERN JAPANESE FICTION:
  Meiji Restoration (1868): Japan opens to Western literary forms.
  Natsume Soseki (1867–1916): I Am a Cat (1905), Kokoro (1914).
    Kokoro: a novel of psychological alienation and Meiji-era guilt.
    Soseki studied English literature in London; his work is
    a negotiation between Western novel form and Japanese interiority.

  Junichiro Tanizaki (1886–1965): In'ei Raisan (In Praise of Shadows,
    1933) — an essay on Japanese aesthetics and modernity.
    The Makioka Sisters (1948): social novel of Osaka merchant family.

  Yasunari Kawabata (1899–1972): Nobel 1968.
    Snow Country (1956): mono no aware applied to doomed love.
    The formal technique: present-tense fragmentary perception.

  Yukio Mishima (1925–1970): Sea of Fertility tetralogy (1965–1970).
    Obsession with beauty, death, Japanese identity, samurai code.
    Formal range: realism, surrealism, Noh drama.

  Haruki Murakami (1949–): global reach, deeply Western influence.
    Norwegian Wood (1987): realist; The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle (1994):
    magical realist + thriller. Kafka on the Shore (2002): parallel
    narratives alternating chapters — the structure enacts the
    characters' disconnection.
    Murakami's formal technique: deadpan surrealism.
    The bizarre event is narrated in the same flat register as cooking
    pasta. The mundane and the uncanny share a syntax.
```

## Latin American Tradition

### The Boom Novel (1960s–70s)

```
THE LATIN AMERICAN BOOM
------------------------
A convergence of major writers and major works in the 1960s
achieved international visibility for Latin American fiction.

KEY WRITERS AND WORKS:
  Gabriel Garcia Marquez (Colombia):
    One Hundred Years of Solitude (1967)
    Magical realism: the Buendia family across generations;
    history of Latin America encoded in family mythology;
    circular time; the miraculous as ordinary.

  Mario Vargas Llosa (Peru):
    The Time of the Hero (1963), Conversation in the Cathedral
    (1969). Structural experimentation: time shifts,
    multiple perspectives, collaged narratives.

  Carlos Fuentes (Mexico):
    Terra Nostra (1975). Historical palimpsest: the Spanish
    conquest, Philip II, the mythological; fiction and history
    interwoven at 800 pages.

  Julio Cortazar (Argentina):
    Hopscotch (Rayuela) (1963). Instructions for reading:
    can be read straight through (chapters 1-56) or in
    prescribed non-linear order (a different reading).
    The book has two "endings" and a "Table of Instructions."
    This is metafiction as structure.

MAGICAL REALISM — THE THEORY:
  The "real maravilloso" — Alejo Carpentier (Cuban):
    the marvellous is real in Latin America because
    European categories of the rational/irrational don't
    apply to a history defined by indigenous cosmologies,
    African diaspora, colonial violence.
  Garcia Marquez: the magic is not fantasy — it is
    the way the actual history FEELS. A general who
    survived seventeen assassination attempts and thirty-two
    civil wars (in One Hundred Years) is magical realism
    drawn directly from actual Colombian history.
```

### Jorge Luis Borges: The Foundation

```
BORGES (Argentina, 1899-1986)
------------------------------
The most influential Latin American writer on international
metafiction and postmodern literature.

KEY FORMAL INNOVATIONS:
  1. The story as philosophical argument
     Borges's stories are literary thought experiments:
     "The Library of Babel" (infinite library containing
     all possible books) = a formal argument about infinity
     and combinatorics.
     "The Garden of Forking Paths" = hypertext fiction
     before hypertext: a novel where all possible plots
     branch and are all simultaneously real.

  2. False erudition
     Stories present invented authors, non-existent books,
     fabricated bibliographies as if real. The apparatus of
     scholarship is itself a fiction.
     Borges: "It is a laborious madness and an impoverishing
     one, the madness of composing vast books... It is better
     to pretend that these books already exist."

  3. Infinity and iteration
     Borges is obsessed with the infinite and with
     self-reference. The Library of Babel, the Aleph,
     Funes the Memorious (perfect memory), the Map
     as large as the territory. These are philosophical
     paradoxes given narrative form.

INFLUENCE:
  Borges influenced Calvino (metafiction), Eco (The Name
  of the Rose), Pynchon, DeLillo, the entire postmodern
  tradition. He also influenced magical realism by showing
  that the fantastic and the intellectual are not opposed.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"World literature" is not just "literature from around the world"**
As a theoretical concept (Goethe coined Weltliteratur in 1827; Moretti, Damrosch revived it), it refers to how texts circulate between national traditions and how they are transformed in translation and reception. It involves questions about unequal cultural power (which traditions get translated into English?), canon formation, and the relationship between local specificity and global claim.

**Rasa is not just "emotion in literature"**
Rasa theory is a formal system that distinguishes the emotions depicted (sthayi-bhava) from the aesthetic emotions experienced by the audience (rasa). A tragedy depicting sorrow (shoka) produces in the audience not sorrow but the aesthetic experience of Karuna-rasa — related to but distinct from ordinary grief. This is a claim about the transformation that art performs on raw emotion.

**Magical realism is not fantasy**
Fantasy creates worlds that are not real. Magical realism depicts the real world but from a perspective in which the boundary between material and spiritual, natural and supernatural, is not fixed. It is a culturally-specific mode: it works because the cultures it represents have cosmologies in which the magical is not categorically separate from the everyday.

**The ghazal is not just any Persian/Urdu poetry**
It is a specific formal structure: pairs of rhyming lines (sher/bayt), a repeated radif (refrain word or phrase) after the qafia (rhyme word), and the poet's name or pseudonym in the final couplet (maqta). Each couplet is relatively independent — ghazals are characterized by the discontinuity between their couplets, held together only by the formal constraint, not by logical or narrative progression.
