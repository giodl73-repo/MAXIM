# Critical Theory

## The Big Picture

Literary critical theory is the set of epistemological frameworks for reading and interpreting texts. These are not merely methods — they are competing philosophical positions about what texts are, what they mean, and how we know. The 20th century produced a proliferation of competing schools, each with different answers to: What is the proper object of literary study? What kind of knowledge does literary interpretation produce?


## Layer 1: New Criticism (1930s–1960s)

New Criticism (Cleanth Brooks, Robert Penn Warren, I.A. Richards, William Empson, Allen Tate) is the dominant Anglo-American critical approach of the mid-20th century. Still the foundation of close reading as practiced in classrooms.

### Core Tenets

```
NEW CRITICISM — KEY CLAIMS
----------------------------
1. The Intentional Fallacy (Wimsatt & Beardsley, 1946)
   The author's intended meaning is IRRELEVANT to
   the meaning of the poem. You cannot know the intention
   anyway; even if you could, it would not determine
   what the poem means.
   → The text is autonomous. Meaning is IN the words,
     not behind them.

2. The Affective Fallacy (Wimsatt & Beardsley, 1949)
   The reader's emotional response is IRRELEVANT to
   the meaning of the poem. "This moved me" is a
   psychological fact about you, not a critical judgment.
   → The text is the object of study, not your reaction.

3. Close Reading
   Read the text minutely. Account for every word, image,
   metaphor, ambiguity, tension, paradox.
   Seven Types of Ambiguity (Empson, 1930) — a systematic
   taxonomy of how literary language generates multiple
   simultaneous meanings.

4. The Well-Wrought Urn (Brooks, 1947)
   The poem is a unified, self-sufficient object.
   Its characteristic qualities: irony, tension, paradox,
   ambiguity resolved into organic unity.
   The best poems are those in which contradictions are
   most fully and satisfyingly resolved.
```

### The Structuralist Influence

New Criticism's insistence on the text as autonomous object connects to structuralism's scientific aspirations:

```
NEW CRITICISM → STRUCTURALISM
-------------------------------
New Criticism: texts have internal structure; reading
  is an activity of discovering that structure.
  Emphasis: meaning, irony, organic unity.

Russian Formalism (Shklovsky, Jakobson, Tomashevsky):
  "Literariness" (literaturnost) = what makes language
  literary. Not the content but the formal devices.
  Defamiliarization (ostranenie): art renews perception
  by making the familiar strange.
  Compare: optimization for surprise/complexity vs.
  optimization for transmission efficiency.

Structuralism (Jakobson, Levi-Strauss):
  Saussure's structural linguistics applied to culture.
  The sign = signifier + signified, arbitrary relation.
  Language is a SYSTEM OF DIFFERENCES, not a catalog of
  positive terms. "Cat" means what it means only because
  it differs from "bat," "mat," "cut."
  → Applied to literature: narrative structure = deep grammar
  → Applied to myth (Levi-Strauss): myth = binary oppositions
    (culture/nature, raw/cooked, life/death) resolved or mediated
  → Applied to poetry (Jakobson): poetic function =
    projection of equivalence from paradigm to syntagm
    (sounds/rhythms that organize the horizontal sequence)
```

## Layer 2: Structuralism — The Formal Turn

### Saussure's Foundation

Ferdinand de Saussure's **Course in General Linguistics** (reconstructed lectures, published 1916) is the origin of 20th-century structuralism:

```
SAUSSURE'S KEY DISTINCTIONS
-----------------------------
langue vs. parole
  langue  = the language system (abstract, social,
            shared by all speakers)
  parole  = individual speech acts (concrete, particular)
  → Linguistics studies langue; parole is the data

synchronic vs. diachronic
  synchronic  = the system at one moment in time
                (structural linguistics)
  diachronic  = historical change over time
                (historical/comparative linguistics)
  → Saussure advocates synchronic study as primary

signifier vs. signified
  signifier   = the sound-image, the form
  signified   = the concept
  sign        = signifier + signified
  The relation is ARBITRARY — there is no natural
  connection between the sound "tree" and the concept
  of a tree. (Exception: onomatopoeia, but rare.)

  THEREFORE:
  Meaning is produced by DIFFERENCE, not by
  positive reference to the world.
  "Dog" means what it means because it is not "cat,"
  "fog," "log" — because it differs from everything
  else in the system.
```

### Levi-Strauss and Structural Anthropology

Claude Levi-Strauss applied Saussure to myth in the 1950s–60s:

```
LEVI-STRAUSS — THE STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS OF MYTH
------------------------------------------------
Thesis: Myths from different cultures exhibit the same
deep structure — a set of binary oppositions that the
myth mediates.

The Oedipus myth analysis:
  Column A (overrating blood relations)
    Cadmus seeks his sister Europa
    Oedipus marries his mother
  Column B (underrating blood relations)
    Brothers kill each other (Eteo./Poly.)
    Oedipus kills his father
  Column C (denial of autochthony — born from earth)
    Cadmus kills the dragon
    Oedipus kills the Sphinx
  Column D (persistence of autochthony)
    Labdacus, Laius, Oedipus — names all mean
    "swollen/lame/left-footed" = difficulty walking

  The myth mediates the contradiction:
  Are humans born of woman or of earth (autochthony)?
  The myth cannot resolve this but "thinks through" it
  by arranging its elements in a structure that makes
  the contradiction visible.

This is formal analysis of myth:
  Binary oppositions (A/B, C/D) as the primitive units
  The mytheme (not the word or sentence but a narrative
  unit) as the relevant level of analysis
  Synchronic reading: read across all versions at once

Relation to Chomsky:
  Both are looking for DEEP STRUCTURE beneath surface forms.
  Chomsky: all languages have the same deep syntactic
           organization beneath different surface structures.
  Levi-Strauss: all myths have the same deep structural
           oppositions beneath different surface narratives.
```

## Layer 2b: Marxist Literary Criticism

```
MARXIST CRITICISM — BASE/SUPERSTRUCTURE MODEL

Marx's base/superstructure:
  Base (Basis): economic relations of production
  Superstructure: law, politics, religion, art, literature
  Claim: superstructure is determined (in the last instance)
         by the economic base.
  Literary application: literature is superstructure —
         it reflects, reproduces, or contests economic conditions.

GEORG LUKACS (1885–1971): REALISM AND IDEOLOGY
  The Historical Novel (1937), Studies in European Realism (1950).
  Realism = the capacity to represent the totality of social forces
  operating through individual characters.
  Balzac (arch-conservative politically) is a great realist
  because his novels reveal the logic of capitalism against
  his own intentions. The form exceeds the author's ideology.
  Modernism (Joyce, Kafka) = pathological: it fragments the
  social totality into isolated subjectivities. Decadent form.
  Lukacs's formalism: the correct form (realism) contains
  more historical truth than incorrect content (conservative ideology).

LOUIS ALTHUSSER (1918–1990): IDEOLOGY AND ISAS
  Ideology and Ideological State Apparatuses (1970).
  ISAs: institutions that reproduce ideology non-coercively
  (schools, churches, media, literature).
  Ideology: "the imaginary relationship of individuals
  to their real conditions of existence."
  Ideology is not a set of false beliefs; it is a lived
  practice that constitutes subjects.
  Literary application: texts interpellate (hail) readers
  into subject positions. "Hey, you!" — ideology calls,
  and you turn around, recognizing yourself as the subject
  being addressed. The realist novel interpellates a
  bourgeois subject who believes in individual psychology
  and free will as natural.

FREDRIC JAMESON (1934–2024): THE POLITICAL UNCONSCIOUS
  The Political Unconscious (1981): "Always historicize!"
  The literary text is a "socially symbolic act" —
  it produces imaginary solutions to real social contradictions.
  The text cannot openly acknowledge the contradiction
  (ideology forecloses this); it displaces it into formal
  problems. Reading for the political unconscious = reading
  the formal tensions as symptoms of historical contradiction.
  Example: the unresolvable formal tensions in Conrad's
  Lord Jim are symptoms of the contradiction between
  the official ideology of imperial heroism and the
  material reality of colonial exploitation.

FRANKFURT SCHOOL: ADORNO AND THE CULTURE INDUSTRY
  Horkheimer + Adorno, Dialectic of Enlightenment (1944):
  The "culture industry" (film, radio, mass-market fiction)
  produces standardized products that create false needs
  and pacify the working class.
  The culture industry is not propaganda but a system:
    - Standardization: each product is the same template
    - Pseudo-individuation: surface variety, structural sameness
    - The happy ending as ideology: the world is bearable
  Adorno's aesthetics: only modernist art that refuses
  easy consumption can preserve critical consciousness.
  Beckett = good; jazz = ideological (Adorno's most contested claim).

CRITIQUE OF MARXIST CRITICISM:
  Reductionism risk: all texts become class documents.
  The base/superstructure model is too mechanical;
  later Marxists (Gramsci, Raymond Williams) introduced
  "hegemony" and "structures of feeling" to complicate it.
  Jameson's "always historicize" becomes: always find the
  contradiction — a method that can become a formula.
```

## Layer 3: Poststructuralism and Deconstruction

### The Crisis Point: 1966

Jacques Derrida's paper "Structure, Sign and Play in the Discourse of the Human Sciences" (delivered at Johns Hopkins, 1966) is often marked as the moment structuralism became poststructuralism:

```
DERRIDA'S ARGUMENT (very compressed)
--------------------------------------
Structuralism assumes a CENTER — a fixed point outside
the system that anchors and organizes it (God, the human
subject, Being, Presence, Logos).

But: the center is itself a sign within a system of
signs. It cannot stand outside the system.

Therefore: the system is not closed. There is no fixed
ground. Meaning is DEFERRED — it is always "about to
arrive," never fully present. The word "sign" always
refers to another sign, never to a pure signified.

DIFFERANCE (Derrida's neologism):
  A portmanteau of "differ" and "defer" (French
  "differer" does both; "differance" with -ance instead
  of -ence is audibly indistinguishable in French —
  you can only hear the difference in writing).
  Meaning is produced by difference AND deferral —
  never present, always displaced.

DECONSTRUCTION (not "taking apart"):
  A reading practice that finds the internal tensions
  and instabilities in a text — the moments where the
  text undermines its own stated meaning.
  Every text contains the seeds of its own undoing.

  Example: Plato's Phaedrus argues AGAINST writing
  (writing is dead memory; live dialogue is better).
  But Plato's critique of writing is... written.
  The argument against writing uses writing.
  This self-undermining is a deconstructive aporia.
```

### Foucault: Discourse and Power

Michel Foucault is not a literary theorist but his influence on literary study is enormous:

```
FOUCAULT'S KEY CONCEPTS FOR LITERARY STUDY
--------------------------------------------
DISCOURSE
  Not just language — the entire system of practices,
  institutions, knowledges, and power relations that
  produce what counts as "true" or "normal" in a given
  historical period.

  "Sexuality" is not a natural fact that medicine
  describes. "Sexuality" as a category was PRODUCED
  by 19th-century medical discourse. The discourse
  created the objects it appeared to describe.

  Applied to literature:
  "Literature" is itself a discursive formation — a
  set of institutions (the university, the publishing
  industry, the review system) that produces what
  counts as "literary."

THE AUTHOR FUNCTION
  Foucault (1969 lecture "What Is an Author?"):
  The "author" is not a biographical person but a
  FUNCTION — a means of organizing the circulation
  of discourse. We ask "Who wrote this?" to determine
  how to read it, what it means, what status it has.
  → The author function is a product of legal/economic
    regimes (copyright) and discursive norms.
  → Barthes's "Death of the Author" (1967) is related:
    the author does not control meaning; the reader
    produces meaning at the moment of reading.

GENEALOGY
  Foucault's historical method: trace how current
  "natural" categories (madness, sexuality, discipline)
  are historical constructions with contingent origins.
  Applied to literature: canon formation has a genealogy.
  What counts as great literature is not transhistorical
  value but the product of historical power relations.
```

## Layer 4: Postcolonial Theory

### Edward Said: Orientalism

Edward Said's **Orientalism** (1978) is the founding text:

```
ORIENTALISM — CORE ARGUMENT
-----------------------------
The West (Europe/America) produced a body of scholarly
and literary knowledge about "the East" (Middle East,
Asia, North Africa) — Said calls this "Orientalism."

This knowledge is not neutral description.
It is a form of POWER:
  It produces the "Orient" as an object of knowledge.
  It defines the Orient as irrational, exotic, backward,
  timeless — in contrast to the rational, modern, dynamic
  West.
  This discursive construction ENABLED colonialism by
  producing the colonized as objects to be managed.

Foucault's influence: Orientalism is a discourse that
produces its objects (just as medical discourse produces
"the homosexual" as a type of person).

Gramsci's influence: hegemony — the Orient came to
represent itself through Western Orientalist categories.
Colonial subjects internalized the colonizer's image
of them.

LITERARY EXAMPLES Said analyzes:
  Flaubert's Egypt (in his letters and Salammbo)
  Nerval's Orient
  The novels of Conrad and Kipling
```

### Spivak and the Subaltern

Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988) pushed postcolonial theory further:

```
CAN THE SUBALTERN SPEAK?
-------------------------
Spivak's argument:
  The subaltern (the colonized, the marginalized — especially
  women under imperialism) cannot speak within the dominant
  discourse. If she speaks in the colonizer's language,
  she is heard according to the colonizer's categories.
  If she speaks outside it, she cannot be heard at all.
  The very structures of knowing that postcolonial theory
  inherits from Western academia are contaminated by the
  colonial project.

This is an epistemological argument:
  Not just "minority voices are suppressed."
  But: "the very conceptual vocabulary of 'speaking,'
  'representing,' 'the subject' is shaped by colonial
  history in ways we cannot simply undo."

BHABHA: HYBRIDITY
  Homi Bhabha's **The Location of Culture** (1994):
  Colonial discourse produces "hybridity" — a third space
  between colonizer and colonized, where neither culture
  is pure and both are transformed by the encounter.
  The "mimic man" (colonized subject who mimics the
  colonizer) is "almost the same, but not quite" —
  and this almost-ness is a form of subversion.
```

## Layer 5: Feminist and Gender Theory

```
FEMINIST LITERARY CRITICISM
-----------------------------
First wave (1970s): recover women writers excluded from canon.
  Elaine Showalter "A Literature of Their Own" (1977):
  tradition of women's writing, not just women in male texts.
  Virginia Woolf "A Room of One's Own" (1929):
  the material conditions (money, space, time) required for
  women's writing. Judith Shakespeare thought experiment.

Second wave (1980s): critique of representation.
  Images of women in male-authored texts.
  How "woman" functions as a sign in patriarchal discourse.
  Sandra Gilbert + Susan Gubar, The Madwoman in the Attic (1979):
  women writers internalize the male-authored literary tradition
  and experience "anxiety of authorship" — the inverse of Harold
  Bloom's male "anxiety of influence."

FRENCH FEMINIST THEORY — THREE MAJOR FIGURES

JULIA KRISTEVA (1941–):
  Intertextuality: no text is original; every text is a mosaic of
  quotations from prior texts (further developed from Bakhtin).
  Kristeva coined the term "intertextuality" (1966) in her
  reading of Bakhtin for a French audience.
  Abjection (Powers of Horror, 1980):
    The abject is what we expel to constitute the self as subject —
    bodily waste, the corpse, the maternal body.
    Literature of abjection (Céline, Artaud) makes visible
    the boundary-setting that constitutes the subject.
    Horror literature as a genre operates through abjection.
  The Semiotic and the Symbolic:
    Symbolic: structured language, the Law, the paternal
    Semiotic: pre-linguistic drives, rhythm, tone, the maternal
    Poetic language disrupts the Symbolic with semiotic impulses.
    This maps to: the structured/computational dimension of language
    vs. the rhythmic/excessive dimension that exceeds syntax.

HÉLÈNE CIXOUS (1937–):
  Écriture féminine ("feminine writing") — not a fixed style
  but a mode of writing that exceeds phallocentric logic:
  non-linear, excessive, close to the body, multiple.
  "The Laugh of the Medusa" (1975):
  "Write yourself. Your body must be heard."
  The call is political, not essentialist: women must claim writing
  from a tradition that has excluded female embodied experience.
  Critical debate: does écriture féminine essentialize "the feminine"?
  Cixous's position: the feminine is not biologically fixed;
  it is a mode of writing available to any subject who
  exceeds the economy of the Proper (property, propriety).

LUCE IRIGARAY (1930–):
  Speculum of the Other Woman (1974):
  Psychoanalysis (Freud, Lacan) constructs "woman" as lack —
  as the negative term defined by the absence of the phallus.
  Irigaray mimics and subverts this construction:
  she takes the male philosophical texts (Plato, Freud) and
  reads them against themselves, showing how they suppress
  and exclude the feminine.
  Mimicry as critical strategy: perform the patriarchal text
  so faithfully and completely that its exclusions become visible.
  Formal parallel: a proof by contradiction that reveals the
  axioms' inconsistency by deriving them faithfully to an absurd conclusion.
  "This Sex Which Is Not One" (1977):
  Female sexuality is multiple, non-unified — not "one" but plural.
  Challenges the (male) model of a unified, bounded, singular subject.

JUDITH BUTLER: GENDER PERFORMATIVITY
  Gender Trouble (1990):
  Gender is not a natural fact but a PERFORMANCE —
  a repeated set of acts that constitutes gender through
  repetition. There is no pre-discursive gender behind
  the performance; the performance IS gender.

  Applied to literature:
  Literary characters "do" gender through their speech
  and action. The text is a site where gender norms are
  reproduced OR subverted.

  Butler draws on Derrida (performative speech acts
  cite prior speech acts) and Foucault (discourse produces
  subjects). Gender performance is a form of citationality.
  Drag: makes the performative construction visible.
  Literary analog: cross-dressed characters who reveal
  gender as performance (Shakespeare's comedies; Virginia
  Woolf's Orlando).
```

## Layer 6: Ecocriticism

The most recent of the major critical schools, emerging in the 1990s. ASLE (Association for the Study of Literature and Environment) founded 1992; journal ISLE launched 1993.

```
ECOCRITICISM
------------
The study of the relationship between literature and
the physical environment.

Key questions:
  How does literature represent nature?
  What values and assumptions does it embed?
  How do literary representations of nature
  enable or disable environmental action?

THE NATURE-CULTURE BINARY
  Western literary tradition has separated nature
  (passive, object, backdrop) from culture (active,
  subject, meaning-making). Ecocriticism questions this.

  Raymond Williams "The Country and the City" (1973):
  the idealization of the country in English pastoral
  literature is an ideological move that obscures real
  agricultural labor and class relations.

LAWRENCE BUELL: THE ENVIRONMENTAL IMAGINATION (1995)
  The foundational text of American ecocriticism.
  Buell identifies four features of "environmental texts":
    1. The nonhuman environment is not merely framing but
       is present as a character or co-protagonist
    2. Human interest is not the only legitimate interest
    3. Human accountability to the environment is part of
       the text's ethical orientation
    4. Some sense of the environment as a process, not just
       a backdrop (change, nonhuman time scales)
  Applied to Thoreau's Walden: it meets all four criteria.
  Applied to most "nature writing": it often fails criterion 2.

ECOCRITICISM IN PRACTICE — A READING
  The Tempest (Shakespeare): Caliban's relationship to the island.
    Traditional reading: Caliban as colonial subject (postcolonial).
    Ecocritical reading: Caliban as native ecological knowledge-holder.
    "I'll show thee every fertile inch o' th' island" —
    Caliban knows the island's ecology; Prospero knows magic.
    The colonial dispossession is also an epistemological dispossession:
    Caliban's local knowledge is displaced by Prospero's textual magic.

  POSTHUMANISM:
  The challenge to anthropocentrism: humans are not
  the only actors; the nonhuman has agency.
  Timothy Morton "Ecology Without Nature" (2007):
  "nature" as a stable category is itself the problem.
  "Dark Ecology" (2016): ecological thinking after we abandon
  the romantic-pastoral idea of nature as pure/separate.
  Jane Bennett "Vibrant Matter" (2010): political ecology —
  nonhuman objects (food, electricity, metal) have "thing-power."
  Actor-Network Theory (Latour) applied to literary analysis:
  what nonhuman actors does the text mobilize?
```

## Layer 7: Psychoanalytic Criticism

```
PSYCHOANALYTIC CRITICISM — CORE CONCEPTS

FREUD'S LITERARY APPLICATIONS
  "Creative Writers and Daydreaming" (1908):
    Literary fiction = adult version of the child's play.
    The writer transforms daydream (wish-fulfillment) into art
    by giving it formal pleasure (aesthetic "incentive bonus")
    that allows the reader to access material that would
    otherwise be repressed. Literature as disguised wish-fulfillment.
  "The Uncanny" (Das Unheimliche, 1919):
    Unheimlich = un-homely; what was familiar and has become strange.
    More precisely: what was repressed and has returned.
    Freud's examples: doubles, automata, the severed hand,
    compulsive repetition, the "evil eye."
    Literary form: the Gothic novel, horror, ghost stories.
    The uncanny is a quality of effect, not of subject matter.
    E.T.A. Hoffmann's "The Sandman" is Freud's primary text.

DREAMWORK ↔ LITERARY FORM
  Primary process (dream logic) ↔ literary devices:
    Condensation: one element represents many → metaphor, symbol
    Displacement: emotional charge shifts from real object to proxy → metonymy
    Secondary revision: dreamwork imposes narrative coherence → plot
    Representability: abstract idea converted to visual image → imagery
  This mapping is precise: Roman Jakobson's "Two Aspects of Language"
  (1956) identified condensation/metaphor and displacement/metonymy
  as the two fundamental axes of language — connecting Freud to
  structural linguistics.

LACAN'S THREE REGISTERS (RSI):
  Real: the unsymbolizable remainder; what escapes language.
       Not "reality" but the traumatic kernel that resists representation.
  Symbolic: the order of language, law, social structure.
            The subject is constituted by entry into the Symbolic.
            The "signifying chain" (Saussure's langue, for Lacan).
  Imaginary: the order of images, the ego, identification, dyadic relations.
             The mirror stage (1936): the infant identifies with
             its mirror image, constituting the ego as an external image.

  Applied to literature:
    Narrative = symbolic order
    Character psychology = imaginary
    The text's irresolvable gaps = the Real
    The drive toward resolution in narrative = desire structured by
    the symbolic (what we pursue) vs. drive (what we circle around
    without resolution).

DESIRE AND THE OBJET PETIT A:
  Lacan's objet a: the object-cause of desire — not what we want
  but the fantasy object that organizes wanting.
  Literary application: what does the text's protagonist desire?
  Answer: not the named goal (the girl, the treasure, the truth)
  but the objet a that keeps desire in motion.
  The plot's engine is the gap between the goal (stated object)
  and the objet a (what desire is actually organized around).
  Hitchcock's "MacGuffin" (the briefcase in Pulp Fiction,
  the microfilm in North by Northwest) is the objet a exactly:
  the object that matters because characters desire it,
  not because of any intrinsic property.

LACAN ↔ FEMINISM: THE GAZE
  Lacan's gaze (the look that precedes the subject).
  Laura Mulvey "Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema" (1975):
  applied Lacanian gaze theory to Hollywood film.
  The camera's gaze = masculine; woman = object of gaze.
  The male spectator identifies with the gaze;
  the female spectator must adopt a masculine position
  or accept being the object of her own look.
  This became a foundational text for feminist film theory.
```

## Comparison Table: Critical Schools

| School | Primary Question | Key Concepts | Key Figures | Limitation |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| New Criticism | What does the text mean in itself? | Irony, tension, organic unity, close reading | Brooks, Empson, Tate, Ransom | Ignores history, author, reader, power |
| Russian Formalism | What makes language literary? | Defamiliarization, literariness, skaz | Shklovsky, Jakobson, Tomashevsky | Formalism without politics |
| Structuralism | What is the deep grammar? | Langue/parole, binary oppositions, mytheme | Saussure, Levi-Strauss, Jakobson | Centers cannot hold; poststructuralism follows |
| Deconstruction | Where does the text undermine itself? | Differance, aporia, trace, supplement | Derrida, de Man | Endless deferral; no political grip |
| Marxist | What ideological work does the text do? | Base/superstructure, hegemony, ideology | Lukacs, Althusser, Jameson, Gramsci | Reductivism risk; all texts become ideology |
| New Historicism | How do text and context interpenetrate? | Negotiation, circulation, power, anecdote | Greenblatt, Gallagher | "Thick description" risks losing the text |
| Postcolonial | Who speaks? On whose terms? | Orientalism, subaltern, hybridity, mimicry | Said, Spivak, Bhabha | Western theory applied to anti-western project |
| Feminist | How is gender constructed in/by the text? | Performativity, the gaze, patriarchal discourse | Showalter, Butler, Cixous, Irigaray | Multiple feminisms; no unified position |
| Psychoanalytic | What does the text desire/repress? | Uncanny, displacement, condensation, suture | Freud, Lacan, Kristeva | Theory may explain theory more than texts |
| Ecocriticism | How does the text represent the nonhuman? | Nature/culture binary, agency, posthumanism | Williams, Morton, Buell | Recent; methods still developing |

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want to... | Use this school |
|-------------|----------------|
| Read a poem closely for meaning, tension, irony | New Criticism |
| Understand the structural grammar of a narrative | Structuralism / narratology |
| Find the internal contradictions in a text | Deconstruction |

## Common Confusion Points

**Deconstruction is not the same as "take apart and critique"**
Deconstruction is not simply critique or analysis. It is a specific reading practice that finds the moments where a text's logic undermines its own stated meaning — the aporetic moments, the trace of what the text tries to exclude but cannot. It does not "destroy" the text; it reads the internal tensions more rigorously.

**Structuralism and poststructuralism are not opposites**
Poststructuralism is structuralism's self-critique. It accepts the Saussurean framework (language as system of differences) but questions the assumption that the system is stable, closed, and can be known scientifically. Derrida worked within and against structuralism; he did not reject it.

**Foucault is not a literary critic**
Foucault's methods (genealogy, discourse analysis) were developed to study the history of medicine, punishment, sexuality, and knowledge. Literary scholars imported these concepts because they are useful for understanding how literature functions within systems of power and knowledge. The application is productive but also a translation.

**The "death of the author" does not mean authors don't exist**
Barthes (1967) and Foucault (1969) are arguing against a specific model of interpretation in which the author's intention is the final authority on meaning. This is not a biographical claim that authors don't exist — it is a methodological claim that authorial intention does not determine meaning, and that the reader's role in producing meaning has been undervalued.
