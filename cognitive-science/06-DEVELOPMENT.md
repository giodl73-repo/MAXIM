## Piaget's Stage Theory

Jean Piaget (Swiss, 1896–1980): the most influential developmental psychologist of the 20th century. Children are *active constructors* of knowledge, not passive recipients. Development proceeds through **stages** — each representing a qualitatively different mode of understanding.

### Four Stages

```
AGE     STAGE              KEY FEATURES + LANDMARK TASKS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
0-2     SENSORIMOTOR       Object permanence (milestone: ~8 months)
                           Circular reactions (discover body)
                           Deferred imitation (by end)
                           Language emerges at end

2-7     PREOPERATIONAL     Symbolic thought (language, drawing)
                           Egocentrism (Three Mountains Task)
                           Centration (focus on one feature)
                           Failure of conservation tasks
                           Animism: attribute life to objects

7-11    CONCRETE           Conservation mastered
        OPERATIONAL        Decentering
                           Seriation + classification
                           Concrete logical operations
                           Reversibility understood
                           But: only with tangible objects

12+     FORMAL             Hypothetico-deductive reasoning
        OPERATIONAL        Abstract reasoning ("If A then B...")
                           Systematic experimental thinking
                           Proportional reasoning
                           (not all adults reach this fully)
```

### Key Piaget Experiments

**Object Permanence (A-not-B task)**:
```
Phase 1: Object hidden at location A → infant reaches for A (success)
Phase 2: Infant watches object moved to location B
         Infant still reaches for A (Piaget: lacks object permanence)
         Piaget: "Out of sight = out of mind" until ~8 months
```

**Three Mountains Task** (egocentrism):
```
Child sits at side of table with a model of three mountains.
A doll is placed at different position.
Question: "What does the doll see?"
Preoperational child: describes their OWN viewpoint (egocentrism)
Concrete operational child: correctly takes doll's perspective
```

**Conservation of Number**:
```
Two rows of 6 coins, equally spaced.
"Are there the same number?" → Yes
Experimenter spreads one row out to look longer.
"Are there the same number?" → Preoperational child: No, there are more in the longer row
(centration on length; can't simultaneously consider length + density)
Concrete operational child: Yes, you just spread them out (understands reversibility)
```

**Conservation of Liquid**:
```
Two identical glasses with same amount of water.
Pour one into taller, narrower glass.
Preoperational: taller glass has more water
Concrete operational: same amount (compensation: taller but narrower)
```

## Baillargeon — Infant Competence Undermined by Performance

Piaget's methods required active reaching. **Renée Baillargeon** (1987+) used **looking-time paradigms** — infants look longer at unexpected/impossible events — which reveal competence that performance tasks miss.

```
VIOLATION-OF-EXPECTATION PARADIGM:
  Habituate infant to an event.
  Show two test events: EXPECTED (possible) vs UNEXPECTED (impossible).
  If infant stares longer at unexpected → they expected the expected event
  → they have a mental model that was violated.
```

**Object permanence at 2.5–5 months**:
```
Possible event: screen rotates, stops when it would hit the box (box still behind)
Impossible event: screen rotates past where box should be (box "gone" through screen?)

Infants at 3.5 months stare longer at impossible event.
→ They EXPECTED the box to stop the screen → they know the box is still there
→ Object permanence exists much earlier than Piaget claimed (~8 months)
```

**Lesson**: Piaget was measuring *performance* limitations (motor ability, task demands), not *competence*. The cognitive knowledge is present much earlier; the infant just can't demonstrate it in Piaget's production tasks.

**Core Knowledge Systems** (Spelke, Carey): Infants have innate systems for:
- Object (persistence, cohesion, contact causality)
- Agent (intentional, goal-directed, rational)
- Number (approximate number sense, 0-3 exact)
- Space (layout, navigation)
- Social (face, voice, group membership)

## Vygotsky — Sociocultural Development

Lev Vygotsky (1896–1934, Russian, died young of tuberculosis): Development is inherently *social*.

**Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)**:
```
                       CANNOT DO with
                       any help (too far)
                           ↑
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ZPD: Can do with assistance of more        │
  │  capable other (parent, teacher, peer)      │
  │  This is where LEARNING happens             │
  │  This is where to target instruction        │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                           ↓
                    CAN DO independently
                    (already mastered)
```

**Scaffolding**: Support provided by a more capable other that is gradually removed as competence develops.
- Not just teaching — the learner should be doing the work
- Scaffold does what the learner can't yet do alone
- Scaffold is reduced as the learner internalizes

**Language drives thought** (vs Piaget's "thought drives language"):
- Children first speak (external, social)
- Then: **private speech** (talk aloud to themselves while working — observable in 3–7 year olds)
- Then: **inner speech** (internalized, silent — what Vygotsky called "verbal thinking")
- Private speech correlates with task difficulty and disappears as tasks become mastered

**Piaget vs Vygotsky on private speech**:
- Piaget: egocentric speech (just noise, not communicative, immature)
- Vygotsky: self-regulatory speech (functional, becomes inner speech)
- Resolution: Vygotsky was right — private speech increases with task difficulty, decreases as task mastered

## Theory of Mind

**Theory of Mind** (ToM): The ability to attribute mental states — beliefs, desires, intentions, knowledge — to oneself and others, and to understand that others' mental states can differ from one's own and from reality.

### False Belief Task (Baron-Cohen, Leslie & Frith 1985)

**Sally-Anne Test**:
```
SCENE:
  Sally puts marble in basket. Sally leaves room.
  Anne moves marble to box.

QUESTION: "Where will Sally look for her marble?"

Correct answer: BASKET (where Sally believes it to be)
Wrong answer:   BOX (where it actually is)

Results:
  Neurotypical children under 4: mostly answer BOX (fail)
  Neurotypical children over 4-5: mostly answer BASKET (pass)
  Children with autism (verbal IQ matched): ~80% answer BOX (fail)
  → Linked to impaired theory of mind in autism
```

**Why 4 is the watershed**:
- Under 4: fail false belief, don't understand that others' beliefs can be wrong
- Around 4: sudden transition (not gradual) — suggests a maturational component
- Some evidence for 13–15-month infants on looking-time tasks (violation-of-expectation) → perhaps earlier implicit ToM, later explicit ToM

**Level 1 vs Level 2 perspective-taking**:
- Level 1 (2-3 years): "I see a dog; does the person across from me see the dog?" (blocked by barrier?)
- Level 2 (4-5 years): "What does the dog look like *from their perspective*?" — the same object looks different from different angles

**Theories of ToM**:

| Theory | Claim | Evidence |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **Theory-Theory** (Leslie, Wellman) | Children build a theory of mind — change theories with experience | The 4-year transition suggests theory revision |
| **Simulation Theory** (Goldman) | We understand others by mentally simulating their perspective | Mirror neurons cited (controversially) |
| **Nativist** (Leslie) | ToM module is innate; autism = module damage | Baron-Cohen's "mindblindness" |

**Autism and ToM**: Baron-Cohen's "mindblindness hypothesis" — many autistic individuals have difficulty with false belief tasks. But:
- Not universal in autism
- ToM deficit may be downstream of earlier deficit (joint attention, shared intentionality)
- Many autistic adults pass standard ToM tests but still experience social difficulty → tests may be too simple

## Marshmallow Test — And Its Replication Crisis

**Mischel et al. (1972)**: Stanford marshmallow test.
- Child offered 1 marshmallow now OR 2 marshmallows if they wait 15 minutes
- Follow-up studies: children who waited longer in preschool had better SAT scores, better outcomes in adolescence and adulthood
- Became widely cited as evidence that willpower/self-control is a stable trait and key life success predictor
- Entered popular culture: "the marshmallow kid" narrative

**Watts, Duncan & Quan (2018)** — the replication with large, diverse, nationally representative sample:
```
N=918 (vs Mischel's N~90, Stanford children of graduate students)
Include: SES controls, cognitive ability controls, behavior controls

Original finding: delay time → SAT scores, age-15 outcomes
After controlling for SES and cognitive ability: effect drops to ~HALF
After controlling for all confounders: effect nearly disappears

Conclusion: The relationship between delay of gratification and
outcomes is largely explained by socioeconomic background.
```

**What this means**:
- Privileged children can afford to wait — a marshmallow today, two later; both are guaranteed
- Lower-SES children who grab now may be responding *rationally* to an uncertain environment
- The marshmallow task measures trust in the experimenter as much as willpower
- Self-control probably does matter, but it's not independent of context

**Broader lesson**: Single-lab studies with convenience samples often fail to replicate with diverse samples. Effect sizes shrink dramatically when confounders are controlled. This is the core of the **replication crisis** in psychology.

## Kohlberg — Moral Development

**Kohlberg (1958–1981)**: Building on Piaget, proposed 6 stages of moral reasoning in 3 levels.

**Method**: Moral dilemmas (Heinz dilemma: should Heinz steal a drug to save his dying wife?). Scored on *reasoning*, not verdict.

```
LEVEL I — PRECONVENTIONAL (before social norms internalized)
  Stage 1: OBEDIENCE + PUNISHMENT ORIENTATION
            "It's wrong because you'll get punished"
            Avoid punishment, obey power
  Stage 2: INSTRUMENTAL PURPOSE + EXCHANGE
            "What's in it for me?"
            Self-interest, fair exchange

LEVEL II — CONVENTIONAL (social conformity)
  Stage 3: MUTUAL INTERPERSONAL EXPECTATIONS
            "Good boy/nice girl"
            Please others, maintain relationships
  Stage 4: SOCIAL SYSTEM + CONSCIENCE
            "Law and order"
            Uphold society's rules; fulfill duty

LEVEL III — POSTCONVENTIONAL (principled ethics)
  Stage 5: SOCIAL CONTRACT + INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS
            Laws are social contracts; can be changed
            Utilitarian; protect individual rights
  Stage 6: UNIVERSAL ETHICAL PRINCIPLES
            Justice, human dignity, equality
            Laws are valid only if just; civil disobedience
            (Kohlberg said most people never reach this)
```

**Heinz dilemma scoring**:
- Stage 1: Heinz shouldn't steal — he'll go to jail
- Stage 2: Heinz should steal — his wife's life is worth more to him
- Stage 3: Heinz should steal — what a husband does for his wife
- Stage 4: Heinz shouldn't steal — the law must be upheld
- Stage 5: Heinz should steal — life takes precedence over property rights
- Stage 6: Heinz must steal — human life has absolute value

**Gilligan's critique** (1982, "In a Different Voice"):
- Kohlberg's theory values *justice* (impartial, rule-based)
- Women tend to reason in terms of *care* (relationships, responsibility, context)
- Kohlberg's scale undervalues care-based reasoning — women often scored Stage 3
- Not that women are less moral — they're using a *different* moral framework
- Gilligan proposed the **ethics of care** as a parallel developmental sequence

**Current status**:
- Kohlberg's stages are partially replicable cross-culturally but Stage 6 is very rare
- Haidt's "social intuitionist model" (2001): moral judgments are *primarily intuitive* (System 1), not the product of deliberate reasoning (Kohlberg's view). Reasoning is post-hoc rationalization. Evidence: the "harmless taboo violations" that feel wrong but can't be justified rationally (incest between consenting adults with contraception — most people say "wrong" but can't articulate valid harm argument).

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| When do children develop object permanence? | Piaget: ~8 months (reaching). Baillargeon: 3-5 months (looking). Explicit ToM needed for full appreciation. |
| What is the ZPD? | The range between what a child can do alone and what they can do with skilled assistance |
| When do children pass false belief? | ~4-5 years (explicit task); possibly ~13-15 months (implicit looking-time task) |
| Does the marshmallow test predict outcomes? | Much weaker than originally claimed; largely confounded with SES |
| What drives development: nature or nurture? | Both; interactionist consensus. Innate systems (Spelke's core knowledge) + social scaffolding (Vygotsky) |
| Why do autistic individuals fail false belief? | Proposed ToM deficit; but may reflect earlier joint-attention difficulty; many autistic adults pass standard tests |

## Common Confusion Points

**Piaget's stages are sequential, not age-locked**: Ages are averages; there's wide variation. A child from a culturally different background may show concrete operations earlier or later. Cross-cultural studies show the sequence is universal; the ages vary.

**Baillargeon doesn't disprove Piaget**: She showed *competence* exists earlier than Piaget's *performance* tasks revealed. Both are right: knowledge is present early; the ability to act on it (reaching, responding) comes later. This is the performance/competence distinction.

**Vygotsky is not opposed to Piaget**: They have different emphases (social vs. individual construction) and different positions on language/thought direction. Modern developmental psychology integrates both: individual constructivism + social scaffolding.

**Theory of Mind ≠ empathy**: ToM is cognitive ("what does she *believe*?"). Empathy is affective ("how does she *feel*?"). They're related but dissociable — you can have high cognitive ToM with low affective empathy, or vice versa.

**Kohlberg's stages ≠ moral maturity**: Stage 6 reasoning doesn't make someone more virtuous. Haidt's work suggests moral behavior is driven by intuitions and social emotions, not by the ability to reason at a high stage.
