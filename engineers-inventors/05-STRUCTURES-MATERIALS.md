# Structures and Materials — Eiffel, Roebling, Bessemer, Goodyear

## Era Overview

```
MATERIALS REVOLUTION AND GREAT STRUCTURES: 1840–1900
======================================================

  1840s ─── GOODYEAR: Vulcanized rubber (1844).
            Rubber becomes stable across temperature range.

  1851 ─── Crystal Palace (Paxton): prefabricated cast iron and glass.
           First modular building system.

  1855 ─── BESSEMER: Bessemer converter patent. Mass steel production begins.

  1856 ─── First Bessemer steel production (Sheffield, UK).
           Steel price drops from £50–60/ton to £6–7/ton by 1870.

  1869 ─── Suez Canal opens (de Lesseps, French engineering).
           John Roebling dies from tetanus (Brooklyn Bridge toe injury).

  1869 ─── Transcontinental Railroad (US): steel rails, Bessemer steel.

  1883 ─── BROOKLYN BRIDGE (completed): Washington Roebling directs,
           John Roebling's design, Emily Warren Roebling manages.
           Longest suspension bridge in world until 1903.

  1884 ─── HOME INSURANCE BUILDING (Chicago): first skyscraper.
           Steel frame. 10 stories. Bessemer steel columns.

  1889 ─── EIFFEL TOWER: wrought iron lattice. 300 meters.
           World's tallest structure until 1930 (Chrysler Building).

  1900 ─── Steel production: UK, US, Germany all producing millions of tons/year.
           The structural material of the 20th century is established.
```

## Charles Goodyear (1800–1860)

### Bio Snapshot

American inventor from New Haven, Connecticut. Imprisoned multiple times for debt. Obsessed with rubber from the early 1830s — natural rubber was promising but useless: brittle in cold, sticky and smelly in heat. Tested rubber formulations for a decade while going bankrupt repeatedly. Died $200,000 in debt, never profiting from his discovery. The Goodyear Tire company was founded by Frank Seiberling in 1898 — 38 years after Goodyear died — and named in his honor.

### Vulcanized Rubber (1844)

**Natural rubber's problem**:

```
NATURAL RUBBER (LATEX) — THE PROBLEM
======================================

  Natural rubber (polyisoprene from Hevea brasiliensis tree):
    Below 0°C:  Brittle. Cracks. Unusable.
    10–35°C:    Flexible and useful.
    Above 35°C: Sticky, softens. Loses shape.
    At 60°C+:   Melts. Smells.

  Result:
    Waterproof rubber boots sold in US → customers returned them
    melted in summer, rigid and cracked in winter.

  Goodyear's experiment (1839, disputed):
    Accidentally drops rubber-sulfur mixture on hot stove.
    The material doesn't melt. It CARBONIZES at edges but the center
    is stable and elastic across temperatures.
    Insight: heat + sulfur treats rubber.

  WHY VULCANIZATION WORKS (discovered later):
    Natural rubber: long polyisoprene chains, tangled but not bonded.
    Sulfur + heat: creates CROSS-LINKS between chains.
    Covalent sulfur bridges prevent chains from sliding past each other.
    Result: elastic network that returns to original shape.
    Temperature-stable: cross-links hold at high temperature.
    Flexible at low temperature: network bends without shattering.
```

**The patent battles**: Thomas Hancock in England independently developed vulcanization by 1843 (from analyzing Goodyear's samples sent to England) and filed a British patent before Goodyear could. Charles Macintosh's company (makers of the eponymous waterproof coat) used Hancock's process. Goodyear spent much of his life in patent litigation.

**What vulcanized rubber enabled**:
- Reliable pneumatic tires (Dunlop, 1887; without vulcanization, tires would deform in heat)
- Electrical insulation (rubber around wires — the basis of all electrical wiring)
- Hoses, gaskets, belts
- Waterproof clothing
- Soles of shoes
- All modern rubber goods

## Henry Bessemer (1813–1898)

### Bio Snapshot

English inventor. Hertfordshire. Largely self-taught. Over 100 patents on various inventions (including gold paint). Made his fortune from the Bessemer process for mass-producing steel. Later turned his attention to his own newspaper's typesetting processes and to eliminating seasickness (a steamship with a stabilized cabin).

### The Bessemer Process (1855)

**Context**: Before Bessemer, steel was expensive because it required careful controlled heating of iron with charcoal or other carbon sources — wrought iron (too soft) or cast iron (too brittle). "Steel" required skilled puddlers working for hours. The cost was £50–60 per ton.

```
BESSEMER CONVERTER
====================

  PROBLEM: Iron from blast furnace = pig iron (cast iron).
           Too much carbon (2–4%). Brittle. Cannot be worked cold.
           Wrought iron = too little carbon (<0.02%). Soft. Bends.
           Steel = intermediate carbon (0.1–1.5%). Strong, tough.
           How to produce steel reliably and cheaply?

  INSIGHT: Air blast through molten pig iron burns out excess carbon.

  THE CONVERTER:
    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  EGG-SHAPED VESSEL, 15–30 TONS CAPACITY           │
    │                                                    │
    │  Lined with refractory brick                      │
    │  Trunnions for tilting (pour in molten iron,      │
    │  later tilt to pour out steel)                    │
    │                                                    │
    │  Bottom: TUYERES (air injection nozzles)          │
    │  Air blast: ~25 psi through the molten metal      │
    └────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PROCESS:
    1. Tilt converter: pour in 10–30 tons of molten pig iron
    2. Return to upright: start air blast from bottom
    3. Oxygen in air + carbon in iron → CO and CO₂ (burns off)
    4. Silicon, manganese also oxidize (slag forms on top)
    5. Violent reaction: 20–25 minutes. White-hot flames from mouth.
    6. When carbon level right (judgment by flame color): stop blast
    7. Add alloy elements (manganese to re-carburize)
    8. Tilt: pour molten steel into ladles

  RESULT:
    - 10–30 tons in 20–25 minutes
    - No external fuel needed (the reaction is exothermic)
    - Steel price: £50–60/ton (1856) → £6–7/ton (1870)

  STEEL ENABLES:
    - Long-span railway bridges (wrought iron too weak)
    - Steel-framed buildings (the skyscraper)
    - Steel-hulled ships (Brunel's Great Britain was iron; steel ships from 1870s)
    - Steel rails (replace iron rails that broke under heavy traffic)
```

**The limitation**: Bessemer's process required low-phosphorus iron. British and German iron ores were mostly high-phosphorus, which made the steel brittle ("red-shortness"). Gilchrist Thomas and Gilchrist (1878) solved this with a basic lining (limestone) that captured phosphorus in the slag — the "basic Bessemer" or "Thomas" process. This opened up most of Europe's iron ore deposits to steelmaking.

**The Open-Hearth furnace** (Siemens-Martin, 1860s): Bessemer was eventually replaced by the Open-Hearth furnace, which could use scrap steel, was easier to control, and produced more consistent quality. Open-Hearth dominated until the 1950s, when the Basic Oxygen Furnace (BOF) — a modern development of Bessemer's idea — took over.

## John Roebling (1806–1869) and Washington Roebling (1837–1926)

### Bio Snapshot

**John Roebling**: German-American civil engineer. Emigrated to Pennsylvania (1831). Invented the wire rope (1841). Designed and built the Allegheny Aqueduct (1845), Delaware Aqueduct (1848), Niagara Falls Suspension Bridge (1855), Cincinnati-Covington Bridge (1866). Died July 1869 from tetanus after a ferry crushed his foot at the Brooklyn Bridge site.

**Washington Roebling**: John's son. Served in Civil War (built bridges under fire). Took over Brooklyn Bridge construction. Developed caisson disease (decompression sickness) working in the bridge's pneumatic caissons — left partially paralyzed. Directed construction from his Brooklyn Heights apartment window using binoculars, with his wife Emily Warren Roebling as the on-site manager and his communications link to the engineers. Brooklyn Bridge opened 1883.

### Wire Rope

Before Roebling, ropes were made of hemp fiber — strong in tension but rotted and had inconsistent breaking strength. Roebling developed machine-wound wire rope from iron (later steel) wires twisted together in strands, then strands twisted into rope.

```
WIRE ROPE — ROEBLING'S DESIGN
================================

  Advantage over hemp:
    - Higher tensile strength per diameter
    - Does not rot
    - Consistent, predictable failure load
    - Can be inspected visually for broken wires

  Construction:
    Individual wires (smallest unit)
    → Strands (6–8 wires twisted together)
    → Rope (6–8 strands around a core, same twist direction or opposite)

  Twist direction:
    Regular lay: strands twist opposite to wire twist
    Lang's lay: strands and wires twist same direction
    Different properties: different applications

  Roebling's contribution:
    MACHINE-SPINNING process → consistent quality
    SUSPENSION BRIDGE cable: parallel wire bundle (not twisted)
    → wires side by side → individual tension → more efficient
    Brooklyn Bridge cables: each 15.75 inches diameter, 3,578 parallel wires.
```

### The Brooklyn Bridge

```
BROOKLYN BRIDGE — ENGINEERING CHALLENGES
==========================================

  SPAN: 1,595 feet center span (489m).
  LONGEST suspension bridge in world until 1903.

  CHALLENGES:

  1. FOUNDATIONS — PNEUMATIC CAISSONS:
     The towers must sit on bedrock, but the East River is 30–45 feet deep
     with 44 feet of sand/mud above bedrock.
     Solution: pneumatic caissons — wooden boxes, airtight,
     compressed air keeps water out while workers dig down.
     Workers: "sandhogs." Worked in air at 2–3 atmospheres.
     Decompression sickness (the bends) was not understood.
     20+ workers died. Washington Roebling was permanently disabled.

  2. WIRE CABLES:
     Each cable: 5,282 parallel galvanized steel wires.
     Wire strung back and forth across the span (not pre-twisted).
     Air-spinning method: haul one wire at a time across → bundle.
     This method (Roebling's invention) is still used for large suspension bridges.

  3. THE FRAUD:
     J. Lloyd Haigh, the wire contractor, substituted inferior wire.
     About 1/5 of the cable wire was below specification.
     When discovered, John Roebling's original design had included a
     6x safety factor. The substitution reduced this to ~4x.
     Washington added extra suspenders as compensation.
     The bridge has never shown structural problems.

  4. STIFFENING TRUSSES:
     Suspension bridges can oscillate in wind (see Tacoma Narrows, 1940).
     Roebling added diagonal wire stays and stiffening trusses.
     The bridge has survived all wind events.
```

## Gustave Eiffel (1832–1923)

### Bio Snapshot

French civil engineer. École Centrale, Paris. Specialized in iron construction — bridges, viaducts. His company built hundreds of iron bridges across France, Hungary, Peru, and Indochina. The Eiffel Tower was built for the 1889 Paris World's Fair. He also designed the internal iron armature of the Statue of Liberty (Bartholdi's sculpture, Eiffel's structure). After the Eiffel Tower, he turned to aerodynamics and meteorology.

### Iron Lattice Engineering

```
EIFFEL'S STRUCTURAL INNOVATIONS
==================================

  PROBLEM: Building tall iron structures that don't blow over.

  DESIGN PRINCIPLES:
    Wind load increases with height.
    A solid structure: enormous wind force. Requires massive base.
    A LATTICE structure: wind passes through. Much lower drag.

  EIFFEL'S LATTICE BRIDGES:
    Maria Pia Bridge (Porto, 1877): 160m arch span.
    Garabit Viaduct (France, 1884): 165m arch span, 122m above river.
    Calculated using precise structural analysis.
    Each member sized for the actual load it carries.
    Tapered trusses: thicker where stress is highest.

  EIFFEL TOWER (1889):
    300 meters tall. 7,300 tons of iron.
    Designed to withstand 11x the wind load actually experienced.
    The four base legs curve inward in a mathematically precise shape.
    The curve is not aesthetic — it is the structural form that puts
    the tower's weight directly above the foundation regardless of lean.

    AT THE BASE: each of 4 legs covers ~125 m² → 4 hydraulic jacks
    allow leveling. The tower is trued after major temperature changes.

    THERMAL EXPANSION:
    Iron expands ~11mm/°C × 300m height = 18cm across a 50°C range.
    The tower is shorter in winter than summer by ~18 cm.
    The top also leans ~15 cm away from the sun on hot days.

  ORIGINAL PURPOSE:
    Intended as a temporary structure — to be dismantled 1909.
    Saved because it was useful as a radio antenna.
    Still in use as a broadcast antenna.
```

**After the Tower**: Eiffel built the first systematic wind tunnel for aerodynamic research (1909). He conducted drag measurements on objects and established principles for streamlined design. His aerodynamic data was used by early aircraft designers, including the Wright Brothers' contemporaries.

## Common Confusion Points

**"The Eiffel Tower is ugly — they wanted to tear it down."**
It was controversial at first (many Parisian artists and intellectuals protested). It was saved by being useful as a radio/telegraph tower. It became beloved over time. The structure is an engineering masterpiece: the curved base legs are mathematically derived to carry wind loads without shear stress at the foundations.

**"Bessemer steel is the same as modern steel."**
The Bessemer process was replaced by the Open-Hearth furnace (1900s–1950s) and then the Basic Oxygen Furnace (1950s–present). Modern steel uses continuous casting and various alloying elements not available to Bessemer. The principle (blowing oxygen through molten iron) persists in the BOF.

**"Goodyear discovered vulcanization by accident."**
The "accident" story (dropping rubber on a stove) is Goodyear's own account and may be somewhat mythologized — he had been systematically mixing sulfur with rubber for years before the supposed accident. The "eureka" moment was recognizing what the accidental heating revealed, after years of systematic work.
