# Electrical Pioneers — Faraday, Edison, Tesla, Westinghouse, Marconi

## Era Overview

```
THE ELECTRICAL AGE: 1820–1920
================================

  1820 ─── OERSTED: Electric current produces magnetic field.
           Foundation for electromagnetism.

  1831 ─── FARADAY: Electromagnetic induction.
           Changing magnetic field produces electric current.
           → Electric generators possible.

  1834 ─── FARADAY: First homopolar electric motor (rotating disk in magnet).

  1844 ─── MORSE: Operational telegraph. Washington–Baltimore.
           First practical electrical long-distance communication.

  1866 ─── ATLANTIC TELEGRAPH cable operational (second attempt).
           Transatlantic communication: minutes vs. weeks.

  1876 ─── BELL: Telephone patent.

  1879 ─── EDISON: Practical incandescent light bulb. Pearl Street system begins.

  1882 ─── EDISON: Pearl Street DC power station, Manhattan. First central power station.

  1885 ─── WESTINGHOUSE: AC transformer system. Practical long-distance AC power.

  1888 ─── TESLA: AC induction motor patent. Polyphase system.

  1893 ─── WAR OF CURRENTS: World's Columbian Exposition (Chicago World's Fair)
           lit by AC (Westinghouse/Tesla). AC wins the public debate.

  1895 ─── NIAGARA FALLS power station: AC polyphase. 1,000 HP to Buffalo.

  1901 ─── MARCONI: Transatlantic wireless telegraph signal (Cornwall to Newfoundland).

  1904 ─── FLEMING: Vacuum tube diode (valve rectifier). Radio detection.
  1907 ─── DE FOREST: Triode amplifier (audion). Radio amplification.
  1912 ─── TITANIC: Radio distress signals. Wireless saves lives.
```

## Michael Faraday (1791–1867)

### Bio Snapshot

English physicist and chemist. Born to a poor blacksmith family. No formal education — apprenticed as a bookbinder. Read the books he bound. Attended lectures at the Royal Institution and wrote to Humphry Davy asking for a job. Appointed Davy's assistant 1813. Became one of the greatest experimental scientists in history. Refused knighthood and burial at Westminster Abbey — religious modesty (Sandemanian sect).

### Electromagnetic Induction (1831)

**Context**: Oersted (1820) showed that electric current produces a magnetic field. The natural question: does a magnetic field produce a current?

```
FARADAY'S DISCOVERY (August 29, 1831)
=======================================

  EXPERIMENT:
    Two coils of wire wound on an iron ring.
    Primary coil: connected to battery.
    Secondary coil: connected to galvanometer.

  RESULT:
    When battery connected to primary → brief pulse in secondary.
    When battery disconnected from primary → brief pulse (opposite) in secondary.
    When battery steadily connected → NO current in secondary.

  INSIGHT:
    It is not the current in the primary that matters.
    It is the CHANGE in magnetic field that induces current.
    Steady magnetic field → no current.
    Changing magnetic field → induced EMF.

  FARADAY'S LAW:
    EMF = -dΦ/dt
    (EMF is negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the loop)

    The minus sign: Lenz's Law — induced current opposes the change
    (conservation of energy; if it aided the change, we'd have perpetual motion).

  THIS DISCOVERY ENABLES:
    Electric generators: rotate a coil in a magnetic field → changing flux
                        → continuous EMF → continuous current.
    Transformers: AC primary coil → changing B field → AC secondary coil.
    Induction motors: rotating magnetic field → induced current in rotor.
```

**The Electric Motor** (1821): Faraday built the first device to produce continuous rotational motion from electricity — a wire rotating around a magnet (in a pool of mercury, which completed the circuit). The principle: a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force perpendicular to both.

**Faraday as theoretical thinker**: Faraday introduced the concept of "field lines" — lines of force surrounding magnets and charges. He thought of electric and magnetic effects as stresses in space, not as action-at-a-distance. Maxwell later took this intuition and mathematized it as electromagnetic field theory. Without Faraday's field concept, Maxwell's equations might not have taken the form they did.

## Thomas Edison (1847–1931)

### Bio Snapshot

Ohio, Michigan. Briefly attended school — teacher called him "addled"; mother pulled him out and homeschooled him. Self-taught from the public library. Telegrapher at 16. Menlo Park laboratory 1876 — the first industrial research laboratory. Over 1,093 patents (US). Deaf from childhood (possibly scarlet fever, possibly a telegraph-operator blow to the ear).

### The Industrial Research Laboratory

**Menlo Park (1876)**: Edison's most important invention was arguably the process of invention itself — the organized, systematic research laboratory.

```
MENLO PARK MODEL
=================

  BEFORE EDISON (typical invention):
    Individual inventor, working alone or with one assistant.
    Trial and error until something works.
    No systematic division of labor.
    No specialized equipment.

  MENLO PARK:
    15–30 engineers and machinists working together.
    Specialized skills: chemists, machinists, glassblowers.
    Edison as director: identifies goals, monitors progress, provides vision.
    "Minor invention every 10 days, a big thing every 6 months."
    Machine shops, chemical labs, library — all under one roof.
    This is the R&D lab model — still standard in technology companies.

  Edison's results from this model:
    - Phonograph (1877)
    - Carbon microphone (telephone transmitter, 1877)
    - Practical incandescent light bulb (1879)
    - Pearl Street Station / DC power distribution system (1882)
    - Motion picture camera (Kinetoscope, 1891)
    - Alkaline storage battery (years of work, 1900s)
```

### The Light Bulb and DC Power System

**The problem with previous incandescent bulbs**: Incandescence — making a filament glow — was known since 1802 (Humphry Davy's carbon arc). Earlier bulb attempts failed because filaments burned out too quickly. Edison's systematic approach: test 6,000 materials. The winner: carbonized bamboo filament in a vacuum, which lasted 1,200 hours.

**The system insight**: Edison's brilliance was not the bulb itself — it was the entire system:

```
EDISON'S ELECTRIC POWER SYSTEM (1882)
=======================================

  Not just: make a bulb that works.
  But: design the complete system to replace gas lighting.

  SYSTEM COMPONENTS:
    - Power station (central generation, Pearl Street)
    - Distribution network (underground cables)
    - Meters (to charge customers for actual usage)
    - Junction boxes, switches, fuses
    - The bulb itself
    - High-resistance filament (parallel circuit works)

  KEY DESIGN INSIGHT:
    Gas lighting was parallel-connected. Each lamp independent.
    Early electric lights: low-resistance arc lamps in series.
    Add one lamp → changes resistance → all lamps change brightness.

    Edison designed HIGH-RESISTANCE FILAMENTS in PARALLEL.
    Each lamp independent. Add/remove lamps freely.
    High resistance → low current → thin wires possible → economics work.

  Pearl Street (September 4, 1882):
    82 customers. 400 lamps. 1 mile radius.
    DC generation at 110 volts.
    Became the template for electrical utilities worldwide.
```

**The limitation of DC**: Direct current (DC) cannot be stepped up or down in voltage using transformers. To minimize power loss over distance (P = I²R), you need high voltage for transmission. Edison's DC system required a generating station every mile — practical in dense Manhattan, uneconomical elsewhere.

## Nikola Tesla (1856–1943)

### Bio Snapshot

Serbian-American inventor. Studied electrical engineering in Graz, Austria (did not complete his degree). Worked for Continental Edison in Paris, then emigrated to US in 1884. Briefly worked for Edison in New York (famously, Edison allegedly offered $50,000 for improving dynamos, then said he was joking). Worked with Westinghouse. Later years increasingly eccentric and isolated. Died alone in New York City hotel, nearly broke.

### AC Induction Motor and Polyphase Systems

**The problem with AC in 1885**: Alternating current could be transformed to high voltage for transmission, but no one had an AC motor that worked well. DC motors were practical; AC motors were not.

```
TESLA'S AC INDUCTION MOTOR (1887–1888)
========================================

  INSIGHT: A rotating magnetic field can be created by running
  two or three AC currents out of phase through separate coils.

  THREE-PHASE SYSTEM:
    Phase A: sin(ωt)
    Phase B: sin(ωt + 120°)
    Phase C: sin(ωt + 240°)

    Three coils arranged 120° apart → their combined magnetic field
    ROTATES at the frequency of the AC supply.

  INDUCTION MOTOR:
    Place a squirrel cage rotor (conductive bars in a cylinder) inside.
    Rotating magnetic field → induces current in rotor bars.
    Induced current in rotating field → force on rotor → torque.
    Rotor chases the rotating field, always slightly behind (slip).
    No electrical contact to rotor needed. No brushes. Low maintenance.

  WHY THIS MATTERS:
    Self-starting (no starting circuit needed).
    Robust (no brushes to wear out).
    Speed determined by AC frequency (60 Hz in US → 3600 RPM synchronous).
    Easily manufactured at scale.

  EVERY INDUCTION MOTOR IN THE WORLD USES THIS PRINCIPLE:
    Industrial motors, HVAC compressors, pumps, fans, elevators.
    Tesla's motor runs inside virtually every factory and building.
```

### The War of Currents

Westinghouse licensed Tesla's patents and promoted AC for power distribution. Edison promoted DC.

```
WAR OF CURRENTS: TECHNICAL vs. POLITICAL
==========================================

  TECHNICAL REALITY:
    AC advantage: transformer (step up for transmission, step down for use)
                  → high voltage long distance → lower losses
    AC advantage: induction motor (Tesla's — no brushes)
    DC advantage: at the time, AC motors were new; DC motors proven

  EDISON'S CAMPAIGN:
    Publicly electrocuted animals with AC to demonstrate danger.
    Lobbied for AC to be used in electric chairs ("Westinghoused").
    The electric chair was designed to use AC — making AC the "death current."

  WESTINGHOUSE/TESLA WIN:
    1893 World's Columbian Exposition: 100,000 AC lamps.
    1895 Niagara Falls hydroelectric station: AC polyphase to Buffalo.
    AC's transmission advantage was undeniable at the scale of national grids.

  MODERN NOTE:
    Long-distance high-voltage DC (HVDC) transmission has made a comeback
    for very long distances (>600 km) and undersea cables.
    Edison's DC was not wrong — just wrong for the 1890s infrastructure.
    Modern power electronics (IGBT converters) make DC practical at scale.
```

## George Westinghouse (1846–1914)

### Bio Snapshot

American entrepreneur and engineer. Inverted the railway air brake at 22 (1869) — awarded the patent, founded Westinghouse Air Brake Company. Made a fortune. Used it to bet on AC electricity. Founded Westinghouse Electric (1886). Licensed Tesla's AC patents. Built Niagara power station. Later went bankrupt (1907 panic) despite the AC win.

### The Railway Air Brake (1869)

```
WESTINGHOUSE AIR BRAKE — THE PROBLEM
======================================

  Pre-1869 train braking:
    Brakemen stationed on top of cars.
    Engineer whistles signal.
    Brakemen manually turn brake wheels on each car.
    At 30 mph: train travels 100+ feet before brakes applied uniformly.
    Derailments and collisions common.

  Westinghouse solution:
    Compressed air reservoir on every car.
    Single train line runs through all cars.
    Engineer reduces pressure in train line → all car reservoirs
    automatically apply brakes SIMULTANEOUSLY.
    Fail-safe: if train line breaks (cars separate), brakes apply automatically.

  WHY IT WORKED:
    All brakes apply at essentially the same moment.
    Train stops in a controlled, uniform manner.
    Fail-safe by design: brake failure = brakes apply, not release.

  This made high-speed passenger rail possible.
  Westinghouse was 22 years old.
```

**Westinghouse as entrepreneur**: He created the transformer-based AC power distribution system by hiring engineers (including William Stanley, who built the first commercial AC transformer system in 1886), licensing Tesla's motor patents, and building the industrial infrastructure to manufacture and deploy AC systems. He was primarily a system architect and entrepreneur, not a lone inventor.

## Guglielmo Marconi (1874–1937)

### Bio Snapshot

Italian inventor. Irish-Italian parentage. Self-educated in physics, reading Hertz's papers on radio waves. Italian government declined to fund his experiments; he moved to England. Founded British Marconi Company 1897. Shared Nobel Prize in Physics 1909 (with Braun). Later supported Italian Fascism under Mussolini.

### Wireless Telegraphy

```
FROM HERTZ TO MARCONI: RADIO TIMELINE
=======================================

  1864 ─── MAXWELL: Predicts electromagnetic waves travel at light speed.
  1887 ─── HERTZ: Produces and detects radio waves in lab.
           "Of what use is it? It proves Maxwell right."
           Hertz saw no practical application.

  1895 ─── MARCONI, age 21: Transmits and receives over 1 mile (Bologna).
  1897 ─── Transmits across Bristol Channel (14 miles).
  1899 ─── Transmits across the English Channel (30 miles).
  1901 ─── TRANSATLANTIC: Poldhu (Cornwall) to St. John's (Newfoundland).
           3,500 km. The "S" in Morse code received.

  MARCONI'S INNOVATIONS (vs. Hertz):
    - Tuned transmitter and receiver (same resonant frequency)
    - Antenna connected to ground (increases range dramatically)
    - Coherer improved → magnetic detector (1902)
    - Multiple overlapping antennas for directional control
    - Business model: sell equipment AND lease the communication service
```

**Patent disputes**: Oliver Lodge (British) and Aleksandr Popov (Russian) both demonstrated wireless telegraphy around the same time (1895–1896). Whether Marconi's patents were fully valid is disputed. The US Supreme Court voided some Marconi patents in 1943 (ruling in favor of Tesla's earlier patents). In practice, Marconi commercialized wireless communication while his competitors did not.

**Legacy**: Marconi's company became the standard for ship-to-shore wireless. Every ship's radio operator ("Sparks") in the early 20th century was trained on Marconi equipment. The Titanic's radio distress calls used Marconi equipment. The BBC was initially operated under Marconi company licenses.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Edison invented electricity."**
Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction (1831). Edison commercialized electric lighting and power distribution (1879–1882). Electricity as a physical phenomenon long predates either.

**"Tesla's AC was better than Edison's DC."**
For 1890s power distribution over distances greater than ~1 mile: yes. Modern high-voltage DC transmission (HVDC) is actually preferable for very long distances and undersea cables — using power electronics that weren't available in 1890. The current situation: AC distribution grids everywhere, but HVDC for major interconnects.

**"Marconi invented radio."**
He invented the first commercially practical wireless telegraph system. The theoretical foundation was Maxwell (1864) and Hertz (1887). The first person to transmit a wireless signal is disputed among Lodge, Popov, and Marconi, depending on what "transmission" means. Marconi's value was commercialization and the antenna-ground system that made long-range practical.
