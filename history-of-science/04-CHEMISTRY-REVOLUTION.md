# The Chemical Revolution — Phlogiston, Oxygen, and the Quantitative Turn

## The Big Picture

The Chemical Revolution (roughly 1770-1830) is the canonical case of a Kuhnian paradigm shift — a coherent, successful research program (phlogiston theory) displaced by a new one (oxygen chemistry) through accumulation of anomalies and Lavoisier's systematic program of quantitative measurement.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              CHEMICAL REVOLUTION -- TIMELINE                     |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                   |
|  1703         1756   1766   1771    1789    1803   1811   1869   |
|   |            |      |      |       |       |      |      |     |
|   v            v      v      v       v       v      v      v     |
|  Stahl        Black  Cavendish Scheele Lavoisier Dalton Avogadro Mendeleev|
|  Phlogiston   Fixed  Inflamm.  "Fire   Traite   Atomic  Hypothesis Periodic|
|  theory       air    air       air"    Elem.    theory          Table    |
|               CO2    H2       O2 (?)          weights                    |
|                       Priestley O2 1774                                   |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

KEY CONCEPTUAL TRANSFORMATION:
Qualitative: "substances have properties by virtue of containing
             principles (phlogiston, sulfur, mercury)"

Quantitative: "substances combine in fixed weight ratios;
              mass is conserved in all transformations"
```

## Layer 1: Phlogiston Theory — The Paradigm That Worked

### Stahl's System

Georg Ernst Stahl (1659-1734) developed phlogiston theory as a systematic account of combustion and related phenomena. It was not a stupid or obviously wrong theory — it organized a large body of chemical knowledge.

```
PHLOGISTON THEORY:

CORE CLAIM:
Combustible substances contain phlogiston (from Greek: fire)
Burning = release of phlogiston from substance into air
Air "becomes saturated" with phlogiston when it can't absorb more
(explains why fires go out in closed containers)

APPLICATIONS:
+--------------------------------------------------+
| PHENOMENON    | PHLOGISTON EXPLANATION           |
+--------------------------------------------------+
| Combustion    | Substance releases phlogiston    |
| Calcination   | Metal releases phlogiston        |
| (roasting)    | leaving a "calx" (metal oxide)   |
| Respiration   | Animals absorb phlogiston from   |
|               | food, release it via lungs       |
|               | (air in closed vessel gets       |
|               | saturated, animal suffocates)    |
| Reducing calx | Calx + carbon: carbon gives its  |
| back to metal | phlogiston to the calx           |
+--------------------------------------------------+

WHY IT SEEMED TO WORK:
- Explained all three phenomena (combustion, calcination,
  respiration) under one principle
- Predicted: charcoal (pure phlogiston) should reduce calxes --
  correct! (C + FeO --> Fe + CO2, though mechanism misunderstood)
- Productively organized chemical research for 80 years
```

### The Anomaly Phlogiston Couldn't Handle

The decisive anomaly: **weight gain on calcination**.

```
THE WEIGHT PROBLEM:

Phlogiston predicts:
Metal (with phlogiston) --> Calx (without phlogiston) + phlogiston
Mass(metal) > Mass(calx)  [phlogiston left, so calx weighs less]

Observed (carefully measured):
Mass(calx) > Mass(metal)
Iron rusting GAINS weight.
Lead heated in air GAINS weight.

Phlogiston theorists' responses:
1. Phlogiston has "negative weight" or "levity" (makes things lighter)
   -- Defended by some; increasingly ad hoc
2. "Perhaps we're measuring wrong"
   -- Possible but repeatable by multiple experimenters
3. The calx absorbs something from the air
   -- This is the correct answer, but requires abandoning phlogiston
```

Robert Boyle had observed weight gain in the 1670s but explained it as fire-particles absorbing into the metal. The problem sat unsolved in the phlogiston framework for a century.

## Layer 2: The Discovery of Gases

### Setting the Stage for Lavoisier

Before Lavoisier, several chemists isolated and characterized new gases, though they interpreted them in phlogiston terms:

```
GAS DISCOVERIES (phlogiston interpretations in parentheses):

Joseph Black (1756): "Fixed air" = CO2
  Limestone (CaCO3) heated releases a gas that doesn't
  support combustion and extinguishes flames
  (He doesn't call it phlogiston -- good empirical work,
  cautious interpretation)

Henry Cavendish (1766): "Inflammable air" = H2
  Metals + acids --> gas that burns
  Phlogiston interpretation: metals are rich in phlogiston;
  acid releases it as gas
  CORRECT interpretation (later): acid dissolves metal,
  H2 released from acid's hydrogen

Carl Wilhelm Scheele (1771-72): "Fire air" = O2
  Isolated oxygen from heating silver carbonate, etc.
  Did not publish until 1777 -- priority controversy
  Phlogiston interpretation: "fire air" is air depleted of
  phlogiston (backwards from correct interpretation)

Joseph Priestley (August 1, 1774): "Dephlogisticated air" = O2
  Heated mercuric oxide with burning glass
  Candle burns brightly in it; mice live longer in it
  Phlogiston interpretation: air without phlogiston (dephlogisticated)
  -- correct observation, wrong interpretation
  He never abandoned phlogiston, despite being the discoverer of
  oxygen
```

Priestley's discovery is the clearest example of **theory-laden observation**: he observed oxygen but described it in phlogiston terms. Paradigm change required someone who could *conceptualize* the gas differently.

## Layer 3: Lavoisier — The Program of Quantitative Chemistry

### Who Lavoisier Was

Antoine Lavoisier (1743-1794) was not primarily a discoverer of new substances — he was a systematizer who redesigned chemical theory through quantitative measurement.

His day job: **Fermier Général** (tax farmer). He was a financier who collected taxes for the crown, which funded his laboratory and eventually got him guillotined during the Terror (April 8, 1794).

### The Oxygen Theory — Key Experiments

```
LAVOISIER'S CANONICAL EXPERIMENT (1772-1774):

Mercury + air (sealed) --> heated by burning glass
Mercury calx (HgO) forms, air volume decreases

Reverse:
Mercury calx + heat --> mercury + gas
The recovered gas supports combustion better than air.

LAVOISIER'S INTERPRETATION:
Mercury absorbed a specific component of air (oxygen)
during calcination -- that's why the air decreased
and why the calx weighed more.
The same component, released, supports combustion.

VERSUS PHLOGISTON INTERPRETATION (Priestley):
Mercury calx heated --> mercury + dephlogisticated air
(calx "absorbed" phlogiston from mercury,
becoming dephlogisticated air when released)

WHAT DECIDED IT:
Quantitative measurement: the WEIGHT of oxygen absorbed
equals the WEIGHT GAINED by the calx -- conservation of mass.
Phlogiston's negative-weight version cannot account for this.
```

### Conservation of Mass — The Foundational Principle

```
LAW OF CONSERVATION OF MASS (Lavoisier, formalized ~1785):

"In every operation, there is an equal quantity of matter
before and after the operation; the quality and quantity
of the principles are the same; what changes are only
their combinations."

EXPERIMENTAL BASIS:
Sealed retort: reactants --> products, weigh entire system
Mass before = Mass after (to experimental precision)

ANTI-PHLOGISTON ARGUMENT:
If phlogiston is a substance, it has mass.
When it leaves during combustion, the ash should be lighter.
It is sometimes heavier.
Therefore phlogiston is not a substance in the usual sense.

Or: phlogiston doesn't exist.
```

### Lavoisier's Naming Reform

Lavoisier recognized that the old alchemical names encoded phlogiston theory — reform required new names:

| Old Name | New Name | Significance |
|----------|----------|--------------|
| Dephlogisticated air | Oxygen (oxy = sharp + gen = producer) | Names the principle, not the theory |
| Inflammable air | Hydrogen (hydro = water + gen = producer) | Cavendish showed burning H2 makes water |
| Fixed air | Carbonic acid gas | Names composition, not property |
| Calx of mercury | Mercury oxide | Composition-based name |
| Vitriol | Sulfuric acid | Systematic acid nomenclature |

The 1787 *Methode de Nomenclature Chimique* (Lavoisier et al.) reformed chemical naming across the board — systematic names reflect composition. This was a **linguistic revolution** that encoded the new theory. Phlogiston chemists who learned the new names were halfway converted.

### Lavoisier's Treatise (1789)

*Traite Elementaire de Chimie* — the first modern chemistry textbook:
- Defined element operationally (cannot be further decomposed by current methods)
- Listed 33 elements including light and heat (caloric) — not all correct, but principled
- Explained combustion, respiration, calcination using oxygen
- Included conservation of mass as a foundational law
- Organized as an axiomatic system in the Euclidean tradition

The definition of element — "what cannot be further decomposed" — is still operationally correct. Lavoisier's caloric (heat as a fluid) was wrong, but it was a productive error: it led to quantitative calorimetry and eventually to thermodynamics.

## Layer 4: Dalton — Atomic Theory and Combining Ratios

### John Dalton (1766-1844)

Dalton developed atomic theory in the context of explaining **why** elements combine in fixed weight ratios:

```
DALTON'S POSTULATES (1803-1808):

1. Matter consists of indivisible atoms
2. All atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties
3. Atoms of different elements differ in mass and properties
4. Chemical compounds are formed when atoms combine in
   fixed, simple whole-number ratios
5. In chemical reactions, atoms are rearranged but not created
   or destroyed (conservation of mass explained)

THE LAW OF MULTIPLE PROPORTIONS:
If two elements form more than one compound, the weights
of one element combining with a fixed weight of the other
are in simple integer ratios.

Example: Carbon + oxygen:
Carbon monoxide (CO): 12g C + 16g O
Carbon dioxide (CO2): 12g C + 32g O
Ratio: 16:32 = 1:2  (simple integer ratio)
```

### Dalton's Limitations

| Limitation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| No electronic structure | Atoms are indivisible billiard balls |
| Wrong atomic weights | Used "greatest simplicity" rule (H2O not HO) incorrectly |
| No explanation for why atoms combine | Just described the ratios |
| No geometry | Stereochemistry not possible in Dalton's framework |

Dalton's atomic weights were wrong because he assumed the simplest formula when unknown — water is H2O not HO, which he assumed. This gave hydrogen weight 1 and oxygen weight 8 (wrong; should be 16). It took 50 years to sort out.

## Layer 5: Avogadro's Hypothesis — Ignored for 50 Years

### The Hypothesis

Gay-Lussac (1808) had measured combining volumes of gases: hydrogen + oxygen --> water, always in 2:1:2 volume ratios. Dalton couldn't explain this because he thought atoms of the same element can't bond to each other.

Amedeo Avogadro (1776-1856) proposed in 1811:

**Equal volumes of gases, at the same temperature and pressure, contain equal numbers of particles.**

```
AVOGADRO'S HYPOTHESIS EXPLAINS GAY-LUSSAC:

2 H2 + O2 --> 2 H2O
2 volumes + 1 volume --> 2 volumes

If equal volumes have equal particles:
2 H2 molecules + 1 O2 molecule --> 2 H2O molecules

This requires O2 (diatomic), not O atoms.
Dalton's assumption: simplest formula = HO (wrong), O is atomic.
Avogadro's correction: formulas can involve diatomic molecules.
```

### Why It Was Ignored for 50 Years

```
REASONS FOR NEGLECT (1811-1858):

1. BERZELIUS'S ELECTROCHEMICAL THEORY
   Jons Jacob Berzelius (dominant chemist, 1810s-1840s):
   opposite charges attract --> compounds held together electrically
   H2 molecule requires bonding two identical atoms -- no charge
   difference -- impossible in Berzelius's framework

2. CONFUSION BETWEEN ATOMS AND MOLECULES
   No clear distinction yet between "atom" (indivisible particle)
   and "molecule" (combination of atoms)
   If atoms combine to form elemental molecules (H2, O2),
   why don't they all combine into compounds?

3. INSTITUTIONAL DOMINANCE
   Berzelius's authority in European chemistry was extraordinary
   Contradicting him required strong evidence + institutional support

RESOLUTION (1858):
Cannizzaro's paper at Karlsruhe Conference (1860):
systematically applied Avogadro's hypothesis to derive
consistent atomic weights
Showed all anomalies resolved if hypothesis accepted
Within a few years, nearly universally accepted
```

The Avogadro neglect is a Lakatosian case: the hard core of Berzelius's electrochemical theory included "no homoatomic bonds," and the protective belt absorbed inconsistencies. Only when Berzelius's framework became generally degenerating did Avogadro's solution gain traction.

## Layer 6: The Periodic Table — Prediction as Validation

### Multiple Discoverers

The periodic table emerged in the 1860s from multiple independent investigations — a case of simultaneous discovery driven by the availability of Cannizzaro's consistent atomic weights (1858):

```
PRECURSORS:
Dobereiner's triads (1829): groups of 3 elements with
middle element's atomic weight = average of outer two
(Li, Na, K; Ca, Sr, Ba; Cl, Br, I)
-- noticed periodicity but couldn't systematize

Newlands' Law of Octaves (1865):
Elements ordered by atomic weight show repeating properties
every 8th element (like musical octaves)
-- too rigid; ridiculed at Chemical Society ("why not alphabetical?")
-- Newlands was largely correct

MENDELEEV (1869):
Dmitri Mendeleev arranged 63 known elements by atomic weight,
grouped by similar chemical properties.

MEYER (1869):
Lothar Meyer independently produced nearly identical table.
Meyer published slightly later but also deserves credit.
```

### Why Mendeleev Won — Prediction

Mendeleev's table had gaps — positions where the pattern predicted an element that didn't yet have a known occupant. He named them:

```
MENDELEEV'S PREDICTIONS:

Eka-boron (1871 prediction):
Atomic weight ~44, oxide EBO3, specific gravity ~3.5

Discovered as Scandium (1879):
Atomic weight 44.96, oxide Sc2O3, specific gravity 2.99
Close enough to be clearly the predicted element.

Eka-aluminum (1871 prediction):
Atomic weight ~68, specific gravity ~5.9,
low melting point, volatile chloride

Discovered as Gallium (1875):
Atomic weight 69.72, specific gravity 5.91
Gallium chloride GaCl3, low melting point (30C)
Match is remarkably good.

Eka-silicon (1871 prediction):
Atomic weight ~72, specific gravity ~5.5,
valence 4, forms compounds similar to silicon

Discovered as Germanium (1886):
Atomic weight 72.63, specific gravity 5.35
All properties as predicted.
```

This is the clearest **historical case of prediction as scientific validation**. Mendeleev didn't just organize known elements — he predicted unknown ones with enough specificity that their discovery confirmed the theory. This is Lakatosian "novel fact prediction" in its clearest form.

### Why Atomic Weight Ordering Had Anomalies

The periodic table ordered by atomic weight had several inversions (Ar/K, Co/Ni, Te/I). These were known anomalies that Mendeleev explained away or ignored. Resolution came only with Moseley's X-ray spectroscopy (1913) showing atomic number (proton count) is the correct ordering principle.

## Layer 7: Organic Chemistry — Vital Force Refuted

### Wohler's Urea Synthesis (1828)

```
VITAL FORCE THEORY:
Organic compounds can only be produced by living organisms
because they require a special "vital force" (Lebenskraft)
not present in inorganic matter.

This was not obviously wrong: in 1820, NO organic compound
had ever been synthesized from purely inorganic materials.

WOHLER'S SYNTHESIS (1828):
NH4CNO (ammonium cyanate, "inorganic") --> CO(NH2)2 (urea, "organic")
Just heating the salt produced urea -- an animal metabolite.

SIGNIFICANCE:
No vital force needed. Organic chemistry is continuous with
inorganic chemistry -- just involves carbon compounds.
This opened the possibility of synthetic organic chemistry.

CAVEAT:
The sharp "refutation of vital force" narrative is somewhat
oversimplified. Vitalism had many forms; Berzelius himself
said Wohler's synthesis didn't settle the question (ammonium
cyanate came from animal materials -- cyanic acid from urea).
But the conceptual damage was done: organic synthesis became
a legitimate research program.
```

### Kekulé and Structural Chemistry

August Kekulé (1829-1896) developed **structural theory** — the idea that each atom has a fixed valence (bonding capacity) and that molecular structure determines properties:

```
STRUCTURAL THEORY:
Carbon is tetravalent (makes 4 bonds)
Structure of molecule, not just composition, determines properties

ISOMERS explained:
C2H6O can be:
  - Ethanol (C2H5-OH): drinkable alcohol
  - Dimethyl ether (CH3-O-CH3): anesthetic gas
  Same molecular formula, different connectivity, different properties.
  Composition alone insufficient -- need structural formula.

BENZENE PROBLEM:
Benzene C6H6: formula implies many double bonds, but
benzene is unusually stable and doesn't react like alkenes.

KEKULE'S SOLUTION (1865):
Hexagonal ring with alternating single and double bonds.
(Later understood as delocalized electrons -- quantum mechanics
explains the unusual stability)

KEKULE'S OWN ACCOUNT:
He dreamed of a snake biting its own tail (Ouroboros).
This story is probably apocryphal but illustrates that
visual/spatial intuition drove structural chemistry.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Phlogiston was not a stupid theory.** It organized chemistry for 80 years, made correct predictions about reducing agents, and explained why certain materials burn. It had one decisive anomaly (weight gain) that turned out to be fatal.

**Lavoisier did not discover oxygen.** Scheele (1771) and Priestley (1774) did. Lavoisier's contribution was the correct **interpretation** of what oxygen is and how it functions in combustion — and the systematic program of measurement that made the interpretation compelling.

**Avogadro's neglect was not irrational.** Berzelius's electrochemical theory was the dominant framework, and within that framework homoatomic molecules (H2, O2) made no sense. Neglect was not stupidity — it was paradigm-bound reasoning.

**Mendeleev and Meyer discovered the periodic table simultaneously.** Mendeleev's fame rests largely on the precision of his predictions for undiscovered elements. Meyer's contributions are often underemphasized in popular accounts.

**Wohler's urea synthesis did not immediately kill vitalism.** The cleaner statement is that organic chemistry became separated from vitalism as a *research program* during 1828-1860. Vitalism in various forms persisted in biology well into the 20th century.
