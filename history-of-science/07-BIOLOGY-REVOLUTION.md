# The Biological Revolution — Cells, Evolution, and the Molecular Basis of Life

## The Big Picture

The biological revolution unfolded across three conceptual waves: cell theory (1838-1858) established the basic unit of life; evolutionary theory (1858-1940s) explained biological diversity and change; molecular biology (1944-1970s) grounded all of biology in chemistry and information theory.

## Layer 1: Cell Theory

### Schleiden and Schwann (1838-1839)

Cell theory emerged from improved microscopy (achromatic lens corrections of the 1820s eliminated chromatic aberration, enabling higher resolution):

```
CELL THEORY FOUNDERS:

SCHLEIDEN (1838): botanist
- All plant tissues consist of cells
- The cell is the fundamental unit of plant structure
- New cells arise from within existing cells
  (this part was WRONG -- he thought cells formed from
  the nucleus outward, like crystallization)

SCHWANN (1839): zoologist
- Extended Schleiden's claim to animals
- All organisms consist of cells or products of cells
- The cell is the fundamental structural unit of life
- (Also wrong about formation mechanism)

VIRCHOW (1855): pathologist
"Omnis cellula e cellula"
(Every cell from a cell)
Cells only arise from pre-existing cells -- not spontaneously.
This was the CORRECT formation rule.
Virchow applied this to pathology: disease is altered cell function.
```

**Why Virchow's rule matters**: "Every cell from a cell" is a profound anti-spontaneous-generation claim. It means life has been continuous since the origin of life — there has been no interruption. Every cell in your body is a descendant of the first cell that formed ~3.8 billion years ago through unbroken cell division.

### The Cell Theory's Significance for Biology

```
BEFORE CELL THEORY:                AFTER CELL THEORY:

Biology is: taxonomy, anatomy,     Biology is organized around:
           physiology (qualitative) - Cell structure and function
                                   - Cell reproduction
           No unifying framework   - Processes that happen in cells

Disease explanation: miasma,       Disease explanation: altered
humors, divine punishment          cellular function (Virchow)
                                   --> cellular pathology

Growth/development:                Growth = cell division + specialization
mysterious "vital processes"       Development = differential gene expression
                                   (we now know)
```

## Layer 2: Darwin and Wallace — Natural Selection

### The Priority Problem

Charles Darwin (1809-1882) had the theory of natural selection by 1838-1842 but did not publish. His delay was caused by:
- Knowing the social and religious implications
- Wanting more evidence
- Ongoing work on barnacles (1846-1854)

Alfred Russel Wallace (1823-1913) was a naturalist collecting specimens in Malaya. He independently developed natural selection in February 1858 and sent a manuscript to Darwin. Darwin wrote urgently to Lyell: "I never saw a more striking coincidence... even his terms now stand as heads of my chapters."

```
RESOLUTION: JOINT PRESENTATION
July 1, 1858: Linnean Society of London
Wallace's essay + two unpublished Darwin extracts
read to the society.

THOMAS BELL (President): "The year 1858 has not been marked
by any of those striking discoveries which at once
revolutionise... a department of science."

(He was wrong. This is the understatement of 19th-century science.)

Darwin published Origin of Species November 24, 1859.
First edition: 1,250 copies sold on day of publication.
```

### The Four Key Elements of Natural Selection

```
NATURAL SELECTION REQUIRES:

1. VARIATION
   Individuals in a population differ from each other.
   (Darwin knew this empirically; didn't know the mechanism)

2. HERITABILITY
   Variation is passed from parents to offspring.
   (Darwin knew this empirically; Mendel had the mechanism
   but they never connected)

3. DIFFERENTIAL REPRODUCTION
   Some variants leave more offspring than others.
   This is selection -- NOT random.

4. SUFFICIENT TIME
   Small differences compound over geological time.
   Lyell's uniformitarianism established that Earth was old enough.

RESULT: Populations change over time.
Accumulated changes over sufficient time: new species.

DARWIN'S MECHANISM:
Variation + heritability + selection + time -->
adaptation + speciation + the tree of life
```

### What Darwin Didn't Know

```
DARWIN'S GAPS (filled later):

1. MECHANISM OF HEREDITY
   Darwin knew traits are inherited but not HOW.
   He proposed "pangenesis" (gemmules from all body parts
   circulate to gonads) -- WRONG.
   Mendel (1865) had the answer; Darwin never learned of it.
   Darwin's cousin Galton tried to test pangenesis (blood
   transfusion between rabbits -- failed). Darwin's theory was wrong.

2. ORIGIN OF VARIATION
   Darwin could only say variation "just happens."
   De Vries (1900): mutations.
   Genetics (1900-1930s): explained heritable variation.

3. AGE OF EARTH
   Kelvin's thermodynamic calculation: ~20-100 My.
   Darwin needed much more time.
   Rutherford's radioactivity (1904): Earth is billions of years old.
   Darwin was right to need the time; Kelvin was wrong about the age.

4. SPECIATION MECHANISM
   Darwin had natural selection but not a clear mechanism
   for how one species becomes two.
   Mayr (1942): allopatric speciation (geographic isolation)
   as dominant mechanism.
```

### Social Implications and Misuses

```
SOCIAL DARWINISM:
Herbert Spencer (NOT Darwin): "survival of the fittest"
Applied natural selection to human social competition
Conclusion: poverty is natural, helping the poor is anti-evolutionary
This is: (a) not what Darwin said, (b) naturalistic fallacy,
         (c) not even correct evolutionary biology

EUGENICS:
Galton (Darwin's cousin): improve the human stock by selective breeding
Implemented in: US forced sterilization laws, UK marriage restriction proposals
                Nazi Germany extermination program
Biology provided a pseudo-scientific cover.
The Nazi program was explicitly grounded in evolutionary theory's misapplication.

Darwin himself: opposed to slavery (used his influence politically),
did not advocate eugenics, did not draw social policy conclusions
from natural selection.

The misuse of Darwinism for social policy is a sociology-of-science
case study: how scientific theories are deployed in contexts
far beyond their epistemic domain.
```

## Layer 3: Mendel's Genetics (1865)

### What Mendel Did

Gregor Mendel (1822-1884), Augustinian friar and amateur botanist, conducted breeding experiments with peas from 1856-1863. His results:

```
MENDEL'S KEY RESULTS:

EXPERIMENT: Cross tall peas x short peas

F1 generation: all tall  (dominant)
F2 generation: 3 tall : 1 short  (recessive reappears)

MENDEL'S INTERPRETATION:
- Each trait controlled by two "factors" (alleles)
- One factor from each parent
- Factors don't blend -- they're discrete
- "Dominant" factor masks "recessive"

NOTATION (modern):
T = tall (dominant)  t = short (recessive)

Parents: TT x tt
F1: all Tt (all tall -- T dominant)
F2: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt
    3 tall : 1 short (phenotype ratio)

LAW OF SEGREGATION:
Factors separate during gamete formation.
Each gamete gets one factor of each pair.

LAW OF INDEPENDENT ASSORTMENT:
Factors for different traits sort independently.
(True only for genes on different chromosomes)
```

### Why It Was Ignored for 35 Years

Mendel published in the Proceedings of the Natural History Society of Brno, 1866. He sent reprints to prominent biologists. His results were ignored until 1900.

```
REASONS FOR NEGLECT:

1. WRONG AUDIENCE
   Mendelian paper was read to local scientific society.
   The mainstream biology community studying heredity
   (Francis Galton, W.F.R. Weldon) was biometrically oriented:
   they studied continuous variation, not discrete factors.
   Mendel's discrete ratios didn't connect to their program.

2. NOBODY WAS READY FOR THE ABSTRACTION
   "Factors" are an abstract entity -- not cells, not fluids,
   not gemmules. Biologists in 1866 thought in terms of physical
   substances, not mathematical relationships.
   The Mendelian concept was too abstract for its time.

3. DARWIN-MENDEL DISCONNECT
   Darwin's evolution needed heredity.
   Mendel had the mechanism.
   They didn't communicate.
   Darwin apparently had a copy of Mendel's paper in his library;
   the pages were uncut. He never read it.

4. INCORRECT FOLLOW-UP
   The one person Mendel consulted (Karl Nageli, botanist)
   dismissed him and sent him to study hawkweeds (Hieracium).
   Hawkweeds reproduce largely asexually -- Mendel couldn't
   reproduce his ratios. He died thinking his work had failed.
```

**Rediscovery (1900)**: Three botanists independently rediscovered Mendelian ratios:
- Hugo de Vries (Netherlands)
- Carl Correns (Germany)
- Erich von Tschermak (Austria)

All three searched the literature AFTER getting their results and found Mendel's paper. De Vries initially failed to cite Mendel; Correns confronted him; Mendel was properly credited.

## Layer 4: Morgan and Chromosome Theory

### Thomas Hunt Morgan (1866-1945)

Morgan initially **skeptical** of Mendelism and Darwinism. His fruit fly experiments converted him.

```
MORGAN'S FLY LAB (Columbia, 1908 onward):

DROSOPHILA melanogaster advantages:
- 2-week generation time
- 4 pairs of chromosomes (simple genetics)
- ~1000 eggs per female
- Cheap and easy to maintain
- Large numbers --> statistical power

KEY DISCOVERY (1910): WHITE-EYED FLY
Normal flies: red eyes
One mutant male: white eyes

Cross white-eyed male x normal (red) female:
F1: all red eyes
F2: 3 red : 1 white, BUT white is only in males

SEX-LINKED INHERITANCE:
White-eye gene is on the X chromosome.
Males: XY -- only one copy of X-linked genes
Females: XX -- two copies

X^W (white) is recessive; masked in XX females
X^w Y males show white eyes -- only one X, no backup
```

### Genetic Mapping

Morgan's student Alfred Sturtevant (1891-1970) at age 20 had an insight: if genes are arranged linearly on chromosomes, the frequency of recombination between them should reflect their physical distance.

```
GENETIC MAPPING (Sturtevant, 1913):

RECOMBINATION:
During meiosis, chromosomes cross over and exchange segments.
Genes far apart on a chromosome: more likely to be separated.
Genes close together: less likely to be separated.

MEASUREMENT:
Recombination frequency = (number of recombinant offspring)
                          / (total offspring)

1 centimorgan (cM) = 1% recombination frequency

STURTEVANT'S MAP (first genetic map, 1913):
Mapped 6 X-linked genes of Drosophila.
Used recombination frequencies as "distances."
Produced a linear ordering consistent with all pairwise measurements.
This is a remarkable achievement: a map of an invisible structure
inferred purely from breeding experiments.

MODERN VALIDATION:
Genetic maps correspond to physical maps.
Recombination hotspots and coldspots are real.
Genes are linear on chromosomes -- Sturtevant was right.
```

## Layer 5: The Modern Synthesis (1930s-1940s)

### What Needed to Be Unified

In 1920, biology had two incompatible camps:
- Mendelians: genetics explains heredity; mutations create new traits
- Biometricians (Weldon, Pearson): evolution involves continuous variation, Mendelian ratios can't explain it
- Naturalists (field biologists): they observed species formation but couldn't connect it to genetics

### The Synthesis

```
MODERN SYNTHESIS:

Population genetics + natural selection + Mendelian genetics

KEY ARCHITECTS:
Ronald Fisher (1890-1962):
  The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection (1930)
  Showed Mendelian genetics + large population + selection
  = quantitative continuous variation
  (many genes of small effect = continuous distribution)
  Fisher reconciled Mendelians and biometricians.

Sewall Wright (1889-1988):
  Genetic drift: small populations undergo random allele
  frequency changes independent of selection.
  Genetic drift + selection + migration = evolution.

J.B.S. Haldane (1892-1964):
  Mathematical models of selection at specific genes.
  Calculated selection intensity required for observed
  evolutionary rates.

Ernst Mayr (1904-2005):
  Systematics and the Origin of Species (1942)
  Biological species concept: species = reproductively isolated populations.
  Allopatric speciation: geographic isolation -> reproductive isolation -> speciation.

Theodosius Dobzhansky (1900-1975):
  Genetics and the Origin of Species (1937)
  Connected laboratory genetics to field population genetics.
  Showed natural populations have enormous genetic variation.

George Gaylord Simpson (1902-1984):
  Tempo and Mode in Evolution (1944)
  Brought paleontology into synthesis.
```

## Layer 6: DNA and Molecular Biology

### Avery's Experiment (1944)

The fundamental question: what is the genetic material? Protein was the leading candidate (proteins have 20 amino acids -- more information capacity than 4-nucleotide DNA).

Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty published in 1944:

```
GRIFFITH'S TRANSFORMATION (1928):
Heat-killed virulent Pneumococcus (smooth, encapsulated) +
live non-virulent (rough, non-encapsulated) -->
live virulent bacteria appear in mice.
The dead bacteria "transformed" the live ones.
What carried the transformation factor?

AVERY et al. (1944):
Isolated the transforming factor.
Treated it with:
- Protease (destroys protein): transformation continues
- RNase (destroys RNA): transformation continues
- DNase (destroys DNA): NO TRANSFORMATION

Therefore: DNA is the genetic material.

RESISTANCE:
Many prominent geneticists (including Dobzhansky) were skeptical.
Avery himself was cautious: "if DNA is really the transforming
factor -- and there is serious reason to believe it is -- then
nucleic acids must be regarded as possessing biological
specificity."
Avery was not nominated for Nobel Prize; controversy never resolved
during his lifetime. He died in 1955.
```

**Confirmation (1952)**: Hershey-Chase experiment. Bacteriophage (virus infecting bacteria) infects bacteria. Using radioactive labeling:
- ^32P (labels DNA): found INSIDE bacteria
- ^35S (labels protein): found OUTSIDE bacteria

Therefore: DNA, not protein, is injected into bacteria. DNA is the genetic material.

### Watson and Crick (1953)

```
THE DOUBLE HELIX (Nature, April 25, 1953):

INPUTS:
1. Chargaff's rules (1950):
   In DNA: [A] = [T] and [G] = [C] (from any species)
   Base pairing was implied but Chargaff didn't see it.

2. Franklin's X-ray diffraction (Photo 51, 1952):
   Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography of DNA.
   Showed: helical structure, two strands, base spacing.
   Franklin did not give Watson direct access to Photo 51 --
   her supervisor (Wilkins) showed it to Watson without her
   knowledge. This is the "Franklin controversy."

3. Pauling's alpha-helix (1950):
   Linus Pauling used model building to discover the
   alpha helix in proteins. Watson and Crick used the
   same technique for DNA.

4. Electron density data from Franklin/Wilkins lab.

WATSON-CRICK MODEL:
Two antiparallel strands wound in a double helix.
Strands connected by base pairs: A-T, G-C (hydrogen bonds).
Base pairs in the interior, sugar-phosphate backbone outside.

"IT HAS NOT ESCAPED OUR NOTICE that the specific pairing
we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying
mechanism for the genetic material." -- Watson & Crick
```

### The Central Dogma (Crick, 1958)

```
CENTRAL DOGMA:

DNA --> RNA --> Protein

TRANSCRIPTION:         TRANSLATION:
DNA --> mRNA           mRNA --> Protein
(nucleus)              (ribosomes)

"The central dogma states that once 'information' has
passed into protein it cannot get out again." -- Crick

INFORMATION FLOW:
Sequence in DNA specifies sequence in RNA specifies
sequence in protein. Not the reverse.
(With one exception: reverse transcriptase in retroviruses)

REVERSE TRANSCRIPTION EXCEPTION (Temin/Baltimore, 1970):
Retroviruses (including HIV): RNA --> DNA
(reverse transcriptase enzyme)
This doesn't violate central dogma -- it's a special case.
Protein --> DNA never happens.
Protein --> RNA never happens.

RNA WORLD HYPOTHESIS:
Before DNA, RNA may have been both information carrier
AND catalyst (ribozymes).
Ribo-Enzyme: RNA with enzymatic activity (discovered Altman/Cech, 1980s)
This explains origin-of-life chicken-and-egg problem:
which came first, DNA (stores information) or proteins (catalyze)?
Answer: maybe RNA did both.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Darwin did not invent "survival of the fittest."** Spencer coined it; Darwin later used it as a synonym for natural selection in later editions of Origin. Darwin's term was "natural selection."

**Wallace co-discovered natural selection.** Wallace is systematically underemphasized in popular accounts. He developed natural selection independently, in many ways more clearly than Darwin on certain points. He also proposed what we now call "biogeography" as a field.

**Mendel's ratios are only approximate.** Actual ratios vary due to sampling variation, linkage, incomplete penetrance, etc. Mendel's reported ratios are suspiciously clean — some statisticians (Fisher) suspected data smoothing, though the evidence is debated.

**Watson and Crick did not discover DNA.** DNA was identified by Miescher in 1869. Avery showed DNA is the genetic material in 1944. Watson and Crick determined its three-dimensional structure, which revealed the replication mechanism. The Nobel Prize (1962) was for structure, not discovery.

**The Franklin controversy is real but complex.** Wilkins showed Photo 51 to Watson without Franklin's knowledge. Franklin was also working toward the double helix structure independently. She died of cancer in 1958 at 37, and the Nobel Prize was not awarded posthumously to her. Whether she was treated unjustly is clear; exactly how decisive Photo 51 was to Watson and Crick remains debated.
