# Breeding Systems and Parental Care

## The Big Picture

Avian mating systems range from strict monogamy to extreme polygyny and polyandry, with the dominant pattern being social monogamy with extra-pair copulation (EPC). The advent of molecular paternity testing revealed that many "monogamous" birds engage in extra-pair mating — transforming avian behavioral ecology in the 1990s. Parental care is the most elaborate in the vertebrate world, and its distribution (biparental, uniparental male, uniparental female) maps onto mating system ecology.

```
BREEDING SYSTEM OVERVIEW
==========================

MATING SYSTEM CLASSIFICATION:
+------------------+-----------------------------+----------------------+
| System           | Pattern                     | Bird examples        |
+------------------+-----------------------------+----------------------+
| Monogamy         | 1 male + 1 female           | ~90% of species      |
|  Social M.       | pair bond; may have EPC     | Most passerines      |
|  Genetic M.      | no extra-pair young         | Fulmar, some raptors |
+------------------+-----------------------------+----------------------+
| Polygyny         | 1 male + >1 female          | ~2% of species       |
|  Resource defense| male holds territory        | Red-winged blackbird |
|  Female defense  | male herds females          | some peacock-pheasants|
|  Lek             | male displays only          | Grouse, manakins     |
+------------------+-----------------------------+----------------------+
| Polyandry        | 1 female + >1 male          | <1% of species       |
|  Classic         | female defends territory    | Jacana, phalaropes   |
|  Cooperative     | female + multiple male help | Acorn woodpecker?    |
+------------------+-----------------------------+----------------------+
| Polygynandry     | multiple males + females    | acorn woodpecker     |
|  Cooperative     | shared breeding + care      | (groups)             |
+------------------+-----------------------------+----------------------+

WHY ~90% MONOGAMY IN BIRDS (vs ~5% in mammals)?
  Precocial young (Galliformes, Anseriformes): less care needed
  Altricial young (most Passeriformes): intensive care required
  Pair investment: both parents needed for enough food delivery
  -> obligate biparental care -> monogamy stable
```

## Extra-Pair Copulation: The Hidden Dimension

```
EXTRA-PAIR PATERNITY (EPP)
============================

DISCOVERY (molecular era, 1980s+):
  DNA fingerprinting showed social pair ≠ genetic pair
  "Faithful" monogamous species: 5-35% of offspring from EPC
  Result: overthrew simple view of bird monogamy

SPECIES VARIATION:
  House sparrow (Passer domesticus): ~20-30% EPP
  Reed bunting (Emberiza schoeniclus): ~50-60% EPP (very high)
  Dunnock (Prunella modularis): extensive; complex multi-male groups
  Seabirds (albatross, gannet): <5% EPP (colony wide mate familiarity)
  Raptors: low EPP generally

FEMALE BENEFITS OF EPC:
  Better genes hypothesis: seek extra males of higher genetic quality
  Genetic diversity: reduce genetic similarity among offspring
  Insurance: insurance against male sterility or poor genes
  Material benefits: some males provide food during copulation
  Sperm competition: promote best sperm if multiple males present

MALE RESPONSES:
  Mate guarding: follow female closely during fertile period
  Sperm competition: frequent copulation with own mate
  Differential investment: males give less care when low paternity confidence?
    (complex; not always demonstrated; female can counter by hiding fertility)

RESOLUTION:
  In many species, male and female fitness peaks at EPC are misaligned
  Male wants to sire EPP offspring; female wants EPP from quality males
  Result: complex behavioral arms race of signaling and counter-signaling
```

## Nest Types

```
NEST DIVERSITY
===============

CUP NESTS: most common; depression cup of woven material
  Materials: grass, bark, hair, feathers, spiderweb
  Placement: shrub, tree fork, cliff, ground
  Examples: robin, thrush, sparrow

DOMED NESTS: enclosed; small entrance hole
  Better insulation; reduced predator detection
  Examples: wren (Troglodytes), some warblers, long-tailed tit

CAVITY NESTS: excavated hole or natural cavity
  Primary cavity nesters: excavate own (woodpeckers)
  Secondary: use abandoned holes (tits, bluebirds, kestrels)
  Competition for cavities: intense; nest box programs exploit this

BURROW NESTS: excavated in ground or bank
  Kingfisher, sand martin, puffin, burrowing owl
  Protection from aerial predators

PLATFORM NESTS: large flat structure; repeated use
  Eagles, ospreys, herons: accumulate over years; up to 2 tonnes (bald eagle)

PENDANT NESTS: hanging from branch tips; difficult predator access
  Orioles (Icterus), weaverbirds (Ploceidae)
  Weavers: elaborate interwoven structures; male competence signal?

NO NEST:
  Emperor penguin: single egg on feet, covered by brood patch; Antarctica
  Some terns, nightjars: scrape in sand/rock
  Brood parasites: lay in host's nest

GROUND NESTS (cryptic):
  Shorebirds, larks, gamebirds: speckled eggs; camouflage
  Distraction display when predator approaches
```

## Egg Biology

```
EGG CHARACTERISTICS
====================

CLUTCH SIZE:
  Determined by: optimal allocation of parental effort
  Lacks (1954): optimal clutch = maximum lifetime production
    -> smaller clutches when: short breeding season, high adult survival,
       food limited, high predation risk
  Experimental tests: enlarged clutches -> reduced adult survival
  Interspecific variation:
    Albatross, petrels, guillemot: 1 egg (K-strategy; long-lived)
    Blue tit: up to 14 eggs; short-lived; insurance strategy
    Great gray shrike: 5-7 eggs; temperate; variable

EGG STRUCTURE:
  Shell: calcium carbonate + protein matrix (formed in shell gland ~20hrs)
  Membranes: inner + outer shell membranes; gas exchange + pathogen barrier
  Albumen: protein + water; cushion + nutrition
  Yolk: lipid + protein; primary embryo nutrition
  Chalaza: twisted albumen strands; suspend yolk centrally

EGG COLORATION:
  Background color: protoporphyrin (brown-red), biliverdin (blue-green)
  Spots/streaks: added in shell gland during rotation
  Cryptic: ground-nesters (shorebirds, larks); match substrate
  Aposematic: cuckoo eggs mimic host (some populations)
  Conspicuous: cavity nesters (often white; no predator selection)
  Recognition: guillemot individual egg patterns; colony conditions

INCUBATION:
  Most birds: body heat transfer via brood patch (bare skin + vascularity)
  Megapodes: mound heat (fermentation + solar); no contact
  Emperor penguin: male incubates 65 days on feet (-40 C; no food)
  Incubation period: 11 days (passerines) to 80 days (wandering albatross)
```

## Chick Types and Parental Care

```
PRECOCIAL vs ALTRICIAL CHICKS
================================

PRECOCIAL:
  Hatch: eyes open, down-covered, mobile soon after hatch
  Parental role: lead to food, warmth, protection (not direct feeding)
  Examples: ducks, geese, shorebirds, gamebirds
  Incubation: longer (more development before hatch)
  Energetic cost: large egg (more yolk)
  Advantage: chick independence enables large brood sizes
  Grade: superprecocial (megapode: fully independent at hatch; flies day 1)

ALTRICIAL:
  Hatch: naked/downy, eyes closed, helpless
  Parental role: brooding + intensive direct feeding required
  Examples: passerines, raptors, herons, most birds
  Incubation: shorter; chick completes development ex-ovo
  Growth rate: extraordinarily rapid (passerine nestling: <20 days to fledge)
    Robin (Erithacus): 13 days in nest; doubles mass every 2 days initially
  Advantage: shorter nest site exposure; lower predation loss per nest cycle

SEMI-PRECOCIAL / SEMI-ALTRICIAL: intermediate states
  Gulls: hatch with eyes open + down; mobile but parent-fed
  Raptors: helpless at hatch but parent-fed; develop in nest
```

### Parental Investment Strategies

```
WHO CARES? PARENTAL INVESTMENT DISTRIBUTION
=============================================

BIPARENTAL CARE (most birds):
  Both sexes incubate + feed chicks
  Enforced by energetic demands of altricial young
  Males contribute: roughly equal in most passerines
  Conflicts: each parent would benefit from MORE effort from partner
    -> potential for desertion once chicks partially independent

MALE-ONLY CARE (rare):
  Classic polyandry (jacanas, phalaropes):
    Female larger, brighter; defends territory; males incubate + care
    Females may mate with multiple males
    WHY? If male can rear offspring alone, female is freed for additional matings
    Resource-holding potential: female size + aggression exceeds male

FEMALE-ONLY CARE:
  Lek species (grouse, peacock): male contributes only sperm
  Common in polygynous species where male spreads effort across many females

COOPERATIVE BREEDING:
  >3% of bird species breed in groups with helpers at the nest
  Helpers: typically young adults from previous season (often sons)
  Examples: Florida scrub-jay (Aphelocoma coerulescens), superb fairy-wren
  WHY help?
    Kin selection: helpers are relatives -> inclusive fitness
    Ecological constraints: territory vacancies rare -> wait + help
    Experience: helpers gain breeding experience (delayed benefit)
    Direct fitness: group size -> territory + nest success
  Acorn woodpecker: communal breeding; shared granary; complex multi-male
```

## Brood Parasitism

```
BROOD PARASITISM
=================

OBLIGATE BROOD PARASITISM:
  Birds that NEVER build own nests; rely entirely on host species
  ~1% of bird species (~100 spp); multiple independent origins

CUCKOOS (Cuculidae):
  Common cuckoo (Cuculus canorus): European + Asian; textbook case
  Gentes (host-specific female lineages):
    Different female lines specialize on different hosts
    (reed warbler gens, meadow pipit gens, etc.)
    Egg mimicry: each gens lays egg matching host egg color + pattern
    Transmitted: female offspring inherit mother's gens; males don't

  Arms race:
    Hosts evolve: egg recognition + rejection (acrocephalus warblers)
    Cuckoo evolves: better egg mimicry + thicker shells (rejection-resistant)
    Hosts evolve: learned rejection (requires seeing own eggs first)
    Cuckoo counter: rapid laying, remove one host egg to avoid detection

  Nestling:
    Cuckoo: ejects all host eggs within first hours
    Larger than host; powerful ejection; host parents continue feeding
    Cuckoo nestling call mimics multiple chicks; begging frequency very high
    Serotonin theory: cuckoo call triggers extraordinary feeding response

COWBIRDS (Molothrus atteratus):
  Brown-headed cowbird: N. America; >200 host species
  Generalist (vs cuckoo specialist): lay in any suitable nest
  No egg mimicry (generalist can't mimic all hosts)
  Hosts that reject eggs: cuckoo-type behavior evolved
    (Yellow warbler builds floor over cowbird eggs)
  Hosts that accept: naive -> cowbird numbers high in disturbed landscapes
  Conservation concern: forest fragmentation increases cowbird access
    to interior forest nesters (Kirtland's warbler was at risk)

HONEYGUIDES (Indicatoridae):
  Brood parasites of cavity nesters; chick has bill hooks to kill host nestlings
  Guide large mammals (including humans) to bee nests for mutual benefit
    (humans/honey badgers open hive; honeyguide gets beeswax + larvae)
  Mutualism + brood parasitism in same species
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Social monogamy ≠ genetic monogamy**: ~90% of birds are socially monogamous (form pair bonds) but only a fraction are genetically monogamous (no extra-pair offspring). DNA paternity testing showed 5-30% extra-pair young in many "monogamous" species. The pair bond is real; exclusivity is not.

**Female choice in leks**: In lek mating systems, females choose males but males provide nothing material — only sperm/genes. Zahavian handicap principle: male display signals genetic quality. The "sexy sons" hypothesis: females prefer flashy males because sons will inherit flashiness and attract mates. Empirical support varies.

**Brood patch location**: Female passerines develop brood patches (bare vascularized skin) for heat transfer. In some species (waterfowl, phalacrocoracids), males also develop brood patches when they share incubation. In emperor penguins, both sexes have brood pouches (feathered flap over egg on feet).

**Cuckoo gentes are matrilineal**: Female cuckoos belong to one gens (host-specific line); male cuckoos are not gens-specific. Males can mate with females from any gens. Egg-mimicry gene is on the W chromosome (female-specific, equivalent to Y in male-heterogametic birds), so only transmitted through females. This explains why female cuckoos are host-specific but male cuckoos are generalists.

**Altricialness vs intelligence**: Altricial chicks require intensive parental care, but this does not mean they develop slower cognition. Corvids (altricial) are among the most cognitively capable birds. Precocial chicks (geese) are mobile quickly but show less flexible problem-solving. The relationship between developmental mode and adult cognition is not simple.
