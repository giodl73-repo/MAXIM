# Ornithology — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Birds are the most visible and intensively studied class of vertebrates. ~10,600 species; roughly one new species described per year from cryptic splits. The modern synthesis of avian biology integrates Darwinian macroevolution with comparative genomics, behavioral ecology, and conservation biology. The molecular phylogenetic revolution (since ~1990) overturned most of the classic morphology-based taxonomy.

## Major Lineages: The Phylogenetic Map

```
AVES PHYLOGENY (Jarvis et al. 2014; Prum et al. 2015)
=======================================================

PALAEOGNATHAE (flightless ratites + tinamous)
  Struthioniformes  -- ostriches (Africa)
  Rheiformes        -- rheas (South America)
  Casuariiformes    -- emus + cassowaries (Australia)
  Apterygiformes    -- kiwis (New Zealand)
  Dinornithiformes  -- moas (NZ; extinct)
  Tinamiformes      -- tinamous (C/S America; CAN fly)

NEOGNATHAE (all other birds)
  |
  +-- GALLOANSERAE
  |   Galliformes   -- chicken, turkey, grouse, quail (~290 spp)
  |   Anseriformes  -- ducks, geese, swans (~170 spp)
  |
  +-- NEOAVES (the big radiation -- ~95% of birds)
      +-- Columbimorphae: Columbidae (pigeons), Mesitornithidae
      +-- Mirandornithes: Phoenicopteriformes (flamingos), Podicipediformes (grebes)
      +-- Strisores: Apodiformes (swifts+hummingbirds), Caprimulgiformes (nightjars)
      +-- Opisthocomiformes: Hoatzin (strange; leaves fermenting crop)
      +-- Gruiformes: cranes, rails (~200 spp)
      +-- Charadriiformes: shorebirds, gulls, terns, alcids (~350 spp)
      +-- Aequornithes: loons, penguins, albatrosses, boobies, herons
      +-- Accipitrimorphae: eagles, hawks (Accipitriformes)
      +-- Strigiformes: owls (~230 spp)
      +-- Coraciimorphae: kingfishers, rollers, bee-eaters
      +-- PASSERIFORMES (perching birds) -- ~6,500 spp; 60% of all birds
```

Passeriformes is by far the largest order: ~6,500 species in ~140 families. Within Passeriformes, the Oscines (songbirds) with learned vocal learning (~4,500 spp) and the Suboscines (~1,000 spp, mostly South America) are the two major groups.

## Key Diagnostic Features

```
BIRD DIAGNOSTIC CHARACTER SUITE
==================================

SKELETAL:
  Fused clavicles (furcula = "wishbone")
  Keeled sternum (for flight muscle attachment) -- absent in ratites
  Pneumatic bones (hollow; connected to respiratory system)
  Fused pelvis (synsacrum)
  Fused ankle (tarsometatarsus)
  Reduced/fused digits on wing (carpometacarpus)

INTEGUMENTARY:
  Feathers (unique to Aves; modified reptilian scales)
  Bill (no teeth in modern birds; but Hesperornithes had)
  No external ears (feather auricular coverts)
  Scales on tarsi/feet (conserved reptilian feature)

PHYSIOLOGICAL:
  Endothermy (homeothermy): core T maintained ~41 C
  Avian lung: parabronchial, unidirectional air flow (+ air sacs)
  4-chambered heart; complete double circulation
  High metabolic rate: ~5-10x mammals of same size
  Uricotely: excrete uric acid (conserves water; embryo in shell)
  Binocular/monocular vision configuration varies by ecology
```

## Feathers: Structure and Function

```
FEATHER TYPES AND FUNCTIONS
=============================

CONTOUR FEATHERS: outer surface; aerodynamic; waterproofing
  Rachis (shaft) -> barbs -> barbules -> hamuli (hooks)
  Hamuli zipper barbules together (stroke of preening re-zips)
  Vaned: symmetrical (body feathers)
  Asymmetrical: flight feathers (aerodynamic asymmetry = lift)

FLIGHT FEATHERS:
  Primary feathers: distal wing; thrust + steering (10 per wing)
  Secondary feathers: proximal wing; lift (6-40 per wing by species)
  Tertials: innermost secondaries; rest against body
  Tail feathers (rectrices): braking, steering, display (12 typically)

DOWN FEATHERS: insulation; no hamuli; fluffy
  Undercoat in most birds
  Remicle down (aftershaft): both insulation + structural

SEMIPLUME: intermediate; structure + insulation

FILOPLUME: sensory; detect contour feather movement; proprioception

BRISTLE: around bill in insectivores; tactile; filter feeders

FUNCTION SPECTRUM:
  Flight -> Insulation -> Waterproofing -> Display -> Camouflage
  Brood patch: brood spot where feathers lost for incubation
```

## The Avian Respiratory System

```
AVIAN LUNG: UNIDIRECTIONAL AIRFLOW
=====================================

PROBLEM: Flight metabolic demand >> mammalian lung efficiency
SOLUTION: Cross-current gas exchange + unidirectional air flow

ANATOMY:
  Lungs: rigid, don't change volume
  Air sacs (9): anterior group + posterior group
    Act as bellows; lung doesn't move

AIRFLOW CYCLE (2 breath cycles for complete turnover):
  Inspiration 1:
    Air -> trachea -> posterior air sacs (air sacs expand)
  Expiration 1:
    Air pushed from posterior sacs -> lungs (parabronchi)
  Inspiration 2:
    Fresh air -> posterior sacs; spent air from lungs -> anterior sacs
  Expiration 2:
    Anterior sacs contract -> air exits via trachea

RESULT:
  Air flows through lungs in ONE direction (caudal -> cranial in parabronchi)
  Cross-current exchange with blood capillaries
  Efficiency: ~30% O2 extraction per pass (vs ~25% mammal tidal)
  At high altitude: bar-headed geese have higher affinity hemoglobin
  + parabronchial efficiency enables flight at 8,000m+
```

### Engineering Bridges

The Linnaean classification hierarchy is a strict tree — each node has exactly one parent, ranks are fixed, and every organism occupies exactly one position. This is a normalized taxonomy, structurally equivalent to a schema with enforced foreign-key hierarchy: kingdom → phylum → class → order → family → genus → species maps cleanly to five or six levels of a parent-child table. The analogy breaks exactly where ontology design breaks: when you need to represent multiple inheritance (hybrids) or when the hierarchy is contested (taxonomic splits), a pure tree fails and you need a directed acyclic graph.

Cladistics is that DAG. Modern phylogenetic trees are not strict Linnaean hierarchies — they are bifurcating trees (approximately DAGs with no cycles) where node positions are inferred, edges are weighted by evolutionary distance, and disagreements between datasets produce polytomies (unresolved branching). Taxonomic checklists that disagree about species limits (IOC vs. Clements vs. eBird) are versioned ontology conflicts — same data, different edge definitions, incompatible schemas. The checklist wars in ornithology are structurally identical to the problem of merging two independently evolved domain models.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Reptiles" is paraphyletic**: Birds are crown reptiles (Archosauria). A cladogram of reptiles without birds is paraphyletic. Crocodilians are the sister group — more closely related to birds than to lizards or snakes.

**Ratites are not monophyletic**: Classic view: ratites (flightless paleognaths) form a natural group. Molecular data: kiwis are closer to extinct moas; ostriches are the sister to all other paleognaths; flightlessness evolved independently multiple times (2-5 events) in the group.

**Tinamous are ratites that fly**: Tinamous (South America) have a similar molecular position to ratites but retain flight. They have a deeply keeled sternum — flight muscles intact. This is part of the evidence for multiple independent flightlessness in the clade.

**Passeriformes phylogeny is still being resolved**: Internal ordinal topology within Neoaves was one of the most difficult problems in vertebrate phylogenetics. Different datasets (Jarvis 2014 vs Prum 2015) produced different topologies. The Neoaves radiation was rapid (10-15 My after K-Pg) → poor lineage sorting signal.

**Species counting is dynamic**: ~10,600 species is the consensus of one major checklist (Clements/eBird or IOC). Species concepts differ: some checklists recognize 11,000+ using more splitting criteria. New species are regularly added via range expansions, remote surveys, and splitting formerly "polytypic" species by vocalization.
