# Bird Song and Communication

## The Big Picture

Bird song is a model system for studying vocal learning, sexual selection, neural plasticity, and cultural evolution. The parallels with human language learning — critical periods, template-matching, cultural transmission — made avian song one of the most productive model systems in neuroscience. The vocal learning system has evolved independently in songbirds, parrots, and hummingbirds (the three avian vocal learners), with convergent brain circuitry.

```
BIRD COMMUNICATION OVERVIEW
=============================

ACOUSTIC:
  Song:  complex, learned vocalization; territorial + mate attraction
  Call:  brief, often innate; contact, alarm, flock cohesion
  Drum:  non-vocal (woodpeckers: bill on resonant surface)

VISUAL:
  Plumage: coloration, patterns, iridescence; species + sex recognition
  Display: posture, crest, tail spreading; courtship + threat
  Mimicry: brood parasites (Vidua finches mimic host plumage)

CHEMICAL:
  Preen gland (uropygial) secretions: some evidence for mate choice
  Crested auklet: citrus scent; possible signal
  Limited evidence compared to mammals + insects

SCALE OF VOCAL DIVERSITY:
  Oscine songbirds: largest vocal repertoires in birds
  Brown thrasher (Toxostoma rufum): >3,000 song types
  Nightingale (Luscinia megarhynchos): ~200 song types
  Sedge warbler (Acrocephalus schoenobaenus): large repertoire;
    repertoire size correlates with arrival date + female preference
```

## Acoustic Structure of Bird Song

```
SONG STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS
==========================

SPECTRAL ANALYSIS (sonogram = time x frequency x amplitude):
  Element (note): smallest unit; single continuous sound
  Syllable:       repeated unit; one or more elements
  Phrase:         combination of syllables
  Song:           complete vocalization (one territorial sequence)
  Bout:           series of songs with pauses

  Example (chaffinch, Fringilla coelebs):
    [intro phrases: descending pitch elements]
    [trill: rapid repeated syllable]
    [terminal flourish: upswept "zip"]
    Total: ~2.5 seconds

FREQUENCY RANGE:
  Most birds: 1-8 kHz
  Lowest: kakapo (Strigops habroptila) booming: ~100 Hz
  Highest: brown creeper (Certhia americana): >10 kHz
  Temporal resolution: birds hear faster temporal patterns than humans
    Can hear separately two clicks 2ms apart (humans: ~5ms)

SOUND PRODUCTION:
  SYRINX (not larynx):
    Located at tracheal bifurcation (bronchi junction)
    Pairs of membranes (tympaniform membranes)
    Intrinsic muscles (oscines: 4-7 pairs; suboscines: 0-2 pairs)
    Two-sided: each bronchus can produce different notes simultaneously
    -> "two-voice phenomenon": polyphonic production
    Hermit thrush: simultaneously produces two independent frequency sweeps
```

## Vocal Learning: The Neural System

```
AVIAN VOCAL LEARNING NEURAL CIRCUIT
======================================

THREE GROUPS OF VOCAL LEARNERS:
  Oscines (songbirds): ~4,500 spp; classic learners
  Parrots (Psittaciformes): ~350 spp; independent evolution
  Hummingbirds (Trochilidae): ~360 spp; independent evolution

  All three evolved independently convergent neural circuits for
  vocal learning -- "vocal learning evolved multiple times"
  (but from different ancestors, in different brain regions)

SONGBIRD BRAIN CIRCUITS (best studied, in Taeniopygia guttata -- zebra finch):

  POSTERIOR VOCAL LEARNING PATHWAY (AFP -- Anterior Forebrain Pathway):
    Necessary for song acquisition and modification
    Not needed for production of learned song in adults
    +----------+     +-----------+     +----------+
    |Area X    | --> | DLM (thal)| --> |LMAN      |
    |(basal    |     |           |     |(lat. mag. |
    | ganglia) |     |           |     | nucleus) |
    +----------+     +-----------+     +----------+
                                           |
                                           v
                                        RA (robust nucleus
                                        arcopallium)

  MOTOR PATHWAY (direct song production):
    HVC -> RA -> nXIIts (hypoglossal motor nucleus) -> syrinx
    HVC: timing/sequence; high-order command
    RA: motor program; recruits syringeal muscles

  AUDITORY INPUT:
    Field L (auditory cortex analogue) -> HVC + LMAN
    Provides auditory feedback for comparison with template

SONG LEARNING PHASES:
  Critical period (sensitive phase):
    Young bird memorizes tutor song as template
    Subsong phase: babbling; no structured output
    Plastic song: imprecise but identifiable elements
    Crystallization: stereotyped adult song
    After crystallization: limited modification possible
```

## Song Learning: The Process

```
SONG LEARNING DEVELOPMENTAL SEQUENCE
======================================

PHASE 1: MEMORIZATION (before plastic song)
  Young bird HEARS tutor song -> stores template
  Timing: <90 days post-hatch (zebra finch); can persist longer in wild
  Template: neural representation of target song
  Mechanism: auditory memory, not motor output yet

PHASE 2: SENSORIMOTOR LEARNING (plastic song)
  Bird produces vocalizations; compares with template via auditory feedback
  Trial-and-error refinement: iterative adjustment
  AFP pathway active: hypothesis testing; error signal
  Babbling -> subsong -> plastic song -> convergence toward template

PHASE 3: CRYSTALLIZATION
  Adult song stabilizes; becomes stereotyped
  AFP less active; motor pathway (HVC-RA) dominant
  Bilateral connection from AFP to motor pathway pruned
  Some species (canary, mockingbird): annual plastic phase
    -> add/modify songs seasonally; HVC neurons regenerate

SONG DIALECTS:
  Cultural transmission creates geographic song dialects
  Adjacent populations have similar songs; distant populations differ
  Young birds learn from local males -> copy local dialect
  Dialect boundaries: can be sharp (forest-based species) or gradual
  Hybrid zones: young birds learn from both dialect types -> intermediate

SENSITIVE PERIOD EXPERIMENTS:
  Marler (1970s): white-crowned sparrow
    Normal development: learn species-typical song from tutor
    Deafened before critical period: develop subsong, never crystallize
    Isolated (no tutor): produce simple "innate" species song (template only)
    Taped tutor: learn from recording -> confirms auditory not visual/social
    Taped heterospecific tutor: don't learn alien song -> species recognition filter
```

## Song Function: What Song Does

```
SONG FUNCTIONS
===============

TERRITORIAL DEFENSE:
  Males sing to repel intruding males
  Familiar neighbor: reduced response ("dear enemy" effect)
    Neighbors are less threatening than strangers
  Stranger: aggressive response (playback experiments confirm)
  Song posts: elevated, visible singing locations -> acoustic + visual
  Night singing: some species sing through night (nightingale, mockingbird)

MATE ATTRACTION + ASSESSMENT:
  Females assess song for mate quality
  Repertoire size: larger = older/better condition?
    Marsh wren, great reed warbler: large repertoire -> female preference
    Sedge warbler: repertoire size predicts arrival date; early = high quality
  Song rate: correlates with male condition (honest signal)
  Complexity: developmental stress reduces song complexity (Lemon et al.)
    Young birds in poor condition develop less complex songs
    -> song = developmental stress indicator (Zahavian handicap)

SPECIES RECOGNITION:
  Closely related species in sympatry: more divergent songs (character displacement)
  Playback studies: birds respond to own species song; not heterospecific
  Hybrid zones: intermediate songs; sometimes reduced female response

CONTACT CALLS:
  Maintaining flock cohesion (corvids, tits)
  Parent-chick recognition (gulls, penguins) -- essential in dense colonies
  Alarm calls: interspecific mobbing calls (chickadee, tit species)
    Mobbing calls understood across species boundaries
```

## Mimicry

```
VOCAL MIMICRY IN BIRDS
========================

SPECIES MIMICRY:
  Lyrebirds (Menura: Australia): most extraordinary mimic
    Superb lyrebird: mimics up to 20 species; chainsaws, cameras
    Mimicry: expands effective repertoire; female preference for complexity
  Mockingbirds (Mimus): ~200 mimicked species in individual's repertoire
  Marsh warbler (Acrocephalus palustris): mimics ~80 African + European species
    European population: incorporate African species learned in winter quarters

ACOUSTIC MIMICRY IN BROOD PARASITISM:
  Vidua finches (Viduidae): obligate brood parasites of estrildid finches
    Nestlings mimic host nestling calls + mouth patterns
    Male adult mimics male host song -> attracts female host (not finch female)
    Speciation: Vidua species speciation tracks host speciation
  Common cuckoo (Cuculus canorus): egg mimicry (not vocal)
    But: cuckoo female call deters host from nest re-use

FUNCTIONAL ROLE DEBATE:
  Mimicry as "dishonest advertising"?
    -> fool rivals with sounds of multiple species = larger territory?
  "Hard to imitate" hypothesis: complex mimicry = honest fitness signal?
  Both may operate in different systems
```

## Lek Systems and Elaborate Vocal Display

```
LEK SYSTEMS AND SONG
======================

LEK: aggregated male display ground; females visit to choose mates
  Males: pure mating role; no parental care
  Selection: intense directional selection -> elaborate display

ACOUSTIC LEKS:
  Kakapo (Strigops habroptila -- New Zealand):
    Males excavate "bowl" to amplify booming call
    Infrasound component (100-200 Hz): carries > 5 km
    Males boom for months; females visit
    100% lekking; most endangered parrot (conservation success story)

  Cock-of-the-rock (Rupicola spp.): visual + acoustic lek
    Males: bright orange plumage; compete for display perches
    Social hierarchy: dominant male gets most matings

  Black grouse (Tetrao tetrix):
    Early morning lek display: bubbling "rookooing" + tail fanning
    Males fight for central lek positions -> most matings

COMPARATIVE PATTERN:
  Lek species: intense male-male competition + elaborate display
  Pair-bond species: more subdued display; pair coordination important
  Cooperative display: some manakins (Chiroxiphia) -- two males coordinate
    display; junior male helps senior male gain matings -> kin selection
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Song vs call**: Both are vocalizations but functionally and structurally different. Song is typically longer, more complex, learned (in oscines), and used for territory/mate attraction. Calls are shorter, often innate, and used for contact/alarm. The distinction is blurry in some species.

**Vocal learning evolved 3 times in birds**: Oscines, parrots, and hummingbirds all learn vocalizations, but from independent evolutionary origins. The brain circuits are convergent (similar areas involved) but not homologous. This is one of the best examples of convergent neural evolution.

**Mimicry ≠ learning**: A bird that mimics another species is demonstrating vocal learning ability, but learning doesn't require mimicry. White-crowned sparrows learn conspecific song without mimicking other species. Mimicry is one application of a general vocal learning capacity.

**Song template**: The template is not just "hear song -> output song." Deafened birds develop abnormal songs even if they heard normal songs before deafening. The template must be maintained by ongoing auditory feedback (self-monitoring). Deafening after crystallization gradually degrades song in species that don't have annual re-learning.

**Syrinx vs larynx**: Birds have a syrinx; humans have a larynx. Different anatomical position (bronchial junction vs top of trachea) and different developmental origin. The syrinx has two independent halves; the larynx doesn't. This is why birds can produce polyphonic sounds and humans cannot.
