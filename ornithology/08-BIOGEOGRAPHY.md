# Biogeography and Island Biology

## The Big Picture

Avian biogeography is the study of how bird species are distributed across the planet — and why. It integrates evolution (speciation, extinction), ecology (dispersal, niche limits), and geology (continental drift, climate history). Birds are the premier biogeographic study group because they are well-documented, can disperse over water, and show clear patterns at multiple scales. Island birds provided the empirical basis for the Theory of Island Biogeography (MacArthur and Wilson 1967), one of ecology's most influential frameworks.

```
BIOGEOGRAPHIC PATTERNS AND PROCESSES
========================================

PATTERN                           PROCESS
-------                           -------
Endemism (unique species)    <--  Isolation + divergence
Latitudinal diversity        <--  Productivity + stability gradients
gradient (more spp tropics)
Island species-area          <--  Equilibrium of immigration + extinction
relationship
Disjunct distributions       <--  Vicariance (land bridge + tectonic)
                                  OR long-distance dispersal
Ecological equivalents       <--  Adaptive radiation (convergent or
across continents                  independent niche filling)
```

## Biogeographic Regions

```
WORLD BIOGEOGRAPHIC REGIONS (Sclater 1858; Wallace 1876)
==========================================================

  NEARCTIC        North America
  NEOTROPICAL     South + Central America
  PALEARCTIC      Europe, N Africa, N Asia, Japan
  AFROTROPICAL    Sub-Saharan Africa
  INDO-MALAYAN    India, SE Asia, Philippines
  AUSTRALASIAN    Australia, New Guinea, NZ
  OCEANIC         Pacific islands
  ANTARCTIC       Antarctica + Southern Ocean

BIRD SPECIES RICHNESS BY REGION:
  Neotropical: richest (~3,700 breeding spp)
  Afrotropical: 2nd (~2,300 spp)
  Indo-Malayan: 3rd
  Australasian: ~1,800 spp
  Palearctic: ~1,100 spp
  Nearctic: ~900 spp

LATITUDINAL GRADIENT:
  Tropics: more species at all taxonomic levels
  Causes (multiple, debated):
    1. Evolutionary time: tropics stable longer -> accumulation
    2. Productivity: more energy -> more niches
    3. Climate stability: less extinction risk
    4. Area: more land area in tropics
    5. Niche packing: narrower niches -> more species
```

## Island Biogeography Theory

### The MacArthur-Wilson Model

```
MacARTHUR-WILSON EQUILIBRIUM MODEL (1967)
==========================================

KEY INSIGHT: island species richness is DYNAMIC equilibrium
             between immigration and extinction

  IMMIGRATION RATE:        EXTINCTION RATE:
  decreasing in S          increasing in S
  (as S -> mainland pool,  (more species -> lower avg
  fewer new immigrants)     population size -> more
                            extinction)

  Rate                         S*
   |  Immigration  Extinction
   | \            /
   |  \          /
   |   \        /
   |    \      /
   |     \    /
   |      \  /
   |       \/      <- Equilibrium species number (S*)
   |
   +---S*---------S (species richness)

PREDICTIONS:
  S* increases with island AREA (larger -> less extinction)
  S* decreases with island DISTANCE from mainland (less immigration)
  TURNOVER: even at S*, species composition changes (extinctions balanced
             by new colonizations)

SPECIES-AREA RELATIONSHIP:
  S = cA^z
  (log-log form: log S = log c + z * log A)
  z typically 0.2-0.4 for true oceanic islands
  z ~ 0.1-0.2 for continental habitat patches
  Practical implication: habitat fragmentation is mathematically predicted
  to reduce species richness proportionally to area loss
```

### Tests of the Theory

```
EMPIRICAL TESTS
================

DEFAUNATION EXPERIMENT (Simberloff + Wilson 1969):
  Small mangrove islands (Florida Keys) fumigated -> all arthropods removed
  Monitored recolonization over 2 years
  RESULT: species richness recovered to pre-fumigation levels
          composition different; turnover demonstrated
  CONFIRMED: immigration/extinction equilibrium
  LIMITATION: arthropods not birds; small islands; short time

KRAKATAU ERUPTION (1883):
  Volcanic island sterilized; complete biota loss
  Bird recolonization documented ~1908-1980s
  ~36 species by 1920; leveled off ~30-40 species
  CONFIRMED: recolonization from adjacent Sumatra/Java
  PROBLEM: not controlled experiment; area of original island changed

BIRD POPULATION SURVEYS:
  Many island ornithological surveys confirm S-A relationship
  British Isles + offshore islands: clean log-linear S vs A relationship
  Caribbean islands: area + distance effects both documented
  Colonization rates: small isolated islands lower than large close ones
```

## Island Evolution: The Adaptive Radiation

```
ISLAND ADAPTIVE RADIATION
============================

DEFINITION: Single colonist lineage diversifies into multiple ecological niches
            (formerly occupied by diverse mainland species or vacant)

DARWIN'S FINCHES (Galapagos; Geospizinae in Thraupidae):
  Colonization: ~2 Mya; single South American finch ancestor
  Current: 18 species (some treat 15) across different islands
  Diversification: bill shape + size adapted to food type

  BILL TYPES:
  +-----------------------------------+
  | Species              | Diet       |
  +-----------------------------------+
  | Geospiza magnirostris| Large seeds|
  | G. fortis           | Med seeds  |
  | G. fuliginosa       | Small seeds|
  | Platyspiza crassirostris | Buds   |
  | Cactospiza pallida  | Cactus+tool|
  | Camarhynchus spp.   | Insects    |
  +-----------------------------------+
  Tool use: woodpecker finch uses spine/twig to extract larvae
  (one of few known tool-using birds)

  ADAPTIVE RADIATION MECHANISM:
  Character displacement: competing species diverge in bill size
    -> reduce niche overlap -> coexistence
  Documented: Geospiza fortis bill size depends on which other
    Geospiza species is present (Grant & Grant multi-decade study)

HAWAIIAN HONEYCREEPERS (Drepanidinae):
  ~50 species (many extinct); single rosefinch ancestor ~5-7 Mya
  Bill diversity: extraordinary (broader than Darwin's finches)
  Nectar feeders (sickle bills), seed eaters (parrots bills), insectivores
  40 species extinct since Polynesian + European contact
  Remaining ~20+ species threatened by avian malaria + pox
  (Introduced Culex mosquitoes carry Plasmodium relictum -- devastating to naive birds)
```

## Dispersal vs Vicariance

```
DISPERSAL vs VICARIANCE IN BIOGEOGRAPHY
==========================================

VICARIANCE: geographic barrier SPLITS an ancestral population
  -> allopatric speciation without dispersal
  Example: Atlantic Ocean opens (100 Mya) -> African vs S. American birds diverge
  Tectonic prediction: sister species on opposite sides of barrier

DISPERSAL: colonization of new area by crossing a barrier
  Example: single passerine colonizes isolated island -> speciation
  Prediction: molecular dating gives colonization age; compare with barrier age

DISTINGUISHING THEM:
  If bird lineage age = barrier age -> vicariance (continental drift)
  If bird lineage age younger than barrier -> dispersal (across existing barrier)

EXAMPLES:
  Ratites: classic vicariance claimed (Gondwana breakup carried them apart)
    MOLECULAR REFUTATION: ratite divergence dates YOUNGER than Gondwana separation
    -> multiple DISPERSAL events (ancestors flew; flightlessness evolved after)
    This overturned 50 years of vicariance-based ratite biogeography

  Passeriformes: Gondwana origin (molecular)
    Originated in Australia/Gondwana; dispersed globally
    Major passerine families have Gondwana molecular divergences
    -> then colonized Northern Hemisphere (contra former "northern hemisphere origin")

  Transoceanic dispersal: many documented
    Cattle egret (Bubulcus ibis): colonized Americas from Africa ~1940s; confirmed
    Barn owl: crossed Pacific independently multiple times (molecular evidence)
```

## Endemism Hotspots

```
AVIAN ENDEMISM PATTERNS
=========================

ENDEMIC BIRD AREAS (EBAs, BirdLife International):
  ~218 EBAs globally; each has >=2 restricted-range (<50,000 km^2) spp
  Overlap with plant and reptile endemism hotspots
  High endemism correlates with: isolation, topographic complexity, stability

TOP ENDEMISM REGIONS:
  Andes (Ecuador-Peru): ~600+ restricted range spp
    Altitude gradients create many isolated "sky islands"
    Each mountain valley can have distinct hummingbird + tanager species

  Atlantic Forest (Brazil): ~200 restricted range spp
    ~15% of original area remains; most threatened avifauna globally

  Madagascar: ~100% of breeding landbirds endemic (8 endemic families)
    Mesites, asities, ground-rollers, coua cuckoos
    Long isolation (88 Mya) + adaptive radiation

  New Guinea: ~800 endemic species
    Bird of paradise (Paradisaeidae): 42 species; extreme sexual selection
    Each valley system can have distinct species assemblages

  Hawaii: high endemism (see above); catastrophic losses
```

## Island Syndrome

```
ISLAND SYNDROME -- PREDICTABLE EVOLUTIONARY CHANGES ON ISLANDS
================================================================

CHARACTER          ISLAND TREND           MECHANISM
---------          ------------           ---------
Body size          Dwarfism (if large ancestor)  Resource limitation
                   Gigantism (if small ancestor)  Released from predation/competition
Wing size          Reduced (flightlessness)       No predators -> no flight benefit
Egg size           Often increased               Fewer, larger chicks favored
Clutch size        Reduced                        Lower mortality rate -> less replacement needed
Tameness           Increased                      No predator experience
Nesting behavior   Ground nesting more common     No mammalian predators (oceanic islands)
Plumage            Less elaborate (some spp)      Reduced conspecific competition?

GIGANTISM EXAMPLES:
  Dodo (Rodrigues, Mauritius): Raphus cucullatus ~20 kg from pigeon-sized ancestor
  Giant flightless rails: Apatornithidae; worldwide (often extinct post-contact)

DWARFISM:
  Dwarf mammoths on Mediterranean islands
  Comparable avian examples less common (birds already relatively small)
```

## Continental Drift and Avian Distribution

```
TECTONICS AND AVIAN BIOGEOGRAPHY
===================================

GONDWANA (S. America + Africa + Australia + Antarctica + India)
  Broke up: 180-100 Mya
  Carried ancestral avian lineages with it

TETHYS SEA CLOSURE (Miocene, ~20 Mya):
  Africa-Eurasia connection established
  -> interchange of African + Eurasian bird families
  Sunbirds (Africa) exchange; many migrants use this route

BERING LAND BRIDGE (Pleistocene, intermittent):
  North America - Asia connections during glacial maxima
  -> Palearctic-Nearctic faunal exchanges
  Many waterfowl, shorebirds, raptors have circumpolar distributions

GREAT AMERICAN INTERCHANGE (3 Mya, Panama):
  North + South America connected for first time in ~65 Myr
  S. American birds move north (mockingbirds, hummingbirds, tyrant flycatchers)
  N. American birds move south (some tanagers; limited effect)
  S. American endemic families largely retain S. America

WALLACE'S LINE (SE Asia):
  Deep-water strait between Bali + Lombok; Borneo + Sulawesi
  Despite geographic proximity: dramatic faunal discontinuity
  Asian placental mammals: west of line
  Australian marsupials: east of line
  Birds: less restricted (can fly) but some strong patterns
    Cockatoos: east of Wallace's Line
    Pheasants: west
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Island biogeography equilibrium is about number, not composition**: MacArthur-Wilson predicts that species richness reaches an equilibrium. It does NOT predict that the species at equilibrium are the same ones over time — turnover happens. This is an important distinction for conservation: protected areas at equilibrium are not static.

**Darwin's finches are not primitive birds**: They are derived Passeriformes (tanager family), among the most evolutionarily derived birds. The radiation is recent (~2 Mya) and rapid. "Primitive" in common speech is not a phylogenetic term.

**Ratites: dispersal not vicariance**: The intuitive story (Gondwana broke apart carrying ostriches, kiwis, emus on separate plates) was falsified by molecular clocks showing ratite divergences post-date Gondwana breakup. The actual story (flying ancestor dispersed and became flightless multiple times) is counterintuitive but better supported.

**Wallace's Line is not impermeable**: Many bird families (especially vagile ones) cross Wallace's Line. The barrier is most significant for non-flying vertebrates. For birds, the line marks probability gradients, not hard limits. The pattern exists but is less dramatic than for mammals.

**Species-area exponent z varies**: z ~0.25 is a rule of thumb. Oceanic true islands tend toward higher z (0.3-0.4); habitat patches in a matrix tend toward lower z (0.1-0.2). This matters for conservation: predicted extinction debt from habitat loss differs by z.
