# Conservation: Decline, Threats, and Recovery

## The Big Picture

Avian conservation is simultaneously one of the best-resourced (birds are the most monitored taxa) and most urgent (one billion birds lost from North America since 1970; ~160 extinctions since 1500) conservation domains. The combination of intensive data (eBird, breeding bird surveys, Christmas bird counts) with known biology makes birds both canaries-in-the-coal-mine and tractable conservation targets.

```
AVIAN CONSERVATION STATUS OVERVIEW (2024)
==========================================

Total bird species:         ~10,600
  Extinct since 1500:       ~160+ confirmed (BirdLife)
  Possibly extinct:         ~30+ (not seen in decades)
  IUCN Critically Endangered: ~230
  IUCN Endangered:          ~450
  IUCN Vulnerable:          ~900
  Near Threatened:          ~1,000
  Estimated declining:      ~3,700 (of 10,600)
  Estimated stable/increasing: ~6,900

NORTH AMERICA (since 1970, Rosenberg et al. 2019 Science):
  3 billion birds lost (29% of breeding bird population)
  Grassland birds: -53%
  Forest birds: -29%
  Shorebirds: -37%
  Aerial insectivores: -32%
  Winners: waterfowl (+34%; hunting + wetland management investment)

EUROPE:
  ~600 million birds lost 1980-2017 (~17% reduction)
  Farmland birds: most severe decline (-55% since 1980)
```

## Island Extinctions: The Worst Cases

```
ISLAND EXTINCTION PATTERN
===========================

STATISTICS:
  ~90% of bird extinctions since 1500 have been on islands
  (islands = ~20% of bird species but ~90% of extinctions)
  Pattern: humans arrive -> introduce rats, cats, pigs -> mass extinction
  Pre-Polynesian Pacific: estimated 800-2,000 species extinct
    via subfossil record (Pimm et al.; Steadman 1995)
    Most never formally described -- "dark extinction"

CAUSES OF ISLAND VULNERABILITY:
  1. Long isolation without mammalian predators
     -> lost anti-predator responses (tameness, ground nesting)
  2. Small population size -> demographic + genetic vulnerability
  3. Limited range -> any local event = population event
  4. Specialized to local resources -> less resilient to change

KEY INVASIVE SPECIES:
  Rats (Rattus norvegicus, R. rattus):
    Reach virtually all inhabited islands; eat eggs + chicks
    Tropic birds, petrels, terns: dramatic decline near rat-infested coasts
    Lord Howe Island Woodhen (Gallirallus sylvestris): near extinction from rats
    New Zealand: 68+ species extinct since Polynesian arrival (kiore + human)

  Domestic cats (Felis catus):
    ~1.3-4 billion birds killed annually in US alone
    Island extinctions: Stephens Island wren (Traversia lyalli): extinct by 1895
      last specimen killed by the lighthouse keeper's cat (disputed legend but real)
    Feral cats on islands: primary driver of several extinctions

  Brown tree snake (Boiga irregularis):
    Accidentally introduced to Guam ~1950
    Eliminated all native forest birds (12 species lost; 2 extinct)
    -> demonstrates single introduced predator can eliminate entire avifauna

BIOSECURITY SYSTEMS:
  New Zealand: world's most sophisticated biosecurity
  Rat eradication from islands: large-scale examples
    South Georgia (2015): 750 tonnes rodenticide; rat-free verified
    Macquarie Island: cats + rabbits + rodents eradicated; seabird recovery
```

## Farmland Bird Decline

```
FARMLAND BIRD DECLINE IN EUROPE
==================================

EVIDENCE:
  European farmland bird index: -55% since 1980
  UK: 15/20 formerly common farmland species declining
  Most species measured by breeding bird survey show declines

SPECIES EXAMPLES:
  Grey partridge (Perdix perdix):     -92% UK since 1967
  Skylark (Alauda arvensis):          -54% UK since 1967
  Lapwing (Vanellus vanellus):        -53%
  Yellow wagtail (Motacilla flava):   -55%
  Tree sparrow (Passer montanus):     -95% UK since 1970

PROXIMATE CAUSES:
  Loss of winter stubble (now plowed immediately)
    -> seed food for buntings, sparrows, finches
  Drainage of field margins + hedgerows
    -> invertebrate habitat loss
  Insecticide use
    -> 50-60% insect abundance decline in farmland
  Earlier silage cutting
    -> nesting curlew, lapwing; chick survival near zero in intensified grassland
  Herbicide use
    -> weed seeds eliminated from crops -> no winter food

MANAGEMENT APPROACHES:
  Agri-environment schemes (EU Common Agricultural Policy):
    Buffer strips, winter cover crops, delayed mowing
    Mixed results: partial recovery in best-managed areas
  Higher Level Stewardship (UK): payments for wildlife-friendly farming
  Targeted interventions: grey partridge recovery programs with gamekeeper input

  LIMITATION: scheme uptake voluntary; financial incentives often insufficient
  to offset crop-value loss; patchwork implementation vs landscape-scale need
```

## Climate Change and Avifauna

```
CLIMATE CHANGE EFFECTS ON BIRDS
==================================

RANGE SHIFTS:
  ~40 km northward shift per decade (European breeding birds)
  Altitude: ~11m upward per decade (montane species)
  Winners: expanding northward (little egret, collared dove in UK)
  Losers: specialists at range margins (arctic species squeezed from above)
    Snow bunting, ptarmigan: reduced alpine habitat

PHENOLOGICAL MISMATCH:
  Great tit (Parus major) + caterpillar peak:
    Peak insect abundance advancing ~9 days/decade (Dutch study)
    Great tit hatching: advancing ~4 days/decade
    MISMATCH: 5-day gap; chick-food synchrony declining
    Population: declining in areas with strong mismatch
  Pied flycatcher (Ficedula hypoleuca): long-distance migrant
    Migration timing: controlled by equatorial photoperiod (cannot advance easily)
    Breeding insect peak: advancing in Europe
    STRONG mismatch: ~9-day gap; population: -90% in some Dutch populations

EXTREME WEATHER:
  Heat waves: chick mortality; adult physiological stress
  Late snowstorms: shorebird breeding failure (Arctic)
  Drought: reduced insect availability; nest failure
  Storms: seabird mortality; exhausted migrants

CORAL BLEACHING:
  Coastal seabirds (tropicbirds, shearwaters): fish prey distribution shifts
  Redistributes fish schools -> foraging range must increase

FUTURE PROJECTIONS (2100):
  Models: 10-30% of bird species at extinction risk from climate alone
  Interaction: climate + habitat loss + disease = much higher
  Most at risk: mountain species (nowhere to go up)
                island endemics (sea level rise)
                Arctic/Antarctic specialists (habitat loss)
```

## Recovery Programs: What Works

```
AVIAN CONSERVATION SUCCESS STORIES
=====================================

CALIFORNIA CONDOR (Gymnogyps californianus):
  Population: 22 individuals in 1987 -> captive breeding program
  All wild birds captured; captive breeding; reintroduction
  Current: ~500 total (wild + captive); 300+ wild
  Key tools: micromanaging captive breeding; lead poisoning mitigation
             (switch from lead to copper bullets in condor range)
  Primary ongoing threat: lead shot in carcasses

PEREGRINE FALCON (Falco peregrinus):
  DDT -> eggshell thinning -> reproductive failure -> near extinction
  US/Europe: endangered by 1970s
  DDT banned (US: 1972; Europe: phased)
  Captive breeding (Cornell Raptor Research; Hawk Mountain)
  Reintroduction: "hacking" (release at towers)
  Current: fully recovered; urban populations worldwide
  Conservation lesson: remove the cause -> recovery possible

WHITE-TAILED EAGLE (Haliaeetus albicilla):
  Extinct Britain by 1918 (persecution + habitat)
  Reintroduction: Scotland 1975 (Norwegian birds)
  England: reintroduction 2019 (Isle of Wight)
  Current: ~150 breeding pairs UK; expanding
  Resistance: farming community; sheep predation concerns (real but minor)

CHATHAM ISLANDS BLACK ROBIN (Petroica traversi):
  Population: 5 individuals in 1980 (including just 1 breeding female: "Old Blue")
  Cross-fostering: eggs/chicks moved to Chatham Island tit nests
  Result: population grew to 250+ (all descended from Old Blue)
  Key: behavioral innovation (cross-fostering) + dedicated monitoring
  Classic: single-female bottleneck survived; no major inbreeding depression (yet)

NEW ZEALAND KAKAPO (Strigops habroptila):
  Flightless parrot; largest parrot; nocturnal; boom display
  Population: ~50 in 1990s
  Intensive management: all individuals on predator-free islands
  Supplementary feeding (reduces inter-annual breeding variability)
  Artificial insemination: genetic diversity management
  Current: ~250 (2024); slow but increasing
```

## Monitoring Systems

```
AVIAN MONITORING INFRASTRUCTURE
=================================

CITIZEN SCIENCE:
  eBird (Cornell Lab): >1 billion observations; global; real-time
    Largest biodiversity database; species distribution models
    Migration: "wave" tracking through N. America each spring
    Challenge: detection probability variation; observer skill
  Christmas Bird Count (Audubon): US/Canada; >100 yrs data
  Breeding Bird Survey (USGS/BTO): roadside counts; 3,000+ routes
    Standard methodology; trends over decades

RINGING/BANDING:
  Individual marking for survival + movement data
  European ringing schemes: >200M birds ringed since 1900s
  Bar-tailed godwit non-stop migration: verified by satellite tag + light-level
    geolocators

RADAR ORNITHOLOGY:
  NEXRAD weather radar (US): documents migration waves
    ~2 billion birds visible crossing on major nights
    Radar data: quantify decline in migration intensity over decades
  BirdScan, MERLIN: automated species ID from radar

REMOTE SENSING:
  Satellite tracking: small GPS tags (0.5g) for passerines now feasible
  Acoustic monitoring: Song Meter recorders; automated AI ID (BirdNET)
  Camera traps: seabird colonies, nest monitoring
  eDNA: detect bird presence from water/air samples (emerging)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Threat | Affected taxa | Mitigation approach |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Island invasives (rats, cats) | Island endemics; seabirds | Island biosecurity; eradication |
| Farmland intensification | Farmland birds, aerial insectivores | Agri-environment schemes; buffer strips |
| DDT/organochlorines | Raptors (thin eggshells) | Ban + captive reintroduction |
| Lead poisoning | Vultures, condors, eagles | Switch to non-toxic shot |
| Window collisions | All species | Bird-friendly glass; close blinds at night |
| Light pollution | Nocturnal migrants | Lights Out programs (cities) |
| Climate mismatch | Long-distance migrants; phenological specialists | Landscape conservation; emission reduction |
| Avian malaria (Hawaii) | Hawaiian honeycreepers | Mosquito control; captive breeding above malaria line |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Extinct in the wild" ≠ extinct**: California condor and kakapo are not extinct — they survive under intensive management. "Extinct in the wild" (IUCN category EW) means no wild populations but captive/managed populations survive. Conservation resources are being applied.

**Raptors recovered after DDT ban = DDT ban sufficient**: Peregrine falcon + bald eagle + osprey recovered because the primary cause (DDT) was removed. Birds not constrained by the same single cause (habitat loss, multiple pesticides) have not recovered similarly. DDT ban is not a template for all avian recovery.

**"3 billion birds lost" is not 3 billion extinctions**: Rosenberg et al. (2019) quantified ABUNDANCE decline (total individuals), not extinction events. Very different numbers: ~160 extinctions since 1500 vs 3 billion individuals lost from common species. Both are alarming but in different ways.

**eBird data is not unbiased**: eBird has massive observer bias toward accessible, birder-frequented areas. Tropical forests, remote areas, and nocturnal species are underrepresented. Statistical abundance models (STEM, BirdDistributionModels) attempt to correct for detection probability but the underlying data bias persists.

**Island eradication programs are feasible but expensive**: Rat eradication on South Georgia (2015) cost ~$10M for 3,746 km^2. Cost per km^2 decreases with island size (logistics scale). Political will, funding continuity, and preventing re-invasion are the limiting factors, not technical feasibility for most islands.
