# Ecology and Foraging Strategies

## The Big Picture

Avian foraging ecology is the study of how birds acquire food efficiently under predation risk and competition. The theoretical framework — Optimal Foraging Theory (OFT) — treats foraging as an optimization problem with a defined currency (typically energy rate) and constraints (travel time, handling time, prey availability, predation risk). Birds are among the most tractable systems for testing OFT because their foraging can be observed directly, food items counted, and energetics measured.

## Optimal Foraging Theory

```
OPTIMAL FORAGING THEORY (OFT)
================================

CENTRAL PREMISE:
  Natural selection favors individuals that maximize net energy intake
  per unit time (or maximize another relevant currency)

  E/T ratio = (Energy gained) / (Time spent foraging)
  Subject to: travel time, search time, handling time, risk

PREY MODEL (Pulliam 1974):
  Forager encounters prey types ranked by profitability (E/h)
  E = energy from prey type i
  h = handling time for prey type i
  Decision: include or exclude lower-ranked prey types

  KEY PREDICTION: Include lower-ranked prey type ONLY IF:
    E1/h1 > E1 / (h1 + s1)  (where s = search time for type 1)
  When type 1 is abundant (short s1): exclude type 2
  When type 1 is rare (long s1): include type 2

  Real birds: roughly follow predictions; tend to be more discriminating
              when prey is abundant; take lower-quality prey when scarce

CENTRAL PLACE FORAGING (CPF):
  Forager must return to central place (nest, roost)
  Optimal load: increasing load has diminishing return
  -> leave for load at which: marginal value of adding item < cost of extra load

  PREDICTIONS:
  Load should increase with travel distance (less frequent trips)
    -> verified in oystercatchers (Haematopus ostralegus) carrying mussels
    -> bees carrying pollen loads also confirm (same model)
```

## Guild Analysis: Foraging Categories

```
AVIAN FORAGING GUILDS
=======================

AERIAL FORAGERS:
  Aerial insectivores: catch insects in flight
    Swifts: pursue flying insects at altitude; aerial screen
    Swallows: lower level; often near water
    Flycatchers (tyrant + Old World): sally from perch; return
    Distinctive: flicker response to moving prey; pursuit flight
  Aerial plunge divers: dive from height into water
    Gannets: 30m height, 100 km/h impact; binocular vision required
    Kingfishers: hover + shallow dive (fresh water)
    Terns: flutter + plunge; skim surface

PERCH-AND-WAIT HUNTERS:
  Raptors: soar/perch; watch for movement; stoop
    Red-tailed hawk: perch hunting, roadside utility poles
    Peregrine: high-altitude patrol; stoop at pigeon speed
  Herons (Ardeidae): stand motionless; strike with neck spring
  Kingfishers: perch over water; dive

FOLIAGE GLEANERS:
  Probe foliage surface or interior for arthropods
  Old World warblers (Phylloscopus): hover-glean or active search
  Vireos: slow, methodical; large prey per item
  Treecreepers, nuthatches: bark surface; different microhabitats
  Resource partitioning: several species on same tree; different foraging zones
    (MacArthur 1958: 5 warbler species on spruce trees; vertical zonation)

GROUND FORAGERS:
  Probers: bill into substrate for invertebrates
    Godwits, curlews: tidal flat probing; tactile bill tip
    Woodcock: earthworm detection via Herbst corpuscles in bill tip
  Scratchers: rake ground with feet
    Towhees, juncos, Galliformes
  Pivoting: swing bill sideways (spoonbills; scan for fish)
  "Run-stop-peck": plovers (visual; no probing)
```

## MacArthur's Warbler Study: Resource Partitioning

```
MACARTHUR'S WARBLER STUDY (1958)
===================================

Classic demonstration: coexistence via microhabitat partitioning
5 species of Dendroica warblers on spruce trees in Maine

  Cape May warbler:     Top of tree, outer branches; hover-glean
  Yellow-rumped warbler: Middle-outer zones; wide range
  Black-throated green:  Mid-outer branches; intermediate
  Blackburnian warbler:  Top-outer; highest zone predominantly
  Bay-breasted warbler:  Lower-interior zone; deep in crown

  +---------------------------------------+
  |      TREE (schematic)                 |
  | Top:  Cape May, Blackburnian          |
  | Mid:  Yellow-rump, BT Green           |
  | Low:  Bay-breasted                    |
  | Outer/inner axis also differs         |
  +---------------------------------------+

RESULT: Competitive exclusion avoided by temporal + spatial partitioning
LEGACY: Foundational community ecology; "niche differentiation" paradigm
MODERN CRITIQUE: Overlap substantial; character displacement + priority
                  effects also likely; coexistence not fully explained
```

## Sensory Ecology of Foraging

```
SENSORY ADAPTATIONS FOR FORAGING
===================================

VISION:
  Tetrachromatic: UV + blue + green + red (vs trichromat humans)
  UV vision: flowers with UV guides; plumage with UV components
  Binocular/monocular trade-off:
    Predators (owls, eagles): forward-facing; binocular overlap wide
    -> depth perception for strike accuracy
    Prey species (sparrows): lateral eyes; wide visual field; 360 near
    -> early detection of predator
  High density fovea: 2x human photoreceptor density
  Temporal resolution: detect 300+ fps (useful in forest maneuver)

OLFACTION (stronger than assumed):
  Procellariformes (seabirds): excellent olfaction
    Petrels locate nest burrows and food patches by scent at sea
  Vultures (Cathartidae, New World): detect dimethyl sulfide from decaying flesh
    Turkey vulture (Cathartes aura): finds hidden carcasses under forest canopy
    Old World vultures: locate carcasses visually; no olfaction
  Kiwis: nostrils at bill tip; olfactory foraging in litter

TOUCH (bill tip mechanoreceptors):
  Herbst corpuscles in bill tip (shorebirds):
    Remote touch: detect prey vibration in mud through bill tip
    Even through opaque substrate
  Shovel-bill spoonbill: lateral sweeping; sensory not visual
  Wrybill (Anarhynchus frontalis): only bird with laterally deflected bill
    -> sweeps under river stones for insect larvae

ECHOLOCATION (rare in birds):
  Cave swiftlets (Aerodramus): click echolocation for dark-cave navigation
  Oil bird (Steatornis caripensis): low-resolution echolocation (audible clicks)
  Not used for prey detection (unlike bats)
```

## Caching Behavior and Spatial Memory

```
FOOD CACHING IN BIRDS
========================

SCATTER HOARDING: bury/hide food in thousands of scattered sites
  Corvids: Clark's nutcracker, Eurasian jay, western scrub-jay
  Paridae: black-capped chickadee, willow tit
  Recovery: spatial memory; can recall 1,000+ cache sites over months

HIPPOCAMPUS VOLUME:
  Caching species: larger hippocampus relative to telencephalon
  Seasonal variation: in some species, HPC enlarges in fall (cache season)
  Neurogenesis: new neurons added to HPC seasonally in chickadees
    -> suggests memory capacity expands for caching season
    -> fundamental discovery: adult neurogenesis in HPC (controversial but supported)

COMPARATIVE:
  Marsh tit (Parus palustris): caches + episodic-like memory
  Non-caching species (e.g., great tit Parus major): smaller HPC

SOCIAL LEARNING OF CACHE LOCATIONS:
  Scrub-jays can recall WHICH individual observed their cache
  Protect caches: re-cache if observed by competitors
    (mental state attribution? "I know that you know where I cached it")
  Clayton & Dickinson (1998): "tulip ware" experiment showing
    cache protection behavior (evidence for theory of mind in birds -- contested)
```

## Predator Avoidance and Foraging Trade-offs

```
FORAGING - PREDATION RISK TRADE-OFF
======================================

LIMA-DILL MODEL:
  Forager must balance: food gain vs predation risk
  Higher predation risk -> reduce foraging time + use safer patches
  -> physiological cost: reduced food intake; reduced body condition

VIGILANCE:
  Scan frequency: look up for predators while foraging
  Trade-off: scanning reduces food intake per unit time
  Group size effect (dilution): in larger flocks, each individual
    scans less per unit time (others detecting; diluted risk)
    -> larger flock -> more food per bird (Pulliam 1973)
  "Lazy sentinel" problem: why scan at all if others scan?
    -> reciprocal altruism or relatedness-based sharing

FOOD MASS:
  State-dependent safety: hungrier birds take higher risks
  "Starvation vs predation" trade-off:
    Overwintering small birds: minimum fat reserves (higher predation risk
    if fat because flight slower) vs maximum fat (insurance against cold snap)
    Optimal fat reserve = function of starvation probability + predation probability
    Different species, populations tune reserve level by habitat danger

PATCH CHOICE:
  Safest patch not always most productive
  Shrubs near cover: higher predation risk (ambush predators)
  Open ground: lower predation risk (can see predator coming)
  Actual choice: intermediate patches + vigilance behavior adjustment
```

## Ecosystem Functions: Seed Dispersal and Beyond

```
BIRDS AS ECOSYSTEM ENGINEERS
================================

SEED DISPERSAL (ornithochory):
  ~30% of tree species in temperate forests; higher in tropics
  Fleshy fruit: nutritional reward for dispersal
    Cherry, elder, holly: thrushes, waxwings, robins
    Avocado, large Neotropical fruits: co-evolved with megafauna (now: large birds + bats)
  Distance dispersal: important for gene flow + colonization
    Nutmeg in Borneo: hornbills disperse >1 km
    Mistletoe (Viscum): specialized dispersal by mistle thrush; sticky seeds
      deposited on branches while wiping bill

POLLINATION (secondary role):
  Sunbirds, hummingbirds, honeyeaters: important in tropics/southern hemisphere
  North temperate: minor (few bird-adapted flowers)

PEST CONTROL:
  Insectivorous birds: tree tit studies show tree protection from insect damage
    when birds present vs exclosed (up to 50% reduction in herbivory some studies)
    Applied: nest boxes in orchards; "bird cafes" in citrus groves

BIOTURBATION:
  Shorebirds, Galliformes: soil disturbance during probing/scratching
  Woodpeckers: dead wood excavation creates cavities for other species
    "Keystone species" for forest cavity availability

CARRION ECOSYSTEM:
  Vultures: prevent anthrax, botulinum, rabies from spreading via carcasses
    Rapid consumption before bacteria spread
    Sub-Saharan Africa: Gyps vulture population crash -> dog population increase
    -> increased rabies + anthrax transmission documented
```

### Engineering Bridges

Optimal Foraging Theory is constrained optimization with a clear objective function: maximize net energy gain per unit time (E/T). The prey model formalizes the diet selection problem: given N prey types ranked by profitability (energy/handling time), the optimal forager takes the highest-ranked types until the next-ranked type's inclusion drops the overall rate. This is a marginal-value calculation — add an item to the diet if its profitability exceeds the current average rate. The math is identical to a greedy scheduling algorithm: sort tasks by value/cost, include in descending order until marginal value drops below threshold.

The Marginal Value Theorem (MVT) governs patch departure timing: leave a patch when the instantaneous return rate drops to the average rate across all patches in the environment, accounting for travel time. This is a diminishing-returns optimization with a benchmark comparison — the same structure as deciding when to stop optimizing a feature and ship, or when to stop caching and compute: the marginal gain from staying must exceed the opportunity cost of not being elsewhere.

Central Place Foraging (CPF) — when birds must return to a fixed point (nest, colony) — adds the constraint that round-trip travel cost scales with distance. This converts the patch-selection problem into a variant of the traveling salesman: minimize total cost subject to return-to-origin constraint. The optimal solution loads up at closer patches even if they are lower quality, matching the load to travel cost. This is exactly the throughput calculation for a batch processing system with fixed round-trip latency: fewer, larger batches for distant destinations.

MacArthur's resource partitioning across competing species — the ecological equivalents of competitors dividing niche space to avoid total overlap — maps to load balancing across heterogeneous workers. Each species evolves to exploit a subset of the resource gradient more efficiently than competitors, converging on a stable partition where no species can improve by switching. This is Nash equilibrium in a continuous strategy space, the same outcome as an auction-based resource allocation system at equilibrium.

## Common Confusion Points

**Optimal foraging doesn't mean perfect**: OFT predicts behavioral optima given the assumptions of the model (known prey distribution, stable environment, no predators). Real animals deviate from predictions: they have cognitive constraints, respond to predation risk, and must learn prey distribution. OFT is better viewed as a null model (what should happen) against which to test.

**Vigilance is not just anti-predator**: Some vigilance in social birds functions for monitoring group members, competitor detection, and mate surveillance. "Vigilance" measured as "head-up scanning" conflates multiple functions.

**Resource partitioning explains coexistence -- or does it?**: MacArthur's warbler result is real but the interpretation has evolved. Modern community ecology recognizes that stable coexistence requires more than observed niche differences — it requires niche differences sufficient to overcome competitive effects. Character displacement may have driven the partitioning.

**Scatter hoarders don't recall everything**: Recovery rates in caching species: Clark's nutcracker recovers ~70-80% of caches after 6-9 months. This is remarkable but not perfect. Unclaimed caches function as seed dispersal (inadvertent). The ecological function of caching (seed dispersal + food storage) may be why it evolved.

**"Keystone species" has a specific meaning**: A keystone species has an ecological effect disproportionate to its biomass. Woodpeckers as keystone cavity providers qualify — removing them dramatically reduces biodiversity of cavity-nesting birds. Not all ecologically important birds are keystones in this technical sense.
