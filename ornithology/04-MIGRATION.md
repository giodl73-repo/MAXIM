# Migration: Navigation and Orientation

## The Big Picture

Bird migration is one of the most spectacular feats in animal biology: individuals navigate to the same breeding or wintering sites across thousands of kilometers, often with no prior experience of the route. The mechanisms involve a multi-cue integration system — magnetic compass, celestial navigation, olfactory mapping, landmark recognition — that remains an active research frontier. Migration also drives conservation challenges: migratory species require protection at both ends of the route and at stopover sites.

```
MIGRATION OVERVIEW
===================

WHAT IS MIGRATION?
  Directed, seasonal movement between breeding and non-breeding areas
  Triggered by endogenous programs + proximate cues

STATISTICS:
  ~40% of bird species undertake some form of migration
  ~5 billion birds migrate to and from N. America annually
  ~5 billion birds cross the Mediterranean twice yearly (E. hemisphere)
  Arctic tern: ~70,000 km round trip/year (POLE to POLE)
  Bar-tailed godwit: 11,000 km non-stop (Alaska to New Zealand)
  Ruby-throated hummingbird: crosses Gulf of Mexico (900 km, non-stop)

ALTITUDINAL MIGRATION:
  Vertical migration (mountain -> valley)
  Not all birds migrate long-distance; altitudinal shifts in many species
  Clark's nutcracker (Nucifraga columbiana): cache food + altitudinal shift

PARTIAL MIGRATION:
  Some individuals of a population migrate; others are resident
  Robins (Erithacus rubecula): northern populations migratory;
  southern populations resident; same species
```

## The Endogenous Migratory Program

```
CIRCANNUAL CONTROL OF MIGRATION
=================================

MIGRATORY RESTLESSNESS (Zugunruhe):
  Caged migratory birds show directed nocturnal activity in spring/fall
  Demonstrates ENDOGENOUS programming (occurs in constant conditions)
  Direction corresponds to natural migratory direction
  Duration proportional to normal migration distance of species

ZUGUNRUHE EXPERIMENTS:
  Garden warbler (Sylvia borin):
    Spring Zugunruhe: SW -> NE orientation (toward breeding ground)
    Fall Zugunruhe: SW orientation (toward wintering ground, Africa)
    Intermediate direction change: corresponds to route bend around Alps

  CONCLUSION: migratory direction, timing, and duration are heritable
              (cross-breeding experiments with different race Sylvia)

PHOTOPERIOD REGULATION:
  Primary proximate cue: increasing day length (spring) -> gonadal
  development -> LH/FSH -> sex hormones -> migratory behavior
  Decreasing day length (fall) -> suppresses gonads -> fall migration

CIRCANNUAL RHYTHM:
  Endogenous ~1-year oscillator (persists in constant photoperiod)
  Entrained by annual light cycle
  Allows anticipation of migratory season before environmental change
```

## The Navigation Toolkit

Birds use a multi-layered navigation system. Different cues dominate at different scales and developmental stages.

```
BIRD NAVIGATION CUE HIERARCHY
================================

CUE TYPE          RANGE          CONDITION         RELIABILITY
---------         -----          ---------         -----------
Magnetic field    Global         Always available  Low temporal variability
Sun compass       Long-range     Daytime + clear   Requires time compensation
Star compass      Medium-range   Night + clear     Requires learning
Olfactory map     Local-regional Developing        High resolution near home
Landmarks         Local          Visual conditions High resolution locally
Infrasound        Unknown        Unknown           Long-range?
Polarized light   Short-range    Dawn/dusk         Calibration reference?

HIERARCHY IN PRACTICE:
  Young first-year birds: magnetic compass + vector (direction + distance)
    -> reach correct winter latitude without knowing specific site
  Adult experienced birds: true navigation (map sense)
    -> can correct for displacement to unfamiliar locations
    -> requires a map (olfactory most supported) + compass
```

### The Magnetic Compass

```
MAGNETIC COMPASS IN BIRDS
===========================

DISCOVERY:
  Wiltschko (1965): European robins in circular cage rotate toward
  magnetic N; rotate 180 deg when external field reversed -> compass

PROPERTIES:
  Inclination compass (not polarity): uses field inclination relative to gravity
  -> distinguishes "poleward" from "equatorward" (not N vs S by polarity)
  Implication: upside-down bird would perceive reversed compass (demonstrated)

LOCATION OF MAGNETORECEPTORS:
  Cryptochrome hypothesis (radical pair mechanism):
    Light-sensitive molecule (Cry4) in eye; quantum spin correlation in
    electron pairs affected by magnetic field -> neural signal
    Located in: photoreceptor cells of right eye (lateralized!)
    Evidence: covering right eye disrupts magnetic orientation;
              left eye OK -> right eye necessary in European robins
    Quantum biology: entangled radical pair state (temperature-insensitive)

  Magnetite hypothesis (iron mineral particles):
    Fe3O4 magnetite crystals in beak skin (trigeminal nerve)
    Could provide intensity + polarity information
    Evidence mixed: magnetite present but nerve disruption results vary
    Most likely: BOTH mechanisms function in complementary roles

CALIBRATION:
  Magnetic compass is calibrated by celestial cues at twilight
  Young birds learn to align magnetic compass with rotation of star field
  -> magnetic compass offset corrected annually
```

### Celestial Navigation

```
SUN COMPASS
============

  Requires: sun position + time of day (circadian clock)
  Clock-shift experiments:
    Fast-forward clock by 6 hours -> bird orients 90 deg wrong
    Confirms sun compass uses internal clock for compensation
  Redundancy: on overcast days, can use UV polarization patterns
              at zenith to infer sun position even through clouds

STAR COMPASS (NOCTURNAL MIGRATION)
====================================

  Emlen (1967): indigo buntings in planetarium
    Rotate star field 90 deg -> birds orient 90 deg rotated
    Occlude center of star field (North Star) -> orientation disrupted
    Use of center of rotation of night sky (celestial north = Polaris area)

  Development:
    Young birds observe night sky rotation during first summer
    Learn center of rotation (= geographic north) from rotation pattern
    Imprinting: critical period before first fall migration
    Flexible: if raised under artificial star pattern, orient by that

  Stars vs constellations:
    Not using specific constellations (Ursa Major, Orion)
    Using rotation axis of sky = geographic north
    More reliable than specific constellations (axial precession changes
    which star is at the pole over 26,000 yr cycles -- sun compass robust)
```

### The Olfactory Map

```
OLFACTORY NAVIGATION HYPOTHESIS
=================================

Proposed: Papi (1970s) for homing pigeons; controversial 40+ years

Evidence:
  Olfactory nerve section -> impairs homing in pigeons (not magnetics)
  Deflector loft experiments: alter wind direction at loft -> systematic
    displacement of release routes
  Field experiments: birds released at unfamiliar sites can use local
    atmospheric odors to determine their position relative to home

Mechanism:
  Young birds: learn to associate wind direction with odors from home area
  At unfamiliar site: detect familiar/unfamiliar odors + infer direction
    toward home ("odor map")
  Evidence for birds other than pigeons: petrels (olfaction strongly
    used for at-sea navigation)

Problems:
  Magnetic sensory ablation also disrupts homing -> olfaction not the only
  Difficult to test in truly clean experiments
  May be more important in experienced adults than in young migrants
```

## Migration Ecology: Routes and Corridors

```
MAJOR MIGRATION ROUTES
=======================

FLYWAYS (waterfowl and shorebirds):
  East Atlantic Flyway: Greenland/Iceland -> W Europe -> W Africa
  Central Asian Flyway: Siberia -> S/SE Asia; largest flyway
  East Asian-Australasian Flyway: Siberia/Alaska -> Australia
  Americas Flyways: Pacific, Central, Mississippi, Atlantic (US USFWS divisions)

LAND BRIDGES AND BOTTLENECKS:
  Raptors + soaring birds must cross over land (cannot soar over water)
  BOSPHORUS (Turkey): millions of raptors in 6-week window
    Honey buzzards, black kites, white storks concentrate here
  PANAMA: N. American migrants funnel through
    25+ million birds (Raptors, swallows, falcons, tanagers)
  CAPE MAY (N. America): concentration point; autumn hawk counts

TRANSOCEANIC ROUTES:
  Bar-tailed godwit (Limosa lapponica baueri):
    Non-stop Alaska -> New Zealand: 11,000 km in ~8-9 days
    No food/water; continuous flight; pre-migration mass doubles
    Organ resorption: gut, liver, kidneys shrink before departure
    Fuel: subcutaneous + intramuscular fat (30-35% body mass at departure)
    Heart + pectoral muscle: maintained or increased
  Ruby-throated hummingbird: Gulf of Mexico 900km non-stop
    Doubles body mass (from 3g to 6g) before crossing
```

## Stopover Ecology

```
STOPOVER BIOLOGY
=================

DEFINITION: resting + refueling site during migration
IMPORTANCE: often rate-limiting for entire migration
  Time at stopover > time actually flying (in most species)

REQUIREMENTS AT STOPOVER:
  Food (insects, fruits, nectar): rapid fueling
  Low predation risk: select dense vegetation
  Water
  Roost sites (nocturnal migrants)

FUELING RATES:
  Spring: rapid fueling (need to arrive at breeding ground early)
    Up to 10% body mass/day gain in optimal habitat
  Fall: slower acceptable (more time); young birds learning

HABITAT QUALITY:
  Studies: same individuals use same stopover sites in consecutive years
  Site fidelity: stops are critical nodes in migration network
  Habitat degradation at stopover -> population-level consequences
    even if breeding and wintering habitats are intact

NOCTURNAL MIGRATION:
  ~75% of migrants fly at night (Passerines, most shorebirds)
  Reasons: thermals cooler + less variable; predator avoidance; daylight for feeding
  Orientation: star compass + magnetic compass
  Altitude: 1,000-3,000m typical; thermal inversions affect
```

## Navigation Failure and Vagrancy

```
VAGRANCY AND ORIENTATION ERRORS
=================================

VAGRANCY: bird occurring far outside normal range
  Reverse migration: mirror-image flight of normal direction
    Fall: individual oriented SW instead of NW -> ends up in wrong continent
    Occurs in young birds; endogenous program ± magnetic error
  Overshooting: migrates too far in one direction
  Weather displacement: storm blows bird off course

RARITIES (observer perspective):
  Great birding events: rare vagrant (e.g., Siberian species in Britain)
  Provides evidence for navigation errors + normal distribution tails

EXPERIMENTAL DISPLACEMENT:
  Birds taken from one location, released elsewhere:
    Young naive birds: continue in original direction (vector navigation)
    Experienced adult birds: correct toward home (true navigation = map + compass)
  Classic: Manx shearwaters released in Boston -> returned to Wales in 12 days
  (navigated 5,000 km to unfamiliar area -> required map not just compass)
```

## Conservation Implications

```
MIGRATORY CONNECTIVITY AND CONSERVATION
=========================================

MIGRATORY CONNECTIVITY:
  Link between specific breeding and specific wintering populations
  High connectivity: population uses one wintering area
    -> decline in that area = decline in whole population
  Low connectivity: mixed populations in winter
    -> more resilient (redundancy)

THREATS TO MIGRATORY SPECIES:
  Habitat loss (breeding + wintering + stopover)
  Light pollution: nocturnal migrants disoriented by artificial light
    -> window collisions (1 billion bird deaths/yr in US from windows)
    -> prolonged circling city centers; exhaustion
  Wind turbines: migrating raptors + bats at risk
    -> siting critical (ridge lines, migration bottlenecks)
  Communication towers: collision risk; lights attract migrants
  Climate change: phenological mismatch (earlier spring migration
    may not match earlier insect emergence at breeding sites)
  Stopover degradation: Red Sea coastal development affecting
    migratory shorebirds critically dependent on invertebrate abundance

INTERNATIONAL TREATIES:
  MBTA (Migratory Bird Treaty Act, US, 1918): federal protection
  African-Eurasian Waterbird Agreement (AEWA): migratory waterbird protection
  Convention on Migratory Species (CMS/Bonn Convention): global framework
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Migratory birds don't "know" their destination innately**: Young first-year birds follow an endogenous vector (direction + distance). They reach the correct latitude/longitude for their winter range without prior experience but may not reach the exact species-typical wintering site. Adults with experience use true navigation (map + compass) to reach specific sites.

**The magnetic compass is an inclination compass**: Birds don't detect magnetic north like a needle compass. They detect the inclination of the field (angle from horizontal). Near the equator where inclination is near zero, some species lose orientation. Near magnetic field reversals during geological time, avian orientation would have been disrupted.

**Star navigation uses the rotation axis, not Polaris specifically**: Emlen demonstrated that birds learn the center of sky rotation, not specific constellations. Birds raised under planetarium skies rotating around Betelgeuse (not the real pole) orient toward Betelgeuse in subsequent migration. This explains how birds compensate for 26,000-year precession of the Earth's axis.

**Migration is not just N-S**: Many migrations are NE-SW, diagonal, or circular. European warblers migrating to sub-Saharan Africa often fly SW from Europe, then turn more southward — the orientation is a vector that bends, and this bending is encoded in the migratory program.

**Stopover habitat is as critical as breeding/wintering habitat**: A chain is as strong as its weakest link. Population modeling shows that loss of key stopover sites can cause population declines even if breeding and wintering habitats are intact. Migratory species conservation requires international cooperation across the entire flyway.
