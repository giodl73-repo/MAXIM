# Systematics and Major Groups

## The Big Picture

Avian systematics underwent a revolution from 1987 (Sibley and Ahlquist DNA hybridization) through 2014-2015 (whole-genome phylogenomics), overturning many morphology-based groupings. The current consensus is the Jarvis (2014) and Prum (2015) phylogenomic trees, though some internal Neoaves topology remains contested. Modern checklist species concepts (IOC, Clements) differ in how many species to recognize.

```
AVIAN CLASSIFICATION HIERARCHY
================================

CLASS: Aves
  ORDER: e.g., Passeriformes
    SUBORDER: e.g., Oscines (Passeri)
      FAMILY: e.g., Fringillidae (finches)
        GENUS: e.g., Fringilla
          SPECIES: e.g., Fringilla coelebs (chaffinch)
            SUBSPECIES: e.g., F. c. coelebs (nominate)

SPECIES CONCEPTS:
  Biological Species Concept (BSC): reproductively isolated
  Phylogenetic Species Concept (PSC): diagnosable by unique character
  PSC splits more species (some estimates: 18,000+ vs 10,600)
  IOC and Clements use intermediate (BSC-weighted)
  New species described annually by vocalisation analysis
```

## Palaeognathae: The Ancient Jawed Birds

```
PALAEOGNATHAE (~60 spp)
=========================

Characters:
  Palaeognathous palate (different pterygoid-vomer articulation)
  Most are large and flightless (ratites)
  Tinamous are flightless-grade but retained flight

MAJOR GROUPS:

  Struthioniformes  -- Ostriches
    Struthio camelus: largest living bird (160 kg); Africa
    S. molybdophanes: Somali ostrich (recently split)
    Bipedal; fastest land bird; only 2 toes

  Rheiformes  -- Rheas
    Rhea americana, Pterocnemia pennata: South America
    Flightless; 3 toes; similar niche to ostriches

  Casuariiformes  -- Emus + Cassowaries
    Dromaius novaehollandiae (emu): Australia; 3rd largest bird
    Casuarius spp.: cassowaries (3 spp); rainforest; dangerous
      Casque on head: display + running through vegetation?

  Apterygiformes  -- Kiwis
    5 species; New Zealand
    Nocturnal, flightless, small
    Nostrils at TIP of bill (unusual among birds)
    Largest egg relative to body size: 20% of female body mass
    Male incubates in Apteryx australis

  Tinamiformes  -- Tinamous
    ~50 spp; Central and South America
    CAN fly (weakly); have keel + flight muscles
    Palaeognathous but flighted; basal position in Palaeognathae tree
    Cryptic; rarely seen; distinctive calls
```

## Galloanserae: Chicken + Duck Branch

```
GALLIFORMES (~290 spp)
========================

Characters: chicken-like; ground nesters; cryptic females; elaborate males
  Strong legs for scratching; seed + invertebrate diet
  Many have wattles, combs, spurs (sexual selection)

FAMILIES:
  Phasianidae (pheasants, peafowl, turkeys, quail, grouse):
    Largest family; ~250 spp
    Domesticated: chicken (Gallus gallus domesticus) -- most numerous bird
    Wild turkey (Meleagris gallopavo): key North American game bird
    Peacock (Pavo cristatus): sexual selection textbook case
    Ptarmigan (Lagopus): Arctic; white winter plumage
  Cracidae (curassows, chachalacas): Neotropical
  Megapodiidae (megapodes): mound builders; do not incubate with body heat

ANSERIFORMES (~170 spp)
========================

Characters: webbed feet; lamellate bill (filter-feeding); waterproofing
  Down most effective insulation; precocial young

FAMILIES:
  Anatidae (ducks, geese, swans):
    Diverse; surface dabbling to deep diving
    Mallard (Anas platyrhynchos): ancestor of most domestic ducks
    Eider ducks: down collected commercially (Iceland, Russia)
    Geese (Anser, Branta): long-distance migrants; V-formation
  Anhimidae (screamers): South America; not typical anseriform
  Anseranatidae (magpie goose): Australia; basal
```

## Neoaves: The Major Radiation

### Columbimorphae

```
COLUMBIFORMES (~350 spp)
=========================
  Pigeons + doves: one family (Columbidae)
  Crop milk: protein + fat secretion fed to chicks (unique in birds)
  Drink by suction (most birds tilt head back)
  Passenger pigeon (Ectopistes migratorius): extinct 1914
    Once most numerous bird in N. America; billions; flock-hunted to extinction
  Dodo (Raphus cucullatus): Mauritius; extinct by 1680s; flightless relative
```

### Strisores: Swifts and Hummingbirds

```
APODIFORMES (~500 spp)
======================

  Trochilidae -- HUMMINGBIRDS (~360 spp):
    Exclusively New World; primarily Neotropical
    Smallest birds: Mellisuga helenae (bee hummingbird): 1.6g
    Hover; nectar specialists; also insectivores for protein
    Torpor: nightly torpor (body T drops 20-30 C) to conserve energy
    Bill diversity extreme: matching flower morphologies

  Apodidae -- SWIFTS (~100 spp):
    Fastest birds in level flight
    Common swift (Apus apus): spends ~10 months/yr airborne
    Tiny legs; cannot perch on flat surfaces; can only take off from edge
    Nest of saliva: swift nests (edible bird's nests; bird's nest soup)
    Echolocation: some cave swiftlets (Aerodramus) echolocate in dark caves

CAPRIMULGIFORMES (~110 spp):
  Nightjars; nighthawks; frogmouths
  Cryptic; nocturnal; insectivores
  Wide gape; rictal bristles; muted colors
  Whip-poor-will (Antrostomus vociferus): summer N. America; winter tropics
```

### Waterbirds (Aequornithes)

```
AEQUORNITHES (waterbirds)
==========================

  Gaviiformes (loons): ~5 spp; diving birds; N. hemisphere
    Solid bones (heavier -> sink; aids diving)
    Feet at extreme posterior -> poor on land; excellent diving

  Sphenisciformes (penguins): ~20 spp; Southern hemisphere
    All flightless; aquatic; countershaded
    Emperor penguin: deepest diver (560m); most cold-adapted

  Procellariiformes (tube-noses): ~140 spp; albatrosses, petrels, shearwaters
    Salt gland -> nasal tube -> discharge marine salt
    Dynamic soaring (albatrosses)
    Olfaction: best developed in birds; locate food at sea by smell
    Wandering albatross: largest wingspan (3.5m); longest-lived (72 yr record)

  Pelecaniformes: pelicans, herons, egrets, ibises, spoonbills
    Pelicans: gular pouch; not used to store fish long-term; scoop + drain
    Great blue heron: wait-and-strike; long neck S-curve coils for strike

  Suliformes: boobies, gannets, cormorants, frigatebirds, anhingas
    Gannets: plunge diving from 30m; ~100 km/h impact
    Frigatebirds: kleptoparasites + aerial foragers; largest wing:body ratio
```

### Shorebirds, Gulls, Auks (Charadriiformes)

```
CHARADRIIFORMES (~350 spp)
===========================

  Scolopacidae (sandpipers, godwits, snipe): probing bill; tundra breeding
    Bar-tailed godwit: 11,000 km non-stop flight (Alaska -> NZ)
    Snipe: "drumming" with tail feathers during display

  Charadriidae (plovers): short bill; visual foraging; run-stop-look
    Lapwing: distraction display ("broken wing")

  Laridae (gulls): generalist opportunists; colony nesters; complex molt
    Herring gull (Larus argentatus): 4-year to adult plumage

  Alcidae (auks): Northern hemisphere penguins (convergent)
    Great auk (Pinguinus impennis): extinct 1844; original "penguin"
    Razorbill, guillemot, puffin: living alcids; dive for fish

  Stercorariidae (skuas): kleptoparasites; pirate birds
```

### Raptors

```
RAPTORS -- CONVERGENT HUNTERS
================================

ACCIPITRIFORMES (~260 spp):
  Accipitridae: hawks, eagles, kites, harriers, Old World vultures
    Buteo (buzzards/red-tailed hawks): soaring; rodent specialists
    Accipiter (forest hawks): short wings; rapid pursuit; bird hunters
    Aquila (true eagles): large; diverse prey; soar
    Gyps (griffon vultures): obligate scavengers; naked head (hygienic)
    Pandionidae (osprey): fish-only diet; reversible outer toe; spiny tarsus

FALCONIFORMES (~65 spp):
  Convergent with Accipitriformes (not closely related!)
  MOLECULAR PHYLOGENY: Falconidae is closer to parrots + passerines
  Falco: "notch" on upper bill; strike killing (vs Accipiter's grip)
  Peregrine falcon: fastest animal; worldwide; urban recovery story

STRIGIFORMES (owls; ~230 spp):
  Nocturnal raptors; also independently evolved hunting strategy
  Asymmetric ear placement (Asio, Tyto): stereo hearing for 3D location
  Silent flight: fringed primary feathers damp turbulence noise
  Facial disc: parabolic reflector for sound
  Forward-facing eyes: binocular vision for depth perception
  Fixed orbits: must rotate entire head (270 deg neck rotation)
```

### Passeriformes: The Dominant Order

```
PASSERIFORMES (~6,500 spp) -- 60% OF ALL BIRDS
================================================

CHARACTERS:
  Anisodactyl foot: 3 toes forward, 1 backward (hallux)
  Locking toe flexor tendon: grip maintained without muscle effort
    (perch passively while sleeping)
  Syrinx (vocal organ): passerines have most complex syrinx
  Oscines (songbirds): learned vocalizations; critical period; cultural transmission

SUBORDERS:
  Tyranni (Suboscines): ~1,100 spp; mostly S. America
    Tyrant flycatchers (Tyrannidae): largest family in Americas
    Cotingas, manakins, antbirds: Neotropical radiation
    Calls: largely innate (genetically programmed, not learned)

  Passeri (Oscines): ~5,400 spp; worldwide; learned song
    Corvidae (crows, ravens, jays, magpies): most intelligent birds
      Tool use (New Caledonian crow; Corvus moneduloides)
      Episodic memory; prospective cognition; self-recognition
    Paridae (titmice, chickadees): scatter hoard; spatial memory
      Hippocampus volume correlates with hoarding extent
    Turdidae (thrushes): American robin, European blackbird; migrants
    Fringillidae (finches): seed specialists; Darwin's finches (now separate: Thraupidae)
    Parulidae (N. American warblers): ~120 spp; neotropical migrants
    Ploceidae (weaverbirds): elaborate nest construction; Africa
    Icteridae (New World blackbirds, orioles, meadowlarks)
```

### Engineering Bridges

The species concept debate is the entity identity problem. When are two populations "the same thing"? The Biological Species Concept (BSC) defines species by reproductive isolation — two populations are distinct species if they don't successfully interbreed where they meet. This is a behavioral/genetic test: identity is defined by interface compatibility. The Phylogenetic Species Concept (PSC) defines species by diagnosability — a species is the smallest monophyletic group with a unique derived character. This is a structural test: identity is defined by internal composition. You get more species under PSC (~20% more in most groups) because PSC splits populations that the BSC would lump as one species.

Checklist disagreements (IOC ~11,000 spp vs. Clements ~10,700 spp vs. HBW ~10,600 spp) are versioned ontology conflicts over the same underlying data — different teams applying different species concepts to the same specimens and genetic data. The "split" or "lump" decision for each contested taxon is effectively a schema migration that propagates downstream: banding databases, range maps, and conservation lists all need updating. This is why birding apps track which checklist version they use, and why switching between list authorities can add or subtract dozens of species from a life list without a single new bird being observed.

The Neoaves radiation at 66–56 Mya shows incomplete lineage sorting and rapid cladogenesis — the phylogenetic equivalent of a dependency graph where 30 packages were all published in the same month and their dependency relationships are still being resolved. Polytomies (unresolved branching points) in published bird phylogenies reflect genuine ambiguity from this rapid radiation, not missing data.

## Common Confusion Points

**Falcons are not hawks**: Falconiformes and Accipitriformes independently evolved raptorial lifestyle (convergent evolution). Molecular phylogenies place falcons closer to parrots and passerines than to eagles/hawks. Falcons kill by striking (tomial tooth on bill); hawks/eagles kill by grip.

**Old World vultures vs New World vultures**: Completely different families. Old World vultures (Accipitridae): related to eagles/hawks. New World vultures (Cathartidae, now Cathartiformes): related to storks and shorebirds by molecular data; naked head evolved independently. Both are obligate scavengers — convergent morphology, different phylogenetic position.

**"Crow" intelligence vs other birds**: New Caledonian crows (Corvus moneduloides) make and use stick tools spontaneously. Crows and ravens pass the mirror test (some studies). Corvids have a pallium-based brain organization convergent with mammalian neocortex — similar cognitive outputs via different neural architecture.

**Songbirds vs passerines**: All songbirds (Oscines) are passerines; not all passerines are songbirds. Suboscines (tyrant flycatchers, antbirds, cotingas) are passerines with largely innate vocalizations. Songbirds have learned vocalizations, critical periods, and cultural transmission of songs.

**Checklist species counts vary**: ~10,600 (Clements) to ~11,200 (IOC) reflects species concept differences, not different discoveries. The gap is in how allopatric populations (subspecies in BSC; separate species in PSC) are treated.
