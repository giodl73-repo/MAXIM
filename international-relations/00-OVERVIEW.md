## The Great Debates

International Relations as an academic discipline has been structured by a series of "great debates":

```
IR GREAT DEBATES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FIRST DEBATE (1930s-1940s):
  IDEALISM vs. REALISM
  Idealism (Norman Angell, Woodrow Wilson):
  War is irrational; international law, collective
  security, and liberal institutions can eliminate it.
  WWII answered this debate for a generation.
  Realism (E.H. Carr *The Twenty Years' Crisis*, 1939;
  Morgenthau *Politics Among Nations*, 1948): power,
  not law, governs international relations.

  SECOND DEBATE (1960s):
  CLASSICAL vs. BEHAVIORAL/SCIENTIFIC
  Should IR be humanistic (historical, interpretive)
  or scientific (quantitative, hypothesis-testing)?
  Morgenthau vs. Kaplan.
  Correlates of War project (COW): systematic
  quantitative data on wars, alliances, capabilities.
  Result: both approaches survive; co-exist uneasily.

  THIRD DEBATE / INTER-PARADIGM (1970s-80s):
  REALISM vs. LIBERALISM vs. WORLD-SYSTEMS THEORY
  Three paradigms contesting explanatory primacy.
  The "inter-paradigm debate" — no resolution, but
  recognition that paradigms address different questions.

  FOURTH DEBATE (1980s-1990s):
  POSITIVISM vs. POST-POSITIVISM
  Can IR be a science? Do objective facts about the
  international system exist independently of how
  we interpret them?
  Constructivism, feminist IR, postcolonial IR
  emerge in this debate.
```

## Why IR Matters (to the Learner's Context)

The problems of contemporary international relations are directly relevant to someone working at the intersection of technology and global politics:

- **Nuclear war**: Still the existential risk; 12,500+ warheads globally; deterrence stability under multipolarity being tested
- **Climate**: The collective action problem at the largest scale; every IR theory has different implications for climate governance
- **Technology governance**: Internet governance (ICANN vs. ITU), AI standards, semiconductor supply chains, encryption export — all IR problems
- **Pandemic**: COVID showed limits of WHO authority; international health is an IR problem
- **Trade**: US-China tech decoupling, export controls on semiconductors, WTO disputes — the learner's domain intersects directly

## Common Confusion Points

**Realism ≠ cynicism**: Realism is an analytical framework, not an endorsement of power politics. Structural realists (Waltz) explicitly say the theory describes constraints, not prescriptions. A realist can be morally against war while analytically predicting that states will pursue power.

**Anarchy ≠ chaos**: In IR, "anarchy" means the absence of a central authority above states — no world government. It does not mean disorder. The international system has anarchy at its core but can have considerable order (through balance of power, institutions, norms).

**Levels of analysis are not mutually exclusive**: The three images (individual, state, system) are not competing answers but different lenses. Cuba 1962 can be analyzed at all three levels simultaneously: Kennedy and Khrushchev's personal psychology (Level 1), US domestic politics and Soviet bureaucratic process (Level 2), US-Soviet bipolar rivalry (Level 3). The most sophisticated accounts use all three.

**Paradigms are not teams**: Academic IR sometimes looks like realists vs. liberals vs. constructivists competing for primacy. In practice, the paradigms illuminate different aspects of the same phenomena. Most working IR scholars use multiple frameworks.
