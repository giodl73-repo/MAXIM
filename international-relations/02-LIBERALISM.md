## Kant's Perpetual Peace (1795)

Kant's essay is the ur-text of liberal IR. Written during the French Revolutionary Wars, it proposed conditions for permanent peace:

**First Definitive Article**: The civil constitution of every state shall be republican.
- Under republican government, citizens must consent to war
- Citizens bear the costs (military service, taxation, devastation)
- Therefore citizens will be reluctant to approve wars except in genuine self-defense

**Second Definitive Article**: The right of nations shall be based on a federation of free states.
- A "pacific federation" (*foedus pacificum*), not a world government
- States voluntarily enter and remain; sovereignty preserved
- The zone of peace expands as more states join

**Third Definitive Article**: The law of world citizenship shall be limited to conditions of universal hospitality.
- Strangers have the right not to be treated as enemies
- The basis for cosmopolitan law and human rights norms

Kant's prediction: the Pacific Federation would eventually encompass all republics, creating permanent peace within the zone of liberal democracies. He was describing what we now call the "democratic peace."

## Democratic Peace Theory

### The Empirical Claim

**Doyle (1983, 1997)** formalized the Kantian observation:
- Liberal democracies (Kant's republics: representative government, rule of law, market economy, civil liberties) do not fight each other
- They do fight non-democracies
- This is one of the most robust empirical findings in the quantitative IR literature

```
DEMOCRATIC PEACE — THE EMPIRICAL RECORD
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FINDING:
  Since Kant wrote (1795), there have been almost zero
  wars between liberal democracies (as defined by:
  free elections + civil liberties + rule of law).

  Candidates for exceptions (and why they don't count):
  • Finland-France (1939-40): Finland allied with Germany;
    France/Britain declared war on Finland peripherally.
    Did France and Finland actually fight? Barely.
  • Pakistan-India 1999 (Kargil): Pakistan qualified as
    democracy at the time? Contested.
  • "War of 1812": US and UK — was either liberal
    democracy by today's definition in 1812? Debated.

  COMPARED TO:
  Democracies fight authoritarian states regularly.
  Authoritarian states fight each other regularly.
  Only the dyadic relationship between two democracies
  is peaceful.

  THE STATISTICAL FINDING:
  Bruce Russett (*Grasping the Democratic Peace*, 1993):
  Controlled for all reasonable confounders (geography,
  wealth, alliance, IGO membership), the democratic
  dyad effect holds.
```

### Explanatory Mechanisms

Why do democracies not fight each other?

```
COMPETING EXPLANATIONS FOR DEMOCRATIC PEACE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NORMATIVE EXPLANATION (Doyle, Russett):
  • Democracies develop norms of bounded competition
    (peaceful dispute resolution, respect for law)
  • Applied externally: when facing another democracy,
    these norms extend outward
  • Expect the other democracy to similarly constrain
    itself → low fear, no preventive war logic

  STRUCTURAL/INSTITUTIONAL EXPLANATION:
  • Democratic leaders need public consent for war
  • Audiences costs: democratic leaders who back down
    from threats face electoral punishment;
    but so do leaders who start wars they lose
  • These constraints make commitments more credible
    and signals more costly to bluff

  LIBERAL PEACE (Kantian triangle, Russett-Oneal):
  Democracy + Trade + IGO membership all reduce conflict.
  The three reinforce each other.
  Dyads with all three (two democracies trading heavily
  and sharing IGO membership) have near-zero conflict.

  THE SELECTION EFFECT CRITIQUE (Rosato, Spiro):
  Democracies are not peaceful with each other because
  of their democratic nature — they are peaceful with
  each other because they are allied (in NATO) and
  share strategic interests.
  Remove alliance/power correlation and the democratic
  peace may be spurious.
  Counter (Russett): democratic peace holds even
  controlling for alliance.
```

## Liberal Institutionalism

Robert Keohane (*After Hegemony*, 1984) is the foundational text for liberal institutionalism (also called "neoliberal institutionalism").

**The puzzle**: Waltz predicts that without a hegemon, cooperation collapses. The British hegemony was declining in the early 20th century; order collapsed. US hegemony was declining in the 1970s. Would order collapse again?

**Keohane's answer**: No, because institutions persist. Once created, international institutions change the strategic environment: they reduce transaction costs, provide information, create reputational linkages, and make repeated interaction the norm. Cooperation under anarchy is possible without a hegemon, given sufficient institutional infrastructure.

```
KEOHANE'S INSTITUTIONS ARGUMENT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PROBLEM:
  Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma: defection is rational
  in single-shot PD; cooperation possible in iterated
  PD with shadow of the future.
  BUT: anarchy = no enforcement; can't be sure others
  won't defect when it suits them.

  INSTITUTIONS PROVIDE:
  1. INFORMATION:
     Monitoring compliance; reducing uncertainty about
     others' behavior. If I can verify you're cooperating,
     I'm more willing to cooperate.

  2. TRANSACTION COST REDUCTION:
     Don't renegotiate from scratch each time.
     GATT rounds reduce costs of trade negotiations.
     Standing rules lower costs of reciprocity.

  3. REPUTATIONAL LINKAGE:
     Defecting in one area hurts you in others.
     States care about reputation for compliance.
     Institutions create "nesting" — one defection
     affects your standing across issue areas.

  4. ISSUE LINKAGE:
     Tie cooperation in one area to another.
     Increases value of maintaining cooperation.

  KEY CLAIM:
  Absolute gains matter more than relative gains
  in many issue areas (trade, environment, health).
  If everyone benefits from trade liberalization,
  the realist concern about relative gains is
  not the primary driver.
```

## Keohane-Nye: Power and Interdependence

Robert Keohane and Joseph Nye (*Power and Interdependence*, 1977) introduced the concept of complex interdependence as an alternative to the realist state-centric military power model.

```
COMPLEX INTERDEPENDENCE vs. REALISM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  REALIST WORLD:
  • States are the main actors
  • Military force is the main tool
  • Security dominates the agenda

  COMPLEX INTERDEPENDENCE:
  • Multiple channels: transnational corporations,
    NGOs, international institutions, individuals —
    not just governments
  • Absence of hierarchy among issues: economics,
    environment, health can dominate security
    in specific relationships
  • Military force unusable among some dyads
    (US-Canada, US-EU)

  "SENSITIVITY" vs. "VULNERABILITY" INTERDEPENDENCE:
  Sensitivity: how quickly does a change in one country
  affect another? (Oil price shock: immediate effect on
  importing countries)
  Vulnerability: how costly is it to escape dependence?
  (Can you substitute? At what cost?)

  US-China example:
  High sensitivity: US markets for Chinese exports;
  Chinese Treasury holdings of US bonds.
  Vulnerability being reduced: US semiconductor policy,
  Chinese domestic substitution — decoupling is
  reducing vulnerability on both sides, slowly.
```

## Liberal International Order

The post-WWII liberal international order (see political-history/06-POSTWAR-ORDER.md) is the institutional expression of liberal IR theory:

- NATO (collective security among democracies)
- WTO (open trade, rule-based dispute settlement)
- IMF/World Bank (financial stability, development)
- UN (collective security, multilateral norms)
- EU (deep integration among democracies)

Ikenberry's thesis: the order is "constitutional" — the US self-binds through institutions in exchange for other states accepting its leadership. The order has public goods properties that benefit even non-democratic states (open trade, financial stability).

## Critiques of Liberalism

```
CRITIQUES OF LIBERAL IR
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  REALIST CRITIQUE:
  • Democratic peace is spurious: democracies allied
    vs. shared authoritarian threats; remove that
    correlation and peace may not follow from democracy
  • Institutions are epiphenomenal: they work when
    great powers want them to; when security concerns
    dominate, institutions don't constrain
  • "Liberal peace" is a period-specific artifact of
    US hegemony and the Cold War alliance structure

  CONSTRUCTIVIST CRITIQUE:
  • Liberal theory still treats interests as given
    (states want prosperity and security);
    constructivists ask how interests are constructed
  • "Liberal peace" creates an in-group/out-group:
    democracies are "in"; non-democracies are potential
    threats → can generate confrontation with China, etc.

  EMPIRICAL CHALLENGES:
  • Democratic backsliding (Hungary, Poland, US 2016-21):
    the zone of peace may be shrinking as democracies
    become hybrid regimes
  • Trade-conflict relationship: Germany-France 1914
    (high trade before war); US-China today
    → high interdependence didn't prevent competition
  • WTO Appellate Body crisis: US blocking appointments
    from 2019 → institutional crisis within liberal order

  POSTCOLONIAL CRITIQUE:
  • Liberal order was built by and for Western powers
  • "Liberal" institutions often served imperial interests
    (IMF structural adjustment = neocolonial extraction)
  • Non-Western states were not design partners
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Liberal answer |
|----------|----------------|
| Why is democratic peace so robust? | Normative (shared norms of bounded competition) + institutional (audience costs, executive constraint); Kantian triangle effect |
| Do institutions matter or are they epiphenomenal? | Keohane: institutions reduce transaction costs, provide information, enable cooperation even without hegemon |
| Does trade prevent war? | Commercial liberalism: trade creates mutual gains interests → reduces war incentive; but high-interdependence pairs can still compete (China-US) |
| Is US hegemony necessary for world order? | Hegemonic stability: probably for certain public goods; but Keohane argues institutions can maintain cooperation even as hegemony declines |
| Can China be accommodated in the liberal order? | Liberal hope: economic integration socializes preferences; realist fear: China is challenging the order's fundamental rules |
| Are relative or absolute gains more important? | Neoliberal institutionalism: absolute gains dominate in most economic issue areas; realism: relative gains dominate in security |

## Common Confusion Points

**Democratic peace ≠ pacifism of democracies**: Democracies fight frequently — just not against other democracies. The US has been at war in non-democracies (Korea, Vietnam, Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya) consistently. The democratic peace is a dyadic finding, not a claim about democratic pacifism.

**Liberal institutionalism ≠ world government**: Keohane and Nye are not proposing supranational authority replacing states. Institutions work within the anarchic system by changing the incentive environment — not by creating enforcement authority. States comply because it's in their interest; not because they're compelled.

**Commercial peace has limits**: The "sweet commerce" argument (trade makes people less violent) has some empirical support but is clearly not deterministic. Germany and Britain were deeply interdependent in 1913 and went to war in 1914. The causal story is: trade raises the cost of conflict, but doesn't eliminate it if the security stakes are high enough.

**Kantian republics ≠ modern liberal democracies**: Kant's notion of a republic (representative government with rule of law) was much more limited than modern liberal democracy. Some historical cases that qualify as "republics" by Kant's definition would not by contemporary democratic standards. This is relevant because the democratic peace finding is sensitive to the definition of democracy.
