## Classical Realism: Morgenthau

Hans Morgenthau (*Politics Among Nations*, 1948) built the foundational American IR realist framework. Written for postwar policy audience; the most influential IR textbook of the 20th century.

### Six Principles of Political Realism

Morgenthau's famous six principles:

1. **Political realism believes that politics, like society in general, is governed by objective laws that have their roots in human nature.** The political world is governed by regular patterns.

2. **The main signpost of political realism is the concept of interest defined in terms of power.** The key concept is *national interest*, operationalized as power.

3. **Realism assumes that the key concept of interest defined as power is an objective category which is universally valid but not with a meaning that is fixed once and for all.** Power means different things in different historical contexts.

4. **Political realism is aware of the moral significance of political action.** Moral considerations exist; they are constrained by political realities.

5. **Political realism refuses to identify the moral aspirations of a particular nation with the moral laws that govern the universe.** Nationalism does not equal universal morality; realism is skeptical of ideological foreign policy.

6. **The autonomy of the political sphere.** The political sphere has its own logic; cannot be reduced to economics, ethics, or law.

```
MORGENTHAU: THE NATIONAL INTEREST CONCEPT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NATIONAL INTEREST = SURVIVAL + POWER PRESERVATION

  Defined in terms of power:
  • Military capability
  • Economic capacity
  • Geographic position
  • National morale and cohesion
  • Quality of government and diplomacy

  POLICY IMPLICATIONS:
  • Alliances are temporary (interests change)
  • Don't moralize foreign policy (Vietnam as counterexample)
  • Keep channels open to adversaries
  • Balance power; don't seek dominance
  • "The national interest is the interest of the
    state, not of humanity" — a frank limitation

  CRITIQUE:
  • How do we know what the "national interest" is?
  • Who defines it? (Public choice problem)
  • Human nature as cause is unfalsifiable
  • Ignores domestic politics (neoclassical realism fixes this)
```

## Structural Realism: Waltz

Kenneth Waltz (*Theory of International Politics*, 1979) is the most rigorous realist theory. It is explicitly a scientific theory with a defined domain of application.

### The Three-Image Argument

Waltz's prior book (*Man, the State and War*, 1959) examined where war originates — at what "image" or level of analysis:

- **Image 1** (individual/human nature): War comes from human nature. Problem: all humans have aggressive impulses; not all states fight all the time.
- **Image 2** (state/domestic): War comes from particular types of states (authoritarian, capitalist, etc.). Problem: different state types have fought all kinds of wars.
- **Image 3** (systemic/structural): War comes from the structure of the international system — anarchy plus distribution of capabilities. This is Waltz's answer.

### The Structural Theory

```
WALTZ'S STRUCTURAL REALISM — CORE LOGIC
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  DEFINING CHARACTERISTIC OF INTERNATIONAL POLITICS:
  ANARCHY — no central authority above states.
  States must be self-help actors.

  STRUCTURAL VARIABLES:
  1. Ordering principle: Anarchy (vs. hierarchy/domestic)
  2. Character of units: Formally equal (states)
  3. Distribution of capabilities (polarity):
     Unipolar / Bipolar / Multipolar

  WALTZ'S KEY CLAIMS:
  (a) STRUCTURE CONSTRAINS:
  Regardless of what type of state you are (democratic,
  authoritarian, capitalist), anarchy pushes you toward
  the same behaviors: self-help, power accumulation,
  balancing.

  (b) BALANCE OF POWER IS THE TENDENCY:
  States balance against threats (power concentrations).
  If one state grows too powerful, others balance against it.
  This produces approximate equilibrium.

  (c) BIPOLAR > MULTIPOLAR FOR STABILITY:
  Cold War bipolarity was more stable than the
  multipolar system of 1914-1939 because:
  • Fewer alliance decisions = fewer miscalculations
  • Two major nuclear powers = deterrence more
    credible and stable
  • Responsibility for maintaining order clearer

  (d) STATES ARE UNITARY RATIONAL ACTORS:
  For systemic theory purposes, treat states as
  billiard balls — internal structure doesn't matter.
  (This is the "black box" assumption — criticized
  by neoclassical realists who open the box)
```

### Defensive vs. Offensive Realism

Waltz's theory implies defensive realism: states want security, not power maximization per se. States want to survive and maintain their position; they don't inherently want to expand.

**Mearsheimer's offensive realism critique** (*The Tragedy of Great Power Politics*, 2001): Waltz is wrong about state motivations. Anarchy doesn't push states to seek security — it pushes them to seek power maximally. Because the future is uncertain and intentions are unknowable, the only safe position is hegemony: the ability to dominate the system.

```
DEFENSIVE vs. OFFENSIVE REALISM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  DEFENSIVE REALISM (Waltz, Jervis, Van Evera):
  • States want security
  • Power is a means to security, not an end
  • Excessive expansion is dangerous (costly, provokes
    balancing coalitions — Mearsheimer ignores this risk)
  • Security dilemma: defensive buildup looks offensive
    → accidental escalation is the main danger

  OFFENSIVE REALISM (Mearsheimer):
  • States want power — as much as possible
  • The optimal strategy is hegemony in your region;
    prevent others from achieving hegemony in theirs
  • "Stopping power of water" (oceans) limits ability
    to achieve global hegemony (hence: no world hegemon)
  • Regional hegemons exist (US in Western Hemisphere)
  • Tragedy: states pursuing rational strategies
    → conflict inevitable

  IMPLICATIONS FOR CHINA:
  Defensive realism: China can be "satisfied" as a
  great power; accommodation possible
  Offensive realism (Mearsheimer): China will seek
  hegemony in Asia just as US sought Western Hemisphere
  hegemony; conflict with US structural, not contingent
```

## Mearsheimer: The Tragedy of Great Power Politics

John Mearsheimer's 2001 book is the systematic statement of offensive realism and the clearest prediction that China-US conflict is structurally overdetermined.

### The Five Assumptions

Mearsheimer builds from five assumptions about the international system:

1. The international system is anarchic (no central authority)
2. Great powers possess offensive military capability
3. States can never be certain about other states' intentions
4. Survival is the primary goal of great powers
5. States are rational actors (mean-ends reasoning)

From these five assumptions, Mearsheimer derives: great powers maximize relative power because that's the only rational response to anarchy + uncertain intentions + offensive capability.

```
MEARSHEIMER'S CHINA PREDICTION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ARGUMENT:
  The US achieved hegemony in the Western Hemisphere in
  the 19th century (Monroe Doctrine; expelled European powers).
  No other great power has achieved regional hegemony since.

  China's rise → China will seek regional hegemony in Asia
  (just as US sought it in the Americas)

  US will try to prevent this
  (just as it prevented German/Soviet European hegemony
   and Japanese Asian hegemony)

  → Structural conflict is inevitable; not about
    China being "bad" — about structural logic

  PREDICTION (2001): "If China continues to grow
  economically, it will attempt to dominate Asia the
  way the United States dominates the Western
  Hemisphere today."

  STATUS (2024): Largely confirmed in trajectory;
  disputed on inevitability (interdependence
  argument says the theory underweights costs
  of conflict in integrated economies)
```

## Neoclassical Realism

Randall Schweller, Fareed Zakaria, William Wohlforth (1990s-2000s): Waltz's structural theory explains long-run systemic tendencies but cannot explain specific foreign policy choices. Opening the "black box" of domestic politics reveals:

- State leaders may not accurately translate systemic incentives into policy (misperception, cognitive bias)
- Domestic mobilization capacity affects whether states can act on structural incentives
- Ideology and nationalism channel responses to structural pressure

```
NEOCLASSICAL REALISM — THE TRANSMISSION BELT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SYSTEMIC INPUT:
  (Waltz: distribution of capabilities, polarity)
       │
       ▼
  INTERVENING DOMESTIC VARIABLES:
  • State-society relations (can government extract
    resources for foreign policy?)
  • Leadership perceptions (do leaders accurately
    perceive the power balance?)
  • Ideology (does it channel or distort responses?)
  • Internal cohesion (can the state act?)
       │
       ▼
  FOREIGN POLICY OUTPUT:
  (actual behavior — may deviate from what pure
   structural theory predicts)

  EXAMPLE:
  Waltz predicts rising powers are balanced against.
  Germany was not balanced against sufficiently in
  the 1930s. Why not?
  Neoclassical: British elite misperceptions (Hitler
  as rational, not ideological), domestic constraints
  (public anti-war sentiment), economic vulnerability
  (sterling and trade) → appeasement despite structural
  incentives to balance.
```

## Realism and Nuclear Weapons

Realist theory intersects with deterrence theory directly:

- Nuclear weapons create a special case: the cost of war is so catastrophic that rational great powers avoid direct conflict
- Mearsheimer and Waltz both argued that nuclear deterrence stabilizes great power competition
- Waltz infamously argued that nuclear proliferation could be stabilizing — more nuclear states means more deterrence and less war (controversial)
- The security dilemma is most dangerous in conventional military competition; nuclear deterrence dampens it

```
REALISM AND DETERRENCE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  REALIST DETERRENCE LOGIC:
  States are rational + catastrophic nuclear costs
  → No first strike (rational actor wouldn't do it
    if retaliation is certain)
  → Stability requires secure second-strike capability
  → MAD is actually stable by realist logic

  WALTZ ON PROLIFERATION:
  "The Spread of Nuclear Weapons: A Debate" (with Sagan):
  Waltz: More nuclear states → more deterrence →
  fewer wars. Stability of Indian-Pakistani deterrence.

  SAGAN'S COUNTER: Organizational accidents,
  irrational leaders, miscalculation —
  proliferation increases risks.

  Current assessment: Both were partly right.
  Indian-Pakistani nuclear rivalry has been deterred
  (no full-scale war since both tested, 1998)
  but the risks are real and accidents nearly happened.
```

## Critiques of Realism

```
CRITIQUES — ORGANIZED BY PARADIGM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LIBERAL CRITIQUE:
  • Ignores cooperation: states cooperate extensively
    in trade, international law, institutions
  • Democratic peace: democracies don't fight each other
    — regime type matters, not just anarchy
  • Absolute vs. relative gains: states care about
    getting richer, not just stronger than rivals

  CONSTRUCTIVIST CRITIQUE:
  • "Anarchy is what states make of it" (Wendt):
    the realist anarchic structure is not given;
    it's a social construction
  • Identities and interests are not fixed by
    the system; they are constructed through interaction
  • Norms and ideas matter: Nuclear weapons in Britain
    not feared the same as in North Korea (Wendt)

  EMPIRICAL CRITIQUE:
  • Balance of power doesn't reliably emerge:
    US hegemony since 1991 without serious balancing
    (Wohlforth: "stability of a unipolar world")
  • Bandwagoning more common than balancing in
    specific contexts (weak states joining strong)
  • Democratic peace is robust empirically — realism
    can't explain why democracies don't fight each other
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Realist answer |
|----------|----------------|
| Why does war happen? | Anarchy + distribution of power + security dilemma; rational states pursue power, conflict follows |
| Do alliances last? | No — alliances are temporary coalitions of interest; balance against the most threatening, not the most powerful |
| Will China challenge the US? | Offensive realism: yes, structurally determined; defensive realism: possible accommodation if China is "satisfied" |
| Does trade prevent war? | Realism: no — relative gains concerns mean trade doesn't prevent conflict between competing great powers |
| Are nuclear weapons stabilizing? | Waltz: yes — secure second strike → mutual deterrence → great powers avoid direct conflict |
| Does foreign policy follow rational interest? | Structural realism: yes; neoclassical: sometimes distorted by domestic politics and misperception |

## Common Confusion Points

**Waltz vs. Morgenthau**: Often conflated. Morgenthau is a classical realist who grounds behavior in human nature; Waltz is a structural realist who grounds it in the anarchic system. Waltz deliberately excluded human nature from his theory to make it more scientific and falsifiable.

**Defensive realism ≠ pacifism**: Defensive realism doesn't say states shouldn't have military forces or should pursue disarmament. It says states want security and will not expand beyond what security requires. The security dilemma is still a problem — defensive measures can still trigger arms races.

**Mearsheimer is not endorsing Chinese expansion**: When Mearsheimer predicts China will seek regional hegemony, he is not saying China is right to do so or that US resistance is wrong. He is describing what structural theory predicts. This is sometimes lost in popular discussion of the Thucydides Trap argument.

**Realism doesn't predict specific wars**: Structural realism predicts tendencies (balancing, power competition) not specific outcomes (X will attack Y in year Z). The theory is too thin to predict specific events — it explains patterns over long time periods.
