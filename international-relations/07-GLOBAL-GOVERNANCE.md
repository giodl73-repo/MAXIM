## G7 and G20: Informal Governance

The G7 (Group of Seven) and G20 are "club" governance mechanisms — not treaty-based organizations but recurring summits of major economies.

```
G7 / G20 — STRUCTURE AND FUNCTION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  G7:
  US, UK, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Canada
  (+ EU as non-enumerated participant)
  Started: 1975 (oil crisis + Bretton Woods collapse)
  Function: macro-economic policy coordination;
  originally financial; now broader (security, climate,
  pandemic, AI)
  Character: Western liberal democracies club
  Legitimacy deficit: represents ~40% of world GDP but
  ~10% of world population

  G20:
  G7 + China, India, Brazil, Russia, South Africa,
  Australia, Indonesia, South Korea, Mexico, Saudi Arabia,
  Argentina, Turkey, EU, African Union (2023)
  Started: 1999 (finance ministers); summits since 2008
  Function: global economic coordination; became
  leaders' forum after 2008 financial crisis
  Character: covers ~85% of world GDP, 75% of world trade
  Legitimacy: more representative than G7;
  still a club (Africa Union has 1 seat for 54 countries)

  G20 vs. G7:
  G20 can address issues requiring China/India/Brazil;
  harder to reach consensus → less ambitious outcomes
  G7 can move faster but without the "global South"
```

## Climate Governance

Climate change is the paradigmatic global governance challenge: a classic collective action problem where all countries must act but each has incentive to free-ride.

```
CLIMATE GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  UNFCCC (1992):
  Framework convention — established the principle that
  states should avoid "dangerous anthropogenic interference"
  with the climate system.
  Universal (197 parties); annual COP (Conference of Parties).
  No binding emissions targets; just the framework.

  KYOTO PROTOCOL (1997):
  First binding emissions targets — for developed countries.
  Annex I states (OECD + transition economies) committed
  to specific reductions.
  Flaws:
  • US never ratified
  • China, India exempt (developing countries)
  • Compliance mechanism weak
  • Canada withdrew after falling behind (2011)
  Died in practice if not in name.

  PARIS AGREEMENT (2015):
  The current framework. Key innovation: NDCs.

  NDC (Nationally Determined Contribution) ARCHITECTURE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Each country submits its own emissions reduction pledge  │
  │ (NDC) — self-determined, not internationally imposed     │
  │                                                          │
  │ Countries must update NDCs every 5 years                 │
  │ (ratchet mechanism — pledges should increase over time)  │
  │                                                          │
  │ Transparency mechanism: countries must report progress   │
  │                                                          │
  │ GOAL: Limit warming to 1.5°C / well below 2°C            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  THE GAP:
  Current NDCs (even if fully implemented): ~2.5-3°C warming
  Required for 1.5°C: ~45% cut in global emissions by 2030
  Gap is large; no enforcement mechanism to close it.

  WHY IT'S HARD:
  Collective action: each country benefits from others
  reducing emissions; each prefers to free-ride.
  Equity: historical emissions (industrial countries);
  vs. current emissions (China #1 total; India growing).
  Technology: clean energy transition requires capital
  that developing countries may not have.
  Domestic politics: carbon pricing faces opposition;
  fossil fuel interests strong.
```

## Pandemic Governance

COVID-19 demonstrated both the potential and limits of international health governance.

```
PANDEMIC GOVERNANCE — COVID LESSONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  WHO AUTHORITY:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ WHO can declare a PHEIC (Public Health Emergency         │
  │ of International Concern) and make recommendations       │
  │                                                          │
  │ WHO CANNOT:                                              │
  │ • Enter China to investigate independently               │
  │ • Override national public health decisions              │
  │ • Mandate quarantine or travel restrictions              │
  │ • Enforce reporting requirements                         │
  │                                                          │
  │ IHR (International Health Regulations):                  │
  │ Countries must report "potential PHEICs."                │
  │ China reported COVID to WHO: December 31, 2019           │
  │ SARS began in November 2002; reported March 2003.        │
  │ The incentive structure punishes early reporting         │
  │ (economic costs of warning; political costs of           │
  │  acknowledging outbreak).                                │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  COVAX:
  WHO-coordinated global vaccine procurement mechanism.
  Goal: ensure equitable vaccine access to low-income states.
  Result: Failed for high-income countries (bought
  directly; didn't contribute enough to COVAX);
  partially succeeded for low-income.
  Lesson: rich countries defected from multilateral
  mechanism when bilateral options were faster.

  IHR REFORM NEGOTIATIONS (2022+):
  Post-COVID negotiations for stronger WHO authority:
  Pandemic Accord (negotiated 2022-2024):
  Contentious: developing countries wanted IP waivers
  for vaccine production; developed countries opposed.
  WHO governance: should WHO have stronger reporting
  authority? Great powers resistant.

  LESSON: Health governance faces the same dilemmas
  as all international governance: sovereignty claims
  trump collective action needs at critical moments.
```

## Financial Governance

```
INTERNATIONAL FINANCIAL GOVERNANCE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FSB (Financial Stability Board, 2009):
  Created after 2008 crisis; G20 body.
  Coordinates national financial regulators;
  identifies systemic risks; recommends standards.
  No enforcement power; works through peer pressure
  and national regulatory implementation.

  BASEL ACCORDS (Basel Committee on Banking Supervision):
  Basel I (1988): Capital adequacy ratios (8% of risk-weighted assets)
  Basel II (2004): Three pillars; risk weights; disclosure
  Basel III (2010, revised post-COVID): Higher capital requirements;
  leverage ratio; liquidity coverage ratio; countercyclical buffers.
  Process: central bank governors negotiate standards;
  implemented in national law (EU CRD; US Federal Reserve rules).
  No international enforcement; implementation varies.

  THE "TOO BIG TO FAIL" PROBLEM:
  Post-2008 designation: Systemically Important Financial
  Institutions (SIFIs) face higher capital requirements.
  Global SIFIs list maintained by FSB.
  Still not resolved: interconnection means SIFI failure
  can't be allowed; implicit government guarantee remains.

  CRYPTO AND DIGITAL ASSETS (emerging):
  FATF (Financial Action Task Force): AML/CFT standards
  for crypto assets — travel rule, exchange registration.
  No comprehensive international regulatory framework yet.
  Learner context: this intersects directly with Microsoft's
  financial services customers and Azure compliance requirements.
```

## Internet Governance: The Splinternet Risk

Internet governance is directly relevant to the learner's domain.

```
INTERNET GOVERNANCE — COMPETING MODELS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  MULTI-STAKEHOLDER MODEL (current):
  ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers):
  Manages DNS (domain names), IP address allocation.
  "Multi-stakeholder" governance: governments, civil society,
  private sector, technical community all participate.
  No formal state control; US government historically
  had oversight (until 2016 "IANA transition").

  ITU (International Telecommunication Union):
  UN agency; governments only; "multilateral" governance.
  China and Russia want ITU to govern the internet:
  → Formal government control
  → "Sovereign" internet model

  COMPETING VISIONS:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┐
  │ MULTI-STAKEHOLDER (US/EU/UK)       │ GOVERNMENT CONTROL (China/Russia) │
  │                                    │                                   │
  │ • Open, global, interoperable      │ • Sovereign internets             │
  │ • Limited government role          │ • Governments control content     │
  │ • Technical community leads        │ • ITU governance                  │
  │ • Civil society participating      │ • No cross-border data flows      │
  │ • Free data flows                  │   without government approval     │
  └────────────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────┘

  THE SPLINTERNET:
  A fragmented internet with different versions in
  different jurisdictions.
  • China: Great Firewall (separate domestic internet)
  • Russia: SORM (deep packet inspection, Runet)
  • EU: GDPR creates de facto data localization pressure
  • India: data localization requirements
  Current state: not yet fully splintered; the TCP/IP layer
  remains globally interoperable; the application/content
  layer is increasingly fragmented.

  STANDARDS COMPETITION:
  5G standards (Huawei vs. Ericsson/Nokia/Qualcomm);
  AI standards (ISO/IEC JTC 1 SC 42);
  Cloud standards: whoever sets standards shapes
  the architecture.
  The learner's context: Azure compliance, Microsoft's
  role in internet governance (MSFT submitted extensively
  to ICANN, ITU, ISO on AI standards).
```

## UN Reform Debates

```
UN REFORM — MAIN PROPOSALS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  UNSC EXPANSION:
  The P5 reflects the 1945 power distribution.
  Germany, Japan, India, Brazil, African seats
  all proposed as permanent members.
  Problem: P5 must agree to expand (they vote on
  amendment); each P5 member has allies and rivals
  among proposed additions; China blocks Japan;
  US equivocates on India; P5 won't expand their
  own privileged club easily.

  VETO REFORM:
  Require agreement of multiple P5 members for a veto
  (joint veto); limit veto in mass atrocity cases;
  voluntary veto restraint (France proposed this for R2P).
  Status: no P5 member will agree to weaken its veto.

  R2P (RESPONSIBILITY TO PROTECT):
  World Summit 2005: states responsible to protect
  populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing,
  crimes against humanity.
  If a state fails to protect, the international
  community has responsibility to act.
  Invoked for Libya 2011: NATO used it for military action.
  Russia/China: vetoed Syria intervention;
  argued Libya intervention exceeded its mandate
  → effectively ended R2P as operational norm
  (states now associate R2P with regime change).

  GENERAL ASSEMBLY REFORM:
  GA resolutions pass with simple majority but represent
  unequal populations (Vanuatu = China). No veto at GA.
  Weighted voting proposals: weighted by population?
  GDP? HDI? Never agreed; smaller states
  protect their equal vote.
```

## The Crisis of Multilateralism

```
MULTILATERALISM UNDER STRESS (2016-2024)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  US RETRENCHMENT (Trump 2017-21):
  Withdrew from: Paris Agreement, WHO, UNESCO,
  JCPOA (Iran nuclear), TPP, UNHRC.
  America First = institutions are net cost if they
  constrain US unilateral action.
  Impact: severely damaged multilateral trust;
  partially restored by Biden.

  GREAT POWER COMPETITION:
  US-China contest over standards, institutions,
  norms undermines multilateral solutions.
  G20 communiqués on Ukraine: Russia present;
  consensus impossible on key paragraphs.
  UNSC: paralyzed on Ukraine, Gaza.

  NORMATIVE FRAGMENTATION:
  China/Russia offer alternative "multilateralism":
  SCO (Shanghai Cooperation Organisation),
  BRICS (expanding: Brazil, Russia, India, China,
  South Africa + Saudi Arabia, Iran, UAE, Ethiopia).
  BRICS is not an alternative institution but
  a political signaling platform.
  SCO is a security cooperation mechanism without
  collective defense clause.

  WHAT REMAINS FUNCTIONAL:
  Technical multilateralism (food safety standards,
  aviation safety, shipping, weather data, GPS)
  mostly works because states benefit and
  great power competition doesn't dominate.
  Political multilateralism under severe stress.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is "governance without government"? | Rosenau: managing global issues through norms, institutions, and coordination without a central enforcement authority |
| Why is the Paris Agreement designed with NDCs? | Previous top-down targets (Kyoto) failed because key states (US, China) wouldn't accept binding commitments; NDC model is bottom-up — countries set their own pace |
| What is ICANN and why does it matter? | Manages DNS and IP allocation; key internet governance body; China/Russia want ITU (UN intergovernmental) to replace it — the governance fight is about state control vs. multi-stakeholder |
| What is the splinternet? | Fragmented internet with incompatible national versions; content/application layer increasingly fragmented (China's Great Firewall); TCP/IP layer still globally interoperable |
| Why is R2P effectively dead? | Libya 2011 used it for regime change; Russia/China blocked Syria; states now associate R2P with Western interventionism; won't invoke it |
| What is the G20's advantage over the G7? | Includes China, India, Brazil, Russia — covers 85% of world GDP; G7 without these is politically incomplete for global economic issues |

## Common Confusion Points

**NDCs are voluntary**: Paris Agreement NDCs are not internationally binding. Countries can submit any target they choose. The commitment is to submit targets and report progress — not to achieve any specific target. This is both the strength (countries participate) and weakness (inadequate targets, poor compliance) of the Paris architecture.

**WHO declared COVID a PHEIC on January 30, 2020**: WHO responded faster to COVID than to previous outbreaks. The criticism is that it was slow to declare a "pandemic" (March 11, 2020) and that it was diplomatically deferential to China in the early weeks. The WHO's constraints are structural (no inspection authority, depends on member state reporting), not primarily about individual decision failures.

**ITU vs. ICANN is not a proxy war for "internet freedom"**: The ITU vs. multi-stakeholder debate is framed as authoritarian vs. free internet governance. But ITU governance could be designed with civil liberties protections; and ICANN's multi-stakeholder model has its own accountability problems. The substance is about who controls the domain name system and routing, which has real governance implications but isn't solely about free speech.

**BRICS is not an alternative to G7**: BRICS has no secretariat, no enforcement mechanism, no shared currency, and no genuine institutional infrastructure. It is a political platform for coordination and signaling among the member states. The New Development Bank (NDB) is a real institution, but it is small relative to the World Bank.
