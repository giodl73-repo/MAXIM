## United Nations System

### Charter and Structure

The UN Charter (signed June 26, 1945; entered force October 24, 1945) established the fundamental framework. The Charter's drafters deliberately tried to correct the failures of the League of Nations: the League lacked US participation and had no enforcement mechanism.

```
UN SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SECURITY COUNCIL (SC)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 15 members: 5 permanent (P5: US, UK, France, Russia,     │
  │ China) + 10 elected for 2-year terms                     │
  │                                                          │
  │ ARTICLE 25: Members accept decisions of SC as binding    │
  │ → SC can authorize force, impose sanctions,              │
  │   demand ceasefires                                      │
  │                                                          │
  │ VETO POWER: Any P5 member can block any substantive      │
  │ resolution                                               │
  │ → Design reflects 1945 reality: great powers won't     │
  │   accept binding decisions against their interests     │
  │ → Consequence: SC gridlocks when P5 interests conflict   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  GENERAL ASSEMBLY (GA)
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 193 member states, equal voting (one state, one vote)    │
  │ ARTICLE 11: GA makes recommendations, not binding        │
  │ decisions on peace and security                          │
  │                                                          │
  │ Majority in GA ≠ majority of world population            │
  │ (Vanuatu has same vote as China)                         │
  │                                                          │
  │ GA as political theater:                                 │
  │ Non-binding resolutions; legitimacy signaling;           │
  │ norm-creation (UDHR adopted as GA resolution)            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SECRETARIAT:
  Secretary-General: Ban Ki-moon, Guterres, Kofi Annan
  Administrative and diplomatic functions
  No independent enforcement capacity

  PRINCIPAL JUDICIAL ORGAN: ICJ
  World Court; hears disputes between states;
  advisory opinions; no compulsory jurisdiction
  (states must consent to jurisdiction)
```

### UNSC Veto Dynamics

```
VETO USAGE BY COUNTRY (selected)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  RUSSIA (+ USSR): ~120 vetoes since 1945
  Most common: Middle East (protecting Syria,
  preventing action against Soviet-aligned states)

  US: ~80 vetoes since 1945
  Most common: Israel-related resolutions
  (Middle East peace process, settlements)

  CHINA: ~15 vetoes (mostly since 1971,
  when PRC replaced ROC on the Council)
  Recent: vetoing Syria/Myanmar action

  UK/FRANCE: fewer; often aligned with US

  STRUCTURAL EFFECT:
  SC can authorize military action against
  non-P5 states that lack a patron.
  → Gulf War (1990): Iraq invaded Kuwait;
    USSR/China didn't veto; UNSC authorized force
  → Libya (2011): Russia/China abstained;
    UNSC authorized no-fly zone

  SC is paralyzed when P5 interests conflict:
  → Ukraine (Russia vetoes any action against itself)
  → Syria (Russia/China veto action against Assad)
  → Israeli-Palestinian conflict (US vetoes most resolutions)
```

## WTO and Trade Law

### GATT to WTO

General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (1947): Created as provisional arrangement while the International Trade Organization (ITO) charter was being negotiated. US Senate rejected ITO (1950); GATT became permanent de facto.

GATT operated through "rounds" of multilateral tariff negotiations:
- Kennedy Round (1964-67): major tariff cuts
- Tokyo Round (1973-79): extended to non-tariff barriers
- Uruguay Round (1986-94): GATT transformed into WTO with dispute settlement mechanism

```
WTO CORE PRINCIPLES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  MOST-FAVORED-NATION (MFN):
  Any trade advantage granted to one member must be
  extended to all WTO members.
  If US reduces tariff on Japanese cars, must do the
  same for all WTO members' cars.
  Exception: regional free trade agreements (EU, USMCA)
  can have preferential tariffs within the group.

  NATIONAL TREATMENT:
  Once goods are inside a country, they must be treated
  no worse than domestic goods (no discriminatory
  domestic taxes, regulations).

  BOUND TARIFFS:
  Members commit to maximum ("bound") tariff rates.
  Can always charge less; can't charge more.
  Provides predictability for exporters.

  DISPUTE SETTLEMENT MECHANISM (DSM):
  Panels hear complaints; Appellate Body reviews
  panel decisions; if found in violation, must
  change policy or face authorized retaliation.

  THE DSM CRISIS:
  US blocked new Appellate Body appointments starting 2019:
  argued AB was over-reaching (legislating through
  dispute settlement, not just applying rules).
  By 2019: Appellate Body dropped below quorum;
  can no longer function.
  Most WTO disputes now unresolvable at appellate level.
  → Structural crisis in international trade law
```

## IMF and International Financial Institutions

### IMF Function

The International Monetary Fund (1944, Bretton Woods) was designed to:
1. Maintain fixed exchange rates (the Bretton Woods system, now gone)
2. Lend to countries with balance of payments deficits
3. Conduct economic surveillance

```
IMF EVOLUTION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  1944-71: BRETTON WOODS MANAGER
  Fixed exchange rates; IMF managed parity adjustments;
  provided short-term BOP financing.

  1971+: SURVEILLANCE AND CRISIS LENDING
  Bretton Woods collapsed (Nixon shock 1971).
  IMF's mandate evolved:
  • Bilateral consultations (Article IV): annual
    country reviews of economic policies
  • Crisis lending: Mexico 1982, Asian crisis 1997-98,
    Greece 2010-18, Argentina multiple times

  CONDITIONALITY:
  IMF loans come with policy conditions
  (structural adjustment programs):
  • Fiscal austerity (reduce deficits)
  • Currency devaluation
  • Privatization of state enterprises
  • Trade and capital account liberalization
  = The Washington Consensus (see POSTWAR-ORDER)

  CRITICISM OF CONDITIONALITY:
  • "Washington Consensus" imposed on developing
    countries by Washington-based institution
  • Austerity deepens recessions (Greek example)
  • One-size-fits-all; ignores political economy
  • Stiglitz (former World Bank Chief Economist):
    IMF conditionality made Asian crisis worse (1997-98)

  VOTING SHARES:
  IMF voting is weighted by economic contribution.
  US: ~17% of votes (and >15% required for key decisions)
  → US has effective veto on IMF decisions
  China: ~6% (rising; underrepresented vs. share of global GDP)
  → Governance reform is a continuous demand from
     emerging market economies
```

## ICC and International Courts

```
INTERNATIONAL COURTS — JURISDICTION TYPES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ICJ (International Court of Justice):
  → Between states (inter-state disputes)
  Jurisdiction: only with consent of both parties
  Advisory opinions: can be requested by UN organs
  Enforcement: SC can authorize enforcement; rarely does
  Examples: Nicaragua vs. US (1986); Qatar vs. UAE (2018)

  ICC (International Criminal Court, 2002):
  → Individual criminal accountability
  Rome Statute: 124 states party
  NOT party: US, China, Russia, India
  Jurisdiction: crimes against humanity, war crimes,
  genocide, aggression; only when national courts
  unwilling/unable to prosecute

  ICC ENFORCEMENT PROBLEM:
  No police force; depends on state cooperation.
  Sudan's Al-Bashir: ICC warrant (2009); traveled freely
  for years; Sudan never surrendered him.
  Russia's Putin: ICC arrest warrant (2023, Ukrainian
  children deportation); Russia won't extinguish;
  some ICC party states would have obligation to arrest
  if Putin entered their territory.

  WTO PANELS:
  [See above; Appellate Body in crisis]

  UNCLOS ARBITRATION:
  UN Convention on the Law of the Sea tribunals.
  Permanent Court of Arbitration.
  Philippines vs. China (2016): China rejected
  jurisdiction; ignored ruling; illustrates enforcement
  problem for institutional decisions.
```

## Regional Institutions

```
REGIONAL INSTITUTIONS — KEY CASES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations, 1967):
  10 members; "ASEAN Way" = consensus, non-interference,
  no criticism of members' domestic affairs.
  South China Sea: ASEAN struggles to present unified
  front (Cambodia and Laos are effectively Chinese proxies).
  Security: no Article 5 equivalent; not a security alliance.
  Economic: ASEAN+3 free trade, RCEP.

  AFRICAN UNION (2002, successor to OAU):
  55 African states.
  "African solutions to African problems" — but limited
  capacity. AU peacekeeping missions (Somalia, CAR, Mali)
  are understaffed and underfunded.
  Norm: "non-indifference" vs. OAU's "non-interference"
  (allowing AU to intervene in crises, at least in theory).
  R2P applied in Libya (2011) — African states mixed.

  EU (see POSTWAR-ORDER):
  Deepest regional integration; binding supranational law;
  European Court of Justice enforceable decisions.
  Security: still primarily NATO; EU has PESCO (Permanent
  Structured Cooperation) for defense but limited.
  After Ukraine 2022: significant increase in EU defense
  integration and spending.

  GULF COOPERATION COUNCIL (GCC):
  Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman.
  Primarily economic coordination; Saudi Arabia dominant.
  Qatar crisis (2017-21): Saudi blockade of Qatar (disputes
  over ties to Iran, Qatar hosting Al Jazeera, Turkey).
  Resolution via Kuwait/US mediation.
```

## Do Institutions Matter?

```
INSTITUTIONS EFFECTIVENESS DEBATE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LIBERAL INSTITUTIONALIST CASE:
  • WTO dramatically reduced global tariffs since 1947
    (average tariffs: ~40% in 1947 → ~5% today)
  • DSM resolved hundreds of trade disputes
  • IMF surveillance: prevented some crises; managed others
  • UN peacekeeping: 70+ operations; reduced civil war
    duration in some cases

  REALIST CRITIQUE:
  • Institutions work when great powers want them to;
    they break when great powers don't
  • US blocked Appellate Body: WTO dispute settlement
    effectively paralyzed
  • Russia vetoes any SC action against itself
  • China ignores UNCLOS rulings in South China Sea
  → Institutions are epiphenomenal; power determines outcomes

  SYNTHESIS:
  Both are right about different situations.
  Institutions are most effective:
  • In economic issue areas (trade, finance) where
    all parties benefit from rules
  • Among states with broadly shared interests
  • When enforcement is less relevant (most follow
    rules because they want to)
  Institutions are weakest:
  • When great powers are in direct conflict
  • In security issues where sovereignty is directly
    contested
  • When a great power decides to exit
    (US from Paris Agreement, UNESCO, JCPOA)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why does the UNSC have veto power? | Design choice: great powers won't accept binding decisions against their interests; veto = great power buy-in. Consequence: gridlock when P5 interests conflict |
| What is MFN principle? | Any trade advantage given to one WTO member must be extended to all; prevents bilateral trade discrimination |
| Why is the WTO DSM in crisis? | US blocked Appellate Body appointments (2017-); argued AB was legislating through dispute settlement; AB now below quorum |
| What is IMF conditionality and its critique? | Loans require policy conditions (austerity, liberalization, privatization); critics: one-size-fits-all, deepens recessions, serves Washington Consensus ideology |
| Why can't the ICC arrest Putin? | No police force; depends on state cooperation; Russia not a party; ICC party states have legal obligation but political reluctance to arrest |
| Do institutions matter? | Yes for economic cooperation; less so for security when great powers are directly involved |

## Common Confusion Points

**UN GA resolutions are not binding**: Only UNSC resolutions under Chapter VII of the UN Charter are binding on all member states. GA resolutions are recommendations. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights is a GA resolution — morally significant, legally not binding as such (though its principles have entered customary international law).

**The WTO is not "free trade"**: The WTO is a managed trade system — it sets rules for how trade can be restricted (permissible tariffs, safeguard clauses, anti-dumping rules, exceptions for health/security) rather than eliminating all barriers. "WTO-compliant" means within the rules; not barrier-free.

**IMF ≠ World Bank**: IMF: macroeconomic stability, balance of payments financing, crisis lending, short-term. World Bank: long-term development lending, infrastructure, poverty reduction. IMF is the ER; World Bank is development investment. Both funded by member state contributions weighted by GDP.

**"UN voted to condemn Russia" is not actionable**: GA votes on Ukraine (condemning Russia's invasion) were important for diplomatic signaling and norm expression. They have no legal force. Russia remains on the Security Council with veto power. The gap between legal authority and political reality is a permanent feature of the UN system.
