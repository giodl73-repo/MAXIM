# Constructivism: Wendt's Social Theory of IR

## The Big Picture

```
CONSTRUCTIVISM — CORE CLAIMS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  AGAINST BOTH REALISM AND LIBERALISM:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  REALISM AND LIBERALISM SHARE:                         │
  │  • States have fixed interests (security/power OR      │
  │    prosperity/peace)                                    │
  │  • Anarchy has a given structure                        │
  │  • Material factors (capabilities, economic power)      │
  │    are primary                                          │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CONSTRUCTIVISM SAYS:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  • Identities and interests are CONSTRUCTED through     │
  │    social interaction, not given by nature or structure │
  │  • Anarchy is what STATES MAKE OF IT                   │
  │  • Ideas, norms, and culture matter materially          │
  │  • The same material distribution of power means        │
  │    different things to differently constituted actors   │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  EXAMPLE:
  500 British nuclear weapons vs. 5 North Korean ones.
  Which is more threatening to the US?
  Material: NK has fewer.
  Constructivist: NK is an adversary; UK is an ally.
  The interpretation of the material fact requires
  understanding the social relationship.
  (Wendt's own example)
```

## Wendt: Anarchy Is What States Make of It

Alexander Wendt ("Anarchy Is What States Make of It," *International Organization*, 1992; *Social Theory of International Politics*, 1999) is the central constructivist text.

### The Three Anarchies (Cultures of Anarchy)

Wendt argues that anarchy — the absence of a central authority — does not have a single logical or structural implication. Different "cultures of anarchy" can exist:

```
WENDT'S THREE ANARCHIC CULTURES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  HOBBESIAN ANARCHY:          LOCKEAN ANARCHY:         KANTIAN ANARCHY:
  "Each against all"          "Live and let live"      "All for one"

  States treat others         States treat others      States treat others
  as enemies:                 as rivals:               as friends:
  • No territorial limits     • Accept others'         • Collective security
    on violence                 sovereignty            • Peaceful dispute
  • Anything goes in          • Don't exterminate        resolution
    pursuit of security         rivals                 • Help defend allies

  Pre-Westphalian era;        The current              Emerging in zones
  war of all against all;     international system     of democratic peace
  survival the only norm      for most states          (EU, NATO area)

  NOT INEVITABLE:
  The culture is reproduced by states' practices.
  If states begin treating each other differently,
  the culture can change.
```

### Identities and Interests

Wendt's key insight: interests are not prior to interaction. States don't know what they want until they interact with others and develop an identity.

A state's identity (who it is) shapes its interests (what it wants). Identities are formed through:
- Internal processes (domestic culture, history, political system)
- Intersubjective interaction with other states

**Socialization**: States learn what role to play through repeated interaction. A new state entering the international system is socialized into existing norms.

```
WENDT'S SOCIAL CONSTRUCTION OF INTERESTS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  REALISM: Structure (anarchy + capabilities) →
  State behavior (power seeking)

  WENDT: Structure is constituted by agents through
  practice; identities are formed through interaction;
  interests derive from identities.

  Process:
  Interaction → Identity construction → Interest formation
       ↑                                         │
       └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
               (reciprocal; not linear)

  EXAMPLE: US-Soviet relations
  "Enemy" identity: states treat each other as enemies
  → military buildups → threat perception amplified
  → self-fulfilling prophecy

  Gorbachev's "new thinking": changed Soviet identity
  (not an empire maintaining power by force but a
  great power with shared interests in stability)
  → US gradually updated its threat perception
  → Changed identity → Changed interests → Cold War ended

  This is not possible in pure structural realist theory.
  Structural realism can't explain how the Cold War ended
  through an identity change rather than a power shift.
```

## Norm Life Cycle: Finnemore and Sikkink

Martha Finnemore and Kathryn Sikkink ("International Norm Dynamics and Political Change," *IO*, 1998) developed the norm lifecycle model:

```
NORM LIFECYCLE — THREE STAGES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STAGE 1: NORM EMERGENCE
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Norm entrepreneurs + organizational platforms           │
  │                                                          │
  │ Norm entrepreneurs: individuals or organizations        │
  │ with strong notions of appropriate behavior who        │
  │ seek to change existing standards.                      │
  │ Examples: Henri Dunant (Red Cross/Geneva Convention),   │
  │ Eleanor Roosevelt (UDHR), anti-slavery campaigners.     │
  │                                                          │
  │ They need platforms: international organizations,       │
  │ treaty bodies, states, media.                           │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
             │
             ▼  Tipping point: critical mass of states adopts

  STAGE 2: NORM CASCADE
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Rapid spread of norm adoption                            │
  │                                                          │
  │ Mechanism: socialization, imitation, legitimation       │
  │ States adopt norms to:                                   │
  │ • Maintain legitimacy and status in the community        │
  │ • Avoid naming-and-shaming by norm entrepreneurs        │
  │ • Sincere belief in the norm (internalization process)  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
             │
             ▼

  STAGE 3: INTERNALIZATION
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Norm becomes taken-for-granted; no longer contested     │
  │                                                          │
  │ Examples of internalized norms (post-cascade):          │
  │ • Sovereignty (don't invade other states arbitrarily)   │
  │ • No slavery                                            │
  │ • No chemical/biological weapons use (mostly)           │
  │ • Non-use of nuclear weapons since 1945                  │
  │   (the "nuclear taboo" — Tannenwald)                    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  TIPPING POINT (Norm threshold):
  When approximately 1/3 of states (Finnemore-Sikkink
  estimate) adopt a norm, it typically cascades.
  Not a precise number — depends on which states adopt
  (great powers' adoption accelerates cascade).
```

### Examples of Norm Diffusion

- **Abolition of slavery**: British abolitionism → pressure on trading partners → became international norm (late 19th century). Economic interests ran contrary to the norm; the norm succeeded anyway.
- **Women's suffrage**: Began in New Zealand (1893); cascaded through Western democracies; now essentially universal norm (even where violated in practice).
- **Anti-torture norm**: The Convention Against Torture (1984) emerged from torture survivors and human rights NGOs; cascaded through state ratifications; US ratified (but waterboarding controversy shows internalization is incomplete).
- **R2P (Responsibility to Protect)**: Adopted at World Summit 2005; Libya intervention (2011) attempted invocation; still contested.

## Securitization Theory (Copenhagen School)

Barry Buzan, Ole Wæver, and Jaap de Wilde (*Security: A New Framework for Analysis*, 1998) developed securitization theory:

**Core claim**: Security is not an objective condition but a speech act. When a state actor successfully labels something a "security threat," it moves that issue from the normal political sphere into an exceptional sphere where normal rules don't apply.

```
SECURITIZATION — THE PROCESS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SECURITIZING ACTOR:
  (typically state official, military, media)
         │
         ▼
  SECURITIZING MOVE:
  "X is an existential threat to Y.
   Therefore we need emergency measures."
  (Presentation of an issue as security threat)
         │
         ▼
  AUDIENCE ACCEPTANCE:
  The securitization only works if the audience
  accepts the framing. Audiences: parliament,
  public, media, international community.
         │
         ▼
  EXCEPTIONAL MEASURES:
  Once securitized, normal democratic/legal
  constraints can be suspended.
  "Emergency powers," "war on X" rhetoric.

  EXAMPLES:
  • 9/11: Terrorism securitized → "War on Terror" →
    suspension of normal surveillance law (PATRIOT Act),
    detention without trial (Guantanamo)
  • Climate change: some actors try to securitize
    ("existential threat to civilization") →
    debate about whether this helps (emergency
    measures) or hurts (removes from democratic deliberation)
  • Immigration: securitization of migration →
    militarization of borders, emergency detention

  DE-SECURITIZATION:
  Moving an issue back into the normal political sphere.
  The Copenhagen School argues this is often desirable:
  democratic deliberation is better than emergency politics.
```

## Postcolonial IR

Mainstream IR theory (realism, liberalism, constructivism) was developed by Euro-American scholars and reflects Euro-American assumptions. Postcolonial IR critiques this:

- Jill Tickner, Arlene Tickner, Pinar Bilgin: IR theory assumes the state as natural unit, ignores how colonial history created particular types of states
- Amitav Acharya (*The End of American World Order*, 2014): "Global IR" — multiple world orders, not a single liberal order. ASEAN, African Union, etc. have their own normative frameworks.
- The "standard of civilization" concept (Gong): 19th-century IR included explicit criteria for which polities qualified as "civilized" and thus entitled to sovereignty. Non-Western states were "tutored" by colonial powers before being admitted to the club. This history shapes present-day asymmetries.

```
POSTCOLONIAL IR — KEY CHALLENGES TO MAINSTREAM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  TO REALISM:
  Security for whom? Colonial states were built
  to extract, not protect; post-colonial states
  have different security logics than Westphalian
  European states.

  TO LIBERAL INSTITUTIONALISM:
  IMF/World Bank conditionality reproduced colonial
  extraction under different institutional form.
  Democratic peace: democracy was for European
  populations; colonial populations were excluded.

  TO CONSTRUCTIVISM:
  Whose norms cascade? The norm lifecycle is
  dominated by Western norm entrepreneurs.
  Indigenous and non-Western normative frameworks
  are systematically marginalized in international
  institutions.

  CONTRIBUTION:
  Historical depth: IR theory abstracts from history;
  postcolonial IR insists the history matters.
  Present-day power asymmetries are products of
  specific historical processes, not natural givens.
```

## Feminist IR

Cynthia Enloe (*Bananas, Beaches and Bases*, 1990), J. Ann Tickner (*Gender in International Relations*, 1992), Laura Sjoberg:

**Core claims**:
- IR's core categories (state, national interest, security, diplomacy) are gendered — they reflect masculine assumptions and experiences
- Women are not absent from international politics; they are systematically made invisible
- Security as conventionally understood (military, state-centric) ignores human security (poverty, violence against women, reproductive rights)

```
FEMINIST IR — KEY CONTRIBUTIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  EMPIRICAL:
  Where are the women? (Enloe)
  • Camp followers, military wives, sex workers near
    bases are part of international relations
  • Domestic workers in diplomatic households
  • Female combatants in insurgencies
  • Women in development organizations
  All invisible to state-centric IR.

  THEORETICAL:
  Morgenthau's "statesman" is gendered:
  • Self-interest, autonomy, rationality = masculine
  • Relationship, care, context = feminine
  Feminist IR: human security includes
  freedom from domestic violence, reproductive
  autonomy, economic independence.

  NORMATIVE:
  Women's participation in peace negotiations:
  UN Security Council Resolution 1325 (2000):
  Women, Peace and Security agenda.
  Empirical finding: peace agreements including women
  are more durable (Inclusive Security research).
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Constructivist answer |
|----------|-----------------------|
| Why is "anarchy" compatible with cooperation? | Anarchy is what states make of it — Kantian, Lockean, or Hobbesian cultures are all possible; the culture is reproduced by practice and can change |
| How do international norms spread? | Norm lifecycle: entrepreneur → organizational platform → tipping point → cascade → internalization |
| Why did the Cold War end? | Identity change: Gorbachev's "new thinking" changed Soviet identity → changed interests → changed behavior; structural realism can't explain this |
| What is securitization? | A speech act that moves an issue from normal politics to exceptional emergency sphere; audience must accept the framing; can suspend normal democratic constraints |
| What does postcolonial IR add? | Historical grounding for present-day asymmetries; critique of Western norm dominance; multiple world orders, not one liberal order |
| What does feminist IR add? | Gendered assumptions in IR theory; making women visible; human security vs. state-centric security |

## Common Confusion Points

**Constructivism doesn't deny material reality**: Wendt explicitly says material factors matter — but they don't have given meanings. The US and North Korea both have nuclear weapons; the material fact is real. What the material fact *means* for US foreign policy depends on the social relationship. Constructivism adds interpretation to material capabilities; it doesn't replace them.

**Norm internalization doesn't mean perfect compliance**: The nuclear taboo (no use since 1945) is a strong internalized norm. But Russia's occasional nuclear threats in Ukraine 2022 show even strong norms are under stress. Internalization means the norm is taken-for-granted, not that it's never violated.

**Wendt's constructivism is "systemic"**: Wendt deliberately stayed at the systemic level (like Waltz) to engage Waltz on his own ground. Other constructivists (Finnemore, Sikkink, Katzenstein) work more at the unit level (domestic politics, organizational culture). They're all "constructivist" but Wendt's specific claims are about the system level.

**Securitization is not just "crying wolf"**: The Copenhagen School's concept has a specific meaning — a speech act that successfully moves an issue into an exceptional sphere. Failed securitizing moves (someone declares climate change a military emergency and no one accepts it) are just rhetoric. Successful securitization has institutional consequences: emergency laws, military deployments, suspension of normal oversight.
