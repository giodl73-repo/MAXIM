## Graham Allison: Essence of Decision

Graham Allison (*Essence of Decision: Explaining the Cuban Missile Crisis*, 1971; revised with Zelikow 1999) applied three analytical lenses to the Cuban Missile Crisis.

### Model I: Rational Actor

The standard realist model: treat the state as a unitary rational actor with clear preferences and a means-ends calculation.

**Cuban Missile Crisis application**:
- USSR wanted to shift the strategic balance (place missiles in Cuba → close the missile gap; intimidate US over Berlin)
- US wants to prevent a strategic threat and cannot tolerate missiles in Western Hemisphere
- Rational actors: each side calculates costs and benefits; Kennedy imposes blockade (less escalatory than air strike); Khrushchev accepts removal in exchange for no-invasion pledge + secret Jupiter removal

Model I answer: rational bargaining produced an outcome acceptable to both parties.

```
MODEL I STRENGTHS AND LIMITS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STRENGTHS:
  • Parsimonious: explains many outcomes
  • Predictions: if you know preferences and constraints,
    can predict choices
  • Useful for: crisis bargaining, deterrence theory,
    trade negotiations

  LIMITS:
  • Who "is" the state? Which preferences?
  • Real decision-making rarely as coherent as model implies
  • Ignores how bureaucratic politics shapes options
  • Ignores how organizations generate options
  • Decision-makers often make choices that are locally
    rational but globally suboptimal (Janis: groupthink)
```

### Model II: Organizational Process

States are not unitary actors — they are collections of organizations with standard operating procedures (SOPs), pre-programmed responses, and limited attention.

**Cuban Missile Crisis application**:
- Why did the Soviets deploy missiles so obviously (on open-deck ships, in a recognizable pattern)?
  - Soviet missile deployment units had SOPs developed for deployment in USSR and Warsaw Pact
  - They used the same procedures; camouflage not in the SOP
  - Khrushchev assumed US wouldn't notice before deployment complete
- Why did Kennedy get a naval blockade option rather than just air strike?
  - Navy had pre-programmed blockade procedures ready
  - Air Force's air strike options required 72 hours minimum
  - Options presented to ExComm were shaped by what organizations had ready

```
MODEL II — KEY INSIGHT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "Where you stand depends on where you sit."
  (Allison's Bureaucratic Politics maxim)

  Organizations have interests:
  • Navy wants to demonstrate independent value
  • Air Force wants to conduct the air strike
  • CIA wants intelligence operations
  • State wants diplomacy
  Each organization advocates for options that involve it.

  SOPs limit options:
  The "outputs" of organizations (options presented to
  leaders) are constrained by what organizations have
  programmed themselves to produce.
  Leaders choose among options they're given;
  options are shaped by organizational capabilities.

  LESSON FOR MODERN ORGANIZATIONS:
  This is directly applicable to large technology
  organizations. What options Microsoft presents to
  leadership are shaped by what business units have
  developed, what the engineering culture can execute,
  what the compliance organization will approve.
  The learner's VP experience confirms this daily.
```

### Model III: Bureaucratic Politics

"Politics, not policy" — outcomes reflect the pulling and hauling among competing bureaucratic actors with different interests, not rational calculation or organizational process.

**Cuban Missile Crisis application**:
- Why did Kennedy choose blockade over air strike?
  - Not just strategic calculation but internal politics:
  - Robert Kennedy argued air strike was a "Pearl Harbor in reverse"
  - Admiral Anderson (CNO) wanted to run blockade without civilian interference
  - McNamara (SecDef) fought for civilian control of naval operations
  - Dean Rusk, McGeorge Bundy, Robert McNamara each had different preferences
  - The blockade outcome reflected which coalition won the ExComm argument

## Prospect Theory and Loss Aversion in Foreign Policy

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's prospect theory applied to IR:

```
PROSPECT THEORY IN FOREIGN POLICY
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STANDARD RATIONAL CHOICE:
  States should be risk-neutral over equivalent gains
  and losses (a 50% chance of gaining $100 = 50%
  chance of losing $100 in expected value).

  PROSPECT THEORY FINDING:
  Losses loom larger than equivalent gains.
  Loss aversion coefficient: ~2x.
  People (and states) are risk-accepting to avoid
  losses and risk-averse to secure gains.

  FOREIGN POLICY IMPLICATION:
  States in the "domain of losses" (perceiving their
  current position as a loss from a reference point)
  take bigger risks than states in the "domain of gains."

  EXAMPLES:
  • Hitler's decision to attack USSR (1941):
    German military in domain of gains (winning)
    should have been more risk-averse;
    instead, maximalist risky strategy.
    Jervis/Levy: leaders framing their position as
    "losing" take extreme risks.

  • Japan's Pearl Harbor decision:
    US oil embargo cut off 80% of Japan's oil supply.
    Japan perceived this as catastrophic loss.
    Pearl Harbor was a high-risk/high-uncertainty gamble
    from a domain of losses perspective.

  • Israeli preventive war logic:
    Iran's nuclear program perceived as existential
    loss in prospect theory terms → drives willingness
    to take risks (military strike) that expected
    value calculations might not support.

  THE REFERENCE POINT PROBLEM:
  What counts as a "loss"?
  Reference point depends on aspiration levels,
  prior positions, domestic framing.
  Leaders actively manage reference points
  (framing potential actions as avoiding losses
   vs. achieving gains to affect risk tolerance).
```

## Groupthink (Janis)

Irving Janis (*Groupthink: Psychological Studies of Policy Decisions and Fiascoes*, 1972) studied catastrophic foreign policy failures:

```
GROUPTHINK SYMPTOMS AND HISTORIC CASES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  GROUPTHINK SYMPTOMS:
  1. Illusion of invulnerability (overoptimism)
  2. Collective rationalization (ignore warning signs)
  3. Belief in group's moral superiority
  4. Stereotyped views of adversary (weak, stupid)
  5. Self-censorship (members don't voice doubts)
  6. Illusion of unanimity (silence = consent)
  7. Direct pressure on dissenters
  8. Self-appointed "mindguards" (filter info to leader)

  CASES:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ BAY OF PIGS (1961):                                     │
  │ Kennedy advisors didn't question flawed CIA plan.       │
  │ Arthur Schlesinger later wrote he suppressed doubts.    │
  │ Group pressure silenced dissent.                        │
  │ Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, moral            │
  │ certainty about anti-Castro goals, stereotyping         │
  │ Cuban military, self-censorship.                        │
  │                                                         │
  │ PEARL HARBOR (1941):                                    │
  │ US commanders not psychologically prepared for          │
  │ Japanese attack; dismissed warning signs.               │
  │ Stereotyping: Japan wouldn't dare attack US directly.   │
  │                                                         │
  │ IRAQ WMD ASSESSMENT (2002-03):                         │
  │ Intelligence analysts under pressure to find WMD;      │
  │ dissenting assessments filtered;                        │
  │ Bush administration's certainty reinforced groupthink   │
  │ in both intelligence community and cabinet.             │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ANTIDOTES (Janis):
  • Devil's advocate role (formalized dissent)
  • Second-chance meeting (explicit reconsideration)
  • Bring in outside experts
  • Leader doesn't express preference early
  • Red team (challenge assumptions explicitly)

  ORGANIZATIONAL PARALLEL:
  Groupthink is endemic in large organizations generally.
  Microsoft product decisions, VSTS prioritization, Azure
  roadmap — all subject to the same dynamics.
  The antidotes are well-known; implementing them
  requires active design against natural social pressure.
```

## Two-Level Games (Putnam)

Robert Putnam ("Diplomacy and Domestic Politics: The Logic of Two-Level Games," *IO*, 1988):

**International negotiations are simultaneously games at two levels**:

```
TWO-LEVEL GAME STRUCTURE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LEVEL I (International): Negotiation between governments
  LEVEL II (Domestic): Ratification by domestic constituencies

  WIN SET: The set of agreements at Level I that would be
  ratified at Level II.

  Small win set → strong bargaining position internationally.
  "My legislature won't accept X" = genuine constraint
  that makes the negotiator's position credible.

  Large win set → weak bargaining position.
  "My parliament will accept almost anything" →
  adversary knows they can push harder.

  EXAMPLES:
  Trade negotiations:
  US Trade Representative's win set defined by
  Congressional ratification + industry lobbying.
  Congress won't ratify deals harmful to
  steel industry → gives steel industry veto
  over trade deals + weakens US negotiating position.

  Climate negotiations:
  Obama at Paris 2015: executive agreement (not treaty)
  to avoid Senate ratification.
  Win set: what could be done without Senate.
  Paris Agreement's NDC structure partly designed to
  avoid treaty ratification requirements.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Leaders sometimes prefer small win sets (limits
  what they can concede).
  Leaders sometimes prefer large win sets (enables
  reaching agreement).
  Domestic politics is not separate from international
  negotiations; it is the material from which
  international negotiations are made.
```

## Leader Psychology

Individual leadership matters in foreign policy analysis. Key approaches:

```
LEADER PSYCHOLOGY FRAMEWORKS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  OPERATIONAL CODE (Nathan Leites, Alexander George):
  Leaders have stable beliefs about:
  • The nature of the political universe (zero-sum?)
  • Optimal strategies for pursuing goals (accommodation
    vs. conflict; unilateral vs. multilateral)
  These beliefs, derived from formative experiences,
  shape how leaders interpret information and make choices.

  Analysis: Code operational codes from public speeches,
  writings, actions. Used to predict how leaders will
  respond to situations.

  Stalin's operational code: fundamental conflict
  between capitalism and communism; tactical cooperation
  possible; strategic competition permanent.
  → Explains post-WWII behavior better than material
    power analysis alone.

  COGNITIVE STYLES:
  Are leaders "detail-oriented" (process enormous amounts
  of information) or "big picture" (delegate)?
  Do they seek to process contradictory information
  or reduce cognitive dissonance?
  Nixon: extensive written analysis; strong conceptual
  framework; could hold contradictions.
  Kennedy: oral briefer; tested advisors against each other.
  Bush 43: preferred simplified briefings;
  reduced nuance in decision process.

  EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE:
  Self-awareness, empathy, self-regulation in high-stakes
  decision-making.
  Research: leaders with higher emotional intelligence
  more effective in international negotiations.
  Churchill: high charismatic intelligence; volatile
  emotional regulation (depressive episodes; drinking).
```

## Coercive Diplomacy and Signaling Theory

```
COERCIVE DIPLOMACY — THEORY AND APPLICATION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  COERCIVE DIPLOMACY (Alexander George):
  Using threats (or limited force) to make an
  adversary comply without having to fight a full war.
  "Compellence" (Schelling): forcing adversary to
  change behavior they've already started.
  "Deterrence": preventing adversary from starting
  an action.

  REQUIREMENTS FOR SUCCESS:
  1. CREDIBILITY: Adversary believes threat will be executed
  2. ADEQUACY: Threat large enough to overcome adversary's
     resistance
  3. CLARITY: Adversary understands what behavior is required
  4. OPPORTUNITY: Adversary can comply (can't threaten
     someone to do the impossible)
  5. ASYMMETRY: Issue matters more to you than adversary

  SIGNALING THEORY:
  Costly signals: signals that are expensive to send
  credibly demonstrate commitment (mobilizing troops,
  moving naval assets, economic sanctions).
  Cheap talk: signals that are free to send are less credible
  (diplomatic statements alone).

  KEY: Observable actions that are costly to fake
  communicate resolve more effectively than verbal
  threats.

  US-CHINA TAIWAN EXAMPLE:
  US "strategic ambiguity":
  Won't say yes or no to defending Taiwan.
  Prevents Taiwan independence (US won't support) and
  Chinese invasion (US might oppose).
  China: how much of this is credible vs. cheap talk?
  Post-2022 (Ukraine): China recalculating after Russia
  invasion → US resolve question intensified.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Framework | Question answered | Best use |
|-----------|------------------|---------|
| Model I (Rational Actor) | What policy would a rational unitary state choose? | Strategic forecasting; arms control; crisis bargaining |
| Model II (Organizational Process) | Why did the policy come out THIS way, not the theoretically optimal way? | Understanding implementation failures; institutional path dependence |
| Model III (Bureaucratic Politics) | Who won the internal debate and why? | Understanding specific policy choices where bureaucratic interests matter |
| Prospect Theory | Why did the state take such a high risk? | Explaining seemingly irrational risk-taking in losing positions |
| Groupthink | Why did the group fail to consider obvious alternatives? | Explaining catastrophic decision failures |
| Two-Level Games | What constrains the negotiator's agreement space? | Trade, arms control, climate negotiations |
| Coercive Diplomacy | Will a threat/limited force change the adversary's behavior? | Crisis management; sanctions design |

## Common Confusion Points

**Model I is not wrong — it's incomplete**: The rational actor model is the baseline. Most foreign policy analysis starts there. Models II and III explain deviations from the Model I prediction. A good analysis uses all three — they're additive, not mutually exclusive.

**Groupthink ≠ consensus**: Groupthink is social pressure suppressing dissent. Genuine consensus (everyone really agrees after vigorous debate) is fine. The problem is when dissent is self-censored before the debate, producing false consensus. Janis's "devil's advocate" antidote works precisely because it institutionalizes challenge rather than relying on members to volunteer it.

**Prospect theory's reference point is not the current state**: The key variable in prospect theory foreign policy applications is the reference point — what counts as "breaking even." This is often the status quo, but it can be an aspirational state ("we're losing ground from where we should be"), a historical baseline ("we deserve our sphere of influence"), or a threat comparison ("this would be catastrophic"). Different reference points produce different risk calculations from the same objective position.

**Two-level games can be manipulative**: Negotiators sometimes use their domestic constraints strategically — citing legislative limits that they actually have some ability to change or influence. The US Trade Representative's invocation of Congressional opposition is sometimes genuine and sometimes a negotiating tactic. The other party can't always distinguish.
