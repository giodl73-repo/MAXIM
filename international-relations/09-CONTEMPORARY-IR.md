### Taiwan Strait

```
TAIWAN STRAIT — THE FLASHPOINT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  THE LEGAL-POLITICAL AMBIGUITY:
  1972 Shanghai Communiqué: US "acknowledges that all Chinese
  on either side of the Taiwan Strait maintain there is but
  one China and that Taiwan is a part of China."
  ("Acknowledges" was deliberately ambiguous)

  1979 Taiwan Relations Act (TRA):
  US will provide Taiwan defensive weapons;
  will maintain capacity to "resist any resort to force or
  other forms of coercion" affecting Taiwan's security.
  Does NOT commit US to military defense.

  STRATEGIC AMBIGUITY:
  US has never said yes or no to defending Taiwan.
  Purpose: prevents Taiwan independence (US won't support it)
  AND prevents Chinese invasion (US might respond).
  Biden broke with ambiguity multiple times (2021-23) stating
  US would defend Taiwan militarily; White House walked back
  each statement.

  MILITARY BALANCE SHIFT:
  1996 Taiwan Strait Crisis: US sent two carrier groups;
  China had no counter. Clear US military superiority.
  2024: A2/AD means US cannot operate freely within
  ~1,000km of Chinese coast without taking severe losses.
  RAND assessment: US faces serious challenges defending Taiwan
  against sustained Chinese operation.

  CHINA'S CALCULUS:
  Xi's goal: reunification in his era; explicitly stated.
  Military option: costs and risks very high (even for China).
  Gray zone: military exercises, disinformation, economic pressure,
  political interference — preferred current approach.
  Taiwan's position: de facto independent; does not declare
  independence (trigger for war); maintains ambiguity.

  WHY IT MATTERS TO TECH:
  TSMC produces ~90% of world's most advanced chips in Taiwan.
  Military conflict = destruction/interdiction of global chip supply.
  This is why US is pursuing CHIPS Act (domestic fabs),
  TSMC Arizona fab ($65B commitment), and Japan/Netherlands fabs.
  The semiconductor supply chain vulnerability is a strategic
  liability driving policy — not just economic calculation.
```

### South China Sea

```
SOUTH CHINA SEA — LEGAL AND MILITARY DIMENSIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NINE-DASH LINE:
  China claims ~90% of South China Sea within "nine-dash line."
  Basis: historical claims; no clear legal foundation under UNCLOS.
  UNCLOS (which China has ratified): EEZ = 200 nautical miles
  from coast. Most of South China Sea exceeds this from any coast.

  PHILIPPINES vs. CHINA (2016):
  International arbitration under UNCLOS Annex VII.
  Tribunal: China's nine-dash line has no legal basis under UNCLOS.
  China rejected tribunal's jurisdiction and ignored ruling.
  US, EU, Japan welcomed ruling; China called it "null and void."
  → Illustrates enforcement gap in international law

  ISLAND-BUILDING CAMPAIGN:
  2014-2016: China dredged and built artificial islands on
  submerged reefs (Spratly Islands) — created ~3,200 acres of land.
  Installed: military airstrips, hangars, radar, missile systems.
  Under UNCLOS: artificial islands on submerged features cannot
  generate territorial waters or EEZ.
  Fait accompli: islands are now built; military capability installed.

  FREEDOM OF NAVIGATION OPERATIONS (FONOPs):
  US Navy regularly transits through South China Sea near
  China's claimed territorial waters.
  Purpose: refuse to recognize Chinese territorial claims through
  non-recognition and practice.
  China protests each FONOP; no military confrontation to date.

  ASEAN DIVISION:
  Philippines, Vietnam oppose Chinese claims most directly.
  Cambodia, Laos effectively Chinese proxies in ASEAN.
  → ASEAN cannot produce unified position.
  → Bilateral deals between China and individual ASEAN members.
```

### European Security Implications

```
EUROPEAN SECURITY — POST-2022 TRANSFORMATION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NATO ENLARGEMENT:
  • Finland: applied May 2022; joined April 2023
    (ended 75 years of non-alignment)
  • Sweden: applied May 2022; joined March 2024
    (ended 200 years of non-alignment)
  • NATO now borders Russia with ~1,300km new frontier (Finland)

  DEFENSE SPENDING:
  NATO 2% of GDP commitment: most members below this pre-2022.
  Germany: reversed Zeitenwende ("turning point"); announced
  €100 billion special defense fund; committed to 2% goal.
  Poland: 4% of GDP (2024); major weapons procurement.
  The free-rider problem dramatically reduced (temporarily).

  EU DEFENSE INTEGRATION:
  EU has no Article 5 equivalent but:
  • European Peace Facility: off-budget EU arms transfers to Ukraine
    (~€12 billion through 2024)
  • PESCO: Permanent Structured Cooperation defense projects
  • EU Defence Industrial Strategy (2024): coordinated procurement
  Post-Ukraine: pressure for genuine EU strategic autonomy
  (Macron's long-stated goal) has more momentum.

  RUSSIA'S STRATEGIC POSITION:
  Western assessment: Russian conventional military proved weaker
  than pre-war estimates; severe equipment losses; manpower costs high.
  But: Russia is reconstituting; defense industry on war footing;
  North Korean ammunition and Iranian drones filling gaps;
  nuclear deterrence continues to constrain Western response.
  Long-war scenario: Russia's willingness to sustain attrition
  vs. Western willingness to sustain Ukraine support.

  LESSONS FOR IR THEORY:
  Liberal institutionalism: UNSC paralyzed (Russia vetoed);
  but G7, NATO, EU demonstrated coordination capacity.
  Constructivism: Norms against territorial conquest matter
  (GA resolutions condemned Russia 141-5, 7, 35 abstentions);
  but didn't stop the war.
  Realism: confirmed that nuclear deterrence constrains responses;
  sovereignty norms insufficient against determined great power.
```

```
  │                                                          │

  │ • Hambantota: Sri Lanka's debt crisis was from           │
  │   international bond markets, not primarily China.       │
  │ • Most BRI loans are commercial, not strategic.          │
  │ • China has renegotiated more than seized assets.        │
  │ • Many BRI projects are overpriced and poor quality.     │
  │   The problem is economic waste, not strategic trap.     │
  │                                                          │
  │ NUANCED ASSESSMENT:                                      │
  │ BRI is significant infrastructure finance where World    │

  African Union added 2023 (54 countries, 1 seat).

  • R2P as regime change cover (Libya outcome)
  These aren't just theory — they are positions at actual negotiations.
```

## The Polycrisis Concept

```
POLYCRISIS — COMPOUNDING SYSTEMIC RISKS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ORIGIN:
  Edgar Morin (1970s French sociologist): complex systems
  Adam Tooze (historian) popularized: "polycrisis" as
  simultaneous, interacting crises that compound each other
  in ways that exceed the sum of their parts.

  WHY "POLYCRISIS" IS ANALYTICALLY USEFUL:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ TRADITIONAL IR FRAMEWORK: handle crises one at a time    │
  │ • Great power competition (one analysis)                 │
  │ • Climate change (another analysis)                      │
  │ • Pandemic governance (another)                          │
  │ • Democratic backsliding (another)                       │
  │                                                          │
  │ POLYCRISIS INSIGHT: these interact                       │
  │ • COVID → supply chain disruption → inflation →          │
  │   political populism → democratic backsliding            │
  │ • Climate → food insecurity → migration →                │
  │   right-wing politics → Paris Agreement defection        │
  │ • Ukraine war → energy crisis → industrial recession →   │
  │   reduced climate investment                             │
  │ • AI disruption → labor dislocation → political anger  │
```

## Common Confusion Points

**US-China competition is not Cold War 2.0**: The Cold War involved limited economic interdependence, clear ideological blocs, and nuclear bipolarity. US-China competition involves deep economic interdependence, no clear bloc alignment (most states hedge), and a more complex multipolar military environment. The analogy misleads more than it clarifies.

**Taiwan's legal status is genuinely ambiguous**: The US does not officially recognize Taiwan as a sovereign state. The US acknowledges (but doesn't endorse) the Chinese position on "one China." Taiwan's government (Republic of China) constitutionally still claims to be the legitimate government of all China — though this is increasingly a legal fiction. The ambiguity is deliberately maintained by all parties.

**Sanctions are not just punitive — they have distributive effects**: Secondary sanctions (targeting third-country entities that trade with the sanctioned state) are contentious internationally. US OFAC sanctions have extraterritorial reach because dollar transactions clear through US correspondent banks. India buying Russian oil and trading in rupees/roubles is partly an attempt to avoid dollar-denominated exposure. The dollar's role as reserve currency is a source of US sanctions power — and a reason why BRICS+ members discuss alternatives.

**The "rules-based international order" is contested as a phrase**: Western states use "rules-based order" to mean the post-1945 multilateral system (UN Charter, WTO, human rights conventions). China and Russia argue this phrase is a Western substitute for actual international law: they prefer "international law" and "UN-centered multilateralism," arguing the "rules-based order" is US hegemony dressed as universal principles. The phrase choice reveals the underlying normative contest.

**Green industrial policy creates alliance tensions**: The US Inflation Reduction Act's domestic content requirements for EV/battery subsidies violate WTO non-discrimination principles and put US allies (EU, South Korea, Japan) at competitive disadvantage. Climate policy and trade law are increasingly in conflict; the WTO dispute settlement paralysis (Appellate Body crisis) means no resolution mechanism is functioning.

**AI in military applications is not science fiction**: Semi-autonomous systems are already deployed (CIWS, Iron Dome, loitering munitions). The debate is not about whether autonomous systems exist but about the conditions under which lethal decision-making can be fully delegated. The accountability gap is legal, not technical: military commanders remain responsible under international humanitarian law for effects of systems they deploy — how this applies to a fully autonomous system with unforeseeable behavior is unresolved.
