# Power Transition Theory and Hegemonic Wars

## The Big Picture

```
POWER TRANSITION THEORY — CORE LOGIC
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STATIC WORLD (stable):         DYNAMIC WORLD (dangerous):
  ┌────────────────────┐         ┌──────────────────────────┐
  │  DOMINANT POWER    │         │   DOMINANT POWER         │
  │  (satisfied, sets  │         │   (declining)            │
  │   the rules)       │         │   ↑                      │
  │                    │         │   POWER GAP CLOSING      │
  │  GAP               │   →     │   ↓                      │
  │  (challenger far   │         │   CHALLENGER             │
  │   behind; costly   │         │   (rising; dissatisfied; │
  │   to challenge)    │         │   approaching parity)    │
  │                    │         │                          │
  │  CHALLENGER        │         │   DANGER ZONE:           │
  │  (rises gradually) │         │   When challenger ~80%   │
  │                    │         │   of dominant power →    │
  │                    │         │   war most likely        │
  └────────────────────┘         └──────────────────────────┘

  KEY VARIABLES:
  1. Power gap (how close is the challenger?)
  2. Dissatisfaction (does the challenger reject current rules?)
  3. Rate of change (how fast is power shifting?)
```

## Organski: Power Transition Theory

A.F.K. Organski (*World Politics*, 1958) developed power transition theory as a critique of balance of power theory.

**Balance of power theory** (realist): states balance against power concentrations; equilibrium → stability.

**Organski's challenge**: Balance of power predicts war when power is equal (multipolar, competitive). But empirically, war is most likely during power transitions, not when balance is achieved.

```
ORGANSKI'S HIERARCHY MODEL
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  THE DOMINANT POWER:
  At the top of the international hierarchy.
  Sets the rules of the order (trade, finance, security
  norms, international institutions).
  The order reflects the dominant power's interests.
  "Satisfied" with the order (it was built for them).

  GREAT POWERS:
  Can challenge but not yet at parity.
  Some are satisfied (allies of dominant power);
  some are dissatisfied (potential challengers).

  MIDDLE AND SMALL POWERS:
  Operate under the rules set by the top.

  THE DANGER PERIOD:
  When a rising dissatisfied great power approaches
  parity with the dominant power (~80-120% of
  dominant power's capabilities):
  • The dominant power has declining ability to enforce
    the order
  • The rising power has growing capability to challenge
  • Both have incentives for preventive war:
    - Dominant: fight now before the challenger is stronger
    - Challenger: fight before the dominant can mobilize
      against the challenge
  → War most likely here
```

## Gilpin: Hegemonic War and Change

Robert Gilpin (*War and Change in World Politics*, 1981) extended power transition theory:

**Core claim**: International orders are built by and for the dominant power (hegemon). Orders are inherently conservative — they favor the status quo that the hegemon created. But the distribution of power changes continuously (differential growth rates, technological change). When the distribution of power no longer matches the distribution of benefits in the order, war is the mechanism of change.

```
GILPIN'S CHANGE MECHANISM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PHASE 1: HEGEMONIC ORDER
  Dominant power creates order (institutions, norms,
  rules) that serves its interests.
  Challenger states grow under this order
  (free riding on public goods; economic development).

  PHASE 2: POWER SHIFT
  Differential growth rates: the challenger grows
  faster than the hegemon (often because it is
  growing from a lower base; also because it
  can free-ride on hegemonic public goods).
  Technology shifts advantage.
  The hegemon faces: aging infrastructure, costly
  global commitments, declining growth rate.

  PHASE 3: DISSATISFACTION
  The rising challenger finds the existing order
  does not serve its interests.
  The rules were written for the old hegemon.
  The challenger wants a different order.

  PHASE 4: RESOLUTION
  If peaceful: the dominant power accommodates
  the challenger (increases its share of benefits
  in the order, reforms institutions).
  If not: hegemonic war resets the order.

  HISTORICAL EXAMPLES:
  • Napoleonic Wars: Reset European order (1815)
  • WWI/WWII: US replaced UK as hegemon (1918/1945)
  • Cold War end: US emerged as unipolar (1991)
```

## The Thucydides Trap

Graham Allison (*Destined for War: Can America and China Escape Thucydides' Trap?*, 2017) popularized power transition theory for the China-US context.

### The Historical Survey

Allison identified 16 cases since the 15th century where a rising power threatened to displace a ruling power:

```
ALLISON'S THUCYDIDES TRAP CASES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  16 CASES IDENTIFIED:
  12 resulted in war (75%)
  4 were peaceful (25%)

  PEACEFUL CASES (What worked?):
  Case 5: UK-US (late 19th century)
  How: UK accommodated US in Western Hemisphere;
  shared language, culture, values; both benefited from
  trade; UK redirected attention to continental rivals.

  Case 10: US-Soviet (Cold War)
  How: Nuclear deterrence made war too costly;
  bipolar stability; US strategy of containment
  (not rollback) avoided direct military conflict.

  Case 14: UK-US hegemony transfer (post-WWII)
  How: WWII demonstrated both needed each other;
  Bretton Woods gave US what it wanted; UK
  willing to cede hegemony.

  VIOLENT CASES (What failed?):
  Case 8: UK-Germany (1890-1914)
  Case 9: Russia-Austria-Germany (1914)
  Case 12: US-Japan (1937-41)
  (Germany-France multiple cases)

  CHINA-US ASSESSMENT:
  Allison: the structural conditions for war are present;
  it is not inevitable; deliberate policy choices can
  navigate it; but the base rate (75% war) should
  generate serious concern.
```

### Critiques of the Thucydides Trap Argument

```
CRITIQUES OF THUCYDIDES TRAP
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SELECTION BIAS:
  Allison chose the cases; different case selection
  criteria might yield different base rates.
  Many power transitions happened peacefully without
  being "threatening" enough to make his list.

  NUCLEAR DETERRENCE IS NEW:
  None of Allison's 16 historical cases involved
  nuclear weapons on both sides.
  MAD fundamentally changes the calculation.
  The cases are not comparable.

  INTERDEPENDENCE IS NEW:
  China-US trade: ~$700 billion/year (pre-tariff wars).
  US debt: ~$1 trillion in Chinese holdings.
  No historical case had this level of economic
  integration between rising and declining powers.
  This raises costs of war significantly.

  CHINA'S STRATEGIC CULTURE:
  China has not historically been expansionist in
  the same way as European great powers in Allison's
  cases. The "Middle Kingdom" model is hierarchical
  but not territorial-expansionist.
  Counter: contemporary CCP behavior suggests
  assertiveness that contradicts this reading.

  POWER ASYMMETRY REMAINS LARGE:
  US GDP: ~$28 trillion; China: ~$18 trillion.
  US military budget: ~$900 billion; China: ~$300 billion.
  China is rising but has not yet reached parity —
  the danger period may be 10-20 years away,
  or Chinese growth may slow before parity is reached.
```

## Hegemonic Stability Theory

Charles Kindleberger (*The World in Depression*, 1973): Developed in economic context (see political-history/06-POSTWAR-ORDER). The global economy requires a hegemon to provide public goods:

1. Open markets (hegemon accepts imports even when others don't)
2. Stable reserve currency
3. Long-term lending in financial crisis
4. Coordination of macroeconomic policy
5. Security guarantees

```
HEGEMONY AND PUBLIC GOODS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PUBLIC GOODS PROBLEM:
  Open trade and financial stability benefit everyone
  but any single state would rather free-ride.
  → Without a hegemon, public goods are undersupplied.

  THE HEGEMONIC SOLUTION:
  A single dominant power bears the cost of providing
  these goods because:
  • It gets the most benefit (largest economy)
  • It can't be excluded from the benefits it provides
  • Its political/ideological preferences shape the order

  HEGEMONIC STABILITY THEORY PREDICTIONS:
  Hegemony → openness and order
  Decline of hegemony → fragmentation, protectionism

  HISTORICAL EVIDENCE:
  British hegemony (1815-1914): gold standard, free trade
  Interwar period (no hegemon): competitive devaluations,
  Smoot-Hawley, depression, war
  American hegemony (1945-present): Bretton Woods, GATT/WTO,
  open international economy (despite US relative decline)

  KEOHANE'S CHALLENGE:
  After Hegemony (1984): Once institutions are created,
  they can maintain cooperation even as hegemony declines.
  The US is declining relatively; institutions persist.
  → Hegemony necessary for creating the order;
  not necessary for maintaining it.
```

## UK-US Power Transfer: The Peaceful Case Study

The late 19th/early 20th century transfer of global hegemony from Britain to the United States is the most-studied peaceful transition.

```
UK-US PEACEFUL TRANSITION — WHY IT WORKED
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SHARED CHARACTERISTICS:
  • Common language
  • Common legal tradition (common law)
  • Similar political culture (parliamentary,
    constitutional democracy)
  • Protestant heritage
  • Already extensive trade ties
  → "Anglosphere" solidarity made accommodation easier

  STRATEGIC ACCOMMODATION:
  • UK progressively withdrew from Western Hemisphere
    (Venezuela 1895 arbitration; Hay-Pauncefote Treaty 1901
    — gave US control of future Panama Canal)
  • UK accepted US Monroe Doctrine
  • US gradually took over strategic responsibilities
    (Pacific, then Atlantic)

  ECONOMIC LOGIC:
  • US could pay for British war debts (WWI, WWII)
    in ways that made British financial dependence on
    US a feature, not a bug
  • Bretton Woods gave US monetary dominance while
    Britain retained economic recovery assistance

  WWII ACCELERATION:
  • Both were fighting the Axis threat simultaneously
  • Coordination built institutional habits
  • Lend-Lease made US-UK interdependence explicit

  CONTRAST WITH GERMANY:
  UK vs. Germany had NONE of these shared characteristics:
  competing imperial interests, no common values frame,
  rival navies, German challenge to global trade order
  → War.
```

## Does Economic Interdependence Stabilize Power Transitions?

```
INTERDEPENDENCE AND POWER TRANSITION — THE DEBATE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LIBERAL ARGUMENT (interdependence stabilizes):
  High US-China trade and financial interdependence
  raises cost of conflict dramatically.
  • China holds ~$1 trillion in US Treasuries;
    dumping them would hurt both economies
  • US relies on Chinese manufacturing for consumer goods,
    pharmaceuticals, electronics components
  • Chinese growth depends on US market access
  → War would be economically catastrophic; rational
     actors won't start it.

  REALIST RESPONSE (interdependence doesn't prevent war):
  1. Pre-WWI: UK-Germany trade was very high in 1913;
     didn't prevent war. Norman Angell was wrong.
  2. Relative gains: what matters is who gains more
     from trade, not mutual gains. If China is growing
     faster through trade, the US has reason to
     restrict trade even at mutual cost.
  3. Interdependence creates vulnerability:
     Dependence on Chinese semiconductors or drugs
     is a security liability, not a peace bond.
     → US semiconductor export controls (2022-) reflect
        this logic.

  INTERDEPENDENCE + DECOUPLING:
  US tech export controls (2022-2024) suggest both
  sides are actively reducing vulnerability.
  Decoupling doesn't reduce costs of war in the near term
  but prepares for a world where conflict is possible.
  Decoupling and military buildup are inconsistent with
  the liberal "interdependence prevents war" thesis.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Power Transition answer |
|----------|-------------------------|
| When is war most likely? | When challenger approaches 80-100% of dominant power's capabilities AND is dissatisfied with the existing order |
| Why does Gilpin say wars happen? | The distribution of power shifts but the distribution of benefits in the order doesn't adjust; war resets the order |
| What are the peaceful conditions? | Shared cultural/institutional characteristics (UK-US), nuclear deterrence (US-USSR), accommodation by dominant power |
| What is Thucydides Trap? | Allison's argument that rising powers threatening dominant powers tend to go to war (12/16 historical cases) |
| Does interdependence prevent war? | Liberal: yes, raises costs; Realist: no, pre-WWI UK-Germany example; current US policy suggests decoupling, not relying on interdependence |
| What is hegemonic stability? | Kindleberger: dominant power provides public goods (open trade, stable currency, security) that the system needs but won't self-provide |

## Common Confusion Points

**Power transition ≠ power balancing**: Realist balance of power theory says states balance against power concentrations → equilibrium. Power transition theory says parity (balance achieved) is dangerous, not stable. They make opposite predictions about what conditions produce war.

**Dissatisfaction is the key variable Allison underweights**: Allison's Thucydides Trap focuses on power gaps. But Organski's original theory emphasizes dissatisfaction with the existing order. A rising power that is satisfied (benefits from the existing order) won't fight to change it. China's dissatisfaction with specific rules (intellectual property, Taiwan, South China Sea) is the variable that matters as much as power parity.

**The "Thucydides Trap" quote is frequently misunderstood**: Thucydides in *History of the Peloponnesian War* said: "It was the rise of Athens and the fear that this inspired in Sparta that made war inevitable." He was analyzing why Sparta launched the war, not arguing that rising powers always go to war. The trap is about the *dominant* power's fear and potential for preventive war, not just the rising power's ambitions.

**Hegemonic stability theory has a public goods problem**: Kindleberger assumes the hegemon benevolently provides public goods. But hegemons also extract rents from the order they create — the dollar standard, seigniorage, favorable trade terms. The order is partly public good and partly private appropriation. This is the dependency theorist critique of hegemonic stability.
