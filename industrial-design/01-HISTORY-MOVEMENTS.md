# Industrial Design History: Bauhaus, Streamlining, and Modernism

## The Big Picture

Industrial design as a named discipline is roughly 100 years old, though its concerns are as old as manufacturing. The history is a series of responses to industrialization -- some embracing the machine, some resisting it, some trying to redeem it.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|           INDUSTRIAL DESIGN MOVEMENTS TIMELINE                       |
|                                                                      |
|  1750  Industrial Revolution begins (UK)                             |
|  1830  Crystal Palace Exhibition: industrial products on display     |
|  1851  Henry Cole / Owen Jones: reform through good taste            |
|  1880  Arts and Crafts Movement: William Morris                      |
|  1907  Deutscher Werkbund: design + industry synthesis               |
|  1919  Bauhaus founded: Walter Gropius                               |
|  1925  Art Deco: luxury modernism for mass market                    |
|  1930  Streamlining: American commercial design (Loewy, Geddes)      |
|  1933  Bauhaus closed by Nazis; faculty disperses globally           |
|  1945  Post-war American consumer product design                     |
|  1953  Ulm School of Design (Germany): neo-Bauhaus                   |
|  1955  Braun design begins: Rams era starts                          |
|  1969  Moon landing: industrial design in extreme environment        |
|  1976  First Apple computer: product design enters computing         |
|  1981  Memphis Group: postmodern reaction                            |
|  1998  iMac: design returns to Apple identity                        |
|  2007  iPhone: product design becomes cultural event                 |
|  2010  Parametric/computational design mainstream                    |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## The Pre-Industrial Problem: Why Design Emerged

Before the Industrial Revolution, objects were designed and made by the same person or guild. The craftsman understood material, process, and use simultaneously. Industrialization severed this:

```
PRE-INDUSTRIAL                 INDUSTRIAL
--------------------------     ----------------------------------
Craftsman designs + makes      Engineer designs machine
                               Machine makes object
Object adapted to use          Object adapted to machine process
by maker's judgment            by engineer's specification

Quality controlled by          Quality "controlled" by
maker's reputation             inspection (after the fact)

Limited production             Mass production
(scarcity maintains value)     (abundance creates new problem:
                                how to distinguish one product
                                from another)

One off or small batch         Millions of identical objects
```

The result: mass-produced objects that were technically adequate but aesthetically debased, confusing to use, and divorced from human context.

## Arts and Crafts Movement (1880-1920): The First Response

**Core figures:** William Morris (UK), John Ruskin (critic), Gustav Stickley (USA), Charles Rennie Mackintosh (Scotland)

**The argument:** Industrialization degrades both the product (cheap, ugly, alienated from use) and the maker (no creative satisfaction in tending a machine). Return to handcraft.

```
MORRIS'S POSITION:
  "Have nothing in your houses that you do not know to be
   useful, or believe to be beautiful."

  This sounds like Rams -- but Morris's method is different:
  -- Rams: achieve this through industrial production
  -- Morris: achieve this by rejecting industrial production

THE IRONY:
  Morris's hand-made textiles, wallpaper, and furniture
  were beautiful and functionally excellent.
  They were also extraordinarily expensive.
  Only wealthy patrons could afford them.
  The original workers whose conditions Morris lamented
  could not afford the products that were supposed to
  redeem their labor.

LEGACY:
  The visual vocabulary (stylized natural forms, flat pattern,
  grid-based composition) influenced: Bauhaus, Art Nouveau,
  decorative arts throughout the 20th century.
  The ideological position: impossible to maintain commercially.
```

## Deutscher Werkbund (1907): The Synthesis Attempt

**Founded:** Peter Behrens, Herman Muthesius (Germany)

The Werkbund attempted what Morris refused: embrace industrial production while demanding design standards.

```
WERKBUND INNOVATION: AEG CORPORATE IDENTITY (1907-1914)

Peter Behrens hired as "artistic consultant" to AEG (electrical company):
  -- Designed: factory buildings, worker housing, products, advertising
  -- First complete corporate identity in history
  -- Same visual language applied at all scales

  AEG PRODUCTS BEHRENS DESIGNED:
    Electric arc lamps, fans, kettles, clocks
    -- Systematic proportions
    -- Standardized parts across product lines
    -- Machine-appropriate forms (no applied ornament)

  THE LESSON: Industrial production does not require ugliness.
  Systematic design intelligence can produce coherent beauty.

BEHRENS'S STUDENTS AT AEG:
  Le Corbusier, Walter Gropius, Mies van der Rohe
  (Three of the four most influential architects of the 20th century
   learned design thinking from Behrens)
```

## Bauhaus (1919-1933): The Paradigm Shift

The Bauhaus school (Weimar, then Dessau, then Berlin) transformed design education and practice globally. Its closure by the Nazis dispersed its faculty to the US, UK, and Israel, spreading its influence worldwide.

### Founding Principles

```
BAUHAUS EDUCATIONAL STRUCTURE

WALTER GROPIUS'S VISION:
  Reunify the fine arts and applied arts
  -- Art school + craft school = single institution
  -- Every student learns both: formal/conceptual work AND a craft
  -- Theory informs making; making informs theory

WORKSHOP STRUCTURE:
  Every workshop had TWO teachers:
    Master of Form (fine artist, e.g., Paul Klee for weaving)
    Master of Craft (technical craftsman)
  Together: intellectual + technical = design

PRELIMINARY COURSE (Vorkurs):
  Johannes Itten, then László Moholy-Nagy
  -- Explore basic visual elements: line, plane, volume, texture
  -- No reference to art history; start fresh
  -- Materials investigation: properties of wood, metal, paper
  -- This course structure survives in most design education today

WORKSHOPS:
  Weaving (textiles)
  Pottery
  Metal workshop (lamps, teapots)
  Carpentry (furniture)
  Mural painting
  Stage workshop (theater design)
  Typography and graphic design (under Herbert Bayer)
```

### Key Bauhaus Figures and Works

| Figure | Role | Key Works | Legacy |
|--------|------|-----------|--------|
| Walter Gropius | Director (1919-28) | Bauhaus building Dessau | Architecture education |
| Marcel Breuer | Furniture | Wassily Chair (1925), Cesca Chair (1928) | Tubular steel furniture |
| Herbert Bayer | Typography | Universal typeface | Universal sans-serif; graphic design |
| Marianne Brandt | Metal workshop | MT49 bedside lamp, ashtrays | Women as industrial designers |
| Wilhelm Wagenfeld | Lighting | Wagenfeld lamp (MT8) | Design classic, still produced |
| Paul Klee | Formal master | Theory of art as geometry | Color theory, visual arts education |
| Wassily Kandinsky | Formal master | Color/form theory | Visual abstraction theory |
| László Moholy-Nagy | Vorkurs | Light-Space Modulator | New Bauhaus Chicago (1937) |

```
THE WASSILY CHAIR (Marcel Breuer, 1925):
  -- Named for Wassily Kandinsky (who admired it)
  -- Tubular steel frame; canvas or leather straps
  -- First chair designed specifically for industrial materials
  -- Tubular steel: uniform, standardized, lightweight
  -- Canvas: no padding needed if geometry is correct
  -- The chair "shows" its structure: no ornament concealing joinery
  -- Still in production today: Knoll International
  -- Bridge to software: the code IS the design; no decorative layer
```

### Bauhaus Political Context and Diaspora

```
BAUHAUS UNDER POLITICAL PRESSURE

WEIMAR PERIOD (1919-1925):
  Founded in progressive Weimar; pressured by conservative local politics
  Right-wing attack: "Bolshevist" internationalism; "un-German"

DESSAU PERIOD (1925-1932):
  Moved to Dessau; expanded; most productive period
  1928: Gropius resigns; Hannes Meyer (communist) takes over
  1930: Ludwig Mies van der Rohe appointed to stabilize

BERLIN (1932-1933):
  Nazi election victories force closure of Dessau school
  Private school in Berlin; closed by Gestapo July 1933

THE DIASPORA:
  -- Walter Gropius: Harvard GSD (transformed American architecture education)
  -- Ludwig Mies van der Rohe: Illinois Institute of Technology
  -- László Moholy-Nagy: New Bauhaus Chicago (1937), became Institute of Design
  -- Herbert Bayer: advertising, graphic design in USA
  -- Marcel Breuer: Harvard with Gropius; later own practice

THE RESULT: Bauhaus principles became the foundation of
American design education from 1940s-1970s.
The visual language of modernism (grid, sans-serif, geometric form)
became the visual language of American corporate identity.
```

## American Streamlining (1930s): Design as Commerce

American industrial design emerged as a commercial discipline in the Depression era. The goal was to stimulate consumer spending by making products more attractive.

```
THE FIVE PIONEERS OF AMERICAN INDUSTRIAL DESIGN

RAYMOND LOEWY (1893-1986):
  -- Most famous industrial designer of the 20th century
  -- French immigrant; began as fashion illustrator
  -- "MAYA principle": Most Advanced Yet Acceptable
    (public will not buy what looks too far ahead)
  -- COCA-COLA CONTOUR BOTTLE (1915, but redesigned by Loewy for)
  -- LUCKY STRIKE PACKAGE (1940): white package; simple typography
  -- STUDEBAKER AVANTI (1962): aerodynamic automobile
  -- SHELL LOGO, BP LOGO, EXXON LOGO
  -- AIR FORCE ONE INTERIOR (1962): still the livery today
  -- NASA: Skylab interior design (ergonomics in space)
  -- On cover of TIME magazine (1949): only designer ever

NORMAN BEL GEDDES (1893-1958):
  -- Streamlined everything: cars, ocean liners, airplanes
  -- "Futurama" at 1939 World's Fair: imagined 1960s America
  -- Proposed jet aircraft, helicopters, superhighways (before all existed)

HENRY DREYFUSS (1904-1972):
  -- AT&T telephone (Model 500, 1949): the classic black phone
  -- John Deere tractors: agricultural ergonomics
  -- Wrote "Designing for People" (1955): founding ergonomics text
  -- Humanscale (1974): the body measurement reference

WALTER DORWIN TEAGUE (1883-1960):
  -- Kodak cameras (Brownie): photography for everyone
  -- Boeing 707 interior

HAROLD VAN DOREN (1895-1957):
  -- Toledo Scale, Air-King Radio
  -- Wrote "Industrial Design" (1940): first design textbook
```

### The Streamlining Aesthetic

```
STREAMLINING CHARACTERISTICS

  VISUAL:
    -- Teardrop and cigar shapes (aerodynamic profiles)
    -- Horizontal speed lines (painted or molded)
    -- Chrome highlights
    -- Enclosed mechanical parts (no visible moving components)
    -- Rounded corners everywhere

  LOGIC (for transport):
    -- Genuine aerodynamic benefit for aircraft, trains, automobiles
    -- At 300+ mph: shape matters enormously
    -- Train shape reduces drag; aircraft shape is aerodynamics itself

  THE IRONY (for everything else):
    -- Streamlined refrigerators have zero aerodynamic benefit
    -- Streamlined pencil sharpeners, radios, toasters: pure styling
    -- "Streamlining" became a synonym for "modern" regardless of function
    -- The style was applied without the engineering rationale

  CRITIQUE:
    -- Form following fashion, not function
    -- Made objects look faster/newer/more valuable than they were
    -- Violates Rams' principle #6 (honesty)
    -- But: STIMULATED consumer spending during the Depression
    -- "Good design" vs "effective commercial design" = not the same thing
```

## Ulm School of Design (Hochschule fur Gestaltung, 1953-1968)

The Ulm School was the neo-Bauhaus -- founded by Inge Scholl and Otl Aicher (dedicated to the memory of Sophie Scholl) to rebuild German culture after Nazism.

```
ULM'S CONTRIBUTION

FOUNDED: 1953, Ulm, West Germany
  Otl Aicher (typography, visual identity systems)
  Tomás Maldonado (semiotics, design theory)
  Max Bill (art/design, first director)
  Hans Gugelot (product design)

KEY DEPARTURE FROM BAUHAUS:
  -- Bauhaus: intuitive, artistic, workshop-based
  -- Ulm: systematic, theoretical, scientific basis for design
  -- Ulm introduced: semiotics, ergonomics, operations research,
     cybernetics, information theory INTO design education

THE BRAUN CONNECTION:
  Hans Gugelot and Otl Aicher worked directly with Braun
  This collaboration produced: the Braun design language
  (see 02-BRAUN-RAMS.md for full coverage)

ULM'S BROADER LEGACY:
  Otl Aicher: Munich 1972 Olympic visual identity system
  -- Pictograms: the visual language of international communication
  -- Every sports pictogram in every Olympics since: derived from Aicher
  -- Every airport signage system: descendant of Aicher's work
  Lufthansa corporate identity (Aicher, 1962): still in use
```

## Postmodernism and the Memphis Group (1981)

By the 1970s, functionalism felt authoritarian. The reaction was Memphis.

```
MEMPHIS GROUP (Ettore Sottsass, 1981-1988)

FOUNDED: 1981, Milan, Italy
NAMED: for Bob Dylan song "Memphis Blues Again"
KEY FIGURES: Ettore Sottsass, Michael Graves, Andrea Branzi

THE REJECTION OF FUNCTIONALISM:
  -- Functionalism said: decoration is crime (Adolf Loos, 1913)
  -- Memphis said: decoration is joy, identity, humor
  -- Functionalism said: design must be honest about its materials
  -- Memphis said: laminate surfaces are allowed to lie

VISUAL CHARACTERISTICS:
  -- Bright colors (fuchsia, teal, yellow, chartreuse)
  -- Geometric patterns on surfaces (stripes, squiggles, checkerboard)
  -- Objects that reference other objects (bookcase shaped like temple)
  -- Surprising material combinations (terrazzo patterns on furniture)
  -- Deliberate visual noise

CARLTON ROOM DIVIDER (Sottsass, 1981):
  -- Most iconic Memphis object
  -- A bookcase/room divider shaped like an abstract human figure
  -- Brightly colored geometric laminate surfaces
  -- Deliberately non-functional (awkward proportions for actual book storage)
  -- The "idea" of a bookcase, not an optimized bookcase

LEGACY:
  -- Direct influence on early 1980s graphic design (and MTV aesthetics)
  -- Established that objects could be ironic, playful, provocative
  -- Led to Italian design as global cultural force
  -- Today: pastiche of Memphis aesthetic has recurred in 2010s design culture
```

| Movement | Core Principle | Key Figures | Legacy |
|----------|---------------|-------------|--------|
| Arts & Crafts | Handcraft as moral good; reject machine | Morris, Ruskin | Aesthetics without solution to scale |

## Common Confusion Points

**Bauhaus was not minimalist.**
Bauhaus work explored full range of visual expression -- Klee's paintings, Oskar Schlemmer's theatrical work, the mural workshop. The minimalist association comes from specific products (Breuer chairs, Wagenfeld lamp) and the typography workshop, not the full school.

**Streamlining was not dishonest design -- it was commercial design.**
The distinction matters. Loewy's MAYA principle was pragmatic wisdom about what consumers will accept, not an instruction to deceive. A refrigerator that looks streamlined is not technically more functional, but if it makes people more likely to buy and use a good product, that is a design argument, not fraud.

**Bauhaus closure by Nazis does not mean Bauhaus = left-wing politics.**
Bauhaus was closed because its international faculty, Jewish faculty members, and modernist aesthetics were incompatible with Nazi ideology. But Bauhaus faculty had diverse political views. Mies van der Rohe actually attempted to negotiate with Nazi authorities to keep the school open; he was not an anti-fascist activist.

**The Ulm School was a distinct institution, not a Bauhaus successor.**
Ulm shared Bauhaus ideals but departed significantly: systematic/scientific vs intuitive/artistic, semiotics-based vs intuition-based. Calling Ulm "the new Bauhaus" is correct only in the sense that it shared the mission of rigorous design education.
