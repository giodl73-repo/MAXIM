# Design Criticism and Cultural Theory

## The Big Picture

Design criticism is the intellectual framework for asking whether design is doing what it should,
whether what it should do is what it claims, and for whom. The function vs. form debate is the
classical axis; contemporary criticism adds ethics, politics, access, and power.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              DESIGN CRITICISM: THE LANDSCAPE                         |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                      |

|  "Artefacts have politics"        Attention economy design           |
|  (Langdon Winner)                 Algorithmic harm                   |
|                                                                      |
```

## The Function vs. Form Debate

**Louis Sullivan (1896):** "Form ever follows function." The originating statement, from
the essay "The Tall Office Building Artistically Considered." Sullivan was arguing for a
new architecture appropriate to the steel-frame skyscraper — not neo-Gothic ornament bolted
onto a structural grid. Function (a building of offices, with floors of identical plan stacked
vertically) should generate the form.

**The misreading:** Sullivan's "function" was not mechanical. He included the social function,
the cultural meaning, the purpose of the building in the landscape. His actual ornamental
work (Chicago Stock Exchange, Wainwright Building) is profuse and organic. He meant: let
the function tell you the form, including its civic and cultural function.

**Adolf Loos and "Ornament and Crime" (1908):** More radical. Ornament was literally criminal:
it wasted labor, created objects that would go out of fashion, exploited workers who had to
produce it. The evolutionary argument: "primitive" cultures use ornament; modern civilization
rejects it. (Racist premise; useful polemic.) Loos argued for smooth, unadorned surfaces as
the appropriate expression of modernity.

**The Bauhaus position:** Gropius and the Bauhaus (1919-1933) synthesized: design should
embrace industrial production, use machine aesthetics, eliminate ornament, and produce objects
accessible to all. This is both a formal position (clean geometry) and a social one (good
design for everyone, not just the wealthy who could afford craft).

**Dieter Rams and the Braun synthesis:** Ten principles (02-BRAUN-RAMS.md) that codified
functionalism for consumer electronics. Not "ornament is crime" (Loos) or "form follows
machine" (Bauhaus) but "less but better" — every element earns its place by contributing to
function. Rams's designs are visually restrained but not cold; the restraint is in service
of clarity of use.

**Robert Venturi's counter (1966):** "Less is a bore." Learning from Las Vegas (Venturi, Scott
Brown, Izenour, 1972) argued that vernacular and commercial design — Las Vegas neon, roadside
architecture — was genuinely communicating through ornament and symbolism, and that Modernist
rejection of this was elitist and impoverished. Postmodern design followed.

## Victor Papanek: Design for the Real World (1971)

**The argument:** The title signals the provocation. Papanek argued that industrial design was
serving the interests of commerce rather than humanity. The design profession was complicit in:
- Designing useless, ecologically harmful products for wealthy markets
- Ignoring the 80% of humanity that could benefit most from design
- Treating planned obsolescence as legitimate professional work
- Producing objects that injured, deceived, or manipulated users

**The opening gambit:** "There are professions more harmful than industrial design, but only a
very few of them." And: "In an age of mass production when everything must be planned and
designed, design has become the most powerful tool with which man shapes his tools and
environments (and, by extension, society and himself)."

**What Papanek wanted instead:**
- Design for disability and medical equipment
- Design for developing world contexts (not Western-market exports to different climates/cultures)
- Design for education and low-income users
- Design that prioritizes need over wants manufactured by advertising
- Designers who refuse certain commissions on ethical grounds

**Impact:** Papanek was professionally marginalized by the American design establishment when
the book came out. He was right about most of it. The field of inclusive/universal design, the
movement of design for social impact, and design for development ("appropriate technology") all
have him as an intellectual ancestor.

**What he got wrong:** His specific design proposals were often naive about economics and
manufacturing. His hostility to all professional consumer design was too categorical. But the
diagnostic critique was largely correct.

## Anti-Design Movements

**The Italian Anti-Design movement (1960s-70s):** Studio Alchimia (1979) and Memphis (1981)
were reactions against functionalist orthodoxy — specifically against Modernism's claim that
good design was universal, scientific, and eternal.

**Memphis (Ettore Sottsass, 1981):**
```
MEMPHIS MANIFESTO (implicit):
  - Bright colors, clashing patterns, "bad taste" references
  - Kitsch, pop culture, vernacular form
  - Surfaces as decoration, not "honest expression of material"
  - Anti-functionalist: function is not the point
  - Designed to provoke, not to function optimally
  - Anti-Bauhaus: "the grey furniture of the regime"

KEY EXAMPLE: Carlton bookcase (Sottsass, 1981)
  - Asymmetric, unstable-looking
  - Brightly colored laminate surfaces
  - Visually complex
  - Not a "good bookcase" in the Rams sense
  - Completely clear about what it is: a cultural statement
```

Memphis was not kitsch accidentally — it was theoretically kitsch, deploying bad taste as
critical practice. The argument: Modernism's "good design" claims were ideological, not
universal. Memphis exposed this by making the ideological commitment visible.

**Post-Memphis:** The 1980s luxury goods boom, Memphis influence on fashion and advertising,
then the backlash back to simplicity in the 1990s (Muji, Apple's first industrial design
period under Jonathan Ive). The cycle of simplicity-ornament-simplicity is a recurring feature
of Western design culture.

## Artefacts Have Politics: Langdon Winner (1980)

**The argument:** Objects can be political. Winner's canonical example: Robert Moses's
Long Island parkway bridges, which were allegedly built too low for buses. If so (the
historical claim is disputed), this was racial and class exclusion built into concrete —
poor people, who predominantly used buses, could not reach Jones Beach.

The general claim: technical choices embed social choices. The "neutrality" of technology is
ideological. When designers choose a form, material, interface, or system, they are making
choices that distribute benefits and burdens across populations.

**Latour's "speed bump" (1992):** Bruno Latour analyzed the hotel key with a large, heavy fob
attached — a device that "asks" guests to leave their key at the desk by making it annoying
to carry. The political claim: the hotel management's desire for keys to be left at the desk
was delegated to the fob. The fob is a "politician" — it enforces a policy without requiring
a human enforcement agent. Objects are "non-human actants" in social networks.

**Design as policy:** From Winner and Latour, design critics developed the argument that every
designed object is a form of policy — it enables certain behaviors, disables others, benefits
certain users, disadvantages others. "Policy through design" is invisible policy, often more
durable than legal policy (laws can be repealed; a bridge's height is very difficult to change).

**Contemporary examples:**
- Urban design hostile to homeless people (spikes on benches, boulders under bridges)
- Voting booth design that advantages some users and creates barriers for others
- Website design that makes opt-out difficult (dark patterns — see below)
- Car-dependent urban planning that disadvantages non-car-owners

## Design Ethics and Dark Patterns

**Dark patterns (Harry Brignull, 2010):** User interface patterns that use deceptive,
manipulative, or obstructive techniques to get users to do things they would not do if
they understood what was happening.

**Taxonomy of dark patterns:**

| Pattern | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Roach motel | Easy to get in, hard to get out | Subscription signup 1 click; cancel requires phone call |
| Trick question | Default checkbox catches inattentive users | "Uncheck this box to not receive spam" |
| Misdirection | Visual design diverts attention from important information | Confirmation button prominent; cancel tiny |
| Hidden costs | Additional charges revealed only at checkout | "Free trial" that bills without warning |
| Forced continuity | Free trial becomes paid without notification | Requirement to enter credit card for "free" trial |
| Confirm-shaming | Shame the user for declining | "No thanks, I don't want to save money" |
| Disguised ads | Ads designed to look like editorial content | Native advertising without disclosure |
| Bait and switch | Advertise one thing, deliver another | Advertise X at price Y, offer Z |

**Legal trajectory:** Dark patterns were a fringe design criticism concept in 2010; by 2023
the FTC had issued enforcement actions against multiple companies for dark patterns (ROSCA
violations — Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act). The EU's Digital Services Act (2022)
explicitly prohibits dark patterns for designated platforms.

**The attention economy critique (James Williams, "Stand Out of Our Light", 2018):**
The advertising-based internet business model means tech companies profit from user attention,
not user benefit. Interfaces are optimized for engagement (time on platform) not wellbeing.
This is dark pattern at the system level — not individual UI tricks but a structural design
toward extraction of attention regardless of user benefit.

This connects to the Papanek critique: the design profession is serving commercial interests
that are frequently opposed to user interests, and is largely complicit in this.

## The Function/Aesthetics Synthesis

The sophisticated contemporary view rejects both pure functionalism and pure formalism as
incomplete:

**Function includes aesthetics:** How a product makes a user feel is functional — it affects
whether they use it, whether they use it correctly, whether they recommend it. The emotional
and aesthetic response is part of the product's function. Donald Norman's "Emotional Design"
(2004) made this argument systematically: attractive things work better (they are approached
with less anxiety, used more confidently, forgiven when they fail).

**Form is meaning, not decoration:** Viktor Frankl and Umberto Eco pointed to the semiotic
function of objects — things mean things to people, and this meaning affects use. A hospital
designed to look welcoming affects patient outcomes (research supports this). A tool designed
to look precise affects the care with which it is used. The "meaning" of a form is functional.

**The Rams paradox:** Rams claimed his designs were "not fashionable" and would last. But
Rams-influenced design is now a highly recognizable aesthetic style that is very much in
fashion. The attempt to escape fashion by going to functional essentials produced a fashion.
This is not hypocrisy — it is an illustration that aesthetics cannot be eliminated by
declaring it irrelevant.

## Decolonial and Inclusive Design Criticism

**Universal design:** The movement (Mace, 1980s) to design products usable by the widest
possible range of people without adaptation. The seven principles:
1. Equitable use
2. Flexibility in use
3. Simple and intuitive use
4. Perceptible information
5. Tolerance for error
6. Low physical effort
7. Size and space for approach and use

**Decolonial critique of universal design:** The critique from postcolonial scholarship: who
defines "universal"? Whose body, whose context, whose needs are assumed as the default? Most
"universal" design is designed for an assumed user who is male, right-handed, English-speaking,
middle-class, and physically normative. Design that is "universal" for that user profile is
not universal.

Examples: voice recognition systems that have higher error rates for women and non-native
English speakers; medical devices calibrated on datasets of predominantly light-skinned patients
(pulse oximetry, dermatology AI); doorknob/handle designs that disadvantage people with limited
grip strength.

**Appropriate technology (Schumacher, "Small is Beautiful", 1973):** Technology appropriate
to the actual scale, resource context, and cultural context of users — not transplanting
Western technology to different contexts. A water pump that can be repaired with local skills
and materials outperforms a technically superior pump that requires imported spare parts.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Form follows function settles the debate":** It doesn't — Sullivan's maxim is ambiguous on
what counts as "function." Aesthetic experience is a function. Cultural meaning is a function.
The maxim is a pointer, not a decision procedure.

**"Dark patterns are just bad UX":** They are intentional. The distinction between accidental
bad design and dark patterns is intent. Dark patterns are the product of deliberate optimization
(usually A/B tested) for commercial outcomes at user expense. Calling them "bad UX" obscures
the agency involved.

**"Papanek wanted no consumer design":** He wanted designers to spend a fraction of their work
on socially useful design and to refuse some commissions. He wasn't opposed to consumer goods;
he was opposed to harm and irrelevance as default professional outputs.

**"Memphis was just bad design that became fashionable":** Memphis was theoretically motivated
and correctly diagnosed something real about Modernism's ideological claims. That subsequent
generations used its aesthetic as a style (without the theoretical content) is a separate matter.
The substance of the critique (that "good design" claims are politically loaded) was correct.

**"Design criticism is about taste":** Some is. But the most important design criticism is
about power: who benefits from design choices, who bears costs, whose interests the design
profession serves, and whether the claims made for design (solving real problems, being
universal, being neutral) are accurate.
