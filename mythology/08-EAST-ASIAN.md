# East Asian Mythology

## The Big Picture

East Asian mythology divides into two major streams — Chinese and Japanese — plus Korean and Vietnamese traditions that are heavily Chinese-influenced. These traditions do not fit the Western mythology template cleanly: both are deeply syncretic (mixing folk religion, Taoism, Buddhism, Confucianism, and local shamanism), both evolved continuously rather than being "crystallized" in a classical period, and both are still active living traditions rather than historical curiosities.

## Chinese Creation: Pangu and Nu Wa

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    CHINESE CREATION MYTHS                         │
│                                                                  │
│  PANGU — THE BODY-AS-WORLD CREATION MYTH                        │
│  Source: Sanwu Liji (~3rd c. CE); a relatively late myth        │
│                                                                  │
│  Before creation: the universe is a chaos egg (hundun)          │
│  Pangu sleeps inside the egg for 18,000 years                   │
│  He wakes; chaos separates:                                      │
│    — Light, pure qi rises → heaven (yang)                       │
│    — Heavy, turbid qi descends → earth (yin)                    │
│  Pangu stands between them, growing 10 feet per day for 18,000  │
│  years, holding heaven and earth apart                          │
│  (Compare: Egyptian Shu holding sky Nut above earth Geb)        │
│                                                                  │
│  Pangu dies; his body becomes the world:                        │
│    — Breath → wind and clouds                                   │
│    — Voice → thunder                                            │
│    — Left eye → sun; right eye → moon                          │
│    — Four limbs → cardinal mountains                            │
│    — Blood → rivers                                             │
│    — Flesh → soil                                               │
│    — Hair → stars; body hair → plants                          │
│    — Sweat → rain                                               │
│    — Parasites on his body → humanity                          │
│                                                                  │
│  The "parasites become humans" variant is unusual and suggests   │
│  a different origin for this sub-element                        │
│                                                                  │
│  NU WA — THE CREATOR OF HUMANITY AND COSMIC REPAIR               │
│  Source: Shanhaijing, Huainanzi, and many later texts           │
│                                                                  │
│  Nu Wa is a goddess with a human upper body and serpent lower   │
│  body. She and her brother/consort Fu Xi (also serpent-tailed)  │
│  are the first beings; sometimes described as intermarrying     │
│  after a cosmic flood                                           │
│                                                                  │
│  CREATING HUMANITY:                                             │
│  Nu Wa walks the earth alone and feels lonely                   │
│  She takes yellow earth and shapes human figures from it        │
│  First careful ones → nobility and wealthy people               │
│  Tired; drags a rope through mud → drops become common people   │
│  (folk etiology of social class)                                │
│                                                                  │
│  REPAIRING HEAVEN:                                              │
│  The cosmic pillar Buzhou Mountain is broken by the water god  │
│  Gonggong (in a rage after losing a battle)                     │
│  The sky tilts; floods and fires overwhelm the earth           │
│  Nu Wa:                                                         │
│    — Melts five-colored stones to patch the sky                │
│    — Cuts off the legs of a great sea turtle to use as four    │
│       pillars holding up the sky                               │
│    — Kills the black dragon causing floods                     │
│    — Builds dams of reed ash to stop the floods               │
│  Sky repaired — but now slightly tilted (why sun/moon move     │
│  from east to west; why rivers in China flow east to sea)      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Chinese Culture Heroes

```
THE THREE SOVEREIGNS AND FIVE EMPERORS

Three Sovereigns (mythological period, no fixed list):
  Often: Fu Xi, Nu Wa (or Shennong), and Huang Di

FU XI — culture bringer
  — Taught humanity: fishing, hunting, animal husbandry
  — Invented the Eight Trigrams (bagua) → basis of I Ching
  — Serpent lower body; often paired with Nu Wa

SHENNONG (Divine Farmer)
  — Taught agriculture and the use of plants as medicine
  — Tasted 100 plants to test their properties; poisoned hundreds
    of times per day (his transparent body let him observe effects)
  — Invented the plow

HUANG DI (Yellow Emperor)
  — Semi-historical figure; associated with ~2697 BCE
  — Culture hero par excellence:
    — Defeated Chiyou (horned demon warrior) with the help of
      drought goddess Ba and "south-pointing chariot" (compass?)
    — His consort Leizu discovered silk when a cocoon fell into tea
    — His minister Cang Jie invented writing (birds' footprints)
    — First to make clothing from fabric (not animal skins)
  — Inner Canon of the Yellow Emperor (Huangdi Neijing) — foundational
    text of Chinese medicine attributed to him
  — Mythically ascended to heaven on a dragon

YU THE GREAT — Tamer of Floods
  — The great flood: Yellow Emperor's time or later
  — Yu's father Gun tried to stop the flood with soil-dam; failed;
    was executed by the gods
  — Yu succeeds by dredging channels, not damming
    (nine years of work; didn't enter his own house for 13 years)
  — His labor was so intense his body became partly disabled
  — Became the first king of the Xia dynasty → the first dynasty
  — The "Great Plan" (Hongfan): cosmological ordering principle
    associated with Yu

HOUYI — The Divine Archer
  — In primordial times, ten suns appeared simultaneously
    (normally they take turns) → crops dying, earth scorching
  — Houyi shoots down nine of the ten suns with his bow
  — Reward: elixir of immortality from the Queen Mother of the West
  — His wife Chang'e steals and drinks the elixir
    → She floats to the moon; lives there with the jade rabbit
    → Mid-Autumn Festival (mooncakes) commemorates her story
```

## Sun Wukong and Journey to the West

```
JOURNEY TO THE WEST (Xi You Ji) — Wu Cheng'en, ~1592 CE

Not ancient myth but a 16th-century novel that crystallized centuries
of Buddhist and folk tradition into the canonical Chinese mythological
narrative. The characters and events are now as culturally fundamental
as Homer's epics to the West.

SUN WUKONG — THE MONKEY KING:
  — Born from a stone egg on Flower-Fruit Mountain
  — Learns 72 transformations (can become any form) and cloud-somersault
    (travels 108,000 li in one leap)
  — Steals the Dragon King's magical staff (Ruyi Jingu Bang):
    — 8.1 tons; can grow to fill the sky or shrink to a needle
    — Kept behind his ear when small
  — Descends to hell; crosses out his name and all monkeys' names
    from the Book of Death → monkeys become immortal
  — Crashes the Jade Emperor's court; demands recognition as
    "Great Sage Equal to Heaven" (Qítian Dàshèng)
  — Causes chaos in heaven; even the combined forces of heaven
    cannot subdue him
  — Only Buddha can stop him: bets Sun Wukong he cannot leap off
    his palm; Sun Wukong leaps to the ends of the universe but
    only reaches Buddha's fingers; returns to find he never left
  — Imprisoned under Five Elements Mountain for 500 years

THE PILGRIMAGE:
  — Tang Dynasty monk Xuanzang (historical: 602–664 CE; real
    pilgrimage to India for Buddhist scriptures) is chosen
    by Guanyin to fetch scriptures from the Western paradise
  — Guanyin recruits protectors:
    — Sun Wukong (freed from the mountain)
    — Zhu Bajie (Pigsy): pig-man; formerly a heavenly general
      demoted for drunken advances on Chang'e
    — Sha Wujing (Sandy): water monster; formerly jade general,
      demoted for breaking a crystal cup
    — White Dragon Horse: dragon prince demoted, becomes the horse
  — 81 disasters (tribulations) on the 14-year journey
  — Arrive at Lingshan (Spirit Mountain); obtain the scriptures
  — All are enlightened; Sun Wukong becomes "Victorious Fighting
    Buddha"

SUN WUKONG'S CULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  — Trickster; rebel against authority; overcomes by cleverness
  — Read in 20th century as anti-imperial hero (Mao praised him)
  — Ancestor of Son Goku (Dragon Ball), countless anime/game characters
  — The 72 transformations = mastery of all forms = ultimate freedom
```

## The Eight Immortals

```
EIGHT IMMORTALS (Ba Xian) — Taoist mythology; mostly Tang/Song Dynasty origin

Each is a historical or semi-historical figure who achieved immortality:

LÜ DONGBIN — Most famous; Taoist master; sword on back;
  purifies the world of evil; patron of barbers

LI TIEGUAI (Iron-Crutch Li) — Old man with iron crutch; carries
  a gourd of medicine; patron of the sick and pharmacists

ZHANG GUOLAO — Old man riding a white donkey (backwards);
  can fold the donkey into paper and unfold it when needed

HAN ZHONGLI — Fat man with a palm-leaf fan; holds elixir of life

HE XIANGU — Only woman of the Eight; floats on lotus flowers;
  became immortal by eating a powdered mother-of-pearl stone

LAN CAIHE — Ambiguous gender; wanders, sings; carries flower basket

CAO GUOJIU — Relative of a Song Dynasty empress; carries jade tablet

HAN XIANGZI — Young man with flute; patron of musicians;
  allegedly a nephew of the poet Han Yu

THE IMMORTALS' PARTY TRICK: Each crosses the sea using their
  own magical object (each "crossing" the sea differently)
  → Symbol of facing life's challenges each in one's own way
  → Their sea-crossing appears on countless decorative objects
    (fans, vases, embroideries) as a good-luck theme
```

## The Chinese Zodiac

```
THE TWELVE ANIMALS — The Jade Emperor's Race

Origin story (folk tradition): The Jade Emperor invited all animals
to a race; the first 12 to cross a river would become zodiac signs.

ORDER AND MYTH:
  1. Rat: hitched a ride on the Ox; jumped off at the last moment
  2. Ox: hardworking; almost won
  3. Tiger: strong swimmer but slowed by current
  4. Rabbit: hopped across on stepping stones; lucky
  5. Dragon: helped others (blew wind for the Rabbit; made rain);
      arrived fifth despite being able to fly → character before victory
  6. Snake: hid in Horse's hoof; startled him; came in sixth
  7. Horse: sixth (one version) or seventh after startled by Snake
  8. Goat/Ram: cooperated with Monkey and Rooster; arrived 8th
  9. Monkey: crossed on a raft; 9th
  10. Rooster: found the raft; 10th
  11. Dog: stopped to play in the water; late; 11th
  12. Pig: stopped to eat and sleep; last; 12th

The Cat was not invited (or was deceived by the Rat) — explains
why cats hunt rats

Each animal rules one year in a 12-year cycle; personality traits
attributed to those born in each year. Sixty-year cycle (5 elements
× 12 animals) is the full cycle in Chinese astrology.
```

## Amaterasu and Susanoo

```
AMATERASU (天照大神) — GODDESS OF THE SUN

The supreme deity of Shinto; ancestor of the imperial family.
Her full name: Amaterasu Ōmikami — "Great Divinity Illuminating Heaven"

AMATERASU AND SUSANOO — THE CONFLICT:

Susanoo (storm god) is sent to rule the seas but weeps endlessly
for his dead mother Izanami. Izanagi banishes him.
Before going, Susanoo visits Amaterasu to say farewell.
Amaterasu, suspicious, challenges him to a contest:
  — They exchange objects; each chews and spits them as beads
  — From Susanoo's sword: three female kami (Amaterasu claims
    them as hers, since they emerged from her brother's sword)
  — From Amaterasu's jewels: five male kami (Susanoo's)

Susanoo, "victorious," goes on a rampage:
  — Breaks down the rice paddy divisions (destroys agriculture)
  — Fills in irrigation ditches
  — Defiles Amaterasu's sacred weaving hall: throws a flayed horse
    through the roof; the shock kills one of Amaterasu's weavers
    (or Amaterasu herself is injured by her shuttle)

AMATERASU RETREATS TO THE CAVE (Ama-no-Iwato):
  She hides in the Cave of Heaven; the world goes dark
  All the eight million kami (yaoyorozu no kami) are alarmed
  The eight million kami hold a noisy party outside the cave:
    — Ame-no-Uzume dances on an overturned tub, her dance
      becoming bawdier and bawdier; the kami burst out laughing
    — Amaterasu hears the laughter; curious that anyone could
      be happy while she is hiding; peeks out
    — Another kami holds up a mirror (Yata no Kagami) to the crack
    — Amaterasu sees her own brilliant reflection; fascinated
    — A powerful kami pulls her out; a rope is stretched across
      the cave entrance to prevent re-entry
  → World restored; light returns

The mirror Yata no Kagami becomes one of the Three Sacred Imperial
Treasures of Japan (along with the Kusanagi sword and Yasakani jewels)
→ The regalia the Emperor holds today, housed in shrines
Susanoo is banished

SUSANOO'S REDEMPTION:
After banishment, Susanoo kills the eight-headed serpent Yamata no Orochi
  — Finds an old couple weeping; their daughters eaten one by one
  — Susanoo disguises a princess as a comb in his hair
  — Sets out eight vats of sake (rice wine); Orochi drinks all eight
    with each of its heads; falls unconscious
  — Susanoo cuts it apart; in its tail finds the sword Kusanagi
    (later one of the Three Sacred Treasures)
  — Marries the princess; settles in Izumo
→ Susanoo's character arc: violent and destructive → heroic slayer
```

## Kami: The Shinto Concept

```
KAMI (神) — THE SHINTO UNDERSTANDING OF THE SACRED

Kami are NOT "gods" in the Western sense. The word resists direct
translation. Key properties:

WHAT KAMI ARE:
  — Numinous presences in natural phenomena: a striking mountain,
    a twisted tree, a powerful waterfall, a white fox, a rock
    with unusual shape can all be kami
  — Ancestral spirits (including the Emperor's divine ancestors)
  — Abstract forces: the spirit of growth, musubi (creative force)
  — Named deities: Amaterasu, Susanoo, etc. — but these exist on
    a continuum with unnamed local kami, not in a separate category
  — "Eight million" kami (yaoyorozu) — "eight million" means
    innumerable, not a specific count

KEY CHARACTERISTICS:
  — Impure/pure polarity (kegare/harae): kami are associated with
    purity; death, blood, and certain bodily processes are kegare
    (ritual pollution); this is why shrines and hospitals are
    traditionally separated
  — Kami can be benevolent (nigimitama) or rough/violent (aramitama)
    — The same kami can have both aspects; different shrines may
      enshrine the aramitama and nigimitama separately
  — Kami are not omnipotent or omniscient; they are powerful,
    localized presences that respond to worship and care

SHRINES vs. TEMPLES:
  — Jinja (shrine) = Shinto; marked by torii gates
  — Tera/Ji (temple) = Buddhist
  — Japan historically practiced "shinbutsu shūgō" — the blending
    of Shinto and Buddhist practice, largely dissolved in 1868
    (Meiji-era forced separation)

MATSURI (festival):
  — The primary form of kami interaction
  — The kami is temporarily "invited" into a portable shrine (mikoshi)
    and paraded through the community — bringing blessing, checking
    on the people, accepting offerings
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Chinese mythology has no single authoritative text**: Unlike the Greek Theogony, there is no single canonical account of Chinese creation. The Shanhaijing (Classic of Mountains and Seas) is the most important source but is encyclopedic, not narrative. Different myths coexist without reconciliation — Pangu appears in 3rd-century CE texts, long after most "classic" mythology was written.

**The Jade Emperor is not ancient**: He became the supreme deity of popular Chinese religion in the Song Dynasty (~10th–13th c. CE), not in antiquity. He was essentially promoted by popular worship from a relatively minor deity. Earlier Chinese religion had no single supreme deity.

**Journey to the West is a 16th-century novel, not ancient myth**: Wu Cheng'en wrote it ~1592 CE. However, Sun Wukong has roots in Tang Dynasty stories (7th–10th c.) and the historical monk Xuanzang (602–664 CE) was real. The novel crystallized existing traditions into the canonical form.

**Amaterasu is the supreme deity — the Emperor's ancestor**: This theological-political point was fundamental to Japanese imperial ideology. The Emperor (Tennō = "heavenly sovereign") was the direct descendant of Amaterasu. This claim was central to State Shinto in the Meiji era (1868–1945) and was officially renounced by Emperor Hirohito on January 1, 1946 (the "Declaration of Humanity").

**Kami are not equivalent to gods**: The Western "god" concept implies a specific kind of personhood, moral interest in humanity, and theological position. Kami are numinous presences — some highly personified, some barely so. "Eight million kami" doesn't mean eight million individually named gods; it means sacred power is everywhere, in everything. The closest Western concept is perhaps mana (Polynesian) or pneuma (Greek), not "deity."

**Shinto and Buddhism coexisted for 1,200 years**: The Meiji government's forced separation (shimbutsu bunri, 1868) imposed an artificial divide on a tradition that had fluidly blended the two systems since the 6th century. Many "Shinto myths" absorbed Buddhist cosmology; many Buddhist temples incorporated kami worship. The separation we see today is partly a 19th-century political construction.
