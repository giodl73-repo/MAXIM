# Celtic & Arthurian Mythology

## The Big Picture

Celtic mythology survives almost entirely through manuscripts written by **Christian monks** in medieval Ireland (7th–12th c.) and Wales (11th–13th c.). No pre-Christian Celtic texts exist. The monks preserved the stories but filtered them through a Christian worldview, rationalizing or muting the explicitly religious content. Arthurian legend is a later layer: a medieval Christian chivalric tradition overlaid on a Celtic substrate.

## Irish Mythology — The Mythological Cycle

The Mythological Cycle tells the pre-history of Ireland through five successive invasions (Lebor Gabála Érenn, "Book of Invasions"):

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         FIVE INVASIONS OF IRELAND                                │
│                                                                  │
│  1. CESSAIR (granddaughter of Noah — obviously Christian overlay)│
│     Came before the Flood; all but one drowned                   │
│                                                                  │
│  2. PARTHOLONIANS                                                │
│     Cleared plains, dug lakes, established crafts                │
│     Wiped out by plague after 300 years                          │
│                                                                  │
│  3. NEMEDIANS                                                    │
│     Fought the Fomorians (primal chaos beings)                   │
│     Defeated and dispersed — descendants become Fir Bolg and     │
│     Tuatha Dé Danann                                             │
│                                                                  │
│  4. FIR BOLG                                                     │
│     Bags of earth; divided Ireland into provinces                │
│     Defeated at First Battle of Mag Tuired by Tuatha Dé Danann │
│                                                                  │
│  5. TUATHA DÉ DANANN                                             │
│     "The peoples/tribes of the goddess Danu"                     │
│     Divine race; brought four sacred treasures                   │
│     Fought two great battles:                                    │
│     — First Mag Tuired: vs. Fir Bolg (won)                     │
│     — Second Mag Tuired: vs. Fomorians (won)                   │
│     Then defeated by SONS OF MIL (Milesians = historical Irish)  │
│     → Retreat underground: become the Síde (fairy mounds)      │
│       Aos Sí / Sídhe                                             │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Tuatha Dé Danann

```
Principal divine figures of Irish mythology:

THE DAGDA — "The Good God"; Chief of the Tuatha
  — Great club: kills with one end, revives with other
  — Magic cauldron (one of four treasures): infinite food
  — Harp Uaithne: plays seasons into being
  — Father of many divine children

THE MORRIGAN — Triple goddess of war, fate, sovereignty
  — Three aspects: Badb (battle crow/scald crow), Macha (sovereignty/horses),
    Morrigan proper (or Nemain = frenzy)
  — Shape-shifts into crow, wolf, eel, heifer
  — Predicts and incites battle; washes the armor of those about to die

LUGH LÁMHFHADA — "Long-Arm" / god of all arts
  — Killed Balor of the Evil Eye (Fomorian leader) — his grandfather!
  — Spear (Gáe Dearg): one of four treasures
  — Harvest festival Lughnasadh (August 1) named for him

DIAN CÉCHT — Divine physician
  — Made silver hand for Nuada (King who lost hand in battle)
  — Note: a king must be physically perfect → silver hand restored status

MANANNÁN MAC LIR — God of the sea and the Otherworld
  — Rules Tír na nÓg (Land of the Young)
  — Magic items: horse Aonbharr (walks on sea), sword Fragarach ("The Answerer"),
    cloak of invisibility, pigs that revive when eaten

THE DAGDA'S CAULDRON, LUGH'S SPEAR, NUADA'S SWORD, LÍA FÁIL:
  Four sacred treasures brought from four mythical cities
  (Falias/Gorias/Finias/Murias)
```

## The Fomorians

```
The Fomorians are the primal opposition to the Tuatha Dé Danann:
  — Chaos, darkness, blight, destructive powers of nature
  — Associated with the sea and undersea
  — Balor of the Evil Eye: one eye kills all it looks upon
    → myth: Balor imprisoned his daughter (prophecy her son kills him)
    Cian sneaks in, they have Lugh → Lugh fulfills prophecy at Second Mag Tuired

The Fomorians are not entirely evil — they intermarry with Tuatha Dé
(Bres, a Tuatha king, is half-Fomorian; the boundary is permeable).
They represent what must be overcome but also what cannot be entirely defeated.
```

## The Ulster Cycle — CuChulainn

The Ulster Cycle is the Irish equivalent of the Iliad: warrior heroics, geasa (sacred prohibitions), and tragedy.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE TÁIN BÓ CÚAILNGE                          │
│           "The Cattle Raid of Cooley" — Irish national epic      │
│                                                                  │
│  CONTEXT: Queen Medb of Connacht argues with husband Ailill      │
│  over who has more wealth. He has the White-Horned Bull (Finnbennach)│
│  She wants the Brown Bull of Cooley (Donn Cúailnge, in Ulster)   │
│  → demands it → rebuffed → invades Ulster with vast army         │
│                                                                  │
│  PROBLEM FOR ULSTER:                                             │
│  Curse of Macha (wronged goddess): all Ulster warriors laid low  │
│  by labor pains ("cess") during the crisis — except CuChulainn   │
│  (not subject to the curse — semi-divine)                        │
│                                                                  │
│  CUCHULAINN alone defends Ulster:                                │
│  Fights single combat at fords (Irish convention) daily          │
│  Kills dozens of champions including his foster-brother Ferdiad  │
│  (the Ferdiad combat: 4 days, with mutual care at night)         │
│                                                                  │
│  RESOLUTION:                                                     │
│  Ulster warriors recover; Connacht retreats                      │
│  The two bulls fight; the Donn Cúailnge kills Finnbennach        │
│  then itself dies of exhaustion                                  │
│  → The quest was ultimately futile for everyone                  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### CuChulainn — Biography

```
BIRTH: Dechtine (sister of King Conchobar) + ? (possibly Lugh in disguise)
CHILDHOOD NAME: Sétanta
NAME CHANGE: Killed Culann the smith's fierce hound defending himself
  → offered to serve as Culann's hound until a replacement trained
  → "Cú Chulainn" = "the hound of Culann"

TRAINING: With warrior-woman Scáthach on Isle of Skye (Shadow's Land)
  — Learns martial arts including the Gáe Bulga (barbed spear thrown with foot)
  — Affair with Aoife (Scáthach's rival); they have son Connla

WIFE: Emer (won her against her father's obstacles)

GEIS (sacred prohibitions):
  — Must never refuse hospitality
  — Must never eat dog meat
  — → At death, enemies exploit these: he is offered dog meat by a crone
    (refusing would violate hospitality; accepting violates his geis on eating dog)
    His power diminishes; he is killed

RIASTRAD (battle frenzy / warp-spasm):
  CuChulainn transforms in battle — body contorts, one eye sinks,
  other bulges, heart beats like thunder, hero-halo appears over head
  Analogy: berserker in Norse tradition

DEATH: Enemies summon three Crones (fulfilling each geis in sequence)
  Weakened, impales himself upright on his own spear tied to a stone
  Dies standing, a crow landing on his shoulder showing he's dead
```

## The Fenian Cycle — Finn Mac Cumhaill

```
Later cycle; more romantic and fantastic than Ulster Cycle

FINN MAC CUMHAILL (Fionn mac Cumhaill):
  Leader of the Fianna (elite warrior band)

THUMB OF KNOWLEDGE:
  As a boy, Finn serves Finnegas the poet, who fishes the Salmon of Knowledge
  (the salmon ate the hazelnuts of wisdom that fell into the Well of Segais)
  Finnegas cooks the salmon — Finn burns his thumb touching it, sucks it
  → gains the salmon's wisdom; thereafter can access wisdom by sucking thumb

KEY STORIES:
  Pursuit of Diarmuid and Gráinne:
  — Finn's betrothed Gráinne falls for Diarmuid Ua Duibhne (has a love spot)
  — They elope; Finn pursues for years across Ireland
  — Diarmuid eventually killed by magic boar (Finn could save him with water
    from his hands but twice lets it fall before reaching Diarmuid)
  — Gráinne eventually returns to Finn (Stockholm syndrome or pragmatism)

OISÍN IN TÍR NA NÓG:
  — Finn's son Oisín ("little deer" — mother was deer-woman)
  — Taken by Niamh of the Golden Hair to the Otherworld (Tír na nÓg)
  — 300 years pass as if days in the Otherworld
  — Returns; touches Irish soil and becomes ancient immediately
  — Meets St. Patrick — creates framing for Fenian tales in Christianized form
```

## The Welsh Mabinogion — Four Branches

The Mabinogion (compiled ~1350–1410 CE, stories older) contains four mythological "branches":

```
FIRST BRANCH — PWYLL PRINCE OF DYFED
  Pwyll accidentally offends Arawn (king of Annwn/Otherworld)
  → spends a year ruling the Otherworld in Arawn's place
  Returns; marriage to RHIANNON (divine woman on white horse)
  Son Pryderi mysteriously disappears at birth; Rhiannon falsely accused
  Eventually found; Rhiannon's innocence proved

SECOND BRANCH — BRANWEN DAUGHTER OF LLŶR
  BRÂN THE BLESSED (giant king of Britain) gives his sister Branwen
  to king of Ireland; she is ill-treated
  → Brân invades Ireland with British army
  Catastrophic war: almost everyone killed on both sides
  Brân wounded by poisoned spear; commands his severed head be taken to London
  (his severed head continues to speak, feast, and provide counsel for years)
  The CAULDRON OF REBIRTH: warriors killed can be revived, but mute (can't be questioned in oath-taking)

THIRD BRANCH — MANAWYDAN SON OF LLŶR
  Enchantment falls on Dyfed — all people and animals vanish
  Manawydan and Pryderi survive; Pryderi and Rhiannon trapped in enchanted fort
  Manawydan outwits the enchanter (Bishop Llwyd) by threatening his wife
  transformed into a mouse

FOURTH BRANCH — MATH SON OF MATHONWY
  Most mythologically rich branch
  MATH requires his feet to be held by a virgin maiden except in war
  His footholder Goewin raped by Gilfaethwy; Math punishes the brothers
  by transforming them into paired animals three times
  LLEU LLAW GYFFES: born under three curse (his mother Arianrhod refuses
  to name him, arm him, or let him have a human wife)
  → Gwydion overcomes each curse through deception
  Lleu given Blodeuwedd (woman made from flowers) as wife
  She and lover Gronw Pebyr plot Lleu's death; Lleu becomes an eagle
  Gwydion finds and restores Lleu; Gronw killed; Blodeuwedd turned into owl
```

## The Druids

```
Druids were the sacred specialist class of Celtic society — a complex role combining:
  — Priests / ritual specialists
  — Judges (custodians of law and arbitration)
  — Poets / historians / oral tradition keepers (bardic sub-class)
  — Prophets and diviners
  — Philosophers / educators

WHAT WE KNOW (and don't):
  Primary sources: Julius Caesar (De Bello Gallico), Strabo, Pliny
  — Roman observations during conquest of Gaul; obviously biased
  Caesar: "They hold many views about the stars and their motion, about the
  magnitude of the world and the earth, about the nature of things"
  — 20-year training in oral tradition (deliberately no written tradition)
  — Human sacrifice reported by Romans (Wicker Man) — disputed by scholars
  — Sacred groves (nemeton) as ritual spaces
  — Mistletoe cutting (golden sickle, collected when oak-grown = powerful)

WHAT WE DON'T KNOW:
  The druids left no written records by design.
  Everything comes from hostile Roman observers or late Irish texts
  filtered through Christian transmission.
  Modern "druidry" is largely 18th-century romantic reconstruction (Iolo Morganwg).
```

### The Round Table — Key Characters

```
ARTHUR: Celtic/Roman warrior king of Britain; son of Uther Pendragon
        Conceived through Merlin's deception; raised in secret
        Pulls sword from stone (Excalibur) OR receives from Lady of the Lake

MERLIN: Wizard, prophet; Celtic seer archetype (cf. Irish Druid-prophet)
        Son of demon + holy woman (medieval rationalization)
        Adviser to Arthur; imprisoned by Nimue (Lady of the Lake)

GUINEVERE: Arthur's queen; daughter of King Leodegrance
           Love affair with Lancelot = catastrophic betrayal

LANCELOT DU LAC: Greatest knight; raised by Lady of the Lake (French invention)
                 Only fails Grail quest because of adultery with Guinevere

GAWAIN: Arthur's nephew; honor-focused warrior; "Sir Gawain and the Green Knight"
        (14th c. poem: tests of courage, honesty, and chivalric virtue)

PERCEVAL/PARSIFAL: The Grail knight — pure fool; Grail question ("Whom does it serve?")

MORDRED: Arthur's son (by his sister Morgan le Fay or half-sister Morgause in Malory)
         Betrays and kills Arthur at Camlann; Arthur kills Mordred

THE HOLY GRAIL: Cup of the Last Supper (Christian overlay on pagan vessel/cauldron)
               Sustains the Fisher King (wounded ruler = blighted land)
               Only a pure knight can ask the Grail question and heal the wasteland
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Celtic" is a language family, not an ethnic or cultural monolith**: Irish, Welsh, Breton, Cornish, Scottish Gaelic, Manx, and Gaulish are all Celtic languages. Their mythologies share elements but are not identical — Welsh and Irish traditions differ considerably.

**No pre-Christian Celtic writing survives**: All Celtic myths were preserved by Christian monks who adapted them. What looks pagan often has a Christian frame. The "druids" as described by Julius Caesar are a Roman colonial perspective — potentially accurate, certainly incomplete, possibly distorted for propaganda.

**Arthur is not definitively historical**: There is no contemporary 5th-century source naming Arthur as a historical person. He may be based on a real leader, a composite of leaders, or may be entirely legendary. Modern DNA analysis of early medieval British remains hasn't resolved this.

**Merlin is not Celtic in origin**: The Merlin of Arthurian legend is Geoffrey of Monmouth's creation (1138), a merger of two figures: Myrddin Wyllt (a Welsh madman-prophet) and Ambrosius (a Romano-British figure). The wizard-adviser Merlin is essentially a 12th-century literary creation.

**Geis (geasa)**: The magical taboos that bind Irish heroes. Violating a geis weakens or destroys the hero — the setup for nearly every hero's tragic death. CuChulainn's death is engineered by setting up conflicting geasa. This is a distinctly Irish mythological mechanism with no exact Greek or Norse parallel.
