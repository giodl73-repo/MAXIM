# Norse Mythology

## The Big Picture

Norse mythology is the pre-Christian religion of the Germanic peoples of Scandinavia. Its primary sources are two Icelandic texts from ~1220 CE: the **Prose Edda** (Snorri Sturluson) and the **Poetic Edda** (anonymous compilation). Both were written by Christians 200+ years after Norway/Iceland converted (~1000 CE), which colors their content.

## Creation — Ginnungagap to Midgard

```
IN THE BEGINNING:
  Ginnungagap: the vast void between Niflheim (cold) and Muspelheim (fire)
  Ice from Niflheim meets fire from Muspelheim → melt → first being

PRIMORDIAL BEINGS:
  Ymir: first frost giant (jötunn), born from the melt
    — Fed by Auðumbla (primordial cow) licking salty ice blocks
    — Auðumbla licks free Búri (first god) from ice

  Búri → son Borr → (with giantess Bestla) → three sons:
    ODIN, VILI, VÉ

CREATION OF THE WORLD:
  Odin, Vili, Vé kill Ymir
  Ymir's blood drowns all frost giants (except Bergelmir and wife — proto-flood)
  From Ymir's body:
    Flesh → Earth (Midgard)
    Blood + sweat → seas, lakes
    Skull → sky (four dwarves hold up the corners: Austri/Vestri/Norðri/Suðri)
    Brains → clouds
    Eyebrows → the wall of Midgard (protecting against Jotunheim)
    Bones → mountains
    Hair → trees

CREATION OF HUMANS:
  Odin, Hœnir, Lóðurr (? = Loki?) find Ask (ash) and Embla (elm) — the first people
  Gifts: Odin = breath/life, Hœnir = sense/motion, Lóðurr = blood/color
```

### The Vanir

```
The Vanir are a separate family of gods associated with fertility,
wisdom, and magic. After the Aesir-Vanir War (first war), a peace
treaty leads to an exchange of hostages:

Aesir send: Hœnir + Mimir to Vanir
Vanir send: Njörðr + Freyr + Freyja to Aesir

Vanir hostages become fully integrated into Asgard.
The Aesir hostages work out badly — Mimir is beheaded (Odin
preserves his head with herbs and consults it for wisdom).

Main Vanir: Njörðr (sea, winds, fishing), Freyr, Freyja
```

## The Norns — Fate

```
THREE NORNS weave fate at Urðarbrunnr (Well of Urð):

Urð (Wyrd)    — What was (past; cognate with English "weird" = fate)
Verðandi      — What is (present; becoming)
Skuld         — What shall be (future/obligation)

"What the Norns decree cannot be undone — not even by the gods."
Odin himself is fated to die at Ragnarok.

Other norns (lesser) attend every birth, weaving individual fates.

Analogy: Greek Moirai (Clotho spins, Lachesis measures, Atropos cuts)
— parallel structure suggests PIE origin of fate-weaving goddesses
```

## Ragnarok — The Twilight of the Gods

```
TRIGGERS:
1. Baldur's death (set in motion by Loki)
2. Loki bound with entrails of his son (his writhing = earthquakes)
3. Fimbulwinter: 3 winters without summer, world wars, family murder
4. Signs: Heimdall blows Gjallarhorn, Yggdrasil shakes

THE BATTLE:
  Naglfar (ship made of dead men's nails) sails from Hel with Loki
  Jormungandr rises from ocean depths
  Fenrir breaks free from bonds (Gleipnir)
  Surtr leads fire giants from Muspelheim
  Aesir and Einherjar (Valhalla warriors) ride from Asgard

KEY BATTLES:
  Odin vs. Fenrir: Fenrir swallows Odin → Víðarr (Odin's son) kills Fenrir
  Thor vs. Jörmungandr: both kill each other (Thor 9 steps then falls)
  Freyr vs. Surtr: Freyr has no sword (gave away); Surtr kills him
  Tyr vs. Garm (Hel's hound): both kill each other
  Heimdall vs. Loki: both kill each other

AFTERMATH — RENEWAL:
  Surtr burns the nine worlds
  World sinks into sea
  Earth rises again, green and fertile
  Survivors: Víðarr, Váli, Móði+Magni (Thor's sons), Höðr+Baldur return
    (from Hel), Lif and Lífþrasir (human pair) survive in Hoddmímis wood
  A new hall rises: Gimlé

"Then shall the golden tables gleam in the grass
 which the gods once owned in the olden days."
                          — Völuspá stanza 61
```

## The Edda Sources

```
POETIC EDDA (Elder Edda):
  Anonymous compilation, ~1270 CE (Codex Regius manuscript)
  Eddic poetry: older style, alliterative, often dramatic monologue
  Key poems:
  Völuspá — the seeress's prophecy (creation → Ragnarok)
  Hávamál — Odin's wisdom sayings, rune poem
  Vafþrúðnismál — Odin questions the giant Vafþrúðnir
  Grímnismál — Odin describes the Nine Worlds
  Skírnismál — Freyr's wooing of Gerðr
  Hymiskviða — Thor and the brewing cauldron
  Lokasenna — Loki insults all the gods at a feast

PROSE EDDA (Younger Edda):
  Written by Snorri Sturluson (~1220 CE), Icelandic statesman/poet
  Three sections:
  — Gylfaginning: cosmology and myths (questions of King Gylfi)
  — Skáldskaparmál: poetry and kennings
  — Háttatal: metrics
  Snorri was a Christian writing about paganism as history/literature
  His "Euhemeristic" framing (treating gods as ancient humans who were
  deified) was a Christian rationalization — take with skepticism
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Loki is NOT simply "evil"**: Loki is the Aesir's problem-solver for most of the myths — he regularly gets them out of trouble as readily as he causes it. The transition to unambiguous antagonist only solidifies after Baldur's death, when he refuses to weep and is subsequently bound.

**Valkyries choose the slain, not the victorious**: Valkyries select warriors who die in battle to go to Valhalla — they don't determine who wins battles (though Odin does grant victory through them as agents).

**Valhalla is not for everyone**: Only warriors slain in battle qualify for Valhalla. Those who die of disease, old age, drowning, or non-combat causes go to Hel (which is not equivalent to Hell — it's grey and neutral, not a place of punishment). Freyja claims half the battle-slain for Fólkvangr.

**The "Norns" are not the "Fates" exactly**: The Greek Moirai spin a thread that can be cut (ending life). The Norse Norns carve or weave fate; the metaphor is different and arguably the Norse conception is less metaphysically deterministic. But the parallel structure (three female fate-weavers) almost certainly reflects a common PIE origin.

**Snorri euhemerizes**: Snorri's Prose Edda frames the Aesir as ancient Asian warriors from Troy who traveled to Scandinavia and were worshipped as gods. This is a medieval Christian rationalization (euhemerism: gods = deified humans), not an authentic Norse belief about their own religion. Ignore this framing for the mythology itself.
