# Abrahamic Mythology: Comparative Analysis

## The Big Picture

The Abrahamic traditions (Judaism, Christianity, Islam) are not usually framed as "mythology" within their own communities — they are living religions with billions of adherents who regard their foundational narratives as historical and sacred. This guide uses "mythology" in the academic sense: narrative traditions that encode cosmology, ethics, and communal identity, without making claims about their truth. The comparative mythology framework is especially productive here because the three traditions share a common ancestor but diverged significantly, and all three show clear parallels with and borrowings from the broader ancient Near Eastern mythological world.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           ABRAHAMIC TRADITIONS — OVERVIEW                        │
│                                                                  │
│  SHARED FOUNDATION:                                              │
```

## Genesis: Two Creation Accounts

The book of Genesis contains TWO distinct creation accounts, written at different times and with different theological concerns:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  TWO CREATION ACCOUNTS IN GENESIS                │
│                                                                  │
│  ACCOUNT 1 — Genesis 1:1–2:3 ("P" or Priestly source)            │
│  Composition: Probably 6th century BCE (Babylonian exile period) │
│                                                                  │
│  STRUCTURE: Six days of creation + Sabbath rest                  │
│  Day 1: Light and dark (sky and earth not yet made)              │
│  Day 2: Firmament (the dome separating upper/lower waters)       │
│  Day 3: Dry land + vegetation                                    │
│  Day 4: Sun, moon, stars (after vegetation! — anti-astrology)    │
│  Day 5: Sea creatures + birds                                    │
│  Day 6: Land animals + humanity (LAST created)                   │
│  Day 7: God rests → sanctifies the Sabbath                       │
│                                                                  │
│  KEY FEATURES:                                                   │
│  — Orderly, formulaic, majestic: "And God said... and it was so" │
│  — Humanity created in God's "image" (tselem) and "likeness"     │
│  — Male AND female created simultaneously in God's image         │
│  — Hebrew "Elohim" (plural form used for God) — grammatically    │
│    plural, theologically singular → possibly a vestige of the    │
│    pre-monotheistic divine assembly                              │
│  — The firmament (raqia) is a solid dome holding back upper      │
│    waters — ancient Near Eastern cosmological model              │
│                                                                  │
│  NEAR EASTERN PARALLEL:                                          │
│  — Enuma Elish: Tiamat killed; body split into sky and earth     │
│  — Genesis 1: The "deep" (tehom) over which God's spirit moves   │
│    — "tehom" and "Tiamat" are cognates (both from the same       │
│       Semitic root, both referring to the primordial waters)     │
│  — Genesis 1 is a deliberate theological revision of Enuma Elish │
│    The Babylonian model: creation through violence and divine    │
│    combat. The Israelite response during the Exile: creation     │
│    through sovereign divine speech alone, no conflict needed.    │
│                                                                  │
│  ACCOUNT 2 — Genesis 2:4–3:24 ("J" or Yahwist source)            │
│  Composition: Probably 10th–9th century BCE (earlier than P)     │
│  God's name: YHWH Elohim (Yahweh) rather than Elohim alone       │
│                                                                  │
│  ORDER IS DIFFERENT:                                             │
│  — Earth and sky created → but no rain, no humans                │
│  — God forms the man (adam) from earth (adamah) → breathes       │
│    life (nishmat chayyim) into his nostrils                      │
│  — God plants a garden (Eden); places the man there              │
│  — God creates animals and brings them to the man to be named    │
│  — No suitable partner found among animals                       │
│  — God creates the woman from the man's rib (tsela — also        │
│    translatable as "side")                                       │
│                                                                  │
│  KEY FEATURES:                                                   │
│  — Intimate, anthropomorphic God: walks in the garden; asks      │
│    "Where are you?"                                              │
│  — The man is created FIRST (vs. Account 1: simultaneous)        │
│  — Animals created AFTER the man (vs. Account 1: before)         │
│  — Two trees: Tree of Life + Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil  │
│  — The serpent (nachash) — not called Satan; it is simply a      │
│    crafty beast                                                  │
│                                                                  │
│  THE FALL NARRATIVE:                                             │
│  Eve → serpent's suggestion → eats from tree of knowledge        │
│  Adam → Eve → eats                                               │
│  Result: knowledge (they see they are naked); shame              │
│  God discovers this by walking in the garden                     │
│  Punishments: serpent cursed (crawls, enmity with humans);       │
│    woman: pain in childbirth, subordination; man: labor, death   │
│  "By the sweat of your face you shall eat bread, until you       │
│   return to the ground, for out of it you were taken"            │
│  Expulsion from Eden → guarded by cherubim with flaming swords   │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Divine Council and Ancient Israelite Religion

One of the most significant findings of modern biblical scholarship:

```
THE DIVINE COUNCIL — ISRAELITE RELIGION WAS NOT ALWAYS MONOTHEIST

The Tanakh contains traces of an earlier Israelite religion in which
YHWH was one of many divine beings — the supreme deity of a divine
assembly, but not the only deity:

TEXTUAL EVIDENCE:
  — Genesis 1: "Elohim" (plural; "Let us make man in our image")
  — Genesis 3: "the man has become like one of US, knowing good and evil"
  — Genesis 6: "sons of God" (bene elohim) marry human women;
    produce the Nephilim (giants) → Pre-flood divine-human mixing
  — Psalm 82: "God [Elohim] stands in the divine assembly [adat-el];
    he judges among the gods [elohim]" — God rebukes the other
    gods for failing to administer justice; strips them of divinity
  — Deuteronomy 32:8–9: "When the Most High (Elyon) apportioned
    nations... he fixed borders according to the number of the sons
    of God. But YHWH's portion is his people, Jacob his allotment"
    → The Most High divides nations among divine beings; YHWH gets
       Israel as his portion. Implies YHWH is not the Most High.
       (This verse is clearer in the Dead Sea Scrolls version than
        in the later Masoretic text, which was altered)

THE DEVELOPMENTAL MODEL (scholarly consensus):
  Stage 1: Pre-Israelite Canaanite polytheism
  Stage 2: Monolatry — worship YHWH exclusively but other gods exist
           (earliest form of Israelite religion; the First Commandment
           "you shall have no other gods before me" assumes other gods
           exist but commands exclusive devotion to YHWH)
  Stage 3: Monotheism — other gods do not exist or are powerless
           (develops primarily during the Babylonian exile, 6th c. BCE;
           Second Isaiah: "I am the first and I am the last; besides
           me there is no god" — Isaiah 44:6)
  Stage 4: Full monotheism with subordinate supernatural beings
           (angels and demons replace the old divine council)

THE CANAANITE CONNECTIONS:
  — El (the head of the Canaanite pantheon) and YHWH were possibly
    originally distinct deities who merged: El gives his name;
    YHWH gives his distinctive personality
  — Asherah (El's consort; fertility goddess) may have been worshipped
    alongside YHWH in early Israelite religion
    — Inscriptions at Kuntillet Ajrud (~800 BCE): blessings invoked
      "by YHWH of Samaria and his Asherah"
    — Repeated prophetic condemnations of Asherah poles in the temple
      imply these were common practice
  — Baal (storm deity) worshipped alongside YHWH in some periods;
    the prophetic condemnations of "going after Baal" are relentless
```

## Islamic Mythology: Djinn, the Seven Heavens, and Iblis

```
DJINN (JINN) — A THIRD ORDER OF BEING

In Islamic theology and pre-Islamic Arabian tradition:
  — Three orders: Angels (from light), Humans (from clay), Djinn
    (from "smokeless fire" or "the tip of fire")
  — Djinn are not simply "evil spirits" — they are a complete
    civilization with free will, prophets, judgment, and diversity
  — The Quran chapter 72 is named "Al-Jinn" — a group of djinn
    heard Muhammad reciting the Quran; converted to Islam
  — Djinn can be Muslim or kafir (unbeliever), good or evil
  — They share the physical world with humans but are generally
    invisible

TYPES (in folk tradition, not strictly Quranic):
  — Jinn: the generic term; also the weakest class
  — Ifrit (Afreet): powerful, dangerous; often connected with
    fire; the Quran mentions one ifrit offering to bring Solomon
    the Queen of Sheba's throne instantly
  — Marid: associated with water; powerful; associated with the
    sea; the classic "genie in a lamp" (Arabian Nights tradition)
  — Si'la: shapeshifting djinn
  — Ghul: haunt deserts and graveyards; the origin of "ghoul"

SOLOMON AND THE DJINN:
  — Solomon (Suleiman) in Islamic tradition: commanded the djinn
    by a special ring (seal of Solomon) given by God
  — They built temples for him, transported things magically
  — Quranic: "among his forces were djinn and humans and birds"
  — The Arabian Nights "genie" tradition descends from this

THE SEVEN HEAVENS (As-Samawat):
  Quranic cosmology: seven heavens stacked above each other
  In hadith tradition, associated with specific prophets and content:
  1st Heaven: Adam (Moon)
  2nd Heaven: Jesus and John the Baptist (Mercury)
  3rd Heaven: Joseph (Venus)
  4th Heaven: Idris/Enoch (Sun)
  5th Heaven: Aaron (Mars)
  6th Heaven: Moses (Jupiter)
  7th Heaven: Abraham (Saturn)
  → At the highest point: the Divine Throne (Arsh)
  → The "Lote Tree of the Utmost Boundary" (Sidrat al-Muntaha):
    the tree at the edge of what even Gabriel can know; beyond it
    is only God — this is where Muhammad went on the Night Journey

THE NIGHT JOURNEY (Isra and Mi'raj):
  — Muhammad transported from Mecca to Jerusalem in one night
    on the Buraq (winged horse-like creature)
  — Ascended through the seven heavens; met the prophets at each
  — Reached the presence of God; received the commandment of
    50 daily prayers (Moses bargained it down to 5)
  — Central to Islamic cosmology; the event commemorated as
    Laylat al-Miraj
```

## Comparative Eschatology

```
END-TIMES ACROSS THE ABRAHAMIC TRADITIONS

JEWISH ESCHATOLOGY:
  — The Messianic Age (Olam Ha-Ba): a future era of universal peace
    and justice, brought by the Messiah (anointed king of Davidic
    lineage); the Temple rebuilt; exile ended
  — Resurrection of the dead (techiyat ha-meitim): the righteous dead
    resurrected in the Messianic Age
  — No well-defined "hell" in the Tanakh; Gehenna (originally a
    valley outside Jerusalem where child sacrifice happened) becomes
    a purgatorial concept in later Jewish thought
  — The World to Come (Olam Ha-Ba) as spiritual realm

CHRISTIAN ESCHATOLOGY (varies enormously by tradition):
  — Second Coming of Christ (Parousia)
  — Final Judgment: the living and the dead judged
  — Heaven (eternity with God) and Hell (eternity separated from God)
  — Revelation/Apocalypse: the seven seals, trumpets, bowls;
    the Beast (666), Antichrist, the Great Whore of Babylon,
    Armageddon, the Millennium (1,000-year reign of Christ),
    the New Jerusalem descending from heaven
  — Significant diversity: Catholic purgatory; Protestant
    "already/not yet" tension; premillennial/postmillennial/
    amillennial debates; rapture doctrine (specifically modern:
    Darby, 1830)

ISLAMIC ESCHATOLOGY (Yawm al-Qiyama — Day of Resurrection):
  SIGNS OF THE HOUR:
  Minor signs: decline of morals, wars, etc. (many ongoing)
  Major signs (Ten portents in hadith tradition):
    — Dajjal (the Antichrist): a one-eyed deceiver
    — Return of Jesus ('Isa): descends in Damascus; defeats Dajjal;
      breaks the cross (corrects Christian misinterpretation);
      dies naturally; is buried next to Muhammad in Medina
    — Gog and Magog (Ya'juj and Ma'juj): destructive nations
      currently held back by a barrier; released at the end
    — The smoke; the sun rising in the West; the Beast of the Earth;
      three great landslides; the fire

  AFTER DEATH (intermediate state):
  — Barzakh: the grave / intermediate state between death and
    resurrection; the Munkar and Nakir (angels) question the dead
  — Sirāt: the bridge over hell that all must cross; the righteous
    pass quickly; the wicked fall into Jahannam (hell)
  — Janna (paradise): seven levels; the highest is Firdaws
    — The houris (companions of paradise): a subject of much
      scholarly and popular discussion; "74 virgins" is not
      the actual hadith number; the theology is complex
  — Jahannam (hell): seven levels; fire; described in graphic detail
    — For Muslims: possibly a purgatorial purification; exit possible
    — For kafiroon (unbelievers): permanent (Quran is not uniformly
      clear on this; theologians debate)
```

## Near Eastern Flood Parallels

```
THE FLOOD IN COMPARATIVE MYTHOLOGY

The flood narrative appears across the ancient world with startling
consistency — almost certainly because multiple traditions drew on the
same Mesopotamian literary tradition:

TEXT              DATE          FLOOD HERO   DURATION   BIRDS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Atrahasis Epic    ~1700 BCE     Atrahasis    7 days     Not specified
Sumerian flood    ~2100 BCE     Ziusudra     7 days     Not specified
Gilgamesh XI      ~1300 BCE     Utnapishtim  7 days     Dove, swallow,
                                                          raven
Genesis 6-9 (J)   ~950 BCE      Noah         40 days    Raven, dove ×3
Genesis 6-9 (P)   ~550 BCE      Noah         150 days   Same
Berossus          ~300 BCE      Xisuthros    —          Three doves
Ogyges (Greek)    —             Ogyges       —          Regional flood
Deucalion (Greek) —             Deucalion    9 days     Not quite same
                                                          structure
Hindu (Satapatha) ~800 BCE?     Manu         —          A great fish

WHAT THE PARALLELS TELL US:
  — The Mesopotamian tradition is the oldest written form
  — Genesis is a theological transformation, not independent origin
  — The J source (older, ~950 BCE) and P source (6th c. BCE, exile)
    were woven together, creating apparent contradictions:
    J: 40 days of rain; birds sent 7 days apart
    P: 150 days of flood; the ark rests 7 months after the waters
  — The Priestly author, writing during the Babylonian Exile, wrote
    knowing the Babylonian flood myths intimately (this was their
    context) and deliberately reframed them: the cause is moral
    (human violence), not noise; the outcome is covenant (not
    ad-hoc immortality for one family); the God is just, not
    frightened by the flood he himself caused
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Genesis doesn't say Satan tempted Eve**: The serpent in Genesis 3 is "crafty" (arum) — not identified as Satan, Lucifer, or the devil. It is a beast. The identification of the serpent with Satan is a later interpretive tradition, developed in Second Temple Jewish texts (Wisdom of Solomon, 2nd c. BCE; 2 Enoch) and consolidated in Christian interpretation. The Tanakh text itself makes no such identification.

**"Lucifer" appears exactly once in the entire Bible**: Isaiah 14:12 — "How you have fallen from heaven, O morning star (helel ben-shachar), son of the dawn!" — is a taunt against the king of Babylon, not a cosmological account of Satan's fall. "Lucifer" (light-bringer) is Jerome's Latin translation of "morning star." Its conflation with Satan is a later theological development, not the biblical text's own claim.

**Kabbalah's Tikkun Olam is not synonymous with social justice**: In Lurianic Kabbalah (16th c.), Tikkun Olam refers specifically to the mystical act of gathering scattered divine sparks through prayer, ritual, and ethical life to restore cosmic wholeness after the Breaking of the Vessels. The modern usage (broadly, social activism / repairing the world) is a 20th-century reinterpretation that substantially departs from the original mystical-cosmological meaning.

**Islamic Jesus is not the same as Christian Jesus**: In Islamic theology, 'Isa (Jesus) is a prophet and the Messiah, born of a virgin, who performed miracles and was raised to heaven without dying on the cross (the crucifixion was an illusion or a substitute). He will return at the end of times. He is emphatically not divine and not the Son of God in the Christian sense.

**The biblical flood narrative is not one story — it's two edited together**: The Documentary Hypothesis (Wellhausen, 1878) identifies at least two strands (J and P) woven through Genesis. The contradictions in the flood story (40 days vs. 150 days of rain; one pair of each animal vs. seven pairs of clean animals; God says "YHWH" in J, "Elohim" in P) are evidence of editorial combination, not inconsistency in a single author.

**Djinn are not simply "genies" or demons**: The "genie in a lamp" (Marid; marids are associated with the sea and captivity) is specifically the Arabian Nights literary tradition (1001 Nights, compiled 8th–18th c., not a religious text). In Quranic theology, djinn are a third order of rational, free-willed beings — some are Muslim, some not — who will be judged alongside humans on the Day of Resurrection.
