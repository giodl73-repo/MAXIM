# Hindu Mythology

## The Big Picture

Hindu mythology is not a single tradition — it is the accumulated theological production of one of the world's oldest continuous civilizations, spanning roughly 3,500 years from the Rigveda (~1500 BCE) to the living tradition today. It is encyclopedic: 18 major Puranas, two massive epics (the Mahabharata alone is 10× the Iliad and Odyssey combined), countless Upanishads and Tantric texts.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                HINDU MYTHOLOGY — TEXTUAL LAYERS                   │
│                                                                  │
│  SHRUTI ("heard" — revealed, not authored):                      │
│  — Rigveda (~1500–1200 BCE): 1,028 hymns; oldest Sanskrit text  │
│  — Samaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda (other three Vedas)         │
│  — Upanishads (800–200 BCE): philosophical commentary on Vedas  │
│                                                                  │
│  SMRITI ("remembered" — composed, not revealed):                 │
│  — Mahabharata (~400 BCE–400 CE compiled): 1.8 million words    │
│    Includes Bhagavad Gita (Chapters 25–42 of Book 6)           │
│  — Ramayana (~500–100 BCE): 24,000 verses; Valmiki version      │
│  — Puranas (3rd–18th c. CE): 18 major, 18 minor                │
│    Vishnu Purana, Shiva Purana, Bhagavata Purana, etc.          │
│  — Dharma Shastras: Manu's Laws, etc.                           │
│                                                                  │
│  AGAMAS / TANTRAS:                                               │
│  — Sectarian texts (Shaiva, Vaishnava, Shakta)                  │
│  — Ritual and meditative instruction                            │
│                                                                  │
│  CRITICAL NOTE ON INTERPRETATION:                                │
│  There is NO single "Hindu mythology" — Shaivites, Vaishnavites, │
│  and Shaktas have radically different theologies. Brahma who     │
│  creates in one text is humbled or absent in others. Vishnu is  │
│  supreme deity or subordinate depending on sect. These are      │
│  living, contested traditions, not a fixed canon.               │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Vedic Pantheon (Early Layer)

The oldest layer of Hindu mythology features devas (gods) strongly tied to natural forces:

```
THE MAJOR VEDIC DEITIES:

INDRA — King of the Devas; storm, thunder, war, rain
  — Most hymns in the Rigveda are addressed to Indra
  — Wields the vajra (thunderbolt); mounted on the elephant Airavata
  — Slays Vrtra, the cosmic serpent blocking the waters
    → Waters released → rivers flow → fertility restored
  — Parallels: Norse Thor, Greek Zeus (PIE *dyḗus pəter → Zeus/Dyaus Pita)
  — Later: demoted to regional deity as Vishnu and Shiva rise

VARUNA — Cosmic order (Rita), oaths, water, sky
  — With Mitra: guardians of Rita (cosmic/moral order)
  — Omniscient; witnesses all oaths and punishes their violation
  — More ethically developed than Indra; the "conscience" of Vedic gods
  — Possibly cognate with Greek Ouranos (sky)

AGNI — Fire (sacrificial fire, domestic fire, lightning)
  — Second-most hymned Vedic deity
  — Mediator between humans and gods: fire carries sacrifice upward
  — Three forms: fire on earth, lightning in sky, sun in heaven
  — Every Hindu ritual still involves fire (agni)

SOMA — Ritual drink; plant deity; the moon
  — Soma = the drink = the plant = the deity = the moon (all identified)
  — The Rigveda's Book 9 is entirely devoted to Soma
  — Ritual drink pressed from an unidentified plant; possibly Ephedra,
    fly agaric, or Syrian rue — still debated
  — Grants immortality, truth-speech, poetic inspiration

SURYA — Sun
USHAS — Dawn (personified; the most beautiful Vedic poems)
VAYU — Wind
YAMA — First human to die; king of the dead
RUDRA — Storm; ancestor of Shiva; wild, dangerous, healing
```

## The Trimurti: Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva

Post-Vedic synthesis produces the Trimurti — three aspects of the divine managing cosmic functions:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE TRIMURTI                                    │
│                                                                  │
│  BRAHMA — The Creator                                            │
│  — Creates the universe at the beginning of each cosmic cycle   │
│  — Four heads (originally five; fifth burnt off by Shiva)       │
│  — Four Vedas emerged from his four mouths                      │
│  — Consort: Saraswati (knowledge, arts, music)                  │
│  — Almost no active worship (few temples); creator-role does    │
│    not sustain ongoing devotion. His work is done at creation.  │
│                                                                  │
│  VISHNU — The Preserver                                          │
│  — Maintains and sustains creation through cosmic cycles        │
│  — Descends to earth in avatars when dharma is threatened       │
│  — Rests on the cosmic serpent Shesha (Ananta) on the milky    │
│    ocean (Kshira Sagara) between creations                      │
│  — Consort: Lakshmi (wealth, fortune, beauty)                   │
│  — Attributes: conch (shankha), discus (Sudarshana Chakra),    │
│    mace (Kaumodaki), lotus                                      │
│  — Color: dark blue-black                                       │
│                                                                  │
│  SHIVA — The Destroyer/Transformer                               │
│  — Destroys at end of each cosmic cycle; makes room for new     │
│    creation; destruction = liberation                            │
│  — Simultaneously: ascetic (meditates on Mount Kailash),        │
│    householder (married to Parvati/Shakti), cosmic dancer       │
│    (Nataraja — dancing destroys and recreates the universe)     │
│  — Attributes: trident (trishula), drum (damaru), third eye     │
│    (destroys with fire when opened), crescent moon in hair,    │
│    Ganga flows from his matted locks, snake Vasuki on neck      │
│  — Lingam: phallic column = his primary form of worship         │
│  — Consort: Parvati (and her fierce forms: Durga, Kali)        │
│  — Sons: Ganesha (elephant head; remover of obstacles),         │
│           Kartikeya/Murugan (war god, peacock rider)            │
│                                                                  │
│  NOTE: The "Trimurti as Trinity" parallel to Christianity is     │
│  a popular comparison but misleading. Brahma is barely          │
│  worshipped; Vishnu and Shiva are rivals, not members of a      │
│  unified theology. Most Hindus are Vaishnavites or Shaivites    │
│  — devotees of one supreme deity who encompasses all others.    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Vishnu's Dashavatara: The Ten Avatars

Avatar (avatāra) = descent; Vishnu descends in bodily form to restore dharma:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  DASHAVATARA (TEN AVATARS OF VISHNU)              │
│                                                                  │
│  1. MATSYA (Fish)                                                │
│     — Saves Manu (Hindu Noah) and the Vedas from the great flood│
│     — Pulls Manu's boat to safety; destroys the demon Shankhasura│
│                                                                  │
│  2. KURMA (Tortoise)                                             │
│     — Samudra Manthan (Churning of the Ocean of Milk)           │
│     — Supports Mount Mandara as pivot for the churning          │
│     — Ocean churned by gods and asuras; produces amrita         │
│       (immortality nectar), Lakshmi, poison Halahala,           │
│       Dhanvantari (divine physician), divine horse, etc.        │
│                                                                  │
│  3. VARAHA (Boar)                                               │
│     — Demon Hiranyaksha pushes Earth (Bhudevi) to bottom of sea │
│     — Varaha dives in, tusks the earth, lifts it back up        │
│                                                                  │
│  4. NARASIMHA (Man-Lion)                                         │
│     — Demon Hiranyakashipu has boon: cannot be killed by man     │
│       or beast, by day or night, inside or outside, on earth    │
│       or in sky, by any weapon                                  │
│     — Narasimha: neither man nor beast; at dusk (neither day   │
│       nor night); on a threshold (neither inside nor outside);  │
│       on his lap (neither earth nor sky); with claws            │
│       (not a weapon) — every condition of the boon exploited    │
│     — Devotee Prahlada protected; tyrant father destroyed       │
│                                                                  │
│  5. VAMANA (Dwarf)                                               │
│     — Demon king Mahabali rules the three worlds justly but     │
│       has displaced the gods. Vishnu appears as a dwarf brahmin │
│     — Asks for three steps of land; Mahabali grants it          │
│     — Vamana becomes gigantic Trivikrama (three strides):       │
│       First step = earth; second = sky; third = Mahabali's head │
│       Mahabali pushed to the underworld; his humility honored   │
│                                                                  │
│  6. PARASHURAMA (Rama with Axe)                                 │
│     — Brahmin warrior who destroys the warrior (Kshatriya)      │
│       caste 21 times to avenge his father's murder              │
│     — Still considered alive ("Chiranjeevi" = immortal)         │
│                                                                  │
│  7. RAMA — Hero of the Ramayana                                 │
│     — Model king, dutiful son, devoted husband                  │
│     — Rescues Sita from Ravana with help of Hanuman             │
│     — The "maryada purushottama" — the ideal man               │
│                                                                  │
│  8. KRISHNA — Hero of the Mahabharata                           │
│     — Most complex avatar: divine trickster, lover, warrior,    │
│       cosmic deity. Bhagavad Gita = his teaching.               │
│                                                                  │
│  9. BUDDHA — Gautama Buddha (Vaishnava absorption of Buddhism)  │
│     — Appeared to lead demons/bad souls astray from Vedic truth │
│     — A theological move: by incorporating the Buddha as a      │
│       Vishnu avatar, Vaishnavism absorbs Buddhist devotees      │
│                                                                  │
│  10. KALKI — The Future Avatar                                   │
│     — Will appear at the end of the Kali Yuga on a white horse  │
│     — Destroys evil and corruption; ends the current cycle      │
│     — Has not yet come                                          │
│                                                                  │
│  EVOLUTIONARY BIOLOGY NOTE:                                      │
│  The first four avatars (Fish → Tortoise → Boar → Man-Lion)     │
│  loosely parallel the evolutionary sequence aquatic → amphibian │
│  → land mammal → proto-human. This parallelism was noticed in   │
│  the 19th c. and is sometimes cited in popular literature.      │
│  It is almost certainly coincidental; the Puranic texts had     │
│  different cosmological logic in mind.                          │

## The Mahabharata and Bhagavad Gita

```
MAHABHARATA — THE GREAT EPIC

Scale: 1.8 million words (10× Iliad + Odyssey); 100,000 shlokas (verses)
Attributed to the sage Vyasa; composed/compiled 400 BCE–400 CE
Central conflict: The Kurukshetra War between two branches of the
  Kuru dynasty — the five Pandavas vs. their hundred cousins the Kauravas

THE FIVE PANDAVAS:
  — Yudhishthira (eldest; dharma incarnate; son of Dharma-god)
  — Bhima (strength; son of Vayu/Wind)
  — Arjuna (archer; son of Indra; protagonist of Bhagavad Gita)
  — Nakula and Sahadeva (twin sons of the Ashvins)
  All five are married to the same woman: Draupadi

CAUSE OF THE WAR:
  — Yudhishthira loses everything in a dice game with the Kauravas
    (rigged by Shakuni, the Kauravas' uncle)
  — The Pandavas' wife Draupadi is dragged into the assembly hall;
    Duhshasana attempts to disrobe her; her sari miraculously extends
    infinitely (Krishna's protection)
  — Twelve years forest exile + one year incognito (conditions of the
    dice loss); Kauravas refuse to return the kingdom
  — War becomes inevitable

THE BHAGAVAD GITA (Book 6: "Bhishmaparvan," Chapters 25–42):
  — Arjuna sees his teachers, uncles, and cousins arrayed against him
  — He collapses: "I see no good in killing my kinsmen"
  — Krishna (his charioteer and cousin) delivers the Gita

  GITA'S CORE TEACHINGS:
  1. The Self (Atman) is eternal; the body dies, not the soul
     "Just as a man puts on new garments... the soul similarly
      puts on new material bodies" (2.22)
  2. Nishkama Karma: action without attachment to results
     "You have a right to perform your prescribed duties, but you
      are not entitled to the fruits of your actions" (2.47)
  3. Three paths (Yoga) to liberation:
     — Jnana Yoga: the path of knowledge
     — Bhakti Yoga: the path of devotion
     — Karma Yoga: the path of right action
  4. Krishna reveals his universal form (Vishvarupa, Chapter 11):
     Arjuna sees all creation, time itself, worlds being destroyed
     "I am Time, the destroyer of all worlds" — the vision of
     unmediated cosmic reality is terrifying, not beautiful
  5. Krishna is the supreme deity; surrender to him is highest path

THE WAR:
  — 18 days on the Kurukshetra plain
  — Nearly everyone dies; entire warrior aristocracy annihilated
  — The Pandavas "win" but inherit a devastated world

AFTERMATH:
  — Yudhishthira's kingdom: 36 years of rule
  — Krishna killed by a hunter's arrow (prophecy from the war)
  — The Pandavas walk north to the Himalayas (mahaprasthana);
    all die on the mountain except Yudhishthira
  — Yudhishthira enters heaven; finds his enemies there, his family
    in what appears to be hell; the illusion is dispelled; all are
    in heaven — the test of Yudhishthira's dharma
```

## The Ramayana

```
RAMAYANA ("Journey of Rama") — Valmiki's version, ~24,000 verses

CHARACTERS:
  Rama: 7th avatar of Vishnu; prince of Ayodhya
  Sita: Rama's wife; daughter of Earth (Bhudevi), raised by King Janaka;
        found in a furrow in a field (name = "furrow")
  Lakshmana: Rama's devoted younger brother
  Hanuman: Divine monkey; son of Vayu (wind); Rama's greatest devotee
  Ravana: Demon king of Lanka; ten heads; powerful ascetic who earned
          boons from Brahma; abductor of Sita
  Vibhishana: Ravana's righteous brother; joins Rama's side

NARRATIVE SUMMARY:

  Act 1 — Rama wins Sita by stringing Shiva's bow (that no one else
           can even lift). Married; the ideal couple.

  Act 2 — Kaikeyi (one of Rama's father Dasharatha's wives) uses two
           boons owed to her to demand: (1) her son Bharata gets the
           throne, (2) Rama is banished for 14 years.
           Dasharatha is bound by his word; Rama accepts the exile
           without protest (dharma over personal desire).
           Dasharatha dies of grief. Bharata refuses the throne;
           places Rama's sandals on it as regent.

  Act 3–5 — Forest exile. Ravana abducts Sita through deception
             (sends a golden deer to distract Rama; crosses the
             Lakshmana rekha protection line when Lakshmana leaves).
             Sita imprisoned in Lanka (Sri Lanka).

  Act 5–6 — The search. Hanuman leaps across the ocean to Lanka;
             finds Sita; is captured but escapes after setting Lanka
             on fire with his burning tail.
             Rama and Lakshmana build a bridge (Rama Setu) to Lanka
             with the help of the monkey/bear army (Vanarasena).
             Ravana's brothers counseled peace; he refused.

  Climax — Battle; Ravana finally killed by Brahmastra (divine arrow)
            when Rama targets his navel (where his life force dwells).

  Sita's Fire Test (Agni Pariksha):
            Rama says he cannot accept Sita back without proof of her
            purity — what will people say of a king who takes back
            a wife who lived in another man's house?
            Sita walks into fire; Agni (fire god) vouches for her
            purity; she emerges unharmed.

  Afterward — Return to Ayodhya; coronation (Rama Rajya = ideal rule).
              Later (Uttara Kanda, sometimes considered appendix):
              Rama exiles Sita a second time on public pressure;
              she is pregnant; gives birth to sons Lava and Kusha in
              Valmiki's forest; is finally called to court but asks
              Mother Earth to take her back; Earth opens; Sita descends.

MORAL COMPLEXITY:
  The Ramayana presents difficult choices: Rama's treatment of Sita is
  ethically troubling by modern standards and was contested even in
  ancient retellings. Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas (16th c.) softens the
  story; Valmiki's original is starker. The epic has 300+ versions
  across South and Southeast Asia; A.K. Ramanujan's essay "Three
  Hundred Ramayanas" catalogs the diversity — in some, Sita is the
  protagonist; in some, Ravana is sympathetic.
```

## Cosmic Time: The Yuga Cycles

One of the most distinctive Hindu cosmological contributions — time as cyclically degrading:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE YUGA CYCLE                                  │
│                                                                  │
│  A Mahayuga = four yugas = one complete cycle of human history   │
│                                                                  │
│  SATYA YUGA (Krita Yuga) — The Golden Age                       │
│  Duration: 1,728,000 human years                                │
│  — Dharma stands on all four legs                               │
│  — Humans are virtuous, long-lived (100,000 years), truthful    │
│  — No disease, no need for work                                 │
│                                                                  │
│  TRETA YUGA — The Silver Age                                     │
│  Duration: 1,296,000 human years                                │
│  — Dharma loses one leg                                         │
│  — Virtue decreases by 1/4; humans still noble but need ritual  │
│  — Ramayana occurs in this yuga                                 │
│                                                                  │
│  DVAPARA YUGA — The Bronze Age                                   │
│  Duration: 864,000 human years                                  │
│  — Dharma on two legs; humanity at half capacity                │
│  — Disease and discord appear; need for more complex ritual     │
│  — Mahabharata occurs at the cusp of Dvapara/Kali transition   │
│  — Krishna departs; Kali Yuga begins                            │
│                                                                  │
│  KALI YUGA — The Iron Age (Current)                             │
│  Duration: 432,000 human years                                  │
│  — Dharma on one leg; majority of virtue has left the world    │
│  — Discord, deception, greed, shortened lifespans              │
│  — Began: ~3102 BCE (traditional date = when Krishna departed) │
│  — We are ~5,100 years into a 432,000-year era                 │
│                                                                  │
│  MAHAYUGA TOTAL: 4,320,000 years                                │
│                                                                  │
│  LARGER CYCLES:                                                  │
│  1,000 Mahayugas = 1 Kalpa (one day of Brahma)                  │
│  = 4.32 billion years = one creative cycle of the universe     │
│  (strikingly close to Earth's actual age: ~4.5 billion years)  │
│                                                                  │
│  Brahma's night = 4.32 billion years (universe dissolved)       │
│  Brahma's full life = 311 trillion years                        │
│  After Brahma dies, a new Brahma is born; the cycle restarts   │
│  → No beginning; no end; time is cyclic at all scales          │
│                                                                  │
│  COMPARE:                                                        │
│  Greek: Hesiod's five ages (Gold → Silver → Bronze → Heroic →  │
│          Iron) also shows degradation over time                 │
│  Norse: Three ages from creation to Ragnarok (no restart)      │
│  Abrahamic: Linear time (creation → fall → redemption → end)   │
│  Hindu: Cyclic, multi-scale, no ultimate end — just more cycles │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Devi: Goddess Traditions

```
SHAKTI — THE DIVINE FEMININE

Shakti is not simply "the goddess" — she is the active creative power
without which even Shiva is an inert corpse ("Shava" = corpse without Shakti).

PARVATI (Uma) — benign consort of Shiva; love, devotion, fertility
  → Fierce forms when invoked against evil:
  DURGA — Warrior goddess; rides a lion/tiger; buffalo-demon Mahishasura

           → Only a female power can destroy him
           Ten arms; each holding a weapon given by the male gods
           Battle lasts nine days → Navaratri festival
  KALI — Durga's ultimate fury; black skin, tongue out, garland of skulls
          Stands on Shiva's chest; her bloodlust can only be stopped
          when she accidentally steps on her husband
          Worshipped especially in Bengal; Tantric traditions

LAKSHMI — Vishnu's consort; wealth, fortune, beauty, good luck
  — Emerged from the Samudra Manthan (ocean churning)
  — Worshipped at Diwali (festival of lights)

SARASWATI — Brahma's consort; knowledge, arts, music, learning
  — White swan; veena; books; peacock

RADHA — Krishna's beloved (supreme devotee); Gopi mythology
  — Not his wife but his deepest love; represents the soul's
    longing for the divine in Vaishnavism

THE TRIDEVI:
  Lakshmi (Vishnu's consort) + Parvati (Shiva's) + Saraswati (Brahma's)
  = female counterpart to the Trimurti

DASHA MAHAVIDYAS (Ten Great Wisdom Goddesses — Tantric tradition):
  Kali, Tara, Tripura Sundari, Bhuvaneshvari, Bhairavi, Chhinnamasta,
  Dhumavati, Bagalamukhi, Matangi, Kamala
  — Each represents a different aspect of cosmic feminine power

## Common Confusion Points

**There is no one "Hindu mythology"**: Vaishnavism, Shaivism, Shaktism, and Smartism have different hierarchies of deities. In Shaiva theology, Shiva is the supreme being who encompasses Vishnu and Brahma. In Vaishnavism, Vishnu is supreme. In Shaktism, Devi is the absolute. These traditions developed over millennia; there is no authoritative reconciliation.

**Brahma is not Brahman**: Brahma (with a short "a") is the creator deity — one of the Trimurti, rarely worshipped. Brahman (with a long "an") is the impersonal absolute reality of Hindu philosophy (the Upanishads). Brahmin (with an "in") is the priestly caste. Three different concepts, similar words.

**Avatar ≠ disguise**: In English, "avatar" now means digital persona/representation. In Hindu theology, an avatar is a *genuine* descent of the divine — Vishnu actually enters the world in bodily form. It is not a disguise or illusion; it is a real embodiment with full divine power constrained to a particular form.

**The Mahabharata is not simply "good vs. evil"**: The Pandavas win but are not simply good. Yudhishthira gambled his wife away. Arjuna hesitates to do his duty. Draupadi's treatment is troubling. The Kauravas have legitimate grievances. The war destroys an entire civilization. The text's moral complexity is part of its greatness — dharma (right action) in conflict with itself is its central theme.

**The Gita's "kill without attachment" is not a license for violence**: The context is a soldier on a battlefield who has a duty (svadharma) as a warrior. Krishna's argument is metaphysical (the Atman is deathless) and about non-attachment (do not fight for personal gain or hatred). The Gita has been read as pacifist (Gandhi's primary scriptural text) and as martial. Its meaning is contested.

**The yuga system is cosmological, not literally historical**: The degradation from Satya to Kali Yuga is not a historical claim about specific civilizations. It is a cosmological framework. The 432,000-year Kali Yuga means we are in an early phase with ~426,000 years remaining — not an imminent end. The numerical elegance (4:3:2:1 ratio, base multiples of 432,000) suggests mathematical construction for cosmological order.
