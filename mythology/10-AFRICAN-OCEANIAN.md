# African and Oceanian Mythology

## The Big Picture

"African mythology" and "Oceanian mythology" are not single traditions — they are continental/regional categories encompassing hundreds of distinct cultures, languages, and mythological systems. This guide focuses on the best-documented and most influential traditions within each region, with necessary acknowledgment that the surface is barely scratched.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           AFRICAN AND OCEANIAN MYTHOLOGY — OVERVIEW               │
│                                                                  │
│  AFRICA:                                                         │
│  — 54 countries; 3,000+ ethnic groups; 2,000+ languages         │
│  — No monolithic "African mythology"                            │
│  — Best-documented traditions: Yoruba (West Africa), Akan        │
│    (Ghana/Côte d'Ivoire), Dogon (Mali), Bantu (East/South),     │
│    Egyptian (covered in 05-EGYPTIAN.md)                         │
│  — Most traditions oral; recorded by anthropologists 19th–20th c.│
│  — Diaspora transmission: Yoruba mythology survived slavery via  │
│    Candomblé, Santería/Lucumí, Trinidad Orisha, Vodou (partly) │
│                                                                  │
│  OCEANIA:                                                        │
│  — Three regions: Melanesia, Micronesia, Polynesia               │
│  — Polynesia (Hawaii, Tahiti, Samoa, Tonga, New Zealand Maori,  │
│    Easter Island/Rapa Nui): shared mythology from common         │
│    ancestral Polynesian culture                                 │

│  — Ancestor spirits integrated with current living society      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Yoruba Mythology

The Yoruba (southwestern Nigeria, Benin, Togo) have one of the most sophisticated and well-documented mythological systems in Africa. Yoruba religion also became globally influential through the African diaspora.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  YORUBA COSMOLOGY AND ORISHAS                     │
│                                                                  │
│  OLODUMARE / OLORUN — The Supreme Being                         │
│  — The ultimate divine reality; infinite, unknowable            │
│  — Not directly worshipped (no shrines, no offerings to him)    │
│  — Transcendent; communicates with the world through the Orishas │
│  — Compare: the Gnostic "distant god" or Deist "watchmaker"     │
│  — "Olorun" = "owner of the sky/heaven"                        │
│                                                                  │
│  THE ORISHAS — Divine intermediaries (~401 or more)              │
│  — Emanations from Olodumare; they are both divine beings AND   │
│    natural/human forces (they can deify historical persons)     │
│  — Each has: domains, colors, numbers, sacred days, food        │
│    preferences, personality, and ritual protocols               │
│  — Orisha worship is highly personal: a person "belongs" to an  │
│    Orisha (identified by divination); the Orisha is their divine │
│    patron, ancestor, and in some sense their deeper self        │
│                                                                  │
│  THE CREATION MYTH:                                              │
│  Olodumare sends Obatala to create the earth from primordial    │
│  water. Obatala is given a snail shell of sand, a five-toed hen,│
│  and a palm nut.                                               │
│  He pours sand from the shell; the hen scatters it; solid land  │
│  forms (Ile-Ife, the sacred city, is the center)               │
│  Olodumare breathes life into clay figures Obatala has shaped → │
│  humanity                                                       │
│  Note: Obatala once created humans while drunk on palm wine;     │
│  that is why some people are born with disabilities — Obatala's │
│  patronage of people with disabilities has this mythological root│
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Major Orishas

```
ESHU-ELEGBA (also Elegba, Legba, Eshu)
  — Orisha of crossroads, doorways, communication, chance
  — Messenger between humans and the orishas
  — MUST be propitiated first before any other orisha in ritual
    (he opens or closes the way; without his permission, offerings
    do not reach the other orishas)
  — Trickster; loves disruption; tests humans
  — Colors: red and black (or yellow); number 3; lives at crossroads
  — Representation in diaspora: Papa Legba (Haitian Vodou),
    Exu (Candomblé), Eleggua (Santería/Lucumí)
  — Western misidentification: colonial missionaries conflated Eshu
    with the devil due to his crossroads association and trickster
    nature. He is NOT evil — he is the principle of dynamic change,
    communication, and the necessity of opening paths.

SHANGO (Chango, Xango)
  — Orisha of thunder, lightning, fire, justice, dancing
  — Historical layer: 4th Alafin (king) of the Oyo Empire;
    after his death, deified as the thunder god
  — Color: red; number 6; weapon: double-headed axe (oshe)
  — The most widely worshipped Orisha in the diaspora
  — Fierce, passionate, just; punishes liars and thieves with
    lightning
  — Representation: Shangó (Santería), Xangô (Candomblé),
    associated with Saint Barbara (Catholic syncretism)

OGUN
  — Orisha of iron, war, hunting, labor, technology, justice
  — The patron of everyone who works with iron: blacksmiths,
    soldiers, surgeons, taxi drivers, engineers
  — Before modern machinery, oaths sworn on iron belonged to Ogun;
    today: courtroom oaths in West Africa sometimes on iron
  — Color: green and black (or dark green); number 3 or 7
  — Lives at the edge of the forest; independent, fierce, solitary
  — Representation: Ogum (Candomblé), Ogún (Santería)

YEMOJA / YEMANJÁ
  — Orisha of the ocean, rivers, motherhood, children
  — "Mother of Waters"; mother to many orishas
  — Color: blue and white; number 7; the ocean and river
  — Deeply beloved; Yemanjá celebrations (especially February 2
    in Brazil) involve thousands launching offerings into the sea
  — Representation: Iemanjá (Candomblé), Yemayá (Santería),
    associated with Our Lady of Regla (Catholic syncretism)
  — One of the most widespread surviving African deities in the
    diaspora

OSHUN
  — Orisha of rivers, love, fertility, beauty, wealth, divination
  — The sweetness of life; honey, sensuality, art
  — Color: yellow and gold; number 5; rivers and sweet water
  — The youngest Orisha but extremely powerful
  — Stories: When the orishas first came to earth, they forgot to
    invite Oshun; nothing worked — crops failed, children died.
    Olodumare sent for Oshun; she arrived, fixed everything.
    → The lesson: do not forget sweetness, beauty, and love.
  — Representation: Oxum (Candomblé), Ochún (Santería),
    associated with Our Lady of Charity (Cuban Virgen de la Caridad)

IFA AND THE CORPUS OF DIVINATION
  — Ifa is both an Orisha (deity of wisdom and divination) and a
    vast corpus of literary/ritual knowledge
  — The Babalawo (divination priest) uses the Ifa corpus of 256
    "odus" (chapters) containing thousands of verses
  — Each odu contains: myths (patakis), prescriptions, proverbs,
    and cosmological teachings
  — UNESCO declared Ifa an Intangible Cultural Heritage (2005)
  — The most sophisticated divination system in Africa; the Yoruba
    claim it contains a solution to every human problem
```

## Yoruba Diaspora: Candomblé, Santería, Vodou

```
THE DIASPORA RELIGIONS

The transatlantic slave trade (1500s–1800s) transported millions of
Yoruba, Fon, and other West Africans to the Americas. Their religious
traditions survived through syncretism with Catholicism:

CANDOMBLÉ (Brazil)
  — Closest to original Yoruba practice; least syncretic
  — Orisha correspond to Catholic saints (camouflage during slavery)
  — Major centers: Salvador da Bahia; Recife
  — Initiated practitioners (filha/filho-de-santo) "receive" orishas
    in possession ceremonies; the Orisha speaks through them

SANTERÍA / LUCUMÍ / REGLA DE OCHA (Cuba, Puerto Rico, USA)
  — More syncreted than Candomblé; Catholic saint names standard
  — Babalawo priests perform Ifa divination
  — Major centers: Havana; Miami; New York; now global

TRINIDAD ORISHA / SHANGO BAPTISTS (Trinidad)

VODOU (Haiti) — more complex:
  — Primarily Fon/Ewe from Dahomey (Benin) in origin, not Yoruba
  — Lwas (lwa) rather than orishas; similar structure
  — Papa Legba (gateway), Ogou (iron/war), Erzulie Freda (love),
    Baron Samedi (death), Maman Brigitte (cemeteries)
  — Vodou has been severely misrepresented in Western media
    ("voodoo" as dark magic) — the actual tradition is a complex
    African-derived religion with rich theology

COMMON THREAD:
  — Possession by divine beings (orishas/lwas) as central ritual
  — The deity enters the practitioner's body; they become a "horse"
    the deity rides; the practitioner may not remember the episode
  — Offerings, music, dance, animal sacrifice as ritual elements
  — Living synthesis of African religion surviving displacement
```

## Anansi: The Spider Trickster (Akan)

```
ANANSI (also Ananse) — AKAN SPIDER TRICKSTER

Origin: Akan people of Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire
Character: Spider; the supreme trickster; owns all stories

THE GREAT MYTH:
  Originally the Sky God (Nyame/Nyankopong) owned all stories
  Anansi bought them from Nyame
  Price: a hornets' nest, a python, a leopard
  Anansi fills the hornets' nest by getting the hornets to prove
    it fits them (they fly in; he covers the hole)
  He ties up the python by betting he can prove the python is
    longer than a certain branch (the python lies down to measure;
    Anansi ties him)
  He traps the leopard in a pit trap
  Presents all three to Nyame; receives all the stories
  → Now all stories belong to Anansi; they are called "spider stories"

DIASPORA SURVIVAL:
  — Anansi stories survived the Middle Passage; documented in
    Jamaica (Anansi), Suriname (Anansi), and the American South
    (Brer Rabbit — the trickster rabbit who escapes by cleverness
     is almost certainly descended from Anansi traditions)
  — Neil Gaiman's "Anansi Boys" (2005) brought Anansi to a
    wide contemporary audience

SIGNIFICANCE:
  — Trickster who wins through cleverness against the powerful
  — Enslaved people preserved and valued Anansi stories partly
    because they validated cleverness over force — survival strategy
    encoded in narrative
```

## Aboriginal Australian: The Dreamtime

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE DREAMTIME (TJUKURPA)                       │
│                                                                  │
│  NOTE: "Dreamtime" is a translation; "Dreaming" or the term in  │
│  each specific language is preferred by most Aboriginal          │
│  communities. "Tjukurpa" is the Anangu (South Australia) term.  │
│  There are 250+ Aboriginal language groups, each with distinct  │
│  traditions. What follows is generalized from documented examples│
│  and should not be treated as universal.                        │
│                                                                  │
│  WHAT IS THE DREAMING:                                           │
│  — NOT primarily about sleep or dreams                          │
│  — It is the ontological foundation of reality:                 │
│    the creative time when Ancestor Beings shaped the world AND  │
│    the timeless present in which those creative acts continue   │
│  — Past event AND present reality AND law all at once           │
│  — W.E.H. Stanner called it "everywhen" rather than "a time"   │
│                                                                  │
│  THE ANCESTOR BEINGS:                                            │
│  — In the beginning, the earth was featureless                  │
│  — Ancestor Beings emerged (from underground, from the sky,     │
│    from the sea) and moved across the landscape                │
│  — As they moved, they sang, hunted, fought, loved, died        │
│  — Their actions created the physical features of the landscape:│
│    — A battle created a mountain range                          │
│    — A journey carved a river                                   │
│    — Where they camped → waterholes                             │
│    — Where they ate → valleys                                   │
│  — When their journeys ended, they sank back into the earth     │
│    or transformed into geographical features                    │
│                                                                  │
│  SONGLINES (DREAMING TRACKS):                                    │
│  — Every Ancestor Being's journey is encoded in a song          │
│  — The song maps the geography: each verse corresponds to a     │
│    specific location on the route                               │
│  — To sing the song is to re-walk the route; the land is a     │
│    musical score                                                │
│  — Bruce Chatwin's "The Songlines" (1987) introduced this to    │
│    wide Western audiences (though Chatwin's interpretation is   │
│    contested; Aboriginal communities have mixed views of it)    │
│                                                                  │
│  OWNERSHIP AND RESPONSIBILITY:                                   │
│  — Particular clans "own" particular Dreamtime stories and     │
│    songlines; they are the custodians and have obligations to  │
│    maintain them through ceremony                              │
│  — The land is not property to be owned in the Western sense   │
│    but a relationship of mutual obligation                     │
│  — Country (the specific ancestral landscape) is a living,     │
│    responsive entity that requires care                        │
│                                                                  │
│  EXAMPLES OF ANCESTOR BEINGS:                                    │
│  — Rainbow Serpent: widespread across many groups; the great    │
│    creative and destructive serpent of the waterways           │
│  — Wandjina: ancestral creator beings from the Kimberley        │
│    region (Western Australia); cloud and rain spirits          │
│    depicted in rock art without mouths (they speak through     │
│    rain; if the rock art fades, they may be retouched by the   │
│    appropriate custodians to maintain the relationship)        │
│  — Baiame: sky god among southeastern groups (Kamilaroi,        │
│    Wiradjuri); creator; father figure                          │
│  — Yowie, Bunyip: creatures of the bush; Bunyip is a dangerous  │
│    water creature (regional variations in description)          │
│                                                                  │
│  AGE:                                                            │
│  — Aboriginal Australians have the world's longest continuous   │
│    cultural tradition (~65,000 years, based on archaeological  │
│    evidence)                                                    │
│  — Some Dreaming stories may encode actual geological events:   │
│    — Stories of the sea rising to cover land (correctly         │
│      describe post-glacial sea level rise ~10,000 BCE)         │
│    — Stories of volcanic eruptions that match geological dates  │
│  → The Dreaming may preserve real memory across tens of        │
│    thousands of years — an unprecedented level of oral          │
│    transmission fidelity                                        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Maori Mythology

The Maori of Aotearoa/New Zealand represent one of the most well-documented Polynesian mythological traditions, recorded extensively in the 19th century:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    MAORI MYTHOLOGY                                 │
│                                                                  │
│  CREATION:                                                       │
│  Te Kore (the Void/Nothingness) → Te Pō (the Night/Darkness)    │
│  Multiple Pō (nights) of increasing differentiation             │
│  → Te Ao Mārama (the World of Light)                            │
│                                                                  │
│  RANGINUI AND PAPATŪĀNUKU:                                       │
│  The Sky Father (Rangi) and the Earth Mother (Papa) are locked  │
│  in a tight embrace; their children are trapped in darkness     │
│  between them                                                   │
│  The children debate: should they push them apart or kill them? │
│  Most agree to push them apart                                  │
│  Tānemahuta (god of forests) lies on his back; legs push up     │
│  the sky; the separation creates light                          │
│  Rangi weeps → rain; Papa grieves → mist from the earth        │
│  Tāwhirimātea (god of winds and storms) is angry at the         │
│  separation; attacks his brothers in rage                       │
│                                                                  │
│  THE CHILDREN OF RANGI AND PAPA:                                 │
│  — Tānemahuta: forests, birds, humans (fashioned from earth)    │
│  — Tūmatauenga: humans, war (ancestor of the human race)        │
│  — Tangaroa: sea and sea creatures                              │
│  — Rongo: cultivated plants, peace                              │
│  — Haumia-tiketike: wild plants, uncultivated food              │
│  — Rūaumoko: earthquakes and geothermal activity (unborn child  │
│    still inside Papa — still moving causes earthquakes)         │
│                                                                  │
│  TĀNE CREATES HUMANITY:                                          │
│  Tāne fashions a woman from the red earth of Kurawaka           │
│  (from Papa's body — his mother's body)                         │
│  Breathes life into her nostrils: Hineahuone (Earth-Formed Maid)│
│  Tāne and Hineahuone have a daughter: Hinetītama               │
│  (Tāne, not knowing his own daughter, marries her)              │
│  Hinetītama discovers Tāne is her father; flees to the          │
│  underworld (Rarohenga/Te Pō) in shame                         │
│  Becomes Hinenuitepō — the Great Woman of Night/Death           │
│  "I will gather our descendants." → Death enters the world     │
│                                                                  │
│  MĀUI — THE GREAT TRICKSTER-HERO                                │
│  — Semi-divine trickster; one of the most important Polynesian  │
│    mythological figures (versions across Hawaii, Tahiti, Fiji,  │
│    Samoa, Cook Islands, and New Zealand)                        │
│                                                                  │
│  MĀUI'S EXPLOITS:                                                │
│  1. FISHING UP AOTEAROA:                                        │
│     Māui tricks his brothers into taking him fishing            │
│     He uses his grandmother's jawbone as a magical fishhook     │
│     Baits it with his own blood; casts it into the sea         │
│     Catches a giant fish; pulls it up — it becomes the North    │
│     Island of New Zealand (Te Ika a Māui — "the fish of Māui") │
│     His brothers hack at it while he is gone (he told them not  │
│     to) → the irregular, mountainous surface of the North Island│
│     → The South Island = his canoe (Te Waka o Māui)            │
│                                                                  │
│  2. SLOWING THE SUN:                                             │
│     The sun moved too quickly; days too short                   │
│     Māui and his brothers weave ropes of flax; wait at the sun's│
│     rising point; lasso the sun as it emerges                  │
│     Beat it with his grandmother's jawbone until it promises to │
│     move more slowly → longer days                              │
│                                                                  │
│  3. BRINGING FIRE:                                               │
│     Māui quenches all fires in the world; forces his ancestor   │
│     Mahuika (fire goddess) to give him fire                    │
│     She gives him one nail's fire at a time; he extinguishes    │
│     each by dipping it in water; she runs out; in fury throws  │
│     her last fire; it sets the forest ablaze                    │
│     Māui calls rain; saves himself; fire now lives in specific  │
│     trees (kaikōmako, māhoe) from which friction fire is made   │
│                                                                  │
│  4. DEATH OF MĀUI — HE FAILS TO ACHIEVE IMMORTALITY:           │
│     Hinenuitepō (Death goddess) sleeps; birds accompany Māui   │
│     He plans to enter her body (like a worm through the earth) │
│     and exit her mouth → humanity would gain immortality        │
│     One of the birds (the pīwakawaka / fantail) laughs          │
│     Hinenuitepō wakes; sees Māui; crushes him between her thighs│
│     Māui dies — the first death of a man; mortality confirmed   │
│     "Had it not been for that little bird, we would have won"  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Mana and Tapu

```
MANA AND TAPU — POLYNESIAN COSMIC FORCES

These concepts run throughout Polynesian (and Melanesian) cultures:

MANA
  — Spiritual power, authority, efficacy
  — Not a personal quality you are born with, but a force that can
    be accumulated, transferred, lost, and stolen
  — A chief has mana; a great warrior has mana; the mana of a
    great ancestor can be inherited; spiritual leaders manage mana
  — Objects can have mana (a weapon that has won many battles)
  — Actions can gain or lose mana
  — To disrespect a chief or desecrate a sacred site destroys mana
  — Mana is relational: it depends on your connections to ancestors,
    the land, and the community
  — Mana whenua ("mana of the land"): authority derived from being
    the original people of a place

TAPU (also "taboo" — this Polynesian word entered English)
  — Sacred restriction; a prohibition on access or behavior
  — Something tapu is charged with sacred power; contact without
    appropriate standing is dangerous
  — The chief's head was highly tapu in many Polynesian cultures
    (extremely rude or dangerous to touch it)
  — Tapu restrictions regulated:
    — What could be eaten, and by whom (women often tapu from
      certain foods or from men's eating areas)
    — Access to sacred sites, tools, or persons
    — Behavior during warfare, birth, death, planting, and harvest
  — NOA is the opposite of tapu: free from restriction, ordinary

HOW THE TWO WORK TOGETHER:
  The world is managed through the tension between tapu (sacred
  restriction) and noa (ordinary/free). A chief is tapu; food
  prepared by a chief would transfer tapu to those who ate it,
  potentially harming commoners. Hence the elaborate protocols
  around chiefs' food, clothing, and persons.

  Mana is the power that flows through the system; tapu is the
  restriction that channels and protects it.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"African mythology" is not one thing**: Africa is the most genetically and linguistically diverse continent on Earth. Yoruba theology is no more "African mythology" than Greek theology is "European mythology." The Dogon of Mali have a completely different cosmological system (involving detailed astronomical knowledge that puzzled 20th-century anthropologists); the Zulu have yet another; Egyptian mythology is covered separately. Any summary risks serious oversimplification.

**Eshu/Elegba is not a devil**: Colonial missionaries conflated Eshu with Satan because he lives at crossroads (a European symbol of the devil) and is a trickster. In Yoruba theology, Eshu is the principle of dynamic change and the necessary messenger. He is morally ambiguous, not evil. He must be honored first because nothing works without communication. The misidentification caused serious theological distortion in diaspora communities, some of whom were taught to fear him.

**Vodou ≠ "voodoo dolls" and curses**: The popular Western image of "voodoo" (a pejorative corruption of the word) bears almost no relation to Haitian Vodou, which is a complex African-derived religion with sophisticated theology, a rich oral literary tradition, highly organized ritual practice, and a deep community ethic. The "voodoo doll" trope is a Western fantasy with no significant basis in actual practice.

**The Dreamtime is not a past time**: The most common misunderstanding. The Dreaming is not "a long time ago when the Ancestors walked." It is an ontological reality that is simultaneously past, present, and future — a foundation underlying all of existence. To say "in the Dreamtime..." as if it were historical narrative misses the concept entirely. "Everywhen" (Stanner) is a better approximation.

**Mana is not simply "magical power"**: The gaming concept of mana (in Dungeons & Dragons, World of Warcraft, etc.) derives from the Polynesian concept but strips it of context. Polynesian mana is relational and social — it is not an internal resource but a position in a network of relationships with ancestors, land, and community. You cannot "use up" mana by casting spells. You build or lose mana through correct or incorrect relationships.
