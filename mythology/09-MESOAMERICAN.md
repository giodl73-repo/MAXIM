# Mesoamerican Mythology

## The Big Picture

Mesoamerica — the culture zone running from central Mexico through Guatemala and Belize to Honduras and El Salvador — produced some of the most sophisticated and distinctive mythologies in the world. Three major traditions: Aztec (Mexica), Maya, and Inca (South America, Andean — geographically distinct but included here as the major New World complex). All three were active, living civilizations with intact priesthoods and mythological traditions at the moment of Spanish conquest (1519–1572). The primary sources are therefore complex: some pre-conquest codices survive, but most texts were written down after conquest, filtered through Catholic missionaries and Spanish colonial frameworks.

## Aztec Mythology: The Five Suns

The central Aztec cosmological myth explains the current world as the fifth and final attempt at creation — and the reason why human sacrifice was not optional:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE FIVE SUNS (AZTEC)                         │
│                                                                  │
│  Each "Sun" = a complete world-age; each destroyed               │
│  Current world = the Fifth Sun = the Sun of Movement (Nahui Ollin)│
│                                                                  │
│  SUN 1 — Nahui Ocelotl (4-Jaguar)                                │
│  Patron: Tezcatlipoca (Smoking Mirror; Black god; darkness)      │
│  Inhabitants: Giants who ate acorns                              │
│  Destruction: Tezcatlipoca's foot bitten off by a great crocodile│
│    → He falls; jaguars devour the giants at darkness             │
│                                                                  │
│  SUN 2 — Nahui Ehécatl (4-Wind)                                  │
│  Patron: Quetzalcóatl (Feathered Serpent; Wind god; sky)         │
│  Inhabitants: Humans who ate pine nuts; turned into monkeys      │
│  Destruction: Hurricane wind; Tezcatlipoca overthrows Quetzalcóatl│
│                                                                  │
│  SUN 3 — Nahui Quiahuitl (4-Rain)                                │
│  Patron: Tlaloc (Rain god)                                       │
│  Inhabitants: Humans who ate wild seeds; turned into birds       │
│  Destruction: Rain of fire; Quetzalcóatl destroys it in revenge  │
│                                                                  │
│  SUN 4 — Nahui Atl (4-Water)                                     │
│  Patron: Chalchiuhtlicue (goddess of water, rivers, lakes)       │
│  Inhabitants: Humans who ate maize; turned into fish             │
│  Destruction: 52-year flood; the sky fell                        │
│  Note: Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcóatl rebuild sky (become pillars)│
│                                                                  │
│  SUN 5 — Nahui Ollin (4-Movement) — CURRENT SUN                  │
│  Created at Teotihuacan:                                         │
│  — The gods gather in darkness; two volunteer to become sun/moon │
│  — Tecuciztecatl (proud, wealthy) and Nanahuatzin (humble, scab)│
│  — Both must throw themselves into a great bonfire               │
│  — Tecuciztecatl approaches four times; each time he flinches    │
│  — Nanahuatzin approaches once; leaps without hesitation         │
│    → He becomes the sun                                          │
│  — Tecuciztecatl finally jumps; becomes the moon                 │
│    (Originally as bright as the sun; one of the gods hits the    │
│     moon with a rabbit to dim it — why there is a rabbit in      │
│     the moon / why the moon is dimmer)                           │
│                                                                  │
│  THE PROBLEM: Both sun and moon sit on the horizon; neither      │
│  moves. The gods must die to set the sun in motion.              │
│  Quetzalcóatl kills all the gods; their blood sets the sun       │
│  moving.                                                         │
│                                                                  │
│  THE IMPLICATION:                                                │
│  The sun moves because the gods died to make it so.              │
│  But the Fifth Sun can still end: it will be destroyed by        │
│  earthquake — on a day Nahui Ollin.                              │
│  To delay this, the sun requires blood to continue moving.       │
│  Human sacrifice = feeding the sun = keeping creation alive.     │
│  This is not bloodthirst — it is cosmic maintenance.             │
│                                                                  │
│  The Aztec universe runs on debt:                                │
│  The gods sacrificed themselves; humans owe the gods their lives.│
│  Sacrifice repays the cosmic debt that sustains the world.       │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Aztec Major Deities

```
QUETZALCÓATL (Feathered Serpent / Precious Twin)
  — One of the most important deities in Mesoamerican religion
  — Feathered serpent: quetzal (the bird, representing sky/wind/life)
    + coatl (serpent, representing earth/water/death) = BOTH
  — Function varies by context:
    As Ehécatl: wind god; cleaned the roads for rain
    As patron of learning: invented writing, the calendar, arts
    As the planet Venus: both morning and evening star
    As the god-king of Tula: culture hero; the "good" king
  — The Tula legend: Tezcatlipoca tricks Quetzalcóatl into
    drunkenness and incest (with his sister); Quetzalcóatl, shamed,
    departs east on a raft of serpents; promises to return
  — One tradition: he set himself on fire; his heart became Venus
  — The "Quetzalcóatl returns as Cortés" story: Later Spanish
    sources suggest Moctezuma II thought Cortés might be the
    returning Quetzalcóatl (his arrival in 1519 = 1 Reed year,
    the predicted return year). Historians debate how much of
    this was real belief vs. post-conquest rationalization.

TEZCATLIPOCA (Smoking Mirror)
  — Eternal rival of Quetzalcóatl
  — Black obsidian mirror where he sees all human acts
  — One foot missing (bitten off in the creation of the world);
    replaced by an obsidian mirror or a serpent
  — Night sky, earth, sorcery, war, discord
  — The trickster of the Aztec cosmos; not evil per se, but
    represents unpredictable, dangerous power

HUITZILOPOCHTLI (Hummingbird of the South / Left)
  — Patron deity of the Mexica; sun god; god of war
  — The Aztec tribal god that grew to cosmic importance
  — His myth: his mother Coatlicue is sweeping when she finds
    a bundle of feathers; tucks it in her belt; becomes pregnant
  — Her 400 children (the stars) and daughter Coyolxauhqui (moon)
    decide to kill her in shame
  — Huitzilopochtli is born fully armed; kills Coyolxauhqui;
    dismembers her body (she rolls down the hill of Coatepec)
    → The sun defeats the moon and stars at dawn every day
  — The Great Temple (Templo Mayor) in Tenochtitlan = Coatepec Hill
    — Coyolxauhqui Stone found at base of main pyramid
    — Sacrifice victims roll down the stairs to re-enact this myth

TLALOC — Rain god; god of fertility, water, lightning
  — Long established deity; one of the oldest in Mesoamerica
  — His victims: children and those with certain skin conditions
    (tears = rain)
  — His paradise (Tlalocan) = lush, wet heaven for those who drowned
    or died by water/lightning

COATLICUE — "She of the Serpent Skirt"
  — Huitzilopochtli's mother
  — Earth goddess; life and death together
  — Necklace of hands and hearts; her face = two serpents facing
    each other; her skirt = writhing snakes

MICTLANTECUHTLI — Lord of Mictlan (the underworld)
  — The dead must travel nine levels of Mictlan to reach the final
    realm; takes four years; requires specific items buried with them
```

## The Maya: Popol Vuh

The K'iche' Maya creation epic — the most complete pre-Columbian mythological narrative to survive:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    POPOL VUH — NARRATIVE MAP                     │
│                                                                  │
│  "In the beginning there was only sky and sea" (Huracán and      │
│  Gucumatz/Kukulcán deliberate in the darkness)                   │
│                                                                  │
│  CREATION ATTEMPTS:                                              │
│  1st: Animals created → they cannot speak the names of the       │
│       gods → condemned to be food                                │
│  2nd: Mud humans → dissolve in water; cannot speak properly      │
│  3rd: Wood humans → speak but have no minds or souls; no         │
│       memory of the gods; they mistreat their animals and        │
│       domestic objects → destroyed in a flood + their own        │
│       possessions revolt: dogs bite, millstones grind, pots burn │
│       → Survivors become the monkeys in the trees                │
│  4th: Maize humans (CURRENT) — successful creation (see below)   │
│                                                                  │
│  THE HERO TWINS — XUN HUNAHPU AND XBALANQUE:                     │
│  (This narrative precedes the creation of maize humans)          │
│                                                                  │
│  The Hero Twins' father (Hunahpu) and uncle (Vucub Hunahpu)      │
│  play the ball game; the noise annoys the lords of Xibalba       │
│  (the underworld); the lords summon them; they fail the traps    │
│  and are sacrificed; Hunahpu's head hung in a calabash tree      │
│                                                                  │
│  Xquic (a Xibalba princess) visits the tree; the head spits in   │
│  her hand; she becomes pregnant; flees Xibalba; gives birth to   │
│  the Hero Twins: Hunahpu and Xbalanque                           │
│                                                                  │
│  THE HERO TWINS' ADVENTURES:                                     │
│  — Defeat Seven Macaw (Vucub Caquix) — a false sun who claims    │
│    to be the sun; they shoot him; his sons fall too              │
│  — Are summoned to Xibalba; survive where their father failed:   │
│    Pass the Dark House (fire not matches), Shivering House,      │
│    Fire House, Jaguar House, Bat House (Hunahpu loses his head   │
│    briefly; they trick Xibalba using a turtle decoy)             │
│  — They win the ball game                                        │
│  — Let themselves be defeated and killed (final trick):          │
│    The lords of Xibalba ask a magician to demonstrate killing    │
│    and revival; the twins are killed and ground to bone powder   │
│    and dumped in a river; they revive (they had tricked the      │
│    lords into killing them in order to rise again)               │
│  — They reveal their identity; the lords beg for their lives     │
│  — The twins spare the lower Xibalba lords; abolish human        │
│    sacrifice to Xibalba; the great lords are defeated            │
│  — The twins ascend: Hunahpu becomes the sun; Xbalanque the      │
│    moon (or: both become the sun and moon together)              │
│                                                                  │
│  CREATION OF MAIZE HUMANS:                                       │
│  The gods find the right material: white and yellow maize        │
│  (found in a mountain by animal messengers)                      │
│  Four men are made from maize dough:                             │
│  — Their vision and knowledge is too great (they can see         │
│    everything; equal the gods)                                   │
│  — The gods "fog" their eyes (like breathing on a mirror)        │
│  — Humans become limited in sight and knowledge                  │
│  → The limitation of human understanding is intentional design   │
│  Four women are created from the men while they sleep            │
│  These eight are the ancestors of the K'iche' Maya lineages      │
│                                                                  │
│  AFTER CREATION:                                                 │
│  The ancestors search for the gods in darkness; pray for the sun │
│  The first sunrise causes all tribes to weep with joy            │
│  The mythological narrative transitions to the historical:       │
│  K'iche' lineages and their territories                          │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Maya Ball Game and Xibalba

```
THE MESOAMERICAN BALL GAME (Pok-a-Tok / Tlachtli)

Physical game: rubber ball; hip-mounted stone rings; players use
hips, knees, elbows (not hands or feet in most versions)
Courts found throughout Mesoamerica; largest at Chichén Itzá

MYTHOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  — The ball = the sun (solar disk metaphor)
  — The game = the daily cycle of the sun through the sky and
    underworld
  — In Popol Vuh: the ball game is what draws the attention of
    Xibalba (the underworld) and precipitates the Hero Twins' story
  — Ball game courts are liminal spaces: the boundary between
    the living world and Xibalba

SACRIFICE IN THE BALL GAME:
  — Archaeological and iconographic evidence: the losers (or the
    winners — contested) were sacrificed after certain games
  — Not every game involved sacrifice; some were probably purely
    athletic
  — The sacrificed player may have been "honored" — dying in a
    ritual context was considered spiritually significant

XIBALBA — THE MAYA UNDERWORLD:
  — "Place of Fear" or "Place of the Rulers of the Dead"
  — 12 lords rule it: One Death and Seven Death are supreme
    (below: Scab Stripper, Blood Gatherer, Pus Master, Jaundice
     Master, Bone Scepter, Skull Scepter, Wing, Packstrap,
     Bloody Teeth, Bloody Claws)
  — Six houses of trial: Dark House, Cold House, Fire House,
    Jaguar House, Razor House, Bat House
  — The dead navigate these before reaching their final realm
  — CONTRAST: Greek Hades (depressing but not actively malevolent);
    Egyptian Duat (trial-based but just); Maya Xibalba (actively
    hostile; the lords seek to kill visitors)
```

## Aztec Calendar: The 52-Year Cycle

```
THE AZTEC CALENDAR SYSTEM

Two interlocking calendars:

XIUHPOHUALLI — 365-day solar calendar
  18 months of 20 days each = 360 days
  + 5 "nameless days" (Nemontemi) = dangerous, unlucky days
  Used for: agricultural cycles, civic events, festival timing

TONALPOHUALLI — 260-day ritual calendar
  13 numbers × 20 day signs = 260 day cycle
  Day signs: Crocodile, Wind, House, Lizard, Serpent, Death,
    Deer, Rabbit, Water, Dog, Monkey, Grass, Reed, Jaguar,
    Eagle, Vulture, Earthquake, Flint, Rain, Flower
  Used for: divination, naming children (born on a day sign),
    auspicious timing for events

52-YEAR CYCLE (Xiuhmolpilli — "Year Bundle"):
  The two calendars align every 52 years (LCM of 365 and 260)
  = one complete "century" in Aztec reckoning

NEW FIRE CEREMONY:
  At the end of each 52-year cycle, the Aztecs feared the world
  might end. They extinguished all fires; waited on a hilltop.
  Priests watched for the Pleiades to pass the zenith at midnight
  — if they did, the world would continue another cycle.
  The first new fire was drilled on the chest of a sacrificed
  captive. Runners carried fire to all temples and households.
  → Cosmic renewal through ritual
```

```
│  → Religion embedded in the landscape of the capital             │
│  → The entire city was a cosmological map                        │
│  → Nothing comparable in other mythological traditions           │

```

## Common Confusion Points

**Human sacrifice was theologically motivated, not bloodlust**: The Aztec ritual sacrifice system operated within a complete cosmological framework — the universe runs on cosmic debt; blood is the currency. This does not minimize the reality of mass human sacrifice, which was horrific by any measure, but it was not random violence. The theological logic is coherent and should be understood on its own terms before being judged.

**Quetzalcóatl and Cortés**: The idea that Moctezuma II believed Cortés was the returning Quetzalcóatl is a post-conquest Spanish narrative, largely constructed from later sources. It served to explain why the Aztecs did not immediately resist. Modern historians (including Camilla Townsend) argue this is largely a myth — Moctezuma was a sophisticated ruler who understood the Spanish were humans, not gods.

**The Inca had no writing — but they had khipu**: Khipu (knotted cords) recorded information in a system not yet fully deciphered. They may have recorded narrative, not just numerical data. "No writing" understates their information technology; their record-keeping enabled an empire 4,000 miles long.

**Popol Vuh is Maya, not Aztec**: The two civilizations are distinct. The Maya are in southern Mexico/Guatemala/Belize/Honduras; their civilization peaked 250–900 CE (Classic period). The Aztec/Mexica empire peaked 1300–1521 CE in central Mexico. The Spanish encountered and conquered both — the Aztec in 1519–1521, the Maya more gradually over decades. They share mythological parallels (feathered serpent deity = Quetzalcóatl in Aztec / Kukulcán in Maya; ball game; creation cycles) because they participated in a shared Mesoamerican cultural horizon.

**The "Aztec Calendar Stone" is not actually a calendar**: The famous stone disk on display in Mexico's National Museum of Anthropology represents the Five Suns myth — it is a cosmological monument depicting the history of world ages, with the Fifth Sun in the center. It encodes calendrical information but is not a functional calendar.
