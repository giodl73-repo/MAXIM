# Mythology — Comparative Overview

## The Big Picture

Mythology is the systematic study of **sacred narratives** — stories that a culture uses to explain the origin of the world, the nature of divinity, the structure of society, and what it means to be human. Every major civilization independently developed remarkably similar narrative patterns, which is the central puzzle that comparative mythology tries to solve.

## Universal Myth Typology

### Creation Myths

```
CREATION FROM NOTHING (ex nihilo):
  Egyptian: sun-god Atum speaks/thinks world into being
  Abrahamic: God speaks ("Let there be light")
  Maori: Te Kore (void) → Te Po (darkness) → Te Ao (light)

CREATION FROM CHAOS / PRIMORDIAL WATERS:
  Mesopotamian: Tiamat (salt water) + Apsu (fresh water) → world
  Greek: Chaos → Gaia → Ouranos
  Hindu: cosmic ocean, Vishnu sleeping on Ananta serpent

WORLD PARENT:
  Maori: Ranginui (sky father) + Papatuanuku (earth mother),
         separated by children to let light in
  Greek: Ouranos (sky) + Gaia (earth) — similar separation

EARTH DIVER:
  Native American (Iroquois, Ojibwe): animal dives into primal waters,
  brings up mud from which earth is formed
  Siberian: loon/duck dives → world formed from mud

WORLD EGG:
  Hindu: cosmic egg (Brahmanda) splits into heaven and earth
  Finnish (Kalevala): cosmic egg laid by bird on Väinämöinen's knee
  Orphic Greek: Phanes hatches from silver egg

DISMEMBERMENT OF PRIMORDIAL BEING:
  Norse: Ymir's body → world (flesh→earth, blood→sea, skull→sky)
  Hindu: Purusha → universe in Rigveda
  Mesopotamian: Tiamat's body → sky and earth
```

### Flood Myths

```
FLOOD MYTH PARALLELS:
  Mesopotamian (Atrahasis, ~1700 BCE):
    Gods send flood; Atrahasis warned by Enki, builds boat, saves animals
  Hebrew Genesis (compiled ~600–400 BCE):
    God floods earth; Noah warned, builds ark, saves animals
  Greek:
    Zeus floods; Deucalion (son of Prometheus) builds chest, survives with Pyrrha
  Hindu:
    Manu warned by fish (Vishnu avatar), builds boat, grounds on mountain
  Aztec:
    4th Sun destroyed by flood; Tata and Nene survive in tree trunk

KEY PARALLEL ELEMENTS:
  Divine decision to flood, single righteous survivor warned by deity,
  vessel built, animals saved, bird sent to find land, sacrifice upon landing,
  covenant/new beginning — appear in ALL major flood myths independently
  or through cultural diffusion along ancient trade routes
```

### Dying-and-Rising Gods

```
DYING-AND-RISING (Frazer pattern):
  Osiris (Egypt): killed by Set, resurrected by Isis, rules underworld
  Dionysus (Greek): torn apart by Titans, reborn — mystery cult
  Attis (Phrygian): dies, Cybele mourns, revival
  Adonis (Phoenician): dies by boar, Aphrodite mourns, part-year revival
  Baldur (Norse): killed by Loki's trick, will return after Ragnarok
  Persephone (Greek): descent/ascent — seasonal myth, Eleusinian Mysteries
  Inanna (Sumerian): descends to underworld, dies, revives

DEBATE: James Frazer (1890) saw these as one pattern.
Modern scholars note significant differences and resist over-reducing.
The "dying god = Christianity" argument (promoted by 20th-c. comparative
mythologists) is academically contested but the structural parallels remain.
```

## Universal Cosmological Themes

### World Tree / Axis Mundi

```
The vertical axis connecting lower, middle, and upper worlds:
  Yggdrasil (Norse): ash tree connecting 9 worlds; eagle at top,
    serpent Nidhogg at roots, squirrel Ratatoskr runs between
  Mount Meru (Hindu/Buddhist): cosmic mountain at center of universe
  Mount Olympus (Greek): home of gods (literal mountain + cosmic concept)
  Ziggurat (Mesopotamian): artificial mountain connecting earth to heaven
  World Mountain (Hindu): Mandara used to churn cosmic ocean
  Djed pillar (Egyptian): Osiris's backbone / axis
  Tree of Life (Kabbalistic): Sefirot connecting Ein Sof to world
  Ceiba tree (Maya): cosmic axis of the Popol Vuh universe
```

### Upper/Middle/Lower World Tripartition

```
COSMIC GEOGRAPHY (nearly universal):
  Upper world = gods, sky, light, order
  Middle world = humans, ordinary reality
  Lower world = dead, chthonic powers, chaos/potential

  Greek:  Olympus / Earth / Tartarus-Hades
  Norse:  Asgard / Midgard / Niflheim-Helheim
  Maya:   Thirteen heavens / Earth / Nine underworlds (Xibalba)
  Hindu:  Svarga / Bhu / Patala (14 worlds in 3 tiers)
  Slavic: Prav (divine order) / Yav (living world) / Nav (death)
  Aztec:  Thirteen heavens / Earth / Nine underworlds (Mictlan)
```

```

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│ NORSE:       Prose Edda (Snorri Sturluson ~1220 CE)          │

│ MESOPOTAMIAN:Enuma Elish (7 tablets, ~1100 BCE)              │
│              Epic of Gilgamesh (Standard Version ~1300 BCE)  │
│ HINDU:       Rigveda (~1500 BCE), Mahabharata (~400 BCE),    │
│              Ramayana (~300 BCE), Puranas (300–1200 CE)      │
│ EGYPTIAN:    Pyramid Texts (2400 BCE), Coffin Texts,         │
│              Book of the Dead (~1550 BCE), temple carvings   │
│ CELTIC:      NO pre-Christian primary sources!               │
│              Irish manuscripts 7th–12th CE (Christian monks) │
│              Welsh: Mabinogion 11th–13th CE manuscripts      │
│ MESOAMERICAN:Popol Vuh (K'iche' Maya, ~1554 CE, post-Conquest)│
│              Colonial-period Aztec codices                   │
│ SLAVIC:      NO primary mythological texts survived          │
│              Reconstructed from folklore, chronicles,        │
│              archaeology, and comparative IE evidence        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The written source problem**: most mythology was originally oral. Written sources reflect the biases of recorders — Christian monks writing Celtic or Norse myths, Spanish friars writing Aztec myths. The religion being described was often already dying or dead when written down.

## Decision Cheat Sheet — Framework Selection

| Question | Best framework |
|----------|---------------|
| Why does every culture have a hero myth? | Campbell monomyth — psychological universals |
| What does a specific symbol mean? | Jungian archetypes — shadow/anima/self |
| What social structure does a myth encode? | Dumézil trifunctionalism (IE only) |
| Why do flood myths appear everywhere? | Diffusion theory OR polygenesis from actual floods |
| What's the underlying logic of a myth? | Lévi-Strauss binary oppositions |
| How related are Greek and Vedic myths? | PIE comparative reconstruction |
| Can we trust the Norse/Celtic sources? | Only with heavy caution — Christianized transmission |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Mythology" ≠ "false religion"**: Within the culture that holds them, myths are sacred truth, not fiction. "Myth" in academic usage means sacred narrative, not falsehood.

**Greek ≠ Roman mythology**: Roman mythology is largely Greek mythology with renamed characters, but there are genuine Roman-specific elements (Janus, Quirinus, the Lares and Penates, the Roman foundation myths of Romulus/Remus). Ovid's *Metamorphoses* is a Roman literary work, not a Greek source.

**The "dying and rising god" template is contested**: Scholars like Jonathan Z. Smith argued the pattern was over-applied. Osiris doesn't really rise — he becomes king of the dead, not the living. The similarities are real but the degree of identity is debated.

**Norse sources are late and Christianized**: The Prose Edda was written by Snorri Sturluson c. 1220 — two centuries after Iceland converted to Christianity. He was a Christian intellectual writing about the old religion as history/literature. His account may have been systematized, rationalized, or altered by his perspective. The Poetic Edda is older source material but also compiled by Christian hands.
