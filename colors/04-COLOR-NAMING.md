# 04 — Color Naming

## Berlin-Kay, Language and Perception, Homer's Wine-Dark Sea

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Berlin and Kay (1969)**: *Basic Color Terms* — cross-linguistic study of 20 languages; proposed universal sequence of basic color term acquisition: (1) black + white, (2) red, (3) yellow OR green, (4) the other (yellow/green), (5) blue, (6) brown, (7-11) purple/pink/orange/gray — languages always acquire in this order, never skip; a language with 3 basic color terms always has black/white/red; deeply influential and controversial
- Why this sequence? Evolutionary/perceptual salience hypothesis: blood (red), fire (yellow), sky (blue) — focal points in environment; but also contested (many confounds in original study)
- **Homer's wine-dark sea** (οἶνοψ πόντος, *oinops pontos*): Homer describes the sea as "wine-dark" or "wine-faced" — never blue; also describes honey as "green" (*chloron meli*); Gladstone (1858) noticed ancient Greeks seemingly didn't distinguish blue; proposed they were color-blind (rejected); later Geiger (1880) noted ancient Sanskrit, Chinese, Hebrew, and Icelandic texts also lack blue terms; blue appears in languages only after they begin dyeing (indigo trade required metalwork and trade infrastructure)
- **Sapir-Whorf hypothesis** (linguistic relativity): does language shape color perception? Strong version (determinism) rejected; weak version has experimental support — speakers of languages with more basic color terms show faster discrimination in peripheral vision for those boundaries; Russian has two basic blue terms (*siniy* = dark blue, *goluboy* = light blue) → Russian speakers faster at discriminating blues at that boundary; Korean has different categories for tight-fit vs loose-fit spatial relations
- **Focal colors**: within any color category, some exemplars are more "central" — a canonical red; these focal colors are more consistent across languages than boundaries; Berlin-Kay found focal colors more universal than boundaries
- **The color blue in antiquity**: Egyptian hieroglyphs (blue = irtyu = lapis lazuli color, they had a word); Sumerian texts lack blue; ancient Chinese texts lack blue; Maya blue (specific pigment, not a general color word); the Pirahã tribe of Amazon (no number words, no color words — extreme case); modern Himba people of Namibia (no distinct blue/green word, great difficulty distinguishing blue/green but excellent at distinguishing greens that English speakers can't)
- **Color naming evolution**: new color terms coined when a new color substance appears — "orange" the color named after "orange" the fruit (the color was previously called "yellow-red" or "saffron" in English); "mauve" coined when the dye was invented (1856); "electric blue" (electricity/gas light era); digital era producing new color-name coinages
- **Color names without simple definitions**: teal (blue-green, named after Eurasian teal duck), chartreuse (yellow-green, named after French liqueur), puce (flea-colored, brownish-pink), cerise (cherry-red, French), vermilion (from vermicle/worm — kermes insect), khaki (Urdu/Hindi for "dust"), magenta (named after Battle of Magenta 1859, shortly after dye discovered)
