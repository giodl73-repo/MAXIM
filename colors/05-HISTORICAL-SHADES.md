# 05 — Historical Shades

## The Biography of Named Colors — Mauve, Puce, Gamboge, Chartreuse

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Each named shade has a specific origin story — often a substance, an event, or a place; tracking these origins reveals art history, chemistry, trade, and cultural history simultaneously
- **Mauve** (1856): William Henry Perkin, 18-year-old chemistry student at Royal College of Chemistry, London; attempted to synthesize quinine (anti-malarial) from coal tar aniline; got a reddish-brown sludge; noticed purple residue; purified to mauve-colored solution that dyed silk; patented as "Tyrian Purple" / "mauveine"; first synthetic aniline dye; launched synthetic organic chemistry and the German/British dye industry; Queen Victoria wore mauve at 1862 World Exhibition → "mauve measles" fashion epidemic; Perkin became wealthy at 18; mauveine chemistry: mixture of methylated phenazine compounds
- **Magenta** (1859): basic fuchsine dye synthesized by Verguin, France; named after Battle of Magenta (June 1859) — French/Piedmontese victory over Austria in northern Italy; vivid purplish-red; one of the three subtractive primaries in CMYK; the dye (rosaniline) coincidentally discovered same year as the battle
- **Gamboge**: yellow-orange pigment from resin of *Garcinia* trees (Southeast Asia, particularly Cambodia — Khmer/Cambodia → gamboge); brilliant, semi-transparent yellow-orange; used in Chinese and Japanese ink painting, medieval European manuscripts; toxic (violent purgative); used as watercolor, now largely replaced by cadmium yellow and synthetic alternatives; the color itself = deep golden yellow
- **Chartreuse** (yellow-green): named after Chartreuse liqueur (made by Carthusian monks, Grande Chartreuse monastery near Grenoble, France, since 1605); the color chartreuse = yellow-green (the color of the Yellow Chartreuse liqueur); "chartreuse green" vs "chartreuse yellow" — confusion because the liqueur comes in two colors; the distinctive color sits between yellow and green on the spectrum
- **Puce**: French for "flea" (*puce*); the color of a flea (dark brownish-pink, reddish-brown); Marie Antoinette is said to have described a new fabric as "puce-colored" (flea-colored), making it fashionable; contested etymology; the actual color debated (some say dark red, some dark brown, some brownish-rose)
- **Vermilion**: from Latin *vermiculus* (little worm) → Old French *vermillon* → English; refers to the kermes insect (crimson) but applied to the brilliant scarlet of mercuric sulfide (HgS); the color name and substance conflated; brilliant orange-red; Chinese red lacquerware; artists' vermilion (mercuric sulfide, now replaced by cadmium red in artists' paints)
- **Cerise**: French for "cherry" (*Prunus cerasus*); bright, vivid red; mid-19th century synthetic dye color; cherry-red
- **Tyrian purple**: see pigments/03; the color has no modern exact equivalent — described as "reddish-purple" or "crimson-purple"; different from modern purple; historical examples in dyed fabrics have been analyzed; modern reproduction shows reddish-crimson more than blue-purple
- **Prussian blue / Cobalt blue / Ultramarine**: three different blues with distinct characters; ultramarine (warm, reddish-blue), Prussian blue (greenish, very dark, intense), cobalt blue (slightly greenish, medium intensity); often confused by name
- **Indigo**: deep blue-violet, from *Indigofera*; the color "indigo" in the visible spectrum (Newton added it to the spectrum partly to get seven colors — matching the seven notes of a musical scale); many colorists question whether indigo is genuinely distinct from blue/violet or just a naming convention
- **Oxford blue vs Cambridge blue**: Oxford = dark navy blue (PMS 282); Cambridge = pale blue (PMS 284); rival traditions of exactly how blue the rival university's color is; both established in 19th century boat race tradition
- Color naming in the modern era: Pantone color of the year, Benjamin Moore and Sherwin-Williams hundreds of named paint colors (Gossamer Wing, Atmospheric, Accessible Beige — all real paint names), HTML named colors (140 standardized names), XKCD color survey (230,000 responses mapping color names to RGB values, producing names like "booger green" and "ugly pink")
