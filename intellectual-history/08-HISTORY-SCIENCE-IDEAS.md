# History of Science as Intellectual History

## The Big Picture

The history of science is the longest-standing and most developed subfield of intellectual
history. It has its own professional discipline (History of Science Society, Isis journal, 1913)
and has gone through multiple methodological revolutions — from hagiography and Whig history,
through internalist-externalist debates, to contextual, sociological, and postcolonial approaches.

## The Major Historiographical Debates

### The Internalist-Externalist Debate

```
  INTERNALIST HISTORY OF SCIENCE:
  Explain the development of science by the logic of ideas themselves.
  Scientific change = response to intellectual problems and anomalies.
  External conditions (society, economy) are irrelevant to content.

  PRACTITIONERS: Alexandre Koyré, A. Rupert Hall, I. Bernard Cohen.

  KOYRÉ (From the Closed World to the Infinite Universe, 1957):
  The Scientific Revolution was primarily a conceptual transformation.
  The shift from finite, hierarchical cosmos to infinite universe.
  Mathematical Platonism: Galileo succeeded because he mathematicized nature.

  EXTERNALIST HISTORY OF SCIENCE:
  Social, economic, political factors shape science's content, not just context.

  HESSEN THESIS (Boris Hessen, 1931):
  Newton's Principia was conditioned by the economic and technological
  needs of 17th-century capitalism (navigation, military, mining).
  Marxist externalism: first major externalist intervention.

  MERTON (Science and Society in 17th Century England, 1938):
  Puritanism and scientific activity are correlated (Puritan ethic matches
  Protestant values with scientific virtues of empiricism and utility).
  Social structure (not class struggle) conditions science.

  RESOLUTION: False dichotomy. Modern historians integrate both.
  Context shapes what problems matter, what counts as a solution.
  Internal logic shapes how scientists respond to those problems.
```

### The "Scientific Revolution" Concept

```
  QUESTION: Was there a "Scientific Revolution" 1543-1687?

  THE CASE FOR:
  Copernicus (1543) to Newton (1687) saw fundamental transformation.
  Mathematization of nature (Galileo).
  Mechanical philosophy replaced Aristotelian natural philosophy.
  New institutions: Royal Society (1660), Académie des Sciences (1666).
  New methodology: experiment, mathematical law.

  THE CASE AGAINST (Steven Shapin, The Scientific Revolution, 1996):
  "There was no such thing as the Scientific Revolution, and this is a
  book about it."
  The period was heterogeneous: no single method, no unified worldview.
  "Science" as we know it didn't exist then (natural philosophy, natural magic,
  alchemy, and what we now call science were intertwined).
  The revolution's features were contested at the time.

  MIDDLE GROUND:
  Real transformations did occur 1543-1687.
  But "revolution" overstates discontinuity; "Scientific Revolution" is a
  19th-century retrospective construction.
  The period shows the beginning of recognizably modern scientific practices,
  not their sudden emergence ex nihilo.
```

## Key Case Studies in History of Science

### Newton and Alchemy

```
  NEWTON'S SECRET LIFE (revealed by Keynes, 1936):
  Newton wrote more about alchemy and biblical prophecy than about physics.
  His private papers: over 1 million words on alchemy.

  WHAT THIS MEANS:
  The "last sorcerer" vs. "father of modern science" dichotomy is false.
  Newton's alchemy and his physics were not separate — both sought unified
  understanding of active principles in nature.
  His alchemical notion of "active principles" may have informed his conception
  of gravitational force acting at a distance.

  METHOD:
  Reading Newton's private papers (acquired by Keynes at auction of
  Portsmouth Collection, 1936) changed the historiography entirely.
  The public Newton (Principia, Opticks) and the private Newton are different.
  Lesson: intellectual history requires archival research, not just
  reading canonical published texts.
```

### Darwin and His Networks

```
  DARWIN'S DELAY (worked on Origin 20 years before publishing):
  Why did Darwin wait 1838-1859?

  TRADITIONALIST ANSWER: He was careful, gathering evidence.
  REVISIONIST (Desmond and Moore, Darwin: The Life of a Tormented Evolutionist, 1991):
  Darwin knew evolution was politically radical (associated with
  Chartism, French materialism, anti-clericalism).
  Delay was partly social fear.

  DARWIN'S NETWORKS:
  Hooker, Lyell, Huxley: Darwin strategically built support before publishing.
  The Wallace scare (1858): Wallace's letter forced Darwin's hand.
  "Darwinism" was partly a social movement organized by Huxley and company.

  LESSON: Scientific ideas are not published in isolation — they require
  social networks, institutions, and strategic timing.
```

### The Pasteur-Koch Episode

```
  PASTEUR AND KOCH (germ theory of disease, 1870s-1880s):
  Both established germ theory, but in different national and political contexts.

  FRENCH-GERMAN RIVALRY:
  Pasteur worked in context of French nationalism after 1870 defeat.
  His anthrax vaccine demonstration (1881) was partly theater/propaganda.
  Koch's methodology (Koch's postulates) was competing German science.

  LATOUR'S READING:
  Pasteur "enrolled" farmers, politicians, public health officials,
  microbes, laboratory instruments into a network.
  The microbe became real through this network.
  Science = negotiation and inscription, not just discovery.

  LESSON: Even "pure" science is embedded in national, political, and
  institutional contexts that shape what gets done and how it's presented.
```

## Science and Colonialism

```
  SCIENCE AND EMPIRE:
  European science and colonialism were deeply intertwined.

  NATURAL HISTORY AND EMPIRE:
  Linnaean taxonomy organized colonial specimens.
  Kew Gardens (London): center of colonial botanical network.
  Plant knowledge extracted from indigenous peoples rarely credited.

  TRANSPORTING CROPS:
  Cinchona (quinine) from Peru to India to fight malaria in British India.
  Rubber from Brazil to British Malaya.
  "Bioprospecting" (or "biopiracy"): systematic extraction of knowledge.

  JOSEPH NEEDHAM AND CHINESE SCIENCE:
  Science and Civilization in China (1954-): massive, still incomplete series.
  Chinese priority in many inventions: printing, gunpowder, compass, cast iron.
  NEEDHAM QUESTION: Why did modern science emerge in Europe, not China?
  Answers: political fragmentation, merchant class, Protestant values (Weber),
  different epistemic culture, specific theoretical traditions.
  The question itself is contested: assumes "modern science" is the goal.

  KNOWLEDGE APPROPRIATION:
  Indigenous mathematical and astronomical knowledge.
  Mayan mathematics, Indian positional notation (Arab transmission to Europe).
  The "transfer" narrative ignores the agency and knowledge of non-Western peoples.
```

## Revisiting the Scientific Revolution

### The Mechanical Philosophy

```
  THE MECHANICAL PHILOSOPHY (17th century):
  Nature = machine; explanations must appeal to matter in motion only.
  No occult qualities, final causes, or vitalist forces.
  Descartes (animals = machines; body = clock), Boyle, Gassendi.

  BUT: What makes bodies move? What is gravity?
  Newton: attraction at a distance is "occult" by mechanical philosophy standards.
  Newton refused to feign hypotheses about the mechanism.
  "Hypotheses non fingo" = I don't fake hypotheses (on gravity's cause).

  THE DISUNITY OF EARLY MODERN SCIENCE:
  No single "mechanical philosophy" was accepted by all.
  Vitalism, animism, alchemy, magnetism all persisted.
  Descartes, Leibniz, Newton had incompatible natural philosophies.
```

### Mathematics and Nature

```
  GALILEO: "The book of nature is written in the language of mathematics."
  Mathematical laws govern physical phenomena.

  THE GALILEAN IDEALIZATION:
  Free-fall on a frictionless inclined plane.
  These conditions never exist in nature; yet the law holds.
  Mathematical abstraction + idealization = new form of knowledge.

  KOYRÉ'S INTERNALISM:
  The mathematization of nature was a CONCEPTUAL revolution.
  Not primarily driven by practical needs or instruments.
  Galileo could have done his thought experiments without telescopes.
  The ideas were prior.

  DISPUTE:
  Zilsel (external): craftsmen's practical knowledge fed into Galileo's science.
  Koyré (internal): the conceptual shift from quality to quantity was primary.
```

## Current Approaches

### Materiality and Instruments

```
  MATERIAL TURN in history of science:
  Scientific knowledge is produced with and through material things.
  Instruments are not neutral: they shape what can be known.

  Shapin and Schaffer (Leviathan and the Air Pump):
  The air pump was a "matter of fact"-producing machine.
  Boyle's experimental program required specific social and material arrangements.
  Facts are certified by witnessing: "gentlemen witnesses" at the Royal Society.

  DASTON AND GALISON (Objectivity, 2007):
  "Objectivity" is a historical concept, not a timeless value.
  18th century: "truth-to-nature" (idealized images).
  19th century: "mechanical objectivity" (suppress interpretation; let instruments speak).
  Late 19th century: "trained judgment" (expert interpretation required).
  Each epistemic virtue responds to specific problems in scientific practice.
```

### Global History of Science

```
  GLOBAL/CONNECTED HISTORY OF SCIENCE:
  Not "diffusion" from European center but exchanges, translations, negotiations.

  PAMELA SMITH: Material knowledge and craft in early modern Europe.
  Knowledge embedded in practice, not just texts.

  KAPIL RAJ (Relocating Modern Science, 2007):
  How colonial encounters generated scientific knowledge.
  The "co-production" model: knowledge produced in negotiation between
  European scientists and local practitioners.

  MOVING BEYOND EUROCENTRISM:
  Islamic Golden Age (8th-13th centuries): algebra, optics, medicine.
  Indian mathematics: zero, positional notation, trigonometry.
  Chinese astronomy, medicine, technology.
  These were not just "pre-modern" or "practical" — they were theorized.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Scientific Revolution" is a retrospective category.**
Scientists in the 17th century didn't know they were in a "revolution." The term
was coined much later. Contemporary practice was more diverse and contested than
the standard narrative suggests.

**Internalism vs. externalism is a false dichotomy.**
No serious historian today is a pure internalist or pure externalist. The debate
was useful in forcing historians to articulate what they were doing. The synthetic
view: social conditions shape which problems matter; internal logic shapes solutions.

**The history of science is not the history of correct ideas.**
Phlogiston, ether, and caloric were not just "wrong" — they were sophisticated
theoretical frameworks that did real work. History of science that dismisses losers
as mere errors distorts the past.

**"Science and colonialism" is not anti-science.**
Historians showing science's entanglement with colonial projects are not arguing
science is bad or false. They are showing that the production of scientific knowledge
involves power relations and has political effects — as does all knowledge production.
