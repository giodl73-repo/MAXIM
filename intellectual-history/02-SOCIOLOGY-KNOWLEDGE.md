# Sociology of Knowledge: Mannheim and Merton

## The Big Picture

The sociology of knowledge asks: how do social conditions shape what people think, believe,
and call "knowledge"? It ranges from Mannheim's general theory of ideological thought to
Merton's institutional sociology of science to Bloor's radical Strong Programme.

## Marx and the Base-Superstructure Model

The founding framework for externalist intellectual history:

```
  MARX'S MODEL:

  +----------------------------------------------+
  |              SUPERSTRUCTURE                   |
  |   Law, Politics, Religion, Philosophy, Art   |
  |   (ideas, institutions, ideologies)          |
  +----------------------------------------------+
            reflects and justifies
  +----------------------------------------------+
  |               BASE                           |
  |   Economic relations of production           |
  |   (who owns the means of production,         |
  |   how labor is organized and compensated)    |
  +----------------------------------------------+

  "The ideas of the ruling class are in every epoch the ruling ideas."
  — The German Ideology (1846)

  IDEOLOGY (in Marx): False consciousness.
  Workers believe capitalism is natural/just because that belief
  serves the interests of the bourgeoisie. The "camera obscura":
  reality appears inverted.

  ECONOMIC DETERMINISM:
  Strong version: ideas simply REFLECT material base.
  Soft version: material conditions "ultimately" determine ideas,
  but there is complex mediation (Engels' later formulations).
```

### Problems with Simple Economic Determinism

```
  1. Ideas often precede the material conditions they seem to express.
     Lutheran ideas about inner conscience preceded capitalist individualism.

  2. Same material conditions can produce very different ideas.
     Industrial capitalism in Germany (Bismarckian) and England (liberal)
     produced quite different political philosophies.

  3. Ideas can themselves be causal — Weber's thesis:
     Protestant ethics were causal in the rise of capitalism,
     not merely a reflection of it.

  4. Autonomy of intellectual fields:
     Mathematics, logic, and formal systems don't obviously "reflect"
     the material base. (Mannheim exempted mathematics and natural science.)
```

## Karl Mannheim: Ideology and Utopia

The systematic theory of the social determination of knowledge.

### Core Concepts

```
  TOTAL VS. PARTICULAR IDEOLOGY:

  PARTICULAR ideology: specific beliefs of an opponent that
  serve their interests; can be "unmasked" without abandoning
  the unmasker's own cognitive standards.

  TOTAL ideology: the entire worldview of an epoch or class is
  seen as ideologically shaped. No thinker escapes this.
  Including the unmasker.

  THE MANNHEIM PARADOX:
  If all thought is socially determined (total ideology),
  then Mannheim's own theory is socially determined.
  What is its claim to truth?

  MANNHEIM'S ANSWER: RELATIONISM (not relativism)
  Different social positions yield different PARTIAL truths.
  Not "anything goes" but "different perspectives capture different aspects."
  The "free-floating intelligentsia" (freischwebende Intelligenz):
  relatively unattached to class interests;
  can synthesize partial perspectives into a wider view.

  CRITICS:
  Who decides which synthesis is best?
  The "free-floating" intelligentsia is itself a social position.
```

### Ideology and Utopia

```
  IDEOLOGY: Thought that serves to maintain the existing order.
  Distorts reality by presenting it as more stable/just than it is.
  The thought of dominant classes (typically).

  UTOPIA: Thought that transcends the existing order; points to alternatives.
  Distorts reality by presenting the possible as actual.
  The thought of subordinate classes seeking change (typically).

  BOTH are situationally determined. Neither has privileged access to truth.

  HISTORICAL ROLE OF UTOPIAS:
  Utopian thinking is necessary for social change.
  "A society which has no will toward things beyond itself, which
  has lost its utopias, tends toward exhaustion and decay."
```

## Robert Merton: Sociology of Science

Merton (Columbia, 1940s-70s) developed a systematic sociology of the social institution
of science — NOT focusing on the content of scientific knowledge (he left that to scientists)
but on the social norms and structures that make science work.

### CUDOS Norms

```
  MERTON'S FOUR NORMS OF SCIENCE (1942):

  C — COMMUNALISM (originally "Communism"):
      Scientific knowledge is a communal product; results must be shared.
      No private ownership of discoveries.
      Secrecy and proprietary claims violate science's norms.

  U — UNIVERSALISM:
      Claims to truth must be evaluated by impersonal, pre-established criteria.
      Acceptance or rejection of knowledge cannot depend on the personal
      attributes of the scientist (race, nationality, religion, class).

  D — DISINTERESTEDNESS:
      Scientists are expected to act for the communal good, not personal benefit.
      Fraud and bias are deviant, not merely inefficient.
      (Note: doesn't say scientists have no interests, just that the norm
      constrains behavior.)

  O — ORGANIZED SKEPTICISM:
      Detached scrutiny of all claims; no immune sacred zone.
      New findings are subjected to communal criticism.
      Peer review as institutional form of organized skepticism.
```

### Merton's Priority Disputes

```
  PRIORITY DISPUTES: Who discovered X first?
  Science has an unusual reward structure:
  - First to publish gets ALL the credit (Newton-Leibniz controversy)
  - Second gets nothing
  - Priority disputes are fierce: Newton vs. Leibniz (calculus),
    Wallace vs. Darwin (natural selection), numerous others

  MERTON'S EXPLANATION:
  The reward system (recognition, reputation, Nobel prizes) creates
  intense competition for priority. Scientists are rewarded for
  original contributions. This explains:
  - Simultaneous independent discoveries (multiple discovery)
  - Priority disputes
  - Self-protective behavior (priority notebooks, rushed publication)

  MATTHEW EFFECT: "To him who has, more shall be given."
  Famous scientists get more credit for the same work than unknown scientists.
  Journal papers by prominent scientists are accepted more readily.
  Named after Matthew 25:29.
```

## The Strong Programme (Edinburgh School)

David Bloor, Barry Barnes, Steven Shapin — University of Edinburgh, 1970s.

```
  STRONG PROGRAMME (Bloor, Knowledge and Social Imagery, 1976):
  Extends sociology of knowledge to the CONTENT of scientific knowledge.

  FOUR TENETS:

  1. CAUSALITY: Sociology should explain scientfic knowledge causally
     (what social conditions cause belief in X?).
     Not just explain false or rejected beliefs, but ALL beliefs.

  2. IMPARTIALITY: The same types of causal explanation apply to
     both true beliefs and false beliefs, successful and unsuccessful
     scientific programs.

  3. SYMMETRY: The same causal factors explain belief in what we
     now take to be true AND what we take to be false.
     No special "rational" explanation for the winner.

  4. REFLEXIVITY: The principles must apply to the sociology of knowledge
     itself. (Mannheim's problem, embraced not solved.)

  KEY CLAIM:
  Even the content of successful science (e.g., what is a "fact") is
  socially negotiated. Social interests shape what scientists accept.

  EXAMPLE (Shapin and Schaffer, Leviathan and the Air Pump, 1985):
  Boyle's air-pump experiments and Hobbes's critique.
  The establishment of "experimental facts" was a political and social
  achievement, not just a cognitive one.
  Different political visions (Boyle's gentlemanly democracy, Hobbes's
  sovereign authority) shaped their epistemologies.
```

### Criticisms of the Strong Programme

```
  1. SELF-UNDERMINING: If the Strong Programme is itself socially caused,
     what is its claim to be correct? (Bloor: this is fine — reflexivity
     is a virtue, not a problem.)

  2. Conflates "conditions for" with "constitution of":
     Social conditions are necessary for scientific knowledge
     (you need institutions, labs, funding), but don't determine the
     content of what is true.

  3. Empirical counterexamples: Some scientific claims resist social
     pressure. The truth about nuclear physics doesn't bend to politics.

  4. ALAN SOKAL AFFAIR (1996):
     Physicist submitted a parody postmodernist article to Social Text.
     It was accepted. Sokal argued this showed the vacuity of the
     "social construction of science" claim.
     Debates continue: Sokal's point was about epistemic standards,
     not about sociological approaches generally.
```

## Bruno Latour and Actor-Network Theory

```
  LATOUR AND WOOLGAR (Laboratory Life, 1979):
  Ethnographic study of a scientific lab.
  "Facts" are constructed through a process of inscription and negotiation.
  A "scientific fact" is a statement that is black-boxed — taken for granted.

  ACTOR-NETWORK THEORY (ANT):
  Both humans and non-humans (instruments, bacteria, machines) are "actants"
  in networks. Agency is distributed.

  EXAMPLE: Pasteur and the microbe.
  Pasteur "created" the microbe in a network of labs, farmers, public health
  officials, animals, microscopes. The microbe became real through this network.

  "We have never been modern":
  Latour argues modernity is defined by a purification (nature vs. society)
  that is never actually achieved — we always have "hybrids."

  RECEPTION:
  Highly influential in science and technology studies (STS).
  Criticized by scientists (Alan Sokal) and some historians for
  apparent implication that nature plays no role in constraining belief.
  Latour himself later distanced from extreme constructivism.
```

## Pierre Bourdieu: Field Theory

```
  BOURDIEU (The Logic of Practice, 1980; Homo Academicus, 1984):
  Applies his general social theory to intellectual fields.

  FIELD: A social space of positions structured by competition for capital.
  Each field has its own rules and stakes.
  The intellectual field: competition for intellectual capital (recognition,
  citations, prestige) according to field-specific standards.

  HABITUS: Internalized dispositions acquired through social position.
  Shapes what you notice, what you find obvious, what questions you ask.

  CULTURAL CAPITAL: Education, cultural knowledge, credentials.
  Different from economic capital; can be converted to and from it.

  APPLICATION TO INTELLECTUAL HISTORY:
  Ideas are produced by agents with specific positions in intellectual fields.
  The "free-floating intelligentsia" (Mannheim) is itself a field position.
  Bourdieu demystifies the apparent autonomy of intellectual work.

  EXAMPLE (Homo Academicus):
  French academic field in May 1968.
  Structural positions in the academy (Sorbonne vs. Grandes Ecoles,
  humanities vs. social sciences) mapped onto political positions
  during the student uprising.
```

## Engineering Bridge: Merton's CUDOS in Open-Source and Engineering Culture

Merton's four norms of science (1942) describe exactly the normative structure of healthy open-source communities and engineering organizations — and their violations explain failure modes:

| Merton's Norm | Science Form | Open-Source / Engineering Form | Violation |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------------------|-----------|
| **Communalism** | Results must be shared; no private ownership of discoveries | OSS: code must be public; contributions merged to commons; no "secret" forks | Proprietary lock-in of research results; stealth forks; knowledge hoarding |
| **Universalism** | Claims evaluated by impersonal criteria, not author's identity | Code review: the PR is evaluated on its merits, not on whether it came from a senior engineer or an intern | LGTM bias ("approving because it came from X"); HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) |
| **Disinterestedness** | Scientists act for the communal good, not personal gain | On-call rotations, documentation, test coverage — work that benefits the system over personal credit | "Not invented here" syndrome; gaming metrics (commits, PRs) for performance review |
| **Organized Skepticism** | All claims subject to communal scrutiny; no sacred zone | CI/CD: every commit tested; no code is trusted without verification; blameless postmortems apply equally to everyone | Sacred cows: "we don't question the legacy auth service"; exempting certain code from review |

**The Matthew Effect** (famous scientists get disproportionate credit) maps directly to attribution dynamics in engineering teams: senior engineers' code reviews carry more weight than junior engineers', popular open-source maintainers' opinions dominate discourse, and academic prestige translates to conference speaking slots that amplify the same voices regardless of content quality.

**Bourdieu's field theory** explains why "meritocracy" in engineering is not neutral: field-specific capital (GitHub stars, conference talks, popular libraries) is accumulated through social position, not just technical quality. The "free-floating intelligentsia" analogue is the staff engineer who can synthesize across organizational silos precisely because they are not fully captured by any single team's interests.

## Common Confusion Points

**Ideology as false consciousness vs. any worldview.**
In Marx, ideology is specifically distorting — it inverts reality. In Mannheim, "ideology"
is any thought that preserves the existing order; "utopia" challenges it. Both differ from
the casual use of "ideology" as "any set of beliefs."

**Merton's norms are prescriptive, not descriptive.**
Merton describes what scientists SHOULD do according to science's institutionalized norms.
He knew actual scientists deviate. The norms are the normative structure, not a description
of behavior. Later sociologists (Collins, Latour) studied what scientists ACTUALLY do.

**Strong Programme symmetry principle.**
Symmetry says: use the same explanatory framework for true and false beliefs. It does NOT say
true and false beliefs are equally good, or that there is no difference. It says the
sociologist should not invoke "rationality" as a special explanatory factor only for
the winning side.

**Science studies vs. anti-science.**
Most practitioners of science studies (including Bloor and Latour) believe science works and
produces real knowledge. They are asking sociological questions about HOW it produces
knowledge, not whether it does. The Sokal affair conflated provocative academic rhetoric
with scientific skepticism.
