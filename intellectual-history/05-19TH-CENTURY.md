# 19th Century: Darwin, Marx, Nietzsche

## The Big Picture

The 19th century produced three ideas that shattered the intellectual foundations of the
Enlightenment world: Darwin's evolution (humans are animals, shaped by natural selection),
Marx's historical materialism (ideas are products of material conditions, history is class
struggle), and Nietzsche's critique of values (God is dead, morality is will to power).
Together they are the intellectual roots of modernity and its discontents.

## Charles Darwin: Evolution by Natural Selection

### The Argument

```
  ON THE ORIGIN OF SPECIES (1859):

  OBSERVATION 1: Organisms produce more offspring than can survive.
  OBSERVATION 2: Offspring vary (slight, random variation).
  OBSERVATION 3: Variation is heritable.

  INFERENCE 1: STRUGGLE FOR EXISTENCE
    More offspring than resources -> competition for survival.

  INFERENCE 2: NATURAL SELECTION
    Individuals with favorable variations survive and reproduce more.
    Over generations, favorable variations become more common.

  INFERENCE 3: DESCENT WITH MODIFICATION
    Over long time, accumulated selection produces new species.
    All life shares common ancestors (universal common descent).

  KEY FEATURES:
  Gradual (not saltational): change accumulates slowly.
  Undirected: no purpose or goal in variation.
  Mechanism: selection, not vital force or divine direction.
  Historical: the geological record is the archive.
```

### Intellectual Context

```
  PREDECESSORS:
  Lamarck (1809): evolution yes, but via inheritance of acquired characters.
  Malthus (Essay on Population, 1798): struggle for existence -> Darwin's key insight.
  Lyell (Principles of Geology, 1830-33): deep time, uniformitarianism.
  (Without deep time, natural selection has no room to work.)

  SIMULTANEOUS DISCOVERY:
  Alfred Russel Wallace independently arrived at natural selection (1858).
  Darwin and Wallace jointly presented to Linnean Society, July 1858.
  Darwin published Origin 15 months later (November 1859).

  WHAT DARWIN DIDN'T KNOW:
  The mechanism of inheritance: no genetics in 1859 (Mendel published 1866,
  unknown to Darwin; rediscovered 1900; synthesis with evolution 1930s-40s).
  Modern Evolutionary Synthesis: Darwin + Mendel + population genetics.
```

### Impact on Intellectual History

```
  IMMEDIATE CONTROVERSIES:
  Religious: Huxley vs. Bishop Wilberforce, 1860 Oxford debate.
  "Are you descended from an ape on your grandfather's or grandmother's side?"
  Human evolution: The Descent of Man (1871) — humans = modified primates.

  SOCIAL DARWINISM (not Darwin):
  Herbert Spencer (1820-1903): "survival of the fittest" (Spencer's phrase,
  not Darwin's) applied to human society.
  Competition between individuals, races, nations is natural and healthy.
  Poor people are unfit; helping them is "interference with nature."
  NOTE: Darwin himself did not endorse Spencer's social application.
  Social Darwinism is a misapplication of the naturalistic fallacy.

  EUGENIC MOVEMENT (Francis Galton, Darwin's cousin):
  Improve human "stock" through selective breeding.
  Galton coined "eugenics" 1883.
  Led to forced sterilizations in US, UK, and provided "scientific"
  basis for Nazi racial policies.
  Another misapplication, but historically consequential.

  PHILOSOPHY OF BIOLOGY:
  Darwin's mechanism was teleology-free: no purpose in nature.
  Shattered the argument from design (Paley: the watchmaker).
  Required rethinking of human nature, ethics, and consciousness.
```

## Karl Marx: Historical Materialism

### The Core Theory

```
  HISTORICAL MATERIALISM:
  The structure of society is determined by the mode of production.
  Mode of production = productive forces + relations of production.

  PRODUCTIVE FORCES: technology, skills, labor power, natural resources.
  RELATIONS OF PRODUCTION: social relations structuring production
  (who owns what, who works for whom, how surplus is extracted).

  BASE AND SUPERSTRUCTURE (see also Module 02):
  Legal, political, religious, philosophical ideas = superstructure.
  Economic base determines superstructure.

  HISTORICAL STAGES:
  Asiatic -> Ancient (slave) -> Feudal -> Capitalist -> (Communist)
  Each stage defined by its dominant relations of production.
  Each contains internal contradictions that drive it to the next stage.

  DIALECTICAL MATERIALISM:
  From Hegel: dialectical logic (thesis, antithesis, synthesis).
  From Feuerbach: materialism over idealism.
  Marx: "standing Hegel on his head" — materialism with dialectical method.
```

### The Theory of Capitalism

```
  LABOR THEORY OF VALUE:
  Commodities have exchange value proportional to socially necessary
  labor time embodied in them.

  SURPLUS VALUE:
  Workers produce more value than the cost of their labor power (wages).
  The difference = surplus value, appropriated by capital owners.

  EXPLOITATION: not personal cruelty but structural feature of capitalist
  relations of production.

  CAPITAL (Das Kapital, Volume I, 1867):
  Capital is not a thing but a SOCIAL RELATION.
  M-C-M' (money buys commodities, sells for more money).
  Accumulation: reinvestment of surplus value in production.
  TENDENCY OF RATE OF PROFIT TO FALL (controversial):
  As capital deepens, the ratio of variable capital (labor) to constant
  (machinery) falls, squeezing the source of surplus value.

  ALIENATION (early Marx, Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts, 1844):
  Workers are alienated from:
  - Their product (doesn't belong to them)
  - Their activity (labor is not self-expression)
  - Their species-being (human essence as free activity)
  - Other humans (social relations distorted by money)
```

### Reception and Influence

```
  MARX'S INFLUENCE:

  MARXISM AS MOVEMENT: Engels systematized; Lenin, Stalin, Mao applied.
  Second International: German Social Democrats, labor movement.
  Bolshevik Revolution (1917): first "Marxist" state.
  Cold War: competing interpretations of Marx (Soviet, Western, Maoist).

  WESTERN MARXISM (1920s-):
  Gramsci (hegemony: cultural dominance sustains class power).
  Lukacs (class consciousness and reification).
  Frankfurt School (Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse): cultural Marxism.
  Althusser (structural Marxism; reading Capital "symptomatic").

  ACADEMIC INFLUENCE:
  Historical materialism as methodology in history, sociology, geography.
  World-systems theory (Wallerstein).
  Post-Marxism (Laclau, Mouffe): discourse and hegemony without class.

  CRITIQUES:
  Methodological: base-superstructure too rigid; ideas have causal force.
  Empirical: the revolution came to agrarian Russia, not industrial Germany.
  Political: 20th century communist regimes.
  Economic: labor theory of value vs. marginalist theory of prices.
```

## Friedrich Nietzsche: Genealogy of Values

### The Critique of Morality

```
  NIETZSCHE's central project: expose the hidden history of moral values.
  Not "are these values true?" but "how did they come to be believed?"
  "Under what conditions did man invent these value-judgments?"

  GENEALOGY OF MORALS (1887):
  Two fundamental value types:

  MASTER MORALITY:
    Strong, noble, affirmative.
    "Good" = what nobles do; "bad" = what is low, weak, slave-like.
    Values created from strength; life-affirming.

  SLAVE MORALITY:
    Created by the resentful and weak ("ressentiment").
    "Evil" = what the strong do; "good" = what is humble, meek.
    Christianity as slave morality: "the meek shall inherit the earth."
    Reactive: defines itself against the master.
    Historical triumph of slave morality in Western civilization.

  NIETZSCHE IS NOT ADVOCATING CRUELTY:
  He is doing genealogy — showing that morality has a history and
  power relations behind it. The claim is descriptive/genealogical,
  not a prescription to "be cruel."
```

### "God is Dead"

```
  THE GAY SCIENCE (1882):
  "God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him."

  NOT a theological claim but a cultural diagnosis:
  The Christian-moral framework that gave meaning to European civilization
  is no longer credible. We cannot go back to it.
  Consequence: the loss of meaning, the advent of nihilism.

  NIHILISM:
  Passive nihilism: nothing matters; despair.
  Active nihilism: destructive clearing away of old values.
  REVALUATION OF ALL VALUES: creating new values beyond Christian morality.

  THE ÜBERMENSCH (Overman):
  Thus Spoke Zarathustra (1883-85).
  Not a political figure or racial category (Nazi misappropriation).
  The self-overcoming individual who creates values in the absence of
  given moral frameworks. An ideal, not a political program.

  WILL TO POWER:
  The fundamental drive is not to survive or seek pleasure,
  but to expand, overcome, and create.
  Applied to knowledge: even the pursuit of truth is will to power.
```

### Nietzsche and the Critique of Truth

```
  PERSPECTIVISM:
  There are no facts, only interpretations.
  Every claim is made from a particular perspective.
  "Objective" knowledge is an illusion or a particular kind of will to power.

  THE PROBLEM OF SCIENCE:
  Nietzsche valued science's honesty but worried about "will to truth":
  the demand for certainty is a new form of asceticism.
  Science will eventually undermine itself (it will show that the drive
  for truth is not itself grounded in truth).

  INFLUENCE:
  Poststructuralism (Foucault, Derrida): genealogy and perspectivism.
  Existentialism (Heidegger, Sartre): authenticity in the absence of given values.
  Post-truth debates: Nietzsche's perspectivism misread as license for denialism.
```

## Other Major 19th-Century Currents

### Positivism (Comte)

```
  AUGUSTE COMTE (1798-1857):
  Law of Three Stages:
  1. Theological: supernatural explanation
  2. Metaphysical: abstract forces and essences
  3. Positive: empirical science, observation, laws

  Society has passed through stages 1 and 2; entering 3.
  Sociology (Comte invented the term) will be the "queen of sciences."
  Scientific understanding of society will enable rational reform.

  INFLUENCE: British utilitarianism, social science, sociology.
  CRITIQUE: Overly optimistic about "social physics."
  Auguste Comte invented sociology AND was personally quite authoritarian.
```

### Utilitarianism (Mill)

```
  JOHN STUART MILL (1806-1873):
  Utilitarianism: the right act maximizes total happiness.
  "The greatest happiness of the greatest number."

  ON LIBERTY (1859):
  The harm principle: the only legitimate reason for limiting liberty
  is to prevent harm to others. No harm to self: no state business.
  Defense of free speech: bad ideas need to be refuted, not suppressed.

  THE SUBJECTION OF WOMEN (1869):
  Legal subordination of women is wrong; based on custom, not reason.
  Gender difference is largely social construction.
  (Co-written with Harriet Taylor.)

  MILL ON LOGIC (A System of Logic, 1843):
  Induction as the basis of empirical knowledge.
  Mill's methods (agreement, difference, concomitant variation):
  causal inference from observation.
```

### Hegel and German Idealism

```
  HEGEL (1770-1831):
  The Phenomenology of Spirit (1807): mind coming to know itself.
  The Logic (1812-16): logic as the structure of reality itself.

  ABSOLUTE IDEALISM:
  Reality is ultimately mind (Geist) unfolding itself through history.
  History is the process of Geist becoming self-conscious.
  The Prussian state (ca. 1820): approached the actualization of Geist.

  DIALECTIC:
  Not mechanical thesis-antithesis-synthesis (a caricature).
  Rather: concepts contain internal tensions that drive their development.
  The slave-master dialectic in Phenomenology: each consciousness requires
  recognition from another. The master needs the slave; the slave becomes free.

  HEGEL'S INFLUENCE:
  Left Hegelians: applied dialectic to critique religion and society.
  Marx: materialist inversion of Hegel.
  Right Hegelians: conservative reading (existing is rational).
  20th century: existentialism, phenomenology, critical theory.
```

## Engineering Bridge: Darwin's Mechanism in Computation

Darwin's three-component mechanism (variation + selection + inheritance) is not just analogous to genetic algorithms — it is the exact computational abstraction they implement. The MIT TCS framing: Darwin's mechanism is a search algorithm over a fitness landscape.

**Genetic algorithms (Holland, 1975)** encode Darwin's mechanism directly:
- **Variation**: mutation operators (bit flips, crossover) generate candidate solutions with random perturbations — exactly "offspring vary (slight, random variation)"
- **Selection**: fitness function evaluates candidates; high-fitness candidates are retained with higher probability — exactly "individuals with favorable variations survive and reproduce more"
- **Inheritance**: the next generation is seeded from survivors — exactly "variation is heritable"

The algorithm does not converge because it is "smart" — it converges because the fitness landscape has structure that selection can exploit over accumulated iterations. Darwin's insight is that no designer is needed; local selection pressure, applied repeatedly, produces global complexity.

**Evolutionary computation taxonomy**:
- Genetic algorithms: fixed-length binary strings; crossover-dominant
- Genetic programming (Koza): variable-length parse trees; evolves programs
- Evolutionary strategies: real-valued vectors; mutation-dominant; used in neural architecture search (NAS)
- Neuroevolution (NEAT, OpenAI ES): evolves neural network weights/topology

**The fitness landscape as a formal object**: evolutionary algorithms operate on a fitness function f: genotype-space → ℝ. The algorithm's performance depends entirely on the landscape's structure — smooth landscapes (with a clear gradient) favor gradient descent; rugged landscapes (many local optima) favor stochastic search like EA. This is the formal bridge between evolutionary biology and optimization theory.

**Inheritance without DNA — the computational generalization**: inheritance in GAs is not biological; it is the preservation of information across generations. Any system with variation + selection + inheritance will exhibit evolutionary dynamics. This is why evolutionary dynamics appear in markets (firms vary, markets select, successful practices spread), programming language ecosystems (languages vary, adoption selects, features are copied), and software architectures (patterns vary, production incidents select, surviving patterns spread).

## Common Confusion Points

**Darwin did not invent evolution.**
Lamarck, Chambers, and others had evolution theories before Darwin.
Darwin's contribution was NATURAL SELECTION as the mechanism. This is what made
evolution scientifically credible and teleology-free.

**Marx and Marxism are not the same.**
Marx wrote in specific 19th-century contexts. Marxism as applied by Lenin, Stalin, Mao
made claims Marx never made. The "dictatorship of the proletariat" was a specific transitional
concept in Marx; it became something else in Soviet practice.

**Nietzsche was not a fascist or a proto-Nazi.**
His sister Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche was a nationalist; she edited his papers selectively.
Nietzsche was contemptuous of German nationalism and anti-Semitism. He broke with Wagner
partly over these issues. Nazi appropriation of Nietzsche was a deliberate distortion.

**Base-superstructure as layered architecture.** Marx's model has a direct structural analogue in systems engineering: the infrastructure layer (hardware, OS, network) determines what the platform layer (runtimes, databases, service mesh) can do, which in turn constrains what application logic is possible. The "superstructure" (the business logic, the user interface, the organizational policies) is not determined in a simple linear way — but it is constrained by the base. Changing the base (migrating from on-prem to cloud) does not automatically change the superstructure (organizational structures, development practices), but it creates new contradictions that eventually force the superstructure to adapt. This is exactly Marx's "ultimately determines, with complex mediation."

**"Survival of the fittest" is Spencer's phrase, not Darwin's.**
Darwin used it in later editions of the Origin only as a shorthand for natural selection.
Spencer's meaning (competitive, quasi-economic) was not Darwin's. Darwin emphasized
fit to environment, not "fitness" as general superiority.
