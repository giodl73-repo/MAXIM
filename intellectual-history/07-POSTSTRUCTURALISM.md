# Structuralism and Poststructuralism

## The Big Picture

Structuralism (Saussure, Lévi-Strauss) analyzed cultural phenomena as systems of differences.
Poststructuralism (Derrida, Foucault, Deleuze) then showed that these structures are unstable,
power-laden, and always already deconstructing themselves. Both movements dominated French
intellectual life from the 1960s-1980s and transformed literary theory, cultural studies,
and historiography internationally.

## Structuralism: Saussure's Legacy

### Ferdinand de Saussure (1857-1913)

```
  COURSE IN GENERAL LINGUISTICS (1916, reconstructed from student notes):
  Not published by Saussure; assembled posthumously.

  SIGN = SIGNIFIER + SIGNIFIED
  Signifier: sound-image (acoustic pattern)
  Signified: concept (mental content)
  Sign: arbitrary combination of the two.

  ARBITRARINESS:
  The connection between signifier and signified is conventional, not natural.
  English "tree" and French "arbre" signify the same concept.
  The sounds are arbitrary; the concept is arbitrary.

  LANGUE VS. PAROLE:
  Langue: the system of language (rules, structure, shared by community).
  Parole: individual speech acts (actual utterances).
  Linguistics studies langue; parole is the data.

  SYNCHRONY VS. DIACHRONY:
  Synchronic: study of language at a moment (the system as it stands).
  Diachronic: study of language change over time (historical linguistics).
  Saussure argued synchronic study is foundational.

  MEANING AS DIFFERENCE:
  "In language there are only differences without positive terms."
  "Dog" means what it means not by any intrinsic property but by
  differing from "cat," "log," "cog," "hog," etc.
  Structure is relational all the way down; no positive essences.
```

### Lévi-Strauss: Structural Anthropology

```
  CLAUDE LEVI-STRAUSS (1908-2009):
  Applied structural linguistics to anthropology.

  KINSHIP (Elementary Structures of Kinship, 1949):
  Incest taboo is universal; kinship systems are structured transformations
  of basic exchange relations. Women are "exchanged" in social systems.
  The structure is binary oppositions (nature/culture, raw/cooked, etc.)

  MYTH (Mythologiques, 1964-71):
  Myths of different cultures are variants of the same structural patterns.
  Each myth mediates binary oppositions (life/death, nature/culture).
  Meaning is in the structure, not in any individual symbol.

  THE "BRICOLEUR" METAPHOR:
  Savage thought works like a bricoleur (handyman): uses available
  cultural elements in new combinations.
  Scientific thought works like an engineer: uses purpose-built tools.
  Both are valid; savage thought is not inferior.

  CRITIQUE (Derrida, Sartre):
  Sartre: structuralism denies human freedom and history.
  Derrida: Levi-Strauss's structure has a concealed center — he privileges
  "nature" and "presence" in ways that undermine his own framework.
```

### Roland Barthes: Semiotics and Myth

```
  BARTHES (Mythologies, 1957; S/Z, 1970; Image Music Text, 1977):

  MYTHOLOGIES:
  Analyzes bourgeois cultural myths (wrestling, wine, the face of Garbo).
  MYTH = second-order signification.
  First order: sign (signifier + signified).
  Second order: the sign itself becomes a signifier for a second concept.
  Example: a photograph of a French soldier saluting the flag = MYTH of empire.
  The photograph is factual; the myth is ideological.

  THE DEATH OF THE AUTHOR (1967):
  Once a text is written, the author's intentions are irrelevant.
  A text is "a tissue of quotations" from many cultural codes.
  Meaning is produced by the READER, not determined by the author.
  (Contrast: Cambridge School's intentionalism.)

  S/Z:
  Close reading of Balzac's Sarrasine.
  Five codes structure any classical narrative text.
  "Writerly" (scriptible) vs. "readerly" (lisible) texts.
```

## Jacques Derrida: Deconstruction

```
  DERRIDA (1930-2004):
  Of Grammatology (1967), Writing and Difference (1967), Margins of Philosophy (1982)

  LOGOCENTRISM:
  Western philosophy privileges:
    Speech over writing
    Presence over absence
    Identity over difference
    The original over the copy

  This is "logocentrism": the assumption that thought is a form of
  self-present logos (reason, speech, meaning) that writing merely
  transcribes imperfectly.

  THE SUPPLEMENT:
  Writing is treated as a supplement to speech — secondary, derivative.
  But Derrida shows writing precedes speech: the logic of the supplement
  reveals that the "original" (speech) is itself always already supplemented,
  marked by absence and différance.

  DIFFERANCE (with 'a' — neither differs nor defers):
  Neologism deliberately undetectable in speech (same pronunciation as "difference").
  Meaning is never present; it is always:
    (1) Differential: produced by difference from other signs.
    (2) Deferred: meaning is always postponed, never fully present.

  DECONSTRUCTION:
  Not destruction but a close reading that reveals how texts
  undermine their own stated intentions.
  The excluded, suppressed term in a binary opposition (writing/speech,
  absence/presence, woman/man) can be shown to be foundational.
  Reversal, then displacement: not just reverse the hierarchy,
  but show that the opposition itself is unstable.

  TRACE:
  No sign is pure presence. Every sign carries the trace of what it is not.
  The trace of absence in presence, of the other in the same.
```

## Michel Foucault: Power-Knowledge

```
  FOUCAULT (1926-1984):
  Archaeology of Knowledge (1969), Discipline and Punish (1975),
  History of Sexuality (1976-84)

  (See also Module 02 for Foucault's methodology.)

  DISCOURSE:
  A system of statements governed by rules about what can be said,
  by whom, under what conditions.
  Discourse is not just language but a set of practices.
  Medical discourse: what counts as illness, who can diagnose, what treatments are valid.

  POWER-KNOWLEDGE (pouvoir-savoir):
  Power and knowledge are not separate; they constitute each other.
  Knowledge claims always presuppose and exercise power.
  Medical knowledge produces patients; criminological knowledge produces criminals.
  "There is no power relation without the correlative constitution of a field of knowledge."

  DISCIPLINE AND PUNISH:
  Emergence of the prison as dominant punishment form in 18th-19th C.
  Not from humanitarian reform but from a new technology of power: discipline.
  Panopticon (Bentham): prison designed so inmates never know when they are watched.
  Constant potential surveillance = self-regulation.
  "Disciplinary society": hospitals, schools, barracks, factories use same disciplinary technologies.
  The "docile body" is produced by power.

  BIOPOWER / BIOPOLITICS:
  The History of Sexuality: emergence of "sexuality" as a concept is a product of power.
  Modern power operates not just by repression but by incitement and classification.
  Biopolitics: management of populations as living entities (birth rate, death rate, health).
  Security, territory, population = lectures at College de France.

  GOVERNMENTALITY:
  Modern power works not by overt coercion but through "conduct of conduct":
  governing how people govern themselves.
  Neoliberalism as governmentality: subjects are "entrepreneurs of themselves."
```

## Jean-François Lyotard: Postmodernism

```
  LYOTARD (The Postmodern Condition, 1979):
  Report on Knowledge commissioned by the Quebec government.

  METANARRATIVES:
  Grand narratives that legitimate knowledge: Enlightenment (emancipation through reason),
  Marxism (liberation through class struggle), science (progress toward truth).

  POSTMODERN CONDITION:
  "Incredulity toward metanarratives."
  These grand legitimating narratives have lost their credibility.
  Not a nihilism but a pluralism: knowledge is legitimate through local, immanent criteria.

  LANGUAGE GAMES (from Wittgenstein):
  Different discourses operate by different rules (science, ethics, narrative, politics).
  The mistake is to impose the rules of one game on another.
  Science tried to delegitimate narrative knowledge; narrative gets its revenge.

  DIFFEREND:
  A dispute where the wrong done to the plaintiff cannot be expressed in the
  dominant idiom of the tribunal. The Holocaust as differend: no language
  exists to speak it that does not already distort it.

  RECEPTION:
  "Postmodernism" became an academic battleground.
  Sokal, Sokal and Bricmont (Fashionable Nonsense, 1997): critique of
  French theorists misusing scientific concepts.
  Habermas: postmodernism is a neo-conservatism masquerading as radicalism.
```

## Gilles Deleuze: Difference and Repetition

```
  DELEUZE (1925-1995):
  Difference and Repetition (1968), Anti-Oedipus (1972, with Guattari),
  A Thousand Plateaus (1980, with Guattari)

  DIFFERENCE WITHOUT NEGATION:
  Western metaphysics thinks difference in terms of identity and negation:
  A is different from B = A is not-B.
  Deleuze: difference is primary, positive, not dependent on identity.

  RHIZOME (with Guattari):
  Traditional thought is arborescent (tree-shaped): hierarchical, with roots,
  trunk, branches. One origin, one logic.
  Alternative: rhizome. Underground network with no beginning, no center, no end.
  Multiple connections; no hierarchical organization.
  The rhizome is the model for networked, non-hierarchical thought.

  DESIRE AS PRODUCTIVE:
  Against Freud (and Lacan): desire is not lack but production.
  Desiring-machines: not a metaphor but a literal claim about how desire works.
  "There is no such thing as the desire for something one lacks."

  "A THOUSAND PLATEAUS" as form:
  The book is itself rhizomatic: chapters can be read in any order.
  A "plateau" = a region of intensity that does not culminate in climax.
```

## The Anglo-American Reception

```
  RECEPTION IN US AND UK:

  LITERARY THEORY (1970s-90s):
  Derrida, Foucault, Lacan, Barthes imported to literature departments.
  Deconstruction became a reading practice (de Man at Yale).
  New Historicism (Greenblatt): literature in its power-knowledge context.
  Cultural Studies (Birmingham): Gramsci + Stuart Hall.

  FEMINIST THEORY:
  Butler (Gender Trouble, 1990): gender is performative, not natural.
  Building on de Beauvoir and Foucault: gender is a citational practice.

  POSTCOLONIAL THEORY (Module 06 in social history):
  Said (Orientalism): discourse-theoretic analysis of colonial knowledge.
  Bhabha: hybridity, mimicry, ambivalence in colonial discourse.
  Spivak: "Can the Subaltern Speak?" — translation of Derrida into
  postcolonial context.

  BACKLASH:
  Sokal Affair (1996): "Transgressing the Boundaries" published in Social Text.
  Responses: science studies scholars, Habermas, Frankfurt School.
  "Culture Wars" in US universities over postmodern literary theory.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Deconstruction is not destruction.**
Deconstruction does not argue that texts mean nothing or are meaningless.
It close-reads texts to show internal tensions and the ways they undermine
their stated positions. It is a reading practice, not a nihilism.

**Foucault is not a relativist about power.**
Foucault shows that "knowledge" is power-laden; he does not argue that all
truth-claims are equally valid. His genealogies have normative implications
(he was politically engaged). The claim that power-knowledge structures exist
does not entail that we cannot critique them.

**Postmodernism and poststructuralism are not the same.**
Poststructuralism is a specific theoretical movement (Derrida, Foucault, Deleuze)
responding to structuralism. Postmodernism is a broader cultural and aesthetic
condition or period description. Lyotard's "postmodern condition" uses both.
The terms are often used interchangeably (incorrectly).

**Barthes's "Death of the Author" is not a blanket claim.**
It applies to the question of what determines a text's meaning (not the author).
It does not mean: authors don't exist, or intent is irrelevant to biography.
The move is: shift authority over meaning from the author to the reader/text.
