# Intellectual History — Landscape and Taxonomy

## The Big Picture

Intellectual history is the study of ideas, their origins, transformations, and social
conditions of production. It sits at the intersection of history, philosophy, sociology,
and the history of science. The field asks: where do ideas come from, how do they change,
and why do some prevail while others vanish?

## The Central Questions

Intellectual history revolves around a few persistent methodological disputes:

```
  +----------------------------------------------------------+
  |           THE CORE METHODOLOGICAL DEBATES                |
  |                                                          |
  |  QUESTION 1: Autonomy of ideas                           |
  |  Do ideas have their own logic independent of society?   |
  |  OR are they determined by social/economic conditions?   |
  |                                                          |
  |  Internalists: Lovejoy's "unit ideas," analytic          |
  |   philosophy of science                                  |
  |  Externalists: Mannheim (sociology of knowledge),        |
  |   Merton (sociology of science), Marxist history         |
  |  Contextualists: Cambridge School (Quentin Skinner)      |
  |   — ideas as speech acts in specific contexts            |
  |                                                          |
  |  QUESTION 2: Continuity vs. rupture                      |
  |  Do ideas evolve gradually?                              |
  |  OR are there sudden breaks (Kuhn's paradigm shifts)?    |
  |                                                          |
  |  Continuists: cumulative science, evolutionary view      |
  |  Rupturists: Kuhn, Bachelard, Foucault (episteme shifts) |
  |                                                          |
  |  QUESTION 3: Who produces ideas?                         |
  |  Great thinkers? Social forces? Disciplines?             |
  |  Institutions? Material conditions?                      |
  +----------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Major Schools of Intellectual History

### 1. History of Philosophy (Internalist Approach)

Traces ideas through primary texts, emphasizing logical argument and conceptual development.

```
  Practitioners: Bertrand Russell, Frederick Copleston
  Method: read texts carefully; trace logical development of arguments
  Focus: the ideas themselves — Plato's Forms, Kant's categories, etc.

  STRENGTH: Rigorous engagement with actual arguments
  WEAKNESS: Can be anachronistic (reading modern concerns into past texts)
            Ignores social conditions of idea production
```

### 2. Cambridge School (Contextualist)

Quentin Skinner, John Pocock, John Dunn. Founded in 1960s Cambridge.

```
  CORE CLAIM: To understand a text, you must reconstruct the
  "linguistic context" in which it was written — what questions
  the author was addressing, what conventions they used.

  IDEAS AS SPEECH ACTS:
  Following J.L. Austin: statements DO things (Skinner applies
  speech act theory to intellectual history).
  Machiavelli wrote The Prince to do something in Florentine
  politics, not to give timeless advice.

  "Texts cannot be understood apart from the historical contexts
  in which they were written."

  STRENGTH: Avoids anachronism; grounds ideas in specific contexts
  WEAKNESS: May over-privilege intention; underweights reception
```

### 3. Sociology of Knowledge (Mannheim)

```
  KARL MANNHEIM (Ideology and Utopia, 1929):
  All thought is "situated" — shaped by the social position of the thinker.

  IDEOLOGY: worldview that legitimizes the existing order
  UTOPIA: worldview that challenges the existing order
  RELATIONISM (not relativism): different groups see partial truths;
  the "free-floating intelligentsia" can achieve wider perspective.

  KEY TENSION: If all thought is socially situated, is sociology of
  knowledge itself so situated? (Mannheim's problem)
```

### 4. History of Science (Kuhn, Lakatos, Feyerabend)

```
  See 03-SCIENTIFIC-REVOLUTIONS.md for full treatment.

  KUHN: Science proceeds via paradigms. Normal science within,
  revolutions between paradigms. Paradigms are incommensurable.

  LAKATOS: Scientific Research Programmes. Hard core + protective belt.
  More refined than Kuhn; accounts for theory change within programmes.

  FEYERABEND: "Anything goes." Science is rhetoric; methods are varied;
  no single method works for all science.
```

### 5. Poststructuralism and Discourse Analysis

```
  FOUCAULT: Ideas are embedded in "discourses" — power-laden systems
  of knowledge. Archaeology of knowledge: how epistemes shift.
  Genealogy (after Nietzsche): trace origins to power relations.

  DERRIDA: Deconstruction. Meaning is deferred; texts undermine themselves.
  The "trace" of the excluded term haunts the privileged term.

  See 07-POSTSTRUCTURALISM.md for full treatment.
```

## Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Mentalités** | Collective mindsets and worldviews of an era (Annales School) |
| **Paradigm** | Kuhn's term: shared assumptions, exemplars, frameworks of a scientific community |
| **Discourse** | Foucault: a system of knowledge/power that defines what can be said/thought |
| **Episteme** | Foucault: the underlying structure of knowledge in an era (like Kuhn's paradigm) |
| **Ideology** | Mannheim: belief system tied to social position; Marx: false consciousness |
| **Begriffsgeschichte** | Reinhart Koselleck: history of concepts (German tradition) |
| **Intertextuality** | Ideas as network of references to other texts (Kristeva, Bakhtin) |
| **Reception history** | How texts were read, adapted, misread across time |

## Periodization

```
  CLASSICAL (pre-500 CE):      Greek rationalism, Roman law, Christian theology
  MEDIEVAL (500-1400):         Scholasticism, Islamic philosophy, Averroes, Aquinas
  RENAISSANCE (1350-1600):     Recovery of classical texts, humanism
  SCIENTIFIC REVOLUTION:       Copernicus, Galileo, Newton — new natural philosophy
  ENLIGHTENMENT (1688-1800):   Reason, progress, critique of authority
  ROMANTIC REACTION (1780-1850): Against Enlightenment rationalism; feeling, history, nation
  POSITIVISM (1830-1880):      Comte, Mill — empiricism, progress, science as model
  LATE 19th CENTURY:           Darwin (evolution), Marx (historical materialism),
                                Nietzsche (critique of values)
  MODERNITY (1890-1940):       Phenomenology, psychoanalysis, logical positivism
  MID 20TH CENTURY:            Existentialism, Frankfurt School, structuralism
  LATE 20TH CENTURY:           Poststructuralism, science studies, linguistic turn
  CONTEMPORARY (2000-):        Digital humanities, global intellectual history
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Intellectual history vs. history of philosophy.**
History of philosophy focuses on logical arguments in canonical texts. Intellectual history
is broader: it includes ideas from science, politics, literature, and asks about social
context, reception, and institutional conditions.

**Intellectual history vs. cultural history.**
Intellectual history traditionally focused on elite thinkers and written ideas. Cultural
history (and its offshoot, the "new cultural history") brought in popular culture, practices,
and material objects. The boundary is porous; many historians work across both.

**Internalism vs. externalism is a false dichotomy.**
Few historians today are pure internalists or externalists. Most combine attention to
the internal logic of ideas with attention to their social and institutional context.
The Cambridge School provides a middle path via linguistic contextualism.

**"The Enlightenment" is contested.**
Whether there was one Enlightenment or many (French, Scottish, German, American) is debated.
Whether it was uniformly progressive or carried colonial and racial assumptions is actively
contested in contemporary scholarship.
