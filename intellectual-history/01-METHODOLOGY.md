# Methodology: How to Write the History of Ideas

## The Big Picture

Writing intellectual history requires choices: What counts as an "idea"? What is its relevant
context? How do you avoid reading present concerns into past texts (anachronism)? The major
methodological schools disagree sharply, and their debates parallel disputes in the philosophy
of history and interpretation theory.

## The Problem of Anachronism

The central methodological danger in intellectual history:

```
  ANACHRONISM: Reading present concepts, questions, and concerns
  into past texts that were written with different purposes.

  EXAMPLES:
  Reading Plato as "anticipating" modern epistemology
  Reading Newton as a scientist (he was a natural philosopher + alchemist)
  Reading Machiavelli as an amoral "realist" in modern IR theory terms
  Reading Darwin as a social Darwinist (he largely wasn't)

  WHY IT MATTERS:
  Distorts what past thinkers were actually doing.
  Misses what problems they were actually solving.
  Projects our categories onto their world.

  PREVENTION (Skinner's approach):
  Ask: What questions was this text written to address?
  What conventions governed writing in this genre at this time?
  What counts as a legitimate move in this discourse?
```

## Quentin Skinner and the Cambridge School

The most influential methodological intervention of the late 20th century.

```
  SKINNER'S CORE THESES (Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas, 1969):

  1. A text cannot be understood apart from its linguistic context.
     The meaning of words and phrases changes over time.
     You cannot assume past authors used terms as we do.

  2. Ideas are SPEECH ACTS.
     Drawing on J.L. Austin: statements don't just describe — they do things.
     "Illocutionary force": what the author was doing in writing this text.
     Writing a treatise on sovereignty is not the same act as writing
     a tract against tyranny, even if they share vocabulary.

  3. Recover the author's "intentions" — but understood broadly:
     Not psychological intentions, but the publicly recoverable
     communicative intention in a specific linguistic context.

  METHOD:
  Step 1: Establish the linguistic context (what vocabulary was available?
           what conventions governed this genre? who was the audience?)
  Step 2: Reconstruct the specific questions or problems the author addressed.
  Step 3: Identify the illocutionary force — what the author was doing.

  RESULT: Machiavelli was doing something very specific in Florentine
  humanist political discourse. Reading him as a timeless advisor
  on power politics is anachronistic.
```

### Criticisms of the Cambridge School

```
  1. Reception problem: even if we recover "authorial intent," texts
     are later read, misread, and transformed. History of reception
     matters as much as original intention.

  2. Language vs. extra-linguistic: focusing on linguistic context
     may underweight economic, material, and institutional factors.

  3. Circles back to intentionalism: arguably just relocates the
     "what did the author mean?" question to the communal level.

  4. Privileged objects: works best for canonical political philosophy
     texts; less applicable to scientific or literary works.
```

## Arthur Lovejoy and the History of Unit Ideas

```
  LOVEJOY (The Great Chain of Being, 1936):
  Method: trace a single "unit idea" across texts and periods.

  UNIT IDEA: a basic philosophical concept or assumption that
  recurs across different intellectual contexts.

  Examples Lovejoy traced:
  - The Great Chain of Being: plenitude, continuity, gradation
    from God/Being through all creatures.
  - "Nature" as normative standard
  - Primitivism vs. progressivism

  STRENGTH: Shows how a single idea changes meaning across contexts.
  WEAKNESS (Skinner's critique): Unit ideas are abstracted from context.
    The "same" idea means different things in different contexts.
    Continuity may be illusory — you're comparing different speech acts.
```

## Reinhart Koselleck and Begriffsgeschichte

```
  KOSELLECK (Futures Past, 1979; Geschichtliche Grundbegriffe, 1972-97):
  Begriffsgeschichte = History of Concepts (German tradition)

  KEY DISTINCTION:
  Sachgeschichte: history of things/events
  Begriffsgeschichte: history of the concepts used to describe them

  CLAIM: Concepts are not just labels for pre-existing realities.
  Concepts structure experience; shifts in concepts mark historical change.

  "SATTELZEIT" (Saddle Period): ca. 1750-1850, when many modern
  political concepts acquired their current meaning:
  - "History" became a singular, self-referential concept (die Geschichte)
  - "Revolution" shifted from cyclical return to forward rupture
  - "Progress," "class," "ideology" all acquired new senses

  TEMPORAL LAYERS:
  Koselleck analyzed time in concepts:
    Erfahrungsraum (space of experience): past present in now
    Erwartungshorizont (horizon of expectation): future anticipated
  Historical consciousness is shaped by the gap between these.

  INFLUENCE: Deep influence on German historiography; less in Anglophone world.
```

## Foucault and Genealogy / Archaeology

```
  FOUCAULT offers two methods:

  ARCHAEOLOGY (The Archaeology of Knowledge, 1969):
  Analysis of "discourses" — regularities in what is said and what
  is excluded in a given period and domain.
  "Episteme": the underlying structure of knowledge that determines
  what counts as valid knowledge in an era.
  The episteme shifts discontinuously (like Kuhn's paradigm shifts,
  but more radical — the subject itself changes).

  GENEALOGY (Discipline and Punish, 1975; History of Sexuality, 1976):
  Trace the origins of concepts and institutions to power relations.
  Following Nietzsche: there is no pure origin, only descent (Herkunft)
  and emergence (Entstehung) in conditions of struggle.
  Not causal history but "effective history" — showing how contingent
  the present actually is.

  EXAMPLE (punishment):
  The modern prison did not emerge from humanitarian concern.
  It emerged from techniques of discipline developed in schools,
  barracks, hospitals — a "carceral society."
  The soul is produced by power, not natural.

  RELATION TO INTELLECTUAL HISTORY:
  Foucault's genealogies ARE intellectual histories of sorts,
  but they reject the idea that ideas have intrinsic development.
  They show how "truths" are effects of power-knowledge.
```

## The Linguistic Turn

```
  THE "LINGUISTIC TURN" (1960s-1980s):
  Broad shift in humanities: language is not transparent medium
  through which reality is described, but constitutive of reality.

  Influences:
  - Ferdinand de Saussure: language as system of differences
  - Ludwig Wittgenstein: meaning as use; language games
  - J.L. Austin: performative utterances; speech acts
  - Roland Barthes, Derrida: texts as self-undermining
  - Hayden White: history as narrative with rhetorical structure

  EFFECT ON INTELLECTUAL HISTORY:
  Ideas are not propositions about an independent reality,
  but moves within a linguistic and discursive field.

  REACTION (Roger Chartier, others):
  "Social history strikes back."
  Language and discourse don't float free of material conditions,
  institutions, and practices.
```

## Whig History

```
  WHIG HISTORY (Herbert Butterfield, 1931):
  Reading history as a story of progressive improvement toward the present.

  Named for the Whig interpretation of English history:
  a march from tyranny to liberty, culminating in the Glorious Revolution.

  IN INTELLECTUAL HISTORY:
  Reading the history of science as a march toward modern science.
  Reading past philosophy as anticipating current positions.
  Dismissing "dead ends" as errors rather than understanding them on their terms.

  PROBLEM: Distorts past by projecting present standards onto it.
  Selects and interprets evidence to confirm the "progress" story.
  Makes intellectual history self-congratulatory.

  ALTERNATIVE: Try to understand why people held the views they did —
  what problems they were solving, what evidence they had,
  what the alternatives were.
```

## Practical Research Methods

```
  PRIMARY SOURCE READING:
  - Read the text carefully; note what it says and what it doesn't.
  - Identify genre (treatise, letter, pamphlet, lecture) — affects interpretation.
  - Find contextual documents: correspondence, criticism, responses.
  - Read contemporary and near-contemporary reception.

  SECONDARY SOURCE STRATEGY:
  - Historiography: how have previous historians interpreted this?
  - Identify the key scholarly debates.
  - Locate your interpretation in relation to existing scholarship.

  AVOIDING PRESENTISM:
  - Ask: what was the range of positions on this question at this time?
  - What could and could not be said (Koselleck's linguistic horizon)?
  - What would success look like to this author in their context?

  CITATION AND EVIDENCE:
  - Every claim about what an author "meant" needs textual evidence.
  - Reception claims need evidence of reception, not just inference.
  - Context claims need contemporary primary sources, not just assertion.
```

## Engineering Bridge: The Hermeneutic Circle as Iterative Refinement

The hermeneutic circle — understanding parts requires the whole; understanding the whole requires the parts; the loop never fully resolves — is structurally identical to several core engineering processes:

**Requirements-implementation feedback.** You cannot fully specify requirements without understanding implementation constraints; you cannot implement without understanding requirements. Every sprint retrospective is a partial hermeneutic resolution: the whole (product vision) is refined by the parts (sprint outcome), which reshapes the next iteration of requirements.

**Compiler multi-pass analysis.** A compiler's first pass sees tokens without knowing types; later passes have full type information that recontextualizes earlier decisions. Semantic analysis is hermeneutic: each pass refines the interpretation of code using knowledge built from previous passes. The abstract syntax tree is a "pre-understanding" revised by subsequent analysis.

**TDD red-green-refactor.** Write a failing test (articulate what the whole should do) → implement just enough to pass (fill in the part) → refactor (revise the whole in light of what the parts revealed). The test is a hypothesis about meaning; the implementation is an interpretation; refactoring is the hermeneutic revision when the interpretation changes your understanding of the hypothesis.

**Code review.** A reviewer must understand the author's intent (whole: what problem is this solving?) to evaluate individual decisions (parts: why this loop structure?). But the parts reveal the actual intent, which may differ from the stated intent — a classic hermeneutic gap. The PR comment thread is a hermeneutic negotiation.

**Skinner's anti-anachronism principle** maps directly to avoiding presentism in code archaeology: when reading a ten-year-old codebase, you must ask "what problem were they solving then, with what tools available, under what constraints?" — not "why didn't they use Docker?"

## Common Confusion Points

**Cambridge School does not deny authorial intent.**
Skinner wants to recover intent — but "intent" means the publicly recoverable communicative
act, not the private psychology. The critique is of anachronistically attributed intentions,
not of intentionalism per se.

**Foucault's genealogy is not causal history.**
Genealogy does not explain why things happened by identifying causes. It shows how
contingent things are — that they could have been otherwise — by tracing the multiple
small power relations and accidents that produced the present.

**The linguistic turn does not mean "it's all language."**
Even theorists associated with the linguistic turn (like Chartier) acknowledge material
conditions. The turn highlighted language's constitutive role, not that language is all
there is.

**Begriffsgeschichte vs. semantic history.**
Koselleck traces concepts (complex cultural-political constructs). Semantic history traces
specific words. A word can persist while the concept changes, or a concept can persist while
being expressed in different words.
