## Sound System

```
FRENCH PHONOLOGY — KEY POINTS
================================

VOWELS — French has ~15 vowel phonemes (English has ~12-14)
  Key distinctions English lacks:
  [y]  — "u" sound: pur, lune, tu (round your lips as for "oo", say "ee")
  [ø]  — "eu" sound: feu, peur, bleu (round lips for "o", say "e")
  [œ]  — "eu" open: peur, cœur, fleur
  Nasals: an/en [ɑ̃], in/ain [ɛ̃], on [ɔ̃], un [œ̃] (merging with in)

CONSONANTS
  R  = uvular fricative [ʁ] — from the back of the throat, NOT like Spanish R
  Silent final consonants — the core French pronunciation rule:
    Most final consonants are SILENT:
    "les" [le], "est" [ε], "grand" [grɑ̃], "Paris" [paʁi]

LIAISON — breaking the silent-consonant rule
  When word ends in normally-silent consonant and next word starts with vowel,
  the consonant IS pronounced and links to the vowel:
    "les amis" → [lezami] (s from les links to amis)
    "vous avez" → [vuzave]
    "ils ont" → [ilzɔ̃]
  Liaison is OBLIGATORY in some contexts, FORBIDDEN in others.
    Obligatory: article+noun, subject pronoun+verb
    Forbidden: et + following word, after singular noun
    Optional: in careful speech between adjective+noun

ELISION — vowel deletion before vowel/h
  le + ami = l'ami    (not "le ami")
  je + ai  = j'ai     (not "je ai")
  de + eau = d'eau    (not "de eau")
  le/la contract to l', se to s', ce to c', etc.

MUTE H vs ASPIRATE H
  Mute H: liaison and elision apply: l'heure, les hommes [lezɔm]
  Aspirate H: no liaison, no elision: le hibou, les haricots [le aʁiko]
  No pronunciation difference — entirely a grammatical category.
```

## Grammar Core

### Articles — Four Categories

```
FRENCH ARTICLES
================
                MASCULINE      FEMININE       PLURAL (M+F)
Definite:       le             la             les
                (the)          (the)          (the)
Indefinite:     un             une            des
                (a/an)         (a/an)         (some)
Partitive:      du (de+le)     de la          des
                (some)         (some)         (N/A)
Negative:       de/d'          de/d'          de/d'
                (any)          (any)          (any)

Partitive — used for uncountable quantities:
  Je veux du pain. (I want some bread.)
  Tu bois de l'eau. (You drink water.)
  After negation: Je ne veux pas DE pain. (not "du")

Contractions:
  de + le = du    (du pain, du lait)
  de + les = des  (des amis)
  à + le = au     (au marché, au cinéma)
  à + les = aux   (aux États-Unis)
  à + la = à la   (no contraction: à la maison)
```

### Verb System — Key Patterns

```
THREE IRREGULAR VERBS (memorize immediately)
  ÊTRE (to be)         AVOIR (to have)      ALLER (to go)
  je suis              j'ai                 je vais
  tu es                tu as                tu vas
  il/elle est          il/elle a            il/elle va
  nous sommes          nous avons           nous allons
  vous êtes            vous avez            vous allez
  ils/elles sont       ils/elles ont        ils/elles vont

-ER VERBS (present tense) — 90% of all French verbs:
  parler: parle, parles, parle, parlons, parlez, parlent
  Note: singular forms (parle/parles/parle) all SOUND the same [paʁl]
  This is why spoken French is misleading — 3 forms, 1 pronunciation.

-IR VERBS (finir):
  finis, finis, finit, finissons, finissez, finissent
  These are "regular" -ir but the pattern: -ss- appears in plural.

-RE VERBS (vendre):
  vends, vends, vend, vendons, vendez, vendent
```

### Passé Composé — The Main Past Tense

```
PASSÉ COMPOSÉ (written + spoken past)
=======================================
Formation: avoir/être + past participle

WITH AVOIR (most verbs):
  j'ai parlé   (I spoke / I have spoken)
  tu as mangé
  il a fini
  nous avons vendu

WITH ÊTRE (verbs of motion + all reflexives) — DR MRS VANDERTRAMP
  devenir, revenir, monter, rester, sortir, venir, aller
  naître, descendre, entrer, rentrer, tomber, retourner, arriver, mourir, partir
  je suis allé(e), tu es parti(e), il est né, elle est morte

AGREEMENT with être — past participle agrees with SUBJECT:
  Il est allé vs Elle est allée
  Ils sont allés vs Elles sont allées

  With avoir — past participle agrees with PRECEDING direct object:
  "Les films que j'ai vus" (j'ai vu + les films preceding → vus, plural)
```

### Negation

```
NEGATION STRUCTURE
===================
  ne ... pas  (not)           Je ne parle pas français.
  ne ... plus (no longer)     Je ne travaille plus.
  ne ... jamais (never)       Je ne mange jamais de viande.
  ne ... rien (nothing)       Je ne vois rien.
  ne ... personne (nobody)    Je ne vois personne.
  ne ... que (only)           Je n'ai qu'un euro.
  ne ... ni...ni (neither...nor) Je ne veux ni thé ni café.

  In spoken French: "ne" is routinely DROPPED:
  "Je parle pas" = "Je ne parle pas" — correct in conversation.
  In writing: keep ne.
```

### Pronouns and Position

```
FRENCH PRONOUN ORDER
=====================
  French object pronouns are MANDATORY and go BEFORE the verb:

  Order: me/te/se/nous/vous  →  le/la/les  →  lui/leur  →  y  →  en

  Je te le donne.       (I give it to you.)
  Il me le montre.      (He shows it to me.)
  Elle lui en parle.    (She talks to him about it.)

  With negative: ne + [pronouns] + verb + pas
  Je ne te le donne pas.

  Tonic (stress) pronouns: moi, toi, lui, elle, nous, vous, eux, elles
  Used after prepositions, for emphasis, in comparisons:
  "C'est pour moi" (It's for me)
  "Elle est plus grande que lui" (She is taller than him)
```

### The T-V Distinction

```
TU vs VOUS
===========
  TU: singular informal — friends, family, peers, children, God in prayer
  VOUS: (1) singular FORMAL — strangers, superiors, older people
        (2) plural — any group of people

  Using "tu" with a stranger is rude (less so in Quebec/casual contexts).
  Using "vous" with close friends sounds distant/ironic.

  Tutoyment (switching to tu) must be mutually agreed.
  In France, business colleagues often remain at vous even after years.
```

## Spoken vs Written French — The Split

```
WRITTEN FRENCH          ←————→        SPOKEN FRENCH
(formal/literary)                      (everyday)

Passé simple used        Only passé composé in speech
(je parlai, il vint)     (j'ai parlé, il est venu)

Subjunctive of être/     Avoided in speech:
avoir: que je sois,      "C'est dommage que tu partes"
que tu aies              → "C'est dommage que tu pars"

Ne...pas retained        Ne dropped: "je sais pas"
in writing

Ce que/dont/où          Simplified: "le truc que je t'ai parlé"
(strict relative clauses) (incorrect but universal in speech)

On = general "one"      On = nous in speech:
(formal)                 "On va au cinéma" = "Nous allons au cinéma"
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | French approach |
|-----------|----------------|
| Biggest hurdle | Pronunciation — liaison, silent letters, nasal vowels, uvular R |
| Second biggest | Gender + agreement system (all adjectives agree, past participles with être) |
| Start speaking | Master avoir/être + passé composé. Skip passé simple entirely. |
| Formality | Default to vous until offered tu |
| Reading | French spelling is highly irregular (historical, not phonetic) |
| African French | Fewer contractions, clearer vowels, often easier to understand |
| Quebec French | Distinct vowels, joual dialect, some archaic features |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Silent letters are everywhere"**
"ils parlent" — the -ent is silent. The -s on "vous", on "ils" — silent.
Final -d, -s, -t, -x, -z generally silent. Only way to know: learn words
as spoken forms, not spelled forms. Liaison forces consonants back into play.

**"The written past is not used in speech"**
The "passé simple" (je parlai, il vint) is only in literature and formal
writing. In conversation, everything past uses passé composé. This catches
learners who study written texts.

**"Adjective position"**
Most adjectives come AFTER the noun: une maison blanche.
Short/common adjectives before: un grand homme, une belle femme.
Some adjectives change meaning by position:
  un homme grand = a tall man
  un grand homme = a great man
  mon ancien collègue = my former colleague
  une maison ancienne = an old house

**"C'est vs Il est"**
c'est + article + noun: C'est un médecin. (He's a doctor.)
il est + adjective of nationality/profession/religion: Il est médecin.
In practice, "c'est" is expanding in spoken French.

**"Du/de la vs de"**
After negation, partitive collapses to "de/d'":
  J'ai du pain. → Je n'ai pas DE pain.
  Il y a des pommes. → Il n'y a pas DE pommes.
