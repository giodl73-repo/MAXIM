## The Big Picture: What Makes Portuguese Distinct from Spanish

```
PORTUGUESE vs SPANISH COMPARISON
==================================

  89% lexical similarity — highest among Romance pairs
  But:

  PRONUNCIATION DIVERGES SHARPLY
  +--------------------------+------------------------------+
  | Feature                  | Portuguese                   |
  +--------------------------+------------------------------+
  | Unstressed vowels        | Reduced/swallowed (EP)       |
  |                          | Clearer in Brazilian         |
  | Nasal vowels             | Distinctive: ã, em, ão, im   |
  | S at end of syllable     | [ʃ] in EP ("sh"), [s] in BP  |
  | Vowel reduction          | EP sounds "mumbled" to       |
  |                          | Spanish speakers             |
  +--------------------------+------------------------------+

  UNIQUE GRAMMAR FEATURES
  - Future subjunctive (virtually extinct elsewhere in Romance)
  - Mesoclisis: object pronouns INSERT into future/conditional
  - Personal infinitive (infinitive conjugates by person)
  - Ter vs Haver: both mean "to have" (ter dominant in BP)
```

## Sound System

```
PORTUGUESE PHONOLOGY
=====================

NASAL VOWELS — Portuguese's signature feature
  ã  [ɐ̃]  — like "ang" without the g: irmã (sister)
  em [ẽ]  — nasal e: também (also)
  ão [ɐ̃w̃] — nasal "ow": irmão (brother), pão (bread)
  im/im [ĩ] — nasal i: sim (yes)
  um [ũ]  — nasal u: um (one/a)

  Nasal vowels affect adjective agreement: bom (m) / boa (f), bons / boas
  grande plural: grandes (same for m/f)

CONSONANTS
  lh = [ʎ] like "ll" in million: filho (son)
  nh = [ɲ] like "ny" in canyon: vinho (wine), banho (bath)
  x  = [ʃ] or [ks] or [z] or [s] — context-dependent, highly irregular

EUROPEAN vs BRAZILIAN PRONUNCIATION
  EP: unstressed vowels brutally reduced
    "Portugal" sounds like "Prugl" to untrained ears
    s/z before consonant → [ʃ]/[ʒ]: "Lisboa" = "Lizhboa"
  BP: vowels clearly articulated, more "musical"
    Better for learners; Brazilian media globally accessible
    -te/-de at end → [tʃi]/[dʒi] in Rio (not in São Paulo)
```

## Grammar Core

### Articles and Gender

```
ARTICLES
=========
Definite:    o (m.sing), a (f.sing), os (m.plur), as (f.plur)
Indefinite:  um (m.sing), uma (f.sing), uns (m.plur), umas (f.plur)

Gender follows Spanish patterns mostly:
  -o → M: o livro, o carro
  -a → F: a casa, a mesa
  Exceptions: a mão (f), o problema (m), o dia (m) — same as Spanish

CONTRACTIONS (obligatory — not optional like Spanish)
  a + o = ao    (to the, masc): Vou ao mercado.
  a + a = à     (to the, fem): Vou à escola.
  de + o = do   (of the, masc): o carro do João
  de + a = da   (of the, fem): a casa da Maria
  em + o = no   (in the, masc): no Brasil
  em + a = na   (in the, fem): na escola
  por + o = pelo (by the, masc): pelo parque
  por + a = pela (by the, fem): pela rua
```

### Verb Conjugation

```
THREE CLASSES: -AR, -ER, -IR

PRESENT (falar/comer/partir):
+-----------+-----------+----------+-----------+
| Person    | -AR       | -ER      | -IR       |
+-----------+-----------+----------+-----------+
| eu        | fal-o     | com-o    | part-o    |
| tu        | fal-as    | com-es   | part-es   |
| ele/ela   | fal-a     | com-e    | part-e    |
| nós       | fal-amos  | com-emos | part-imos |
| vós*      | fal-ais   | com-eis  | part-is   |
| eles/elas | fal-am    | com-em   | part-em   |
+-----------+-----------+----------+-----------+
* vós = archaic in BP. Use vocês instead.

PRETERITE PERFEITO (completed past):
+-----------+-----------+----------+-----------+
| eu        | fal-ei    | com-i    | part-i    |
| tu        | fal-aste  | com-este | part-iste |
| ele       | fal-ou    | com-eu   | part-iu   |
| nós       | fal-ámos  | com-emos | part-imos |
| eles      | fal-aram  | com-eram | part-iram |
+-----------+-----------+----------+-----------+

IMPERFEITO (ongoing past — same function as Spanish imperfect):
  eu falava, tu falavas, ele falava, nós falávamos, eles falavam
```

### The Unique Future Subjunctive

```
FUTURE SUBJUNCTIVE — Portuguese's distinctive feature
======================================================
Used for hypothetical future events and conditional clauses.
Most Romance languages lost this; Portuguese preserved it.

Formation: Based on 3rd person plural preterite minus the -m + person endings
  falar → falaram → falar stem
  eu falar, tu falares, ele falar, nós falarmos, eles falarem

USAGE:
  When you arrive (if you arrive in the future):
    "Quando você chegar, me ligue."   ← future subjunctive REQUIRED
    [When you arrive, call me.]

    Compare Spanish: "Cuando llegues" = present SUBJUNCTIVE
    Portuguese:     "Quando chegar"  = FUTURE subjunctive

  Conditional real situations:
    "Se você vier amanhã, vou comprar ingressos."
    [If you come tomorrow, I'll buy tickets.]
```

### Personal Infinitive

```
PERSONAL INFINITIVE — Another Portuguese unique feature
========================================================
Infinitives conjugate when their subject differs from main clause.

Infinitive endings: -   , -es, - , -mos, -des, -em
  Base: falar  → falar, falares, falar, falarmos, falardes, falarem

  "É importante vocês estudarem."
  (It is important for you [plural] to study.)
  ← The infinitive "estudarem" takes the 3rd plural ending because its
    subject (vocês) differs from no subject in the main clause.

  Compare Spanish: "Es importante que ustedes estudien." (subjunctive)
  Portuguese can use: "É importante que vocês estudem." (same)
  OR personal infinitive:  "É importante vocês estudarem."
```

### Mesoclisis (European Portuguese)

```
MESOCLISIS — object pronouns INSERT into verb forms
====================================================
In EP, when future/conditional has an object pronoun:
  pronoun splits INTO the verb between stem and ending

  Normal (with negation, after subordinator):
    Eu não te vou ligar. (I'm not going to call you.)

  Mesoclisis (in main clause, affirmative, EP):
    Eu ligar-te-ei.  ← "call-you-will-I" (future)
    (I will call you.)

  In BP: mesoclisis is essentially dead.
    BP: Eu vou te ligar. / Eu te ligarei.
```

## Brazilian vs European Portuguese: Key Differences

```
+-----------------------------+------------------+-------------------+
| Feature                     | Brazilian (BP)   | European (EP)     |
+-----------------------------+------------------+-------------------+
| 2nd person pronoun (inform) | você (uses 3rd   | tu (uses 2nd      |
|                             | person agreement)| person agreement) |
| Object pronoun position     | before verb      | after verb (EP)   |
|                             | Me chamo João    | Chamo-me João     |
| Gerund vs infinitive        | Estou estudando  | Estou a estudar   |
|                             | (gerund -ando)   | (a + infinitive)  |
| "ter" vs "haver"            | ter dominant     | haver still used  |
|                             | Tem um problema  | Há um problema    |
| Pronunciation               | Clear vowels     | Reduced vowels    |
| Demonstratives              | esse/este merged | esse (near you)   |
|                             | in practice      | este (near me)    |
| Clitic pronoun (mesoclisis) | Never used       | Common in writing |
+-----------------------------+------------------+-------------------+
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Portuguese sounds like Russian / Slavic?"**
European Portuguese has heavily reduced vowels and sounds nothing like
Spanish. Unstressed vowels are often deleted. Familiarity with Spanish
does NOT prepare you for Portuguese pronunciation — you must re-train
your ear even if reading is trivial.

**"Obrigado / Obrigada — which do I say?"**
The speaker's gender determines the form. A man says "obrigado", a woman
says "obrigada". This trips up English speakers who look at the person
they're thanking, not themselves.

**"Tu vs você in Brazil?"**
BP uses "você" as the standard 2nd-person (takes 3rd-person verb endings).
"Tu" is used in some regions (Rio Grande do Sul, Pará, Nordeste) but
with non-standard agreement in most of Brazil. In EP, "tu" is standard.

**"Future subjunctive vs present subjunctive?"**
In temporal/conditional clauses about future events, Portuguese demands
future subjunctive where Spanish uses present subjunctive:
  Spanish: "Cuando llegues..." (present subj)
  Portuguese: "Quando chegares..." (future subj) or "Quando chegar" (EP)
