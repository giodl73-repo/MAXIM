### Cyrillic — Russian quick-read

```
A=A  Б=B  В=V  Г=G  Д=D  Е=YE  Ж=ZH  З=Z
И=I  Й=Y  К=K  Л=L  М=M  Н=N   О=O   П=P
Р=R  С=S  Т=T  У=U  Ф=F  Х=KH  Ц=TS  Ч=CH
Ш=SH Щ=SCH Ъ=hard sign Ы=Y(back) Ь=soft sign
Э=E  Ю=YU  Я=YA

Traps: Р=R (not P), Н=N (not H), В=V (not B), С=S (not C),
       Х=KH (not X), Я=YA (not backwards R)
```

### Arabic — positional forms

```
Letter changes shape based on position in word:
  ب (ba)  standalone: ب  initial: بـ  medial: ـبـ  final: ـب
Reading direction: RIGHT to LEFT.
Vowels mostly unwritten (diacritical marks optional, used in Quran/children's books).
28 letters total.
```

### Devanagari — Hindi quick-read

## Common Confusion Points

**"Numbers in Korean?"**
Korean has TWO counting systems. Sino-Korean (일/이/삼...) for dates,
money, phone numbers, minutes, and most formal counting.
Native Korean (하나/둘/셋...) for counting objects (with classifiers)
and hours. Using the wrong system sounds wrong, not just foreign.

**"Arabic numbers read left to right?"**
Yes — even though Arabic text is right-to-left, numerals (٠١٢٣٤٥٦٧٨٩)
are read left-to-right (low digit first). The Western numerals we use
(0-9) are actually derived from Arabic.

**"French 70-99?"**
France uses soixante-dix (60+10=70) and quatre-vingts (4×20=80).
Belgium and Switzerland use septante (70) and huitante (80) — simpler
but will mark you as non-French.

**"Japanese counting is the same as Chinese?"**
The Sino-Japanese numbers (ichi/ni/san...) are borrowed from Chinese.
But Japanese also has native numbers (hitotsu/futatsu...) used for
counting objects 1-10. Plus, different counters for different objects:
本 (long thin things), 枚 (flat things), 台 (machines). See 10-JAPANESE.md.
