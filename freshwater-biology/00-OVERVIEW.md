# Freshwater Biology — Lakes, Rivers, Wetlands, and Groundwater: The Full Landscape

## The Big Picture

Freshwater covers less than 1% of Earth's surface and holds only 0.01% of its water, yet supports ~10% of all described species and provides drinking water, food, and water regulation for billions of people. Limnology (the science of inland waters) spans physical, chemical, and biological dimensions across four habitat types: lakes, rivers, wetlands, and groundwater.

## Domain Map

### Domain 1 — Lakes and Stratification (Guide 01)

Lentic (standing water) systems develop seasonal thermal stratification that controls oxygen distribution, nutrient cycling, and species distribution.

```
LAKE VERTICAL ZONES (summer stratification):

  0–2m    EPILIMNION     warm, well-mixed, oxygenated
  ------  THERMOCLINE    temperature drops > 1°C/m
  5–15m   METALIMNION    transition; sometimes O2 minimum
  ------
  Bottom  HYPOLIMNION    cold, stagnant, often anoxic in productive lakes
```

### Domain 2 — River Ecology (Guide 02)

Lotic (flowing water) systems are organized along longitudinal gradients from headwater to mouth — the River Continuum Concept (RCC).

```
RIVER CONTINUUM:
  Orders 1–3   headwaters    shading, leaf litter, macroinvertebrate processing
  Orders 4–6   mid-reaches   open canopy, algae, fine organic matter
  Orders 7–12  large rivers  turbid, phytoplankton, fine suspended particles
```

### Domain 3 — Wetlands (Guide 03)

Transitional zones between aquatic and terrestrial systems. Defined by hydric soils and hydrophytic vegetation.

### Domain 4 — Organisms (Guide 04)

Freshwater biodiversity: fish, amphibians, macroinvertebrates (EPT), algae, macrophytes.

### Domain 5 — Nutrient Cycles (Guide 05)

N, P, Si cycling in freshwater; Redfield ratio analog; limiting nutrient concept.

### Domain 6 — Eutrophication (Guide 06)

P loading → algal blooms → hypoxia → degradation → restoration pathways.

### Domain 7 — Food Webs (Guide 07)

Trophic cascades, keystone predators, bioaccumulation, Carpenter lake experiments.

### Domain 8 — Conservation (Guide 08)

Invasive species, dam impacts, environmental flows, IUCN freshwater crisis.

### Domain 9 — Water Quality (Guide 09)

Physical/chemical parameters, indicator species, monitoring, WHO/EPA standards.

## Limnology Scope

Limnology was defined by F.A. Forel (Lake Geneva studies, 1890s) as the study of lakes. Modern limnology extends to all inland waters and their catchments.

```
LIMNOLOGY = PHYSICAL + CHEMICAL + BIOLOGICAL + SOCIAL dimensions

  PHYSICAL:    Heat budgets, thermal stratification, mixing dynamics,
               light penetration (Secchi depth), hydraulic residence time

  CHEMICAL:    Nutrient concentrations (N, P, Si, C), dissolved O2,
               pH, conductivity, redox chemistry, trace metals

  BIOLOGICAL:  Primary producers (phytoplankton, periphyton, macrophytes),
               zooplankton (Daphnia, copepods, rotifers),
               macroinvertebrates (insects, mollusks, worms),
               fish, amphibians, riparian vegetation

  WATERSHED:   Land use effects on runoff chemistry;
               nutrient export from agriculture, urban areas, forests
```

## Key Threats

```
THREAT HIERARCHY (freshwater biodiversity):

  1. HABITAT LOSS AND DEGRADATION     -- dam construction, channelization,
                                         riparian clearing, wetland drainage
  2. OVEREXPLOITATION                 -- overfishing, groundwater over-extraction
  3. WATER POLLUTION                  -- nutrient loading (eutrophication),
                                         pesticides, heavy metals, pharmaceuticals,
                                         microplastics, PFAS
  4. INVASIVE SPECIES                 -- zebra mussels, Asian carp, Nile perch,
                                         signal crayfish, water hyacinth
  5. CLIMATE CHANGE                   -- altered precipitation patterns, drought,
                                         increased water temperature, phenological
                                         mismatch, glacial melt pulses
  6. DISEASE                          -- chytrid fungus (Batrachochytrium) in
                                         amphibians; crayfish plague; fish viruses
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Limnology is not oceanography's smaller sibling.**
Lakes and rivers operate under physical constraints fundamentally different from oceans: closed basins, short hydraulic residence times, stronger terrestrial connectivity, higher relative nutrient loading per water volume, and stronger effects of catchment land use. Many patterns that hold in marine systems do not translate.

**Eutrophication is primarily a phosphorus problem in freshwater, not nitrogen.**
The Redfield ratio (N:P = 16:1 by atoms) describes optimal phytoplankton growth. In most freshwaters, P is limiting; adding P drives eutrophication. N addition matters but rarely as primary driver. This is why P removal is the target of wastewater treatment in lakes.

**Freshwater biodiversity is disproportionately threatened compared to terrestrial.**
The -83% vertebrate population decline (1970–2016, LPI) in freshwater systems is roughly double the terrestrial rate. Despite covering < 1% of Earth's surface, freshwater systems harbor a grossly disproportionate share of threatened species relative to their area.
