# Lake Stratification: Epilimnion, Thermocline, Hypolimnion, Turnover

## The Big Picture

A lake's vertical structure is governed by one physical property: the density of water. Temperature drives density (with a key nonlinearity at 4°C), and density differences resist mixing. Understanding stratification is prerequisite to understanding oxygen distribution, nutrient cycling, algal blooms, and fish habitat in any standing-water system.

```
LAKE VERTICAL STRUCTURE — SUMMER STRATIFICATION

  Depth   Temperature (°C)    Dissolved O2    Layer
  0m      25°C ─────────────  8–12 mg/L       EPILIMNION
          24°C                (wind-mixed)     (warm, mixed)
          23°C
          ─────────────────── ─────────────── ─── THERMOCLINE ───
          18°C                declining        METALIMNION
          12°C ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─  (O2 minimum in  (transition zone)
          8°C                  some lakes)

          4°C ─────────────── (anoxic in       often anoxic in
          4°C                 productive lakes) productive lakes)
  bottom  4°C

  Temperature scale: hypothetical stratified temperate lake in summer
```

## Section 1 — Water Density Physics

The nonlinear density-temperature relationship of freshwater is the physical foundation of everything that follows.

```
DENSITY OF FRESHWATER vs. TEMPERATURE:

  Temp (°C)   Density (kg/m³)
  0           999.84
  4           999.97  <-- MAXIMUM DENSITY
  10          999.70
  20          998.20
  25          997.05
  30          995.65

  KEY NONLINEARITY:
  - Water CONTRACTS as it warms from 0 to 4°C
  - Water EXPANDS as it warms above 4°C
  - Maximum density at 4°C is the pivot point of all lake mixing dynamics
  - Consequence: ice floats (density 917 kg/m³); lakes freeze from top down

THERMAL STRATIFICATION FORMS BECAUSE:
  Warm surface water (low density) floats on cool deep water (high density)
  Wind mixes only the upper layer (epilimnion)
  Density difference at thermocline resists vertical mixing
  Stratification strengthens through summer; weakens in autumn cooling
```

## Section 2 — The Three Zones

### Epilimnion

The surface mixed layer. Depth typically 3–15 m depending on lake size (wind fetch) and transparency.

```
  EPILIMNION CHARACTERISTICS:
  +-- Actively mixed by wind and convective cooling/heating
  +-- Temperature approximately uniform (< 1°C variation)
  +-- Fully oxygenated (atmospheric equilibrium + photosynthesis)
  +-- Most phytoplankton and zooplankton concentrated here
  +-- Receives 100% of incoming solar radiation at surface
  +-- Rapid temperature changes with weather

  DEPTH CONTROL:
  Large, exposed lakes: epilimnion 10–15 m (wind-driven mixing)
  Small, sheltered lakes: 2–5 m
  Clearwater lakes: deeper mixing (light penetrates, creating
                    deeper warm layer)
  Turbid lakes: shallower mixing (heat absorbed near surface)
```

### Thermocline and Metalimnion

The metalimnion is the layer of rapid temperature change. The thermocline is specifically the depth of maximum temperature gradient (°C/m).

```
  THERMOCLINE CRITERIA:
  Conventionally defined where temperature changes > 1°C per meter depth
  -- or --
  Depth of steepest temperature gradient (maximum dT/dz)

  METALIMNETIC O2 MINIMUM (in some lakes):
  Some thermally stratified lakes develop a dissolved O2 minimum in the
  metalimnion, below the photosynthetically active epilimnion but above
  the anoxic hypolimnion:

  +-- Phytoplankton sink from epilimnion and decompose at thermocline
  +-- Bacterial oxidation consumes O2 faster than it can be replenished
  +-- Creates a temporary O2 sag at metalimnion depths
  +-- Fish may be trapped between warm epilimnion (low O2 tolerance) and
      cold anoxic hypolimnion: "oxygen squeeze" in summer
```

### Hypolimnion

Below the thermocline: cold, dark, and increasingly oxygen-depleted as summer progresses.

```
  HYPOLIMNION DYNAMICS THROUGH THE SEASON:

  SPRING (just after turnover):
  Uniformly cold (~4–8°C); fully oxygenated throughout
  O2: 12–14 mg/L (cold water holds more O2)

  EARLY SUMMER:
  Stratification forms; hypolimnion isolated from atmosphere
  O2 begins declining (bacterial decomposition consumes it)
  O2: 8–12 mg/L

  LATE SUMMER (oligotrophic lake):
  Hypolimnion still has some O2 (2–5 mg/L)
  Cold water productivity limits O2 demand

  LATE SUMMER (eutrophic lake):
  Hypolimnion fully ANOXIC (O2 = 0 mg/L)
  Bacterial metabolism shifts to anaerobic:
    NO3- reduction --> N2 (denitrification)
    SO4²- reduction --> H2S (sulfate reduction)
    CO2 reduction  --> CH4 (methanogenesis)
  Accumulation: NH4+, H2S, CH4, Fe2+, Mn2+, CO2
  These reduced species released during fall overturn --> mixing event
```

## Section 3 — Spring and Fall Turnover

Turnover is the complete mixing of the water column that occurs when the thermal stratification breaks down.

```
TURNOVER MECHANICS:

  FALL TURNOVER:
  Autumn cooling --> surface water cools --> becomes denser
  Surface density approaches hypolimnion density
  Wind mixing now penetrates full depth
  Complete mixing: O2 redistributed to bottom; nutrients redistributed to surface
  Lake "resets" biogeochemically

  SPRING TURNOVER:
  Ice cover keeps lake near 0°C under ice; at 4°C maximum density beneath ice
  Ice melts --> surface warms from 0°C to 4°C --> increases in density
  Water SINKS as it warms from 0–4°C (unusual behavior)
  Complete vertical mixing before surface exceeds 4°C
  Brief homogenous water column; then summer stratification forms

  TURNOVER SEQUENCE DIAGRAM:

  SUMMER:      AUTUMN:       TURNOVER:      WINTER:      SPRING:
  E (25°C)     E (15°C)      ─────────      ICE (0°C)    Turnover
  ─────────    ─────────      E = T = H      < 4°C        ─────────
  M (12°C)     M = E (10°C)  (uniform)      > 4°C        Summer
  H  (4°C)     H = M  (7°C)                 4°C at bot.  builds
```

### Dimictic vs. Monomictic vs. Meromictic

| Lake Type | Turnovers/yr | Climate | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|---------|
| **Dimictic** | 2 (spring + fall) | Temperate continental | Great Lakes, most N. American/European lakes |
| **Warm monomictic** | 1 (winter only) | Subtropical/maritime | Mediterranean lakes; Pacific NW coastal |
| **Cold monomictic** | 1 (summer only) | Sub-arctic/alpine | Mountain lakes, Alaska, Greenland margin |
| **Polymictic** | Continuous/frequent | Shallow + tropical or wind-exposed | Tropical shallow lakes; wind-swept reservoirs |
| **Meromictic** | Never (no complete mixing) | Sheltered + chemical density difference | Below |

## Section 4 — Meromictic Lakes

In meromictic lakes, the deep water (monimolimnion) never mixes with the surface water (mixolimnion). The barrier is typically chemical density, not just thermal.

```
MEROMICTIC LAKE STRUCTURE:

  MIXOLIMNION:    Normal surface mixing (epilimnion-equivalent)
  CHEMOCLINE:     Boundary of sharp chemical/density change
                  (high salt concentration, dissolved organics, or
                   high dissolved CO2 at depth)
  MONIMOLIMNION:  Permanently isolated deep water
                  -- highly anoxic
                  -- extreme chemical reduction (H2S, NH4+, Fe2+)
                  -- can preserve biological archive (annually laminated sediments = varves)

  CAUSES:
  +-- High dissolved solids input at depth (e.g., saline groundwater intrusion)
  +-- Runoff from catchment with high salinity
  +-- Extreme sheltering from wind (narrow fjord-like geometry)
  +-- Deep lakes in sheltered locations with low precipitation mixing

  EXAMPLES:
  Mono Lake (CA): saline; partially meromictic
  Lac Pavin (France): volcanic CO2 injection at depth
  Lake Nyos (Cameroon): volcanic CO2 -- catastrophic overturn 1986 (1,700+ dead)
  Mahoney Lake (BC): meromictic; purple sulfur bacteria at chemocline (massive mat)

  SCIENTIFIC VALUE:
  Meromictic lake sediments preserve laminated (varved) records
  No bioturbation (no benthic organisms in anoxic monimolimnion)
  Annual varves = sub-annual climate resolution for paleolimnology
```

## Section 5 — Light Penetration and the Euphotic Zone

Light drives primary production and interacts with stratification.

```
  EUPHOTIC ZONE: depth where light = 1% of surface irradiance
  Below this: photosynthesis < respiration; net heterotrophic

  BEER-LAMBERT LAW (in water):
  I(z) = I0 × e^(-kd × z)

  Where:
    I(z) = irradiance at depth z
    I0   = surface irradiance
    kd   = diffuse attenuation coefficient (m^-1)
    z    = depth (m)

  Euphotic depth = -ln(0.01) / kd = 4.6 / kd

  kd VALUES:
  Very clear (Lake Baikal, Crater Lake): kd = 0.05–0.1  --> euphotic: 50–90 m
  Oligotrophic clear lake:               kd = 0.1–0.3   --> euphotic: 15–45 m
  Mesotrophic lake:                      kd = 0.3–0.8   --> euphotic: 6–15 m
  Eutrophic (algae-dense):               kd = 1–5       --> euphotic: 1–5 m
  Highly turbid (brown DOC / sediment):  kd = 2–10      --> euphotic: 0.5–2 m

  SECCHI DEPTH:
  Simple transparency measure: depth where white Secchi disc disappears
  Euphotic depth ≈ 2.0–2.5 × Secchi depth (empirical relationship)
  Typical ranges: 0.5 m (turbid eutrophic) to 40 m (ultra-clear)
```

## Section 6 — Dissolved Oxygen Dynamics

```
O2 SATURATION AT EQUILIBRIUM (freshwater at 1 atm):

  Temperature (°C)   O2 saturation (mg/L)
  0                  14.6
  5                  12.8
  10                 11.3
  15                 10.0
  20                  9.1
  25                  8.3
  30                  7.6

  DEPARTURES FROM SATURATION:
  Supersaturation (> 100%): intense photosynthesis on sunny days;
                             can reach 150–200% in macrophyte beds
  Depletion (< 100%):        bacterial decomposition; fish kills below ~2 mg/L;
                             acute lethal for most fish below ~1 mg/L

  HYPOLIMNETIC O2 DEPLETION RATE:
  Oligotrophic lake: ~0.1–0.5 mg/L/day during stratification
  Mesotrophic:       ~0.5–1.0 mg/L/day
  Eutrophic:         ~1–5 mg/L/day --> anoxia within weeks of stratification
```

## Section 7 — Internal P Loading

The link between hypolimnetic anoxia and eutrophication acceleration — a positive feedback loop:

```
  EXTERNAL P LOAD (from catchment) --> eutrophication
         |
         v
  ALGAL BLOOM BIOMASS SETTLES TO HYPOLIMNION
         |
         v
  BACTERIAL DECOMPOSITION CONSUMES O2 --> ANOXIA
         |
         v
  IRON-PHOSPHORUS BINDING BREAKS DOWN UNDER ANOXIA:
  Under oxic conditions:  Fe(OH)3 binds P tightly (sorption)
  Under anoxic conditions: Fe(OH)3 --> Fe²⁺ (dissolved); P released
  P concentration in anoxic hypolimnion can reach 1–5 mg/L
         |
         v
  FALL TURNOVER MIXES P-RICH HYPOLIMNION TO SURFACE
         |
         v
  NEXT YEAR'S ALGAL BLOOM LARGER (even if external load reduced)
         |
         +--> INTERNAL LOAD -- can exceed external load in long-degraded lakes
              This is why lake restoration requires addressing BOTH internal
              and external loading
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why does ice float on water? | Water maximum density at 4°C; ice (0°C) less dense |
| When does fall turnover occur? | Surface cools to match hypolimnion density; wind mixing penetrates full depth |
| What creates the metalimnetic O2 minimum? | Phytoplankton settling and decomposing at thermocline; O2 consumed faster than supplied |
| Why is a meromictic lake scientifically valuable? | Annually laminated sediments (varves); perfect preservation; high-resolution paleorecord |
| Why does deepening the anoxic layer worsen eutrophication? | Fe-P binding breaks under anoxia; internal P loading accelerates external P effect |
| What limits thermocline depth in small sheltered lakes? | Low wind fetch = shallow mixing; thermocline forms very shallow |
| Why does a warm, shallow lake have more severe blooms? | Faster stratification + faster O2 depletion; P-limited water = sensitive to any P addition |

## Common Confusion Points

**The thermocline is not a fixed depth.**
The thermocline rises and falls with weather: wind deepens it; calm periods let it shoal. It also deepens progressively through summer in typical dimictic lakes as surface heating accumulates. A single sounding does not characterize the thermocline for a season.

**Anoxic ≠ uninhabitable.**
The anoxic hypolimnion supports anaerobic bacteria, archaea, and in some lakes, anaerobic protists. What it excludes is aerobic respiration — no fish, no aerobic invertebrates, no aerobic decomposers. The anoxic zone is biologically active at the microbial level.

**Turnover does not mean the lake is "clean" after the event.**
Fall turnover redistributes accumulated bottom-water nutrients to the surface, potentially triggering a late-season algal pulse ("fall bloom") if nutrients are elevated. Turnover resets the temperature profile but not the nutrient loading history.

**The Secchi depth to euphotic depth conversion is empirical and variable.**
The factor of 2–2.5× works for most lakes but breaks down in brown-water (humic) lakes where DOC absorbs light strongly. In such lakes, Secchi depth underestimates euphotic depth because the disc disappears due to absorption, not scattering.
