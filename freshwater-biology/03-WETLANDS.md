# Wetlands: Classification, Hydrology, Blue Carbon, Ecosystem Services

## The Big Picture

Wetlands are defined by three co-occurring features: hydric soils, hydrophytic vegetation, and wetland hydrology. They are the most productive ecosystems per unit area on Earth, the largest single source of atmospheric methane, and the most effective per-hectare carbon sinks when preserved. They occupy a physical continuum from upland to deep water and are classified by multiple overlapping systems.

```
WETLAND CONTINUUM — UPLAND TO OPEN WATER

  UPLAND         WETLAND TYPES               OPEN WATER
  ──────┬────────────────────────────────────┬──────────
        │Marsh   Swamp   Bog    Fen  Riparian│
        │        Shrub   Peat  bog  wetland  │
        │        wetland       fen           │
  Dry   │← wet soil ← standing water →─────→│ Aquatic
  soil  │  (seasonally   (semi-permanently   │ system
        │   saturated)    to permanently     │
        │                 flooded)           │

  HYDROPERIOD (duration of inundation/saturation) is the primary defining variable:
  Short (<30 days/yr): seasonally wet -- wet meadow
  Moderate (30–100 days): irregularly flooded marshes, wet prairies
  Long (>100 days): regularly to semi-permanently flooded
  Permanent: always wet -- deep marsh, swamp
```

## Section 1 — Cowardin Classification (USFWS 1979)

The standard US classification system. Five systems, each subdivided by subsystem, class, subclass, water regime, water chemistry, and special modifiers.

### Five Systems

```
  MARINE:           Tidal wetlands on open ocean coast; saltmarsh, mangrove
  ESTUARINE:        Tidal influenced by salt/fresh mixing; brackish marsh
  RIVERINE:         Inland waters in channels with defined banks (rivers + streams)
  LACUSTRINE:       Inland standing water > 8 ha OR > 2 m deep; lakes + ponds
  PALUSTRINE:       All non-tidal freshwater wetlands -- the largest category
                    Includes: marshes, swamps, bogs, fens, wet meadows, playas
```

### Palustrine Subclassification (most important for freshwater biology)

| Class | Water Regime | Dominant Vegetation | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|---------|
| Aquatic Bed | Permanently flooded | Submergent or floating macrophytes | Pondweed beds |
| Emergent (persistent) | Semi-permanent to permanent | Cattail, bulrush, reed | Prairie pothole marsh |
| Scrub-Shrub | Seasonally to semi-permanent | Willows, alders | Riparian shrub wetland |
| Forested | Seasonally to semi-permanent | Trees > 3 m | Bottomland hardwood swamp |
| Unconsolidated Bottom | Intermittent to permanent | Bare sediment | Mudflat |

### Marsh vs. Swamp vs. Bog vs. Fen

```
  MARSH:
  +-- Emergent herbaceous vegetation (graminoids, sedges, rushes, cattails)
  +-- Mineral soil (no peat, or thin peat)
  +-- Mineral nutrient input from surface or groundwater flow
  +-- High productivity; important waterfowl habitat
  +-- Examples: prairie potholes, tidal freshwater marshes, Great Lakes coastal

  SWAMP:
  +-- Wooded (trees or shrubs dominate)
  +-- Seasonally to permanently flooded
  +-- Mineral soil or shallow peat
  +-- Examples: cypress swamps, bottomland hardwood (southern US),
                black spruce muskeg (boreal)

  BOG:
  +-- Ombrotrophic (rain-fed only; no groundwater input)
  +-- Sphagnum moss dominant; peat-accumulating
  +-- Extremely nutrient-poor, low pH (3.5–4.5), high DOC
  +-- Carnivorous plants (sundews, pitcher plants) supplement nutrient
  +-- Cold-climate dominant (boreal, maritime)

  FEN:
  +-- Minerotrophic (groundwater and/or surface water input)
  +-- Sphagnum + brown mosses + sedges
  +-- Nutrient-poor but richer than bog (especially Ca, Mg)
  +-- pH slightly higher than bog (4.5–7.5 depending on water chemistry)
  +-- Grading: poor fen (bog-like) --> rich fen (more mineral influence)
  +-- Examples: subarctic fens, calcareous fens (limestone groundwater)
```

## Section 2 — Hydric Soils

Hydric soils develop under conditions of prolonged saturation/flooding. They are one of the three mandatory criteria for wetland delineation (US Army Corps of Engineers methodology).

```
  HYDRIC SOIL DEVELOPMENT:
  Saturated conditions --> oxygen diffusion rate < microbial consumption rate
  --> ANAEROBIC CONDITIONS in soil matrix

  REDUCTION SEQUENCE (oxidized --> reduced, as O2 depleted):
  O2 consumed first (Eh > +300 mV)
  NO3- reduced to N2 (denitrification; Eh +200 to +300 mV)
  Mn4+ reduced to Mn2+ (purple-black to gray; Eh +100 to +200 mV)
  Fe3+ reduced to Fe2+ (brown-red to gray/olive; Eh -100 to +100 mV)
  SO4²- reduced to S²- (H2S; Eh -100 to -200 mV)
  CO2 reduced to CH4 (methanogenesis; Eh < -200 mV)

  MORPHOLOGICAL INDICATORS:
  Low chroma (<2) matrix color: gray, olive-gray colors from reduced Fe
  Redoximorphic concentrations (mottles): orange-red spots (oxidized Fe)
    around root channels where O2 enters from roots
  Depleted matrix: gray matrix (reduced zone)
  Sulfidic materials: black; H2S smell

  MUNSELL COLOR SYSTEM used for hydric soil identification:
  Chroma <= 2 = indicator of hydric conditions
  (Chroma is the intensity of color in the Munsell system; low = grayish)
```

## Section 3 — Hydroperiod

Hydroperiod (the annual pattern of water presence and depth) is the master variable controlling wetland type, community composition, and function.

```

  Water

  1.0 |      ████

  0.6 |    ████████

  0.2 |  ████████████
  0.0 |──────────────────────────────────────────────────>
       Jan  Feb  Mar  Apr  May  Jun  Jul  Aug  Sep  Oct  Nov  Dec

  This wetland is dry June–September --> determines:
  - Which plant species can establish and survive
  - Which invertebrates complete life cycles (need dry-down for some)
  - Fish exclusion (cannot survive annual drying = amphibian advantage)
  - Waterfowl use timing
  - Nutrient cycling (aerobic processing during dry-down)
```

## Section 4 — Wetland Carbon Dynamics and Blue Carbon

### Carbon Sequestration vs. Methane Emission

Wetlands are both the world's most important carbon sinks (per area) and the largest natural source of atmospheric methane. The net climate impact depends on wetland type, temperature, and carbon storage timescale.

```
  CARBON DYNAMICS IN PEATLANDS:

  NET PRIMARY PRODUCTION (NPP): ~500–2000 gC/m²/yr in productive wetlands
  DECOMPOSITION: < NPP because:
    Waterlogged conditions limit aerobic decomposition
    Sphagnum contains phenolic compounds that resist decay
    Low pH inhibits bacterial activity in bogs

  NET C ACCUMULATION (long-term peat): ~10–30 gC/m²/yr
  (slow but consistent over thousands of years)

  METHANE (CH4) PRODUCTION:
  Methanogenesis occurs in anoxic zone below water table
  CH4 moves to surface by: ebullition (bubbles), diffusion, plant transport
  (Sphagnum, sedges, reeds have aerenchyma = gas channels)

  TYPICAL FLUX RATES:
  Bog: C seq. 15–30 gC/m²/yr | CH4 emission 1–10 gC/m²/yr
  Fen: C seq. 20–80 gC/m²/yr | CH4 emission 10–50 gC/m²/yr
  Tropical peat swamp: C seq. up to 200 gC/m²/yr | High CH4

  NET RADIATIVE FORCING:
  CO2 forcing of carbon storage is negative (cooling)
  CH4 forcing is positive (warming) -- CH4 has GWP100 = 28–34
  For most peatlands on 100-yr timescale: net cooling
  For drained/degraded peatlands: massive net warming
```

### Global Peat Carbon

```
  GLOBAL PEAT STATISTICS:
  Area: ~3–4 million km² (roughly size of India + Pakistan)
  Carbon stored: ~500–600 Gt C (= ~30% of all soil carbon despite 3% of land area)
  Northern hemisphere peatlands: ~90% of global peat area

  DRAINED PEATLAND:
  ~15% of global peatlands drained for agriculture (especially SE Asia, Europe)
  Drained peat oxidizes rapidly: 1–3 kg CO2/m²/yr from oxidation alone
  SE Asia peat swamp conversion: releases ~2 Gt CO2/yr globally (all drained peat)
  = ~5–6% of global fossil fuel emissions

  Rewetting drained peatland:
  Stops CO2 release within years
  Carbon sequestration resumes within decades
  Most cost-effective climate mitigation per dollar (often < $10/tonne CO2)
```

## Section 5 — Ecosystem Services

Wetlands deliver disproportionate ecosystem services per unit area:

```
  WETLAND ECOSYSTEM SERVICES:

  PROVISIONING:
  +-- Fish production (many fish spawn/feed in wetlands)
  +-- Wild rice, cranberries, waterfowl hunting
  +-- Medicinal plants
  +-- Peat as fuel (traditional)

  REGULATING:
  +-- FLOOD STORAGE: 1 ha of wetland stores ~3,000 m³ of floodwater
      Prairie potholes in North Dakota estimated to reduce flood damage
      by $400M/yr
  +-- WATER QUALITY: N removal via denitrification; P sedimentation
      Constructed wetlands treat wastewater; remove 60–90% of N
  +-- CARBON SEQUESTRATION: 250–1,000 gCO2/m²/yr (varies by type)
  +-- CLIMATE BUFFERING: evapotranspiration cools local climate

  CULTURAL:
  +-- Biodiversity: ~40% of world's species use wetlands at some point
  +-- Birdwatching (migratory shorebirds depend on wetland stopover)
  +-- Cultural value (sacred in many traditions)

  SUPPORTING:
  +-- Nutrient cycling (N, P, Si cycling)
  +-- Sediment trapping
  +-- Primary production base for adjacent systems
```

## Section 6 — Ramsar Convention

The Convention on Wetlands of International Importance (Ramsar, Iran, 1971) is the oldest modern intergovernmental environmental treaty focused on a single ecosystem type.

```
  RAMSAR KEY ELEMENTS:
  +-- Contracting Parties: 172 countries (2024)
  +-- Ramsar List: 2,500+ sites designated of international importance
  +-- Total area: 257 million ha (comparable to Greenland)
  +-- "Wise Use" principle: sustainable use without essential character change

  RAMSAR CRITERIA FOR LISTING:
  Group A: Sites containing representative, rare, or unique wetland types
  Group B: Sites important for biodiversity:
    B1: Regularly supports 20,000+ waterbirds
    B2: Regularly supports 1% of biogeographic population of waterbird species
    B3: Important fish habitat/spawning/feeding
    B5: Important for non-avian animal species

  LIMITATIONS:
  No enforcement mechanism -- "moral obligation" on parties
  Can designate sites but cannot prevent drainage if government permits it
  Listing protects against international pressure but not domestic law
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Swamp ≠ bog in ecological usage.**
Popular usage conflates all wet areas. Technically: swamp = wooded, mineral soil, variable hydrology. Bog = peat-forming, Sphagnum-dominated, rain-fed, very acidic. A cypress swamp is nutrient-richer than a bog by orders of magnitude.

**Wetland CH4 emissions do not negate peatland carbon storage value.**
The 100-year global warming potential of CH4 is ~28–34× CO2. But peatland CH4 is emitted on the same timescale as carbon is sequestered. On millennial timescales, peatlands are net cooling agents because CO2 drawdown exceeds CH4 warming. Draining them reverses both: stops CH4, massively accelerates CO2 release.

**Constructed wetlands are not wetland restoration.**
A constructed wetland for wastewater treatment is an engineered system optimized for N/P removal. It is not equivalent to natural wetland biodiversity value. "Compensatory mitigation" wetlands created to replace destroyed wetlands frequently fail to replicate biodiversity or carbon function.

**Ramsar designation does not mean a wetland is legally protected.**
Ramsar is an international treaty with no enforcement. A Ramsar site can be legally drained by a national government — and many have been. The designation creates political/diplomatic pressure, not legal protection.
