# Freshwater Organisms: Fish, Amphibians, Macroinvertebrates, Algae, Macrophytes

## The Big Picture

Freshwater biodiversity is structured across all trophic levels from phytoplankton to apex predators. Each major taxonomic group contributes distinct ecological functions and provides different information about ecosystem health. Bioassessment uses these organisms as biological indicators — translating species composition and abundance into water quality metrics.

```
FRESHWATER FOOD WEB — SIMPLIFIED STRUCTURE

  INPUTS:                   ALLOCHTHONOUS              AUTOCHTHONOUS
  (energy sources)          (from land: CPOM, DOC)     (in-water: algae, macrophytes)
                                       |                       |
                                       v                       v
  PRIMARY PRODUCERS:                              ALGAE (periphyton, phytoplankton)
                                                  MACROPHYTES (submerged, emergent)
                                                       |
  GRAZERS / SHREDDERS:   Macroinvertebrates (EPT, snails, cladocerans, copepods)
                                       |
  SECONDARY CONSUMERS:   Small fish (minnows, dace), large invertebrate predators
                                       |
  APEX PREDATORS:        Piscivorous fish (bass, pike, trout), otters, herons
```

## Section 1 — Fish

### Freshwater Fish Orders

Freshwater fish diversity is organized around ~10 major orders that contain most ecologically and commercially important species.

```
  KEY ORDERS:

  CYPRINIFORMES (Minnows, Carp, Loaches):
  +-- Largest freshwater order: ~4,500 species
  +-- Cyprinidae (minnows/carp): toothless jaws; pharyngeal teeth
  +-- Examples: common carp, roach, chub, bream, bleak
  +-- Ecological role: mid-water and benthic grazers; suspended particle feeders
  +-- Bioassessment: sensitive to turbidity, organic pollution, flow modification
  +-- INVASIVE: common carp -- bioturbation; destroys macrophytes; worsens eutrophication

  SALMONIFORMES (Trout, Salmon, Charr, Whitefish):
  +-- Cold, high-O2 requirement; indicator of clean, cool headwaters
  +-- Anadromous (salmon, sea trout): marine feeding + freshwater spawning
  +-- Pacific salmon: semelparous (die after spawning); marine N returned to catchment
  +-- Atlantic salmon: iteroparous (can spawn multiple times)
  +-- O2 threshold: > 7 mg/L preferred; eggs need > 8 mg/L in hyporheic
  +-- Temperature limit: most species < 20°C; summer thermal barrier

  PERCIFORMES / PERCIDAE (Perch, Pike-perch, Darters):
  +-- Perch (Perca): mid-level predator; tolerant of moderate enrichment
  +-- Walleye (Sander vitreus): piscivore; cold water; important sport fish
  +-- Darters (Etheostoma, 250+ spp.): benthic N. America; extreme sensitivity indicators

  ESOCIFORMES (Pike, Pickerel):
  +-- Pike (Esox lucius): top predator; ambush; controls prey community
  +-- Key piscivore in European lake ecology

  SILURIFORMES (Catfish):
  +-- Benthic; barbels for chemosensory detection
  +-- Tolerant of low O2, turbidity; often indicator of degraded systems
  +-- INVASIVE: European catfish (Silurus glanis) established in UK/W. Europe

  CENTRARCHIDAE (Sunfish, Bass, Bluegill -- N. American):
  +-- Largemouth bass (Micropterus salmoides): key trophic cascade player
  +-- Bluegill: prey base; limnological experiment keystone (see Guide 07)
  +-- INVASIVE: established in many countries outside N. America
```

### Freshwater Fish Life Strategies

| Guild | Description | Water Quality Indicator |
|-------|-------------|------------------------|
| Obligate headwater (rheophilic) | Require cold, fast, O2-rich water | Salmonids: Class A clean water |
| Benthic invertivores | Feed on macroinvertebrates in substrate | Sensitive species like darters |
| Mid-water planktivores | Feed on zooplankton; schooling | Common in mesotrophic lakes |
| Piscivores | Feed on other fish | Indicate sufficient food web complexity |
| Generalists (tolerant) | Survive wide range of conditions | Carp, sunfish, bullheads: degraded conditions |

## Section 2 — Amphibians

### Life Cycles and Freshwater Dependency

Most amphibians have complex life cycles requiring aquatic habitat for at least the larval stage:

```
  TYPICAL ANURAN (Frog/Toad) LIFE CYCLE:
  [Terrestrial adults] -- migrate to pond in spring
          |
          v
  [Amplexus + egg deposition] -- in water; eggs protected by jelly
          |
          v
  [Tadpoles] -- entirely aquatic; gills; herbivorous to omnivorous
          | metamorphosis (hormonal; triggered by thyroid hormones)
          v
  [Froglets] -- emerge from water; lung-breathing; return to terrestrial
          |
          v
  [Adult frogs] -- overwinter in water (many spp.) or terrestrial

  HABITAT REQUIREMENTS:
  Breeding: Standing or very slow water; no fish (most pond breeders) OR
            fast streams (stream-breeding salamanders)
  Tadpoles: Avoid fish predation; need algae + detritus
  Adults: Moist terrestrial habitat; forest interior preferred
  Overwintering: Under mud at lake bottom (many frogs); absorb O2 through skin

  THREATS:
  Chytrid fungus (Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis, Bd): most devastating
  wildlife disease ever recorded; responsible for >90 amphibian extinctions
  since 1970; reduces skin sodium transport; kills by cardiac arrest
  B. salamandrivorans (Bsal): decimating European salamanders
```

### Amphibian Bioassessment Value

Amphibians serve as sensitive indicators because:
- Permeable skin absorbs contaminants directly
- Biphasic life cycle exposes them to both aquatic and terrestrial stressors
- Site fidelity (breed at natal ponds) makes local conditions decisive
- Population declines are integrative signals of cumulative stress

## Section 3 — Macroinvertebrates and EPT Groups

Macroinvertebrates are animals visible to the naked eye (> 0.5 mm) living on or in the substrate. They are the most widely used bioassessment tool in freshwater systems.

### EPT — The Three Indicator Groups

EPT = **E**phemeroptera (mayflies) + **P**lecoptera (stoneflies) + **T**richoptera (caddisflies).

```
  EPHEMEROPTERA (Mayflies):
  +-- Hemimetabolous (no pupal stage): egg --> nymph --> adult
  +-- Nymphs: aquatic; 1–3 yr; gills along abdomen; flat for clinging
  +-- Adults: very short-lived (hours to days); don't feed; mass emergence
  +-- EPT sensitivity: variable -- some very sensitive (Heptageniidae),
      some moderately tolerant (Baetidae)
  +-- FFG: scrapers (algae), gatherers (FPOM), filter feeders

  PLECOPTERA (Stoneflies):
  +-- Hemimetabolous; nymphs aquatic 1–3 yr (some to 7 yr)
  +-- Require cold, well-oxygenated, clean water (most families)
  +-- Chloroperlidae, Perlidae, Peltoperlidae: sensitive predators
  +-- Key diagnostic value: their absence indicates O2 depletion, warming, toxins
  +-- Adults: poor fliers; live only days–weeks

  TRICHOPTERA (Caddisflies):
  +-- Holometabolous (complete metamorphosis): egg --> larva --> pupa --> adult
  +-- Larvae: construct portable cases (sand grains, leaf pieces, bark) or
              build fixed retreats + capture nets; material species-diagnostic
  +-- Rich in species (>14,000 described); diverse FFGs
  +-- Variable sensitivity: some very sensitive, some tolerant
```

### Biotic Indices Using EPT

```
  SIMPLE EPT RICHNESS:
  Count number of EPT taxa (genus-level or family-level) at a site
  High EPT richness (>20 taxa) = reference-quality stream
  Low EPT richness (<5 taxa) = impaired

  HILSENHOFF BIOTIC INDEX (HBI):
  Weighted average of taxon tolerances to organic pollution
  Each taxon assigned tolerance value 0–10 (0 = intolerant; 10 = very tolerant)
  HBI = Σ(ni × ti) / N
  Where ni = abundance of taxon i, ti = tolerance value, N = total organisms

  HBI INTERPRETATION:
  0.00–3.50   Excellent: organic pollution unlikely
  3.51–4.50   Very good: possible slight organic pollution
  4.51–5.50   Good: some organic pollution probable
  5.51–6.50   Fair: fairly significant organic pollution likely
  6.51–8.50   Poor: significant organic pollution probable
  8.51–10.00  Very poor: severe organic pollution evident
```

### Other Important Invertebrate Groups

| Group | Ecology | Bioassessment Role |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Odonata (damselflies/dragonflies) | Nymphs: predatory; 1–5 yr aquatic | Moderate indicator; some sensitive |
| Diptera (midges/crane flies) | Chironomidae: abundant in all water types | Chironomid deformities = toxicity indicator |
| Coleoptera (beetles) | Adults + larvae aquatic (Dytiscidae, Elmidae) | Elmidae: riffle beetles = clean, O2-rich |
| Amphipoda (scuds) | Gammarus: key shredder; tolerant-moderate | Gammarus pulex: common UK bioindicator |
| Isopoda (water louse) | Asellus: tolerant; enriched conditions | High Asellus, low EPT = organic pollution |
| Bivalvia (mussels) | Filter feeders; long-lived (up to 100 yr) | Unionid mussels = pristine rivers only |
| Gastropoda (snails) | Scrapers; spiral shells | Sensitive species (Ancylidae) in clean streams |

## Section 4 — Diatoms and Algae

### Diatoms (Bacillariophyta)

Diatoms are unicellular algae with ornate silica cell walls (frustules). They are the most widely used algal bioassessment group.

```
  DIATOM STRUCTURE:
  +-- Frustule: silica (SiO2) box with two valves (epitheca + hypotheca)
  +-- Symmetry: pennate (elongated, bilaterally symmetric) or
                centric (circular, radially symmetric)
  +-- Frustule ornament species-diagnostic; preserved in sediment for millennia

  DIATOM ECOLOGICAL GUILDS (benthic):
  High-profile: large erect forms; best light access; sensitive to disturbance
  Low-profile: small adnate forms; resist high current; tolerant of abrasion
  Motile: move through biofilm; tolerant of high nutrient, disturbance

  DIATOM INDICES FOR WATER QUALITY:
  IPS (Indice de Polluosensibilite Specifique): European standard
  TDI (Trophic Diatom Index): UK standard; based on nutrient tolerance
  Uses: TP (total phosphorus), NO3, pH, BOD tolerance values of each taxon
  Weighted average approach (same logic as HBI above)
```

### Cyanobacteria (Blue-Green Algae)

Not true algae (are bacteria) but function as algal primary producers:

```
  CYANOBACTERIA ECOLOGICAL ROLE:
  +-- Fix atmospheric N2 (many genera: Anabaena, Aphanizomenon)
  +-- Competitive advantage in N-limited conditions
  +-- Gas vacuoles for buoyancy regulation --> float to surface
  +-- Toxin production: microcystins (liver toxins), cylindrospermopsin,
      saxitoxins (neurotoxins) -- public health concern
  +-- Bloom-forming: Microcystis, Aphanizomenon, Planktothrix, Dolichospermum
  +-- Late-succession dominants in eutrophic systems (see Guide 06)
```

## Section 5 — Macrophytes

Aquatic macrophytes are the large plants of freshwater systems: submergents, emergents, floating-leaved, and free-floating.

```
  MACROPHYTE GROWTH FORMS:

  EMERGENT:          Rooted in sediment; stems above water
                     Phragmites (reed), Typha (cattail), Scirpus (bulrush)
                     Dominate shallow margins; high productivity; CPOM input

  SUBMERGED:         Entirely below water; rooted (mostly)
                     Potamogeton, Myriophyllum, Ceratophyllum, Elodea
                     Oxygenate water; reduce turbidity; provide invertebrate habitat

  FLOATING-LEAVED:   Rooted; leaves on surface
                     Nuphar (yellow water lily), Nymphaea (white water lily)
                     Block light to submerged plants below
                     Leaf turnover contributes to CPOM

  FREE-FLOATING:     Not rooted; drift
                     Lemna (duckweed), Salvinia, water hyacinth (Eichhornia)
                     INVASIVE: water hyacinth -- blocks light; depletes O2; clogs waterways
```

### Macrophyte Bioassessment

The EU Water Framework Directive (WFD) uses macrophyte community composition as one of five biological quality elements (BQEs) for ecological status classification:

| Status Class | Macrophyte Indicator |
|-------------|---------------------|
| High (reference) | Species-rich submerged macrophytes; sensitive species present; no invasives |
| Good | Moderate reduction in sensitive species; slight increase in tolerant |
| Moderate | Significant shift toward nutrient-tolerant species; dominance by emergents |
| Poor | Reduced diversity; tolerant species dominate; sparse submerged macrophytes |
| Bad | Essentially no macrophytes or dominated by free-floating mat (duckweed, Azolla) |

## Section 6 — Endemism Hotspots

Freshwater organisms show extraordinary local endemism — many species have ranges of single lake basins or river systems.

```
  GLOBAL FRESHWATER ENDEMISM HOTSPOTS:

  LAKE BAIKAL (Russia):
  +-- ~1,500 endemic species; 80% of invertebrates endemic
  +-- 50-60 endemic fish species; Baikal seal (Pusa sibirica) -- only freshwater seal
  +-- Age (~25 Myr) explains high endemism: time for speciation
  +-- Amphipod (Eulimnogammarus): 350+ endemic species in single genus

  LAKE TANGANYIKA (E. Africa):
  +-- 800+ endemic fish species; highest cichlid diversity in one lake
  +-- 200+ endemic invertebrate species
  +-- Age 9–12 Myr; deep (1,470 m); permanently stratified (meromictic in sense of low mixing)

  LAKE VICTORIA (E. Africa):
  +-- ~500 haplochromine cichlid species evolved in <15,000 yr (post-glacial)
  +-- Fastest documented adaptive radiation in vertebrates
  +-- Most cichlids now extinct/endangered after Nile perch introduction (1950s)

  APPALACHIAN STREAMS (E. USA):
  +-- Highest freshwater mussel diversity globally: 91 species
  +-- Most are threatened or endangered (83% threatened status)
  +-- Highest darter species richness globally (Etheostoma: 250+ sp.)

  MEKONG RIVER:
  +-- 1,000+ fish species; world's most productive inland fishery
  +-- Mekong giant catfish (Pangasianodon gigas): up to 300 kg; critically endangered
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What organisms best indicate clean headwater streams? | Stonefly (Plecoptera) nymphs — most sensitive EPT group |
| What organism indicates organic pollution? | High Chironomidae, Tubificidae, Asellus + low/absent EPT |
| Why are salmonids restricted to headwaters in summer? | Thermal tolerance < 20°C; dissolved O2 requirement > 7 mg/L |
| What killed 90+ amphibian species globally? | Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (chytrid fungus) |
| What do diatoms tell you about phosphorus? | Diatom indices (IPS, TDI) weighted by taxon TP tolerance values |
| Why are Unionid mussels good long-term indicators? | Lifespan 50–100 yr; integrate decades of water quality; host-specific to fish for larval stage |
| What makes Lake Victoria cichlid loss unique? | Fastest adaptive radiation (15,000 yr) + fastest documented extinction wave (50 yr after Nile perch) |

## Common Confusion Points

**EPT richness and EPT %A are different metrics.**
EPT richness = count of taxa regardless of abundance. EPT %A = proportion of total invertebrate abundance that is EPT. Both are used; EPT %A is more sensitive to organic enrichment because tolerant organisms increase in abundance even if EPT are present.

**Cyanobacteria are not algae, though they function similarly.**
Cyanobacteria are gram-negative bacteria (prokaryotes). They are often called "blue-green algae" but are phylogenetically bacteria. Their N-fixation ability, buoyancy regulation, and toxin production are all bacterial-kingdom traits, not algal traits.

**Diatom frustule Si requirement connects to nutrient cycling.**
Diatom blooms deplete dissolved silica (DSi) from the water. Reduced Si availability (from upstream dams that trap Si-bearing sediment, or watershed change) can trigger diatom → cyanobacteria succession even without P loading change.

**Amphibian metamorphosis requires specific triggers.**
Tadpole-to-frog metamorphosis is mediated by thyroid hormones. Environmental endocrine disruptors (atrazine, tributyltin, estrogenic compounds) interfere with metamorphosis timing and sex determination. Intersex frogs from atrazine exposure were documented at agricultural runoff concentrations.
