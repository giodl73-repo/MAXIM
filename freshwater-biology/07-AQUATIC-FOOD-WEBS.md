# Aquatic Food Webs: Trophic Cascades, Keystone Species, Bioaccumulation

## The Big Picture

Freshwater food webs are controlled both from the bottom up (nutrient availability controlling algal production → herbivore → predator) and from the top down (apex predators controlling prey and indirectly controlling lower trophic levels). The Carpenter lake experiments established that top-down control is often decisive in lakes — a result with direct management implications.

```
AQUATIC FOOD WEB — LAKE SYSTEM

  NUTRIENTS (N, P, Si)
       |
       v (bottom-up)
  PHYTOPLANKTON + PERIPHYTON
       |
       v
  ZOOPLANKTON (Daphnia, copepods, rotifers)  <-- KEY INTERMEDIATE
       |                                          (controls algal biomass
       v                                           AND is controlled by fish)
  PLANKTIVOROUS FISH (bluegill, roach, ciscoes, shad)
       |
       v (top-down)
  PISCIVOROUS FISH (largemouth bass, pike, walleye, trout)
       |
       v
  APEX PREDATORS (otters, ospreys, herons, humans)

  TROPHIC CASCADE:
  Piscivore increase --> planktivore decrease --> Daphnia increase --> algae decrease
  Piscivore decrease --> planktivore increase --> Daphnia decrease --> algae increase

  BOTTOM-UP + TOP-DOWN interact; relative strength varies by lake type
```

## Section 1 — The Carpenter Lake Experiments

Stephen Carpenter and colleagues at the University of Wisconsin conducted whole-lake experiments (1985–1990s) at the UNDERC facility that definitively demonstrated top-down trophic cascades in lake ecosystems.

### Experimental Design

```
  CARPENTER et al. (1985–1987) EXPERIMENTAL SETUP:

  Three lakes (Peter, Paul, Tuesday) in Wisconsin:

  PETER LAKE (manipulated):
  Removed piscivorous fish (bass)
  Added planktivorous fish (minnows)

  PAUL LAKE (reference):
  No manipulation; continued monitoring

  TUESDAY LAKE:
  Inverse manipulation: added bass; removed minnows

  MEASUREMENTS:
  +-- Zooplankton biomass and body size
  +-- Phytoplankton biomass (chlorophyll a)
  +-- Water transparency (Secchi depth)
  +-- Nutrient concentrations (P, N)
```

### Results

```
  PETER LAKE (added planktivores, removed piscivores):
  Year 1-2: Daphnia population crashes (planktivore predation)
  Year 2-3: Phytoplankton biomass increases (grazers gone)
  Year 3-4: Water clarity decreases markedly (Secchi -60%)

  TUESDAY LAKE (added piscivores, removed planktivores):
  Year 1-2: Daphnia population increases (planktivore predation released)
  Year 2-3: Phytoplankton biomass decreases dramatically
  Year 3-4: Water clarity increases markedly (Secchi +100%)
  Zooplankton body size: INCREASED (larger Daphnia; small forms grazed out)

  PAUL LAKE (reference): No significant change

  KEY RESULTS:
  1. Top predator (bass) controls water clarity through 3-level cascade
  2. Effect transmitted through 4 trophic levels
  3. Magnitude: ~200% change in Secchi depth with no nutrient change
  4. The cascade is "strong" in lake systems (P limited, closed basin)
```

### Why Lakes Are Especially Susceptible to Trophic Cascades

```
  LAKE CHARACTERISTICS FAVORING STRONG CASCADES:
  +-- Planktonic system: phytoplankton and zooplankton are mobile and responsive
  +-- Few species at each trophic level: less redundancy, stronger per-species effect
  +-- Daphnia is highly effective grazer: can clear an entire epilimnion
  +-- Closed basin: cascade effects not diluted by flow from upstream
  +-- Short generation times: rapid population responses to predator change

  CONTRAST WITH TERRESTRIAL SYSTEMS:
  Terrestrial food webs: many plant species, many herbivores, multiple redundancies
  Cascades weaker: removal of one predator often compensated by other predators
  Lakes: simpler webs, stronger per-trophic-level control
```

## Section 2 — Keystone Species in Freshwater

A keystone species has a disproportionate effect on ecosystem structure relative to its biomass.

### Daphnia — Keystone Grazer

```
  DAPHNIA (water flea, Cladocera):
  +-- Filter feeder: ingests particles 0.1–50 µm (phytoplankton + bacteria)
  +-- Clearance rate: 1–10 mL/individual/hr; dense Daphnia population
      can filter entire lake volume every few days
  +-- Size: D. pulex ~1–3 mm; D. magna up to 5 mm; D. lumholtzi (tropical) 6+ mm
  +-- Vertical migration: daily migration to deep water (photoprotection, avoid predation)
  +-- Population dynamics: exponential growth when food available; parthenogenetic
      (clonal) in favorable conditions; sexual reproduction when stressed

  DAPHNIA POPULATION COLLAPSE IN EUTROPHIC LAKES:
  Cyanobacteria: filaments too large to process; clog filtration apparatus
  Planktivorous fish (roach, bluegill): high visual predation pressure
  --> Loss of large-bodied Daphnia --> community shifts to small rotifers + copepods
  --> Rotifers/small copepods less effective algal grazers
  --> Algal biomass increases
```

### Bass (Micropterus) — Keystone Piscivore in North America

```
  LARGEMOUTH BASS (Micropterus salmoides):
  +-- Ambush predator; visual; attacks prey up to ~50% of own body length
  +-- Primarily controls sunfish (Lepomis gibbosus, L. macrochirus) and minnow populations
  +-- Removal --> planktivore increase --> Daphnia decrease --> algal increase
  +-- Key result: bass abundance directly determines lake water clarity through cascade

  BASS TEMPERATURE SENSITIVITY:
  Optimal: 20–27°C; tolerates 10–35°C
  Summer: retreats to cooler, O2-rich water (avoids hypolimnion)
  Climate change concern: warming lakes may reduce bass effectiveness as apex predator
```

### Pike (Esox lucius) — European/Boreal Keystone Piscivore

```
  NORTHERN PIKE:
  +-- Ambush predator; large size (up to 1.5 m, 25+ kg)
  +-- Controls roach, perch, bream populations in European lakes
  +-- Critical for "biomanipulation" restoration projects in Europe
  +-- Highly susceptible to thermal change: prefers cold, macrophyte-rich margins
  +-- Removal in lake restoration: roach increase --> Daphnia decrease --> bloom increase
```

## Section 3 — River Trophic Structure

River food webs differ from lake food webs in several key ways:

```
  RIVER vs. LAKE FOOD WEB:

  LAKE: Planktonic; algae --> zooplankton --> planktivorous fish
  RIVER: Largely benthic; periphyton/CPOM --> macroinvertebrates --> fish

  RIVER KEYSTONE INTERACTIONS:
  Pacific salmon: TERRESTRIAL --> MARINE --> FRESHWATER LINKAGE
  Anadromous salmon return to spawn and die in freshwater:
  Marine-derived N and P (high 15N and 34S isotope signature)
  deposited in streams as carcass biomass (up to 300 kg/ha in some streams)
  Bears/eagles move carcasses to riparian zone: N fertilizes streamside vegetation
  Up to 25% of streamside tree N may come from salmon marine-derived nutrients

  QUANTITATIVE EXAMPLE:
  Large salmon stream: 3,000 salmon/km; avg. 5 kg carcass = 15,000 kg/km
  Marine-derived N at 3% N content: 450 kg N/km deposited
  Versus: nitrogen fixation in riparian alder: ~50–100 kg N/ha/yr
  Salmon N input can equal or exceed N fixation in watershed
```

## Section 4 — Bioaccumulation and Biomagnification

Lipophilic (fat-soluble) contaminants accumulate in organisms and increase in concentration at each trophic level.

```
  BIOACCUMULATION vs. BIOMAGNIFICATION:

  BIOACCUMULATION: concentration in organism > concentration in water
  Single species; ratio = Bioconcentration Factor (BCF)

  BCF = [contaminant in organism] / [contaminant in water]
  For PCBs in fish: BCF = 10,000–100,000 (organism concentration >> water)

  BIOMAGNIFICATION: concentration increases with trophic level
  Each trophic level is ~10× more concentrated than the one below

  BIOMAGNIFICATION DIAGRAM:

  WATER:    0.00002 µg PCB/L     (1×)
  PHYTOPLANKTON: 0.025 µg PCB/g  (~100,000×)
  ZOOPLANKTON:   0.12 µg PCB/g   (~500,000×)
  SMALL FISH:    1.04 µg PCB/g   (~5,000,000×)
  LARGE FISH:    10.8 µg PCB/g   (~54,000,000×)
  OSPREY/EAGLE:  124 µg PCB/g    (~620,000,000×)

  MECHANISM:
  Lipophilic compounds partition into fat tissue; not excreted
  When predator eats prey: accumulates fat-bound contaminants from entire prey body
  Each trophic transfer concentrates by ~factor 10 (trophic magnification factor)
```

### Priority Contaminants in Freshwater

| Contaminant | Source | Bioaccumulation | Effect |
|-------------|--------|-----------------|--------|
| **PCBs** (polychlorinated biphenyls) | Industrial/historical use | High; BMF ~10/level | Endocrine disruption; carcinogen |
| **Mercury (Hg)** | Coal combustion; gold mining | Very high as methylmercury | Neurotoxin; FDA fish advisories |
| **DDT/DDE** | Agricultural insecticide (banned US 1972) | Very high | Eggshell thinning in raptors; bald eagle decline |
| **PFAS** (per/polyfluoroalkyl) | Firefighting foam; non-stick coatings | Very high; extremely persistent | Endocrine disruption; carcinogen |
| **Atrazine** | Herbicide (agricultural) | Moderate | Endocrine disruption; intersex amphibians |
| **Lead** | Paint, pipes, ammunition | Moderate | Neurotoxin; waterfowl mortality from spent shot |

### Mercury: The Model Contaminant

Mercury in freshwater is uniquely problematic because of methylation:

```
  MERCURY BIOGEOCHEMISTRY:

  ATMOSPHERIC DEPOSITION: Hg0 + HgII from coal combustion
          |
          v
  INORGANIC Hg in water column/sediment
          |
          | METHYLATION (anaerobic bacteria; sulfate-reducing bacteria)
          | Most active in: anoxic sediment, wetlands, hypolimnion
          v
  METHYLMERCURY (MeHg): highly bioaccumulative (log BCF ~5.5)
          |
          v
  ZOOPLANKTON and macroinvertebrates accumulate MeHg
          |
          v
  FISH: MeHg in muscle tissue (not easily excreted)
  Older, larger, piscivorous fish: highest MeHg
          |
          v
  HUMAN CONSUMPTION: FDA advisory for pregnant women:
  < 2 servings/week tuna; avoid shark/swordfish/tilefish/king mackerel
  For most recreational freshwater fish in contaminated areas:
  State-specific consumption advisories (typically 1–2 servings/month)

  Hg METHYLATION ACCELERATED BY:
  Acid deposition (increases Hg solubility)
  Anoxic conditions (eutrophication worsens this)
  Climate warming (increases bacterial methylation rates)
  Drought cycles (alternating wet/dry = episodic anoxia events)
```

## Section 5 — Food Web Indices and Stability

```
  FOOD WEB METRICS:

  CONNECTANCE (C): fraction of possible links that are realized
  C = L / S²  where L = number of links; S = number of species
  Typical lake C: 0.1–0.2

  LINKAGE DENSITY: L/S (links per species)
  More links per species = more omnivory = potentially more stability

  TROPHIC LEVEL (TL):
  TL = 1 + (average TL of prey)
  Primary producers: TL = 1
  Pure herbivores: TL = 2
  Omnivores: intermediate (e.g., large Daphnia TL ≈ 2.1 from algae + bacteria)
  Piscivore: TL ≈ 4–4.5
  Isotopic method: 15N enriches ~3.4‰ per trophic level
  --> Measure 15N in tissue --> calculate effective TL

  ENERGY EFFICIENCY BETWEEN LEVELS:
  ~10% ecological efficiency (10% of energy transferred per trophic step)
  Supports "trophic pyramid" -- less biomass at each higher level
  Exception: suspension feeders on very small phytoplankton may exceed 10%
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What did Carpenter's lake experiments prove? | Top-down trophic cascade exists in lakes; adding/removing piscivores changes water clarity without changing nutrient load |
| Why does biomanipulation (adding bass) clear lake water? | Bass --> planktivore reduction --> Daphnia release --> phytoplankton grazed down |
| Why does biomanipulation eventually fail without P control? | Nutrient loading continues; at high P, even large Daphnia cannot control algae; cascade overwhelmed bottom-up |
| What is the difference between bioaccumulation and biomagnification? | Bioaccumulation = organism > water concentration (per species); biomagnification = increases with trophic level |
| Why is methylmercury worse than inorganic mercury? | Much more lipophilic; easily absorbed by organisms; extremely high bioconcentration factor |
| What indicator organisms have the highest PCB/mercury? | Long-lived piscivores: pike, walleye, muskie, lake trout; plus fish-eating birds (osprey, eagle) |
| What is ecological efficiency? | ~10% of energy transferred per trophic level; limits biomass at higher trophic levels |

## Common Confusion Points

**Trophic cascades are not universal in strength.**
The Carpenter experiments were in nutrient-poor lakes where Daphnia was the primary grazer. In nutrient-rich turbid lakes, or in marine systems, cascades are often weak because multiple species compensate for each other. The cascade strength depends on food web complexity and lake trophic state.

**Bioaccumulation factors apply to specific compounds and tissues.**
Not all environmental contaminants bioaccumulate. Ionically charged compounds (inorganic metals, most pesticides with low log Kow) do not partition into fat and are not biomagnified. The key property is octanol-water partition coefficient (log Kow > 4 = biomagnification risk).

**Mercury advisories apply to fish tissue, not water.**
Water column total Hg concentrations that trigger ecological concern are orders of magnitude lower than drinking water limits. The risk is from eating fish, not drinking the water. Risk assessment must track through the food web, not just the water column.

**Removing Daphnia predators does not always restore the phytoplankton-Daphnia link.**
In hypereutrophic lakes, phytoplankton biomass exceeds Daphnia grazing capacity even at maximum Daphnia density. The grazer becomes food-limited (can't graze all algae) and nutrient-limited (algal quality is poor). Biomanipulation works best when TP < ~50–80 µg/L.
