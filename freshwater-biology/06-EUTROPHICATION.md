# Eutrophication: Causes, Algal Blooms, Hypoxia, Remediation

## The Big Picture

Eutrophication is the enrichment of a water body with nutrients (primarily phosphorus in freshwater), leading to increased primary production, algal blooms, oxygen depletion, and ecosystem degradation. It is the most widespread water quality problem globally and affects 30–40% of large lakes worldwide.

```
EUTROPHICATION CASCADE

  NUTRIENT LOADING (external P + N from catchment)
          |
          v
  INCREASED ALGAL BIOMASS (phytoplankton + periphyton)
          |
          +--------> SHADING: submergent macrophytes decline/disappear
          |
          v
  ALGAL BLOOM SUCCESSION: green algae --> cyanobacteria (late summer)
          |
          v
  ALGAL SENESCENCE + SEDIMENTATION: dead biomass sinks
          |
          v
  BACTERIAL DECOMPOSITION IN HYPOLIMNION: O2 consumed
          |
          v
  HYPOXIA/ANOXIA IN HYPOLIMNION: DO < 2 mg/L or = 0
          |
          +--------> INTERNAL P LOADING: Fe-P bond breaks; P recycled to surface
          |
          +--------> H2S, CH4, NH4+ accumulate: toxic conditions
          |
          v
  FISH KILLS (during overturn or in stratified summer): O2 crash at surface
          |
          v
  ECOSYSTEM REGIME SHIFT: clear water --> turbid stable state
```

## Section 2 — Algal Bloom Succession

The seasonal succession of phytoplankton in a eutrophic lake follows a predictable pattern:

```
  EUTROPHIC LAKE ANNUAL SUCCESSION:

  WINTER/EARLY SPRING (ice-off):
  Diatoms dominate (Asterionella, Stephanodiscus, Aulacoseira)
  Cold, nutrient-rich water from turnover; Si available
  Bloom terminates when Si depleted or temperature rises

  SPRING:
  Diatoms --> green algae transition (Scenedesmus, Chlamydomonas, Chlorella)
  Longer days; warming; nutrients still available
  Clear water phase may occur if large Daphnia control phytoplankton

  SUMMER (eutrophic lakes):
  "CLEAR WATER PHASE" (May–June): Daphnia grazing suppresses algae
  --> Then large Daphnia decline (planktivorous fish predation, food depletion)
  --> Algal release from grazing pressure

  LATE SUMMER:
  CYANOBACTERIA dominate (Microcystis, Aphanizomenon, Planktothrix)
  Why cyanobacteria win in late summer:
    1. N:P ratio falls (denitrification, N-uptake) --> N-fixed by cyanos
    2. Gas vacuoles: buoyancy regulation --> surface scum formation
    3. Slow sink rate: stratified water favors floaters
    4. Large cell/colony size: reduces Daphnia grazing efficiency
    5. Toxin production: inhibits zooplankton grazing

  AUTUMN:
  Cooling, wind mixing; cyanobacteria decline
  Diatoms may have brief autumn bloom with Si-replenished mixing
```

### Cyanobacteria Ecological Competitive Mechanisms

```
  GAS VACUOLES (buoyancy):
  Protein-bound cylinders of gas; collapse under pressure
  When light-saturated near surface: increase ballast; sink slightly
  When light-limited at depth: synthesize vacuoles; rise
  --> Ability to position vertically in water column is unique advantage

  N2 FIXATION:
  Anabaena, Aphanizomenon have heterocysts (specialized N-fixation cells)
  Require: low inorganic N, high light, warm temperature
  Cost: significant carbon energy; Fe requirement
  Advantage: independent N supply when others are N-limited

  COLONY SIZE AND TOXINS:
  Microcystis forms large mucilaginous colonies
  Too large for most Daphnia to ingest efficiently
  Microcystins interfere with protein phosphatase (toxic to liver)
  Paradox: toxin benefit to bloom persistence is debated
  --> Toxin may be a byproduct of physiological regulation, not a defense
```

## Section 3 — Hypoxia and Anoxia

```
  DISSOLVED O2 THRESHOLDS FOR AQUATIC LIFE:

  > 8 mg/L:    Optimal for salmonids
  6–8 mg/L:    Acceptable for most coldwater fish
  4–6 mg/L:    Sublethal stress; behavioral avoidance; reduced growth
  2–4 mg/L:    Hypoxia; significant behavioral impacts; fish migration away
  1–2 mg/L:    Acute hypoxia; mortality for sensitive fish
  < 1 mg/L:    Lethal for most fish; only very tolerant invertebrates
  0 mg/L:      Anoxia; only anaerobic bacteria survive

  HYPOXIC EVENTS:
  Summer hypolimnetic anoxia: develops gradually in stratified eutrophic lakes
  Dissolved in fish: "oxygen squeeze" between warm epilimnion and anoxic hypolimnion
  Nocturnal hypoxia: dense macrophyte beds consume O2 at night (respiration)
  Winter kill: thick ice + snow blocks photosynthesis; decomposition consumes O2

  AMMONIA TOXICITY:
  Anoxic hypolimnion accumulates NH4+
  At fall overturn: NH4+ mixed to surface
  Un-ionized NH3 (determined by pH and temperature) is toxic to fish
  NH3 toxicity threshold: ~0.02 mg/L for sensitive species
  NH3/NH4+ ratio increases with pH and temperature:
  At pH 7: 0.6% is NH3 | At pH 8: 5.6% | At pH 9: 35%
```

## Section 4 — Sediment Phosphorus Release

Internal loading is the re-release of previously deposited P from sediments — often the dominant P source in long-degraded lakes.

```
  MECHANISMS OF P RELEASE FROM SEDIMENT:

  1. REDUCTIVE DISSOLUTION (most important):
     Fe(OH)3-P --> Fe²⁺ + P (anoxic conditions)
     Eh < +200 mV triggers; most active at sediment-water interface

  2. pH-INDUCED RELEASE:
     High algal photosynthesis raises pH above 9
     Ca-P minerals dissolve at high pH
     Or: organic matter decomposition lowers pH, dissolving Ca-P

  3. BIOTURBATION:
     Benthic invertebrates (tubificid worms, chironomid larvae) rework sediment
     Bring P-rich anoxic sediment to oxic surface
     Can release 0.5–2 mg P/m²/day from active benthic

  4. MINERALIZATION:
     Decomposition of buried organic matter releases bound P
     Temperature-dependent: faster in warm hypolimnion summers

  QUANTITATIVE IMPORTANCE:
  External load of typical eutrophic lake: 0.2–0.5 g P/m²/yr
  Internal load: 0.5–3.0 g P/m²/yr (can far exceed external)
  --> Even if external loading is eliminated, lake may remain eutrophic
      for 10–20+ years due to internal loading
```

## Section 5 — Ecological Regime Shifts

Perhaps the most important concept in lake management: lakes can exist in two alternative stable states with self-reinforcing feedbacks, and transitions between them are not easily reversible.

```
  ALTERNATIVE STABLE STATES MODEL (Scheffer et al. 1993):

  CLEAR WATER STATE:               TURBID WATER STATE:
  +-- Macrophytes present          +-- Macrophytes absent
  +-- Low phytoplankton            +-- High phytoplankton (turbid)
  +-- Large Daphnia abundant       +-- Small zooplankton dominate
  +-- Water clear                  +-- Water turbid/green/brown

  SELF-REINFORCING FEEDBACKS:

  Clear water:                     Turbid water:
  Macrophytes stabilize sediment   No macrophytes; sediment disturbed
  --> less P release                --> more P release from benthic
  Macrophytes absorb P             No plant P uptake; P in water column
  Macrophytes shelter Daphnia      Fish can see Daphnia; predation high
  from fish predation              Daphnia reduced; algae not grazed
  Daphnia graze algae              Algae increase turbidity

  TRANSITION THRESHOLDS:
  Below ~P* (lower threshold): only clear state possible
  Between P* and P**: both states possible (hysteresis)
  Above P**: only turbid state possible

       |P**|
  TURBID|   |---clear state possible but---
  ONLY  |   |   unstable; sharp shift to turbid
        |   |   on any perturbation
        |   |
  BOTH  |   |<-- HYSTERESIS REGION -->
  possible   (history-dependent)
        |   |
  CLEAR |   |---turbid state possible but---
  ONLY  |   |   unstable; lake recovers to clear
        | P*|
```

## Section 6 — Restoration Methods

```
  RESTORATION DECISION TREE:

  Is external P loading still elevated?
  YES --> External source control FIRST (mandatory; all else fails without this)
         Wastewater treatment upgrades; riparian buffers; agricultural BMPs
  NO (already controlled) --> Assess internal loading

  Is internal P loading significant?
  YES --> Choose internal restoration method:

    ALUM TREATMENT:
    Al2(SO4)3 applied to lake surface or sediment
    Forms Al(OH)3 floc that binds P and sinks
    Al-P binding is NOT redox-sensitive (unlike Fe-P)
    Al-P stable under anoxic conditions: blocks internal loading
    Duration: 10–30 yr depending on continued external load
    Risk: pH depression if over-applied; toxic to fish at low pH

    HYPOLIMNETIC AERATION/OXYGENATION:
    Inject O2 or air into hypolimnion
    Maintains oxic conditions: keeps Fe-P bound
    Does NOT mix warm and cold water (uses targeted aerators)
    Preserves thermal stratification while preventing anoxia
    Cost: $50,000–500,000+ for large installations; ongoing operating

    SEDIMENT REMOVAL (dredging):
    Physically remove P-rich sediment
    Most effective long-term; eliminates internal load at source
    Cost: very high ($50–500 per m³ dredged); disposal of sediment
    Best for small, shallow lakes with thick nutrient-rich sediment

    HYPOLIMNETIC WITHDRAWAL:
    Drain P-rich hypolimnion during stratification
    Removes accumulated P from lake
    Effective if outflow can be managed; changes water balance

    PHOSPHORUS INACTIVATION WITH MODIFIED ZEOLITE OR LANTHANUM (Phoslock):
    Lanthanum-modified bentonite; binds P irreversibly as lanthanum phosphate
    Applied to sediment surface; newer technology (1990s patent)
    Duration similar to alum; no pH risk

  BIOMANIPULATION:
  Remove planktivorous fish (bream, carp, roach) that suppress Daphnia
  Add piscivores to control planktivores
  Reestablish large-bodied Daphnia population
  Daphnia grazes algae; clears water
  Enables macrophyte establishment
  SUCCESS REQUIRES: P already below switching threshold; sufficient P:depth ratio
```

## Section 7 — Global Eutrophication Status

```
  GLOBAL SCALE:
  ~30–40% of large lakes worldwide are eutrophic or hypereutrophic (UNEP)
  Cost in USA: ~$2.2 billion/yr (drinking water treatment, recreation loss,
               property values, fisheries) -- from Smith et al. estimates

  LARGEST EUTROPHICATION PROBLEMS:
  Lake Erie: improved 1970s–1990s after P limits; re-eutrophying 2000s+
             due to nonpoint (agricultural) P; western basin summer blooms
  Lake Taihu (China): ~2,300 km²; chronic cyanobacteria blooms; 2007 bloom
                      cut off drinking water supply for Wuxi (2M people)
  Baltic Sea: large-scale marine eutrophication from N + P from agriculture
  Gulf of Mexico "dead zone": ~20,000 km²; driven by Mississippi basin N + P

  CLIMATE CHANGE EXACERBATION:
  Warmer temperatures: longer stratification seasons --> more anoxia
  More intense storms: P pulses from erosion events
  Higher CO2: may shift phytoplankton toward cyanobacteria (some evidence)
  Drought: less dilution; concentrated nutrients
  Net effect: eutrophication problems likely to worsen with climate change
              even at constant nutrient loading
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What triggers cyanobacteria dominance? | N:P < 10:1 (mass); stratified warm conditions; large colony size escapes grazing |
| Why does reducing external P loading not immediately restore a lake? | Internal P loading from sediment can exceed external load for 10–20 yr |
| What is alum treatment and when to use it? | Al2(SO4)3 binds P; use when internal loading is high; Al-P is redox-stable unlike Fe-P |
| What is biomanipulation? | Remove planktivorous fish; enhance Daphnia; Daphnia grazes algae clear |
| Why can two lakes with same P load be clear and turbid? | Alternative stable states with hysteresis; history-dependent; both states stable at intermediate P |
| What P concentration threshold separates mesotrophic/eutrophic? | ~35 µg TP/L is a common threshold (Vollenweider reference) |
| What drives summer cyanobacteria blooms in most lakes? | Stratification + warm temperature + P availability + low N:P after growing season N depletion |

## Common Confusion Points

**Removing algaecide does not solve eutrophication.**
Treating with copper sulfate or other algaecides kills the bloom but does not address the nutrient loading. The bloom returns immediately because the cause (P availability) is unchanged. Chemical treatment is symptom management, not restoration.

**Clear water after biomanipulation does not mean the lake is fixed.**
Biomanipulation can produce spectacular short-term (2–5 yr) water clarity improvements. But without concurrent P loading reduction, the food web shifts back as planktivorous fish recover and nutrient loading continues.

**Eutrophication is not just a warm-climate or summer problem.**
Winter ice cover can trap nutrients under ice; spring turnover can deliver a concentrated P pulse. Cold oligotrophic lakes can be eutrophied by modest P additions. The process is driven by nutrient loading, not primarily by temperature.

**"Dead zone" hypoxia in coastal marine systems is driven by N, not P.**
In contrast to freshwater, coastal marine hypoxia (Gulf of Mexico, Baltic, Black Sea) is typically N-limited because seawater has abundant P from deep-water upwelling and marine sediment recycling. Managing agricultural N exports is the priority for marine coastal dead zones; P control is the priority for freshwater lakes.
