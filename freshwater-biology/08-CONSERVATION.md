# Freshwater Conservation: Invasive Species, Dam Impacts, Environmental Flows

## The Big Picture

Freshwater ecosystems support disproportionate biodiversity relative to their area but face the sharpest biodiversity declines of any ecosystem type. The three dominant threats are biological invasion, physical alteration by dams, and altered flow regimes — all of which interact with eutrophication and climate change.

## Section 1 — Invasive Species

### Zebra and Quagga Mussels (Dreissena polymorpha / D. bugensis)

The most ecologically significant freshwater invasives in North America and Europe.

```
  ZEBRA MUSSEL INVASION CHRONOLOGY:
  Origin: Caspian-Black Sea basin; native range
  Europe: spread throughout Danube, Rhine, Thames by 1980s via ship ballast water
  North America: Lake St. Clair 1988 (ballast water from European ship)
  Great Lakes: fully invaded by early 1990s
  Inland lakes: spread by recreational boats; hull fouling, bilge water

  ECOLOGICAL IMPACTS:
  FILTER FEEDING:
  Clearance rate: 1 adult = ~1 L/day at 16°C
  Density: 10,000–100,000/m² on hard substrate possible
  Lake Erie example: phytoplankton biomass decreased 80–90% after invasion
  Zooplankton biomass decreased as algal food source crashed
  Native mussel larvae (glochidia) killed by mussel coating over host fish

  PHYTOPLANKTON SELECTIVITY:
  Dreissena preferentially rejects or fails to ingest cyanobacteria
  --> Selective removal of edible algae; cyanobacteria proportion increases
  Paradox: clearing the water while potentially worsening cyanobacteria blooms

  BENTHIC-PELAGIC COUPLING:
  Zebra mussels move phytoplankton biomass from water column to sediment
  (as pseudo-feces = undigested material deposited on substrate)
  --> Nutrient enrichment of sediment; increased internal loading over time
  --> Changed lake from pelagic-dominated to benthic-dominated

  ENERGY DIVERSION:
  Before invasion: phytoplankton --> zooplankton --> planktivorous fish --> piscivores
  After invasion: phytoplankton --> zebra mussels (new trophic branch)
  Biomass diverted to Dreissena, which is not a food source for native fish
  (native fish not adapted to consume mussels efficiently)
```

### Asian Carp Complex

Multiple Cyprinid species introduced to southeastern US aquaculture in 1970s; escaped during floods.

```
  FOUR SPECIES:
  Silver carp (Hypophthalmichthys molitrix): filter feeder; jumping behavior
  Bighead carp (H. nobilis): filter feeder; very large (up to 45 kg)
  Grass carp (Ctenopharyngodon idella): macrophyte grazer
  Black carp (Mylopharyngodon piceus): molluscivore; threatens native mussels

  CURRENT RANGE:
  Established throughout Mississippi River basin
  Approaching Great Lakes via Chicago Waterway system (Lake Michigan connection)
  Electric fish barriers in Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal since 2002

  ECOLOGICAL THREAT:
  Silver + bighead carp: compete directly with native filter-feeding fish (paddlefish, bigmouth buffalo)
  and zooplankton at base of food web
  Can comprise 70–90% of fish biomass in invaded river reaches
  Silver carp: jumping response to boat motor vibration creates safety hazard
```

### Nile Perch (Lates niloticus) in Lake Victoria

The definitive case study of introduced predator causing mass extinction:

```
  INTRODUCTION HISTORY:
  ~1950s: Introduced to Lake Haplochromis Estates (Uganda side) by British colonizers
          for food/sport fishing; exact date disputed
  1960s–1970s: Population expansion; spread through lake
  1980s: Fishery boom (now largest freshwater fishery by mass in the world)

  ECOLOGICAL IMPACT:
  Before Nile perch expansion: ~500 haplochromine cichlid species; high diversity
  After expansion: ~200+ cichlid species extinct or functionally extinct
  Mechanism: Nile perch directly predates cichlids; most cichlids evolved without
             such large predators (shallow lake; cichlids naive prey)

  ADDITIONAL FACTORS:
  Eutrophication from watershed clearing (Nile perch + eutrophication = combined)
  Overfishing of native species (pre-dating Nile perch expansion)
  Hypoxic zones expanded; cichlid habitat lost

  PARADOX: Enormous fishery value (~500,000 tonnes/yr) vs. biodiversity catastrophe
  Nile perch export (fillets to Europe) became major Ugandan/Kenyan export industry
  Film: "Darwin's Nightmare" (2004) documented social/ecological consequences
```

### Signal Crayfish (Pacifastacus leniusculus) in European Streams

```
  ORIGIN: Pacific Northwest USA
  INTRODUCTION: 1960s as aquaculture species; escaped widely in Europe
  CARRIER STATUS: carrier of crayfish plague (Aphanomyces astaci) --
                  devastating to native European crayfish (white-clawed, stone, noble)
  EUROPEAN NATIVE CRAYFISH: Extremely sensitive; 100% mortality in naive populations

  ECOLOGICAL EFFECTS:
  +-- Eliminates native crayfish
  +-- Bioturbation: burrows destabilize stream banks
  +-- Heavy grazing: removes macrophytes; destabilizes habitat
  +-- Large food source for otter (beneficial) but disrupts rest of community

  MANAGEMENT: Virtual impossibility; cannot be eradicated from established waterways
              Barrier installation can protect isolated headwater populations
```

## Section 2 — Dam Impacts

Large dams (> 15 m height) fundamentally alter river hydrology, thermal regime, sediment regime, and connectivity.

### Thermal Modification

```
  HYPOLIMNETIC RELEASES FROM DEEP RESERVOIRS:

  NATURAL RIVER TEMPERATURE:     BELOW LARGE RESERVOIR:
  Summer: warm                   Summer: abnormally cold
  (ambient + solar heating)      (cold hypolimnetic water released)
  Spring: cold                   Spring: warmer than natural
  (snowmelt)                     (no cold snowmelt pulse)
  Seasonal range: 5–25°C         Seasonal range: 8–14°C
  (highly variable)              (reduced variation)

  ECOLOGICAL EFFECTS:
  +-- Cold tailwaters support trout below warm-water reservoirs (planned)
  +-- But: eliminates native warm-water species adapted to seasonal regime
  +-- Phenological disruption: fish spawning cued by temperature + photoperiod
  +-- Spring flow = cold + photoperiod = spawn cue; dam removes the cold pulse
  +-- Invertebrate hatch timing decoupled from fish feeding cycles
```

### Sediment Starvation

```
  SEDIMENT TRAPPING:
  Dams trap bedload (gravel, sand) and suspended load (silt, clay)
  Trap efficiency: > 95% for large reservoirs with long residence time

  DOWNSTREAM CONSEQUENCES:
  ARMORING: river bed coarsens (fine sediment not replaced by new supply)
            Armored gravel cannot be moved by flows that previously transported it
            --> Reduced channel migration
            --> Reduced creation of new gravel bars (spawning habitat)

  DELTA EROSION: River deltas are maintained by sediment supply
  Nile Delta: Aswan High Dam (1970) --> 98% reduction in sediment to delta
  --> Delta eroding 8 m/yr at some points; saltwater intrusion
  Yellow River: similar delta erosion after dam construction
  Mississippi: wetland loss partly from reduced sediment supply

  BELOW-DAM CHANNEL INCISION:
  Sediment-starved flows erode the bed
  Channel incises --> bank instability
  Groundwater table drops (now below incised channel)
  --> Riparian vegetation stress; loss of floodplain connection
```

### Migration Barriers

```
  FISH PASSAGE PROBLEM:
  Anadromous fish (salmon, shad, lamprey, striped bass) require upstream migration
  to spawn; blocked by dams

  FISH PASSAGE STRUCTURES:
  Fish ladder (pool-and-weir): series of stepped pools; oldest design
  Effectiveness: works for salmon at < 30 m; fails at large dams (> 50 m)

  Fish lift / elevator: mechanical trap + transport
  Bypass channels: re-route fish around dam
  Rock ramp: constructed riffle with gradual slope
  Effectiveness varies dramatically by species, dam height, flow regime

  COLUMBIA/SNAKE RIVER EXAMPLE (Pacific NW, USA):
  8 major dams; Lower Snake River dams particularly damaging
  Snake River salmon runs: historical ~1 million fish/yr --> current < 5% of that
  Lower Snake River dam removal debate: ongoing as of 2024
  4 lower Snake dams produce ~1,100 MW but save ~2 Myr of salmon migration
```

## Section 3 — Environmental Flow Requirements

Environmental flows (e-flows) are the quantity, timing, and quality of water flows required to sustain freshwater ecosystems.

```
  ENVIRONMENTAL FLOW COMPONENTS:

  FLOW COMPONENT          ECOLOGICAL FUNCTION
  +------------------+   +------------------------------------------+
  | Low flows        |   | Maintain pool refugia; concentrate       |
  | (base flow)      |   | organisms for feeding; fish over-winter  |
  +------------------+   +------------------------------------------+
  | High flow pulses |   | Flush fine sediment from gravels;        |
  | (small floods)   |   | access riparian feeding areas;           |
  |                  |   | cue fish migration                       |
  +------------------+   +------------------------------------------+
  | Large floods     |   | Floodplain inundation; lateral           |
  | (bankfull+)      |   | connectivity; floodplain spawning;       |
  |                  |   | riparian vegetation dynamics             |
  +------------------+   +------------------------------------------+
  | Seasonal timing  |   | Cue spawning, migration, hatching;       |
  | of flow          |   | phenological synchrony                   |
  +------------------+   +------------------------------------------+

  TENNANT METHOD (simplest e-flow assessment):
  % of Mean Annual Flow     Ecological Status
  10% MAF                   Minimum (severe degradation likely)
  30% MAF                   Good
  60–100% MAF               Excellent / flushing
```

### Dam Removal: The Evidence Base

```
  DAM REMOVAL OUTCOMES (meta-analysis, Bellmore et al. 2017):

  PHYSICAL RESPONSE (years 1–5):
  +-- Sediment flushing downstream (turbidity pulse)
  +-- Channel morphology change (re-establishment of riffle-pool sequence)
  +-- Substrate coarsening upstream (reservoir deposits exposed)

  BIOLOGICAL RESPONSE:
  Macroinvertebrates: EPT recovery within 2–5 yr after small dam removal
  Fish passage: immediate in most documented cases
  Salmonid recolonization: within 1–5 yr of passage restoration
  Riparian vegetation: recovery 5–20 yr
  Salmon redds (gravel nests): documented within 1–2 yr post-removal

  NOTABLE DAM REMOVALS:
  Elwha River (Washington State): 2 dams removed 2011–2014
  --> 8,000 m above-dam river opened
  --> Chinook salmon detected above former dam site within 1 yr
  --> 30 billion pieces of sediment transported downstream

  White Salmon River (WA): Condit Dam removed 2012
  --> Coho salmon detected upstream within 1 month

  Penobscot River (Maine): Edwards Dam removed 1999
  --> 9 species of anadromous fish re-established
  --> Some species recovery of 1,700+ miles of upstream habitat
```

## Section 4 — IUCN Freshwater Biodiversity Assessment

```
  FRESHWATER BIODIVERSITY STATUS:

  FISH: ~7,000+ freshwater fish species assessed by IUCN (2024)
  ~30% threatened (VU, EN, CR) -- compared to ~14% marine fish
  Highest threat in: SE Asia, N. Africa, Appalachian basin

  AMPHIBIANS: ~4,000+ species (mostly wetland-dependent)
  ~40% threatened overall; freshwater-dependent species higher
  Primary threats: habitat loss, chytrid disease, climate change

  FRESHWATER MOLLUSKS: most threatened major taxonomic group
  North American freshwater mussels (Unionidae): 70%+ threatened
  Primary threats: dam construction, impoundment, sedimentation

  FRESHWATER PLANTS: under-assessed; major declines documented
  Aquatic macrophytes: 50%+ reduction in many rivers since 1950s
  Causes: eutrophication, herbicide runoff, invasive species, boat traffic

  LIVING PLANET INDEX (freshwater vertebrates 1970–2016):
  Average population decline: -83%
  --> Compared with -38% for terrestrial vertebrates
  --> Fastest rate of decline of any ecosystem type tracked
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| How did zebra mussels change Lake Erie? | Filter-fed phytoplankton, reduced it 80–90%; shifted system from pelagic to benthic; increased water clarity but altered food web |
| What stopped Nile perch from being entirely negative? | Massive fishery value; but 200+ endemic cichlid species lost |
| What does a large dam do to water temperature downstream? | Cold hypolimnetic releases in summer; eliminates warm-season temperature cues for native species |
| Where does the delta sediment go after Aswan Dam? | Trapped in reservoir (98%); Nile Delta erodes without replacement supply |
| What is the minimum e-flow for basic ecological function? | ~30% of mean annual flow (Tennant method); 10% MAF = severe degradation floor |
| Does dam removal work for fish recovery? | Yes — documented within 1–5 yr for most anadromous species in multiple case studies |
| What threatens freshwater mussels more than any other group? | Dam construction and reservoir impoundment (need clear, fast-flowing water + specific fish hosts) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Water clarity improvement from zebra mussels is not ecosystem recovery.**
Zebra mussels do increase Secchi depth dramatically. This has been misinterpreted as lake improvement. It is not — it is a fundamental food web restructuring that harms native fish, zooplankton, and native mussels while diverting energy to an invasive species.

**Fish ladders do not solve the migration problem at large dams.**
Pool-and-weir fish ladders are effective for salmon at dams < 30 m high. At large dams (Bonneville is 60 m; Grand Coulee is 168 m), ladders are not practical or effective. Many documented cases where fish ladders exist but passage rates are 1–5% of historical runs.

**Dam removal is not universally safe or easy.**
Sediment trapped behind dam may contain contaminants (PCBs, heavy metals) that are released during removal and removal operations. Reservoir drawdown can cause massive turbidity pulses that temporarily damage downstream habitat. Pre-removal sediment assessment is mandatory.

**Environmental flows are not a single number.**
The "minimum" e-flow number (e.g., 30% MAF) is a coarse approximation. Real e-flow assessments require specification of flow magnitude, duration, frequency, timing (seasonal), rate of change, and quality — all components necessary for different ecological processes.
