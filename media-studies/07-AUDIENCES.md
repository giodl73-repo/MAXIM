# Audiences, Reception, and Participatory Culture

## The Big Picture

Audience studies examines what people actually do with media -- not what it does to them. The field moved from early "hypodermic needle" effects models (media injects ideology directly) to more sophisticated accounts of active audiences who negotiate, resist, and appropriate media texts. The digital era introduced participatory culture -- audiences who produce, remix, and distribute content themselves.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|               AUDIENCE STUDIES LANDSCAPE                         |

## Early Effects Research

### The Hypodermic Needle / Magic Bullet

```
  HYPODERMIC NEEDLE MODEL (1930s-1940s)
  ========================================

  Assumption:
    Mass media directly injects messages into audience.
    Uniform effect on all audience members.
    Passive recipients; no mediation.

  Historical context:
    World War I propaganda seemed extremely effective.
    Nazis used radio/film effectively.
    "Mass" society theory: isolated individuals
    susceptible to manipulation without traditional
    community ties.

  Evidence at the time:
    Orson Welles "War of the Worlds" (1938):
    Radio drama of Martian invasion caused panic.
    (Later research: panic was overstated; only some
    listeners who tuned in late panicked; most
    understood it was fiction)

  Status: Now largely discredited as a model.
  Audiences are not passive. Effects are mediated.
  But: The model was intuitive and historically influential.
```

### Lazarsfeld and Limited Effects

```
  THE PEOPLE'S CHOICE (Lazarsfeld, Berelson, Gaudet, 1944)
  ===========================================================

  Study: 1940 US presidential election.
  Method: Panel study of Erie County, Ohio voters.
  Expected to find: Mass media changed votes.

  ACTUAL FINDING: Media had limited effects on votes.
    Most people voted same party as always.
    Undecided voters were influenced by interpersonal
    contact more than media.
    People sought confirming information,
    avoided dissonant information.

  TWO-STEP FLOW:
  +-----------+         +-----------+         +----------+
  | MASS      |-------> | OPINION   |-------> | GENERAL  |
  | MEDIA     |         | LEADERS   |         | PUBLIC   |
  +-----------+         |(engaged   |         |          |
               direct   | early     |  inter- |          |
               effect   | adopters) | personal|          |
                        +-----------+  flow   +----------+

  Opinion leaders filter, interpret, and retransmit
  media messages to their social networks.
  Interpersonal influence is more powerful than direct
  media exposure.

  Legacy:
    Killed the hypodermic needle model.
    Established that media effects are mediated by
    social context, prior attitudes, selective exposure.
    "Limited effects" tradition dominated 1950s-1960s.
```

## Agenda-Setting

```
  AGENDA-SETTING THEORY (McCombs & Shaw, 1972)
  ================================================

  Research question: Do media change opinions?
  (Limited effects: often no)

  Reframe: Do media determine what people THINK ABOUT?
  (Agenda-setting: largely yes)

  "The press may not be successful much of the time
   in telling people what to think, but it is stunningly
   successful in telling its readers what to think about."
                                        -- Bernard Cohen (1963)

  EVIDENCE:
    1968 Chapel Hill study: Issues ranked most important
    by voters correlated strongly with issues covered
    most in local media.

  AGENDA-SETTING LEVELS:
    Level 1 (object agenda): What issues get coverage?
    Level 2 (attribute agenda): How are issues framed?
    Which aspects of an issue are emphasized?

  Level 2 = FRAMING:
    Drug problem framed as:
    - Public health crisis: treatment, prevention
    - Law enforcement: arrests, prosecution
    - Social problem: poverty, inequality
    Same issue; radically different policy implications.
```

## Cultivation Theory (Gerbner)

```
  CULTIVATION THEORY (Gerbner & Gross, 1976)
  =============================================

  Hypothesis: Heavy TV viewing cultivates a world view
              that matches TV's portrayal of reality.

  TV content analysis (Message System Analysis):
    TV dramatically over-represents:
    - Crime and violence (TV world is much more violent)
    - Law enforcement and medical professionals
    - Dramatic, exciting events

    TV dramatically under-represents:
    - Elderly people
    - Minority characters (improving)
    - Manual labor

  CULTIVATION EFFECT:
    Heavy viewers perceive the world to match TV's world.
    "Mean World Syndrome": heavy viewers perceive
    the world as more dangerous than light viewers
    or the statistical reality.

  Findings:
    Heavy TV viewers overestimate:
    - Likelihood of being victim of crime
    - Number of police and doctors in population
    - Danger in walking alone at night

  CRITIQUE:
    Causation problem: fearful people may seek out
    more crime content, not just cultivate fear from watching.
    Third-variable problem: other correlates of heavy TV viewing.
    Effect sizes are small (mainstreaming + resonance debates).
```

## Reception Theory

### Stuart Hall: Encoding/Decoding

```
  ENCODING/DECODING (Hall, 1973, 1980)
  =======================================

  PRODUCTION -------> TEXT --------> RECEPTION
  (encoding)                         (decoding)

  The encoding and decoding are not equivalent operations.
  Producers encode meaning using dominant cultural codes.
  Receivers decode using their own subject positions.
  The "same" text is decoded differently by different audiences.

  THREE DECODING POSITIONS:

  1. DOMINANT-HEGEMONIC READING:
     Receiver operates within the dominant code.
     Accepts the preferred meaning.
     Example: News story on crime as individual failing;
     viewer accepts that poverty ≠ crime causation.

  2. NEGOTIATED READING:
     Receiver accepts the dominant code but makes
     exceptions for their situation.
     "In general yes, but for people like me..."
     Working-class viewer accepts economic news frame
     but applies it differently to own circumstances.

  3. OPPOSITIONAL READING:
     Receiver understands but rejects the preferred code.
     Decodes the text through a counter-code.
     "This is propaganda for the ruling class."
     Trade unionist watching news story on industrial action:
     decodes "troublemakers" language as class bias.

  Significance: Audiences are not duped.
  But: Oppositional reading still happens within
  structures that reinforce dominant codes overall.
```

### Janice Radway: Reading the Romance

```
  READING THE ROMANCE (Radway, 1984)
  =====================================

  Research question: Why do women read romance novels?
  (Academic feminist consensus: romance is oppressive,
  reinforces patriarchy)

  Method: Ethnographic interviews with Smithton women
  (a specific community of romance readers)

  FINDING: Women's readings are active and resistant.
    Women reported that romance reading gave them:
    - Private space and time away from family demands
    - Emotional experience unavailable in daily life
    - Fantasy of emotionally available, responsive men
    - Vicarious experience of autonomy and adventure

  Radway's conclusion (ambivalent):
    The act of reading = small act of resistance
    (claiming personal time from domestic duties).
    The content = reinforces patriarchal romance.
    BOTH are true simultaneously.

  Method contribution: Go to actual audiences.
  Don't assume from text analysis what audiences "get."
```

## Uses and Gratifications

```
  USES AND GRATIFICATIONS THEORY (Katz, Blumler, Gurevitch, 1974)
  ==================================================================

  SHIFT: From "what does media do to people?"
          To "what do people do with media?"

  Audience is ACTIVE: selects media to satisfy needs.

  CATEGORIES OF GRATIFICATION:
  +-----------------+------------------------------------------+
  | Cognitive       | Information, knowledge, understanding    |
  +-----------------+------------------------------------------+
  | Affective       | Emotional experience, aesthetic pleasure |
  +-----------------+------------------------------------------+
  | Personal        | Reinforcing self-concept, status         |
  | integrative     | (reading confirms my identity)           |
  +-----------------+------------------------------------------+
  | Social          | Connection with others, conversation     |
  | integrative     | material, community membership           |
  +-----------------+------------------------------------------+
  | Tension release | Relaxation, escape                       |
  +-----------------+------------------------------------------+

  CRITIQUE:
    Overly individualistic: ignores structural constraints
    on media choices (what is available? what is affordable?)
    Assumes rational, autonomous choosers.
    Ignores that "needs" are themselves socially constructed
    (needs for entertainment were created by entertainment industries).

## Participatory Culture (Jenkins)

Henry Jenkins's participatory culture framework emerged from fan studies and transformed with the internet:

```
  PARTICIPATORY CULTURE CHARACTERISTICS (Jenkins)
  =================================================

  1. LOW BARRIERS to creative expression and civic engagement
  2. STRONG SUPPORT for sharing creations with others
  3. INFORMAL MENTORSHIP: experienced members pass on
     knowledge to newcomers
  4. BELIEF that members' contributions MATTER
  5. SOCIAL CONNECTIONS: members care about what
     others think of their creations

  CONTRAST WITH TRADITIONAL MEDIA:
  Broadcast: One-to-many. Few producers, many consumers.
  Participatory: Many-to-many. Everyone can produce.

  TRANSMEDIA STORYTELLING:
    Stories unfold across multiple media platforms.
    Each medium makes its own unique contribution.
    Audience engagement = active exploration of storyworld.
    Matrix: films + games + comics + ARGs.

  AFFINITY SPACES (not communities -- less hierarchical):
    Organized around shared endeavor, not identity.
    Newbies and experts interact.
    Knowledge is distributed.
    Examples: fan wikis, modding communities, Reddit.
```

### Fan Culture and Resistance

```
  FANS AS ACTIVE PRODUCERS
  ==========================

  Fan fiction: Re-write official stories.
    "Slash" fiction: Kirk/Spock (M/M); "shipping."
    Often queers officially straight characters.
    Jenkins: "Textual poaching" -- appropriating
    cultural property for own purposes.

  Fan wikis: Collaborative encyclopedias.
    Often more accurate and comprehensive than official.

  Meme culture: Rapid re-contextualization of content.
    "Distracted boyfriend" used for everything.
    "Dogecoin" dog becomes global financial phenomenon.

  Cosplay: Embodied performance of media characters.
    Creative production, community, identity.

  LIMITS:
    Participation ≠ equality.
    Race, class, and gender shape whose participation
    is valued, visible, monetized.
    Platform ownership captures participatory labor.
    YouTube: users create content; Google captures revenue.
    "Cognitive capitalism": user creativity = free labor.
```

## Digital Audiences: Algorithmic Mediation

```
  TRADITIONAL AUDIENCES vs. DIGITAL AUDIENCES
  =============================================

  TRADITIONAL:
    Audience is aggregate demographic (18-49 women).
    Measured by survey/diary (Nielsen ratings).
    Passive exposure (TV on in background).
    Feedback: ratings data, letters, focus groups.

  DIGITAL:
    Audience is individual user profiles.
    Measured by behavioral data (clicks, dwell time, shares).
    Active engagement (commenting, sharing, creating).
    Feedback: real-time A/B testing, engagement metrics.

  ALGORITHMIC PERSONALIZATION:
    Each user sees different content (filter bubble).
    Recommendation algorithm mediates exposure.
    Users in same household may see totally different
    YouTube homepages.

  IMPLICATIONS FOR AUDIENCE RESEARCH:
    "The audience" no longer exists as common experience.
    Shared national media events (moon landing, 9/11)
    becoming rarer as media atomizes.
    Agenda-setting research needs revision:
    Whose agenda? The algorithm's?
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Limited Effects ≠ No Effects**
Lazarsfeld found limited attitude change, not no media effects. Media still shapes what people think about (agenda-setting), what they believe is normal (cultivation), how issues are framed (framing). "Limited effects" referred specifically to direct attitude change on pre-existing political opinions.

**Active Audiences Are Not Free**
Stuart Hall and the reception theorists showed audiences can read against the grain. But: oppositional readings are exceptional; most audiences don't bring a counter-code. And: reading against the grain of individual texts doesn't change the structural conditions of media production.

**Participatory Culture ≠ Democratic Media**
Jenkins's framework can read as utopian. The internet enables mass participation, but the platforms that host participation are owned by corporations that extract value from it. "Prosumers" (producer-consumers) provide free labor. The democratic potential of participation coexists with concentrated ownership and algorithmic control.

**Uses and Gratifications Doesn't Invalidate Political Economy**
"People choose what they want" doesn't mean the choice context is neutral. The options available, the interface design, the algorithmic recommendations -- all constrain and shape choice. Saying audiences have agency doesn't resolve the question of structural power. Both accounts of audience activity and structural constraints are necessary.
