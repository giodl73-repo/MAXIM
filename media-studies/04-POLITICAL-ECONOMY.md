# Political Economy of Media

## The Big Picture

Political economy of media asks: who owns media, what are the economic imperatives of media production, and how does ownership structure shape content? The core claim: media is an industry like other industries, and the profit motive systematically shapes what is produced, what is suppressed, and whose interests are served.

## Ownership Structure and Concentration

### Historical Consolidation

```
  US MEDIA OWNERSHIP HISTORY
  ===========================

  1940s: Hundreds of independent newspapers, radio stations,
         local TV stations. Antitrust enforcement active.

  1945-1975: FCC cross-ownership rules prevent one company
             from owning multiple media types in same market.

  1996 Telecommunications Act:
    Deregulated media ownership.
    Radio: Single company can own 8 stations per market
    (Clear Channel/iHeartMedia: 850 stations nationally)
    TV: Raised national ownership caps.
    Cable: Deregulated cross-ownership.

  Result (1996-2020):
    Radio: Clear Channel bought hundreds of stations.
    Local newsrooms laid off reporters.
    "Voice tracking": DJ records in Chicago, broadcasts
    in dozens of markets as if local.

  2023 landscape:
    Legacy: 5 conglomerates (Comcast, Disney, News Corp,
            Warner Bros. Discovery, Paramount)
    Digital: 5-6 platform giants
    Local news: ~1800 newspapers closed since 2005
```

### Concentration Effects

```
  WHAT DOES CONCENTRATION PRODUCE?
  ==================================

  HOMOGENIZATION:
    Fewer owners --> fewer perspectives.
    Common ownership of TV stations means common coverage.
    Sinclair Broadcasting: requires stations to air
    centrally-produced "must run" content (Conan Nolan,
    terrorism promos) -- local stations with national content.

  SYNERGY PRESSURES:
    Disney owns Marvel, Pixar, Lucasfilm, ESPN, ABC.
    ABC News unlikely to run critical investigative piece
    on Disney theme parks (Jeffrey Epstein relationship
    kept quiet -- ABC/Disney).
    Business interests colonize editorial decisions.

  RISK AVERSION:
    Large media companies have shareholders.
    Shareholders want stable returns.
    Controversial content = advertiser flight = risk.
    Safe, centrist, advertiser-friendly content preferred.

  PUBLIC INTEREST MARGINALIZATION:
    Media defined as business, not public service (US model).
    FCC public interest standard weakened by deregulation.
    Local news, investigative journalism: unprofitable.
    Entertainment, sports, reality TV: profitable.
```

## The Advertising Logic

Dallas Smythe's foundational insight: what does commercial television actually produce?

```
  SMYTHE'S "BLIND SPOT" (1977)
  ==============================

  COMMON ASSUMPTION:
    TV produces programs (content) for audiences.

  SMYTHE'S CORRECTION:
    TV produces AUDIENCES for advertisers.

  +-----------------+      +------------------+
  | MEDIA COMPANY   |      |   ADVERTISERS    |
  | (TV network,    |      | (Ford, P&G, etc.)|
  | newspaper)      |      |                  |
  +-----------------+      +------------------+
         |                         ^
         | Produces content        | Sells audiences
         v                         |
  +-----------------+              |
  | AUDIENCES       +--------------+
  | (viewers,       |
  | readers)        |
  +-----------------+

  The PRODUCT is the audience (their attention/time).
  The CUSTOMER is the advertiser.
  The CONTENT is bait to attract the audience/product.

  Consequence: Content is optimized for maximizing
  the value of audience demographic to advertisers --
  not for informing, educating, or serving public interest.
```

### Audience Commodity

```
  WHAT ADVERTISERS ACTUALLY BUY
  ================================

  Not "eyeballs" -- specific demographic profiles:
    18-49 year old adults with disposable income
    Women 25-54 for household purchasing decisions
    Men 18-34 for beer, cars, sports equipment

  This shapes content:
    Soap operas: targeted at women at home in 1950s
    Sports broadcasting: targeted at men 18-49
    News: 25-54 professional demographic
    Children's TV: sells toys, sugar cereals to parents

  Consequence: Programming for demographics
               not in advertiser sweet spot is
               dropped or never produced:
               Elderly audiences
               Low-income audiences
               Politically inconvenient audiences
```

## Herman and Chomsky: The Propaganda Model

*Manufacturing Consent* (1988): The news media systematically filter out perspectives that threaten elite interests through five institutional filters.

```
  THE FIVE FILTERS
  ================

  FILTER 1: OWNERSHIP
    Large media corporations owned by wealthy individuals
    or institutional investors.
    Owners have economic interests in business-friendly
    coverage. Direct and indirect editorial pressure.
    Example: Rupert Murdoch's political interventions
    in Fox News editorial direction.

  FILTER 2: ADVERTISING
    News organizations dependent on advertising revenue.
    Advertisers can withdraw revenue for hostile coverage.
    Self-censorship by news outlets regarding major
    advertisers.
    GM pulled ads from media critical of auto industry.

  FILTER 3: SOURCING
    Journalism requires sources.
    Sources with official status preferred:
    government officials, corporate PR, think tanks
    (many funded by industry).
    "Official" sources set the terms of debate.
    Alternative perspectives require resources to access,
    document, confirm -- resources news orgs lack.

  FILTER 4: FLAK
    Organized criticism/pressure from powerful groups.
    Advertisers, political lobbies, industry associations.
    Media Research Center: conservative "flak machine."
    Coordinated letter/boycott campaigns against
    coverage perceived as hostile to business/right wing.

  FILTER 5: ANTI-COMMUNISM / DOMINANT IDEOLOGY
    (originally "anti-communism"; updated to dominant ideology)
    The ideological framework within which media operates.
    During Cold War: anti-Soviet filter.
    Post-9/11: anti-terrorism filter.
    Now: market ideology ("free market is natural").
    Perspectives outside this framework = radical, fringe.
```

### Worthy vs. Unworthy Victims

```
  PROPAGANDA MODEL: VICTIM HIERARCHY
  =====================================

  WORTHY VICTIMS:
    Victims of official enemies (Communist states,
    adversary nations) receive extensive sympathetic
    coverage.
    Example: Pol Pot's Cambodia -- extensive coverage.

  UNWORTHY VICTIMS:
    Victims of US allies or US policy receive
    minimal coverage.
    Example: Indonesian atrocities in East Timor
    (US-armed military) vs. comparable events.

  Herman/Chomsky data:
    El Salvador vs. Poland (1980s):
    Both had religious figures murdered by state forces.
    Polish priest: enormous US media coverage.
    Salvadoran archbishop Romero and churchwomen:
    minimal coverage. US was arming El Salvador.

  This asymmetry is systematic, not random.
  It maps onto whether the victims are "useful" for
  US foreign policy narrative.
```

## Public Service Broadcasting: Alternative Model

```
  COMMERCIAL MODEL vs. PUBLIC SERVICE MODEL
  ==========================================

  COMMERCIAL                     PUBLIC SERVICE
  (US model)                     (BBC, PBS, ABC Australia)
  ===========                    ======================

  Audience = product             Audience = citizen/public
  Advertiser = customer          License fee / tax funding
  Content serves market          Content serves public interest
  No universal service           Universal service obligation
  Profitability criterion        Quality, diversity criteria
  Local news: unprofitable       Local news: mandated
  Minority perspectives: absent  Minority service obligations
  Children's TV: toy ads         Children's TV: educational

  BBC (UK):
    Founded 1927 (Lord Reith)
    "Inform, educate, entertain"
    License fee: ~£175/year
    No advertising
    Political independence (contested but real)
    Risk: political pressure (charter renewal by government)

  US PBS:
    Inadequately funded (government + donations)
    "Viewers like you" = partial corporate funding
    Limited scope vs. commercial media
```

## The Political Economy of Digital Media

```
  TRANSITION FROM ADVERTISING TO SURVEILLANCE
  =============================================

  LEGACY ADVERTISING:
    Demographic targeting: 18-49 women, household income X
    Contextual: ads near content category (car ad near auto news)
    Brands paid for placement in specific demographic contexts

  PROGRAMMATIC ADVERTISING:
    Automated real-time bidding (RTB)
    Advertisers bid on individual impressions (user + moment)
    User profile determines ad price
    Cookie-based behavioral tracking

  SURVEILLANCE CAPITALISM (Zuboff):
    Behavioral data extracted from user activity
    --> Processed into prediction products
    --> Sold to advertisers wanting to influence behavior
    Not just targeting: MODIFICATION of behavior
    Google can predict flu outbreaks from search patterns
    Facebook can affect voter turnout through Feed changes

  ECONOMIC DYNAMIC:
    Zero marginal cost of data extraction
    Winner-take-all network effects
    Platform profits = rents on captured attention
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Political Economy Is Not Conspiracy Theory**
Herman and Chomsky explicitly distinguish their model from conspiracy: "We are not claiming that journalists are liars or that editors receive phone calls from owners telling them what to write." The filters operate through institutional incentives, professional socialization, and self-censorship -- not through overt direction. The system produces biased output without anyone intending it.

**Advertising Logic Does Not Mean All Content Is Propaganda**
Commercial media produces genuine investigative journalism, important political coverage, and high-quality entertainment. The political economy argument is structural and probabilistic: advertising dependency systematically biases media toward certain perspectives and audiences, all else equal. Not every story is compromised.

**Public Service Broadcasting Is Not Perfect**
BBC and PBS have their own biases -- institutional conservatism, reliance on official sources, BBC's "both sides" journalistic conventions that can create false balance. The PSB model removes advertising dependency but creates government dependency (license/appropriation), which generates different pressure points.

**Local News Collapse Is a Democracy Problem**
Local investigative journalism covering city councils, school boards, local corruption is nearly gone in hundreds of US communities. This is not just a business story. Without local news, local corruption goes uncovered, civic participation falls, and national partisan media fills the gap. Political economy connects here to democratic theory.
