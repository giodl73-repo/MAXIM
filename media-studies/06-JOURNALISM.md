# Journalism: Norms, Crisis, and Digital Disruption

## The Big Picture

Journalism is the practice of gathering, verifying, and distributing information about public affairs. Its institutional claim to democratic importance rests on the watchdog function -- holding power accountable -- and the public sphere function -- providing shared factual grounding for civic deliberation. Both functions are under severe structural stress from digital disruption of the revenue model.

## Professional Norms: The Objectivity Ideal

### Origins of Objectivity

```
  EMERGENCE OF OBJECTIVITY NORM (late 19th - early 20th c.)
  ===========================================================

  19th century journalism:
    Explicitly partisan press.
    Newspapers were party organs.
    "Yellow journalism" (Hearst, Pulitzer) --
    sensationalist, opinion-driven, circulation wars.

  Why objectivity emerged:
  1. BUSINESS MODEL:
     Wire services (AP, 1846) served papers of all
     political persuasions. Must be acceptable to all.
     Neutral = broadest market.

  2. PROFESSIONALIZATION:
     Journalism schools (Columbia, 1912).
     Professional associations.
     Journalism as profession like law/medicine:
     requires codes of ethics, training, standards.

  3. PROGRESSIVE ERA IDEOLOGY:
     Scientific method as model.
     Facts vs. opinions as distinct categories.
     Journalism could be scientific if sufficiently
     methodical.

  The "View from Nowhere":
    Professional journalism ideology: reporter has no
    perspective, presents "both sides."
    Critics (Jay Rosen, Michael Schudson):
    False neutrality -- the view from nowhere is
    itself a perspective, usually a centrist one.
```

### What Objectivity Actually Means in Practice

```
  OBJECTIVITY COMPONENTS (in practice)
  =======================================

  FACTUAL ACCURACY:
    Claims are verified before publication.
    Sources cited; documentation required.
    Corrections policy when wrong.

  BALANCE (contested):
    Present multiple perspectives.
    "Both sides" -- but: false equivalence problem.
    Climate change "both sides" gives equal weight to
    97% of scientists and 3% of dissenters.
    This is not objectivity -- it is distortion.

  FAIRNESS:
    Give subjects of stories opportunity to respond.
    Report what they say accurately.

  INDEPENDENCE:
    No financial or personal interest in the story.
    Journalists should not accept gifts, investments,
    political roles.

  ATTRIBUTION:
    "Official" sources: statements attributed to sources.
    "According to the White House..."
    Journalist is not responsible for accuracy of
    attributed claims -- but: this enables official lies.
```

## The Revenue Collapse

### The Business Model That Was

```
  NEWSPAPER ECONOMIC MODEL (1950s-1990s)
  ========================================

  REVENUE SOURCES:
  1. CLASSIFIED ADVERTISING: 30-40% of newspaper revenue
     Help wanted, real estate, automotive, personals
     Newspapers had LOCAL MONOPOLY on this
     No other efficient way to reach local buyers/sellers

  2. DISPLAY ADVERTISING: 30-40%
     National brands + local businesses
     Newspapers had reach and demographics data

  3. SUBSCRIPTIONS: 20-30%
     Home delivery + newsstand

  TOTAL: Healthy margins; local monopoly in most markets.
  Most American cities had one major daily by 1960s.
  That monopoly funded newsrooms of 100-500+ reporters.
```

### The Digital Destruction

```
  CLASSIFIED ADVERTISING COLLAPSE
  =================================

  Craigslist (1995):
    Free classified ads online.
    Why pay the newspaper for help wanted?
    Craigslist destroyed ~$5B/year in newspaper classifieds.

  Zillow, Realtor.com: Real estate listings
  AutoTrader, Cars.com: Automotive
  Match.com, Tinder: Personals

  ALL major classified categories migrated online.
  Revenue gone. It did not come back.

  DISPLAY ADVERTISING COLLAPSE
  ==============================

  Google (2000): Search advertising -- intent-based targeting.
    Advertisers reach customers WHEN they are searching.
    Far more effective than contextual newspaper placement.

  Facebook (2012+): Social advertising -- demographic targeting.
    Precisely targeted by age, gender, interests, behavior.
    Reach exactly the 35-45-year-old women interested in X.

  Result:
    ~80% of US digital advertising goes to Google + Meta.
    Newspaper display advertising: $48B (2000) --> $7B (2022).
    TV ad revenue: also in steep decline.

  SUBSCRIPTION CHALLENGE:
    Digital content = free expectation (early internet)
    Paywall experiment: NYT (2011) -- partially works.
    But: Most readers won't pay.
    "The news" is commoditized; subscription requires
    unique value proposition.
```

### Industry Consequences

```
  US NEWSPAPER INDUSTRY DECLINE
  ================================

  Newsrooms eliminated:
    Total US newsroom employees:
    2008: ~75,000
    2023: ~28,000
    (62% decline in 15 years)

  Local news specifically:
    ~1,800 newspapers closed since 2005
    ~500 news deserts: counties with no local news
    Many markets: one "zombie" paper owned by hedge fund
    with skeleton staff

  Hedge fund ownership:
    Alden Global Capital: acquired Tribune, MediaNews Group.
    Strategy: cut newsroom to skeleton, extract cash,
    run until worthless, sell/close.
    Short-term extraction vs. long-term public service.

  Political consequence:
    Fewer local reporters --> less government accountability
    Studies: less local journalism --> more incumbent safety
    (no one covering city hall)
    Less civic participation, higher corruption.
```

## Disinformation and the Information Ecosystem

```
  INFORMATION ECOSYSTEM DEGRADATION
  ===================================

  PRE-DIGITAL:
    Information gatekeepers (editors, publishers)
    filtered content before publication.
    Costs of publishing created scarcity.
    False information had to overcome institutional barriers.

  POST-DIGITAL:
    Zero cost of publication.
    No gatekeeping for online content.
    Disinformation can spread at same speed as accurate info.

  DISINFORMATION vs. MISINFORMATION:
    Misinformation: False content spread without knowing it's false.
    Disinformation: Deliberately false content spread to deceive.
    Malinformation: True content spread to cause harm (doxxing).

  ALGORITHMIC AMPLIFICATION:
    Engagement-optimized feeds favor emotionally arousing,
    novel, outrage-generating content.
    Disinformation is often more emotionally engaging
    than accurate but boring corrections.
    Platforms structurally amplify disinformation.

  MIT Sloan study (Vosoughi et al., 2018, Science):
    False news spreads significantly faster and wider
    than true news on Twitter.
    By 6x for political content.
    Human behavior drove spread, not bots primarily.
```

### The Fox Problem

```
  PARTISAN MEDIA AND EPISTEMOLOGICAL FRAGMENTATION
  ==================================================

  Traditional model:
    Different outlets, different political slant,
    but shared factual reality.
    Disagreement about values and policy.

  Fox News model (post-2016 especially):
    Different facts for different audiences.
    Obama born in Kenya (Fox amplified).
    COVID vaccines implanted microchips.
    2020 election was stolen.

    This is not partisan disagreement about values.
    It is construction of a separate epistemic reality
    for partisan audience.

  Consequence:
    Deliberation requires shared factual premises.
    If citizens inhabit different factual worlds,
    democratic deliberation becomes impossible.
    Not polarization as "disagreement" -- as
    two separate information realities.
```

## The Verification Challenge

```
  VERIFICATION IN DIGITAL MEDIA
  ================================

  TRADITIONAL:
    Reporter calls sources, checks records, gets documents.
    Editor reviews before publication.
    Errors: embarrassing but rare.

  DIGITAL SPEED PRESSURE:
    Twitter/X breaks news in real time.
    Audience expects immediate coverage.
    Outlets publish early ("developing story") and update.
    "First is best" creates incentive against waiting to verify.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Objectivity Is Not Empirical Neutrality**
The journalism "objectivity" norm does not mean reporters have no perspective or that truth is unknowable. It means procedural commitments: verification, attribution, balance, fairness. Violating these produces bias. But the norm can also produce false balance when applied mechanically.

**Platform Companies Are Not Publishers (Legally)**
Section 230 (1996): platforms are not liable for user-generated content. This is why Facebook is not liable for libel by users the way a newspaper is. This creates a structural difference: platforms have incentives to maximize engagement (including harmful content) without the legal liability that historically constrained publishers.

**Disinformation Is Not New**
Yellow journalism, 19th-century partisan press, wartime propaganda -- disinformation has always existed. What is new: zero cost of production + zero cost of distribution + engagement-optimized algorithmic amplification + targeting to maximize psychological impact. The scale and speed are qualitatively different even if the phenomenon is old.

**"Fake News" as Delegitimization Strategy**
"Fake news" entered political discourse as a term for disinformation. It was quickly weaponized to mean "news I don't like" (Trump). This delegitimization of legitimate journalism is itself a disinformation strategy: if all news is fake, audiences default to partisan sources. Terminological precision matters.
