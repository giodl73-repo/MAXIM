## Fired Brick

### From Adobe to Fired Brick: The Transformation

Sun-dried adobe and fired brick share the same raw material — clay-bearing soil —
but the kiln changes the material fundamentally.

```
FIRING PROCESS AND MICROSTRUCTURAL CHANGE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Temperature  Phase                    Structural Change
  ──────────── ─────────────────────    ──────────────────────────────────────
  20–100°C     Drying                   Free water driven off; mechanical only
  100–400°C    Dehydration              Chemically combined water released
  400–600°C    Organic burn-off         Carbon, sulfur compounds combust
  573°C        Quartz inversion         α-quartz → β-quartz (volume change)
  700–900°C    Metakaolinite forms       Clay minerals break down
  900–1000°C   Vitrification begins     Glass phase forms between grains
  1000–1150°C  Optimal firing           Mullite (3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂) crystals form
                                         Glass matrix cements grain structure
  >1200°C      Overfiring / bloating    Too much glass → deformation; glazed brick

  Key mineral: Kaolinite (Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄) → on firing → mullite + silica glass
  Mullite: acicular (needle) crystals interlock within glass matrix
  → mechanical interlocking + glass bonding = strength
```

### Clay Types and Brick Quality

| Clay type | Minerals | Firing temp | Strength | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ball clay | Kaolinite + illite | 1100–1200°C | 20–50 MPa | Fine grain; good plasticity |
| Fireclay | Kaolinite rich | 1200–1350°C | 30–70 MPa | Refractory; furnace lining |
| Surface clay | Mixed / illite | 900–1100°C | 10–30 MPa | Most common brick clay |
| Shale | Laminated clay | 1050–1200°C | 15–60 MPa | Mechanical compression shapes |
| Calcareous clay | Calcium-bearing | 900–1050°C | 10–30 MPa | Risk of lime blowing at high temp |

### Strength Comparison: Fired vs Unfired

```
  UNFIRED (adobe):  f_c = 1–5 MPa  (hand-formed, sun-dried)
  FIRED (engineering brick): f_c = 50–150 MPa  (Class A/B engineering brick)
  FIRED (common facing): f_c = 10–30 MPa  (typical facing brick)

  Why fired is stronger:
  1. Glass matrix: vitrification bonds grains with amorphous silicate glass
  2. Crystal interlocking: mullite needles cross grain boundaries
  3. Reduced porosity: gas bubble volume typically 15–25% (vs 30–40% adobe)
  4. Chemical stability: fired ceramic resists water dissolution; adobe weathers

  Water absorption test (EN 771-1):
    Engineering brick: < 7%  (low absorption = denser, stronger, frost-resistant)
    Facing brick:      7–15%
    Common brick:      15–25%
    → high absorption → freeze-thaw cycling → spalling in cold climates
```

### Kiln Types: Historical Progression

```
  CLAMP kiln: unbaked bricks stacked with fuel (wood/coal/straw) between
    → temperature varies wildly; outer bricks under-fired; inner bricks vary
    → result: heterogeneous batch; sort on site for use class

  UPDRAFT kiln (intermittent): fire below; gases rise through brick load
    → better than clamp; still batch process; heat loss on opening

  DOWNDRAFT kiln (intermittent): flues at top draw gases down through brick
    → more uniform temperature; better product; still batch

  TUNNEL kiln (continuous, modern): bricks on cars pass through:
    [Preheating zone] → [Firing zone 1150°C] → [Cooling zone]
    → continuous production; uniform temperature ± 20°C; low fuel per brick
    → standard since ~1900; modern output: millions of bricks per week per kiln
```

### Greek Orders and Proportional Systems

The Greek orders are not arbitrary stylistic choices — they encode structural
experience about column spacing (intercolumniation), entasis correction, and
the relationship between column diameter and height.

```
GREEK ORDERS: STRUCTURAL AND PROPORTIONAL RULES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ORDER       H : D RATIO   INTERCOLUMNIATION   CHARACTER
  ──────────  ────────────  ──────────────────  ──────────────────────────
  Doric       ~5.5–6:1      ~1.2–1.5 × D        Squat; powerful; earliest
  Ionic       ~8–9:1        ~1.5–2 × D          Medium; voluted capital
  Corinthian  ~9–10:1       ~1.5–2 × D          Tall; acanthus capital

  ENTASIS: column shaft is not straight — it slightly swells at 1/3 height
    → compensates for visual illusion that straight columns look concave
    → also slightly increases load-bearing section at point of maximum bending

  INTERCOLUMNIATION LIMITS:
    Too wide: lintel spans too far → tension failure in marble
    Optimal: ~1.2–2.5 × column diameter
    Parthenon: ~4.3 m span (17-tonne epistyle beams of Pentelic marble)
    → marble tensile ~9 MPa → beam barely adequate at this span

  PROPORTIONAL SYSTEMS:
    Roman Vitruvius codified in De Architectura (~25 BCE)
    Renaissance Palladio, Vignola → canonical orders re-established
    These are structural rules encoded as aesthetic rules — same as
    structural codes in modern form, just expressed differently
```

## Roman Pozzolana Concrete (Opus Caementicium)

This is the most structurally sophisticated material in the ancient world.
Its chemistry was not understood until the 20th century — and it is still
surprising structural engineers working on modern low-carbon materials.

### Raw Materials and Chemical Basis

```
ROMAN CONCRETE CHEMISTRY
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  COMPONENTS:                                                                 │
│  1. Lime (CaO): calcined limestone  CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂  at ~900°C             │
│  2. Pozzolana: volcanic ash from Puteoli (Pozzuoli) near Naples              │
│     Minerals: leucite (KAlSi₂O₆), analcite, smectite clays                   │
│     → reactive aluminosilicates (amorphous glass phase critical)             │
│  3. Seawater: provides Mg²⁺, Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻ ions                                 │
│  4. Aggregate: tuff, pumice, brick rubble (lightweight; reactive)            │
│                                                                              │
│  REACTION SEQUENCE:                                                          │
│                                                                              │
│  Step 1 — Slaking:  CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂  (exothermic; "hot lime")            │
│                                                                              │
│  Step 2 — Pozzolanic reaction:                                               │
│    Ca(OH)₂ + SiO₂ (amorphous) → C-S-H  (calcium silicate hydrate)            │
│    Ca(OH)₂ + Al₂O₃ (amorphous) → C-A-H  (calcium aluminate hydrate)          │
│    → these are the same gel phases as in Portland cement hydration!          │
│                                                                              │
│  Step 3 — Seawater reaction (UNIQUE to Roman concrete):                      │
│    Al-tobermorite crystallizes from C-A-H over decades to centuries          │
│    Al-tobermorite: crystalline phase with high aspect ratio platelets        │
│    → plates bridge cracks; reinforces the C-S-H gel matrix                   │
│    Phillipsite (zeolite) also nucleates in seawater pores                    │
│    → void filling; further strengthening                                     │
│                                                                              │
│  WHY STRONGER AFTER CENTURIES:                                               │
│    Modern OPC: hydration ~95% complete at 28 days; no further strengthening  │
│    Roman pozzolana: pozzolanic + zeolite reactions continue for centuries    │
│    → Pantheon concrete after 2,000 years: stronger than when poured          │
│    → al-tobermorite crystals continue forming from slag/pozzolana precursors │
│                                                                              │
│  WHY IT WAS "LOST":                                                          │
│    Not lost — just ignored as trade routes collapsed after Roman Empire      │
│    Pozzolana deposits are specific to Italian volcanic geology               │
│    Medieval lime mortars used gypsum or calcite — no pozzolanic reaction     │
│    Rediscovery: Smeaton's work on hydraulic lime (Eddystone Lighthouse 1759)│
│    Portland cement patented 1824 → superseded lime-pozzolana systems         │
│    True Roman chemistry understood: 2013 (Jackson et al., American Mineralogist)│
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Voussoir Arch: Structural Logic

The arch is the most important invention in compressive masonry — it allows spans
far beyond what a stone lintel can achieve.

### Arch Mechanics from First Principles

```
VOUSSOIR ARCH GEOMETRY AND FORCE FLOW
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Voussoirs: wedge-shaped stones (or bricks)
  Keystone: the topmost voussoir — locks the arch

           ┌─────────┐
          /│ keystone│\
         / └─────────┘ \
        /  voussoirs    \
       /                 \
  ════╝                   ╚════  ← springings (haunches on abutments)
  │abutment│             │abutment│

  FORCE FLOW:  vertical load → compression along arch curve → horizontal
  thrust at springings → abutments must resist horizontal thrust

  THRUST LINE: line along which resultant compressive force acts
    For arch to stand: thrust line must stay within the middle third
    of the voussoir cross-section at every point
    → if thrust exits middle third: tension develops → arch opens

  SEMICIRCULAR ARCH:  thrust line follows arch curve well at crown
    but generates LARGE horizontal thrust at springings
    → thick abutments or buttresses required

  POINTED (GOTHIC) ARCH:  thrust line more steeply angled at springings
    → SMALLER horizontal thrust for same vertical load → thinner walls
    → this is the structural revolution of Gothic architecture

  QUANTIFYING HORIZONTAL THRUST:
    H = wL² / 8f    (for uniform load w per unit length)
    where L = span, f = rise of arch
    Lower f (flatter arch) → HIGHER horizontal thrust
    Higher f (taller/steeper arch) → LOWER horizontal thrust
    Pointed arch: greater f for given span → lower H → thinner walls
```

### The "Keystone is Not Magic" Principle

A common misconception: the keystone is NOT structurally special. It is simply the
last voussoir placed. Any voussoir can be removed (or was never there — as in a
two-centered arch built from both ends) and the arch would still stand. The arch
works as a system — each voussoir pushes against its neighbour. The keystone
experiences the same compressive stress as the adjacent voussoirs at the crown.

## Roman Vaults and Domes

### Barrel Vault

A barrel vault is an arch extruded along the longitudinal axis.

```
BARREL VAULT THRUST LINES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Plan:                                     Section:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────┐          ╭──────╮
  │  → → → → → lateral thrust → →    │         /        \
  │  ┌────────────────────────────┐  │        │        │
  │  │       BARREL VAULT         │  │        │          │
  │  └────────────────────────────┘  │         \        /
  │  ← ← ← lateral thrust ← ←      │          ╰──────╯
  └──────────────────────────────────┘

  Problem: barrel vault generates CONTINUOUS lateral thrust along entire length
  → must have thick walls or buttresses for the full length
  → no windows in side walls (wall is structural)

  Cross (Groin) Vault solution:
  Two barrel vaults intersecting at 90° → thrust concentrates at FOUR corners
  → walls between the four piers can be removed or opened
  → pendentive: curved triangular surface at intersection

  Ribbed vault (Gothic):
  Stone ribs follow intersection lines → direct load to piers precisely
  → webs between ribs: thin stone infill, minimal structural role
  → enables large windows (clerestory) between piers
```

### The Pantheon Dome: Structural Analysis

```
PANTHEON DOME GEOMETRY (118–125 CE, Emperor Hadrian)
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  Internal diameter: 43.3 m  ← still the world's largest unreinforced         │
│  Thickness at base:  6.4 m  (massive; transitions to lighter concrete)       │
│  Thickness at crown: 1.2 m  (near oculus)                                    │
│  Oculus diameter:    8.7 m  (no glass — open to sky)                         │
│  Springing height:   ~4 m above floor  (drum section below dome)             │
│                                                                              │
│  AGGREGATE STRATEGY (genius of Roman concrete):                              │
│    Base of dome: heavyweight aggregate (basalt, brick) for mass stability    │
│    Middle zone:  medium (tuff, pumice mix)                                   │
│    Crown:        lightweight pumice only → minimizes load on crown           │
│    Net result: lighter top → thrust line stays within thick cross-section    │
│                                                                              │
│  MERIDIONAL COMPRESSION (vertical arches through the dome surface):          │
│    Load flows like a series of arches from oculus to drum                    │
│    → always compressive: fine for pozzolana concrete                         │
│                                                                              │
│  HOOP TENSION PROBLEM (horizontal rings through the dome):                   │
│    In the upper portion: hoop forces are compressive → no problem            │
│    In the lower portion: hoop forces are TENSILE → concrete can't take it    │
│    Roman solution: COFFERING (the decorative recesses in the dome)           │
│    Coffering reduces dome mass → reduces the tensile hoop forces             │
│    Also: stepped outer rings at base = horizontal buttressing                │
│    Roman pozzolana concrete tensile: ~2 MPa (enough to resist small tension)│
│                                                                              │
│  OCULUS STRUCTURAL ROLE:                                                     │
│    The oculus is NOT a weak point. At the crown of a dome, the meridional    │
│    compressive forces converge → crown is under biaxial compression          │
│    Opening the crown reduces the hoop tension that would otherwise build up  │
│    in a solid crown → structurally beneficial as well as functional          │
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Lime Mortar in Ancient Masonry

Most Roman masonry used lime mortar (not pozzolana concrete). Understanding lime
is essential for historic building repair.

```
LIME MORTAR CHEMISTRY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Step 1 — CALCINATION: limestone burned at 900–1000°C
    CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
    Product: quicklime (calcium oxide) — violently reactive

  Step 2 — SLAKING: adding water to quicklime
    CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + heat  (VERY exothermic!)
    "Hot lime" method: add water → violent reaction → heat → steam
    Result: lime putty (calcium hydroxide paste)

  Step 3 — MATURING: stored under water for months to years
    Ca(OH)₂ colloidal particles continue to break down to finer size
    Longer maturation → finer putty → stronger, smoother mortar
    Traditional: stored in "lime pits" for 2–10 years

  Step 4 — CARBONATION (setting):
    Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O
    Mortar absorbs CO₂ from air → slowly reverts to calcium carbonate
    Takes YEARS for full carbonation of thick joints
    → lime mortar must never be sealed (needs access to air/CO₂)

  WHY SELF-HEALING:
    Micro-cracks in lime mortar expose Ca(OH)₂ to CO₂ → local recarbonation
    → hairline cracks heal over time
    → Portland cement mortar: no self-healing; cracks remain or widen

  HYDRAULIC LIME vs NON-HYDRAULIC LIME:
    Non-hydraulic (air lime): pure CaCO₃ → CaO → Ca(OH)₂
      Sets only by carbonation; does not set underwater
      Soft, flexible, breathable; reversible with acid

    Hydraulic lime (NHL): limestone with clay impurities (silica, alumina)
      Clay minerals react at kiln temperature → belite (C₂S) + aluminates
      Same phases as Portland cement → sets by hydration (like OPC)
      Also undergoes carbonation afterwards → double setting
      Sets underwater; harder than air lime; less flexible

    Roman hydraulic concrete: used pozzolana as hydraulic additive to air lime
    → chemical result similar to natural hydraulic lime but controllable
```

## Common Confusion Points

**The keystone is not load-bearing in any special way.** All voussoirs are equally
load-bearing. The keystone is the last placed (therefore geometrically central), but
the arch works as a system of mutual compression between all voussoirs. Remove any
voussoir and the arch collapses.

**Roman concrete could set underwater — modern OPC can too.** The hydraulic
property is not unique to pozzolana. What IS unique is the long-term strengthening
through zeolite crystallization, which OPC does not replicate. Roman marine concrete
also had far lower cracking susceptibility because the pozzolanic reaction generates
less heat than OPC hydration.

**Groin vault and ribbed vault differ structurally.** Groin vault: the intersection
is a sharp line of concentrated stress (difficult to cut accurately in stone).
Ribbed vault: cut stone ribs follow the intersection lines and carry the load —
the web infill is thin and non-structural. The rib system allows lighter construction
and precise load routing to piers, enabling Gothic height.

**Hydraulic lime ≠ Portland cement.** NHL (natural hydraulic lime) NHL3.5 has
compressive strength 3.5–10 MPa at 28 days. OPC achieves 30–60 MPa. Both have
C₂S and C₃A phases. The difference: OPC has ~65% C₃S (fast, high strength) vs
NHL's mostly C₂S (slow, lower strength). NHL retains much of the flexibility and
breathability of air lime; OPC does not.

**Egyptian pyramid construction: not one technique.** The Great Pyramid (Khufu) core
is rough-cut nummulitic limestone from the Giza plateau itself. Fine white Tura
limestone was quarried 12 km away by boat. Red granite for chambers came from
Aswan, 900 km. The structural problem is distribution of ~2.6 million stone blocks —
not the arch or dome problems that later civilizations solved.
