## Fiber-Reinforced Polymers (FRP)

### Fiber Types

```
FRP FIBER COMPARISON
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  GFRP (E-glass, most common):                                                │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
│  E-glass fiber:   E = 72 GPa; σ_u = 2,400 MPa; ρ = 2,550 kg/m³            │
│  ECR-glass:       E = 72 GPa; σ_u = 3,100 MPa; improved corrosion resist.  │
│  AR-glass (alkali resistant): specifically for cement matrix (see GFRC)    │
│                                                                              │
│  CFRP (carbon fiber):                                                        │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
│  Precursor: PAN (polyacrylonitrile) or pitch (less common)                 │
│  Production: PAN fiber → oxidize (stabilize) at 200–300°C                  │
│              → carbonize at 1,000–1,500°C in N₂ atmosphere                 │
│              → graphitize at 2,000–3,000°C for high-modulus grades         │
│                                                                              │
│  PAN-based carbon fiber grades (Toray standard):                            │
│    Standard modulus (SM):   E = 230 GPa; σ_u = 3,500 MPa; ε_u = 1.5%     │
│    Intermediate modulus (IM): E = 290 GPa; σ_u = 5,600 MPa; ε_u = 1.9%   │
│    High modulus (HM):       E = 390 GPa; σ_u = 2,500 MPa; ε_u = 0.6%     │
│    ρ = 1,750–1,800 kg/m³  (lightweight: 22% density of steel)              │
│                                                                              │
│  AFRP (aramid / Kevlar):                                                     │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
│  DuPont Kevlar 29:  E = 70 GPa;  σ_u = 2,900 MPa; ρ = 1,440 kg/m³        │
│  DuPont Kevlar 49:  E = 125 GPa; σ_u = 3,000 MPa  (high modulus)          │
│  AFRP advantages: toughness; low density; impact resistance                │
│  AFRP disadvantages: moisture absorption; UV degradation; compression weak │
│  Limited construction use: post-tensioning tendons; blast-resistant lining │
│                                                                              │
│  BASALT FIBER (emerging):                                                    │
│  Extruded from basalt rock; no synthetic chemistry needed                   │
│  E = 89 GPa; σ_u = 3,200 MPa; ρ = 2,700 kg/m³                            │
│  Better high-temp performance than GFRP; less expensive than CFRP          │
│  Applications: BFRP rebar; mesh for concrete reinforcement                  │
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Matrix Materials

```
POLYMER MATRIX COMPARISON
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  THERMOSETTING (irreversibly crosslinked once cured):

  Epoxy (most common structural):
    E = 3.5–4.5 GPa;  σ_u = 60–90 MPa;  ε_u = 3–8%
    Excellent fiber-matrix adhesion (function of surface finish + sizing)
    High viscosity → needs processing aid (VPI, autoclave, RTM)
    Cure: room temp (slow; 24h+) or elevated temp (120–180°C; fast)
    High chemical resistance; low shrinkage
    Used: aerospace, structural CFRP, GFRP plates, high-end construction

  Vinyl ester (VE):
    E = 3.0–3.5 GPa;  σ_u = 70–80 MPa
    Better chemical resistance than polyester; lower than epoxy
    Lower viscosity than epoxy → better for infusion/pultrusion
    Less expensive than epoxy
    Used: GFRP bridge decks, tanks, pipe

  Polyester (UP):
    E = 2.0–3.5 GPa;  σ_u = 40–90 MPa
    Lowest cost; highest shrinkage; styrene emission (VOC issue → low-styrene grades)
    Hand layup and spray-up of boat hulls, marine, sanitation products
    Less used in structural construction (inferior to VE/epoxy)

  THERMOPLASTIC (can be remelted, reformed):
    PEEK, Nylon, PAEK — emerging; recyclable FRP possible
    Currently niche in construction; mainly aerospace/automotive
    Recycling benefit: FRP recyclability is a major unresolved issue
    → thermoset FRP difficult to recycle (cross-linked → cannot remelt)
    → thermoplastic FRP could enable circular economy
```

### Orthotropic Behaviour: E₁₁ >> E₂₂

```
FRP ANISOTROPY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  UNIDIRECTIONAL (UD) CFRP (60% fiber volume fraction):

  E₁₁ (fiber direction): E_f × V_f + E_m × V_m = 230×0.6 + 4×0.4 = 139 GPa
    Rule of mixtures (ROM): accurate for longitudinal modulus
  E₂₂ (transverse):     ≈ 8–10 GPa  (matrix-dominated; much lower)
    Inverse ROM: 1/E₂₂ = V_f/E_f + V_m/E_m → matrix limits transverse
  G₁₂ (in-plane shear): ≈ 5 GPa  (matrix-dominated)

  FIBER VOLUME FRACTION (FVF):
    Hand layup: V_f ≈ 30–50%
    Vacuum infusion: V_f ≈ 45–55%
    Autoclave: V_f ≈ 55–65%
    Pultrusion: V_f ≈ 50–60%
    Higher V_f → higher longitudinal stiffness/strength; more brittle

  QUASI-ISOTROPIC LAYUP:
    [0/45/−45/90]_s → equal proportions in 4 directions → isotropic in-plane
    E_qiso = E₁₁/2(1+ν₁₂) ≈ 45–55 GPa for CFRP  (compromise; no direction optimal)
    Used where loads are multi-directional or direction unknown
    Trade-off: no direction achieves UD performance

  DESIGN IMPLICATION:
    Align fibers WITH the principal stress direction
    Wasted fibers in wrong direction → cost + weight penalty
    In structural construction: pultrusion profiles (UD) for beams/columns
    In repair: CFRP strips aligned with beam span direction (// to tension)
```

## FRP in Construction Applications

### Bridge Decks (GFRP)

```
GFRP PULTRUDED BRIDGE DECK PANELS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Pultrusion: fibers pulled through resin bath → shaped die → cure
    → continuous profiles of constant cross-section
    → high V_f (55–60%); consistent quality
    → profiles: I-beams, channels, box sections, custom decks

  BRIDGE DECK SYSTEM:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓  ← wearing surface (thin concrete│
  │  ─────────────────────────────  or polymer overlay)             │
  │  ┌──┐ ┌──┐ ┌──┐ ┌──┐ ┌──┐     ← GFRP pultruded deck modules  │
  │  │  │ │  │ │  │ │  │ │  │        (adhesively bonded together)  │
  │  └──┘ └──┘ └──┘ └──┘ └──┘                                      │
  │  ════════════════════════════  ← main steel/concrete girders    │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ADVANTAGES:
  Weight: GFRP deck ~50 kg/m² vs RC deck ~400 kg/m²
  → dramatically reduces dead load → existing bridge upgraded for more live load
  No corrosion: no rebar → no chloride attack in salt-water or marine environment
  Rapid installation: modular; fast replacement during bridge closure
  Service life: projected 75+ years with no maintenance (vs RC deck: 25–35 yr)

  DISADVANTAGES:
  Stiffness: E_GFRP ~25–35 GPa vs E_concrete ~30 GPa → similar, but anisotropic
  Deflection governs: serviceability limit state often governs before strength
  Cost: 2–5× more expensive per m² than RC deck → justified by life-cycle cost
  Fire: thermosetting resin burns; deck loses structural integrity at ~250°C
  Creep: resins creep under sustained stress → long-term deformation
```

### CFRP Post-Tensioning

CFRP tendons used in post-tensioning avoid corrosion entirely — critical for
marine, chemical plant, and bridge structures.

```
CFRP TENDON PROPERTIES vs STEEL STRAND
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Property         Steel strand       CFRP tendon
  ──────────────   ─────────────────  ─────────────────────────────────────
  σ_u              1,860 MPa          2,400–3,000 MPa
  E                195 GPa            130–165 GPa  (lower stiffness → more elongation)
  Density          7,850 kg/m³        1,500–1,600 kg/m³  (20% of steel)
  Corrosion        Susceptible        Immune
  Prestress losses Similar             Similar (no relaxation difference)
  Ductility        Yielding before ULS Brittle (no yield plateau)
  Cost             Low                 8–15× higher than steel
  Magnetic         Yes                 No (MRI-compatible structures)
  Thermal expansion 12×10⁻⁶/°C       0–3×10⁻⁶/°C (very low; anchor elongation control critical)

  APPLICATIONS:
  Bonded CFRP strips for beam strengthening (EBR: Externally Bonded Reinforcement)
    → popular retrofit for insufficient shear or bending capacity
    → Epoxy-bonded to concrete soffit; fiber direction along span
    → ACI 440 / EN 1998 design approach
  Post-tensioning tendons: sea crossing bridges, wave-splash zones, parking decks
  Stay cables: cable-stayed bridges (weight reduction at long spans)
```

### GFRP Rebar

```
GFRP REBAR vs STEEL REBAR
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Property          Steel rebar (B500B)   GFRP rebar
  ──────────────    ───────────────────   ───────────────────────────────
  Tensile strength  500 MPa               600–1,000 MPa
  Modulus           200 GPa               40–55 GPa  ← much lower!
  Density           7,850 kg/m³           2,000–2,100 kg/m³
  Thermal expansion 12×10⁻⁶/°C           9–12×10⁻⁶/°C (similar to concrete OK)
  Ductility         High (yield + plastic) Brittle (linear to fracture)
  Corrosion         Susceptible           Immune
  Cost              Low                   2–4× steel price
  Magnetic/conductive Yes                 No

  CRITICAL: GFRP rebar E = 40–55 GPa vs steel E = 200 GPa
  → crack width in RC with GFRP rebar is larger for same reinforcement area
  → must increase GFRP area to control crack width/deflection
  → design codes (ACI 440.1R, CSA S807, ISIS Canada): different equations from steel

  WHEN TO USE GFRP REBAR:
  Chloride environment: sea walls, jetties, car parks (salt), waterfront structures
  MRI buildings: hospital MRI suite slabs
  Radar-transparent: air traffic control floors, broadcasting studios
  Cathodic protection incompatible environments

  WHERE NOT TO USE:
  Seismic moment frames: no ductility → plastic hinge cannot form safely
  High-sustained-load: creep rupture risk above 60–70% of tensile capacity
  High-temperature fire exposure: thermosetting resin → loss of bond above 250°C
```

## GFRC (Glass Fiber Reinforced Concrete)

```
GFRC PRODUCTION AND PROPERTIES
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  COMPOSITION:                                                                │
│  OPC or white cement + silica fume/microsilica + alkali-resistant (AR)      │
│  glass fibers (12–25 mm length; 0.4–1.0% by weight)                        │
│  + acrylic polymer modifier (for cure, shrinkage)                           │
│  No coarse aggregate (fine sand only, < 2 mm)                               │
│  w/b ~ 0.30–0.40                                                            │
│                                                                              │
│  WHY AR-GLASS:                                                               │
│  Portland cement pore solution pH ~12–13 → alkalis attack normal E-glass   │
│  AR-glass has ZrO₂ content (16–20%): zirconia modifies silica network →    │
│  resists alkali dissolution → 25–50 year durability in cement matrix        │
│                                                                              │
│  PRODUCTION (premix or spray-up):                                           │
│  Premix: AR glass fibers batched with cement slurry → mixed → cast/pressed │
│  Spray-up: cement slurry + chopped AR glass simultaneously sprayed onto mold│
│  → complex double-curved geometries easily formed → architectural freedom  │
│                                                                              │
│  PANEL PROPERTIES:                                                           │
│  Thickness: 8–25 mm  ← very thin vs precast concrete (100–200 mm)          │
│  Weight: 20–60 kg/m² vs PC panel 250–500 kg/m² → much lighter              │
│  Tensile MOR: 10–15 MPa (vs plain concrete 3 MPa)                          │
│  Impact resistance: significantly better than plain concrete                │
│  Fire resistance: non-combustible; A1 class                                 │
│  Freeze-thaw: excellent (no rebar to corrode; dense matrix)                 │
│                                                                              │
│  ARCHITECTURAL APPLICATIONS:                                                 │
│  Complex moulded facade cladding panels: cornice details; rustication        │
│  Traditional appearance with modern performance                              │
│  Cost: < GRC precast stone-facing panels; custom moulds expensive up front  │
│  Notable: Disney Concert Hall, LA (thin-shell titanium but GFRC mockups);   │
│    St. Regis Hotel London; multiple contemporary office facades              │
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Geopolymers

Geopolymers are the main contender for low-carbon Portland cement replacement.

```
GEOPOLYMER CHEMISTRY
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  WHAT ARE GEOPOLYMERS?                                                       │
│  Aluminosilicate material activated by strong alkali                        │
│  No Portland cement → no CaCO₃ calcination → major CO₂ savings            │
│                                                                              │
│  PRECURSORS (must be reactive aluminosilicates):                            │
│    Fly ash (Class F): low Ca; most studied; easy to activate                │
│    GGBS (ground granulated blast furnace slag): Ca-containing; fast setting │
│    Metakaolin (calcined kaolin): most reactive; white colour; pure          │
│    Combinations of above: fly ash + GGBS common                            │
│                                                                              │
│  ACTIVATORS:                                                                 │
│    NaOH solution + Na₂SiO₃ (sodium silicate/waterglass) — most common      │
│    KOH + K₂SiO₃ (potassium-based: slower; better for high-strength)        │
│    Typical molarity: 8–14 M NaOH; SiO₂/Na₂O ratio 1.0–2.0 in silicate     │
│                                                                              │
│  REACTION MECHANISM (simplified):                                           │
│    1. Dissolution: NaOH dissolves aluminosilicate phases from precursor     │
│    2. Polycondensation: dissolved Si-Al oligomers condense into             │
│       Si-O-Al network (N-A-S-H or C-A-S-H with Ca-containing precursors)   │
│    3. Hardening: 3D cross-linked aluminosilicate polymer → geopolymer gel  │
│       Structure: polysialate (Si:Al = 1:1) or polysialate-siloxo (Si:Al = 2:1)│
│                                                                              │
│  MECHANICAL PROPERTIES (fly ash-based, 100°C curing):                      │
│    f_c: 30–80 MPa  (compressive, similar to OPC C30–C80)                   │
│    f_t: 3–6 MPa    (tensile)                                                │
│    E:   15–30 GPa  (lower than OPC concrete)                               │
│    Acid resistance: superior to OPC (no Ca(OH)₂ to dissolve)               │
│    Thermal resistance: stable to 400–1000°C (depending on formulation)     │
│                                                                              │
│  CO₂ COMPARISON:                                                             │
│    OPC concrete: 200–300 kgCO₂e/m³  (including aggregate/transport)        │
│    Fly ash geopolymer: 80–140 kgCO₂e/m³  (40–60% reduction)               │
│    Metakaolin geopolymer: 60–120 kgCO₂e/m³  (depends on calcination)      │
│    NaOH/Na₂SiO₃ activators are energy-intensive: ~1.9 tCO₂/t NaOH        │
│    → high activator dose erodes savings; optimize precursor/activator ratio │
│                                                                              │
│  WHY STILL NICHE:                                                            │
│  Supply: fly ash availability declining as coal plants close                │
│  Long-term durability database: limited field data vs 100+ yr OPC record   │
│  Setting behaviour: variable; difficult to control open time                │
│  Standards: EN 197 doesn't cover geopolymers; bespoke approvals needed     │
│  Codes: ACI, Eurocode 2 do not have geopolymer-specific design rules       │
│  → every geopolymer project needs individual technical assessment          │
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Aerogel Insulation

```
AEROGEL: PHYSICS AND PERFORMANCE
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  WHAT IS AEROGEL?                                                            │
│  Silica (SiO₂) gel with liquid replaced by gas via supercritical drying     │
│  → nanoporous structure; 3D silica network with ~95% air by volume          │
│  → pore size: 20–100 nm  (smaller than mean free path of air at STP ~70nm) │
│  → gas conduction suppressed: Knudsen effect                                │
│                                                                              │
│  THERMAL PROPERTIES:                                                         │
│    λ = 0.013–0.020 W/mK  (monolithic aerogel, ambient pressure)            │
│    λ = 0.015 W/mK typical (vs mineral wool 0.035; EPS 0.038)               │
│    → 2× better insulator per mm than best conventional alternatives         │
│                                                                              │
│  APPLICATIONS IN CONSTRUCTION:                                               │
│                                                                              │
│  Aerogel blanket (flexible composite):                                       │
│    Aerogel particles embedded in fiber mat (glass or polyester)             │
│    λ ~ 0.015–0.018 W/mK; flexible; can be cut with knife                  │
│    Use: pipe insulation; thin wall insulation; historic building retrofit   │
│    Where space is critically limited (listed buildings, thermal bridges)    │
│    Cost: ~£200–600/m² vs mineral wool ~£5–15/m² → 20–40× more expensive   │
│                                                                              │
│  Aerogel granules (loose fill or concrete aggregate):                       │
│    Aerogel-enhanced render: aerogel granules + lime or hydraulic binder     │
│    λ ~ 0.025–0.045 W/mK (reduced by aggregate dilution)                    │
│    15 mm aerogel render ≈ 50 mm mineral wool thermally                     │
│    Used: thin-joint masonry insulation; historic facades                    │
│                                                                              │
│  Transparent aerogel (daylighting):                                          │
│    Silica aerogel granules in double-skin polycarbonate sheet               │
│    Or monolithic clear aerogel in IGU (U-value ~ 0.2 W/m²K vs triple 0.5) │
│    Highly experimental; cost prohibitive for construction scale              │
│                                                                              │
│  DISADVANTAGES:                                                              │
│  Brittle: monolithic aerogel shatters at moderate impact                    │
│  Hydrophilic (standard): moisture absorption → increased λ                  │
│    Hydrophobic treatment (MTMS, TMCS) available → repels water              │
│  Cost: 10–40× premium over conventional insulation                          │
│  Dust: aerogel blanket shedding → respiratory PPE required                 │
│  Fire: silica aerogel itself non-combustible; binders vary                  │
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Phase-Change Materials (PCMs)

```
PCM THERMODYNAMICS AND CONSTRUCTION APPLICATION
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  PCM PRINCIPLE: latent heat storage during phase transition                 │
│  Sensible heat: Q = m × c_p × ΔT  (requires temperature change)            │
│  Latent heat:   Q = m × L_f        (at constant temperature!)               │
│                                                                              │
│  COMPARISON:                                                                 │
│    Concrete: 2,400 kg/m³ × 880 J/kgK × 1°C = 2,112 kJ/m³K (sensible)     │
│    Paraffin wax (PCM): L_f = 200,000 J/kg → 180,000 kJ/m³ per transition  │
│    → equivalent to concrete storing 85°C temperature swing!               │
│    → PCM wallboard 12 mm: holds latent heat of 500 mm concrete slab        │
│                                                                              │
│  PCM TYPES:                                                                  │
│    Organic (paraffins):   T_melt = 18–30°C → comfort zone                  │
│      L_f ~ 150–250 kJ/kg; ρ ~ 850 kg/m³; λ ~ 0.2 W/mK (low)              │
│      Non-toxic; stable; no supercooling; cycles millions of times           │
│      Disadvantage: low λ → slow charge/discharge; flammable                │
│      Encapsulation: microencapsulated in polymer shell → concrete additive  │
│                                                                              │
│    Inorganic (salt hydrates): T_melt = 10–50°C; L_f ~ 200–300 kJ/kg       │
│      Na₂SO₄·10H₂O (Glauber's salt): T_m = 32°C; L_f = 252 kJ/kg         │
│      CaCl₂·6H₂O: T_m = 29°C; L_f = 191 kJ/kg                            │
│      Higher λ than organics; risk of supercooling; phase separation cycles │
│                                                                              │
│  CONSTRUCTION INTEGRATION:                                                   │
│                                                                              │
│  PCM wallboard (microencapsulated in plasterboard):                         │
│    3–5% PCM by board mass → L_f contribution ≈ 30 kJ/m² per board          │
│    Activated at ~21–23°C → absorbs peak day heat → releases at night        │
│    Effect: diurnal temperature swing reduced by 2–5°C in well-insulated rooms│
│    Combine with night purge ventilation: most effective strategy            │
│                                                                              │
│  PCM in concrete:                                                            │
│    Microencapsulated paraffin admixed (5–20% by volume of fine aggregate)  │
│    → mass concrete floor slabs with enhanced thermal storage                │
│    Strength reduction: ~10–20% at 20% replacement → limits use             │
│    Alternative: PCM-filled lightweight aggregate blocks                     │
│                                                                              │
│  PCM in insulation panels:                                                   │
│    PCM-filled panels behind plasterboard → storage + slight insulation     │
│    Rubitherm panels: graphite + paraffin → λ ~ 2 W/mK (fin effect)        │
│    → faster charge/discharge than pure paraffin                             │
│                                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Self-Healing Concrete

```
SELF-HEALING MECHANISMS
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                              │
│  AUTOGENOUS SELF-HEALING (natural):                                          │
│  Unhydrated cement particles remain in concrete for years                   │
│  Crack exposes water to unhydrated C₃S, C₂S → further hydration → C-S-H   │
│  → fills micro-cracks (< 0.1 mm) naturally over weeks/months               │
│  High SCM blends (GGBS): more latent hydraulic material → better autogenous │
│                                                                              │
│  BACTERIAL SELF-HEALING (engineered):                                        │
│  Spore-forming bacteria (Bacillus species: B. pasteurii, B. sphaericus)    │
│  encapsulated in expanded clay pellets or polymer capsules                  │
│  Added to concrete mix; lie dormant until crack provides water + O₂ access │
│  Activated bacteria: metabolize calcium lactate or urea + CaCl₂            │
│    Ureolysis: CO(NH₂)₂ + 2H₂O → 2NH₄⁺ + CO₃²⁻                           │
│              Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → CaCO₃  (calcium carbonate precipitates)       │
```

## Vacuum Insulation Panels (VIPs)

```
VIP TECHNOLOGY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PRINCIPLE: evacuated panel with microporous silica core
    Internal pressure: < 1 mbar → no gas conduction → only solid + radiation
    λ_eff = 0.007–0.010 W/mK  (vs aerogel 0.015; mineral wool 0.035)
    → 5–7× thinner than mineral wool for same U-value

  CONSTRUCTION: 25–30 mm VIP vs 150 mm mineral wool → same U-value
    → valuable where space is critical: historic buildings; retrofits; cold stores

  PROBLEMS:
  Edge heat bridge: foil envelope around panel is metallic → thermal bridge
    → edge U-value degrades whole-panel performance for small panel sizes
    → minimum 600 × 600 mm panel recommended (larger = lower edge:area ratio)
  Durability: if envelope punctured → vacuum lost → λ reverts to ~0.07 W/mK
    → one nail through wall → panel destroyed
    → cannot be cut on site; must use pre-cut sizes
  Life: designed for 25 years vacuum → needs replacement with building refurb
  Cost: ~£40–80/m² vs mineral wool ~£5/m²

  APPLICATIONS:
    Cold store panels and refrigerated vehicles
    Passive house wall retrofits where existing wall cavity too thin
    Listed buildings: add VIP behind internal lining to avoid external change
    Cladding systems (metal-faced sandwich) for thin-profile facade insulation
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| GFRP rebar in chloride environment — design difference vs steel? | Crack width governs: need larger area to achieve same crack width. Use ACI 440.1R not ACI 318. |
| CFRP bonded strips for beam strengthening — when is it appropriate? | Sagging moment (mid-span); tension face accessible; good concrete substrate (pull-off ≥ 1.5 MPa) |
| Geopolymer concrete — can it replace OPC for all applications today? | No: code gaps; variable supply; long-term durability data thin; use in pilot/specialist contexts |
| Aerogel vs mineral wool — when does cost justify aerogel? | Where space is critically limited: thermal bridges; historic building internal insulation; thin wall profiles |
| PCM wallboard — does it replace thermal mass? | No. PCM works in narrow temperature band; thermal mass works across all temperature swings. Both are useful. |
| Self-healing concrete — for structural use today? | Crystalline admixtures: yes, commercial. Bacterial: research stage; not standard specification. |
| VIP panel puncture on site — what happens? | Vacuum lost; λ rises to ~0.07 W/mK (10× worse); panel must be replaced. Mark locations carefully. |
| AFRP (aramid) for bridge tendons — trade-off? | Good tensile strength; very light; but moisture absorption, UV degradation, and poor compression limit use vs CFRP |

## Common Confusion Points

**FRP tensile strength quoted is for the fiber, not the composite.** Raw carbon fiber
σ_u = 3,500 MPa. CFRP unidirectional laminate: σ_u ≈ 1,500–2,500 MPa (lower because
of fiber misalignment, scale effect, matrix contribution). Product data sheets quote
laminate properties; material data sheets quote fiber properties. Never mix the two.

**Geopolymer is NOT a solved technology for all applications.** Media coverage
sometimes presents geopolymer as "ready to replace OPC." The reality: fly ash supply
is declining (coal shutdown); NaOH/Na₂SiO₃ activators have significant embodied
carbon; there are no ISO/EN design codes; long-term performance in aggressive
environments (carbonation, chlorides) is less documented than OPC. It is promising
and justified in specific applications, but not a drop-in replacement.

**PCM is not free energy storage.** PCMs shift thermal storage in time; they do not
generate or destroy energy. A room with PCM wallboard will be cooler in the afternoon
(absorbing heat) and warmer at night (releasing it). If the night temperature is higher
than the PCM melting point, it never re-solidifies → no cooling benefit next day.
Night ventilation to recharge the PCM is essential in hot climates.

**GFRP rebar is not a like-for-like substitution for steel rebar in seismic zones.**
Seismic design of moment frames requires ductile yielding (plastic hinges form in
beams/columns, absorb seismic energy). GFRP is linear-elastic to failure — it cannot
yield. Using GFRP in a seismic moment frame would produce brittle catastrophic failure.
GFRP rebar is suitable only in gravity frames and non-seismic elements.

**E = 40–55 GPa for GFRP rebar vs E = 200 GPa for steel is a critical design issue.**
For deflection and crack control (serviceability limit state), the lower modulus means
cracks are wider and deflections larger for the same bar area. The higher tensile
capacity (strength limit state) is largely irrelevant in typical slab/beam design where
serviceability governs. Design codes for GFRP rebar use different equations precisely
because of this modulus difference.
