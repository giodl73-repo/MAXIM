## Mud Brick (Adobe)

### Soil Composition for Adobe

Adobe is unfired sun-dried brick. The critical variable is soil composition.

```
IDEAL ADOBE SOIL COMPOSITION
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                            │
│  Clay:   15–30%  ← binder; too much → shrinkage cracking   │
│  Silt:   10–30%  ← filler; neutral                         │
│  Sand:   45–75%  ← skeleton; controls shrinkage            │
│  Gravel: 0–15%   ← structural fill                         │
│  Fiber:  0.5–1%  ← straw/grass; crack bridging             │
│                                                            │
│  Field test: form ball in hand, drop from 1 m              │
│    Shatters completely → too sandy (not enough clay)       │
│    Cracks on impact → close to ideal                       │
│    Remains intact → too much clay (shrinkage risk)         │
│                                                            │
│  Plasticity index (Atterberg limits):                      │
│    Ideal PI: 5–15  (measured with liquid limit/plastic     │
│    limit tests; high PI = too much clay)                   │
│                                                            │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Adobe Brick Production

1. Excavate subsoil (topsoil has too much organic matter → decay)
2. Mix with water to plastic consistency — "pugging" (stomped or kneaded)
3. Add fiber (chopped straw 3–5 cm) at 0.5–1% by volume
4. Pack into wooden molds (typical: 300 × 150 × 100 mm)
5. Unmold after 24 hours; cure in shade 2–4 weeks (avoid direct sun → surface cracking)
6. Final compressive strength: 1–5 MPa (vs fired brick: 10–100 MPa)

### Stabilized Adobe

Modern improvement: add 3–8% Portland cement or 5% lime to soil mix before molding.
- Cement-stabilized: resists rain erosion; compressive strength 3–8 MPa
- Lime-stabilized: pozzolanic reaction; better for high-clay soils; slower strength gain
- Bitumen-stabilized: waterproofing; used in some arid-climate road engineering as well

### Adobe Thermal Performance

The genius of adobe in hot-dry climates is **thermal time lag**:

```
THERMAL MASS TIME LAG — ADOBE WALL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Exterior temperature:  peaks at 40°C at 14:00, drops to 15°C at 04:00
                         diurnal swing = 25°C

  Adobe wall 400 mm thick:
    Time lag ≈ 8–12 hours
    Damping factor ≈ 70–80%

  Interior temperature:  peaks at ~27°C at 22:00–02:00 (!)
                         → when exterior has cooled → night ventilation purges heat

  Comparison:
  ┌────────────────────┬───────────────┬────────────────┬─────────────────┐
  │ Wall type          │ Time lag (h)  │ Damping (%)    │ λ (W/mK)        │
  ├────────────────────┼───────────────┼────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ Adobe 400 mm       │ 8–12          │ 70–80          │ 0.5–0.7         │
  │ Brick 215 mm       │ 4–6           │ 50–60          │ 0.6–0.8         │
  │ Rammed earth 400mm │ 10–14         │ 80–90          │ 0.7–1.1         │
  │ Timber 100 mm      │ 1–2           │ 15–25          │ 0.12            │
  │ Insulated stud wall│ < 1           │ 5–10           │ effective 0.04  │
  └────────────────────┴───────────────┴────────────────┴─────────────────┘

  Adobe vs modern insulation: NOT competing strategies — different climates.
  In a hot-dry climate with large diurnal swing: thermal mass wins.
  In a cold-temperate climate with small diurnal swing: insulation wins.
  In hot-humid climates: neither; ventilation is the strategy.
```

## Rammed Earth (Pisé)

Rammed earth packs damp soil in formwork in 150 mm lifts, compacted with a tamper
(historically wooden, now pneumatic).

```
RAMMED EARTH CONSTRUCTION SEQUENCE
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                │
│  1. Form: shuttering (plywood, steel) set to wall thickness    │
│     Typical: 250–500 mm                                        │
│                                                                │
│  2. Fill: damp soil placed in 150 mm loose lifts               │
│                                                                │
│  3. Ram: pneumatic tamper compacts to ~100 mm                  │
│     Density increases: 1,600 → 2,100 kg/m³                     │
│                                                                │
│  4. Strike form: forms moved up; repeat                        │
│                                                                │
│  5. Lift lines visible in finished wall — design feature       │
│                                                                │
│  Typical compressive strength: 2–5 MPa (unstabilized)          │
│                                5–12 MPa (cement-stabilized)    │
│                                                                │
│  Thermal mass: ρ × c_p ≈ 2,100 × 840 = 1,764 kJ/m³K          │
│  vs concrete: 2,400 × 880 = 2,112 kJ/m³K  → comparable       │
│                                                                │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Wattle-and-Daub

The oldest widespread infill system — woven lattice plastered with a clay mix.

### Wattle Construction

**Wattle** = the structural lattice:
- Upright stakes (staves): 25–50 mm diameter hazel or ash, 0.5–1.0 m apart
- Horizontal weave (withies/wattles): flexible hazel rods woven between staves
- Result: a rigid-ish basketwork panel, ~50 mm thick

Hazel was coppiced (cut at ground level every 7–10 years) specifically to produce
straight flexible rods — one of the earliest examples of managed renewable resource
production for construction.

### Daub Mix Proportions

**Daub** = the plaster applied to both faces of the wattle:

```
DAUB MIX COMPONENTS
┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                               │
│  Clay/subsoil:   50–60% by volume  ← binder                   │
│  Straw/hay:      20–30%            ← fiber, crack control     │
│  Animal dung:    10–15%            ← binder + fiber + bacteria│
│  Sand:           10–20%            ← reduces shrinkage        │
│  (+ water to workable consistency)                            │
│                                                               │
│  Animal dung function:                                        │
│    1. Cellulose fiber (undigested grass) → crack bridging     │
│    2. Bacterial breakdown products → carbonation → binds      │
│    3. Reduces shrinkage cracking vs clay-only mixes           │
│                                                               │
│  Application: two coats minimum                               │
│    First coat (rough coat / prick coat): 15–20 mm             │
│    Second coat (float coat): 10–15 mm                         │
│    Final coat (limewash): 2–3 mm lime + water (whitewash)     │
│                                                               │
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Performance

- Compressive strength: 0.5–2 MPa (the wattle frame takes most structural load)
- Thermal resistance: modest — ~0.5 m²K/W per 50 mm panel (better with thick plaster)
- Vapour permeability: excellent — breathable; avoids interstitial condensation
- Fire resistance: poor — wattle ignites; daub provides some surface protection
- Durability: 100–400 years with maintenance; failed daub must be patched

## Post-and-Beam Timber Construction

### Structural Logic

Post-and-beam is the fundamental timber structural system: vertical posts carry
compressive axial loads; horizontal beams span between posts and carry bending.

```
POST-AND-BEAM STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                      │
│  Purlin  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────          │

│               │                   │                   │             │
│  Plate  ──────┴───────────────────┴───────────────────┴──── (top   │
│               │                   │                   │     of wall)│
│               │                   │                   │            │

### Traditional Timber Joints: Mortise-and-Tenon

```
MORTISE-AND-TENON JOINT
         ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
         │                                     │
         │  MORTISE (the hole):                │
         │  Cut in post/receiver               │
         │  Dimensions: tenon width × depth    │
         │                                     │
         │  ┌─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─┐              │
         │  │                  │              │
         │  │   TENON          │              │
         │  │   (the tongue)   │  → beam end  │
         │  │                  │              │
         │  └─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─┘              │
         │                                     │
         │  PEG: 25–35 mm oak dowel driven     │
         │  slightly offset (drawboring) to    │
         │  pull tenon tight into mortise       │
         │  → mechanical lock, no glue needed  │
         │                                     │
         └─────────────────────────────────────┘

  LOAD TRANSFER MECHANISM:
  - Bearing: tenon face bears against mortise face → shear in tenon
  - Peg: resists withdrawal (tension) and rotation
  - Friction: oak dries after assembly → increases friction grip
  - Failure modes: shear through tenon, peg splitting, mortise splitting

  WHY IT WORKS:
  Timber in shear parallel to grain: ~5–8 MPa
  Typical tenon: 50 × 100 mm cross section
  Shear area: 50 × 100 = 5,000 mm²
  Capacity: 5,000 × 7 = 35 kN in shear → adequate for floor beam reactions
```

### Green Oak vs Seasoned Timber

```
  GREEN OAK             SEASONED OAK (air-dried 2–3 yr)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Moisture content:  50–80%          12–20%
  Workability:       Easy to cut     Harder (must re-sharpen)
  Weight:            Heavy (extra water)  Lighter
  Dimensional change: Will shrink/distort  Stable
  Joint fit:         Loose → seasons tight  Stable fit
  Cost:              Less expensive   More expensive

  Medieval practice: build with green oak; mortises slightly oversized;
  timber seasons in situ; joints tighten naturally over 2–3 years.
  Pegs driven off-center (drawboring) pull tenon into socket as it dries.

  Problem: green timber can twist on drying → buildings "rack" slightly
  → post-and-beam framing tolerates racking better than rigid connections
```

## Dry-Stone Construction

Dry-stone walls use no mortar — gravity, friction, and interlocking geometry
provide stability. Still built today in upland UK, Alpine regions, Mediterranean.

```
DRY-STONE WALL ANATOMY
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                  │
│  Section view:                                                   │
│                                                                  │
│     ← batter (inward lean) →                                     │
│                                                                  │
│   ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ← top stone / coping              │
│   ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓                                      │
│   ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ▒▒▒▒▒ ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ← throughstones (full width spans) │
│   ▓▓▓▓ ▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒ ▓▓▓▓ ← hearting (small stones, core)   │
│   ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ← face stones (outer course)       │
│   ─────────────────────────── ← foundation course (widest, flat) │
│                                                                  │
│  Face stones:    irregular but flat face; largest stones low     │
│  Hearting:       small irregular stones fill the core            │
│  Throughstones:  every 0.6–1.0 m vertically; full width tie      │
│  Batter:         face leans inward 1:6 → gravity stabilises wall │
│  Coping:         upright or angled stones cap and shed water     │
│                                                                  │
│  TYPICAL DIMENSIONS:                                             │
│  Retaining wall: base = 50–60% height; batter 1:4 to 1:6         │
│  Field wall: 1.0–1.5 m high; 0.6–0.8 m base; 0.45 m top        │
│                                                                  │
│  STABILITY MECHANISM:                                            │
│  Gravity: mass × g × moment arm > overturning moment             │
│  Friction: coefficient μ ≈ 0.5–0.7 for rough stone on stone      │
│  Interlocking: irregular shapes lock → resist sliding            │
│  Batter: resultant of gravity passes inside base → no overturn   │
│                                                                  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Earthquake Performance

Dry-stone walls perform surprisingly well in earthquakes compared to mortared
masonry — the lack of rigid connections allows small movements to absorb seismic
energy. The Inca dry-stone construction in Cusco survived earthquakes that
destroyed Spanish mortared masonry built on top.

Failure mode: overturning of coping or top courses. Throughstones are critical —
walls without them split and fall outward in both soil pressure and seismic loading.

### Northern Europe: Longhouse and Half-Timber

The Germanic/Scandinavian longhouse combined humans and livestock under one roof
(shared body heat in winter). Walls: wattle-and-daub or turf. Roof: thatch or sod.
Central hearth with no chimney → smoke exits through apex gap (smoke bay) or louver.

Key adaptations:
- **Sod roofs** (Scandinavia): high thermal mass; heavy → steep pitch for snow load
- **Turf walls**: earth insulation; integrated with landscape; low visual profile
- **Deep eaves**: protect daub walls from rain
- **Earth floors**: compacted clay + ox blood → hard, cleanable, some thermal mass

### Sub-Saharan Africa: Banco and Compound Architecture

**Banco** (West Africa): clay + sand + millet husks + water. Sun-dried in situ.
Applied wet over stone or timber armature. Re-coated annually after rains.

```
SAHEL EARTHEN ARCHITECTURE (Mali, Burkina Faso)
  Form: conical or beehive shapes → structural optimization
    Sphere/cone: maximum volume for minimum surface area → minimal material
    Rounded corners: no stress concentrations → resists cracking

  Great Mosque of Djenné (rebuilt 1907, original medieval):
    Largest mud-brick building in the world
    Annual re-plastering: community festival, ~10,000 people
    Structural: protruding palm-wood "toron" → scaffolding + visual texture
    Buttresses integrated into architectural form

  Functional logic:
    Thick earthen walls + small openings → significant thermal lag
    Flat roof: solar shading of walls; sleeping area at night
    Compound form: walls create shade on internal courtyard faces
```

```
VERNACULAR STRATEGIES → MODERN RELEVANCE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  VERNACULAR TECHNIQUE          MODERN APPLICATION / EQUIVALENT
  ──────────────────────────    ─────────────────────────────────────────
  Mud brick thermal mass        Exposed concrete soffit; concrete core
  Wattle-and-daub infill        Non-structural infill panels; partition walls
  Thatch insulation             Sheep's wool, hemp, wood fibre insulation
  Wind tower ventilation        Passive stack ventilation; Monodraught WindCatcher
  Courtyard microclimate        Atrium buildings; green courtyards
  Steep thatch pitch            Standing seam metal roof; minimum pitch rules
  Dry-stone batter              Gabion retaining walls; mechanically stabilized earth
  Timber post-and-beam          Modern timber frame; CLT platform construction
  Adobe thermal mass + small    Passivhaus: airtight envelope + controlled ventilation
    windows                       (different route; same comfort outcome)
  Earth berming                 Green roof; earth-sheltered housing
  Local material sourcing       Low-embodied-carbon specification; low transport miles

  KEY INSIGHT: vernacular buildings in their climate of origin often
  outperform post-war modernist buildings in that same climate — not
  because they are better engineered but because they were calibrated
  over centuries of iterative adaptation to local conditions.
  The problem is that vernacular traditions were disrupted by
  industrialization, leaving a gap that building science is now
  filling with simulation and measurement.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Hot-dry climate, no mechanical cooling? | Adobe/rammed earth walls 400 mm+ for thermal lag; small windows |
| Temperate climate, wet winters? | Timber frame + waterproof render/cladding; steep roof pitch |
| Limited skilled labor; abundant clay soil? | Adobe: simple tools, high thermal performance, very low cost |
| Need breathable wall construction (historic building)? | Lime plaster + natural fiber infill; no cement or impermeables |
| Retaining wall, no mortar, natural stone available? | Dry-stone; battered face; throughstones every 600 mm vertically |
| Vernacular repair — which mortar? | Lime putty mortar, not OPC — matches original flexibility and vapour |
| Maximum unbraced span for solid sawn softwood floor beam? | ~5–6 m for 250 × 75 mm section; truss or engineered wood beyond |
| Why does thatched roof need 45°+ pitch? | Gravity + capillary drainage — water must run off faster than it penetrates |
| Wattle-and-daub cracking — why? | Too much clay in daub; insufficient fiber; apply in cooler weather |

## Common Confusion Points

**Adobe ≠ Rammed earth.** Adobe is formed into bricks, sun-dried, then laid like
conventional masonry with earthen or lime mortar between courses. Rammed earth is
compacted directly in formwork as a monolithic wall. Same material (earthen), very
different construction method, different surface texture and structural behaviour.

**Thermal mass ≠ insulation — they are often opposed.** Adobe is a terrible insulator
(λ ~0.6 W/mK) but an excellent thermal mass. In hot-dry climates the time-lag effect
is the strategy. In cold-temperate climates you want low λ AND high mass, which is
why Passivhaus uses insulation outside (low λ) and exposed concrete inside (mass).

**Green oak movement is predictable and designed-for.** Medieval carpenters used green
timber knowingly. The pegged mortise-and-tenon system is specifically tolerant of
movement. It would be wrong to interpret this as ignorance — it is adaptation.

**Dry-stone walls fail by overturning, not crushing.** The stones are very strong
(granite, limestone). The failure mode is stability (moment equilibrium about the toe),
not material strength. This is why batter (inward lean) is the critical design
variable — it shifts the centre of mass to resist overturning.

**Thatch is not primitive — it is a high-skill tradesman material.** A master thatcher
spends years in apprenticeship. The layering, eave detailing, ridge construction,
and ongoing maintenance schedule for water reed vs long straw are distinct bodies of
craft knowledge. Lifespan comparison: a well-laid water reed roof outlasts a
cheap clay tile roof with no maintenance.

**Vernacular buildings fail when moved to wrong climate.** Adobe buildings exported
to temperate humid climates (e.g., Spanish missions in California) fail without
continuous lime render maintenance — rain erosion. The material is correct for its
origin climate, not universally applicable.
