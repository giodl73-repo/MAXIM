## Core Principles (Invariant Across Movements)

These principles hold regardless of aesthetic. Every movement re-interprets them,
not replaces them.

```
PRINCIPLE        DEFINITION
---------        ----------
Hierarchy        Visual weight signals importance. Reader's eye follows
                 the designed path: largest -> smallest, dark -> light.

Contrast         Difference creates emphasis. Type weight, size, color,
                 texture, positive/negative space.

Alignment        Elements relate to each other spatially. Invisible lines
                 create order. Misalignment signals noise, not intention.

Repetition       Consistent visual elements create cohesion across a system.
                 Repeat color, type, pattern -- not sameness but rhythm.

Proximity        Related elements group together. Unrelated items have
                 more space between them.

White Space      Not emptiness -- active structural element. Air around
(negative space) content lets it breathe and signals quality.

Gestalt          The whole exceeds its parts. Closure, continuation,
                 similarity, figure/ground -- the eye completes patterns.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Design vs. Art"** -- Art is self-expression; graphic design is communication
with an audience and purpose. Design that nobody can read or act on has failed,
regardless of its beauty. This is the Bauhaus thesis made practical.

**"Raster vs. Vector"** -- Raster (JPG, PNG, PSD) is a grid of pixels; zoom in
and it degrades. Vector (SVG, AI, EPS) is mathematics (Bezier curves); infinitely
scalable. Logos must be vector. Photos are raster.

**"The grid is invisible but mandatory"** -- Every well-designed layout is built on
a grid system, even when the final piece looks asymmetric or free-form. The grid
is the constraint you design within, not the output.

**"Font vs. Typeface"** -- Helvetica is a typeface (the design). Helvetica Bold 12pt
is a font (a specific instance). The distinction matters in type design; in daily
use most people say font for both.

**"Modernism is not minimalism"** -- Swiss modernism is restrained but purposeful.
Minimalism (as an aesthetic movement) came later and is often decorative restraint.
The Bauhaus was modernist but very much included color, craft, and playfulness.
