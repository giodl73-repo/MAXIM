# Fungal, Parasitic, and Prion Disease

## The Big Picture

```
THREE PATHOGEN CLASSES THAT DON'T FIT BACTERIAL/VIRAL:

FUNGI: eukaryotes (like us) → hard to target selectively
  Unique targets: ergosterol (membrane sterol vs cholesterol), cell wall (β-1,3-glucan)
  Dimorphic: some switch mold (environmental) ↔ yeast (in host at 37°C)

PARASITES: eukaryotes; complex multi-stage life cycles; large diversity
  Protozoa: unicellular (Plasmodium, Trypanosoma, Leishmania, Toxoplasma)
  Helminths: multicellular worms (nematodes, trematodes, cestodes)
  Ectoparasites: mites, lice, ticks (vectors for other pathogens)

PRIONS: misfolded proteins — no nucleic acid, no conventional pathogen structure
  Completely break the "central dogma" of infectious disease (no DNA/RNA required)
  Catastrophically neurodegenerative, invariably fatal
```

## Fungi

### Antifungal Drug Targets

```
Ergosterol (fungal membrane sterol — replaces mammalian cholesterol):

  Synthesis pathway:
  Acetyl-CoA → ... → Squalene → Lanosterol → Ergosterol

  DRUG TARGETS:
  Azoles (fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole, posaconazole):
    Inhibit 14α-demethylase (CYP51, lanosterol → ergosterol step)
    → accumulate toxic methylated sterols → membrane dysfunction
    Fungistatic (not fungicidal); many drug interactions (CYP3A4)
    Fluconazole: oral/IV, excellent CNS penetration; Candida, Cryptococcus
    Voriconazole: broader — Aspergillus, Fusarium; visual disturbances
    Isavuconazole: mucormycosis

  Polyenes (amphotericin B, nystatin):
    Bind ergosterol directly → pores in membrane → ion leakage → cell death
    Amphotericin B: IV, broad-spectrum, fungicidal; nephrotoxic (tubular injury)
    Liposomal formulations (AmBisome): less nephrotoxic
    Nystatin: topical only (too toxic for systemic use)

  Echinocandins (caspofungin, micafungin, anidulafungin):
    Inhibit β-1,3-glucan synthase → cell wall synthesis failure → cell death
    Fungicidal against Candida; fungistatic against Aspergillus
    IV only; minimal drug interactions; low toxicity
    NO activity against Cryptococcus (no β-1,3-glucan in its wall)
    Preferred for invasive Candida, especially in critical care

  Flucytosine (5-FC):
    Prodrug: taken up by cytosine permease → 5-FU (inside fungus) → DNA/RNA disruption
    Fungistatic; used in combination (never alone — rapid resistance)
    Classic combo: ampho B + 5-FC for Cryptococcal meningitis (induction)
```

### Key Fungal Pathogens

#### Superficial and Cutaneous

| Organism | Disease | Feature |
|----------|---------|---------|
| Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton (dermatophytes) | Tinea capitis, corporis, pedis, unguium (onychomycosis) | Infects keratin; KOH prep shows hyphae |
| Candida albicans | Oropharyngeal (thrush), vaginal, intertrigo, diaper rash | Germ tube at 37°C; pseudohyphae + budding yeast |
| Malassezia furfur | Pityriasis versicolor (hypo/hyperpigmented patches) | "Spaghetti and meatballs" on KOH; needs fatty acid supplementation for culture |

#### Endemic Dimorphic Fungi (Mold in environment, Yeast in host)

```
HISTOPLASMA CAPSULATUM:
  Geography: Ohio/Mississippi River valleys; bird/bat droppings
  Mold form: microconidia → inhaled → lung
  Yeast form: 37°C → intracellular macrophage (survives in phagosome)
  Disease: asymptomatic (90%); acute pneumonia; chronic (COPD); disseminated (AIDS)
  Disseminated: hepatosplenomegaly, adrenal destruction (Addison's), oral ulcers
  Dx: urine/serum Histoplasma antigen (best for disseminated), culture (slow), serology
  Rx: mild-moderate: itraconazole; severe/AIDS: amphotericin B then itraconazole

COCCIDIOIDES IMMITIS:
  Geography: Southwestern USA, Northern Mexico, parts of South America ("Valley Fever")
  In environment: barrel-shaped arthroconidia → inhaled
  In host: spherules (large structures containing endospores; no equivalent in other fungi)
  Disease: asymptomatic (60%); flu-like (40%); chronic pulmonary; disseminated
  Disseminated: meningitis (chronic), bone, skin; Hispanic/Filipino/immunocompromised at risk
  Dx: serology (IgM = acute, IgG = chronic/disseminated), CSF complement fixation (meningitis)

BLASTOMYCES DERMATITIDIS:
  Geography: Mississippi/Ohio river valley + Great Lakes + southeastern US
  In host: broad-based budding yeast (diagnostic feature)
  Disease: pulmonary, skin (verrucous ulcers), bone, CNS
  Dx: culture (definitive), urine antigen (cross-reacts with Histoplasma)
```

#### Opportunistic Fungi

```
ASPERGILLUS FUMIGATUS (and other species):
  Ubiquitous mold; conidia constantly inhaled; cleared by neutrophils + macrophages
  PATHOGENESIS IN IMMUNOCOMPROMISED:
    Invasive pulmonary aspergillosis (IPA): hyphae invade blood vessels → ANGIOINVASION
    → thrombosis, infarction → "halo sign" on CT (ground glass around consolidation = hemorrhagic halo)
    → Air crescent sign (late, during recovery with ANC recovery)
  High risk: prolonged neutropenia (AML, stem cell transplant), high-dose steroids, chronic CGD
  Dx: Galactomannan antigen (serum/BAL) — cell wall component; 1,3-β-D-glucan
  Rx: voriconazole (first-line IPA); isavuconazole; liposomal ampho B

  ALLERGIC BRONCHOPULMONARY ASPERGILLOSIS (ABPA):
    Hypersensitivity (Type I + III) to Aspergillus in asthma/CF lungs
    ↑↑ IgE, peripheral eosinophilia, fleeting pulmonary infiltrates, central bronchiectasis
    Rx: corticosteroids + itraconazole

CANDIDA SPECIES:
  Commensal of skin, GI, vagina → pathogenic with immune disruption or barrier breach
  Risk: neutropenia, broad-spectrum antibiotics, IV catheters, TPN, gut surgery, steroids
  C. albicans: most common; C. glabrata/krusei: azole-resistant (fluconazole-resistant)
  Candidemia: remove catheter + fluconazole (susceptible) or echinocandin (resistant/critical)
  Candidemia has ~40% mortality; fundoscopic exam mandatory (endophthalmitis)

CRYPTOCOCCUS NEOFORMANS / GATTII:
  Environmental: soil, pigeon droppings; inhaled → lung → hematogenous spread to CNS
  Hallmark: POLYSACCHARIDE CAPSULE (enormous — antiphagocytic, anticomplement)
  India ink: capsule appears as halo (CSF directly on slide) — classical finding
  Cryptococcal antigen (serum + CSF): highly sensitive
  Disease: meningoencephalitis; mainly AIDS (CD4 < 100), organ transplant, hematologic malignancy
  Opening pressure: often very high on LP (capsular polysaccharide plugs arachnoid granulations)
    → Serial LPs or lumbar drain for ICP management (↑ ICP = main cause of early mortality)
  Rx: Induction: amphotericin B + flucytosine (2 weeks) → Consolidation: fluconazole (8 weeks) → Suppression (AIDS)

MUCORMYCOSIS (Rhizopus, Mucor, Cunninghamella):
  Angioinvasive — same as Aspergillus but MORE aggressive and faster
  Risk: DKA (high glucose + acidosis → ↓ transferrin binding of iron → ↑ free iron → growth)
        prolonged neutropenia, deferoxamine therapy (iron chelator that mucor can use as siderophore)
  Rhinocerebral (DKA), pulmonary (immunocompromised), cutaneous (trauma)
  Black eschar, necrotic tissue; orbital invasion, cavernous sinus thrombosis
  Dx: tissue biopsy (broad, non-septate or sparsely septate hyphae at right angles)
  Rx: surgical debridement (extensive) + liposomal amphotericin B; correct DKA
  Prognosis: poor (> 50% mortality even with treatment)
```

## Parasites: Protozoa

### Malaria (Plasmodium)

```
SPECIES: P. falciparum (most dangerous), P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi

LIFE CYCLE:
  MOSQUITO (Anopheles female):
    Sporozoites in salivary glands → inoculated into human blood
  ↓
  HUMAN LIVER PHASE (exoerythrocytic):
    Sporozoites → hepatocytes → multiply → merozoites
    P. vivax/ovale: form hypnozoites (dormant in liver) → relapse months-years later
    P. falciparum: no hypnozoites
  ↓
  HUMAN RBC PHASE (erythrocytic):
    Merozoites invade RBCs → ring trophozoite → schizont → 8-32 merozoites → RBC ruptures
    Release → fever spike (pyrogens: GPI, hemozoin) + more RBC invasion
    P. falciparum: ~48-hour cycle (tertian) → continuous high fever in heavy infection
    P. malariae: 72-hour cycle (quartan)
  ↓
  GAMETOCYTES form (some merozoites) → ingested by mosquito → sexual cycle → sporozoites

PATHOGENESIS (P. falciparum — severe malaria):
  PfEMP1 (P. falciparum erythrocyte membrane protein 1): cytoadherence molecule
  → infected RBCs bind endothelium (ICAM-1, CD36) → sequestration
  → Cerebral malaria: microvasculature blockage → coma, seizures
  → Severe anemia: RBC lysis + dyserythropoiesis
  → Hypoglycemia: parasite consumes glucose + quinine-stimulated insulin
  → "Blackwater fever": massive hemolysis → hemoglobinuria → dark urine → AKI

PROTECTIVE FACTORS:
  Sickle cell trait (HbAS): ↓ P. falciparum severity (RBCs sickle under hypoxia → parasite killed)
  Duffy antigen negative (common in West Africa): no receptor for P. vivax → natural resistance
  G6PD deficiency: some protection; BUT anti-malarials (primaquine, dapsone) → hemolysis

TREATMENT:
  Non-falciparum: chloroquine (most still sensitive)
  Falciparum:
    Uncomplicated: artemisinin combination therapy (ACT): artemether-lumefantrine, AS-AQ
    Severe (IV): artesunate (IV) — replaces quinine
  Vivax/ovale with hypnozoites: primaquine (or tafenoquine) to eliminate liver stage
    (check G6PD status first — primaquine causes hemolysis in G6PD deficiency)
  Resistance: chloroquine resistance widespread; artemisinin partial resistance emerging (SE Asia)

PREVENTION: ABCD — Awareness, Bite prevention, Chemoprophylaxis, Diagnosis and treatment
```

### Trypanosomes

```
AFRICAN TRYPANOSOMIASIS (T. brucei gambiense/rhodesiense):
  Vector: Tsetse fly (Glossina)
  Bloodstream: VSG (variant surface glycoprotein) antigenic variation
    → immune system makes Ab → trypanosomes switch VSG → escape → waves of parasitemia
  Stages:
    Stage 1: hemolymphatic — fever, lymphadenopathy (Winterbottom's sign: posterior cervical nodes)
    Stage 2: CNS invasion → sleeping sickness — personality changes, somnolence, coma, death
  Dx: blood film, CSF (stage 2)
  Rx: Stage 1: pentamidine (T.b. gambiense), suramin (T.b. rhodesiense)
      Stage 2: melarsoprol (arsenic-based, toxic), eflornithine (T.b. gambiense), NECT protocol

AMERICAN TRYPANOSOMIASIS (T. cruzi — CHAGAS DISEASE):
  Vector: Reduviid bug (triatomine/"kissing bug") — defecates near bite site → scratched in
  Acute: Romaña's sign (unilateral painless periorbital edema, parasite entered through conjunctiva)
         chagoma (skin nodule), febrile illness, myocarditis
  Chronic (10–30 years later): Cardiomegaly (dilated CM, arrhythmias, heart block), megaesophagus, megacolon
  Mechanism: parasite + autoimmune reaction → destruction of cardiac and enteric neurons (myenteric plexus)
  Dx: blood smear (acute); serology (chronic) — 2 assays required
  Rx: benznidazole or nifurtimox (effective in acute, less so in chronic)
```

### Other Important Protozoa

```
LEISHMANIA:
  Vector: Sandfly (Phlebotomus)
  Forms: Promastigote (flagellated, sandfly) → Amastigote (intracellular, no flagellum, in macrophages)
  Disease spectrum:
    Cutaneous: L. major (Old World): painless ulcer with raised borders ("oriental sore")
    Mucocutaneous: L. braziliensis: destructive lesions of nose/mouth ("espundia")
    Visceral (kala-azar): L. donovani: fever, massive hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia, hypergammaglobulinemia
    Untreated visceral → fatal; immunocompromised (AIDS) → reactivation
  Dx: amastigotes on bone marrow/splenic aspirate; rK39 rapid test (VL); PCR
  Rx: liposomal amphotericin B (VL); pentavalent antimonials; miltefosine (oral)

TOXOPLASMA GONDII:
  Definitive host: cats (sexual cycle in intestine) → oocysts in feces → soil contamination
  Intermediate hosts: virtually all warm-blooded animals (humans — accidental)
  Transmission: ingestion of oocysts (cat litter, unwashed produce) or tissue cysts (undercooked meat, esp. pork/lamb)
  Congenital infection (primary maternal infection during pregnancy):
    Classic triad: chorioretinitis, hydrocephalus, intracranial calcifications
  Immunocompetent: usually asymptomatic; monolike illness
  AIDS/immunocompromised (CD4 < 100): reactivation → RING-ENHANCING brain lesions (multiple, bilateral)
    → toxoplasmic encephalitis; headache, focal deficits, seizures
    Dx: empirical treatment trial vs brain biopsy; serology (IgG + radiologic pattern)
  Rx: pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid; prophylaxis (TMP-SMX) in AIDS CD4 < 100

GIARDIA LAMBLIA:
  Fecal-oral; cysts in contaminated water (mountaineers' disease, daycare)
  Trophozoites in small intestine → malabsorption (no invasion, no tissue damage)
  "Greasy, foul-smelling, floating stools" — fat malabsorption
  Dx: stool O&P (cysts); stool antigen test (sensitive)
  Rx: metronidazole or tinidazole

CRYPTOSPORIDIUM PARVUM:
  Fecal-oral; oocysts extremely chlorine-resistant (Milwaukee 1993: 400,000 cases)
  Self-limited in immunocompetent; severe/chronic diarrhea in AIDS (CD4 < 200)
  Dx: modified acid-fast stain (oocysts stain red/pink), antigen test
  Rx: nitazoxanide (immunocompetent); supportive; no reliable Rx in AIDS without immune reconstitution
```

## Parasites: Helminths

### Nematodes (Roundworms)

| Organism | Transmission | Pathogenesis | Key features |
|----------|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Ascaris lumbricoides | Fecal-oral (eggs → soil → food) | Migration through lung (Löffler's syndrome: eosinophilia + pulmonary infiltrates) | Large roundworm; intestinal obstruction in heavy infections |
| Hookworm (Ancylostoma, Necator) | Skin penetration (walking barefoot) | Iron deficiency anemia (blood-suckers); larvae cause cutaneous larva migrans | "Ground itch" entry; chronic anemia in children |
| Strongyloides stercoralis | Skin penetration | Autoinfection cycle — can persist decades; hyperinfection in immunocompromised (carries gut bacteria → sepsis) | Only worm with autoinfection; ivermectin treatment |
| Trichinella spiralis | Undercooked meat (pork, wild game) | Larvae encyst in skeletal muscle | Periorbital edema, myalgia, eosinophilia, splinter hemorrhages |
| Filaria (Wuchereria, Brugia) | Mosquito bite (microfilariae) | Lymphatic blockage → lymphedema → elephantiasis | Microfilariae in blood (nocturnal periodicity); DEC + albendazole; doxycycline kills Wolbachia |
| Onchocerca volvulus | Black fly (Simulium) | Microfilariae in skin/eye → immune response → river blindness | Second leading infectious cause of blindness; ivermectin mass drug administration |

### Trematodes (Flukes)

```
SCHISTOSOMA:
  Species: S. mansoni (portal/mesenteric veins), S. japonicum (same), S. haematobium (bladder veins)
  Life cycle: Cercaria in freshwater → skin penetration → adult worm in blood vessel
  Eggs lodge in tissue → granuloma → FIBROSIS
    S. mansoni/japonicum: liver fibrosis → portal hypertension → varices (pipe-stem fibrosis)
    S. haematobium: bladder → hematuria → SQUAMOUS CELL CARCINOMA of bladder (Africa)
  Dx: eggs in stool (S. mansoni: lateral spine; S. japonicum: no spine) or urine (S. haematobium: terminal spine)
  Rx: praziquantel (kills adult worms)

LIVER FLUKES (Clonorchis, Fasciola):
  C. sinensis: raw freshwater fish; bile ducts → cholangitis + cholangiocarcinoma
  Fasciola hepatica: watercress/aquatic plants (metacercariae) → liver parenchyma → bile ducts; eosinophilia
  Rx: praziquantel (Clonorchis); triclabendazole (Fasciola — praziquantel not effective)
```

### Cestodes (Tapeworms)

```
TAENIA SOLIUM (Pork tapeworm):
  TWO DIFFERENT DISEASES:
  1. Taeniasis (intestinal): ingest CYSTICERCI in undercooked pork
     → adult tapeworm in intestine; usually asymptomatic
  2. NEUROCYSTICERCOSIS (NCC): ingest EGGS (fecal-oral — self-infection or contaminated food)
     → larvae hatch → invade tissues → cysts in BRAIN
     → MOST COMMON CAUSE OF ACQUIRED EPILEPSY GLOBALLY (especially Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa)
     → Seizures, headache, hydrocephalus
     CT/MRI: calcified or ring-enhancing cysts
     Rx: albendazole (or praziquantel) + corticosteroids (prevent inflammatory reaction as cysts die)
         AEDs for seizure control

ECHINOCOCCUS GRANULOSUS (Dog tapeworm — Hydatid disease):
  Definitive host: dogs (tapeworm in gut) → eggs in feces → soil
  Intermediate host: sheep/cattle/humans (larvae → HYDATID CYSTS in liver > lung)
  Cysts: grow slowly (months-years); contain daughter cysts + protoscolices + hydatid sand
  Rupture risk: anaphylaxis (spilled hydatid fluid is highly allergenic) + seeding
  Dx: imaging (PAIR = Puncture, Aspiration, Injection, Re-aspiration only in expert hands); serology
  Rx: albendazole (not curative alone) + surgical removal; PAIR for selected cases
```

## Prions

### The Paradigm Shift

```
CENTRAL DOGMA OF INFECTION: DNA → RNA → Protein → Disease
PRION EXCEPTION: Protein → Disease (no nucleic acid)

PrP (prion protein): normal cellular protein, predominantly α-helical, GPI-anchored
  PrPᶜ: cellular form (normal) — exact function unclear (copper binding? synaptic?)
  PrPˢᶜ: scrapie form (abnormal) — misfolds into β-sheet-rich conformation
    → infectious agent → converts PrPᶜ to PrPˢᶜ by templated misfolding
    → autocatalytic amplification → accumulates → protease-resistant → insoluble

MECHANISM OF NEURODEGENERATION:
  PrPˢᶜ accumulates in neurons → neurotoxic (mechanism still debated)
  → Vacuolation of neurons ("spongiform change") → neuron death → gliosis
  → No amyloid plaques initially; some diseases develop "PrP amyloid plaques"
  → INVARIABLY FATAL — no treatment alters course
```

### Human Prion Diseases

| Disease | Epidemiology | Genetic form | Acquired form |
|---------|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (sCJD) | 1–2 per million/year; 85% sporadic | gCJD (PRNP mutations: E200K, V210I, D178N) | iCJD: dura/corneal grafts, cadaveric GH; vCJD (BSE-contaminated beef) |
| Variant CJD (vCJD) | UK/France — BSE epidemic 1990s | No | Bovine prion (BSE) from infected beef |
| Gerstmann-Sträussler-Scheinker (GSS) | Rare, familial | PRNP P102L | — |
| Fatal familial insomnia (FFI) | Very rare, familial | PRNP D178N (same mutation as some CJD depending on codon 129 polymorphism) | Sporadic form (sFI) exists |
| Kuru | Fore people (Papua New Guinea) | — | Cannibalism (mortuary feasts, ingesting brain tissue) — now extinct |

### Clinical Features of sCJD

```
RAPIDLY PROGRESSIVE DEMENTIA (weeks-months):
  Cognitive decline, personality change, visual disturbances
  Myoclonus (characteristic — stimulus-sensitive jerking)
  Cerebellar ataxia
  Cortical blindness (Heidenhain variant)
  Akinetic mutism → death (typically within 1 year)

DIAGNOSTIC MARKERS:
  MRI: restricted diffusion ("cortical ribboning") on DWI — cortex + basal ganglia/thalamus
  EEG: periodic sharp wave complexes (classic; late finding)
  CSF: 14-3-3 protein (released from dying neurons — sensitive, not specific)
       RT-QuIC (real-time quaking-induced conversion): prion seeding assay — highly sensitive/specific
  Brain biopsy (rarely needed): spongiform change, gliosis, PrPˢᶜ immunohistochemistry
  Definitive: neuropathology at autopsy

VARIAN CJD DIFFERENCES:
  Younger age (median ~28 years vs ~65 sCJD)
  Psychiatric prodrome → progressive neurological disease
  "Pulvinar sign" on MRI (thalamic hyperintensity)
  Duration: ~14 months (longer than sCJD)
  Tonsil biopsy: PrPˢᶜ detectable (not in sCJD)

INFECTION CONTROL:
  PrPˢᶜ extremely resistant to standard sterilization (autoclave at 134°C + 1 hr required)
  Not destroyed by UV, ionizing radiation, formaldehyde, standard autoclaving
  Surgical instruments that contact CNS of CJD patients must be destroyed or special decontamination
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why are antifungals so limited? | Fungi are eukaryotes — most drug targets are shared with human cells. Only ergosterol (vs cholesterol) and β-1,3-glucan are sufficiently different. |
| Aspergillus vs Mucor: how to distinguish? | Both angioinvasive. Aspergillus: thin, septate hyphae at 45°; Mucor: broad, sparsely septate, right angles (90°). DKA favors Mucor. |
| Malaria relapse vs recrudescence? | Relapse: dormant hypnozoites (P. vivax/ovale) reactivate months later; need primaquine to prevent. Recrudescence: subpatent parasitemia that re-expands — P. falciparum/malariae. |
| Why does AIDS predispose to Toxoplasma encephalitis specifically? | Primary infection seropositivity in many adults; tissue cysts in brain kept dormant by CD4+ T cells; CD4 < 100 → T cell control lost → reactivation |
| Can you cure prion disease? | No. No treatment halts disease progression. Supportive care only. |
| What makes PrPˢᶜ infectious? | It acts as a template that converts normal PrPᶜ to PrPˢᶜ by direct contact — protein-induced protein misfolding, autocatalytic amplification |

## Common Confusion Points

**Dermatophytes vs Candida on skin**
Both cause skin infections but different organisms and treatments. Dermatophytes (tinea): only infect keratinized tissue (skin/hair/nails), KOH shows hyphae without budding. Candida: budding yeast + pseudohyphae on KOH; infects moist intertriginous areas; responds to azoles or nystatin topically.

**Malaria's fever pattern is often irregular in P. falciparum**
Textbooks say "tertian" (48h) or "quartan" (72h) periodicity. In practice, P. falciparum often produces daily or irregular fevers because multiple cohorts of parasites are at different stages. Don't wait for classic periodicity to diagnose.

**Prion protein is encoded by the host — it's not foreign**
PRNP gene is on chromosome 20 in humans. PrPˢᶜ is a conformational variant of the host's own protein. This is why there is no immune response — it's self. The codon 129 polymorphism (Met/Val) in PRNP modifies disease risk: homozygosity (MM or VV) → greater susceptibility to prion diseases.

**Neurocysticercosis treatment needs steroids**
Paradox: killing the cysts with albendazole triggers an inflammatory response as cysts die → can worsen seizures and edema transiently. Corticosteroids co-administered to blunt this. Treatment of NCC is individualized based on cyst number, viability, and location.
