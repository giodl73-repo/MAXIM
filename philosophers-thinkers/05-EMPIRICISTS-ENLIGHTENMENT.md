# The Empiricists and the Enlightenment — Locke, Berkeley, Hume, Rousseau

## The Big Picture

The Empiricists reject the Rationalist starting point. There are no innate ideas; the mind at birth is a tabula rasa (blank slate). All knowledge derives from experience — from sensory impressions. This isn't just epistemology; it's a political and moral program: if ideas come from experience, then human nature is malleable, education matters, and authority cannot appeal to innate natural hierarchy.

## John Locke (1632–1704)

Locke is the bridge from philosophy to politics — his epistemology directly grounds his theory of natural rights and limited government.

### Against Innate Ideas

```
  LOCKE'S CHALLENGE TO RATIONALISM

  Rationalists claim: certain ideas (God, substance, identity)
  are innate — built into the mind prior to all experience.

  Locke's argument (Essay Concerning Human Understanding, 1689):
  1. If ideas were innate, everyone would have them.
  2. Children, idiots, and savages don't have the supposed innate ideas.
  3. Cross-cultural variation shows enormous disagreement on
     supposedly innate moral and metaphysical truths.
  4. Therefore: no innate ideas.

  Instead: the mind is a tabula rasa.
  All ideas come from two sources:
  ─ SENSATION: what the senses report (red, hot, round)
  ─ REFLECTION: the mind noticing its own operations
                (thinking, willing, doubting)
```

### Simple and Complex Ideas

```
  SIMPLE IDEAS ────── Given directly by sensation or reflection.
  (cannot be further  Red, square, pain, hardness. These are the
   analyzed)          ultimate epistemic atoms.

  COMPLEX IDEAS ───── Combinations of simple ideas by the mind:
                     Modes (triangle, gratitude, murder)
                     Substances (man, horse, lead)
                     Relations (larger, father, cause)

  Substance: Locke is skeptical. We group simple ideas together
  and assume there is a "substratum" holding them, but this
  substratum is an "I know not what" — a placeholder, not knowledge.
```

### Primary and Secondary Qualities

```
  PRIMARY QUALITIES ──── Inseparable from the object itself.
  (in the object)        Extension, solidity, motion, number, figure.
                         Our ideas of these resemble how the object is.

  SECONDARY QUALITIES ── Powers in the object to produce ideas in us.
  (not in the object)    Color, smell, taste, sound, heat.
                         These are not intrinsic to the object —
                         they depend on the perceiver's senses.
```

### Political Philosophy — Natural Rights and Limited Government

Locke's Two Treatises of Government (1689) establishes the theoretical foundation for liberal democracy:

```
  STATE OF NATURE:
  Before political society. People are:
  ─ Free and equal (governed by natural law, accessible to reason)
  ─ Have natural rights: life, liberty, and property
  ─ Can punish violators of natural law themselves

  But: inconveniences of state of nature (partial judgment,
  lack of enforcement). People agree to a social contract:

  SOCIAL CONTRACT:
  Individuals transfer enforcement rights to a government.
  Government's purpose: protect natural rights.
  Government's legitimacy: derives from consent of the governed.

  LIMITS: If government violates natural rights (life, liberty, property)
  systematically, the people have a right of revolution.
  (This passage directly influenced Jefferson's Declaration of Independence)
```

This is revolutionary: Locke doesn't argue for democracy per se, but for government by consent, separation of powers, and the right to overthrow tyranny. The American founders read Locke as their philosophical foundation.

## George Berkeley (1685–1753)

Berkeley takes Locke's empiricism one step further and arrives at a counterintuitive conclusion: matter doesn't exist.

### Immaterialism

```
  BERKELEY'S ARGUMENT

  Locke: We know secondary qualities are mind-dependent (color, etc.)
  Berkeley: Why not primary qualities too? Try to conceive of
            extension that is not perceived by any mind.
            You can't — you're imagining it, which is perceiving it.

  Esse est percipi: "To be is to be perceived."

  PHYSICAL OBJECTS are collections of ideas in minds.
  There is no mind-independent matter "underlying" these ideas.

  But: objects don't disappear when we stop looking?
  Answer: God perceives everything continuously.
  Physical objects are ideas in God's mind.

  Implication: this makes God philosophically necessary —
  without a perceiving God, the physical world would blink
  in and out of existence.

  Dr. Johnson's "refutation": kicked a stone and said
  "I refute it thus!" Berkeley's point: the sensation of
  resistance is itself an idea in your mind.
```

**Why Berkeley?** He's important not because immaterialism is true but because:
1. He forces the question: what is the evidence for matter beyond our perceptions?
2. His argument was later influence on phenomenalism (Russell, early Logical Positivists)
3. Hume takes his skepticism even further, turning it on Berkeley too

## David Hume (1711–1776)

Hume is the most rigorous of the empiricists and reaches the most radical conclusions. Kant said Hume "awakened him from his dogmatic slumber." Hume is, in the analytic tradition, probably the single most influential British philosopher.

### The Treatise and the Enquiries

Key works: A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–1740, published anonymously, "fell dead-born from the press"); later Enquiries (1748, 1751) — more polished recasting of the Treatise's main arguments.

### Impressions and Ideas

```
  HUME'S EPISTEMOLOGY

  All mental content divides into:
  ─ IMPRESSIONS: vivid, immediate sensory data (seeing red)
  ─ IDEAS: faint copies of impressions (remembering red)

  ALL ideas derive from impressions.
  If you have a concept you can't trace to any impression,
  it's meaningless.

  FORK: All meaningful claims are either:
  ─ Relations of Ideas: necessarily true by logic/definition
                        (mathematical truths, tautologies)
  ─ Matters of Fact: contingently true by experience
                     (observable facts about the world)

  Hume's fork (ancestor of the logical positivists' verificationism):
  "If we take in our hand any volume of divinity or school
  metaphysics, let us ask: Does it contain any abstract reasoning
  concerning quantity or number? No. Does it contain any
  experimental reasoning concerning matter of fact? No.
  Commit it then to the flames."
```

### The Problem of Causation

This is Hume's most famous and lasting contribution:

```
  WHAT DO WE ACTUALLY OBSERVE WHEN WE SEE CAUSATION?

  Example: billiard ball A hits billiard ball B. B moves.
  We say A caused B to move.

  What we actually observed:
  1. A moving (event 1)
  2. Spatial contiguity (A and B touched)
  3. Temporal succession (B moved AFTER A)
  4. ...and that's it.

  We did NOT observe:
  ─ Necessary connection between A's motion and B's motion
  ─ Any "power" transferring from A to B
  ─ Any reason why B had to move

  WHERE does the idea of necessity come from?

  Hume's answer: HABIT / CUSTOM.
  After observing constant conjunction (A-events followed by
  B-events, repeatedly), the mind forms an expectation.
  Causation = constant conjunction + psychological expectation.
  There is no logical necessity — only psychological compulsion.

  This is devastating:
  ─ The necessity we attribute to causes is not in the world.
  ─ It is a projection of our habits onto experience.
```

### The Problem of Induction

Building on the problem of causation:

```
  THE PROBLEM OF INDUCTION

  Premise: The sun has risen every day in recorded history.
  Conclusion: The sun will rise tomorrow.

  What justifies this inference?

  Option 1: It's logically necessary.
  ─ No. There is no contradiction in the sun not rising tomorrow.

  Option 2: Experience tells us the future will resemble the past.
  ─ But that's exactly what we're trying to justify.
  ─ Circular: "Induction works because in the past, induction worked."

  Hume's conclusion:
  Induction cannot be rationally justified.
  We use it because custom and habit compel us to —
  not because reason demands it.

  This is the "problem of induction" — still unresolved.
  Karl Popper responded with falsificationism:
  we don't need induction; we use deduction (if theory T
  is false, observation O won't occur; O doesn't occur →
  T is false). Science proceeds by falsification, not
  confirmation. Bayesian epistemologists offer a different
  response: induction is probabilistic updating, not
  deductive justification.
```

### Personal Identity

Hume looks for the "self" and finds only a bundle of perceptions:

```
  "When I enter most intimately into what I call myself, I always
  stumble on some particular perception or other — heat or cold,
  light or shade, love or hatred, pain or pleasure. I never catch
  myself at any time without a perception, and can never observe
  anything but the perception."

  The self is a BUNDLE or collection of perceptions — not a
  continuing substance that has experiences.

  This "bundle theory" of personal identity is the empiricist
  counterpart to Buddhist anattā (no-self) and anticipates
  modern discussions in philosophy of mind (Parfit, Reasons and Persons).
```

### Hume on Morality — "Is" and "Ought"

Hume's guillotine: you cannot derive an "ought" from an "is":

```
  From purely descriptive premises ("Jones is suffering"),
  you cannot logically derive normative conclusions
  ("You ought to help Jones") without some moral premise
  in between.

  Moral judgments are not factual judgments.
  They express sentiments — they are "calm passions."
  "Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions,
  and can never pretend to any other office than to serve
  and obey them."
```

This is the is/ought gap — still fundamental to meta-ethics.

## Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)

Rousseau is the Enlightenment's internal critic — he uses Enlightenment tools (natural reason, individual rights) to argue against the Enlightenment's celebration of civilization and progress.

### The Noble Savage — Misread

```
  COMMON MISREAD: "Rousseau thought primitive people are noble savages."

  WHAT HE ACTUALLY ARGUED:
  Natural man (pre-social) is not sophisticated or noble —
  he is simply amoral, self-sufficient, and unreflective.
  He lacks the vices OF civilization but also its virtues.

  Civilization corrupts by creating:
  ─ Amour-propre (self-love defined by comparison with others)
  ─ Inequality (property creates the wealthy/poor distinction)
  ─ Artificial needs (luxury, status)
  ─ Social performance over authentic self

  "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains."
  (Opening of The Social Contract — not about primitive life
   but about how political society has gone wrong)
```

### The General Will

```
  ROUSSEAU'S POLITICAL THEORY

  SOCIAL CONTRACT: Individuals give up natural liberty for civil liberty
  governed by the GENERAL WILL (volonté générale).

  GENERAL WILL vs. WILL OF ALL:
  Will of all = sum of private interests (what everyone wants individually)
  General will = what is genuinely good for the community as a whole

  These can conflict. The general will may override individual preference.

  Danger: Rousseau says society may "force people to be free" —
  force them to obey the general will even against their private desires.
  This was used to justify Jacobin terror (Robespierre cited Rousseau).
  Benjamin Constant calls this the "liberty of the ancients" vs.
  "liberty of the moderns" — collective self-rule vs. individual rights.
```

### Emile — Education and Natural Development

Rousseau's educational treatise argues that children should be allowed to develop naturally rather than forced into adult molds early. The teacher should arrange the environment so that children discover things for themselves. This is the philosophical origin of progressive education (Dewey, Piaget, Montessori's indirect influences).

## Common Confusion Points

**Locke invented the blank slate** — The tabula rasa metaphor was used before Locke (by Avicenna, and the Aristotelian tradition). Locke's contribution is the systematic argument against Descartes' innate ideas and the account of how ideas are built from simple sensory elements.

**Berkeley is obviously wrong** — The objection (kick the stone) misses the target. Berkeley doesn't deny that you feel resistance — he denies that there is a mind-independent material substance causing that feeling. Your feeling of resistance is real; what he denies is the theoretical posit of matter behind it. This is not obviously wrong — it's a serious position in philosophy of perception.

**Hume is a skeptic who denies everything** — Hume is a mitigated skeptic. He thinks dogmatic metaphysics is unjustifiable, but he doesn't think we should stop believing in causation or induction. We can't help but believe in them; reason can't refute them (it also can't support them). We follow "custom and nature" — not because it's rationally validated but because we're the kind of creatures who do.

**Rousseau as proto-totalitarian** — Isaiah Berlin and J.L. Talmon argued that Rousseau's general will concept leads logically to totalitarianism (forcing people to be free). Jacob Talmon called it "totalitarian democracy." The textual basis is real — Rousseau does say the general will can override individual will. But Rousseau also insisted on direct democracy and the impossibility of representation, which limited its scope. The Jacobin use of Rousseau is contested.
