## The Big Picture

## Descartes — The Founder of Modern Philosophy

**René Descartes (1596–1650)** is regularly called the founder of modern philosophy. That's because he started fresh: instead of commenting on Aristotle, he asked what he could know with certainty, independently of tradition and authority.

### The Method of Doubt

```
  DESCARTES' METHOD (Meditations on First Philosophy, 1641)

  Step 1: Doubt anything that can be doubted
           ─ Senses deceive me (bent stick in water)
           ─ I might be dreaming (how do I know I'm not?)
           ─ An evil demon might deceive me about 2+2=4

  Step 2: What remains after maximal doubt?
           ─ Even if an evil demon deceives me about everything,
             there must be something being deceived.
           ─ If I think, I cannot doubt that I think.

  Step 3: Cogito ergo sum
           "I think, therefore I am"
           ─ Not a syllogism (not: "all thinking things exist,
             I think, therefore I exist")
           ─ A direct intuition: in the act of doubting,
             I discover my own undeniable existence as a
             thinking thing.
```

The cogito is the one certainty that survives the method of doubt. From here Descartes reconstructs the rest of knowledge.

### Mind-Body Dualism

```
  CARTESIAN DUALISM

  RES COGITANS (thinking substance)    RES EXTENSA (extended substance)
  ──────────────────────────────────   ──────────────────────────────────
  Mind / soul                          Body / matter
  Unextended, not in space             Extended, in space
  Indivisible                          Divisible
  Private, directly known              Public, known via senses
  Free (not mechanically determined)   Mechanically determined
  Immortal (substance)                 Mortal (configuration)

  These are two entirely different kinds of substance.
  You are your mind. You have a body.
```

**The interaction problem**: If mind and body are completely different substances, how do they interact? When I decide to move my hand (mental event → physical event), and when pain in my hand makes me feel hurt (physical event → mental event) — how does this work? Descartes said they interact through the pineal gland. This was obviously unsatisfactory and drove the next two centuries of philosophy.

### God as Guarantor

Descartes escapes the method of doubt by arguing for God's existence from the idea of God:

```
  ONTOLOGICAL ARGUMENT (Descartes version)

  I have an idea of a supremely perfect being.
  Existence is a perfection.
  A supremely perfect being must have all perfections.
  Therefore: a supremely perfect being must exist.

  Also: CAUSAL ARGUMENT
  I have the idea of an infinite, perfect being.
  This idea must have a cause equal to or greater than its content.
  I am finite and imperfect — I couldn't have produced this idea.
  Therefore: God (infinite, perfect) must have caused this idea.
```

God is then used to guarantee that clear and distinct perceptions are reliable — the method of systematic doubt terminates in re-grounding knowledge through divine guarantee. Critics note this is circular: God guarantees clarity, but the argument for God relies on clear perception (the "Cartesian Circle").

### The Mechanical Universe

Descartes' physics: the physical world is pure mechanism. Animals are automata — machines. The body is a machine. Only humans have minds (Descartes draws this line in a way that remains contested: at what point does mechanism give way to mind?). This laid the groundwork for mechanistic science and the Enlightenment's conception of nature as a machine.

## Spinoza — The Most Systematic

**Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677)** was excommunicated from the Amsterdam Jewish community at age 23 with one of the harshest herem ever issued — no reason was specified, and it has never been lifted. He ground lenses for a living, corresponded with Leibniz, and wrote the most geometrically rigorous philosophical work in the Western tradition.

### The Ethics — Geometric Method

The Ethics (published posthumously, 1677) is written like a geometry textbook: definitions → axioms → propositions → proofs → corollaries → scholia. This is not a stylistic choice — Spinoza believed that philosophical truth has the same logical structure as mathematical truth.

### Substance Monism

```
  SPINOZA'S ONTOLOGY

  Descartes: two substances (mind + matter)
  Spinoza:   ONE substance = God = Nature (Deus sive Natura)

  SUBSTANCE: what exists in itself and is conceived through itself
  ─ Only one thing fits this definition: something that requires
    nothing outside itself to exist.
  ─ That thing is infinite, self-caused, necessarily existing.
  ─ There can only be one substance (two substances couldn't be
    truly independent — they'd limit each other).
  ─ Call it God. Call it Nature. Same referent.

  ATTRIBUTES: the aspects under which substance is conceived
  ─ We know two: Thought and Extension (Descartes' two substances)
  ─ Spinoza: these are TWO ATTRIBUTES of the ONE substance
  ─ God/Nature has infinitely many attributes, of which we know two

  MODES: specific configurations of substance (individual things)
  ─ You, me, this stone — modes of God/Nature
  ─ Not separate substances; modifications of the one substance
```

This is **substance monism** — the strongest possible response to Descartes' interaction problem. If there are not two substances but one, there is no interaction problem. Mind and matter are two descriptions (attributes) of the same underlying reality.

### Spinoza on Freedom and Determinism

```
  DETERMINISM: Everything that exists follows necessarily from
  the nature of God/Nature. There is no contingency in Nature —
  if things had been different, the nature of God would have been
  different. "Things could not have been produced by God in any
  manner or in any order different from that which has in fact occurred."

  FREEDOM: Not the ability to do otherwise (libertarian free will)
  but acting from one's own nature rather than external compulsion.
  ─ A stone is not free: it moves only by external force.
  ─ A person who acts from passion is not free: driven by external
    causes they don't understand.
  ─ A person who acts from adequate ideas is free: their actions
    follow from their own rational nature, fully understood.

  Freedom = activity determined by your own rational nature.
  Slavery = passivity determined by external causes you don't grasp.
```

### The Intellectual Love of God

The highest human good is what Spinoza calls the "intellectual love of God" (amor intellectualis Dei) — understanding one's place within the whole of Nature/God. This is not personal love of a personal deity but a cognitive-emotional state of seeing things sub specie aeternitatis ("under the aspect of eternity") — understanding the necessary structure of all things.

This is a secular mysticism: the experience of understanding the universe as a necessary whole produces something like what the religious tradition calls beatitude.

### Ethics Without Teleology

Spinoza's ethics is non-teleological: there is no cosmic purpose, no design, no end toward which nature aims. Good and evil are not features of nature — they are relative to human desires and interests. "Nothing comes to pass in nature which can be set down to a flaw therein; for nature is always the same."

This is highly influential in naturalistic ethics and the philosophy of mind (Spinoza is regularly cited as a precursor to embodied cognition and affect theory).

## Leibniz — Monads, God, and the Best of All Possible Worlds

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716)** is the other giant of 17th-century philosophy. He co-discovered calculus (with Newton, in a bitter priority dispute), developed symbolic logic, worked on combinatorics, invented the calculating machine, and did all this while working as a court historian and diplomat. His published philosophy is small relative to his manuscripts — he never wrote a systematic summa.

### The Problem of Individuation

What makes an individual thing the individual it is? Descartes: particular location in space and time. Spinoza: mode of substance. Leibniz: its complete individual concept.

```
  LEIBNIZ'S PRINCIPLE OF INDIVIDUAL SUBSTANCE

  Every individual substance has a "complete concept" —
  an infinite set of predicates that would be true of it
  and no other individual.

  Example: Caesar's complete concept includes:
  "crossed the Rubicon," "conquered Gaul," "was assassinated
  in 44 BCE," "loved Calpurnia," every event in his life,
  every relation he ever had.

  If any predicate were different, it wouldn't be Caesar —
  it would be a different possible individual.

  Implication: every individual is unique and numerically distinct
  because their complete concepts are distinct.
  → Principle of the Identity of Indiscernibles:
    No two things can have all the same properties.
    (If they did, they'd be the same thing.)
```

### The Monadology

```
  LEIBNIZIAN ONTOLOGY: MONADS

  The world consists of infinitely many simple, unextended,
  non-interacting substances called MONADS.

  Properties of monads:
  ─ No parts (simple, indivisible)
  ─ No "windows" — they cannot be affected by other monads
  ─ Each contains within itself a "perception" of the whole universe
    (but with varying degrees of clarity)
  ─ Each "unfolds" its complete individual concept over time

  PRE-ESTABLISHED HARMONY:
  If monads don't interact, how does it look like they do?
  ─ God set up each monad at creation to unfold in perfect
    coordination with all other monads.
  ─ Like two clocks set to the same time: they agree perfectly
    but neither causes the other.
  ─ Mind and body don't interact (no interaction problem!);
    God synchronized them from the beginning.

  This seems like an ad hoc solution. Leibniz thought it was
  more elegant than either occasionalism (God intervenes each
  moment) or Spinoza's monism.
```

### The Best of All Possible Worlds

God is omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good. Before creating, God considered all possible worlds — all internally consistent arrangements of possible entities. God then created the world that is best — the one with maximum variety and perfection.

This is **leibnizian optimism** — the claim that this world, despite its evils, is the best that could exist. Evil exists because:
1. **Metaphysical evil** (imperfection) — every created thing is necessarily less perfect than God
2. **Physical evil** (suffering) — necessary for the best overall
3. **Moral evil** (sin) — God permits it because the world with free will is better than one without

Voltaire savaged this position in Candide (1759) after the Lisbon earthquake killed 60,000 people.

### Logic and Symbolic Calculus

Leibniz dreamed of a universal logical language (characteristica universalis) and a calculus of reasoning (calculus ratiocinator) that would mechanically resolve all disputes: "Let us calculate." This is the direct ancestor of:
- Boolean algebra
- Frege's Begriffsschrift
- Mathematical logic
- Computer science

His actual logical work (modal logic, concept containment theory of truth) anticipates developments that fully emerge only in the 20th century.

## Common Confusion Points

**Descartes' cogito is not a syllogism** — He doesn't reason: "All thinking things exist; I think; therefore I exist." That would be circular (the major premise already assumes that there are existing things that think). The cogito is a direct intuition: in the act of doubting, I encounter my own existence immediately. The doubt itself is an act of thinking, and thinking requires a thinker.

**The Cartesian Circle** — Descartes uses God to guarantee that clear and distinct perceptions are reliable. But his argument for God's existence relies on clear and distinct perception of the idea of God. Critics note this looks circular. Descartes scholars dispute whether there really is a vicious circle or whether the argument is structured to avoid it.

**Spinoza was not an atheist** — He was accused of atheism by his contemporaries. He consistently argued God exists. His position is that God = Nature: God is not a personal being who acts from purposes or intervenes in history — God is the necessary, infinite, self-caused substance that is identical to the natural universe. This is closer to pantheism than atheism, but equally far from traditional monotheism.

**Leibniz's "best of all possible worlds" is not naive optimism** — Leibniz means it technically: given the constraints of logic and divine goodness, no better world was logically possible while preserving human freedom and the variety of creation. He's not saying suffering doesn't exist or doesn't matter — he's arguing it's necessary for the best achievable whole. The argument may be wrong, but it's not simple-minded.

**Leibniz and Newton** — They discovered calculus independently and simultaneously (Leibniz ~1675, Newton ~1666). The priority dispute was bitter and nationalistic (English vs. German-speaking mathematicians). Leibniz's notation (dy/dx, ∫) is what everyone uses today. Newton's notation (ẋ for derivatives) is mostly obsolete outside physics.
