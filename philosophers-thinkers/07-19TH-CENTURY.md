# 19th-Century Philosophy — Schopenhauer, Marx, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche

## The Big Picture

The 19th century is defined by reactions to Hegel. Hegel's system claimed to show the rational structure of history and reality; his successors asked whether this was wishful thinking or ideological cover. Four major figures, four different revolts:

```
  HEGEL'S SYSTEM
  "History is the development of Spirit toward freedom.
   The actual is the rational."
         │
         ├── SCHOPENHAUER (1788–1860) ─── Inverts Hegel
         │   "Reality is blind, irrational Will.
         │    History is repetitive suffering."
         │
         ├── MARX (1818–1883) ─────────── Materializes Hegel
         │   "History is class struggle, not Spirit.
         │    Material conditions determine consciousness."
         │
         ├── KIERKEGAARD (1813–1855) ──── Subjectifies Hegel
         │   "The individual, not the system.
         │    Existence cannot be captured by abstraction."
         │
         └── NIETZSCHE (1844–1900) ─────── Diagnoses Hegel
             "Hegel's progress is a secular Christian narrative.
              Kill God, revalue all values, will to power."
```

## Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860)

Schopenhauer published The World as Will and Representation in 1818 — the same year as Hegel's Encyclopedia. He despised Hegel, called him a "windbag" and a fraud, and spent most of his life in obscurity while Hegel dominated Berlin. Then Hegel died, Germany's faith in progress faltered, and Schopenhauer was discovered.

### The World as Will and Representation

```
  SCHOPENHAUER'S ONTOLOGY

  REPRESENTATION (Vorstellung):
  ─ The world as it appears to us (phenomenal world)
  ─ Subject-object structure: there's always a knower and a known
  ─ Governed by the Principle of Sufficient Reason:
    everything in experience has a cause/reason/ground
  ─ This is Kant's phenomenal world — Schopenhauer accepts it

  WILL (Wille):
  ─ The thing-in-itself that Kant left unknowable
  ─ Schopenhauer: we know it from the inside — bodily desire,
    appetite, impulse. We ARE will, not just have it.
  ─ Not one will per person — a single, universal, blind,
    striving force that underlies all of reality
  ─ The will in gravity, in plant growth, in animal hunger,
    in human desire — all expressions of one Will

  CONSEQUENCE:
  The Will has no purpose, no goal, no satisfaction.
  It strives blindly, forever. Each satisfaction is temporary;
  desire immediately reasserts itself. Life is:
    desire → momentary satisfaction → new desire → boredom
    or permanent unsatisfied desire → suffering
```

This is the philosophical foundation of Schopenhauer's pessimism: existence is suffering not by accident but by the nature of the Will.

### Escape from the Will

Three routes of partial or total relief:

```
  1. AESTHETIC EXPERIENCE
     In aesthetic contemplation, you temporarily suspend will and
     desire. You become "a pure subject of knowing" — the will is
     silenced. Music is the highest art because it is the Will's
     own self-expression without the world of representation as
     intermediary.

  2. COMPASSION (Mitleid — "suffering-with")
     Ethical goodness consists in recognizing that the sufferer
     before you is the same Will as you. Empathy breaks the
     illusion of separateness. This is the basis of ethics —
     not reason (Kant) but shared suffering.
     Influenced by Buddhism (dukkha, compassion as fundamental virtue).

  3. ASCETICISM / WILL-DENIAL
     The saint who denies bodily desire — through celibacy,
     poverty, fasting — achieves the closest approximation to
     escape from the Will. This is permanent, not temporary.
     Eastern philosophy (Buddhist nirvana, Hindu moksha) as
     philosophical ideals.
```

### Influence

Nietzsche began as Schopenhauer's disciple and then defined himself against him. Wagner set Schopenhauer's ideas to music (Tristan und Isolde). Freud's concept of the unconscious is Schopenhauer's Will translated into psychology. Thomas Hardy's novels embody Schopenhauerian pessimism. The 20th century's existentialist focus on anxiety and meaninglessness has a Schopenhauerian ancestor.

## Karl Marx (1818–1883)

Marx is simultaneously a philosopher, economist, political theorist, and revolutionary. He's hard to place in philosophy because his project is not just to understand the world but to change it. His philosophical contribution is the materialist conception of history and the analysis of capitalism.

### Against Hegel: Materialism

```
  HEGEL: Geist (Spirit/Ideas) determines history.
          Material conditions are secondary — they express
          the development of ideas.

  MARX: "Standing Hegel on his head."
        MATERIAL CONDITIONS (forces and relations of production)
        determine IDEAS (consciousness, ideology, culture, law).

  BASE AND SUPERSTRUCTURE:

  SUPERSTRUCTURE: law, politics, religion, philosophy,
                  morality, art, culture
                  ↑
  ═══════════════════════════════════════════
                  │
  BASE (ECONOMIC): forces of production
                   (technology, labor, resources)
                   + relations of production
                   (property relations, class structure)

  "It is not the consciousness of men that determines their being,
   but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their
   consciousness." (Preface to A Contribution to the Critique of
   Political Economy, 1859)
```

### Historical Materialism

```
  HISTORICAL MATERIALISM: History is driven by contradictions
  between forces of production and relations of production.

  Stages of history:
  Primitive communism
      → Slave societies (ancient world)
      → Feudalism (medieval)
      → Capitalism (modern)
      → Socialism
      → Communism (classless, stateless)

  MECHANISM: Productive forces develop. They eventually clash
  with existing property relations (e.g., capitalist property
  relations constrain further industrial development).
  This tension generates class conflict.
  Resolution: revolution that establishes new property relations.

  This is a theory of structural contradiction, not conspiracy.
  No single person or group is evil — the system has inherent
  tensions that drive historical change.
```

### Class and Exploitation

```
  MARXIST THEORY OF VALUE:

  Labor theory of value (from Ricardo, developed by Marx):
  The value of a commodity is determined by the socially necessary
  labor time required to produce it.

  SURPLUS VALUE:
  Workers sell their labor power (not their labor) for a wage.
  They are paid the value of labor power (cost of subsistence).
  But they produce MORE value than they are paid.
  The difference = surplus value = appropriated by the capitalist.

  EXPLOITATION is not about individual capitalists being greedy.
  It is structural: the wage relation itself is exploitative
  under capitalism.
```

### Alienation

Marx's early concept of alienation (Entfremdung), most explicit in the 1844 Manuscripts:

```
  Under capitalism, the worker is alienated from:
  1. The product of labor (belongs to capitalist, not worker)
  2. The process of labor (controlled by capitalist, not worker)
  3. Fellow humans (competition rather than solidarity)
  4. Their "species-being" (their capacity for free, conscious,
     creative activity — which is what makes humans distinctively human)

  SPECIES-BEING: Humans are unique in being able to make their
  life-activity an object of will and consciousness — to work
  creatively and consciously. Capitalism turns this into
  forced, mechanical, alienated labor.
```

### Ideology

```
  IDEOLOGY (Marx): A system of ideas that:
  1. Represents the ideas of the ruling class as universal
  2. Obscures the material basis of social arrangements
  3. Makes contingent historical arrangements seem natural/eternal

  "The ideas of the ruling class are in every epoch the
   ruling ideas." (German Ideology)

  Example: "Private property is a natural right" seems universal
  but is an ideology that naturalizes and defends existing
  property relations, which are historically contingent.
```

## Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855)

Kierkegaard is the first existentialist. He rejected Hegel's system on the grounds that it could not accommodate the individual: you can describe humanity in the abstract, but what does that tell me about how I — this individual, this existing person — should live?

### The Three Stages of Existence

Kierkegaard describes three "stages" or "spheres" of existence through which a person might move:

```
  AESTHETIC STAGE
  ─ Lives for pleasure, sensation, immediacy, variety
  ─ Avoids boredom through constant novelty
  ─ The seducer in Either/Or; Don Giovanni
  ─ Internally contradictory: pleasure requires novelty,
    novelty exhausts, leads to despair

  ETHICAL STAGE
  ─ Chooses commitment, duty, fidelity to universal norms
  ─ Marriage, vocation, social responsibility
  ─ Judge Wilhelm in Either/Or
  ─ Better, but: universal ethical demands cannot address
    the individual's particular relation to God

  RELIGIOUS STAGE (specifically the "faith" stage)
  ─ The individual stands before God alone
  ─ Requires "teleological suspension of the ethical" —
    Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac violates
    universal ethics but is commanded by God directly
  ─ This cannot be communicated to others or rationally justified
  ─ "Leap of faith" — irrational, passionate commitment

  Each stage involves genuine choice — not logical progression.
  You can be stuck in the aesthetic forever, or choose to move.
```

### Fear and Trembling (1843)

Kierkegaard's analysis of the Akedah (Abraham and Isaac): if Abraham was right to obey God's command to sacrifice Isaac, this can only be because there is a higher category than the universal ethical — the individual's direct relationship to God. This relationship cannot be mediated by ethics or logic; it is radically particular and incommensurable.

This is Kierkegaard's attack on Hegelian ethics (which submerges the individual in universal rationality) and on Kant's ethics (which makes the universal rational demand supreme).

### Despair — The Sickness unto Death

```
  DESPAIR (Fortvivlelse) = the failure to become a self

  The self is "a relation that relates itself to itself"
  — it is not a fixed substance but an ongoing task of
  becoming, achieved through choosing oneself before God.

  Forms of despair:
  ─ Not knowing one is a self (unconscious despair)
  ─ Refusing to be oneself (despair of weakness)
  ─ Insisting on being the self one wills rather than
    the self one is (despair of defiance — "demonic despair")

  Overcoming despair requires relating correctly to the
  Power that establishes you — i.e., God.
  Kierkegaard's psychology anticipates existentialist analysis
  of authenticity (Heidegger) and bad faith (Sartre).
```

### Pseudonymous Authorship

Kierkegaard published most of his major works under pseudonyms (Johannes Climacus, Anti-Climacus, Victor Eremita, Constantine Constantinus). This is a philosophical technique, not a disguise: each pseudonym represents a perspective or stage that the reader must evaluate from the outside, without Kierkegaard directly endorsing it.

## Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)

Nietzsche is the most cited and most misread major philosopher. He wrote aphoristically, provocatively, and self-consciously as a cultural critic and diagnostician.

### The Death of God

```
  THE PARABLE OF THE MADMAN (The Gay Science §125)

  A madman runs into the marketplace: "God is dead!
  God remains dead! And we have killed him!"

  THE MEANING:
  Nietzsche is not asserting atheism as a logical argument.
  He is diagnosing a cultural event: European culture had been
  structured around Christian metaphysics and morality;
  that structure is now collapsing (through science, critique,
  Enlightenment skepticism).

  "God is dead" means: the framework of values that Christianity
  provided — objective moral truth, afterlife, cosmic justice —
  can no longer be believed with conviction.

  THE CRISIS:
  If God is dead (Christian metaphysics has collapsed),
  what provides:
  ─ The basis for values?
  ─ The sense of purpose?
  ─ The answer to meaninglessness?

  Without a response: nihilism.
```

### Slave and Master Morality

On the Genealogy of Morals (1887) — a historical-psychological account of where morality comes from:

```
  MASTER MORALITY (aristocratic)
  ─ Primary: "I am good" (affirmation)
  ─ Secondary: "what is unlike me is bad" (negation)
  ─ Values: power, strength, nobility, vitality, excellence

  SLAVE MORALITY (reactive, resentment-based)
  ─ Primary: "they are evil" (reactive negation of the powerful)
  ─ Secondary: "therefore we are good" (affirmation through negation)
  ─ Values: humility, pity, weakness as virtue, equality

  RESSENTIMENT (resentment): The psychological mechanism.
  Those who cannot act against the powerful imaginatively
  invert the value system: the powerful are "evil";
  the weak are "good." This is not just wrong — it is
  psychologically corrosive.

  Nietzsche argues Christian morality is slave morality
  — historically the morality of the oppressed Roman underclass
  who couldn't overcome Roman power and instead redefined
  values so that their condition became virtuous.
```

This is a historical diagnosis, not an endorsement of might-makes-right. Nietzsche is analyzing the psychological origin of moral valuations, not arguing that the powerful should crush the weak.

### Will to Power

```
  WILL TO POWER (Wille zur Macht)

  Not: will to dominate others
  Not: crude power-seeking

  Rather: the fundamental drive in all living things to
  express, extend, and enhance their power — their capacity
  for activity, creativity, self-overcoming.

  Power = capacity for growth, mastery of one's own drives,
          creative expression, self-surpassing

  "Man must be surpassed" — the drive is always toward
  going beyond one's current form.

  Application to art: the artist's will to power creates
  forms that organize and master chaotic drives.
  Application to philosophy: the philosopher's will to power
  creates new values and interpretations.
  Application to the Übermensch: not Superman but the person
  who has overcome nihilism through self-created values.
```

### Eternal Recurrence

Nietzsche's most metaphysically provocative idea:

```
  ETERNAL RECURRENCE (Ewige Wiederkehr)

  What if you had to live your life again, exactly as you have
  lived it, infinitely many times — every pain, every joy,
  every moment, unchanged, eternally repeated?

  Would you greet this thought with despair (life as described
  is unbearable to repeat), or with joy ("life is such that
  I would will to live it again")?

  Nietzsche presents this as both a cosmological hypothesis
  and an ethical test: the greatest weight.

  Amor fati ("love of fate"): to will the eternal recurrence
  of your life is to affirm existence completely — not
  despite suffering, but including it.

  This is the opposite of Schopenhauer's will-denial.
  Where Schopenhauer says: escape the will. Nietzsche says:
  embrace it, affirm it, make your life worth recurring.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Schopenhauer is just gloomy** — Schopenhauer's pessimism is systematic and philosophically argued, not a mood. His account of aesthetic experience and compassion as escape from the Will is positive philosophy. His influence on Freud and on the Western reception of Buddhist philosophy is substantial.

**Marx predicted the Soviet Union** — Marx gave no blueprint for post-capitalist society. He described the dialectical movement of history and the contradictions of capitalism. The Soviet Union was built on Lenin's particular reading of Marx, which would have surprised Marx (Russia was not an advanced industrial capitalist economy — it was the last place Marx expected revolution to succeed).

**Nietzsche was a Nazi** — This is directly false. Nietzsche explicitly and repeatedly attacked German nationalism, anti-Semitism, and the state. His sister Elisabeth married a proto-fascist (Bernhard Förster) and Nietzsche despised him. After Nietzsche's mental collapse (1889), Elisabeth controlled his literary estate and selectively edited/forged materials to align with Nazi ideology. The Nazi use of Nietzsche required extensive distortion of his actual positions.

**"Will to power" = desire for political domination** — Nietzsche uses the word "power" (Macht) in a specific sense: not power-over-others but the drive to enhance one's own capacities, overcome oneself, create. The Übermensch is not a conqueror but a creator of new values. The misreading fits the Nazi version, not the text.

**Kierkegaard's "leap of faith" is anti-intellectual** — Kierkegaard is not attacking reason in general; he is attacking the claim that reason can access the individual's relation to God. He makes sophisticated philosophical arguments for why this relation is incommensurable with universal categories. His pseudonymous method requires more philosophical sophistication than Hegel's lectures.
