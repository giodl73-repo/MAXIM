# Medieval and Islamic Philosophy — Augustine, Avicenna, Averroes, Aquinas, Maimonides

## The Big Picture: Transmission and Transformation

Greek philosophy didn't die when Rome declined. It was preserved, translated, and transformed across three intellectual traditions before returning to Western Europe:

```
GREEK PHILOSOPHY
(600 BCE – 200 CE)
         │
         │ [Western Roman Empire collapses, 476 CE]
         │ [Eastern Roman/Byzantine Empire continues]
         │
         v
SYRIAC TRANSLATION MOVEMENT         AUGUSTINE (354–430 CE)
(4th–7th centuries)                 Western synthesis:
Greek texts → Syriac                Plato + Neoplatonism + Christianity
         │
         │ [Islamic conquests, 7th century]
         │
         v
ARABIC TRANSLATION MOVEMENT         ISLAMIC GOLDEN AGE
(8th–10th centuries, Baghdad)       Avicenna (980–1037)
Greek texts → Arabic                Averroes (1126–1198)
+ Original Islamic philosophy       Al-Farabi, Al-Kindi, Al-Ghazali
         │
         │ [Reconquista; Toledo translation schools, 12th century]
         │
         v
LATIN SCHOLASTICISM                 JEWISH SYNTHESIS
(11th–14th centuries)               Maimonides (1138–1204)
Arabic + Greek → Latin              Aristotle + Torah
Aquinas (1225–1274)
         │
         v
EARLY MODERN PHILOSOPHY
(1600 onward)
```

The central problem for all medieval philosophy: **Faith and Reason**. How does revealed religious truth relate to rational philosophical inquiry? Three positions:
1. **Fideism**: faith supersedes reason — they don't compete
2. **Double truth**: religious and philosophical truth are separate and may conflict
3. **Synthesis**: reason and faith are compatible; philosophy clarifies and supports theology

## Augustine of Hippo (354–430 CE)

Augustine is the dominant figure in Western Christian thought for the next 800 years. He bridges Neo-Platonic philosophy and Christian theology.

### Life and Intellectual Path

```
  Augustine's intellectual journey:

  Manichaean dualism (good vs. evil spiritual substances)
       │
       v
  Academic Skepticism (can we know anything?)
       │
       v
  Neoplatonism (Plotinus: the One, emanation, ascent of the soul)
       │ — convinced him of the existence of immaterial reality
       │ — but: found it proud (wisdom by personal effort)
       v
  Christianity (386 CE, conversion)
       │ — grace, not self-elevation, is required
       │ — the will, not just the intellect, is the problem
       v
  Bishop of Hippo (395–430 CE)
  Prolific author against Donatists, Pelagians, Manichaeans
```

### Core Contributions

**The Will and Original Sin**: Before Augustine, the dominant view was that sin is ignorance (Socratic). Augustine shifts it: sin is a defect of the will. We know the good but choose the lesser good through disordered love (libido). Original sin damages the will structurally — it cannot will the good without grace. This is why external philosophical training is insufficient; you need divine assistance.

**Predestination and Grace**: God knows who will be saved (foreknowledge without causation — a difficult position). Grace is necessary and irresistible for the elect. The dispute over whether this makes human freedom meaningless drives controversy through the Reformation (Calvin is essentially Augustinian).

**The Two Cities** (De Civitate Dei — City of God):

```
  CITY OF GOD                     CITY OF MAN
  ─────────────────────────────   ─────────────────────────────
  Those who love God (amor Dei)   Those who love self (amor sui)
  Directed toward eternal good    Directed toward temporal goods
  Will endure eternally           Will perish

  These two cities are mixed together in history — the Church
  does not = City of God, nor State = City of Man.
  The distinction is eschatological, not institutional.
```

**Time and Eternity** (Confessions, Book XI): One of the most sophisticated analyses of time in antiquity. Augustine asks: what was God doing before creation? (Answer: "before" is a temporal concept; time is created with the universe.) He analyzes time as a "distension of the soul" — past, present, and future exist as mental states: memory, attention, expectation.

**Illumination Theory of Knowledge**: We don't abstract universals from particulars (Aristotle). Rather, the mind is directly illuminated by divine truth — God is the "inner teacher" from whom we learn eternal truths.

## Avicenna / Ibn Sina (980–1037 CE)

Ibn Sina is the greatest philosopher-scientist of the Islamic world and one of the most original thinkers of any tradition. He was a physician, astronomer, mathematician, and philosopher — and wrote prolifically while holding political appointments and fleeing political instability.

### The Floating Man

Avicenna's most famous philosophical thought experiment — a predecessor to Descartes' cogito and Husserl's phenomenological reduction:

```
  FLOATING MAN THOUGHT EXPERIMENT

  Imagine a person created fully formed, floating in air,
  with no sensory input:
  - Cannot see, hear, smell, touch, taste
  - No bodily sensation whatsoever
  - No memory of prior experience

  Question: does this person know anything?

  Answer: Yes. They know they exist.
  The self is directly self-evident to itself,
  independent of all sensory input.

  Implication:
  The rational soul (intellect) is not identical to the body.
  It can be known without any bodily input.
  → Non-material substance with its own mode of being.
```

This is a direct predecessor to Descartes' cogito ergo sum (1641) — remarkably, 600 years earlier.

### Philosophy of the Soul

Avicenna distinguishes:
- **Vegetative soul**: nutrition, growth (plants have this)
- **Animal soul**: sensation, movement (animals add this)
- **Rational soul**: intellect and will (humans add this)

The rational soul is immaterial and survives death. It is individuated (my soul is distinct from yours) but connects to the Active Intellect — a cosmic intelligence (the lowest of ten intellects in Avicenna's system, corresponding to the Aristotelian unmoved mover and the Neoplatonic Nous).

### Necessary and Possible Being

This is Avicenna's most important metaphysical contribution — the foundation for one of the standard arguments for God's existence:

```
  NECESSARY vs. POSSIBLE BEING

  Necessary being: exists by its own essence — cannot not exist
  Possible being: exists contingently — could exist or not exist

  Every possible being requires a cause for its existence.
  An infinite chain of possible beings each requiring causes
  doesn't explain why anything exists at all.
  Therefore: there must be a Necessary Being — one whose
  essence just IS existence.

  This Necessary Being = God (in Avicenna's system).

  Aquinas later adopts this argument (Third Way).
```

### Medicine and Science

Ibn Sina's Canon of Medicine (Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb) was the standard medical textbook in both Islamic and European universities until the 17th century. He described contagion theory (before germ theory), quarantine procedures, and systematic clinical observation.

## Averroes / Ibn Rushd (1126–1198 CE)

Averroes was the great commentator on Aristotle — so thorough that Aquinas simply calls him "the Commentator." He read Aristotle more literally and less Platonically than Avicenna.

### The Controversy Over the Intellect

Aristotle's De Anima distinguishes the "active intellect" from the "passive intellect." Averroes interprets the active intellect as a single shared intellect for all humanity — not something individual to each person:

```
  AVERROES' MONOPSYCHISM

  Individual humans have:
  - Passive intellect (the capacity for thought — individual, mortal)

  All humanity shares:
  - Active Intellect (the power that illuminates thought — universal, immortal)

  Implication: there is no personal immortality.
  "You" as an individual do not survive death — only the
  universal Active Intellect persists.
```

This was explosive: it denied personal immortality, which is doctrinally essential to Christianity and Islam. The Latin Averroists (Siger of Brabant, 13th century) were condemned by the Church. Aquinas specifically attacks Averroes' monopsychism in *On the Unity of the Intellect Against the Averroists*.

### Philosophy and Religion — Double Truth?

Averroes argued that philosophy (demonstrative reasoning) and religion (persuasive/imaginative discourse for the masses) operate at different levels. This was read (possibly unfairly) as implying that philosophy and theology can reach contradictory truths — neither is wrong, they just address different audiences. The "double truth" reading was condemned; Averroes likely meant that theological language is a simplified presentation of philosophical truths.

## Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274 CE)

Aquinas is the greatest systematic philosopher-theologian of the Latin Middle Ages. He faced the same crisis his contemporaries did: Aristotle had just been recovered in full (via Arabic translations), and Aristotle taught positions incompatible with Christianity (eternal world, no personal immortality, no divine creation). What to do?

Aquinas' answer: Aristotle is mostly right, but his reasoning needs to be completed and corrected by revealed theology. Faith and reason are not opposed — they address the same truth from different directions.

### The Five Ways (Quinque Viae)

Five arguments for God's existence in Summa Theologiae:

```
  WAY 1: From Motion
  Everything in motion was set in motion by something else.
  Infinite regress is impossible.
  Therefore: an Unmoved Mover exists.

  WAY 2: From Efficient Cause
  Every effect has a cause. Infinite regress impossible.
  Therefore: a First Uncaused Cause exists.

  WAY 3: From Contingency (from Avicenna)
  Everything that exists contingently might not exist.
  If everything were contingent, at some point nothing would exist.
  But we exist. Therefore: something necessarily exists = God.

  WAY 4: From Degrees of Perfection
  Things are more or less good/true/noble.
  Degrees require a maximum (most good, most true).
  That maximum = God.

  WAY 5: From Teleology (the Design Argument)
  Natural things act for ends without intelligence.
  What lacks intelligence can only be directed by something intelligent.
  Therefore: an intelligent director exists = God.
```

None of these arguments has gone uncontested. Hume attacks the cosmological argument (Why can't the universe itself be the necessary being?). Kant argues that all five commit the same error: applying categories of human experience beyond their valid use.

### Essence and Existence

Aquinas accepts Avicenna's distinction between essence (what a thing is) and existence (that it is). In all created beings, essence and existence are distinct — you can know what a phoenix is without knowing whether phoenixes exist. In God alone, essence and existence are identical — God's essence just is to exist. This is why God cannot not exist.

### Natural Law

Aquinas' synthesis of Stoic natural law with Christian theology:

```
  ETERNAL LAW ─── God's rational governance of the universe
       │
       │ participation
       v
  NATURAL LAW ─── Human rational participation in eternal law.
       │           We grasp basic moral principles by reason:
       │           do good, avoid evil; preserve life; form society.
       │
       │ application
       v
  HUMAN (POSITIVE) LAW ─── Specific rules derived from natural law
                             for a particular community.
  DIVINE LAW ───────────── Revealed law (Scripture) guiding us
                             to supernatural ends.
```

Natural law theory is the philosophical basis of Catholic moral theology and remains influential in secular legal philosophy (Finnis, Grisez).

## Maimonides / Moses ben Maimon (1138–1204 CE)

Maimonides is the greatest Jewish philosopher-theologian of the medieval period. Born in Córdoba (Islamic Spain), he fled persecution, eventually settled in Egypt as court physician to Saladin's dynasty.

### The Guide for the Perplexed

His major philosophical work addresses a crisis: educated Jews who know Aristotle find contradictions between philosophical reason and Torah. The Guide aims to resolve these, arguing that apparent contradictions come from misreading either the Torah (which often speaks metaphorically) or Aristotle (who was sometimes wrong).

**Negative Theology (Via Negativa)**: God is so radically different from created beings that no positive attribute can be applied to God in the same sense it applies to things. When we say "God is good," "good" doesn't mean what it means for humans.

```
  NEGATIVE THEOLOGY

  We cannot say what God IS.
  We can only say what God IS NOT.

  "God is not ignorant" ─── God knows
  "God is not multiple" ─── God is one
  "God is not temporal" ─── God is eternal

  All positive attributes attributed to God are really
  negations of their opposites, or attributes of God's
  effects — not of God's essence.
```

**On Creation**: Maimonides argues (contra Aristotle) that the world was created in time, not that it has eternally existed. But he doesn't claim to prove this — he claims that neither side can be demonstrated conclusively.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Medieval philosophy = Scholasticism = Christian theology"** — Medieval philosophy is a multicultural achievement: the texts that would define European thought until the 17th century were preserved, translated, and philosophically developed primarily by Islamic thinkers (Avicenna, Averroes, Al-Farabi) and Jewish thinkers (Maimonides). Aquinas built on Arabic translations of Aristotle.

**Augustine = pessimist about human nature** — Mostly accurate, but Augustine also writes some of the most psychologically acute self-analysis in antiquity (Confessions). The pessimism about unaided reason/will is balanced by optimism about grace.

**Aquinas' Five Ways as proofs** — Aquinas calls them "ways" (viae), not proofs. He doesn't claim deductive certainty; he claims these are the best philosophical arguments for theism. Whether they work depends entirely on whether you accept their cosmological premises (infinite regress is impossible; there must be a first cause). All five have been contested.

**Averroes' "double truth" doctrine** — This is largely a misreading promoted by his critics. Averroes argued that philosophy and religion address the same truth at different levels (demonstrative, dialectical, rhetorical). The "two truths can contradict" reading was attributed to him to justify condemnation.

**Scholasticism as sterile logic-chopping** — This is the Renaissance humanist caricature. Scholastic disputation (quaestio format: objections → response → replies to objections) is a rigorous method for exposing conceptual difficulties. Aquinas' Summa Theologiae is one of the most systematically organized philosophical works in any tradition.
