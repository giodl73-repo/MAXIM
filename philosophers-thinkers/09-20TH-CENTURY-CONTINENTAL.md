## Edmund Husserl — Phenomenology

**Edmund Husserl (1859–1938)** founded phenomenology — the philosophical analysis of the structures of consciousness. He trained as a mathematician under Weierstrass and brought mathematical rigor to the description of conscious experience.

### The Phenomenological Method

```
  HUSSERL'S PROJECT:
  Describe the structures of consciousness as they appear —
  without presupposing scientific theories about the physical world,
  the brain, or historical/cultural context.

  THE EPOCHÉ (phenomenological reduction):
  "Bracket" (set aside) the question of whether objects
  really exist independently of consciousness.
  Attend only to consciousness's own acts and their
  intentional contents.

  The result: pure phenomenological description.
  How does consciousness structure its experience of time?
  Of other persons? Of abstract objects? Of space?
```

### Intentionality

Husserl developed Brentano's insight: all consciousness is intentional — directed at an object.

```
  INTENTIONALITY: Every mental act is about something.

  Consciousness doesn't exist in isolation — it is always
  "consciousness of X." There is no consciousness without
  an object; there is no object of consciousness without
  a conscious act.

  INTENTIONAL STRUCTURE:
  ─ Noesis: the act of consciousness (perceiving, judging,
             desiring, remembering)
  ─ Noema: the intentional content — what the act is "about,"
            the object as intended (as perceived, as judged, etc.)
  ─ The real object (may or may not exist)

  Husserl distinguishes between the object as intended
  (the noema) and the real object. Hallucination and
  genuine perception have the same noetic structure.
```

### The Crisis of European Sciences

Husserl's last major work (1936) argues that mathematical science has forgotten its origin in lived human experience (the Lebenswelt — "lifeworld"). Abstract symbols, once they succeed, become taken for granted — their meaning is sedimented, their original intuitive content forgotten. Science loses touch with the lived world it arose from.

This is the genealogy of what Husserl calls the "crisis": the disenchantment of nature through mathematization leaves human existence without philosophical grounding.

## Martin Heidegger — Being and Time

**Martin Heidegger (1889–1976)** is Husserl's student and the most influential philosopher of the 20th century in the continental tradition. He is also deeply controversial: he joined the Nazi party in 1933, became Rector of Freiburg, and his philosophical commitment to National Socialism (revealed further by the Black Notebooks, 2014) has generated ongoing debate about whether his philosophy can be separated from his politics.

### The Question of Being

Heidegger's central question: what does it mean to be? Western philosophy since Plato has answered this by talking about beings — entities, objects, substances. But it has forgotten Being itself — the condition of possibility for there being anything at all.

```
  BEING vs. BEINGS
  ─ Beings: the entities we encounter — chairs, people, numbers
  ─ Being (Sein): what it is for any entity to be at all
                  The "is" in "there is"

  Heidegger: We understand Being implicitly in all our dealings —
  every use of "is" presupposes understanding of Being — but we
  have not thematized it philosophically.

  Being and Time (1927) approaches the question of Being through
  the analysis of Dasein — the kind of being that asks about Being:
  us, human existence.
```

### Dasein — Being-in-the-World

```
  DASEIN (German: "being-there"):
  Heidegger's term for human existence.
  Not "subject" (too epistemological), not "consciousness" (Husserl),
  not "person" (too anthropological).

  KEY STRUCTURE: Being-in-the-world (In-der-Welt-sein)
  Dasein is not first a subject that then encounters a world.
  It is essentially embedded in a world of practices, concerns,
  and involvements. This is prior to any theoretical stance.

  EQUIPMENT (Zeug):
  We normally encounter things not as objects with properties
  (present-at-hand, Vorhandenheit) but as equipment ready for use
  (ready-to-hand, Zuhandenheit).
  The hammer is not "an object with mass and hardness" while
  you're hammering — it disappears into the activity.

  When equipment breaks, it becomes present-at-hand (an object
  of theoretical attention). This is why science always focuses
  on malfunctioning, disrupted, explicitly attended-to things —
  not the normal flow of practical engagement.

  APPLICATION TO COMPUTING: The system is ready-to-hand while
  it works; it becomes present-at-hand when it crashes. You
  experience your software, not as code with properties, but
  as tools for doing things — until they break.
```

### Anxiety and Authenticity

```
  MOODS (Stimmungen): Not psychological states layered onto
  neutral cognition — moods are the way the world is disclosed
  to Dasein. Mood is ontologically primary.

  ANXIETY (Angst): Unlike fear (which has a specific object),
  anxiety has no specific object — it is the indefinite threat
  of nothingness. Anxiety individualizes Dasein by pulling it
  back from its absorption in social convention (das Man —
  "the They").

  DAS MAN ("the They"):
  The anonymous social world of convention. "One does this."
  "People say." Not any particular person, but the pressure
  of social norms that substitutes for genuine choice.

  AUTHENTICITY vs. INAUTHENTICITY:
  Inauthentic: absorbed in das Man; self-understood through
               social role; fleeing from death and individuality.
  Authentic: owning your thrownness (the situation you find
             yourself in without choosing it); running ahead
             toward death; taking responsibility for your existence.

  BEING-TOWARD-DEATH:
  Death is the one possibility that is wholly mine — not shareable,
  certain, and structurally defining. Authentic existence means
  confronting this rather than hiding behind das Man.
```

## Jean-Paul Sartre — Existence Precedes Essence

**Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980)** is the most publicly prominent existentialist — public intellectual, playwright, novelist, political activist.

### Being-in-Itself and Being-for-Itself

```
  BEING-IN-ITSELF (en-soi): The being of objects.
  ─ Dense, self-identical, without negation or possibility.
  ─ A stone is what it is; it has no distance from itself.

  BEING-FOR-ITSELF (pour-soi): The being of consciousness.
  ─ Consciousness is what it is NOT and is not what it is.
  ─ Consciousness is always a "being that is its own nothingness."
  ─ Consciousness introduces nothingness into the world
    (negation, absence, possibility, future).

  The waiter who plays at being a waiter:
  He acts with mechanical precision — but he cannot BE a waiter
  the way a stone is a stone. He is always more than his role;
  he is always choosing to be-a-waiter. His "waiter-ness" is
  a project, not a nature.
```

### Existence Precedes Essence

Sartre's central doctrine:

```
  FOR A MANUFACTURED OBJECT (paper-knife):
  Essence precedes existence.
  The craftsman has a concept (essence/function) before making it.

  FOR HUMANS (Sartre's atheist existentialism):
  There is no God who conceives human nature before creating humans.
  There is no "human nature" in the sense of a fixed essence.
  Humans exist first (are thrown into the world)
  then create their essence through choices.

  "Man is nothing else but what he makes of himself."

  RADICAL FREEDOM:
  We are "condemned to be free." We cannot escape choice.
  Not choosing is itself a choice.
  Even our facticity (situation, past, body) is always
  interpreted through our projects — we are not determined by it.
```

### Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi)

```
  BAD FAITH: Denying one's freedom by treating oneself as
  an object with a fixed nature.

  EXAMPLES:
  ─ "I can't help it — I was born this way."
  ─ "I had no choice — it's just how things are."
  ─ "I'm just following orders."

  Bad faith uses one's facticity (situation, body, past)
  to deny one's transcendence (freedom to interpret and choose).

  It is not lying to others — it is lying to oneself.
  And it is structurally unstable — you always know, at some
  level, that you are in bad faith, because you are the one
  fabricating the self-deception.
```

## Albert Camus — Absurdism and Revolt

**Albert Camus (1913–1960)** is often grouped with existentialism but rejected the label. He's a philosopher of the absurd, which is distinct.

```
  THE ABSURD (Camus):

  HUMAN BEING: demands meaning, clarity, unity.
  THE WORLD: silent, irrational, indifferent to our demands.
  THE ABSURD: the confrontation between these two — the gap
               between human longing and world's silence.

  THREE RESPONSES:
  1. Suicide — "life is not worth living without meaning."
     Camus: this is giving the absurd too much power.
  2. Philosophical suicide — leap to religion or transcendence
     (Kierkegaard's leap of faith, Sartre's bad faith).
     Camus: this is giving up honest confrontation.
  3. REVOLT — Continue living in full consciousness of the
     absurd, without false hope, without suicide, with defiance.
     "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

  SISYPHUS: condemned to roll a boulder uphill forever.
  Camus: in the descent back down, Sisyphus is master of his
  fate. Consciousness of futility without surrender is revolt.
```

Camus vs. Sartre: major public rupture (1952) over Camus' The Rebel (1951), which argued that political violence in pursuit of absolute ends leads to totalitarianism. Sartre defended the Marxist revolutionary position. Their friendship ended over this.

## Maurice Merleau-Ponty — Embodied Perception

**Maurice Merleau-Ponty (1908–1961)** corrects both Husserl and Sartre by returning to the body:

```
  PROBLEM WITH CARTESIAN/HUSSERLIAN TRADITION:
  Both treat consciousness as the primary term — the body
  as an object that consciousness inhabits or attends to.

  MERLEAU-PONTY'S THESIS:
  The body is not an object of consciousness but the subject
  of perception — the original medium of engagement with the world.

  BODY-SUBJECT vs. BODY-OBJECT:
  ─ When I grasp a glass, I don't represent my hand and the glass
    and calculate contact — I simply grasp. The body-schema
    extends into the tool/world.
  ─ Phantom limb: a person who lost an arm still "feels" it —
    because the body-subject is not reducible to the physical body.

  PRE-REFLECTIVE ENGAGEMENT:
  Most of our being-in-the-world is prereflective — skilled,
  habitual, below the threshold of explicit attention.
  A pianist doesn't think about each finger position;
  a driver doesn't think about steering.
  This pre-reflective engagement is ontologically primary.
```

Merleau-Ponty's philosophy of embodiment directly influences cognitive science (extended mind hypothesis, 4E cognition), phenomenological approaches to AI consciousness debates, and sports science.

## John Rawls — Justice as Fairness

**John Rawls (1921–2002)** revived liberal political philosophy with A Theory of Justice (1971) — the most discussed book in political philosophy since John Stuart Mill.

### The Original Position and Veil of Ignorance

```
  RAWLS'S THOUGHT EXPERIMENT:

  Imagine you must choose the basic principles of your society
  without knowing what position you will occupy in it:
  ─ You don't know your wealth, class, or race
  ─ You don't know your natural talents and abilities
  ─ You don't know your conception of the good, your life plan
  ─ You don't know your generation

  This is the ORIGINAL POSITION behind the VEIL OF IGNORANCE.

  RATIONALE: Choices made from behind the veil are fair —
  they can't be biased toward your actual position because
  you don't know it. This is a device of representation, not
  a real historical moment.

  WHAT PRINCIPLES WOULD RATIONAL PERSONS CHOOSE?
  Rawls argues they would choose:

  PRINCIPLE 1: Equal basic liberties for all
  (liberty principle — lexically prior to principle 2)

  PRINCIPLE 2: Social and economic inequalities are justified
               only if:
               (a) They are attached to positions open to all
                   under fair equality of opportunity
               (b) They benefit the least advantaged members
                   of society (DIFFERENCE PRINCIPLE)

  The difference principle: inequality is justified only
  if it makes the worst-off better off than they would be
  under equality. This is not utilitarianism (maximize total
  welfare); it is maximin — maximize the minimum.
```

### Reflective Equilibrium

Rawls' philosophical method: work back and forth between our considered moral judgments (intuitions about specific cases) and general principles, adjusting both until they cohere. Neither pure theory nor pure intuition is sovereign — they mutually constrain.

## Jürgen Habermas — Communicative Reason

**Jürgen Habermas (b. 1929)** is the last major figure of the Frankfurt School and the most systematic political philosopher of the late 20th century.

```
  FRANKFURT SCHOOL DIAGNOSIS:
  Adorno and Horkheimer: The Enlightenment's project of
  "instrumental reason" (rationality as means-end calculation)
  has turned on itself — the Holocaust is the product of
  fully rationalized murder, not irrationality.

  HABERMAS' RESPONSE:
  Not all reason is instrumental. There is also
  COMMUNICATIVE REASON — reason aimed at mutual understanding.

  COMMUNICATIVE ACTION:
  Action oriented toward reaching understanding with others.
  Contrast with strategic action (using others as means).

  IDEAL SPEECH SITUATION:
  Communication is governed by validity claims:
  ─ Truthfulness (sincerity)
  ─ Rightness (normative legitimacy)
  ─ Truth (propositional accuracy)

  Rational discourse requires conditions where anyone can:
  ─ Question any claim
  ─ Introduce any topic
  ─ Express any need without coercion

  DISCOURSE ETHICS:
  Norms are valid if they could be agreed to by all affected
  parties in an ideal rational discourse.
  This is a procedural theory: the outcome is whatever
  free, non-coerced rational discussion produces.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Phenomenology = description of mental states** — Phenomenology is not introspective psychology. Husserl is not reporting what it feels like to have experiences; he is analyzing the essential structures of different types of conscious acts (perception, memory, imagination, judgment) — their necessary features regardless of particular content.

**Heidegger's Nazi involvement** — Heidegger's political commitment to National Socialism (1933–1934 as Rector, and more enduringly) is documented. The question of how this infects his philosophy is contested. Some (Farias, Faye) argue the philosophy is Nazi ideology in disguise. Most scholars argue the connection is real but complex — Heidegger's critique of modernity and technology, his notion of Volk (people), and his hostility to democratic liberalism are all entangled with his politics, but Being and Time (1927, pre-Nazi) remains philosophically significant.

**Sartre vs. Camus on politics** — Their break (1952) over The Rebel exposed a genuine philosophical difference: Sartre believed the political ends (socialist revolution, anti-colonialism) justified what was needed; Camus believed absolute ends lead to absolute violence and terror regardless of motive. The 20th century's history of revolutionary violence gave Camus' position increasing weight after Sartre's death.

**Rawls vs. utilitarianism** — Rawls explicitly argues against utilitarian maximization of aggregate welfare. The difference principle is not utilitarian because it doesn't allow benefits to the many to outweigh harm to the worst-off. For a utilitarian, enslaving a few for great net welfare gain is permissible. For Rawls, it violates the difference principle and the liberty principle. The distinction is real and important.

**The veil of ignorance as an actual historical contract** — Rawls' original position is a hypothetical device, not a claim that people ever actually agreed to anything behind a veil of ignorance. It is a test for fairness: what principles could be justified to everyone? The justification is hypothetical rational consensus, not actual historical agreement (which Locke required).
