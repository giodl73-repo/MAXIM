# Kant and German Idealism — Kant, Fichte, Schelling, Hegel

## The Big Picture

Kant is the pivot of modern philosophy. He synthesizes rationalism and empiricism, and every major movement after him — Idealism, Romanticism, Marxism, phenomenology, analytic philosophy — defines itself in relation to him.

```

              RATIONALISTS               EMPIRICISTS
```

## Kant — The Copernican Revolution in Philosophy

**Immanuel Kant (1724–1804)** lived and died in Königsberg (now Kaliningrad, Russia) without traveling more than a few hundred kilometers from home. He published almost nothing of importance until age 57. Then: three major Critiques in nine years.

### The Copernican Revolution

Copernicus moved the earth; Kant moved the mind. Instead of asking how our knowledge must conform to objects, Kant asks: what if objects must conform to our knowledge?

```
  PRE-KANT: Mind is passive, receives impressions from objects.
  Knowledge = mind conforming to objects as they are in themselves.

  KANT'S INVERSION: Mind is active, structures experience.
  What we experience is not things as they are "in themselves"
  (Ding an sich) but phenomena — things as they appear to us
  through the mind's structuring activity.

  Like a camera: the world goes through a lens. What you capture
  depends partly on the lens (the mind's structure), not just
  the scene.

  This explains:
  ─ How synthetic a priori knowledge is possible
    (truths that are informative but not dependent on experience)
  ─ Why mathematics and physics work so reliably
    (we impose their structure on experience)
  ─ Why metaphysics about "things in themselves" is impossible
    (we can only know how things appear through our structure)
```

### The Three Critiques

```
  Critique of Pure Reason (1781/87)     ─── What CAN we know?
  Critique of Practical Reason (1788)   ─── What MUST we do?
  Critique of Judgment (1790)           ─── What may we HOPE for?
                                             (also: aesthetics)
```

### First Critique — Structure of the Mind

The First Critique (Kritik der reinen Vernunft) has three main parts:

```
TRANSCENDENTAL AESTHETIC
  Forms of Intuition: Space and Time
  ─ Space is not learned from experience — it's the form in which
    outer sense must present data.
  ─ Time is not learned from experience — it's the form in which
    inner sense (thoughts) must appear.
  ─ Geometry is possible because space is our imposition,
    not an independent discovery. Same for arithmetic (time-order).

TRANSCENDENTAL ANALYTIC
  Categories of the Understanding: 12 Pure Concepts
  ─ Causality, substance, unity, plurality, necessity, etc.
  ─ The mind applies these concepts to organize experience.
  ─ Without categories, experience = "buzzing, blooming confusion"
  ─ Famous example: causation. Hume showed we can't get it from
    experience alone. Kant: we impose it. The category of causation
    is what makes experience causally structured.

TRANSCENDENTAL DIALECTIC
  Where Pure Reason Overreaches
  Three ideas that tempt reason beyond possible experience:
  ─ The Soul (psychology pushes toward an immortal, unitary soul)
  ─ The World as a Whole (cosmology pushes toward totality)
  ─ God (theology pushes toward an unconditioned first cause)

  Antinomies: Kant shows that for each cosmological idea,
  you can prove BOTH a thesis and antithesis:
  "The world has a beginning in time" and
  "The world has no beginning in time" can both be argued.

  Conclusion: These ideas are "regulative" (useful for
  systematizing inquiry) but not "constitutive" (not objects
  of possible knowledge).
```

### Synthetic A Priori

This is Kant's technical problem that motivates the whole First Critique:

```
  ANALYTIC judgment: "All bachelors are unmarried"
  True by definition. Adds nothing to knowledge.

  A POSTERIORI judgment: "It is raining"
  True by experience. Could be false.

  SYNTHETIC A PRIORI: "7 + 5 = 12"
  Not true by definition (no analysis of 7 and 5 gives 12)
  Not true just by experience (it's necessarily true)
  → It is informative AND necessarily true.

  How is this possible? The whole First Critique is the answer:
  Mind imposes mathematical and causal structure on experience —
  so these structures are both necessary (we can't experience
  otherwise) and informative (they're not mere definitions).
```

### The Categorical Imperative

The Second Critique (practical reason = moral reason) establishes the foundation of ethics. The moral law must be derived from reason alone — not from consequences, not from human nature, not from God's commands:

```
  FORMULATIONS OF THE CATEGORICAL IMPERATIVE

  F1: UNIVERSAL LAW FORMULA
  "Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the
   same time will that it should become a universal law."

  Example: lying to escape inconvenience.
  Maxim: "Lie when convenient."
  Can I universalize this? No — if everyone lied when convenient,
  the institution of truth-telling dissolves, and lying becomes
  impossible (no one would believe anything).
  → Lying is prohibited.

  F2: HUMANITY FORMULA
  "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person
   or that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."

  Example: false promise.
  I'm using the other person as a mere means to get money —
  not respecting their rational autonomy.
  → False promises are prohibited.

  F3: KINGDOM OF ENDS FORMULA
  "Act according to maxims of a universally legislating member
   of a merely possible kingdom of ends."

  Imagine a community of all rational beings, each legislating
  moral laws for everyone. Your maxim must be fit to be law
  in that ideal community.
```

**Why pure reason?** Because moral worth requires acting from duty alone — not from inclination, fear, or self-interest. An action is morally worthy only when it is done for the sake of the moral law itself.

### Noumena vs. Phenomena; Freedom

```
  PHENOMENAL WORLD: the world as we experience it
  ─ Subject to causal laws (determinism)
  ─ Objects of scientific knowledge

  NOUMENAL WORLD: things as they are in themselves
  ─ Not directly knowable
  ─ But: we must think of ourselves as noumenal agents
    to make sense of moral responsibility.

  RESOLUTION OF FREE WILL PROBLEM:
  As phenomenal beings: we are determined by causal laws.
  As noumenal agents: we are free — we can legislate our
  own moral law through reason.

  These are not contradictory because they describe
  different aspects (appearances vs. things in themselves).
```

## Fichte — The Absolute Ego

**Johann Gottlieb Fichte (1762–1814)** was Kant's successor in Berlin and the first German Idealist. He radicalized Kant: if the mind structures experience, what are "things in themselves"? The noumenal world is a contradiction — an unknown unknown that we can't even consistently think. Drop it.

```
  FICHTE'S MOVE:

  Kant: Mind (I) encounters world (Not-I) through thing-in-itself.
  Fichte: Drop the thing-in-itself.
          The I posits itself (self-consciousness as foundational).
          The I then posits the Not-I (the world) as the obstacle
          against which self-consciousness defines itself.
          The world is constituted by the activity of the absolute I.

  This is idealism: reality is constituted by mind/spirit.
  Not Berkeley's idealism (individual minds perceive ideas).
  Fichte's absolute I is not individual but transcendental.
```

Fichte also wrote the Addresses to the German Nation (1807) after Napoleon conquered Prussia — arguing for German cultural and national unity. This makes him the philosophical grandfather of German nationalism, which has complicated his legacy.

## Schelling — Nature as Spirit

**Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling (1775–1854)** asked: if Fichte's I constitutes nature, why does nature seem to have its own rational structure independent of any human mind? He argued for a philosophy of nature where nature itself is unconscious spirit:

```
  SCHELLING'S IDENTITY PHILOSOPHY

  Nature and Mind are not opposed — they are the same
  Absolute viewed from two sides.

  Nature = slumbering Spirit (Mind not yet self-conscious)
  Mind = Nature become self-conscious

  The universe is Spirit gradually awakening to itself
  through nature and human consciousness.

  Art is the highest form of this: in aesthetic creation,
  the unconscious (natural) and conscious (mental) are
  identical. The artist creates without fully knowing why —
  that is, nature creates through the artist.
```

Schelling influenced the Romantic movement (Coleridge, Wordsworth experienced his ideas through their German connections), the philosophy of nature, and later phenomenological approaches to embodiment.

## Hegel — The System of Spirit

**Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831)** is the philosopher everyone references and almost no one reads straight through. His prose is famously difficult. But his ideas restructured virtually every field: philosophy, history, political theory, theology, aesthetics, social theory (Marx, Dewey, the Frankfurt School, Gadamer — all response to Hegel).

### Dialectic

The word "dialectic" means different things in different contexts:

```
  PLATONIC DIALECTIC: Reasoning through question and answer
                       to expose contradiction and reach truth.

  KANTIAN DIALECTIC: Contradictions that arise when reason
                      overreaches beyond possible experience.

  HEGELIAN DIALECTIC: Historical and conceptual development
                       through contradiction toward synthesis.

  NOT simply: thesis → antithesis → synthesis
  (Hegel almost never uses this formula himself)

  Actually: a concept or historical form reveals its own
  contradictions in its development; this contradiction drives
  toward a higher form that "sublates" (aufheben) the lower —
  preserving, canceling, and lifting it to a higher level.
```

### The Phenomenology of Spirit (1807)

```
  STRUCTURE: The journey of Geist (Spirit/Mind) through
  successive shapes of consciousness toward Absolute Knowledge.

  KEY SHAPES:
  ─ Sense-certainty: "I know this immediately" → collapses
  ─ Perception: "I know properties" → collapses
  ─ Force and Understanding: "I know laws" → collapses
  ─ Self-Consciousness: Introduction of desire and recognition
  ─ THE MASTER-SLAVE DIALECTIC (crucial):

  MASTER-SLAVE (Herrschaft und Knechtschaft):
  Two self-consciousnesses meet. Each needs recognition
  from the other. Life-and-death struggle. One submits
  (the slave); one dominates (the master).

  Reversal:
  ─ Master gets recognition from someone they've degraded
    → worthless recognition
  ─ Slave works on the world, transforming it
    → finds themselves in their work
    → achieves genuine self-consciousness through labor

  This dialectic is the foundation of Marx's theory of
  alienated labor, existentialist themes of recognition
  (Sartre, Fanon), and psychoanalytic theory (Lacan).
```

### Philosophy of History

Hegel's most influential (and most controversial) doctrine:

```
  "The rational is actual; the actual is rational."

  History is the development of Geist (Spirit/Mind) toward
  freedom — toward full self-knowledge and actualization.

  Three stages:
  ─ Oriental: one is free (the despot)
  ─ Greek/Roman: some are free (citizens; slaves excluded)
  ─ Germanic/Christian: all are free (in principle)

  The state is "the march of God through the world" —
  the institutional form through which Spirit actualizes itself.

  Critique (Popper, The Open Society): This makes the actual
  rational — whatever happened was necessary and rational.
  History as theodicy: everything turns out for the best.
  This justifies the status quo and can't be used for criticism.

  Response (Hegel scholars): Hegel is describing the logic of
  historical development, not endorsing every historical
  outcome. The "rational" in "the rational is actual" is
  descriptive of logical structure, not moral approval.
```

### Logic

Hegel's Science of Logic is one of the most ambitious philosophical works ever written: a systematic derivation of all basic categories of thought, showing how each gives rise to its contradiction and gets resolved in a higher concept. It begins with Being, Nothing, and Becoming — the most abstract possible categories — and develops through hundreds of pages to the Absolute Idea.

```
  BEING ─── Pure indeterminate being (no content)
           = NOTHING (also no content)
           → BECOMING (the unity of being and nothing:
             the transition between them)

  From this beginning, the entire system of categories
  develops through internal contradiction.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Kant said nothing is knowable** — Kant said things in themselves are not knowable. But phenomena — the world as structured by our minds — is fully knowable. Science is possible and reliable precisely because mind imposes structure. Kant is not a skeptic; he's limiting the domain of knowledge to defend its rigor within that domain.

**The categorical imperative is just the Golden Rule** — The Golden Rule ("do unto others as you would have them do unto you") is a preference-based maxim: what do you want done to you? The categorical imperative doesn't ask about your preferences — it asks what can be universalized without contradiction. A masochist who enjoys pain can't use the Golden Rule to justify hurting others; they can't universalize the maxim without contradiction.

**Hegel's dialectic: thesis/antithesis/synthesis** — Hegel almost never uses these terms. This formulation is from Fichte and was applied to Hegel retroactively. Hegel's dialectical development is more nuanced: a concept reveals its own internal tensions; a higher concept "sublates" (aufhebt) both — preserving what was valid while canceling what was contradictory.

**Hegel's philosophy of history is teleological determinism** — Hegel does hold that history has a rational structure. But he's not committed to the view that everything in history was morally justified or that the process had to go exactly as it did. The "rationality" of history is logical structure, not providential guarantee of good outcomes.

**"Hegel is impossible to understand"** — His prose is genuinely difficult. But the Phenomenology of Spirit has a narrative structure that can be followed. The Logic is harder. The best entry point is: read his lectures (Philosophy of History, Aesthetics, Philosophy of Religion) which are clearer than the systematic works; then approach the Phenomenology; tackle the Logic last.
