# Ancient Greek Philosophy — Pre-Socratics, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle

## Intellectual Lineage

```
PRE-SOCRATICS (600–400 BCE)
What is the world made of?
  Thales (water) ─── Anaximander (apeiron) ─── Anaximenes (air)
  Pythagoras (number) ─── Heraclitus (fire / flux / logos)
  Parmenides (Being is one, unchanging) ─── Zeno (motion is impossible?)
  Empedocles (four elements) ─── Anaxagoras (mind = nous)
  Democritus + Leucippus (atoms + void)
         |
         v
  SOCRATES (469–399 BCE)
  Not: "what is the world made of?" but "how should we live?"
  Method: elenchus (cross-examination to expose ignorance)
  Left no writings. Known through Plato and Xenophon.
         |
         v
  PLATO (428–348 BCE)
  Pupil of Socrates. Founded the Academy (~388 BCE).
  Theory of Forms: reality behind appearances.
         |
         v
  ARISTOTLE (384–322 BCE)
  Pupil of Plato. Founded the Lyceum (~335 BCE).
  Tutor of Alexander the Great.
  Rejects the Theory of Forms: universals are in things, not beyond them.
```

## The Pre-Socratics — The First Philosophers

The Pre-Socratics asked: what is the single underlying principle (arche) of all things? This is the first attempt to explain nature without mythology.

```
QUESTION: What is reality fundamentally?

Thales          ─── Water (everything transforms into water)
Anaximander     ─── Apeiron (the Indefinite — no specific substance)
Heraclitus      ─── Fire + Logos (eternal flux governed by rational principle)
                    "You cannot step into the same river twice"
Parmenides      ─── Being: one, unchanging, indivisible
                    Change is illusion — what IS cannot become what IS NOT
Zeno of Elea    ─── Paradoxes defending Parmenides
                    Achilles and the tortoise: infinite subdivision
                    Arrow paradox: a moving arrow is at rest at every instant
Democritus      ─── Atoms (atomon = uncuttable) + void
                    Everything is atoms in different configurations
```

**Why Parmenides and Zeno matter**: Parmenides' argument that change is logically impossible forced every subsequent Greek philosopher to explain change. Plato responds with the Forms; Aristotle responds with potentiality/actuality. Zeno's paradoxes about infinite divisibility remain philosophically alive — they anticipate debates about continuity, limits, and the mathematics of infinity (Cantor, calculus).

**Democritus matters**: The atomic hypothesis — purely philosophical, no experimental basis — turns out to be essentially correct. This is a remarkable case of correct deduction from minimal empirical input.

## Socrates — The Method as the Contribution

Socrates (469–399 BCE) wrote nothing. Everything we know comes from Plato's dialogues, Xenophon's Memorabilia, and Aristophanes' satirical portrait in The Clouds.

```
THE SOCRATIC METHOD (Elenchus)

  Step 1: Ask the interlocutor to define a virtue or concept
          ("What is piety?" "What is courage?" "What is justice?")

  Step 2: Interlocutor gives a definition

  Step 3: Socrates draws out implications that contradict the definition
          or shows the definition excludes obvious cases

  Step 4: Interlocutor revises definition

  Step 5: Repeat until aporia (impasse / acknowledged ignorance)

The goal is not to win an argument but to expose the gap between
thinking you know something and actually knowing it.
```

**The Apology** (Plato): At his trial for impiety and corrupting youth, Socrates argues that his wisdom consists entirely in knowing that he does not know — unlike the politicians, poets, and craftsmen who think they know things they don't. The oracle at Delphi said no one was wiser than Socrates; Socrates interprets this as a riddle: he alone knows the extent of his ignorance.

**The Socratic paradoxes**:
- Virtue is knowledge — wrongdoing is always a form of ignorance
- No one does wrong willingly — if you know what is good, you do it
- It is better to suffer injustice than to commit it

**The trial and execution** (399 BCE): Socrates was tried, convicted, and executed by drinking hemlock. He refused escape (Crito) on grounds that violating the laws would be unjust, even unjust laws. This sets up the tension between civil disobedience and social contract that runs through political philosophy to Thoreau, Gandhi, Rawls.

## Plato — The Theory of Forms

Plato (428–348 BCE) is the pivot: he uses Socratic method but builds a positive metaphysical system in response. All philosophy, Whitehead famously wrote, is "a series of footnotes to Plato."

### The Theory of Forms

```
THE PLATONIC ONTOLOGY

  Visible World (becoming)          Intelligible World (being)
  ┌─────────────────────┐           ┌─────────────────────────┐
  │ Shadows and images  │           │ Mathematical objects     │
  │ (imagination)       │           │ (reasoning)              │
  │                     │     ──>   │                          │
  │ Physical objects    │           │ The Forms (knowledge)    │
  │ (belief)            │           │ — Beauty Itself          │
  └─────────────────────┘           │ — Justice Itself         │
                                    │ — The Good               │
                                    └─────────────────────────┘
  Divided Line (Republic, Book VI)
```

The Form of Beauty is not any beautiful thing — it is what makes all beautiful things beautiful. It is:
- Eternal (not subject to change)
- Non-spatial (not located anywhere)
- Perfectly instantiates the property (unlike any physical beautiful thing)
- Known by reason, not by senses

This is essentially Platonic realism about universals — the position that types/properties exist independently of their instances. This was an active debate in CS-adjacent philosophy-of-mathematics: Platonism vs. nominalism about mathematical objects.

### The Allegory of the Cave (Republic, Book VII)

```
  INSIDE CAVE                          OUTSIDE CAVE
  ┌────────────────────────────┐       ┌─────────────────────┐
  │ Prisoners chained, facing  │       │ Actual objects       │
  │ wall. See only shadows     │  ──>  │ The Sun (= The Good) │
  │ of objects carried past    │       │ Direct knowledge     │
  │ the fire. Take shadows     │       └─────────────────────┘
  │ for reality.               │
  └────────────────────────────┘
  Shadows = sensory appearances
  Objects = Forms
  Sun = The Form of the Good
  Prisoners = ordinary people
  Escaped prisoner = philosopher
  Forced return = philosopher returning to teach/rule
```

The allegory does triple duty: epistemology (degrees of knowledge), metaphysics (degrees of reality), and political philosophy (philosopher-kings must return to govern).

### The Republic — Political Philosophy

Plato's Republic asks: what is justice? The answer is structural:

```
  SOUL                    CITY-STATE (POLIS)
  ┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────────────────┐
  │ Reason           │ ── │ Rulers (Guardians)    │
  │ Spirit/Passion   │ ── │ Soldiers/Auxiliaries  │
  │ Appetite         │ ── │ Producers/Craftsmen   │
  └──────────────────┘    └──────────────────────┘

  Justice = each part doing its proper function
  Injustice = parts out of harmony, reason not ruling
```

The Republic's utopia is deeply anti-democratic: ruled by philosopher-kings who have seen the Form of the Good. The critique has been made ever since (Karl Popper's The Open Society) that this is proto-totalitarian. Plato also advocates censorship of art and poetry (Book X) on grounds that art imitates appearances, which are already imitations of Forms — so art is "twice removed from reality" and corrupts by appealing to emotion.

### Other Key Dialogues

| Dialogue | Topic | Key Argument |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Meno** | Virtue, teachability | Knowledge is recollection (anamnesis) |
| **Phaedo** | Immortality of soul | Soul is akin to Forms — eternal, not composite |
| **Symposium** | Love (eros) | Ladder from physical beauty → Form of Beauty |
| **Theaetetus** | Knowledge | Knowledge ≠ perception; first "justified true belief" analysis |
| **Parmenides** | Self-critique of Forms | Third Man argument: Forms need a Form to explain them |
| **Timaeus** | Cosmology | Demiurge shapes world according to Forms |
| **Laws** | Practical politics | Second-best city; laws instead of philosopher-kings |

**The Parmenides** is remarkable: Plato submits his own Theory of Forms to devastating criticism (the Third Man regress: if F-things are F because they participate in Form-F, and Form-F is itself F, we need another Form to explain what F-Form and F-things have in common — infinite regress). He never fully resolves the objection.

## Aristotle — The Organizer of Knowledge

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) is the first systematic thinker. He wrote on logic, biology, physics, metaphysics, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics. His contribution is not one big idea but an entire intellectual framework.

### Logic — The Organon

Aristotle invented syllogistic logic — the first formal system of inference:

```
  CATEGORICAL SYLLOGISM

  Major premise:  All M are P         All men are mortal
  Minor premise:  All S are M         Socrates is a man
  Conclusion:     All S are P         Socrates is mortal

  Valid forms (moods): Barbara, Celarent, Darii, Ferio...
  (Medieval logicians catalogued 256 forms, 24 valid)
```

This dominated logic for 2,000 years until Frege's Begriffsschrift (1879) superseded it with predicate logic. Frege showed that syllogistic logic can't handle relational reasoning ("every person loves some person") — it can only handle subject-predicate sentences with the same grammatical form.

Aristotle also distinguished:
- **Deduction** (sullogismos): conclusion necessarily follows
- **Induction** (epagoge): generalization from cases
- **Demonstration** (apodeixis): scientific proof from first principles

### Metaphysics — Substance, Form, Matter

Aristotle rejects Plato's separate Forms. Universals don't exist independently — they exist only in particulars.

```
  PLATONIC VIEW         ARISTOTELIAN VIEW
  ┌──────────────┐      ┌────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Form of Dog  │      │ Each individual dog has:       │
  │ (separate,   │      │  Form (dogness) — in the dog   │
  │  eternal)    │      │  Matter (flesh, bone) — in the dog
  └──────────────┘      │                                 │
  Dogs participate in   │ Universal "dog" exists because  │
  the Form.             │ many individuals share a form.  │
                        └────────────────────────────────┘
```

Key Aristotelian distinctions:

| Concept | What It Is | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Substance** (ousia) | Primary being; individual things | This horse, Socrates |
| **Matter** (hyle) | What something is made of | Bronze |
| **Form** (morphe/eidos) | What makes it what it is | The shape of the statue |
| **Potentiality** | What a thing can become | Acorn's potential to become oak |
| **Actuality** | What a thing is fully realized | The oak tree |
| **Four causes** | Complete explanation of anything | (below) |

**The Four Causes** — Aristotle's theory of complete explanation:

```
  To fully explain anything, state all four causes:

  MATERIAL CAUSE    ─── What it's made of         (marble)
  FORMAL CAUSE      ─── What form/pattern it has  (shape of Hermes)
  EFFICIENT CAUSE   ─── What produced it          (the sculptor's action)
  FINAL CAUSE       ─── What it's for             (to honor the god)

  Modern science drops the final cause (teleology) as unscientific.
  Biology may be the exception — function language seems ineliminable.
```

### Ethics — Eudaimonia and Virtue

The Nicomachean Ethics opens: "Every art and every inquiry, every action and pursuit is thought to aim at some good." What is the highest good? Eudaimonia — usually translated "happiness" but better rendered "flourishing" or "living well."

```
  Eudaimonia is not:
  - Pleasure (that's for Epicurus)
  - Honor (that depends on others' opinions)
  - Wealth (a means, not an end)

  Eudaimonia IS:
  - Activity of the soul in accordance with virtue
  - The actualization of our distinctively human capacities
  - A whole life, not a moment (you can't be happy for a day)
```

**Virtue (arete)** is the key concept:

```
  DOCTRINE OF THE MEAN

  Virtue lies between two vices:
  Deficiency ──────── Virtue ──────── Excess

  Cowardice ─────── Courage ─────── Rashness
  Miserliness ───── Generosity ──── Prodigality
  Servility ──────── Dignity ──────── Arrogance

  Virtue is not a fixed midpoint — it's the appropriate
  response to circumstances, as a person of practical
  wisdom (phronesis) would recognize.
```

Phronesis (practical wisdom) is the master virtue — the capacity to perceive the morally salient features of a situation and respond appropriately. This is irreducible to rules; it requires cultivated judgment.

### Politics

Aristotle: "Man is by nature a political animal" (zoon politikon). The polis is not an artifact — it is the natural environment for human flourishing. Outside the polis: either a beast or a god.

Politics is empirical: Aristotle and his students collected 158 constitutional histories. The Politics distinguishes governments by who rules and whether they rule for the common good:

```
  ┌──────────┬────────────────┬──────────────────┐
  │ Who Rules│ Good Form      │ Corrupt Form     │
  ├──────────┼────────────────┼──────────────────┤
  │ One      │ Kingship       │ Tyranny          │
  │ Few      │ Aristocracy    │ Oligarchy        │
  │ Many     │ Polity         │ Democracy*       │
  └──────────┴────────────────┴──────────────────┘
  *Democracy in Aristotle's sense = mob rule for self-interest.
   What we call democracy he'd call polity or mixed constitution.
```

Aristotle's best constitution is a mixed regime where the middle class dominates — neither the very rich nor the very poor, who both tend toward extremes.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Pre-Socratics are primitive"** — They're not. Parmenides' argument that Being cannot come from Non-Being is logically rigorous. Democritus' atoms are a genuine theoretical achievement. The Pre-Socratics established the philosophical demand for natural explanation without appeal to supernatural agency.

**Plato's "Socrates" is not the historical Socrates** — The early dialogues (Apology, Crito, Euthyphro) are probably closer to the historical Socrates. The middle dialogues (Phaedo, Republic, Symposium) use "Socrates" as a mouthpiece for Plato's own theories. The late dialogues (Parmenides, Sophist, Timaeus) have "Socrates" say less and less. Plato became increasingly dissatisfied with his own earlier views.

**Aristotle is not just "Plato made practical"** — Aristotle's metaphysics, logic, and philosophy of science are genuinely original and in direct tension with Plato, not just a pragmatic version of the same thing.

**The Third Man Argument** (Plato, Parmenides) — If all F-things are F because they participate in Form-F, and Form-F is itself F, then Form-F and all F-things need a further Form to explain what they share — infinite regress. Plato raises this himself and doesn't solve it. Aristotle uses this as a reason to abandon separate Forms entirely.

**Eudaimonia ≠ subjective happiness** — Aristotle's eudaimonia is an objective condition: a life of virtuous activity. A person can believe they are happy while living badly (Plato's tyrant who is enslaved to his appetites). This is prescriptive, not descriptive. Modern "well-being" theories (preference satisfaction, hedonism) are the empiricist's response to this.
