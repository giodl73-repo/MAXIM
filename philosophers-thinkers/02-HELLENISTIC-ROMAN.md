# Hellenistic and Roman Philosophy — Epicureans, Stoics, Skeptics, Neoplatonists

## The Shift After Alexander

Alexander the Great's conquests (336–323 BCE) dissolved the Greek city-state as the natural political unit. The question that had animated Aristotle — how to flourish as a citizen of a polis — became less pressing. The new question: how do I live well as an individual in a vast cosmopolitan empire?

```
HELLENISTIC SCHOOLS (323 BCE – 31 BCE)
All four ask the same practical question: How should I live?
They differ in their answer.

  ┌─────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ EPICUREANS  │ Cultivate pleasure (ataraxia). Withdraw.      │
  │             │ Friendship, simple life, freedom from pain.   │
  ├─────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ STOICS      │ Cultivate virtue (arete). Engage.             │
  │             │ Reason governs cosmos; virtue = living by it. │
  ├─────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ SKEPTICS    │ Suspend judgment (epoché). Neither fully      │
  │             │ engage nor withdraw — we can't know enough.   │
  │             │ Ataraxia through non-commitment.              │
  ├─────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ CYNICS      │ Reject convention entirely. Virtue alone      │
  │             │ suffices; social norms are worthless.         │
  └─────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Epicurus — Pleasure as Freedom from Pain

**Epicurus (341–270 BCE)** founded his school — the Garden — in Athens around 307 BCE. The Garden was radical: it admitted women and slaves, forming a genuine philosophical community rather than a lecture hall.

### The Physics

Epicurus adopted Democritus' atomism: everything is atoms and void. The soul is made of fine atoms; death is their dissolution. There is no afterlife. The gods exist but are indifferent to human affairs — they have no reason to intervene in the world.

This physics is a liberation: if death is nothing to me (when I exist, death is absent; when death is present, I am absent), then the fear of death is irrational. Much of human misery comes from fearing what is not actually bad.

### The Ethics

```
TYPES OF PLEASURE (Epicurus)

  Kinetic pleasures ──────── Active pleasures (eating, sex, movement)
  Katastematic pleasures ─── States of contentment (ataraxia, aponia)

  ATARAXIA = tranquility, freedom from mental disturbance
  APONIA = freedom from bodily pain

  Epicurus: katastematic pleasure is the goal.
  Not hedonism in the vulgar sense — not "maximize sensation."
  Rather: achieve stable, undisturbed contentment.
```

The calculus of desire:

```
  Natural and necessary ─────── Pursue (food, shelter, friendship)
  Natural but unnecessary ────── Moderate (feasting, luxury)
  Neither natural nor necessary ─ Avoid (fame, political power)
```

Friendship is central — Epicurus called it the greatest of all goods. This is not inconsistent: friendship produces stable, genuine pleasure, unlike the pleasures of ambition which create dependency on external conditions.

### Influence

Epicurus was enormously popular in antiquity. Lucretius' De Rerum Natura (c. 55 BCE) is the great Epicurean poem in Latin — a philosophical didactic poem explaining atomic physics and its ethical implications. Christianity suppressed Epicureanism (pleasure-seeking seemed immoral). The recovery of Lucretius in 1417 (Poggio Bracciolini found a monastery manuscript) is considered a trigger of the Renaissance and the early scientific worldview. Modern utilitarian ethics traces partly to Epicurus.

## The Stoics — Virtue Is Sufficient for Happiness

Stoicism is one of the most influential philosophical traditions in Western history. Founded by Zeno of Citium (~334–262 BCE) in Athens, it produced three of the greatest philosophical writers: Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius.

### The Physics

The Stoics were materialist monists: everything is corporeal. The cosmos is a single living rational being, governed by the Logos (rational principle, also called pneuma, Zeus, Providence, Nature, Fate). Everything that happens is fated — the expression of the rational structure of the cosmos.

This creates the Stoic problem: if everything is fated, how can anything be genuinely up to me?

### The Dichotomy of Control

```
  WHAT IS UP TO ME (eph' hemin)     │  WHAT IS NOT UP TO ME (ouk eph' hemin)
  ──────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────
  My beliefs (hypolepsis)           │  Body, reputation, property
  My judgments (kriseis)            │  Political power, health
  My desires (orexis)               │  Other people's actions
  My impulses to act (hormê)        │  Death, disease, poverty
                                    │
  These are exercises of rational   │  These are "indifferent" (adiaphora)
  faculty — within my control.      │  — they do not affect virtue.
```

This is Epictetus' central teaching (Enchiridion, opening lines): "Some things are in our control and others not." The key error is treating externals as goods or evils — they are indifferent. Virtue alone is the good; vice alone is the evil.

### The Four Virtues

```
  STOIC VIRTUE SYSTEM

  Wisdom (phronesis/sophia) ─── Knowing what is truly good/evil/indifferent
  Justice (dikaiosyne) ──────── Acting rightly toward others
  Courage (andreia) ─────────── Enduring hardship with reason intact
  Temperance (sophrosyne) ────── Governing desires with reason

  These are not separable — they are aspects of a unified rational
  disposition. "Virtue is one" — if you have one, you have all.
```

### Passions and Emotions

The Stoics distinguished passions (pathe — disturbing emotional reactions based on false judgments about what is good/evil) from good emotional states (eupatheiai — appropriate rational responses):

```
  PASSIONS (false judgment)         EUPATHEIAI (rational equivalents)
  ─────────────────────────         ─────────────────────────────────
  Pleasure (false good present) →   Joy (correct evaluation of present good)
  Desire (false good future)    →   Wish (correct wanting of true good)
  Fear (false evil future)      →   Caution (correct avoidance of true evil)
  Pain (false evil present)     →   [Endurance — no positive equivalent]
```

The Stoics are not arguing for emotional flatness. They're arguing for correct judgment: align your emotional responses with accurate evaluation of what is and is not genuinely good.

### Key Stoic Texts

| Figure | Dates | Key Work | What It Does |
|--------|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Zeno of Citium** | 334–262 BCE | Republic (lost) | Founded the school; virtue = living according to nature |
| **Chrysippus** | 279–206 BCE | Extensive fragments | The systematizer; "without Chrysippus, no Stoa" |
| **Seneca** | 4 BCE–65 CE | Letters to Lucilius, On the Shortness of Life | Practical Stoic ethics; how to live well under Nero's tyranny |
| **Epictetus** | 50–135 CE | Discourses, Enchiridion | Radical internalism; enslaved man who became a philosopher |
| **Marcus Aurelius** | 121–180 CE | Meditations | Private journal of emperor practicing Stoicism against the grain of empire |

**Epictetus** was born a slave. His master broke his leg deliberately; Epictetus told him calmly that the leg would break. The story, probably apocryphal, captures Stoic psychology: external events cannot touch what is truly yours — your rational response to them.

**Marcus Aurelius** governed Rome for 19 years, fought wars constantly, watched children die, managed plague — and spent nights writing private philosophical reminders to practice Stoicism. The Meditations are not a polished treatise but a self-addressed journal of practical philosophy under pressure.

### Cosmopolitanism

The Stoics invented cosmopolitanism: "I am a citizen of the world." Because all humans share the Logos (rational nature), all are members of a single community regardless of ethnicity, gender, or social rank. This is the philosophical foundation for natural law theory and Kantian universal ethics.

## Skepticism — Academic and Pyrrhonian

```
ACADEMIC SKEPTICISM                    PYRRHONIAN SKEPTICISM
(Plato's Academy, ~268 BCE onward)     (Pyrrho of Elis, ~360–270 BCE)

  Arcesilaus, Carneades                  Pyrrho → Timon → Sextus Empiricus

  Moderate skepticism:                   Radical skepticism:
  Cannot know anything with certainty,   For every argument there is an equally
  but can act on what is "persuasive"     strong counter-argument (isostheneia).
  (pithanon).                             Suspend judgment completely →
                                          ataraxia results from not worrying
  Practical: follow appearances,          about what is true.
  don't commit to theories.
```

**Sextus Empiricus** (2nd century CE) is the main surviving source for Pyrrhonism. His Outlines of Pyrrhonism catalogs the "Ten Modes" — arguments showing why perception and reason don't give certain knowledge about how things really are.

The skeptical challenge — how do you know what you know? — is revived by Descartes (as the starting point of his certainty project) and remains central to epistemology.

## Plotinus and Neoplatonism

**Plotinus (204–270 CE)** is the last great ancient philosopher. He lived in Rome, taught widely, and wrote the Enneads (collected by his student Porphyry after his death). Neoplatonism synthesizes Plato with later developments, adding mystical and religious dimensions Plato didn't have.

### The Three Hypostases

```
  NEOPLATONIC ONTOLOGY (Plotinus)

  THE ONE ─────────────────── Beyond all predication, beyond being,
  (to Hen)                     beyond thought. Pure unity, no multiplicity.
       │                       The source of everything by emanation.
       │ (emanation — not      (Emanation is like light from the sun:
       │  deliberate creation)  the sun doesn't choose to emit light.)
       v
  NOUS (Intellect/Mind) ────── Eternal thinking of eternal Forms.
                                First being. Thinking and what is thought
       │                        are identical here.
       │ emanation
       v
  PSYCHE (Soul) ────────────── Mediates between Intellect and matter.
                                Individual souls are parts of World Soul.
       │
       │ further descent
       v
  MATTER ───────────────────── Privation, darkness — not evil exactly,
                                just the absence of being and form.
```

The goal of human life is to ascend through Soul and Intellect back toward union with the One. This ascent is achieved through philosophical meditation and ultimately mystical union (henosis). Plotinus claimed to have achieved this union several times during his life.

### Why Neoplatonism Matters

1. It is the philosophical framework through which Christianity, Islam, and Judaism absorbed Greek philosophy. Augustine was a Neoplatonist before conversion; Islamic philosophy (Avicenna) worked within and against this framework.
2. The concept of emanation (reality flowing from a single source by necessity, not by will) is the metaphysical alternative to creation from nothing.
3. The One that is "beyond being" and "beyond thought" grounds the mystical tradition in all three Abrahamic religions.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Epicurean means pleasure-loving"** — In common usage, yes. In philosophy, Epicurus argues against intense pleasures, which he thinks destabilize tranquility. An Epicurean life is simple, withdrawn, centered on philosophical friendship and moderate pleasures. Think: garden, good friends, no political ambition.

**"Stoic means emotionless"** — In common usage, yes. In philosophy, Stoics distinguish irrational passions (based on false judgments) from appropriate emotional responses (eupatheiai). A Stoic can love their children, feel genuine joy, be moved by beauty — but won't be devastated by loss of externals.

**Epicureans and Stoics are simply opposed** — They differ on withdrawal vs. engagement and pleasure vs. virtue, but agree that: (1) the goal is ataraxia/tranquility, (2) false beliefs about good and evil cause human misery, (3) philosophy is therapeutic. Two different prescriptions for the same diagnosis.

**Plotinus as "later Plato"** — Neoplatonism significantly transforms Plato. The concept of the One (beyond all predication) is not in Plato's dialogues. The systematic hypostases (One → Nous → Psyche) are Plotinus' invention. The emphasis on mystical union has no clear parallel in Plato. Neoplatonism is a creative philosophical development, not commentary.

**Marcus Aurelius' Stoicism vs. his political decisions** — He persecuted Christians (not for religious reasons but for refusing civic duties) and designated his dissolute son Commodus as successor (widely considered catastrophic). Stoic philosophy didn't prevent either. This is regularly cited to show the limits of philosophical ethics as practical guidance.
