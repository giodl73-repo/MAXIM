# 01 — Writing Systems: From Marks to Alphabet

## The Big Picture

Writing is not a natural human capability — it is a technology, invented independently perhaps three or four times in human history and diffused from those origins. Every modern western alphabet traces a single lineage through the Phoenician consonantal abjad. Understanding that lineage gives you the substrate on which all typography operates.

```
WRITING SYSTEM GENEALOGY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3200 BCE  SUMERIAN CUNEIFORM ─────────────────────────────────────────┐

          logographic + phonetic + determinative                        │
```

## Cuneiform: The Original Database Entry

Cuneiform (~3200 BCE, Sumer — modern southern Iraq) is the oldest confirmed writing system. Its origin is unromantic: **it was an accounting tool**.

```
CUNEIFORM DEVELOPMENT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STAGE 1: TOKENS (~3500 BCE)
  Clay tokens of different shapes representing commodities
  Sealed in clay envelopes → impressions made on outside
  → eventually just impress the token shape directly → tablet

STAGE 2: PICTOGRAMS (~3200 BCE)
  [head of an ox] = ox       [ear of barley] = grain
  Problem: cannot write "to pay" or "debt" or "3 years ago"

STAGE 3: PHONETIC REBUS (~3100 BCE)
  Use a picture for its SOUND, not its meaning
  Like writing "believe" as [bee] + [leaf] in English
  Now you can write abstract concepts and proper names

STAGE 4: SYLLABARY (~2800 BCE onward)
  ~600 distinct signs, each representing a CV or CVC syllable
  NOT an alphabet (not broken to individual phonemes)

MEDIUM CONSTRAINT:
  Clay tablet + reed stylus held at ~45° angle
  → Can't draw smooth curves efficiently in wet clay
  → All strokes become: horizontal wedge / vertical wedge / diagonal wedge
  → "Cuneiform" from Latin cuneus = wedge
  MEDIUM DRIVES FORM — the fundamental lesson of letterform history

SPREAD:
  Sumerian → Akkadian → Babylonian → Assyrian → Elamite → Hittite
  Adopted by non-Sumerian languages as the existing infrastructure
  Last dated cuneiform tablet: 75 CE (Babylonian astronomical record)
  Deciphered: 1850s — Henry Rawlinson + Behistun Inscription
  (trilingual cliff inscription: Old Persian + Elamite + Babylonian)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Egyptian Hieroglyphics: The Three-Layer System

Egyptian writing (~3000 BCE) operated on three simultaneous levels — which is why it took until 1822 to decipher it.

```
THREE TYPES OF HIEROGLYPHIC SIGNS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LOGOGRAMS (Word signs)
  [picture of sun] = "sun" or "day" (read as the thing depicted)

PHONETIC SIGNS (Sound signs)
  Uniliteral: [mouth] = r        (one consonant)
  Biliteral:  [arm]   = a + w    (two consonants)
  Triliteral: [scarab] = h + p + r  (three consonants)
  NOTE: Egyptian is CONSONANTAL — vowels not written

DETERMINATIVES (Semantic classifiers, UNPRONOUNCED)
  Added after words to signal semantic category
  [legs] at end of word → verb of motion
  [papyrus roll] → abstract concept / written word
  Determinatives are metadata — the hashtag of 3000 BCE

EXAMPLE OF AMBIGUITY RESOLUTION:
  Same consonants k-t-b could mean different things:
  Add [man working] → craftsman who writes
  Add [papyrus roll] → written document
  Add [legs] → the act of going to write

THREE REGISTERS (simultaneous):
  Hieroglyphics → monumental, cut in stone, slow
  Hieratic      → priestly cursive on papyrus, 10× faster
  Demotic       → everyday script, even faster; distinct system
  (Same writing system, three implementations for different media/context)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**The Rosetta Stone and decipherment**: Discovered 1799 by Napoleon's troops at Rashid (Rosetta) in the Nile Delta. Contains a priestly decree in all three scripts: hieroglyphics (prestige), Demotic (common), and Greek (Ptolemaic administration). Thomas Young identified the royal cartouches (oval rings enclosing names). Jean-François Champollion made the final breakthrough in 1822 — his key advantage was Coptic scholarship. Coptic is the latest form of the Egyptian language (used by Christian Egyptians, still alive in liturgy) and preserves the vowels that hieroglyphics omitted. He could reconstruct pronunciation from Coptic, then verify against Greek transliterations of Ptolemy and Cleopatra in the cartouches.

## The Phoenician Alphabet: Trade as the Driver of Simplification

The Phoenician abjad (~1050 BCE) is the most consequential writing innovation after writing itself. From it descend Greek, Latin, Arabic, Hebrew — virtually every script in the western world and Middle East.

```
PHOENICIAN ALPHABET (~1050 BCE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

22 letters, all consonants (ABJAD — from Arabic: alef, bet, jimel, dal)

'alef  = ox head  → later became Greek Alpha  (A)
bet    = house    → later became Greek Beta   (B)
gimel  = camel    → later became Greek Gamma  (G/C)
dalet  = door     → later became Greek Delta  (D)
he     = window   → later became Greek Epsilon (E)
waw    = hook     → later became Greek Upsilon (Y/V/F)
[... 16 more ...]

Acrophonic principle: each letter named for object whose name
starts with that letter's sound. A is for 'Alef (ox), etc.

NO VOWELS. Why is this sufficient for Semitic languages?

SEMITIC ROOT SYSTEM:
  k-t-b = root meaning "writing/recording"
  kataba  = he wrote       (past tense)
  kitab   = book           (noun)
  katib   = writer         (agent noun)
  maktub  = written        (passive participle)
  maktaba = library        (place noun)

  The three consonants carry the semantic content.
  Vowels carry grammatical function: tense, number, case.
  Knowing the root + context → consonants sufficient.

  Indo-European languages (Greek, Latin, English) are NOT
  root-structured this way. Vowels carry independent semantic
  weight → vowels had to be added to make the alphabet work.

PROTO-SINAITIC ANCESTOR (~1850 BCE):
  Found at Egyptian turquoise mines in Sinai, used by Semitic workers
  They extracted the acrophonic principle from Egyptian hieroglyphics:
  took the uniliteral phonetic signs and re-read them as Semitic abjad
  Oldest known alphabet — engineers adapting existing infrastructure

WHY PHOENICIA?
  Dominant maritime traders in eastern Mediterranean (Tyre, Sidon, Byblos)
  Long-distance trade needs fast, portable, learnable record-keeping
  Egyptian hieroglyphics: years of training; clay tablets: heavy, fragile
  Phoenician alphabet: learnable in days; writable on papyrus/leather/wood
  → Spread wherever Phoenician ships went
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Greek: The Vowel Revolution

Greeks acquired the Phoenician alphabet through trade contact around 800–900 BCE. What they did with it was structurally decisive: **they created the first true alphabet by adding explicit vowel letters**.

```
GREEK ADAPTATION OF PHOENICIAN (~800 BCE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE ENGINEERING PROBLEM:
  Greek is Indo-European — vowels carry full semantic weight
  Several Phoenician consonants have no Greek equivalents
  Those letters were "spare capacity" in Greek phonology

THE SOLUTION — BRILLIANT REUSE:
  Take Phoenician letters whose consonant doesn't exist in Greek
  Repurpose them as vowel letters

  Phoenician 'alef (glottal stop) → Greek Alpha  (A) = /a/
  Phoenician he   (pharyngeal-h) → Greek Epsilon (E) = /e/
  Phoenician het  (pharyngeal)   → Greek Eta     (H) = long /ē/
  Phoenician yod  (y-glide)      → Greek Iota    (I) = /i/
  Phoenician ayin (pharyngeal)   → Greek Omicron (O) = /o/
  Phoenician waw  (w-glide)      → Greek Upsilon (Y) = /u/

RESULT: A TRUE ALPHABET
  Every phoneme — consonant or vowel — has a dedicated letter.
  This seems obvious only because we've used alphabets for 2800 years.
  The Phoenicians managed 3000 years without it.

DIRECTIONALITY STANDARDIZATION:
  Early Greek written in BOUSTROPHEDON:
  "as the ox plows" — left-to-right on line 1,
                      right-to-left on line 2, alternating
  Letters were mirrored on right-to-left lines.
  (~500 BCE: standardized to left-to-right, permanently)

REGIONAL VARIANTS:
  Western Greek (Chalcidian) → Etruscan → Latin
  Eastern Greek (Ionic)      → Classical Greek standard
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**The visual forms**: Many modern capital letters — A, B, E, Z, H, I, K, M, N, O, T, X — are essentially Greek letterforms transmitted through Rome with minimal change over 2,800 years. The Roman monumental capitals on the Trajan Column (113 CE) are the direct ancestors of the uppercase letters in this document.

## Latin Alphabet Evolution: From Chisel to Lowercase

The Latin alphabet passed through distinct phases, each driven by a change in medium or use case:

```
LATIN LETTERFORM EVOLUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ETRUSCAN (~700 BCE)
  Western Greek variant; modified for Etruscan phonology
  Rome absorbed Etruscan culture including the alphabet ~600 BCE
  26 letters used; some dropped (C/G confusion; Q/K/C overlap)

ROMAN MONUMENTAL CAPITALS (~200 BCE – 200 CE)
  Brushed on stone first to guide chisel; then incised
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ WHY STROKE CONTRAST EXISTS:                          │
  │ A chisel cuts wider strokes at 0°/90° and narrow    │
  │ diagonals — exactly replicating a broad-nib pen     │
  │ held at ~30°. Medium → stroke contrast.             │
  │ This is the origin of the thick/thin variation      │
  │ that distinguishes serif fonts to this day.         │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  Trajan Column (113 CE) = definitive canonical model
  Proportions studied/formalized by Renaissance typographers
  → OUR UPPERCASE LETTERFORMS, structurally unchanged

ROMAN CURSIVE (~1st BCE – 4th CE)
  Fast handwriting for wax tablets and everyday papyrus
  Ligatures, loops, connected strokes; precursor to italic
  Hard to read; not the typographic mainstream ancestor

UNCIAL (~300–800 CE)
  Rounded forms suited to quill writing on vellum
  Letters become less angular; a, d, e, g, m, u go round
  Single unified case (no separate minuscule/majuscule yet)
  Used by Irish/Anglo-Saxon monks → Book of Kells

HALF-UNCIAL (~5th–9th century CE)
  Ascenders and descenders begin to develop
  b, d, h, l extend above; g, p, q extend below
  First true precursor to lowercase letters
  Irish/Northumbrian variants → distinctive Insular script

CAROLINGIAN MINUSCULE (789 CE)
  Charlemagne standardized handwriting across the Frankish empire
  Alcuin of York led the reform from Aachen
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ WHAT CAROLINGIAN INTRODUCED:                                 │
  │  - Consistent clear letter forms across all scriptoria       │
  │  - Distinct ascenders (b, d, f, h, k, l) and descenders     │
  │    (g, j, p, q, y) — contrast structure of reading          │
  │  - Used alongside Roman capitals = MIXED CASE                │
  │  - Legible at speed; economical on vellum                    │
  │  - OUR LOWERCASE LETTERS — a, b, c, d, e, f, g ... = this   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

GOTHIC / BLACKLETTER (~11th–15th century)
  Carolingian compressed/angularized in Northern Europe
  More text per page → economical on expensive vellum/parchment
  Gutenberg used Gothic type for the 42-line Bible
  (Readers expected manuscripts to look like this)

HUMANIST MINUSCULE (~1400 CE)
  Italian Renaissance scholars rejected Gothic as "barbarous"
  Revived Carolingian minuscule as the classical ancient hand
  (They thought it was Roman; it was 9th-century Frankish)
  Poggio Bracciolini, Niccolo Niccoli led the revival
  → Nicolas Jenson cut this as metal type in Venice (1470)
  → OUR ROMAN TYPEFACE: "classical" Carolingian in metal

ITALIC (Aldus Manutius, Venice, 1501)
  Condensed version of humanist cursive for portable books
  Fit more text per page; cheaper to print and transport
  Named for Italy; originally a text face, not just emphasis

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The historical irony: Renaissance humanists believed they were reviving ancient Roman handwriting when they revived Carolingian. Their "antique" hand was only 600 years old, not 1,400. But the letterforms they produced became the models Jenson and Aldus Manutius cut in metal — and those metal forms became the ancestors of every roman and italic typeface.

## Other Major Scripts

```
SCRIPT TYPOLOGY COMPARISON
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ARABIC (~400 CE standardized)
  Descends: Phoenician → Aramaic → Nabataean → Arabic
  22 base letters → 28 with dot diacritics
  ABJAD: consonants primary; vowels are optional diacritics (harakat)
  CONNECTED CURSIVE: letters connect within words
  4 positional forms per letter: isolated / initial / medial / final
  RIGHT TO LEFT
  Unicode complexity: joining types + contextual substitution in GSUB
  OpenType Arabic fonts contain hundreds of contextual substitutions

HEBREW (~200 BCE square script)
  Semitic abjad; right-to-left; 22 consonant letters
  "Square Hebrew" from Babylonian exile via Aramaic
  Vowel diacritics (nikud) added by Masoretes (~600–950 CE)
  for liturgical precision; most modern Hebrew text is unvoweled

DEVANAGARI (~1200 CE; ancestor Brahmi ~300 BCE)
  Used for Sanskrit, Hindi, Marathi, Nepali
  ABUGIDA: consonants carry inherent /a/ vowel
  Other vowels = diacritics above/below/before/after consonant
  Distinctive horizontal bar (shirorekha) connecting letters at top
  LEFT TO RIGHT; complex consonant clusters (conjuncts)

CHINESE (Oracle Bone ~1200 BCE → modern)
  LOGOGRAPHIC: one character = one morpheme
  ~50,000 characters historically; ~3,500 for practical literacy
  Semantic radical + phonetic component structure
  Why moveable type didn't spread: 50,000 sorts vs. 26 Latin letters
  Simplified (PRC 1950s) vs. Traditional (Taiwan, HK) split
  OpenType CJK fonts: 10–20+ MB (glyph count drives file size)

JAPANESE (hybrid)
  Three simultaneous systems:
  - Hiragana: 46 syllabic signs (native words, grammar particles)
  - Katakana: 46 syllabic signs (foreign loanwords, technical terms)
  - Kanji: Chinese characters for semantic/content words (~2000 required)
  Traditional: top-to-bottom right-to-left
  Modern: left-to-right (horizontal layout dominant)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Bridge: Writing Systems to Unicode

Unicode is the engineering resolution of script diversity. The Unicode Standard encodes 149,000+ characters across 159 scripts as of Unicode 15.1.

```
UNICODE AND WRITING SYSTEMS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Every code point has properties:
  U+0041  Name: LATIN CAPITAL LETTER A
          Script: Latin
          Category: Uppercase Letter (Lu)
          Bidi Class: Left-to-Right (L)

  U+0627  Name: ARABIC LETTER ALEF
          Script: Arabic
          Category: Other Letter (Lo)
          Bidi Class: Right-to-Left (AL) ← drives RTL rendering

BIDI ALGORITHM (Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm):
  Mixed RTL+LTR text requires Unicode Bidi Algorithm (UAX #9)
  Logical order (Unicode string) ≠ Visual order (rendered)
  "Hello مرحبا world" — Arabic runs right-to-left within LTR context
  LRM (U+200E) and RLM (U+200F) marks control override behavior

FONT COVERAGE:
  Any font covers a SUBSET of Unicode code points
  Missing glyph → OS font fallback (render from different font)
  □ (tofu box) = missing glyph; "Noto" = "No Tofu"
  Noto Pan-CJK: the attempt at universal coverage

CODE POINT → GLYPH (the gap most engineers miss):
  U+0066 + U+0069 ('f' + 'i') = two code points
  A font may substitute the 'fi' ligature glyph for these
  This is GSUB (glyph substitution) in OpenType
  Unicode character = abstract; Glyph = visual representation
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Oldest writing system? | Sumerian cuneiform (~3200 BCE); logistics, not literature |
| Independent writing origins? | Sumer, Egypt, China, possibly Indus Valley; others derived |
| Who invented the alphabet? | Proto-Sinaitic Semites (~1850 BCE); Phoenicians standardized it (~1050 BCE) |
| Who added vowels? | Greeks (~800 BCE), reusing Phoenician letters with no Greek equivalents |
| Where do uppercase letters come from? | Roman monumental capitals; Trajan Column (113 CE) canonical |
| Where do lowercase letters come from? | Carolingian minuscule (789 CE), revived as "antique" by Renaissance humanists |
| Why is Arabic font rendering complex? | 4 positional forms per letter + connected cursive + RTL + bidi algorithm |
| Why are CJK fonts large? | 50,000+ logographic characters vs 26 Latin letters |
| What is an abjad? | Writing system with only consonants (Arabic, Hebrew, Phoenician) |
| What is an abugida? | Consonant-primary with inherent vowel; other vowels as diacritics (Devanagari) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Alphabet vs Abjad vs Abugida vs Syllabary**
An alphabet has separate letters for consonants AND vowels (Greek, Latin). An abjad has only consonant letters; vowels inferred from context (Arabic, Hebrew, Phoenician). An abugida has consonants with inherent vowels; other vowels are diacritics (Devanagari, Ethiopian Ge'ez). A syllabary has one symbol per syllable (Japanese kana, Linear B, Cherokee).

**Demotic is not "simplified hieroglyphics"**
Demotic descended from Hieratic (which descended from Hieroglyphics), but the three forms coexisted with different social registers. It is not a simplification sequence — it is register-specific parallel development, like formal/informal registers in spoken language.

**"Romans invented uppercase, monks invented lowercase"**
Partially right, chronologically wrong. Roman monumental capitals: yes, that is our uppercase. Lowercase is Carolingian (789 CE Frankish), not medieval monastic. Uncial and half-uncial were transitional forms. The Renaissance humanists who created the roman typeface thought they were reviving Roman handwriting — they were reviving 9th-century Frankish administrative script.

**Chinese script has never been alphabetized**
Unlike Japanese (which borrowed Chinese characters and then developed phonetic kana), Chinese remained logographic. Various romanization systems exist (Pinyin, Wade-Giles) for transcription purposes, but the writing system itself never underwent alphabetization. This is not an oversight — the tonal, morphosyllabic structure of Chinese makes the logographic system well-suited to its language.
