# 04 — Type Classification: The Genealogy of Typefaces

## The Big Picture

Type classification is not taxonomy for its own sake — it is a map from visual form to historical context to appropriate use. Knowing that Helvetica is a Neo-Grotesque and Garamond is a Garalde tells you their stress axis, their stroke contrast, their serif structure, and the design ideology behind them. That knowledge is directly applicable when choosing or specifying type.

```
TYPE CLASSIFICATION OVERVIEW (Vox-ATypI System)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HISTORICAL AXIS ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────→
1470       1530      1750      1780      1810      1890–1957
  │          │         │         │         │           │
  ▼          ▼         ▼         ▼         ▼           ▼
Humanist  Garalde  Transitional Didone  Slab Serif   Sans Serif
(Venetian) (Old     (Baskerville)(Bodoni)(Egyptian)  (Grotesque/
           Style)               (Didot)              Neo-Grotesk/
                                                      Geometric/
                                                      Humanist Sans)

STRESS AXIS PROGRESSION:
  Diagonal stress ──────────────────────────────→ Vertical stress
  Humanist         Garalde    Transitional    Didone / all Sans

STROKE CONTRAST PROGRESSION:
  Medium-low ─────────────────────────────────→ Extreme/none
  Humanist   Garalde   Transitional   Didone     (Sans: none or minimal)

SERIF STYLE PROGRESSION:
  Angled, bracketed ──────────────────────────→ Hairline / slab / none
  Humanist          Old Style   Transitional  Didone  Slab   Sans

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The Vox-ATypI classification was developed by French typographer Maximilien Vox in 1954 and adopted by the Association Typographique Internationale (ATypI) in 1962. It remains the standard professional reference system.

## The Classicals: Humanist Through Didone

### 1. Humanist / Venetian (1470s)

The first roman typefaces cut in metal, designed to replicate the humanist manuscript hand of Italian Renaissance scholars.

```
HUMANIST / VENETIAN
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Designer:  Nicolas Jenson (Venice, 1470)
           Francesco Griffo (for Aldus Manutius, 1490s)

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stress axis:    Strongly diagonal (~15–30° from vertical)
                  (mimics broad-nib pen held at calligraphic angle)
  Contrast:       Low to medium (calligraphy has thick/thin but not extreme)
  Serifs:         Angled (oblique), bracketed (curves gracefully into stem)
  Crossbar on 'e': Diagonal (angled, like a pen would naturally make it)
  Letter 'a':     Single-storey (binocular: old Venetian)
                  OR two-storey (depending on specific design)

  VISUAL IDENTIFIER: The diagonal crossbar on the lowercase 'e'
  is the single clearest indicator of Venetian/early Humanist type.

Key faces:     Jenson (Nicolas Jenson, 1470 — the archetype)
               Centaur (Bruce Rogers, 1914 — revival)
               Adobe Jenson (Robert Slimbach, 1996)

USE:
  Book typography where historical warmth is desired
  Literary publishing; art books; historical contexts
  Body text in serious editorial work

WHAT MAKES IT "HUMANIST":
  Every detail traces to the calligraphic tradition:
  - Thick/thin = broad-nib pen at 30°
  - Diagonal serifs = how a pen lifts off
  - Diagonal 'e' crossbar = natural pen stroke
  The typeface is a frozen version of human hand movement
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 2. Garalde / Old Style (1530s–1700s)

The dominant typeface style for 200 years. Named for Garamond and Aldus (Alde) — the principal contributors. Slightly more refined than Venetian.

```
GARALDE / OLD STYLE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Designers: Claude Garamond (Paris, 1530s) — principal originator
           Robert Granjon (1510–1590) — italic specialist
           William Caslon (London, 1720s) — English Old Style
           John Baskerville: transitional (see next section)

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stress axis:    Slightly diagonal (less extreme than Venetian; ~10–20°)
  Contrast:       Low to medium (slightly more than Venetian)
  Serifs:         Bracketed (curved, comfortable join to stem)
  Crossbar on 'e': Horizontal (not diagonal; advance from Venetian)
  Overall:        Refined Venetian; still warm and calligraphic
  Cap height:     Lower relative to ascenders than later faces
  Ascenders:      Often taller than cap height (calligraphic heritage)

Key faces:     Garamond (Claude Garamond — multiple modern revivals:
                         Adobe Garamond Pro, Stempel Garamond, EB Garamond)
               Caslon (William Caslon, 1722 — "When in doubt, use Caslon")
               Palatino (Hermann Zapf, 1949 — humanist renaissance)
               Bembo (Monotype, 1929 — based on Aldus's Griffo type)
               Minion Pro (Robert Slimbach, 1990 — modern Garalde)
               Sabon (Jan Tschichold, 1967 — harmonized Garamond revival)

USE:
  Premium book typography; long-form reading
  Academic publishing; literary contexts
  Body text at 10–12pt print; less suited for small screen text
  "When in doubt, use Caslon" — the failsafe for quality book work

HISTORICALLY:
  Garamond's typefaces were so refined they were used for 200 years
  and then digitized — meaning 16th-century letterforms are on your screen.
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 3. Transitional (1750s)

A middle stage between the calligraphic Old Style and the rational geometry of the Moderns. The key shift: stress axis moves toward vertical; contrast increases.

```
TRANSITIONAL
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Designers: John Baskerville (Birmingham, 1757)
           Pierre Simon Fournier (Paris, 1742)

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stress axis:    Near-vertical (approaching rational geometry)
  Contrast:       Medium to high (increased refinement possible
                  with improved inking and printing technology)
  Serifs:         Bracketed, but thinner and more refined than Old Style
  Overall:        Crisp, sophisticated; bridges warmth and rationality
  Baskerville specifically: high-quality paper + hot-pressed finish
                  required to reproduce the fine strokes; he made paper too

Key faces:     Baskerville (John Baskerville, 1757 — several revivals:
                            ITC New Baskerville, Libre Baskerville)
               Times New Roman (Stanley Morison, 1932 — for The Times of London;
                               newsprint-optimized; the most widely used
                               typeface in history)
               Georgia (Matthew Carter, 1993 — screen-designed transitional;
                        the best screen serif before HiDPI)
               Perpetua (Eric Gill, 1929)
               Plantin (Christophe Plantin influence; Monotype 1913)

BASKERVILLE'S CONTEXT:
  His types were rejected in Britain as "too fancy" and "blinding"
  Benjamin Franklin brought them to America — American printers admired them
  Benjamin Franklin used Baskerville-style type for the Constitution
  Baskerville sold his punches to the French; they influenced Didot

TIMES NEW ROMAN STORY:
  Commissioned by The Times (London) in 1931; published 1932
  Goal: maximum text per column inch on newsprint
  Modified transitional design: condensed slightly, larger x-height
  Used by The Times until 1972; now ubiquitous (Microsoft bundled it)
  Consequence: Times is the "default" face everyone is tired of seeing
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 4. Didone / Modern (1780s–1800s)

Named for Firmin Didot and Giambattista Bodoni — simultaneous rational type design pushed to extreme. Maximally geometric, maximally contrasted.

```
DIDONE / MODERN
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Designers: Firmin Didot (Paris, 1784)
           Giambattista Bodoni (Parma, 1788)

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stress axis:    Perfectly vertical (not calligraphic at all)
  Contrast:       Extreme — hairline thin strokes vs thick stems
                  Ratio can be 10:1 or more
  Serifs:         Hairline — thin, horizontal, unbracketed (no curve join)
  Crossbar on 'e': Horizontal
  Ball terminals: Strong ball terminals on curves
  Overall:        Cold, precise, mathematical; Enlightenment rationalism

VISUAL APPEARANCE:
  At large sizes: stunning, dramatic, highly legible
  At body text sizes: the hairlines disappear or fill in → poor readability
  Specific problem: thin strokes are below the pixel density threshold
  at typical screen sizes → look broken or grainy

Key faces:     Bodoni (Giambattista Bodoni; many revivals: ITC Bodoni,
                       Bauer Bodoni, Berthold Bodoni)
               Didot (Firmin Didot; revivals: Linotype Didot, HTF Didot)
               Walbaum (Justus Erich Walbaum, 1800)
               Fenice (Aldo Novarese, ITC, 1980)

USE:
  Display typography: headlines, book covers, fashion editorial
  Luxury branding: Vogue, Harper's Bazaar, fashion brands
  Large-size contexts: posters, covers, titles
  AVOID: body text, small sizes, low-resolution screens

WHY FASHION MAGAZINES USE BODONI/DIDOT:
  The extreme contrast at large size creates a powerful visual texture
  The hairline serifs feel delicate and precise — luxury aesthetic
  The cold geometry feels modern, not nostalgic
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Mechanicals: Slab Serif

### 5. Slab Serif / Egyptian (1810s)

The first completely new typographic style in 300 years. Industrial rather than calligraphic.

```
SLAB SERIF / EGYPTIAN
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Origin:  Anonymous (attributed to Vincent Figgins, London, c. 1815)
         "Egyptian" was the marketing name; no connection to Egypt
         (Egypt was fashionable after Napoleon's campaigns 1798–1801)

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stress axis:    Vertical (like Didone; rational, not calligraphic)
  Contrast:       Very low (serifs and strokes nearly equal weight)
  Serifs:         Thick, rectangular, unbracketed — the defining feature
  Weight:         Generally heavy
  Overall:        Bold, authoritative, industrial; advertising display

VARIANTS:
  Clarendon (1845): slab serif with slight bracket;
    more refined; newspaper column headings
  Antique: very heavy slabs; low contrast; display
  Scotch Roman with slabs: medium contrast slab

Key faces:     Rockwell (Monotype, 1934 — geometric slab)
               Clarendon (Besley, 1845; Linotype revival)
               Memphis (Rudolf Weiss, 1929)
               Serifa (Adrian Frutiger, 1967 — humanist slab)
               Archer (Hoefler&Co, 2001 — rounded slab; literary feel)
               Roboto Slab (Google, 2012 — screen-optimized)
               Courier (Howard Kettler, 1955 — monospace typewriter)
               American Typewriter (ITC, 1974)

USE:
  Display headlines; advertising copy
  Americana aesthetic (signage, vintage-feel branding)
  Typographic emphasis within text (sidebars, pull quotes)
  Clarendon works surprisingly well for body text

HISTORICAL NOTE:
  "Egyptian" slabs were used extensively in 19th-century advertising
  Wood type for poster printing: slab serifs held up better to repeated
  impression than thin hairline serifs (slab was mechanically robust)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Lineal / Sans Serif (1816–present)

Sans serif (without serifs) emerged as a display novelty and became the dominant typeface category of the 20th century. The ATypI system divides sans serif into four subtypes:

```
SANS SERIF SUBTYPES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                 GROTESQUE          NEO-GROTESQUE       GEOMETRIC        HUMANIST
                    │                     │                 │               │
Era:            1890s–1930s          1957 (peak)          1927            1928+
Example:      Akzidenz-Grotesk      Helvetica           Futura          Gill Sans
                Franklin Gothic       Univers            Avant Garde     Frutiger
                News Gothic            Arial             Century Gothic  Calibri
                                     Folio              Futura PT       Trebuchet MS

Geometry:       Slight humanist       Pure rational      Strict          Humanist
                traces                grid-derived       geometric       (traces of
                                                         forms           calligraphy)

'G' spur:       Present               Present/absent      Absent          Present
Double-storey  Often                  Often              Rarely          Usually
'a' and 'g':

Stroke:         Near-uniform          Very uniform        Uniform         Slight
contrast:                                                                 variation

'O' shape:      Slightly              Near-perfect        Perfect         Slightly
                irregular             oval                oval/circle     oval

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 6. Grotesque (1890s–1930s)

The first commercially successful sans-serif faces. "Grotesque" was a pejorative that stuck. Slight irregularities remain from calligraphic tradition.

```
GROTESQUE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Origin: First commercial sans (Figgins, 1816 — "Two-lines English Egyptian")
        First major grotesque: Akzidenz-Grotesk (Berthold, 1896)

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stroke contrast:  Near-uniform but with slight optical variation
  Forms:            Slightly irregular; not perfectly geometric
  Terminals:        Sheared (oblique cuts, not perfectly horizontal)
  Character:        Rough-edged compared to Swiss Neo-Grotesque;
                    "human" imperfections give it warmth

Key faces:     Akzidenz-Grotesk (Berthold, 1896) — the grandfather
               Franklin Gothic (Morris Fuller Benton, ATF, 1902)
               News Gothic (Morris Fuller Benton, ATF, 1908)
               Monotype Grotesque
               Interstate (Tobias Frere-Jones, 1993 — based on US highway signs)

USE:
  When you want "Swiss neutrality" feel with slightly more warmth
  Vintage/retro contexts (1890s–1930s aesthetic)
  Display headlines; branding with historical credibility
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 7. Neo-Grotesque (1957)

The Swiss school rationalized the grotesque into a perfectly neutral grid-derived form. Helvetica and Univers emerged in the same year (1957) from different Swiss type designers — the two dominant sans-serif families of the late 20th century.

```
NEO-GROTESQUE (Swiss/International Style)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Context:  Swiss International Typographic Style (1950s)
          Grid-based design; rational, universal, objective
          Type should not express personality; it should just communicate

Helvetica (1957):
  Designers: Max Miedinger + Eduard Hoffmann; Haas Type Foundry, Switzerland
  Original name: Neue Haas Grotesk
  Renamed Helvetica (Latin for "Switzerland") 1960 for German market
  Refinements: Helvetica Neue (1983); Helvetica Now (2019)
  CSS: the basis for Arial (Monotype, 1982 — designed as metrically
       compatible Helvetica substitute for Windows/Office)

Univers (1957):
  Designer: Adrian Frutiger; Deberny & Peignot, France
  Key innovation: designed as a SYSTEM — 21 variants on a grid
  Systematically numbered: 55 = Regular, 45 = Light, 65 = Bold,
  56 = Oblique, 47 = Condensed Light, 75 = Black...
  First typeface designed as a coordinated family from the start

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stroke contrast:  Minimal (near-uniform)
  Forms:            Rational, consistent; terminals horizontal
  'G':              Has spur in Helvetica; no spur in Univers regular
  Double-storey:    Yes (both Helvetica and Univers)
  Character:        Intentionally neutral; "transparent"

Key faces:     Helvetica (Miedinger/Hoffmann, 1957)
               Univers (Frutiger, 1957)
               Arial (Monotype, 1982 — Helvetica substitute)
               Folio (Bauer, 1957)

USE:
  When you need "nothing" — signage, wayfinding, UI at scale
  When typeface should not assert personality (government, infrastructure)
  Swiss graphic design aesthetic
  WARNING: overused to the point of banality in some contexts

WHY ARIAL IS NOT HELVETICA:
  Ariel was designed to have identical character widths (metrics) to Helvetica
  so documents typeset in Helvetica would reflow identically in Arial.
  The letterforms are subtly different (more curved 'R' leg, less 'G' spur).
  Using Arial thinking it's Helvetica has been a common user confusion.
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 8. Geometric (1927)

Based on pure geometric forms — circles, straight lines, right angles. Bauhaus ideology applied to type: form follows function, reduced to essential geometry.

```
GEOMETRIC SANS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Context:  Bauhaus school (Dessau, Germany, 1925–1933)
          "Universal" script by Herbert Bayer (1925) — pure geometry,
          single case (abolished uppercase as "unnecessary")

Futura (1927):
  Designer: Paul Renner; Bauer Type Foundry, Frankfurt
  Derived from Bauhaus geometric principles
  But Renner rejected the most extreme Bauhaus forms:
  Futura has 'O' as near-perfect circle; 'a' as single-storey
  but tolerances for readability

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stroke contrast:  Near-zero (instrument-drawn; not handmade)
  Forms:            'O' = near-perfect circle; straight strokes = truly straight
  'a' and 'g':      Single-storey (simplified geometric form)
  Terminals:        Strictly horizontal or circular
  Character:        Futuristic, modern, idealistic; slightly cold

Key faces:     Futura (Paul Renner, 1927) — the archetype
               Avant Garde Gothic (Herb Lubalin, ITC, 1970)
               Century Gothic (Monotype, 1991 — Futura substitute)
               Bauhaus (Ed Benguiat/Victor Caruso, ITC, 1975)
               Nunito (Google Fonts — geometric rounded)
               DIN (industrial German standard, geometric)

USE:
  Display type; headlines; branding with modernist/futuristic feel
  Geometric forms work well at large sizes; less ideal for body text
  Strong associations: NASA (Moon landings → used Futura), IKEA
  Luxury branding (Louis Vuitton, Calvin Klein use Futura variants)
  AVOID: extended body text — single-storey 'a'/'g' hurt legibility

GEOMETRIC vs NEO-GROTESQUE:
  Geometric: theory-first (start with circle, derive forms)
  Neo-Grotesque: observation-first (what does ideal text look like?)
  Geometric can feel mechanical and slightly alien
  Neo-Grotesque feels neutral and "natural" for most readers
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### 9. Humanist Sans (1928+)

Draws on the Old Style / calligraphic tradition but without serifs. The stress axis varies; stroke weight shows slight contrast; letterforms have calligraphic roots. The most readable sans-serif category.

```
HUMANIST SANS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Context:  Reaction against the cold geometry of Swiss/Geometric sans.
          Type should be warm, readable, humanly proportioned.

Gill Sans (1928):
  Designer: Eric Gill; Monotype
  Based on Johnston (1916) — the typeface for the London Underground
  Humanist construction with brush/calligraphic traces
  Classic British feel; BBC, Penguin Books
  Note: Eric Gill's personal history is complicated (historical abuses)

Frutiger (1977):
  Designer: Adrian Frutiger; commissioned for Charles de Gaulle Airport
  Requirement: instantly legible in 3D space from moving vehicle
  Solution: optical balance optimized for legibility; slightly wider forms
  Open apertures (letters 'c', 'e', 's', 'a' are more open)
  Considered by many designers to be the ideal humanist sans
  Renamed Frutiger Neue (2014); influenced Myriad, Meta

DISTINCTIVE FEATURES:
  Stroke contrast:  Slight but present (humanist influence)
  Stress axis:      Varies; slight calligraphic influence
  Forms:            Double-storey 'a' and 'g' (unlike geometric)
  Apertures:        Open (especially: e, c, s, a) → better legibility
  Character:        Warm, readable, approachable

Key faces:     Gill Sans (Eric Gill, 1928)
               Frutiger (Adrian Frutiger, 1977)
               Myriad Pro (Twombly/Slimbach, Adobe, 1992)
               Trebuchet MS (Vincent Connare, Microsoft, 1996)
               Calibri (Lucas de Groot, Microsoft, 2005)
               Meta (Erik Spiekermann, FontFont, 1991)
               Inter (Rasmus Andersson, 2017 — screen-optimized, open source)
               Optima (Hermann Zapf, 1958 — see note below)

USE:
  Body text, UI text, long-form reading in sans
  Most versatile category; humanist sans adapts to many contexts
  Screen interfaces: Inter, Calibri, Trebuchet MS
  Brand/editorial: Gill Sans, Frutiger, Meta

OPEN APERTURES AND LEGIBILITY:
  Aperture = the opening of partially-open letters (e, c, s, a, g)
  Wide aperture: 'c' looks clearly like 'c' vs 'o'
  Narrow aperture: 'c' and 'o' harder to distinguish at speed/small size
  Humanist sans has wider apertures than geometric/neo-grotesque
  → better legibility in running text

OPTIMA SPECIAL CASE:
  Optima (Hermann Zapf, 1958) is NEITHER serif nor true sans
  It has slight flares at stroke terminals — not serifs but not flat
  "Flared sans" or "serifless roman" — its own category
  Letterforms follow humanist construction precisely
  Used for Vietnam Veterans Memorial inscription (Washington, D.C.)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Non-Lineal Categories

```
SCRIPT, DISPLAY, AND MONOSPACE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SCRIPT (cursive / calligraphic)
  Letters connected or appearing connected
  Formal script: Copperplate, Edwardian Script (wedding invitations)
  Casual script: brush scripts, hand-lettered feel
  USE: invitations, branding with personal/handmade feel
  AVOID: body text (absolutely unreadable at small sizes)

BLACKLETTER / GOTHIC TEXTURA
  Medieval German letterform; used by Gutenberg
  Strong vertical strokes; angular; compressed
  Associated: newspapers (masthead of NY Times, LA Times still use it)
  Cultural context: Germanic heritage, heavy metal, tattoos
  Currently: used for aesthetic effect, NOT for readability

DISPLAY
  Designed specifically for large sizes (24pt+)
  Any style; optimized for visual impact, NOT extended reading
  Optical size differences from text versions (more dramatic)

MONOSPACE (fixed-width)
  Every character has identical advance width
  Origin: typewriter mechanics (carriage must advance same distance per key)
  Essential for: code editors, terminals, programming
  Good monospace faces: Courier New, Consolas, JetBrains Mono,
                        Fira Code, Cascadia Code, Source Code Pro
  CRITICAL I/l/1/0/O DISTINCTION in code fonts:
    I (capital i), l (lowercase L), 1 (one) must be clearly distinct
    0 (zero) and O (capital O) must be clearly distinct
    Slashed zero (0̶) or dotted zero common in programming fonts
    This is a hard requirement; failure = bugs (passwords, code)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Classification Decision Tree

```
HOW TO CLASSIFY AN UNKNOWN TYPEFACE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Does it have serifs?
  │
  ├── YES: What kind of serifs?
  │     │
  │     ├── Thick rectangular (slab) → SLAB SERIF / Egyptian
  │     │
  │     ├── Hairline, horizontal, unbracketed → DIDONE / Modern
  │     │                                       (Bodoni, Didot)
  │     │
  │     └── Bracketed (curved join to stem)?
  │           │
  │           ├── Diagonal stress + diagonal 'e' crossbar → HUMANIST/VENETIAN
  │           │                                             (Jenson, Centaur)
  │           │
  │           ├── Near-vertical stress + higher contrast → TRANSITIONAL
  │           │                                           (Baskerville, Times)
  │           │
  │           └── Slightly diagonal stress + medium contrast → GARALDE
  │                                                            (Garamond, Caslon)
  │
  └── NO (sans serif): What is the geometry?
        │
        ├── 'a' single-storey + 'O' near-perfect circle → GEOMETRIC
        │                                                  (Futura, Century Gothic)
        │
        ├── Very uniform strokes + horizontal terminals + 'a' double-storey
        │   → NEO-GROTESQUE (Helvetica, Arial, Univers)
        │
        ├── Slight stroke variation + open apertures + 'a' double-storey
        │   → HUMANIST SANS (Gill Sans, Frutiger, Calibri, Inter)
        │
        └── Slight irregularity + historical texture
            → GROTESQUE (Akzidenz-Grotesk, Franklin Gothic)

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Use Case | Recommended Category | Examples |
|----------|----------------------|----------|
| Long-form book reading, print | Garalde, Transitional | Garamond, Palatino, Baskerville |
| Newspaper / dense text | Transitional, optimized | Times New Roman, Georgia |
| UI / interface text | Humanist Sans, Neo-Grotesque | Inter, Calibri, Helvetica |
| Display / headline, editorial | Didone, Slab, Display Serif | Bodoni, Rockwell, Libre Baskerville |
| Tech / corporate neutrality | Neo-Grotesque | Helvetica, Univers, Arial |
| Modernist / Bauhaus aesthetic | Geometric | Futura, Avant Garde |
| Readable sans at all sizes | Humanist Sans | Frutiger, Gill Sans, Inter |
| Programming / terminal | Monospace | JetBrains Mono, Fira Code, Consolas |
| Brand warmth / handmade | Humanist Serif, Script | Palatino, EB Garamond |
| Signage / wayfinding | Humanist Sans (specifically Frutiger) | Frutiger, Myriad |

## Common Confusion Points

**Helvetica is not "the standard" — Arial is the Helvetica substitute**
Helvetica is a licensed typeface (Monotype). Arial was designed as a metrically compatible clone for Microsoft Windows licensing reasons. Helvetica and Arial look similar but are not identical — the 'R', 'G', 'Q', 'a', 't', 'g' differ subtly. Helvetica has better proportions; Arial has slightly cruder letterforms. That said, at body text sizes on screen, the difference is imperceptible.

**Garamond is not one typeface**
"Garamond" is a category of typefaces based on Claude Garamond's 16th-century designs, with multiple competing digitizations:
- Adobe Garamond Pro (Robert Slimbach, 1989) — from specimen sheets
- Stempel Garamond — based on earlier Garamond specimens
- EB Garamond (Georg Duffner, open source) — from 1592 Egenolff specimen
- ITC Garamond — controversial; heavily reinterpreted; wider and different
If you specify "Garamond" without naming the foundry/version, you may get very different results.

**Futura is not for body text**
Futura was designed for display sizes. Its geometric single-storey 'a' and 'g' hurt legibility in running text. The near-zero stroke contrast and circular 'O' create visual monotony at body text sizes. Use it for headlines, logos, display copy — not 10pt paragraphs.

**"I'll just use system fonts"**
The system font stack (`-apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", Roboto, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif`) gives you: San Francisco on Apple, Segoe UI on Windows, Roboto on Android/Chrome. San Francisco (Apple) and Segoe UI (Microsoft) are both humanist sans — good choices. This is often the right call for interfaces where performance and consistency matter more than brand differentiation.
