# Modern and Postmodern Dance

## The Big Picture

Modern dance was a rebellion against ballet's codified vocabulary. Postmodern dance was a rebellion against modern dance's emotional vocabulary. Each generation rejected what the previous considered sacred -- but the technical demands increased, not decreased.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    THE LINEAGE OF REBELLION                          |
|                                                                      |
|  BALLET          MODERN           POSTMODERN         CONTEMPORARY    |
|  (1700s+)        (1900-1960s)      (1960s-1980s)      (1980s-)       |
|                                                                      |
|  Codified        Body as          Movement =         Synthesis;      |
|  vocabulary      emotional        movement, not      any method      |
|  Pointe/turnout  instrument       expression         valid           |
|  European        Bare feet        Pedestrian ok      Release tech    |
|  narrative       Natural mvmt     Chance ok          Somatic work    |
|  |               |                |                  |               |
|  REJECTED BY --> REJECTED BY -->  REJECTED BY -->   INTEGRATES ALL   |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+

ISADORA DUNCAN --> RUTH ST. DENIS --> MARTHA GRAHAM --> JOSE LIMON
                                   --> DORIS HUMPHREY
                                   --> LESTER HORTON

MERCE CUNNINGHAM (1944-) --> JUDSON DANCE THEATER (1962-1964)
                          --> GRAND UNION (1970-1976)
                          --> CONTACT IMPROVISATION (1972-)
                          --> WILLIAM FORSYTHE (deconstruction)
```

## The Founders: Modern Dance First Generation

### Isadora Duncan (1877-1927): The Original Break

Duncan saw ballet as an "abomination" -- unnatural, corseted, subservient to music. Her alternative:

```
DUNCAN'S PRINCIPLES (vs ballet)

BALLET                          DUNCAN
------------------------        ----------------------------
Pointe shoes                    Bare feet
Corsets + tutus                 Flowing Greek-inspired robes
Codified vocabulary             Natural human movement
Dance serves music              Dance is free from music's control
Turnout                         Natural parallel feet and legs
Linear academic training        Self-developed, intuited
Male choreographers lead        Female body, female authority
European court values           American democracy, Greek ideal
```

Duncan's movement source: breath, solar plexus as center of energy, wave-like motion through the body. She performed to Beethoven, Wagner, Gluck -- not to music written for dance. Danced concert music that was "not meant to be danced to."

Her life: scandalous (free love, unmarried children), tragic (both children drowned in car accident in Seine, 1913), violent end (scarf caught in wheel, 1927). The life performed the philosophy.

### Martha Graham (1894-1991): The Grammar of Emotion

Graham created the most influential technique in American modern dance. Her innovations:

**Contraction and Release:**
```
CONTRACTION                     RELEASE
-----------                     -------
Pelvis tucks under              Pelvis opens, tilts forward
Spine curves inward             Spine lengthens and arches
Hollows the torso               Opens the front body
Breath exhaled                  Breath inhaled
Symbolic: grief, inward         Symbolic: joy, outward
```

The contraction is Graham's fundamental unit -- a deep curvature of the spine that begins at the pelvis. Nothing like it exists in ballet. This is a technically demanding action requiring core strength and spinal articulation.

**Graham's Aesthetic Universe:**
- Greek mythology as primary source material (Clytemnestra, Medea, Jocasta)
- Women as psychologically complex protagonists
- The body as map of internal emotional states
- Collaboration with Louis Horst (musical director, theorist of form)
- Permanent company formed 1926; still performing (without her, she died 1991)

**Canonical Works:**
| Work | Year | Subject | Innovation |
|------|------|---------|-----------|
| Lamentation | 1930 | Grief | Dancer inside a tube of fabric; form IS the grief |
| Primitive Mysteries | 1931 | Native American ritual | Unison ensemble without star |
| Appalachian Spring | 1944 | American frontier | Aaron Copland score; Noguchi set |
| Cave of the Heart | 1946 | Medea | Jealousy visualized |
| Clytemnestra | 1958 | Greek tragedy | First full-evening work |

### Doris Humphrey (1895-1958): Fall and Recovery

Humphrey's technique was conceptually opposite to Graham's:

```
HUMPHREY'S FALL AND RECOVERY PRINCIPLE

  BALANCE (stillness = death)
       |
       v
  FALL (surrender to gravity -- danger -- excitement)
       |
       v
  RECOVERY (regaining balance -- survival -- resolution)
       |
       v
  NEW BALANCE

"All movement lies in the arc between two deaths:
 stillness and collapse."

This is a PHYSICAL PHILOSOPHY: the human condition is
perpetual navigation between control and surrender.
Connects to breath (exhale = fall; inhale = recovery).
```

## Merce Cunningham (1919-2009): The Philosophical Break

Cunningham was trained by Graham, then made the single most radical shift in modern dance. His innovations connect directly to algorithmic thinking.

### The Break with Expression

```
GRAHAM'S ASSUMPTIONS          CUNNINGHAM'S REJECTION
----------------------        ------------------------
Movement = emotion            Movement = movement
Dance tells a story           Dance has no subject but itself
Music + dance = unified       Music + dance = independent
Space has focal center        Space has no hierarchy
Continuity of meaning         No narrative continuity
Performer = character         Performer = themselves
```

### Chance Procedures: Algorithmic Choreography

Cunningham began working with John Cage (composer, partner) on chance methods:

```
CHANCE PROCESS (bridge: this is random number generation applied to choreography)

METHOD 1: Coin tossing (I Ching)
  -- Make list of movements for each body section
  -- Toss coin (or use I Ching hexagrams) to determine order
  -- Result: combinations body would never "choose" naturally

METHOD 2: Random enumeration
  -- Number all movement units
  -- Use random number tables to sequence them
  -- Duration determined separately by same method

METHOD 3: Superimposition
  -- Compose music independently (Cage)
  -- Compose dance independently (Cunningham)
  -- Bring together for first time in performance
  -- Sharing only time and space, not emotional intention

RESULT: A movement sequence that no human intention would produce.
The body is freed from habit and expectation.

BRIDGE TO COMPUTING: This is exactly what generative/algorithmic composition
does -- the composer specifies rules/probabilities, not outcomes. Cunningham
was doing this in 1950s with physical bodies.
```

### The Score System

Cunningham developed Event format: works from repertoire presented in any order, in any space (not just proscenium theatre), any duration. A museum atrium, a gymnasium, a factory floor. The space does not determine the work.

The Judson Church (Washington Square, NYC) workshop became the most concentrated explosion of dance theory in the 20th century.

### Yvonne Rainer's "No Manifesto" (1965)

The most quoted document in postmodern dance theory:

```
NO to spectacle
NO to virtuosity
NO to transformations and magic and make-believe
NO to the glamour and transcendency of the star image
NO to the heroic
NO to the anti-heroic
NO to trash imagery
NO to involvement of performer or spectator
NO to style
NO to camp
NO to seduction of spectator by the wiles of the performer
NO to eccentricity
NO to moving or being moved
```

This is not nihilism -- it is a systematic demolition of every assumption that made dance "dance." What remains after the No's: the bare fact of a body moving in space and time.

**Rainer's "Trio A" (1966):** A 4.5-minute solo with no climax, no spatial focus, no audience address, continuous motion through a task-logic sequence. Teaches itself -- no particular difficulty hierarchy.

### Steve Paxton and Contact Improvisation (1972)

```
CONTACT IMPROVISATION

Principle: Two (or more) bodies in shared physical contact
           follow the point of contact wherever it leads.

           No leader, no follower -- or both simultaneously.
           Weight exchange: lean into, through, away.

TECHNIQUE:
  -- Rolling point of contact (not gripping)
  -- Shared center of gravity navigation
  -- Small dance (micro-movement while standing still)
  -- Catching/releasing falls

POLITICAL DIMENSION:
  -- Egalitarian: no hierarchy of skill initially required
  -- Gender-neutral: any body with any body
  -- Informal: practiced in gyms, parks, workshops
  -- Open score: improvised, not set

STILL ACTIVE: worldwide community; annual "jams"
```

### Trisha Brown (1936-2017)

```
EQUIPMENT PIECES (1968-1971):
  Walking on the Wall -- harness system; walker moves perpendicular
                         to vertical wall as if on floor
  Man Walking Down the Side of a Building -- exactly that

ACCUMULATION (1971):
  Movement 1
  Movement 1 + 2
  Movement 1 + 2 + 3
  ... etc (32 movements in complete version)
  -- No drama; no climax; pure structure visible
  -- Bridge to: recursion, incremental build, additive composition

ROOF PIECE (1971):
  Dancers relay movement phrase rooftop to rooftop across Lower Manhattan
  -- Transmission errors are part of the work (telephone game as art)
```

## William Forsythe (1949-): Deconstruction of Ballet

Forsythe is the bridge figure -- trained in ballet, became director of Ballet Frankfurt (1984-2004), then systematically dismantled ballet's rules while retaining its technical base.

```
FORSYTHE'S METHOD:

BALLET RULE           FORSYTHE'S MANIPULATION
--------------------  -------------------------------------------
Aligned spine         Spine thrown off-axis; torso independent
5 positions           Positions as departure points, not home
Turnout is constant   Turnout mixed with parallel; hip shifts
Linear trajectories   Curved, spiral, off-balance paths
Phrase has beginning  Phrase can start mid-movement; truncated
and end clearly
Port de bras          Arms independent of body; extreme speed

KEY WORK: "In the Middle, Somewhat Elevated" (1987)
  -- Created for Paris Opera Ballet
  -- Aggressive, off-balance, fast
  -- Electronic music (Willems/Bausch)
  -- Pointe shoes + completely non-classical movement
  -- The paradox: looks wrong by classical rules yet requires
     classical training to execute

IMPROVISATION TECHNOLOGIES (1999-2012):
  -- CD-ROM + website-based tools for generating movement
  -- "Synchronous Objects" (2009): data visualization of
     One Flat Thing, reproduced -- movement analytics made visible
```

## The Contemporary Synthesis

Since 1990, concert dance has entered a synthesis phase:

```
CONTEMPORARY DANCE LANDSCAPE

  Release Technique (BMC + Skinner + Alexander influence)
    -- Coordination through minimal effort
    -- Somatic awareness; bone/fluid/tissue imagery
    -- Counters the injury model of pushed training

  Physical Theatre (DV8, Pina Bausch Tanztheater)
    -- Text + movement + design integrated
    -- Wuppertal Tanztheater (Bausch): personal narrative, repetition
    -- DV8: social issues (gender, sexuality, violence) through extreme physicality

  Hip-hop / street / vernacular
    -- Breaking (b-boy/b-girl): power moves, footwork, freezes
    -- Waacking, Voguing, Locking, Popping: ballroom culture
    -- Entered proscenium theatre; complex aesthetic debate about decontextualization

  Interdisciplinary / live art
    -- No boundary between performance art and dance
    -- Xavier Le Roy: Retrospective; audience moves through space
    -- Anne Teresa De Keersmaeker: mathematical structure made physical
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Modern technique is codified, like ballet.**
Martha Graham Technique and Horton Technique are specific, teachable systems with as much precision as ballet -- different vocabulary, same rigor. "Modern dance" does not mean improvised or casual.

**Cunningham's dancers are not emotionless.**
Cunningham removed intentional expression from composition -- he did not forbid emotion. Audiences still see expression. He was removing the choreographer's control over meaning, not the dancer's humanity.

**Judson was not a company.**
Judson Dance Theater was a workshop and informal community using the Judson Memorial Church as a performance venue. No permanent ensemble. Works were made in the workshop and often performed once.

**Contact improvisation is a practice, not a style.**
CI is a physical practice with principles (shared weight, rolling contact, following the point of contact). It is not a look. CI dances can look enormously different. It is closer to a martial arts practice than to a choreographic style.

**Release technique does not mean soft or low-energy.**
Release technique prioritizes efficiency (using minimal tension to produce maximum range of motion). Trisha Brown's later company dancers are physically formidable. "Release" refers to unnecessary muscular gripping, not effort level.
