# Ballet — Technique, Vocabulary, and History

## The Big Picture

Ballet is a codified theatrical dance system developed over 500 years into one of the most technically precise physical disciplines that exists. Its vocabulary is a grammar of movement with each term specifying exact body position, trajectory, and dynamic quality.

```
+-------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    BALLET AS A SYSTEM                             |
|                                                                   |
|  HISTORICAL LAYER         TECHNICAL LAYER        TRAINING LAYER  |
|                                                                   |
|  Renaissance courts  -->  5 Positions          --> Barre work     |
|  Louis XIV           -->  Turnout as base       --> Center work    |
|  Romantic era        -->  Port de bras          --> Allegro        |
|  Russian Imperial    -->  Battements            --> Pointe work    |
|  Diaghilev reform    -->  Adagio / allegro       --> Variations    |
|  Neoclassical        -->  Jumps, turns           --> Pas de deux   |
+-------------------------------------------------------------------+
         |                         |                       |
         v                         v                       v
   PETIPA / VAGANOVA          BIOMECHANICS             AESTHETICS
   Story ballets              Turnout anatomy          Elongated line
   Grande ballets             Impact forces            Weight illusion
   Grand pas de deux          Injury patterns          Stylistic schools
```

## Historical Development

### Phase 1: Court Origins (1489-1700)

```
1489  Bergamo, Italy -- Bergamese court entertainment
      Renaissance court spectacles: poetry + music + dance fused

1581  Ballet Comique de la Reine (Paris)
      -- First documented ballet (5.5 hours)
      -- Established narrative dance as court art

1661  Louis XIV founds Academie Royale de Danse
      -- Louis XIV himself a skilled dancer (played Apollo)
      -- Dance professionalized; moves from court to stage

1681  Le Triomphe de l'Amour
      -- First professional female dancers on stage

1700  Raoul-Auger Feuillet publishes Choregraphie
      -- First formal dance notation system
      -- Encoded the five positions that survive today
```

### Phase 2: Theatrical Formalization (1700-1820)

```
Jean-Georges Noverre (1727-1810)
  "Letters on Dancing and Ballets" (1760)
  -- Argued ballet must express human emotion
  -- Created ballet d'action: dance as drama, not spectacle
  -- Rejected elaborate costumes that impeded movement
  -- Direct ancestor of all dramatic ballet

Jean Dauberval
  La Fille Mal Gardee (1789)
  -- First comic ballet with peasant characters
  -- Still performed today

Carlo Blasis
  "Treatise on the Dance" (1820)
  -- Codified the technical vocabulary formally
  -- Arabesque and attitude positions defined
```

### Phase 3: Romantic Ballet (1820-1870)

```
KEY INNOVATIONS:
  Pointe work appears (Marie Taglioni, 1820s)
    -- Dancing on toe tips creates illusion of weightlessness
    -- Pointe shoes: satin, no padding initially (extreme pain)
    -- Symbolized the Romantic ideal: women as ethereal spirits

  The Romantic tutu:
    -- Long, diaphanous, bell-shaped skirt
    -- Enabled the "floating" effect

  Gaslight effects:
    -- Stage lighting used to create supernatural atmosphere

CANONICAL WORKS:
  La Sylphide (1832)       -- Taglioni; supernatural spirit
  Giselle (1841)           -- Carlotta Grisi; ghost/supernatural
  Coppelia (1870)          -- Mechanical doll; already ironic

ROMANTIC PARADOX: Female dancers became primary artists
while male choreographers/impresarios controlled the form
```

### Phase 4: Russian Imperial Ballet (1870-1909)

```
Marius Petipa (1818-1910) -- the architect of classical ballet

PETIPA'S INNOVATIONS:
  -- Systematized the grand ballet structure
  -- Created the definitive versions: Swan Lake, Sleeping Beauty,
     Nutcracker (with Ivanov), Don Quixote, Bayadere
  -- Developed the grand pas de deux structure:
     Adagio (duo) -> Male variation -> Female variation -> Coda
  -- Corps de ballet as precision instrument
  -- 32-fouette sequence (Swan Lake Act III) as virtuosity test

THE SHORT TUTU arrives:
  -- Reveals legs; enables display of footwork
  -- Ballet Ruslan shortened skirt for visibility

Agrippina Vaganova (1879-1951)
  -- Developed systematic teaching method
  -- Soviet-era codification of Russian school
  -- "Basics of Classical Dance" (1934) still used globally
  -- Vaganova Academy (St. Petersburg) remains elite training
```

### Phase 5: Revolution (Ballets Russes, 1909-1929)

```
Sergei Diaghilev (impresario, not choreographer)
  -- Assembled Nijinsky, Fokine, Massine, Balanchine
  -- Commissioned Stravinsky, Prokofiev, Satie, Picasso, Matisse
  -- Ballet became total artwork: music + design + movement equal

WORKS THAT CHANGED EVERYTHING:
  The Firebird (1910)       -- Fokine; Russian fairy tale
  Petrushka (1911)          -- Fokine; tragedy, puppet as human
  Afternoon of a Faun (1912)-- Nijinsky; 2D Egyptian profile pose
                               Erotic scandal at premiere
  Rite of Spring (1913)     -- Nijinsky/Stravinsky
                               RIOT AT PREMIERE (May 29, 1913)
                               Turned in feet; angular; primitive
                               Sacrificial murder as ballet subject
  Parade (1917)             -- Cocteau/Satie/Picasso
                               Cubist costumes, circus theme
```

### Phase 6: Neoclassical and Beyond (1930-)

```
George Balanchine (1904-1983)
  -- Diaghilev protege; founded School of American Ballet (1934)
  -- New York City Ballet
  -- Neoclassical: ballet vocabulary + Modernist sensibility
  -- Plotless ballets: movement for movement's sake
  -- Aesthetic: fast tempos, long legs, athletic women
  -- "Speed, attack, swiftness" over Romantic softness

KEY BALANCHINE WORKS:
  Apollo (1928)              -- first neoclassical ballet
  Serenade (1934)            -- first American ballet
  Symphony in C (1947)
  Agon (1957)                -- with 12-tone Stravinsky
  Jewels (1967)              -- first full-length plotless ballet

Kenneth MacMillan (Royal Ballet)
  -- psychological realism; Manon, Romeo and Juliet

William Forsythe (1949-)
  -- deconstruction of ballet vocabulary
  -- "In the Middle, Somewhat Elevated" (1987)
  -- extended ballet technique into postmodern territory
```

## Technical Vocabulary: The Grammar

### The Five Positions: Foundation of Everything

```
POSITION    FEET                              ARMS (PORT DE BRAS)
---------   --------------------------------  ----------------------
First       Heels together, toes out 180      Both arms low oval
Second      Feet apart (hip width), toes out  One arm out, one low
Third       One heel at other foot's arch     One up, one low oval
Fourth      One foot ahead, hip width apart   One up, one forward
Fifth       Front heel touching back toe tip  One up, one low oval

NOTE: 180-degree turnout is the IDEAL; most dancers achieve 120-150 degrees.
Forcing beyond natural hip rotation causes injury.
True turnout comes from hip external rotation, NOT from forcing at ankle/knee.
```

### Core Vocabulary by Function

**Building blocks (battements):**

| Term | French meaning | Movement |
|------|---------------|----------|
| Tendu | stretched | Slide foot out until only toe touches floor; trains arch and leg line |
| Degage | disengaged | Like tendu but foot lifts off floor slightly |
| Frappe | struck | Foot beats the floor; develops fast footwork |
| Fondu | melting | Standing leg bends; weight "melts" into plié |
| Developpe | developed/unfolded | Leg draws up to knee then extends; the slow controlled leg lift |
| Grand battement | big beat | Full leg swing to maximum height |

**Foundational positions:**

| Term | Description | Biomechanical function |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| Plie | Knee bend (demi = partial; grand = full) | Tendon conditioning; landing absorption |
| Arabesque | One leg extended behind; various arm configurations | Hip flexor stretch; line |
| Attitude | Leg extended with bent knee | Variation of arabesque line |
| Releve | Rise to ball of foot or pointe | Calf and ankle strengthening |

**Jumps (allegro):**

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Saute | Jump from two feet, land two feet (simple bounce) |
| Echappe | "Escaping" jump: fifth position to second or fourth |
| Assemble | One leg sweeps out; both feet land together |
| Jete | "Thrown": one leg throws out; land on that leg |
| Grand jete | Big leap: full splits in air |
| Cabriole | One leg beats against the other in air |
| Entrechat | Feet cross and re-cross in air (entrechat quatre = 2 crossings) |

**Turns:**

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Pirouette | Spin on one leg (en dehors = turning out; en dedans = turning in) |
| Fouette | Whipping turn: supporting leg stays; gesture leg whips around |
| Chaine | Chain of turns traveling across floor |
| Tour en l'air | Jump with turn (single, double, triple) |

## The Pas de Deux: Structural Analysis

The grand pas de deux is ballet's highest formal structure, analogous to the sonata form in music:

```
GRAND PAS DE DEUX STRUCTURE

1. ENTREE (Entry)
   Both dancers enter; establishes relationship

2. ADAGIO
   Slow, supported section
   -- Man supports woman through extensions, lifts, balances
   -- Showcases line, flexibility, musicality
   -- Requires total physical trust; man must control woman's center of gravity

3. MALE VARIATION
   Solo for male dancer
   -- Showcases jumps, turns, beats
   -- Typically 1.5-3 minutes

4. FEMALE VARIATION
   Solo for female dancer
   -- Showcases pointe work, balances, turns
   -- Often the most musically complex section

5. CODA
   Both together; acceleration to climax
   -- Typically: she does 32 fouettes while he performs tours en l'air
   -- Ends with grand climactic pose (tableau)
```

Different national traditions codified ballet training differently:

```
+------------------+------------------------------------------+

+------------------+------------------------------------------+

+------------------+------------------------------------------+

| (International)  | curriculum; widely used for teaching      |
+------------------+------------------------------------------+
```

## Pointe Work: The Biomechanics

Pointe shoes are not magic -- they are a narrow wooden or thermoplastic "box" packed into a satin shoe. The dancer supports body weight on approximately 3-4 cm2 of contact area.

```
POINTE SHOE ANATOMY

  +-----------+
  |  RIBBON   |  (stabilizes ankle, 2m of satin ribbon)
  +-----------+
  |  SHANK    |  (stiff insole; supports arch; stiffness grade 1-4)
  +-----------+
  |  BOX      |  (hardened toe cap; built from layers of paper/canvas/glue)
  |    []     |  (modern: thermoplastic or Kevlar for pro shoes)
  +-----------+

FORCES ON POINTE:
  Body weight: 50-80 kg
  Contact area: ~3-4 cm2
  Pressure: ~15-20x body weight equivalent per cm2
  Impact forces at landing: 4-8x body weight

REQUIREMENTS:
  -- Minimum 8-10 years of training before pointe work
  -- Bone ossification: growth plates must be closed (typically age 11-12+)
  -- Strong intrinsic foot muscles, calf complex, ankle stability
```

## Injury Landscape

Ballet's technical demands create characteristic injury patterns:

```
INJURY FREQUENCY BY LOCATION (professional dancers)

  FOOT/ANKLE  ████████████████  40%
  KNEE        ████████          20%
  HIP         ██████            15%
  BACK/SPINE  █████             12%
  OTHER       █████             13%

MOST COMMON SPECIFIC INJURIES:
  Stress fractures    -- metatarsals, tibia (repetitive impact)
  Hallux valgus       -- bunions from pointe pressure
  Ankle impingement   -- posterior (pointe) and anterior (grand plie)
  Hip labral tears    -- from extreme turnout forcing
  FAI (femoroacetabular impingement) -- from hip hyperextension range
  Patellar tendinitis -- from jumps; "jumper's knee"
  Eating disorders    -- aesthetic pressure; 2-3x general population rate
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Approach | Why |
|-----------|----------|-----|
| Student's turnout limited (<45 degrees hip ER) | Strengthen deep rotators; avoid forcing from feet | Forced turnout = knee/ankle injury; true turnout comes from hip joint |
| Choosing between Vaganova and RAD training | Vaganova for expressive upper body (port de bras); RAD for structured examination system | Both produce professional dancers; emphasis differs |
| Pointe readiness assessment | Minimum age ~12, sufficient ankle/calf strength, clean releve alignment | Premature pointe = metatarsal stress fracture risk |
| Need to understand a ballet score/notation | Benesh Movement Notation (UK) or Stepanov (historical Russian) | Standard notation systems for ballet reconstruction |
| Choreography requires extreme extensions | Develop active flexibility, not just passive ROM | Performance requires muscular control at end range, not just reaching the position |
| What distinguishes Bolshoi from NYCB | Russian = dramatic/bravura; Balanchine = speed/musical/athletic |
| Why pointe shoes wear out fast | Professional: 12-20 hours of use before box collapses |
| What kills ballet technique | Sickling (foot turned in), rolling in arches, gripping toes |

## Common Confusion Points

**Turnout is a hip rotation, not a foot position.**
Forcing turnout from the ankle or knee while the hip is still forward causes knee and ankle injuries. Correct turnout begins at the hip joint (acetabulofemoral external rotation). Many students appear to have 180-degree turnout while actually rotating from the knee.

**"Balanchine style" is not sloppy technique.**
Balanchine ballets are considered among the hardest to execute. His aesthetic (speed, attack, longer balances, higher extensions) demands greater technical facility than Romantic-era ballet, not less.

**Corps de ballet work is harder than it looks.**
Dancing as a precise unit of 24 dancers moving identically for 45 minutes (Shades scene, Bayadere) requires greater spatial awareness and internal timing than solo work.

**Romantic and Classical are distinct eras.**
Romantic ballet (1820-1870): long skirts, supernatural themes, ethereal women. Classical ballet (1870-1909): short tutus, display of legs, grand structure, Petipa. These are historical periods with specific aesthetic meanings, not levels of quality.

**Male ballet dancers are athletes, not weak.**
Elite male ballet dancers perform tour en l'air (triple rotation before landing), manage 80 kg partners through overhead lifts in arabesque, and sustain 2-hour performances. Training begins at age 8-10 and continues 6 days/week.
