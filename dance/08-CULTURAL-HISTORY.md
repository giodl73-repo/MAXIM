# Dance as Cultural and Political History

## The Big Picture

Dance has never been politically neutral. Who gets to dance, how, in what context, to whose music, on whose terms -- these questions encode the power structures of their historical moments. Reading dance history is reading political history through the body.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|           DANCE AS POLITICAL INDICATOR                                |
|                                                                      |
|  POWER ASSERTS ITSELF      RESISTANCE USES DANCE                     |
|  THROUGH DANCE             AGAINST POWER                             |
|                                                                      |
|  Louis XIV founds academy  Slave dances survive colonialism          |
|  (centralizes culture)     (Congo Square, New Orleans)               |
|                                                                      |
|  Nazi Germany uses ballet  Capoeira disguises as dance               |
|  as national prestige       (resists Portuguese suppression)         |
|                                                                      |
|  Soviet Union exports       Lindy Hop as Black cultural              |
|  ballet (Cold War)          assertion (Harlem Renaissance)           |
|                                                                      |
|  Colonial India bans        Bharatanatyam revival as                 |
|  devadasi dance             anti-colonial cultural identity          |
|                                                                      |
|  Flamenco made "safe"       Gitano communities maintain              |
|  for tourism (regime)       the underground authentic form           |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Dance and Colonialism

### The Suppression of Indigenous Dance

Colonial powers consistently targeted dance as a site of cultural control:

```
COLONIAL DANCE SUPPRESSION EXAMPLES

NATIVE AMERICAN GHOST DANCE (1889-1890):
  Wovoka (Paiute prophet) -- vision of spiritual renewal
  Ghost Dance: circular dance, reunite living and dead
  Spread rapidly across Plains tribes (1889-1890)
  U.S. government banned the dance (Circular 1372, 1883 Indian Religious
  Crimes Code -- dance = "heathenish practices")
  Sitting Bull arrested in connection with Ghost Dance activism
  Massacre at Wounded Knee (Dec 29, 1890):
    290 Lakota killed by 7th Cavalry
    Immediately after Sitting Bull's arrest
    In context of Ghost Dance suppression
  The dance was the political act; the massacre was the response.

BHARATANATYAM AND THE ANTI-NAUTCH MOVEMENT:
  British India, 1890s-1910s:
    Missionaries + Indian social reformers labeled devadasi dancing
    as "prostitution" (moral reform language)
    Anti-Nautch Act (1947, Tamil Nadu): criminalized devadasi practice
    Devadasi = women consecrated to temple service
    Temple dancing had existed for 1,500+ years
  Cultural genocide through "moral reform" framing.

  THE REVIVAL (1930s):
    E. Krishna Iyer: argued Bharatanatyam was classical art
    Rukmini Devi Arundale (Brahmin woman): performed and taught
    Transformed devadasi art into "respectable" concert dance
    -- Saved the art form
    -- Also brahmanized and sanitized it
    -- The original devadasi practitioners were displaced again
    This is the version now taught as "classical Indian dance."
```

### Capoeira: The Martial Art Hidden in Dance

```
CAPOEIRA AND COLONIAL RESISTANCE

ORIGIN: Enslaved African Brazilians, 17th-18th century

FUNCTION:
  A martial art system for self-defense and escape
  -- Encoded as dance to avoid recognition and punishment
  -- Portuguese colonizers banned martial training for enslaved people
  -- Capoeira performed to music (berimbau) as dance/game

  THE DECEPTION:
    From outside: two people playing a physical game with music
    Inside the practice: a full fighting system
    Ginga: the constant swaying movement disguises attack preparation
    Attacks delivered via legs (not arms) -- "innocent" leg movements

HISTORICAL TRAJECTORY:
  1890s: banned in Brazil after abolition (law against "capoeiragem")
  Mestre Bimba: reformed and legitimized capoeira (1930s)
  -- Capoeira Regional: more formalized, included Afro-Brazilian
  -- Mestre Pastinha: Capoeira Angola (older, more traditional style)
  1937: Vargas government: lifted ban; Bimba opens first school
  1972: declared Brazilian national sport
  2014: UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage
```

## Vernacular Dance and the Harlem Renaissance

The relationship between Black vernacular dance traditions and their adoption (and appropriation) by mainstream culture is one of the most significant stories in 20th-century American cultural history.

### Congo Square to the Ballroom

```
TRAJECTORY: AFRICAN AMERICAN SOCIAL DANCE

CONGO SQUARE (New Orleans, 1730s-1850s):
  Sunday gathering space where enslaved people maintained
  African musical and dance traditions
  Drumming, circle dances, spirit possession practices
  -- One of the few sites in North America where African
     cultural practice was tolerated under slavery
  -- Direct source of musical traditions leading to jazz/blues

EMANCIPATION AFTERMATH:
  Cakewalk (1890s): derived from enslaved people mocking
  plantation owners' formal European dancing
  White minstrelsy adopted and decontextualized it
  Became fashionable in white ballrooms (without knowledge of origins)

HARLEM RENAISSANCE (1920s-1930s):
  Savoy Ballroom (opened 1926):
    "The Home of Happy Feet"
    Mixed-race ballroom (one of few in segregated NYC)
    Where Lindy Hop developed and flourished

  THE LINDY HOP:
    Named for Charles Lindbergh's 1927 Atlantic crossing
    ("Lindy" + "hop" as energy metaphor)
    Developed by Frankie Manning, Norma Miller, others at the Savoy
    Key innovation: "break away" -- couple separates for improvisation
    First swing dance with aerial moves (lifts)
    -- Improvisational core
    -- Competitions as community celebration (not just couple dancing)
```

### The Appropriation Pattern

```
APPROPRIATION CYCLE (repeats throughout 20th century)

PHASE 1: Black community develops vernacular dance form
  -- In community context: social, improvisational, communal
  -- Lindy Hop, Twist, Jerk, Hustle, Breakdance, Vogue

PHASE 2: Form becomes visible to white mainstream
  -- Via television, Broadway, mass media
  -- Often in sanitized form

PHASE 3: White artists/entertainers adopt the form
  -- Often explicitly credit origins (sometimes)
  -- Commercial version loses community context

PHASE 4: Form becomes "American" or "universal" culture
  -- Original community attribution fades
  -- Economic benefits flow to mainstream
  -- Original community may face discrimination while their
     art form is celebrated

EXAMPLE: BREAKDANCING / B-BOY
  Origin: South Bronx, 1970s
    DJ Kool Herc's block parties
    Breakbeat (the percussion break isolated and looped)
    B-boys/b-girls: dance during the break
    Cultural context: poverty, gang truce culture, block parties

  Evolution:
    1983-1984: mainstream TV exposure, movies ("Flashdance", "Beat Street")
    Commercial version: Hollywood spectacular moves
    Commercialization proceeds as South Bronx is defunded

  Current:
    Olympic sport (2024 Paris Olympics)
    Global practice; taught in studios worldwide
    Original community debates recognition and representation
```

## Ballroom Culture and Voguing

```
BALLROOM CULTURE (1960s-present)

ORIGIN:
  Black and Latino LGBTQ+ community, New York City
  Began as Black drag balls (Harlem, 1920s-1930s)
  Underground "house" system developed 1960s-1980s

  Houses: substitute family structures for LGBTQ+ youth
  rejected by biological families
  Mothers and Fathers of houses mentor younger members
  Members compete in "balls" in categories

BALL CATEGORIES (a partial list):
  Runway (fashion): walking, posing
  Femme queen realness
  Face: beauty
  Vogue femme
  Old way / New way vogueing
  Butch queen
  Bizarre categories (themed)

VOGUE: THE DANCE FORM
  Named for Vogue magazine poses
  Early vogue (1960s-70s): angular, geometric poses
  "Dipping" and "shade throwing" incorporated

  OLD WAY (1970s-80s):
    Precise angular poses
    Tells a story through frozen images
    Egyptian hieroglyphic inspiration (angular, profile views)
    Slow, deliberate transitions

  NEW WAY (late 1980s):
    Adds: hand performance (articulated hand movements)
    Adds: continuous flow (poses held briefly, then flow)
    Adds: catwalk
    More improvised within structure

  VOGUE FEMME (1990s-present):
    Hyperfeminine; dramatic drops and dips
    Runway, duck walk, death drops, spins
    The most athletic form; requires extreme flexibility

MAINSTREAM CROSSOVER:
  Malcolm McLaren "Deep in Vogue" (1989)
  Madonna "Vogue" (1990) -- massive commercial success
  Documentary "Paris is Burning" (1990) -- Jennie Livingston
  RuPaul's Drag Race (2009-) -- brought language mainstream
  FX "Pose" (2018-2021) -- first with actual ballroom performers

THE TENSION: Culture created by one of America's most
marginalized communities becomes global mainstream aesthetics
while those communities often still face violence and poverty.
```

## Soviet Ballet as State Power

```
SOVIET BALLET: STATE POWER AND ARTISTIC ACHIEVEMENT

EARLY SOVIET PERIOD (1917-1932):
  Initial Bolshevik suspicion of ballet:
    -- Elitist (pre-revolutionary court art)
    -- Not proletarian
    -- Some calls to abolish it
  But: Lunacharsky (Commissar of Culture) protected it
  Rationale: demonstrate Soviet cultural superiority

THE STALINIST TURN (1932-1953):
  Socialist Realism mandated for all arts
  Dance implications:
    -- Ballets must have positive, heroic, nationalist themes
    -- Formalist/abstract work forbidden
    -- "Understandable to the masses"
  Result: enormous production budgets + ideological control
    "Spartacus", "Romeo and Juliet", "Stone Flower" = approved
    Experimental work by Goleizhovsky etc. = suppressed

THE BOLSHOI AND MARIINSKY (KIROV) AS SYMBOLS:
  State investment: elaborate sets, hundreds of dancers, orchestras
  Foreign tours: began 1956 (Bolshoi in London)
  Cold War purpose: demonstrate Soviet cultural superiority
  American response: support for American Ballet Theatre and NYCB
  (George Balanchine, former Diaghilev, now American, seen as
  champion of the "free world" in dance)

DEFECTIONS AS POLITICAL EVENTS:
  Rudolf Nureyev: defected Paris 1961
    -- Mariinsky star; asked to return to Moscow mid-tour
    -- Ran to French police at Le Bourget airport
    -- International incident; huge press coverage
    -- Defection framed as: choosing freedom over tyranny

  Mikhail Baryshnikov: defected Toronto 1974
    -- Bolshoi tour; left during intermission
    -- Joined American Ballet Theatre; then NYCB (Balanchine)
    -- Symbol of artistic freedom vs state control

  Natalia Makarova: defected London 1970
  Alexander Godunov: defected NYC 1979

  Each defection was a geopolitical event with weeks of press.
```

## Modernism, Fascism, and Dance

```
DANCE UNDER FASCISM

NAZI GERMANY:
  Rudolf Laban (creator of Labanotation) initially collaborated:
    -- Designed mass movement spectacles for 1936 Nuremberg rally
    -- His "movement choirs" -- community dance events -- appealed
       to Nazi ideology of collective physical expression
    -- But: his work was then suppressed; called "degenerate"
    -- Laban fled to England 1938; developed LMA in exile

  WHAT WAS PROMOTED:
    German folk dances (Volkstanz): "authentic" German culture
    Gymnastic mass displays: collective body as state symbol
    Ballet (Balanchine's work): too cosmopolitan/Jewish connections

  WHAT WAS BANNED:
    Jazz and swing dance: associated with Black Americans
    Expressionist modern dance: "Jewish Bolshevist degeneracy"
    Mary Wigman's work: increasingly marginalized (she stayed)

MUSSOLINI'S ITALY:
  Similar pattern: folk dance and classical Italian promoted
  Modern international trends suspect
  Futurism (Italian origin) had complex relationship:
    -- Initial alignment with Fascism (Marinetti)
    -- Then: Futurist dance and performance increasingly sidelined

SPAIN UNDER FRANCO (1939-1975):
  Flamenco: state appropriated as symbol of Spanish identity
  Transformed from gitano/underground art to tourist product
  Regional dances (Catalan, Basque) suppressed as nationalist threats
  Flamenco performers faced surveillance and political pressure
```

## The Civil Rights Movement and Dance

```
DANCE IN THE AMERICAN CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT

ALVIN AILEY AND BLACK CONCERT DANCE:
  Alvin Ailey American Dance Theater (founded 1958, NYC)
    -- Primarily Black company; primarily Black aesthetic
    -- Emerged during early Civil Rights era
    -- Goal: bring concert dance to audiences who didn't see themselves

  "Revelations" (1960):
    -- Suite to African American spirituals and gospel music
    -- Most performed dance work in the world
    -- Explicitly claims Black American religious and cultural tradition
       as the highest concert dance subject
    -- Performed in the year of the sit-ins (Greensboro, 1960)
    -- Political act through purely artistic means

  Ailey's training and lineage:
    -- Lester Horton technique (LA-based; integrated company 1940s)
    -- Martha Graham technique
    -- "Revelations" made from the bodies of his own community's movement

ARTHUR MITCHELL AND DANCE THEATRE OF HARLEM:
  Arthur Mitchell: first permanent Black principal dancer at NYCB
  Joined 1969; performed Balanchine throughout the 1960s
  Founded Dance Theatre of Harlem (1969):
    -- After assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. (1968)
    -- Deliberate response: bring ballet to Harlem
    -- Train Black dancers in classical technique
    -- Prove Black bodies can do ballet (against racist stereotype)
  First major Black ballet company.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Dance is not apolitical because it doesn't use words.**
Every dance that appears in public space is operating within political conditions. Who is dancing, where, for whom, to whose music, in what style -- all of these carry political content whether intended or not.

**"Appropriation" is not always the same event.**
Cultural exchange and cultural appropriation differ in power dynamics and economic flow. When the originators of a form receive credit, compensation, and are not simultaneously oppressed for practicing what others profit from, that is exchange. When communities face discrimination while their innovations are monetized for others, that is appropriation. The difference is structural, not a matter of who likes the music.

**The Soviet ballet being state art does not make it artistically inferior.**
Prokofiev's "Romeo and Juliet" and "Cinderella," composed under Soviet conditions, are among the greatest ballet scores. Yuri Grigorovich's choreographies are works of real power. State control distorted and suppressed much -- it did not make everything produced under it mediocre.

**Bharatanatyam "classical" status does not mean ancient unbroken tradition.**
The dance form in its current presentation is a 20th-century reconstruction. Its relationship to its origins is complicated by colonial suppression, brahminization, and decontextualization. This doesn't make it less valid -- it makes its history complex.

**The Ballroom community created Voguing as self-defense and community, not performance art.**
Before Madonna's appropriation made Vogue a global commodity, ballroom was a survival structure for Black and Latino queer youth. The houses, the balls, the categories -- these were about belonging and recognition in a world that offered none. Understanding Voguing without understanding this context is understanding only the surface.
