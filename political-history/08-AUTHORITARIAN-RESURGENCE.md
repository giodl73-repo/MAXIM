## Competitive Authoritarianism

Levitsky and Way (*Competitive Authoritarianism: Hybrid Regimes After the Cold War*, 2010) define:

**Competitive authoritarianism**: Regimes where elections are held and meaningful (they can be lost), but the playing field is systematically tilted in favor of the incumbent through:
- Control of key state resources and media
- Selective harassment of opposition
- Manipulation of electoral rules
- Sporadic but credible use of coercion

```
COMPETITIVE AUTHORITARIANISM — PLAYING FIELD TILT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  DIMENSION          DEMOCRATIC        COMPETITIVE AUTHORITARIAN
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Media access       All parties       State media dominant;
                     can compete       private media
                                       self-censors or captured

  Campaign finance   Legal limits      State resources
                     apply to all      available to incumbent

  State resources    Neutral civil     Civil servants
                     service           campaign for incumbent

  Opposition         Protected by      Selective prosecution
  activity           law               of opposition leaders

  Electoral          Independent       Loyalists on commission
  management

  Elections          Genuinely         Hold them; occasionally
  themselves         uncertain         rig if needed

  KEY: Incumbent doesn't cancel elections; wins them,
       but through structural advantages, not just
       popularity
```

## Russia's Hybrid War Model

Russia under Putin developed a model of political warfare that combines conventional and unconventional tools.

### The Gerasimov Doctrine (Contested)

Valery Gerasimov, Chief of the Russian General Staff, published an article in 2013 (*Military-Industrial Courier*) that was interpreted in the West as describing a doctrine of "non-linear war":

```
GERASIMOV'S "DOCTRINE" (Western interpretation)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "The role of non-military means of achieving political
  and strategic goals has grown, and, in many cases, they
  have exceeded the power of force of weapons in their
  effectiveness."

  TOOLKIT:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ • Information operations (disinformation campaigns)     │
  │ • Cyber attacks (Estonia 2007, Georgia 2008)            │
  │ • Political warfare (funding extremist parties in Europe)│
  │ • Economic coercion (gas supply manipulation)           │
  │ • "Little green men" (unmarked forces — Crimea 2014)    │
  │ • Proxies (Donbas 2014 — deniable military support)     │
  │ • Reflexive control theory (shape adversary's decisions  │
  │   by managing their information environment)            │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  CAVEAT: Mark Galeotti (who popularized the phrase
  "Gerasimov Doctrine") later acknowledged the doctrine
  was a Western analytical construct, not a Russian
  self-described strategy. Gerasimov was describing
  Western hybrid tactics used in color revolutions.
```

### Russia's Information Operations

Russian information operations (Internet Research Agency, GRU) targeted Western democracies:
- 2016 US election: social media manipulation, DNC hack and leak
- Brexit: RT coverage, possible funding of Leave.eu
- European elections: funding of far-right parties (France's RN, Italy's Lega)

The goal is not necessarily to elect specific candidates but to **amplify polarization and undermine trust in democratic institutions** — making democratic societies doubt their own systems.

### Social Credit System

The "social credit system" is often misrepresented in Western media as a single unified score. The reality is more fragmented:

- **Corporate social credit**: Regulatory compliance tracking for businesses — existing compliance systems digitized
- **Financial credit scoring**: Similar to Western credit scores
- **Sectoral blacklists**: Lists of people banned from taking flights or trains for unpaid debts, court judgments — these are the widely reported cases
- **Pilot local programs**: Rongcheng (Shandong) ran a citizen score pilot; it has not been scaled nationally

The unified dystopian score assigning one number to every citizen is not currently deployed at scale. But the infrastructure for pervasive surveillance does exist, and enforcement is real in blacklist programs.

### Technology Export

China exports surveillance technology through:
- **Huawei**: 5G infrastructure with presumed Chinese state access
- **Hikvision, Dahua**: CCTV systems sold globally (70+ countries)
- **ZTE**: Telecom infrastructure
- **CloudWalk**: Face recognition in Zimbabwe
- **Alibaba Cloud**: Data infrastructure

The pattern: BRI financing packages sometimes include surveillance infrastructure. The risk: surveillance architecture that CCP uses domestically can be replicated by purchasing governments.

## Common Confusion Points

**Social credit system ≠ Black Mirror**: The Chinese social credit system in media coverage is often portrayed as a single unified dystopian score. The reality is a set of separate systems: corporate compliance tracking, financial credit, and sectoral blacklists (flight/train bans for court-ordered debt defaulters). Dangerous and worth monitoring, but not yet the unified citizen score of science fiction. The surveillance infrastructure exists to build such a system if Beijing chooses.

**The Gerasimov Doctrine was largely a Western invention**: Mark Galeotti coined the phrase; Gerasimov's 2013 article was describing (and criticizing) Western hybrid warfare methods he thought had been used in the color revolutions, not describing a Russian strategy. Russia does conduct hybrid warfare, but the "doctrine" as a formal strategy was misattributed.

**Performance legitimacy is fragile**: China's authoritarian legitimacy rests heavily on economic performance. The same model that sustained 7%+ growth for 30 years is showing stress (property crisis, youth unemployment, demographic decline). When performance-based legitimacy fails, authoritarian systems face a choice between liberalization (risky) and intensified repression (also risky). No middle ground.

**Authoritarianism and corruption**: The academic literature distinguishes between authoritarian systems that are developmentally effective (Singapore, South Korea under Park, China under Deng) and extractive kleptocracies (Mobutu, Mugabe, Maduro). The former do sometimes deliver economic growth; the latter almost never do. Conflating them obscures the actual comparison with democracy.
