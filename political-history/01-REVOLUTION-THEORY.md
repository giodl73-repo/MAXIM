## Defining Revolution

Not all upheaval is revolution. The scholarly definition requires two things:

1. **Forcible transfer of state power** from one group to another
2. **Structural transformation** — not just who holds power but how power is organized

Crane Brinton's classic typology (*Anatomy of Revolution*, 1938) studied four "great" revolutions (English, American, French, Russian) and found a common fever curve: moderate phase → radical phase → thermidor (conservative reaction). This is descriptive pattern-finding, not causal theory.

The move to causal theory comes in the 1970s-80s.

## Skocpol: Structural Analysis

Theda Skocpol, *States and Social Revolutions* (1979), is the central text. It established comparative-historical analysis as a method and structural theory as the dominant explanatory framework.

### Three Cases: France, Russia, China

Skocpol chose cases with revolutionary outcomes and analyzed them against non-revolutionary cases (Prussia, England, Japan) using "method of agreement and difference" — J.S. Mill's comparative logic.

### Key Insight: Revolutionary Actors Don't Cause Revolutions

The counterintuitive Skocpol thesis: **revolutionary movements don't create revolutionary situations — they exploit them.** The Russian Bolsheviks were a tiny party in February 1917. The state had already collapsed from WWI. They won because they were the best-organized group in a power vacuum.

This is structuralism at its strongest: the revolutionaries' ideology, leadership quality, and organizational skill matter far less than structural preconditions.

### Critiques

- Underweights ideology and leadership (why did Stalin rather than Trotsky consolidate power?)
- Selection bias toward successful revolutions — the model doesn't predict which crises will become revolutions
- Ignores agency of key individuals (Lenin, Robespierre)
- Iranian Revolution (1979) doesn't fit cleanly — no peasant revolt, no landlord class conflict in Skocpol's sense

## Goldstone: Demographic-Structural Model

Jack Goldstone (*Revolution and Rebellion in the Early Modern World*, 1991; revised in *Revolutions*, 2014) extends and refines Skocpol with a focus on population dynamics.

### Core Model

```
GOLDSTONE'S DEMOGRAPHIC-STRUCTURAL PRESSURES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  POPULATION        →    FISCAL STRESS    →    STATE CRISIS
  GROWTH                 (more people to        (can't pay
                          tax, more claims       army or
                          on state)              officials)
      │
      ▼
  ELITE               →    ELITE             →    INTRA-ELITE
  COMPETITION              OVERPRODUCTION         CONFLICT
  (more aspirants          (too many             (factions
   than positions)          educated elites        fight for
                            competing for          shrinking
                            fixed posts)           pie)
      │
      ▼
  POPULAR              →    SUBSISTENCE      →    MASS
  GROWTH                    CRISIS                MOBILIZATION
                            (rising food           (peasants /
                             prices, declining      urban poor)
                             real wages)

  THREE PRESSURES CONVERGING = REVOLUTIONARY CRISIS
```

Goldstone's model explains why revolutions come in waves: the 17th-century general crisis (English, Fronde, Ming collapse, Ottoman crisis) happened simultaneously across Eurasia because similar demographic pressures operated across different political systems.

### 5th Generation States

Goldstone's more recent work identifies "5th generation states" — authoritarian regimes with a single ruling family or party plus a rentier economy. They are vulnerable to a different dynamic: patron-client networks, elite defection when oil rents fall.

## Tilly: Contentious Politics and Repertoires

Charles Tilly approached revolution from a different angle: not why do states collapse but how do people make political claims, and when do those claims escalate to revolution?

### Contentious Repertoires

```
TILLY: REPERTOIRE OF CONTENTION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Pre-modern repertoire:          Modern repertoire:
  ┌─────────────────────┐         ┌─────────────────────┐
  │ • Grain seizures    │         │ • Strikes           │
  │ • Tax rebellions    │   →     │ • Demonstrations    │
  │ • Charivari         │ (18-19c) │ • Marches          │
  │ • Patron petitions  │         │ • Electoral campaigns│
  │ • Crowd actions     │         │ • Petition drives   │
  │   (local, direct,   │         │ (national, modular, │
  │    particular)      │         │  autonomous)        │
  └─────────────────────┘         └─────────────────────┘

  Key shift: from particular, direct, local claims
             to modular, autonomous, national claims

  Modular: same action (strike, march) can be used
           by different groups for different ends
```

### WUNC Framework

Claims gain traction when they display:
- **W**orthiness (respectable actors, symbols of legitimacy)
- **U**nity (clear collective identity, discipline)
- **N**umbers (demonstrating scale of support)
- **C**ommitment (willingness to bear costs, sacrifice)

### Revolutionary Situations vs. Revolutionary Outcomes

Tilly's crucial distinction:

**Revolutionary situation**: Multiple claimants to sovereignty — both old regime and challenger claim to speak for the state. These are common. They don't always produce revolutionary outcomes.

**Revolutionary outcome**: Actual transfer of state power to new coalition, with structural change.

What determines whether a revolutionary situation becomes an outcome? Coalition formation, defection by state forces (especially army), and the capacity of challengers to consolidate a new state apparatus.

## Arendt: Phenomenology of Revolution

Hannah Arendt (*On Revolution*, 1963) comes from an entirely different tradition. Not causal explanation but phenomenological analysis — what does revolution *mean*?

### Two Models of Revolution

```
  KEY DISTINCTION:
  Political revolution (freedom) vs.
  Social revolution (liberation from necessity)

```

### The Council Form

Arendt observed that every major modern revolution spontaneously produced councils — soviets in Russia, Räte in Germany, town meetings in America. These were not Bolshevik inventions but organic political forms.

Her thesis: councils are the genuine expression of *public freedom* — political action as an end in itself. The tragedy of revolutions is that parties and bureaucracies destroy the councils (the Bolsheviks dissolved the soviets once they seized power).

### Critique

Arendt's reading of the American Revolution is idealized — it ignores slavery, property qualifications, and the "social question" (poverty) in America. Her French/Russian comparison is more defensible. Her category of "social revolution" as necessarily leading to Terror is contested.

## Color Revolutions and Their Fate

"Color revolutions" (Serbia 2000, Georgia 2003, Ukraine 2004, Kyrgyzstan 2005) were mostly non-violent transitions using mass mobilization to challenge fraudulent elections.

```
COLOR REVOLUTION MODEL
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Fraudulent election → mass popular protest
         │
         ▼
  Security forces refuse to fire (crucial)
         │
         ▼
  Elite defection from old regime
         │
         ▼
  New leadership elected

  WHY IT WORKS:
  • WUNC requirements met (peaceful, mass, legitimate)
  • International support (EU/US signal recognition)
  • Security forces don't want to be on the losing side

  WHY IT FAILS:
  • Security forces loyal (Belarus 2020, Burma 2021)
  • Old elites reconsolidate (Kyrgyzstan after 2005)
  • No institutional depth to sustain change
  • Russia destabilizes successful cases (Ukraine 2014)
```

## Why Some Revolutions Succeed: Synthesis

```
REVOLUTIONARY SUCCESS CONDITIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NECESSARY CONDITIONS (Skocpol-Goldstone)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 1. State fiscal/military crisis         │
  │ 2. Elite defection (old guard fractures)│
  │ 3. Mass mobilization capacity           │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
               +
  SUFFICIENT CONDITIONS (Tilly + process)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ 4. Security force defection or collapse │
  │    (army won't fire = regime falls)     │
  │ 5. Challenger coalition coherent enough │
  │    to build new state apparatus         │
  │ 6. International context permissive     │
  │    (no great-power intervention to      │
  │     restore old regime)                 │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
               =
  REVOLUTIONARY OUTCOME
  (Not just situation, but transfer +
   structural change)
```

The Iranian Revolution (1979) is the hardest case: no state fiscal collapse in Skocpol's sense (oil revenues high), but a crisis of political legitimacy (Shah's modernization alienated clergy + bazaar merchants + secular left simultaneously). Goldstone's elite overproduction model fits better (rapid urbanization, expanding educated class, frustrated expectations).

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the single most important cause of revolution? | State fiscal/military crisis (Skocpol) — not grievances alone |
| Why do security forces matter so much? | Army willing to shoot = regime survives; refusing = regime falls |
| What made the Bolshevik seizure possible? | Pre-existing state collapse from WWI — they exploited a vacuum |
| Why did the French Revolution become the Terror? | Arendt: social question (poverty) overwhelmed political question (freedom) |
| What is a "revolutionary situation" vs. outcome? | Dual sovereignty is common; actual transfer + structural change is rare |
| Why do color revolutions sometimes fail? | Security forces stay loyal; old elites reconsolidate; no structural depth |
| What is Tilly's "modular repertoire"? | Same action (strike, march) usable by different groups — modern contentious politics |

## Common Confusion Points

**Grievances are necessary but not sufficient**: Every society has grievances. Revolutions require structural crisis (state capacity collapse) to convert grievances into political action at scale. This is the key Skocpol insight.

**Ideology matters, but not in the way populists think**: Revolutionaries' stated ideology often diverges from what they actually do in power. Bolsheviks stated communism; built a party-state bureaucracy. The structure of the crisis shapes the outcome more than the ideology of the victors.

**The "revolution devours its children" pattern**: Thermidor (Brinton), Stalinist purges, Khomeini's elimination of leftist allies — new regimes born from violence tend toward authoritarian consolidation. Arendt's analysis of why this happens (conflating social and political goals) is the most philosophically sophisticated account.

**Color revolutions are not Skocpol revolutions**: Color revolutions involve mass peaceful mobilization against fraudulent elections, not state collapse or class conflict. They are closer to Tilly's contentious politics model. Their sustainability depends on institutional depth that structural revolutions usually establish by force.

**Selection bias in revolutionary success narratives**: We study the French, Russian, and Chinese revolutions because they succeeded and mattered. The far more numerous revolutionary failures (German Revolution 1918-19, Hungarian Soviet 1919, Spanish Civil War 1936-39) are equally instructive for understanding what success requires.
