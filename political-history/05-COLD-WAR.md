## Orthodox Interpretation

The standard post-WWII American narrative (Truman, Acheson, Dean Rusk; historians Arthur Schlesinger Jr., Herbert Feis):

**Core claim**: The Cold War was caused by Soviet expansionism. Stalin violated wartime agreements (Poland, Eastern Europe), pursued global communist revolution, and forced the democratic world to respond.

```
ORTHODOX ARGUMENT:
  Soviet behavior 1945-49:
  • Violated Yalta agreements on free elections in Poland
  • Imposed satellite regimes in Eastern Europe
  • Blockade of Berlin (1948-49)
  • Support for communist parties in France, Italy, Greece
  • Atomic espionage (Rosenbergs, Fuchs)
  → These were aggressive acts requiring US response

  US response was defensive:
  • Truman Doctrine (1947): aid Greece/Turkey against communism
  • Marshall Plan (1948): economic recovery prevents communist
    electoral victories in Western Europe
  • NATO (1949): defensive alliance
  • Containment (Kennan's "Long Telegram", 1946 → NSC-68)
```

Strength: takes seriously Soviet behavior in Eastern Europe; confirmed by post-1991 archival evidence that Stalin was brutal and expansionist there.

Weakness: ignores US provocations, atomic monopoly threats, economic imperialism; treated America as purely reactive.

## Revisionist Interpretation

Emerged in context of Vietnam War, which made US foreign policy motives seem less benign:

**William Appleman Williams** (*The Tragedy of American Diplomacy*, 1959): The US pursued an "Open Door" empire — commercial and market access rather than formal colonies. Cold War was the conflict over who would control the post-colonial world's markets. American policy served economic interests.

**Gabriel Kolko** (*The Politics of War*, 1968): US deliberately sabotaged Soviet-American cooperation to ensure American dominance of post-war reconstruction. The Cold War served American capitalist interests.

```
REVISIONIST ARGUMENT:
  US provoked the Cold War:
  • Atomic bomb dropped on Japan to intimidate USSR,
    not just end Pacific war (Gar Alperovitz, 1965)
  • US atomic monopoly (1945-49) gave aggressive
    capability to coercive diplomacy
  • Truman Doctrine committed US to containing
    social revolution globally, not just Soviet aggression
  • Marshall Plan restructured European economy toward
    American capital interests, excluded USSR
  • US opposed Soviet security interests in Eastern Europe
    (legitimate given Nazi invasion path through Poland)
```

Strength: explains US interventions in Third World (Iran 1953, Guatemala 1954) that had little to do with Soviet threat.

Weakness: minimized genuine Soviet repression in Eastern Europe; treated Eastern European regimes as legitimate Soviet security interests.

## Post-Revisionist / Synthesis

John Lewis Gaddis, Melvyn Leffler, Michael Hogan in the 1970s-80s:

**Core position**: Both superpowers bore responsibility; each responded to the other's actions in ways that escalated; both had legitimate security interests that conflicted; ideology mattered on both sides.

```
POST-REVISIONIST FRAMEWORK:
  Misperception model:
  Each side interpreted the other's defensive measures
  as offensive threats → escalating spiral

  US:  Marshall Plan → economic security for Europe
  USSR: Marshall Plan → US economic imperialism,
         attempt to penetrate Soviet sphere

  USSR: Sphere of influence in Eastern Europe →
        defensive buffer against future German invasion
  US:  Eastern Europe → Soviet expansionism threatening
        Western Europe, violating self-determination

  RESULT: Both sides made moves rational from their
           perspective that the other interpreted as
           threatening → action-reaction escalation
```

Gaddis's later *We Now Know* (1997), using post-Soviet archives, moved him back toward a modified orthodox position: Stalin was more aggressive and ideologically committed than post-revisionists acknowledged.

## Origins Debate: Key Turning Points

### The Truman Doctrine and Containment

Kennan's "Long Telegram" (February 1946): Soviet behavior rooted in combination of Russian tradition and Marxist ideology. Cannot be reasoned with; must be "contained" at every point of expansion. Published as "X Article" in Foreign Affairs (July 1947).

NSC-68 (1950, drafted by Paul Nitze after Kennan left): Vastly expanded the military budget required for containment — from $13 billion to $50 billion annually. Treated every point on the globe as equally vital. Kennan criticized NSC-68 as militarizing what should be a political-diplomatic strategy.

### Korean War as Turning Point

```
KOREA 1950 AS COLD WAR TURNING POINT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  BEFORE KOREA (1945-50):
  • Cold War primarily European
  • US military budget declining
  • Debate about extent of containment

  JUNE 25, 1950: North Korea invades South Korea
  Stalin approved; Kim Il-sung's decision

  TRUMAN'S DECISION: Fight under UN flag
  • Interprets as Soviet-directed global strategy test
  • NSC-68 gets funded (Pearl Harbor effect on budget)
  • US rearms for global containment

  CHINESE INTERVENTION (October 1950):
  • MacArthur approaches Yalu River; China warns
  • China intervenes; UN forces pushed back
  • War stalemated at 38th parallel
  • MacArthur fired for insubordination
    (wanted to bomb China, possibly nuclear)

  CONSEQUENCE:
  Cold War becomes truly global, military, and expensive
  Containment becomes universal, not selective
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Nuclear Balance and Crisis Management

### Deterrence Doctrine Evolution

```

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  1950-57  MASSIVE RETALIATION (Eisenhower/Dulles):
           Any Soviet conventional aggression would trigger
           US nuclear response. "Bang for the buck" —
           cheaper than large conventional army.
           Problem: incredible for small provocations

  1957-    MUTUAL DETERRENCE:
           USSR tested H-bomb (1953), ICBM (1957),
           Sputnik (1957) → US now vulnerable to attack
           Second-strike capability = MAD achieved

  1961-    FLEXIBLE RESPONSE (McNamara/Kennedy):
           Multiple rungs of escalation; don't trigger
           nuclear war for every conventional provocation
           Criticized: lowers threshold for nuclear use
```

## Vietnam as Imperial Overextension

Vietnam is the clearest case of Cold War logic producing strategic disaster.

```
US IN VIETNAM — DECISION CASCADE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  1950: US funds French war effort against Viet Minh
  1954: Dien Bien Phu; Geneva Accords divide Vietnam
  1955: US backs Diem regime in South Vietnam
  1961: Kennedy sends military "advisors" (16,000 by 1963)
  1964: Gulf of Tonkin Resolution — blank check for escalation
         (incident partly fabricated)
  1965: Johnson escalates to 500,000 troops
  1968: Tet Offensive — military stalemate; political defeat
         Johnson doesn't seek re-election
  1969-73: Nixon "Vietnamization" + bombing Cambodia
  1973: Paris Peace Accords; US withdraws
  1975: North Vietnam reunifies country by force
         Saigon falls; US helicopters evacuate embassy

  STRATEGIC ERROR:
  Domino theory: if Vietnam falls to communism,
  all Southeast Asia follows.
  Reality: Vietnam-China rivalry; Soviet-Chinese split;
  Sino-American rapprochement (1972) already happening
  → Vietnam's communist victory didn't trigger the dominos
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Détente and Its Limits

Nixon-Kissinger détente (1969-76): manage Soviet-American competition through negotiation, arms control, linkage.

- SALT I (1972): Strategic Arms Limitation Talks — first US-Soviet arms control agreement; froze number of ICBMs
- ABM Treaty (1972): Limited anti-ballistic missile systems; preserved MAD
- Helsinki Accords (1975): Recognized post-WWII European borders (Soviet interest); included human rights commitments (Western interest — basket III)

**Limits**: Détente didn't stop Soviet expansion in Angola (1975), Ethiopia (1977), Afghanistan (1979). Each intervention eroded détente's domestic US support.

## Common Confusion Points

**Containment vs. rollback**: Kennan's containment was patient — wait for internal Soviet change; don't try to liberate Eastern Europe. NSC-68 and Dulles's "rollback" rhetoric promised liberation but never delivered it (Eisenhower didn't intervene in Hungary 1956 or Czechoslovakia 1968 — containment in practice, not rollback).

**SALT vs. START**: SALT (Strategic Arms Limitation Talks, 1972, 1979) froze numbers of delivery vehicles. START (Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty, 1991, 2010) actually reduced them. Important distinction for arms control history.

**Who "won" the Cold War**: Reagan conservatives claim credit; structural historians say the USSR was already falling; Gorbachev-centered historians credit his choices. The debate reflects underlying political commitments as much as historical evidence.

**The "long peace" paradox**: Scholars note that there was no direct US-Soviet military conflict, which they attribute to nuclear deterrence (Gaddis). But this ignores the millions who died in proxy wars — "long peace" for the great powers only.
