## What Political History Studies

Political history is the study of how political power has been organized, contested, and transformed over time. It sits at the intersection of description (what happened) and explanation (why it happened, why it mattered).

The central tension: is history made by individuals making choices under constraint, or by structural forces that determine outcomes regardless of who occupies the seat of power? Every school of political history takes a position on this. The answer is almost always "both, with interaction effects," which is why the field remains contested.

## Major Schools

### Rankean Tradition (19th century, still foundational)

Leopold von Ranke (1795-1886): history *wie es eigentlich gewesen* — "as it actually was." Primary source fetishism. The archive as sacred. Diplomatic and military history told through documents produced by elites.

Strengths: methodological rigor, archival honesty.
Limits: privileged actors, ignored structures, implicitly nationalist.

### Whig History

Named by Herbert Butterfield (1931) as a critique. Progress narrative: history as the inevitable march toward liberty, constitutional government, Protestant Christianity, and eventually liberal democracy. English historiography's default mode for 200 years.

```
Whig narrative arc:
  Magna Carta → Glorious Revolution → Reform Acts → Welfare State → Liberal Democracy
       ^
  (every event read backward from the present, as pointing toward us)
```

Butterfield's critique: teleological, anachronistic, uses the present to judge the past rather than understanding the past on its own terms. Still the implicit framework of most political commentary.

### Namierite Analysis

Lewis Namier (*The Structure of Politics at the Accession of George III*, 1929): strip ideology away, analyze actual voting behavior and patronage networks. 18th-century English MPs voted for interest, not principle. Structural analysis of political motivation underlying apparent ideological conflict.

Influence: congressional voting analysis in political science, and computationally network analysis of political actors, are Namier done at scale.

### Cambridge School (History of Political Ideas)

Quentin Skinner, J.G.A. Pocock: understand political ideas in their original linguistic context. *What question was this text answering?* Machiavelli's *Prince* means something different when you know which Florentine political debate he was entering.

Contrast with: great texts read as timeless philosophy (Leo Strauss school) vs. as historically embedded speech acts (Cambridge).

### Social History / History from Below

E.P. Thompson (*The Making of the English Working Class*, 1963): history of ordinary people, not elites. Class consciousness as a process, not a given. Subaltern actors in political history.

Political implication: revolutions, elections, and reforms look different when you study who was in the streets vs. who signed the treaties.

### Quantitative Political History / Cliometrics Applied

Acemoglu-Robinson (*Why Nations Fail*, 2012): institutions as causal variable. Natural experiments (settler mortality rates determining institutional form of colonialism, which persists for centuries) to establish causal identification.

The method maps directly to causal inference in empirical sciences generally: the settler-mortality instrument is a randomized-experiment analog — an exogenous shock that creates variation in the treatment (institutional form) without confounding. This is quasi-experimental design logic: find variation you didn't create, verify it's unconfounded, use it to identify a causal effect. The same logic underlies A/B testing with imperfect randomization, regression discontinuity designs, and difference-in-differences. Cliometrics is applied causal inference where you can't run experiments — the same epistemic problem faced in any observational study.

## Periodizations in Detail

```
POLITICAL WORLD-HISTORY TIMELINE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

ANCIENT EMPIRES (3000 BCE – 800 BCE)
  Egypt, Mesopotamia, Persia, China (Shang/Zhou/Qin)
  Core question: How did territorial control first emerge?
  Political form: Tribute empire, god-king, scribal state
  Key mechanism: irrigation monopoly → surplus → army → bureaucracy

CLASSICAL STATE-FORMS (800 BCE – 500 CE)
  Greek polis, Roman Republic → Empire, Han Dynasty
  Core question: citizenship, law, republican vs. imperial logic
  Key tension: self-governing republic vs. efficient empire (Rome)
  Legacy: Roman law, Greek political vocabulary still in use

MEDIEVAL POLITICAL ORDERS (500 – 1400)
  Feudalism, Church/Crown conflict, Islamic caliphates, Mongol empire
  Core question: Sacred vs. secular authority
  Political form: Feudal hierarchy, theocracy, caliphate, sultanate
  Investiture Controversy (1076): Pope vs. Holy Roman Emperor

EARLY MODERN STATE FORMATION (1400 – 1648)
  Renaissance city-states, Reformation, Habsburgs, Ottomans
  Core question: How did the territorial state with monopoly of
  violence emerge from feudal fragmentation?
  Political form: Absolute monarchy, composite monarchy
  Tilly: "War made the state, and the state made war"

WESTPHALIAN ORDER (1648 – 1789)
  Sovereign states, balance of power, mercantilist competition
  Core question: Sovereignty and its limits; dynastic vs. national
  Political form: Absolute monarchy, constitutional monarchy (England)
  Key document: Peace of Westphalia (1648) — cuius regio, eius religio

REVOLUTIONARY ERA (1789 – 1848)
  French Revolution, Napoleonic wars, Latin American independence,
  1848 revolutions across Europe
  Core question: Sovereignty of people vs. monarchy
  Political form: Republics, empires, constitutional monarchies
  Key concept: popular sovereignty, nationalism, modern ideology

IMPERIAL ERA & NATION-STATES (1848 – 1914)
  European nation-states, mass politics, scramble for Africa
  Core question: National self-determination vs. imperial order
  Political form: Parliamentary democracy, authoritarian empire,
  colonial administration

AGE OF TOTAL WAR (1914 – 1945)
  WWI, Russian Revolution, Fascism, Great Depression, WWII
  Core question: Why did liberal order collapse?
  Political form: Totalitarianism, fascism, liberal democracy
  Key rupture: State capacity to mobilize and destroy at industrial scale

POST-1945 ORDER (1945 – 1989)
  Cold War bipolarity, decolonization, welfare states, Bretton Woods
  Core question: Liberal democracy vs. communism
  Political form: Superpower blocs, non-aligned movement, developmental
  state

POST-COLD WAR UNIPOLAR MOMENT (1989 – ~2008)
  American hegemony, globalization, democratization wave
  Core question: Is liberal democracy the universal endpoint?
  Fukuyama: "end of history"; Huntington: civilizational clashes ahead

POLYCRISIS (2008 – present)
  2008 financial crisis, democratic backsliding, China rise,
  pandemic, climate, Russia's revanchism
  Core question: Is the liberal order stable, reformable, or terminal?
  Political form: Contested liberal order, competitive authoritarianism,
  hybrid regimes
```

The disciplines converge on overlapping questions. Skocpol is simultaneously sociological, historical, and political-scientific. Acemoglu writes economic history that is really about political institutions. The methodological debates within each field are as important as the substantive debates.

## Bridges: Political History's Analytical Tools

Political history's methods are isomorphic to tools used across formal disciplines.

**Comparative case analysis → controlled experiment with N=small**: Mill's method of agreement and difference (which Skocpol deploys explicitly) is the qualitative analog of experimental control. Vary one factor, hold others constant; observe whether the outcome changes. The small-N problem is real — confounding is harder to eliminate — but the logical structure is identical to any controlled study.

**Periodization → state machine design**: Every periodization is a claim that a system's governing dynamics changed at a specific transition point. "1648 Westphalia" is a regime change: the system moved from one stable equilibrium (dynastic-religious legitimacy) to another (sovereign territorial states). The historiographical debate about whether the transition was sharp or gradual maps directly to debates about phase transitions vs. continuous drift in dynamical systems.

**Causal inference from natural experiments → observational causal inference**: When historians exploit exogenous shocks (a war's outcome determined by weather, a pandemic's geographic spread, a colonial boundary drawn by an administrator who'd never visited the region) to identify effects, they're doing the same thing as difference-in-differences or instrumental variables estimation. The identifying assumption is always the same: the shock is as-good-as-random with respect to the outcome.

**Structural vs. agent-level explanation → system-level vs. component-level causation**: The Great Man vs. structural history debate is the historical form of a universal problem in complex systems: does the system's behavior arise from component properties (individual leaders) or from network structure and feedback dynamics? The resolution is always the same — both, with interaction effects — but the interesting question is which level is load-bearing for any specific outcome.

## Why Political History Matters

1. **Causal templates**: Revolutions, state collapse, democratic backsliding follow recognizable patterns. The patterns are known because historians have compared cases. Comparative-historical analysis is the method.

2. **Contingency awareness**: Historical literacy resists the fallacy that current arrangements are inevitable or permanent. The liberal international order is 75 years old — a brief window in political time.

3. **Counterfactual discipline**: The tools for thinking about what could have happened differently are also tools for evaluating present-day choices. If you understand that WWI was a contingent catastrophe that didn't have to happen, you reason differently about crisis management.

4. **Institutional design from memory**: Why does the post-WWII order include the IMF, UN, and NATO? Because decision-makers in 1944-49 had a specific theory of what caused two world wars and designed deliberately against it. Understanding that design logic is the prerequisite for evaluating its reform.

## Common Confusion Points

**Political history vs. political science**: History is idiographic (explains particular events in their full particularity); political science is nomothetic (seeks general laws). In practice the best work in both fields blurs this line. Skocpol does comparative historical analysis that reads like both simultaneously.

**Periodization is contested**: The "Westphalian system" of sovereign states is routinely cited as beginning in 1648, but historians of the actual peace negotiations argue it was much messier than the IR theory textbook version. Andreas Osiander's critique: Westphalia didn't establish sovereignty; it settled religious disputes. Periodizations are analytical conveniences, not natural joints in history.

**The archive problem**: Political history is constrained by what was written down and survived. Elite voices are massively overrepresented. Declassified archives — post-Cold War Soviet archives, CIA, British 30-year rule — have genuinely changed historical understanding of specific periods (origins of Cold War, Cuban Missile Crisis decision-making).

**Teleology trap**: Reading history backward from a known outcome — "the Roman Empire was bound to fall," "Hitler was always going to start WWII" — is epistemologically wrong and practically dangerous. Decision-makers didn't know the outcome. The corrective is counterfactual thinking (see 09-HISTORIOGRAPHY).

**Whig history is still the default**: Most political commentary implicitly assumes a progressive arc. When you see phrases like "the right side of history," that is Whig history with the serial numbers filed off.
