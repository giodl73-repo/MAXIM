### The WWII Demonstration Effect

Two events shattered the myth of European invincibility:
1. **France fell in six weeks** (June 1940) — the supposed greatest continental power
2. **Singapore fell to Japan** (February 1942) — 130,000 British troops surrendered; largest British capitulation in history

These demonstrated to colonial subjects that European powers were not invincible. Indian soldiers who had fought for Britain returned home asking why they should accept colonial rule.

### International Norms Shift

- UN Charter (1945): Article 1 includes "self-determination of peoples"
- US anti-colonialism: genuine ideological opposition (Wilson's 14 points; FDR pressed Churchill at Atlantic Charter meeting, 1941); also strategic (wanted to displace British Empire with American informal influence)
- Soviet anti-colonialism: USSR consistently supported anti-colonial movements ideologically and materially
- Bandung Conference (1955): 29 nations; NAM articulates Third World alternative

## British Managed Transfer vs. French Violent Resistance

### British Model: Negotiated Withdrawal

```
BRITISH DECOLONIZATION LOGIC
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PREFERENCE: Negotiate independence before forced out
              → maintain economic/cultural ties through
              Commonwealth → "informal empire" persists

  MECHANISM:
  1. Work with nationalist elites (Nehru, Nkrumah)
  2. Grant internal self-government first
  3. Transfer sovereignty when stable successor clear
  4. Maintain trade, sterling area, military bases

  WORKED: India, Gold Coast/Ghana, Nigeria, Malaya, Kenya
  (eventually, after brutal suppression of Mau Mau)

  FAILED: Palestine (couldn't manage Zionist-Arab conflict,
          handed to UN), Rhodesia (settler minority refused)
```

### French Model: Resistance and Defeat

France tried to maintain empire through military force in two catastrophic wars:

**Indochina (1946-54)**:
- Viet Minh (Ho Chi Minh) fought French colonial restoration
- Dien Bien Phu (1954): French military defeat — garrison surrendered
- Geneva Accords: French withdrew; Vietnam temporarily divided
- US fills the vacuum, inheriting the conflict

**Algeria (1954-62)**:
- Algeria was constitutionally part of France, not a colony (1 million pieds-noirs, European settlers)
- FLN guerrilla war; French used systematic torture (exposed by Henri Alleg's *The Question*, 1958)
- Parachutist nearly overthrew the Fourth Republic (1958) — de Gaulle called back to power
- De Gaulle recognized Algerian independence (Evian Accords, 1962) over settler resistance
- OAS terror campaign in France; Secret Army tried to assassinate de Gaulle

## India Partition

The partition of British India into India and Pakistan (August 1947) is one of the largest forced population movements in history.

```
INDIA PARTITION — KEY FACTORS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  MOUNTBATTEN SPEED:
  Wavell's plan: 1948 transition — gradual
  Mountbatten's plan: August 1947 — immediately
  Reason: Hindu-Muslim violence already starting,
  faster exit seemed safer
  Result: Violence exploded during partition itself

  RADCLIFFE LINE:
  Cyril Radcliffe: drew borders of Punjab and Bengal
  in 5 weeks — had never been to India before
  Maps were inadequate; census data uncertain
  Announced August 17, two days after independence

  SCALE OF VIOLENCE AND MOVEMENT:
  • 10-20 million people moved (largest forced migration
    in history at the time)
  • 200,000 - 2,000,000 killed (estimates vary enormously)
  • Communal massacres: Muslims leaving India,
    Hindus/Sikhs leaving Pakistan
  • Punjab worst affected; Bengal second

  KASHMIR DISPUTE:
  Maharaja of Kashmir (Hindu ruler, Muslim majority
  population) dithered; Pakistani tribal forces invaded;
  Maharaja acceded to India; First Kashmir War (1947-48)
  → Ceasefire line (now Line of Control) divides Kashmir
  Three more wars follow (1965, 1971, 1999 Kargil)
```

## African Decolonization

### Gold Coast 1957: The Template

Kwame Nkrumah's Convention People's Party used Gandhian tactics — strikes, boycotts, non-cooperation — against British rule. The CPP won parliamentary majority; Britain negotiated independence rather than fight. Ghana became the first sub-Saharan African country to achieve independence (March 6, 1957).

Nkrumah's phrase: "Seek ye first the political kingdom, and all else shall be added unto you." Independence first, economic development follows. The logic that African political elites would use across the continent.

### The Congo Crisis (1960-65)

The Congo (Belgian Congo → Republic of Congo → Zaire → DRC) is the paradigm case of decolonization failure:

```
CONGO CRISIS SEQUENCE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  June 30, 1960: Independence from Belgium
        │
        ▼ Days later: Army mutiny; Belgian paratroopers return
        │
        ▼ Katanga province secedes (backed by Belgian mining
        │  interests — Union Minière)
        │
        ▼ UN peacekeeping force deployed (ONUC) — largest
        │  to date
        │
        ▼ Patrice Lumumba (PM) requests Soviet aid to
        │  suppress secession; US/Belgium alarmed
        │
        ▼ Mobutu (army colonel) with CIA backing deposes
        │  Lumumba; Lumumba executed (January 1961)
        │
        ▼ Five years of civil war; Katanga reintegrated (1963)
        │
        ▼ Mobutu seizes power (1965); renames country Zaire;
          rules kleptocratically for 32 years
```

Lumumba's assassination became a symbol of Cold War interference in African decolonization. The CIA's role was confirmed in 1975 (Church Committee).

## The Non-Aligned Movement (Bandung 1955)

The Bandung Conference (April 1955, Indonesia) brought together 29 African and Asian states — representing 54% of world population. No European or American states invited.

Key principles declared:
1. Mutual respect for territorial integrity and sovereignty
2. Mutual non-aggression
3. Non-interference in internal affairs
4. Equality and mutual benefit
5. Peaceful coexistence

This was not neutralism — it was a positive claim to a Third World identity that rejected Cold War binary. Nehru's articulation: "We do not intend to be the plaything of others."

In practice, NAM states took sides selectively, played superpowers against each other for aid, and the movement became institutionally weak. But ideologically it was the seed of a claim to Southern agency in international affairs.

## Fanon: Violence and National Consciousness

Frantz Fanon (*The Wretched of the Earth*, 1961) is the theoretical text of anti-colonial violence.

Fanon was a psychiatrist from Martinique who joined the FLN in Algeria and wrote from within the Algerian revolution.

```
FANON'S ARGUMENT STRUCTURE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  PREMISE: Colonial violence is not incidental but constitutive.
  The colonial order is maintained through violence:
  police, military, spatial segregation (the Manichean city —
  the settler quarter vs. the native quarter).

  ARGUMENT: Violence by the colonized is not merely political
  strategy but psychological necessity:
  • The colonized subject is dehumanized by the colonial order
  • Violence reclaims agency and subjectivity
  • "Violence is a cleansing force. It frees the native from
     his inferiority complex and from his despair and inaction"

  CRITIQUE OF BOURGEOIS NATIONALISM:
  The nationalist elite that inherits the colonial state
  replaces the colonial bourgeoisie but perpetuates the
  same structure. The peasantry is mobilized for
  independence and then abandoned.

  FANON'S PRESCRIPTION:
  • Peasant-centered revolution (contrasting Marxist
    emphasis on urban proletariat)
  • National consciousness must become social consciousness
  • Third World should create a "new humanism"
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

Fanon was wrong about some things (his view of violence as cathartic is psychologically questionable) but his structural analysis of the colonial order and the limitations of bourgeois nationalism proved prescient in many post-colonial states.

## Common Confusion Points

**Decolonization vs. independence**: Formal independence does not equal decolonization in the full sense. Dependency theorists and Fanon argue that economic decolonization didn't happen — the same extraction structures persisted under new flags.

**The Mountbatten speed controversy**: Was partition violence caused by Mountbatten's rushed timetable or was communal violence already inevitable? Probably both: violence was beginning, and the rushed timetable meant the Radcliffe Line was announced after independence, maximizing the chaos of population movement.

**NAM neutralism**: The Non-Aligned Movement was not the same as neutralism or pacifism. NAM states often accepted weapons from both superpowers, and "non-alignment" meant refusing to be permanently aligned in the Cold War binary, not refusing to take positions on specific issues.

**Belgian Congo vs. British Africa timing**: Belgium decolonized the Congo faster than Britain decolonized most of its Africa — and with far less preparation. The Belgians had trained no Congolese civil servants, doctors, or officers of any seniority by 1960. British colonial education policy, for all its paternalism, produced a class of educated nationalists who could govern. The outcome gap between Congo and Ghana reflected this institutional preparation difference.
