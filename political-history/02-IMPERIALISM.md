## Economic Theories of Imperialism

### Hobson: Finance Capital and Underconsumption

J.A. Hobson (*Imperialism: A Study*, 1902) developed the foundational economic theory.

**Argument**: British capitalism generates surplus capital that cannot find profitable investment at home (because workers are underpaid and cannot consume enough). This capital seeks returns abroad — in mines, railways, plantations. Imperialism is the political-military machinery that secures these investment outlets.

```
HOBSON'S MECHANISM:
  Income inequality at home
          │
          ▼
  Workers underpaid → underconsumption
          │
          ▼
  Capitalists have surplus capital with no profitable domestic use
          │
          ▼
  Capital exports to periphery seeking higher returns
          │
          ▼
  Political control (empire) needed to secure investment
          │
          ▼
  Imperial policy = finance capital's foreign policy
```

**Policy implication** (progressive): Fix income inequality at home, and the economic drive to empire disappears. Hobson was a liberal reformer, not a Marxist.

**Influence**: Lenin read Hobson and radicalized the argument.

### Lenin: Imperialism as the Highest Stage

Lenin (*Imperialism: The Highest Stage of Capitalism*, 1917):

- Capitalism has moved from competitive to monopoly stage (cartels, trusts, finance capital domination)
- Banks and industrial capital merge into finance capital
- Capital export replaces commodity export as the dominant form
- World divided among great powers into spheres of exploitation
- Rivalry over re-division of the world is the cause of WWI

```
LENIN'S FIVE FEATURES OF IMPERIALISM:
  1. Concentration → monopolies dominate economic life
  2. Bank + industrial capital merge → finance capital
  3. Capital export (not just goods) becomes decisive
  4. International monopoly combines divide the world
  5. Territorial division of the globe among great powers
```

**Critique**: Lenin wrote during WWI as political pamphlet. Empirically: capital did not consistently flow from wealthy to poor countries (most British investment went to settler colonies — US, Canada, Australia — not India). The timing of colonial acquisition doesn't track capital export peaks well.

### Schumpeter: Atavism Model

Joseph Schumpeter (*Sociology of Imperialism*, 1919): completely rejects the economic explanation.

**Argument**: Modern imperialism is driven not by capitalism but by *pre-capitalist* warrior aristocracies that survived modernization and retained state power (Prussian Junkers, Japanese samurai class). Capitalism itself is inherently pacific — merchants prefer trade to conquest. Empire is atavistic: the dead hand of the past.

**Implication**: Mature capitalism will make war obsolete (the liberal commercial peace thesis — echoed in democratic peace theory in IR).

**Critique**: Explains Wilhelmine Germany plausibly, but not British imperialism (a highly commercialized state). Doesn't explain settler colonialism driven by land-hungry farmers, not aristocrats.

## Robinson-Gallagher: Imperialism of Free Trade

Ronald Robinson and John Gallagher (*Africa and the Victorians*, 1961; earlier article "Imperialism of Free Trade," 1953) revolutionized British imperial history.

**Thesis**: The distinction between "free trade" mid-Victorian era and "imperialist" late-Victorian era is false. Britain preferred *informal empire* — market dominance without the cost of administration — but would formalize control when informal control broke down.

```
ROBINSON-GALLAGHER SPECTRUM:
  Preference: ←─────────────────────────────────────────→
              Informal control          Formal annexation

  When informal works: Buenos Aires (British trade, no colony)
  When informal breaks down: Egypt 1882 (Arabi revolt → invasion)

  The decisive variable: local collaboration
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Empire works when there are reliable local allies    │
  │ (comprador class, collaborating elite)               │
  │                                                      │
  │ When the local collaborative structure collapses,    │
  │ Britain must either formalize control or withdraw    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Peripheral causation**: The Scramble for Africa was not driven by capitalists demanding colonies but by crises on the periphery (Egyptian financial collapse, Zulu wars) that forced British hands. Empire was often *defensive* — preventing rivals from filling vacuums.

## The Scramble for Africa (Berlin 1884-85)

Before 1880: ~10% of Africa under formal European control (coastal enclaves).
By 1914: ~90% under European control.

The Berlin Conference (1884-85) was convened by Bismarck — paradoxically, not to divide Africa (no territorial division occurred there) but to establish rules for the scramble already under way. Key principle: "effective occupation" required to assert territorial claims.

```
SCRAMBLE FOR AFRICA — CAUSES DEBATE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ METROPOLITAN CAUSATION:                              │

  │ → Problem: Africa was marginal in European trade     │
  │                                                      │
  │ STRATEGIC COMPETITION:                               │
  │ States scramble to deny rivals, not for positive gain│
  │ France vs. Britain vs. Germany zero-sum logic        │
```

## Bridges: Imperialism as Systems of Control and Extraction

**Robinson-Gallagher's informal-vs-formal empire spectrum → governance through protocol vs. vertical integration**: Informal empire (market dominance without administrative control) is the geopolitical equivalent of platform control through API and standards rather than ownership. Britain controlled Argentina's trade without a single colony there: the railway network was British-owned, the loans were denominated in sterling, the contracts were governed by English commercial law. When the system worked, no formal control was needed. When Argentina threatened to defect (Arabi revolt analog: a local actor moving outside the system), Britain faced the same choice any platform faces when a major participant violates the terms — enforce or let them exit. The Scramble for Africa was formalization triggered by collaborative structure collapse, not by metropolitan demand for colonies. This is the logic of "vertical integration when transaction costs spike."

**Dependency theory's core-periphery structure → directed graph of value extraction with structural lock-in**: Frank's world-system is a weighted directed graph: value flows from periphery nodes (raw materials, cheap labor) through semi-periphery processing nodes to core nodes (capital goods, finance, technology rents). The graph is not static — positions change — but the structure itself tends to be self-reinforcing. Terms of trade deteriorate for commodity exporters because manufactured goods embody increasing returns (learning curves, network effects, complementary innovation) while commodities don't. This is path dependence at the international level: once in a peripheral position, the infrastructure, skills, institutions, and trade relationships all co-adapt to extraction rather than to diversification. Exit requires coordinated simultaneous change across all these dimensions — which is why the East Asian developmental states that successfully exited (Korea, Taiwan) required state capacity to force the coordination.

**Colonial-era institutional persistence → path dependence as long-run constraint**: Acemoglu's settler-mortality instrument demonstrates that colonial institutional form (extractive vs. settler) predicts present-day GDP centuries later. The mechanism is institutional persistence: property rights regimes, legal systems, educational infrastructure, and bureaucratic traditions all exhibit strong continuity because they create constituencies who benefit from the existing form. This is not mysterious — it is the standard story of technology lock-in applied to institutions. Once an extraction economy's infrastructure (ports, railways, export-oriented legal systems) is built, switching to a diversified development model requires writing off the existing capital stock and rebuilding — which concentrated incumbents will resist.

## Dependency Theory and Neo-Imperialism

### Frank and Cardoso: Dependency Theory

Latin American social scientists challenged the modernization theory assumption that development was a universal process all countries would eventually complete.

Andre Gunder Frank: "development of underdevelopment." The global capitalist system actively *produces* underdevelopment in the periphery to benefit the core. Peripheral elites (comprador bourgeoisie) are integrated into the global system and serve metropolitan interests rather than national development.

Fernando Henrique Cardoso (later President of Brazil): "associated-dependent development" — more nuanced. Some growth possible in periphery but structurally conditioned by core capital requirements.

```
DEPENDENCY THEORY WORLD STRUCTURE:
  CORE                  SEMI-PERIPHERY           PERIPHERY
  (US, Europe, Japan)   (Brazil, Korea,           (sub-Saharan Africa,
                         Mexico)                   Central America)
        │                     │                       │
        ▼                     ▼                       ▼
  High-tech,            Mid-tech,                 Raw materials,
  capital goods,        some industry,            cheap labor,
  finance               exporting to core         exporting to all

  Terms of trade: manufactured goods rise in value faster
                  than commodities = structural disadvantage
```

**Critique**: East Asian success stories (Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) achieved development while integrated into global capitalism — they contradict strong dependency claims. The developmental state model (Evans, Amsden) offers an alternative.

## American Empire

The US case complicates imperial taxonomy:

- No formal colonies (Philippines 1898-1946 is the exception)
- Extensive informal empire: military bases in 70+ countries, economic conditionality through IMF/World Bank, covert interventions (Iran 1953, Guatemala 1954, Chile 1973)
- Ideological anti-colonialism while practicing informal control

Chalmers Johnson (*Blowback*, 2000): US has a "baseworld" — 800+ military installations globally — that functions as imperial infrastructure regardless of formal colonial status.

## Common Confusion Points

**Informal vs. formal empire**: Robinson-Gallagher's key insight is that the two are on a spectrum. The US prefers informal; Britain found it had to formalize repeatedly. The question is always: what mechanism secures the economic relationship?

**Berlin Conference didn't divide Africa**: The conference established rules for the scramble already under way, but drew no territorial lines. The actual territorial division happened through bilateral Anglo-French, Anglo-German, and Franco-German treaties from 1884 to 1914.

**Dependency theory vs. modernization theory**: Modernization theory (Rostow's stages of growth) said all countries would follow the same path as the West given time and capital. Dependency theory said the international capitalist system structurally prevents this. Both are too deterministic; the actual developmental state literature is more empirically grounded.

**Debt trap diplomacy**: The Hambantota Port example (Sri Lanka "leased" port to China after defaulting on BRI loan) is the go-to example. Brautigam's research finds it is more complicated: Sri Lanka strategically offered the lease, and China was reluctant. The debt trap as systematic strategy is not well-evidenced, though the structural dependency effects of BRI financing are real.
