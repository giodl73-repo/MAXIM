## Levitsky and Ziblatt: How Democracies Die

Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt (*How Democracies Die*, 2018) is the central text:

### The Guardrail Concept

```
DEMOCRATIC GUARDRAILS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  FORMAL RULES:              INFORMAL NORMS:
  (written, enforced)        (unwritten, self-enforced)
  ┌────────────────────┐     ┌────────────────────┐
  │ • Constitution     │     │ • Mutual toleration: │
  │ • Election laws    │     │   treat opponents as │
  │ • Term limits      │     │   legitimate, not    │
  │ • Judicial review  │     │   existential threats│
  │ • Free press       │     │                      │
  │   protections      │     │ • Institutional      │
  │ • Anti-corruption  │     │   forbearance:       │
  │   laws             │     │   don't use legal    │
  │                    │     │   powers maximally   │
  │                    │     │   just because you   │
  │                    │     │   can (filling every │
  │                    │     │   court vacancy,     │
  │                    │     │   using filibuster   │
  │                    │     │   on everything)     │
  └────────────────────┘     └────────────────────┘

  KEY INSIGHT: Informal norms matter AS MUCH as
  formal rules. A constitution that nobody respects
  is just paper.
```

### The Authoritarian Playbook

Levitsky-Ziblatt identify a pattern across cases:

```
AUTOCRATIZATION PLAYBOOK (step by step)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STEP 1: WIN ELECTION (legitimacy is essential)
  "The paradox of modern authoritarianism:
   it begins through democratic means"

  STEP 2: CAPTURE THE REFEREES
  • Pack courts with loyalists
  • Appoint loyalists to election commissions,
    anti-corruption agencies, central banks
  • Replace professional civil servants with
    political loyalists

  STEP 3: SIDELINE OR DESTROY OPPONENTS
  • Use legal system selectively against opponents
    (prosecute rivals on corruption charges — often
    real, but selectively enforced)
  • Defund or pressure civil society organizations
  • Use regulatory agencies against critical businesses

  STEP 4: REWRITE THE RULES
  • Change electoral laws to favor incumbents
    (gerrymandering, voter ID, reducing polling places
    in opposition areas)
  • Weaken or eliminate term limits
  • Change constitution to entrench new order

  EACH STEP LOOKS LEGAL: "Unlike the crude
  authoritarians of the 20th century, the would-be
  autocrats of today are skilled politicians.
  They don't destroy democracy — they subvert it,
  step by small step."
```

### The Role of Political Parties as Gatekeepers

Historically, political parties filtered out authoritarian candidates before they could capture democratic politics. Both US parties rejected demagogues internally in 1920s-40s (Father Coughlin, Huey Long). The breakdown of party gatekeeping is key to Trump and Orbán's success.

## Case Studies

### Hungary: Orbán's Blueprint

Orbán's Fidesz won supermajority (2/3) in 2010:

```
HUNGARY AUTOCRATIZATION TIMELINE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  2010: Fidesz wins 2/3 majority → constitutional power
  2011: New constitution passed without opposition input
  2011: Electoral law changed (single-round system, redrew
        constituencies to favor Fidesz)
  2011-13: Constitutional Court stripped of powers;
            packed with Fidesz loyalists
  2013: "Fundamental Law" entrenches Fidesz power
  2014: Media oligarchs aligned with Fidesz acquire
        critical media; state advertising goes only
        to pro-Fidesz outlets
  2016: Soros-funded NGOs targeted (anti-NGO law)
  2017: Central European University forced out of Hungary
  2018: Fidesz retains supermajority — electoral system
        had made this predictable
  2020: Emergency powers during COVID used to rule by decree
  2022: Fourth consecutive supermajority

  EU RESPONSE: Article 7 proceedings (2018);
  Hungary suspended from some EU funding;
  but unanimity requirement in EU means Hungary
  can veto its own sanctions → limited effectiveness
```

Orbán explicitly theorized his project: "illiberal democracy" was his own term (2014 Băile Tușnad speech). Referenced Singapore, China, and Turkey as models.

### Turkey: Erdoğan's Consolidation

Erdoğan's AKP won 2002 on moderate Islamist platform. Gradual consolidation:

- 2007-13: "Ergenekon" and "Balyoz" trials prosecuted secular military officers (genuinely accused of coup plotting, but also served to remove checks on AKP power)
- 2013: Gezi Park protests — crackdown; Erdoğan accuses foreign conspiracies
- 2016: Coup attempt (Gülenist?) → mass purges: 150,000 arrested or fired; state of emergency
- 2017: Constitutional referendum → presidential system; removed parliamentary oversight
- 2023: Won presidential election despite economic crisis (inflation >80%)

### Poland: Backsliding Reversed?

Law and Justice (PiS) won 2015 election:
- Packed Constitutional Tribunal (2015-16)
- Judicial reform that gave parliament control over court appointments
- State media became propaganda arm

EU threatened sanctions; Polish courts resisted. Critical difference from Hungary: **Polish civil society stronger**, courts more independent, opposition more organized.

2023: Opposition coalition won parliamentary election; Tusk PM. Attempted to restore judicial independence — but courts packed by PiS complicated reversal. Demonstration that backsliding can be reversed, but reversal is hard.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is democratic backsliding? | Elected leaders gradually dismantling democratic constraints while maintaining electoral legitimacy — death by a thousand cuts, not coup |
| What are the two informal norms Levitsky-Ziblatt identify? | Mutual toleration (accept opponents as legitimate) + institutional forbearance (don't use every legal power maximally) |
| What is V-Dem's finding on backsliding speed? | Gradual — most cases take 5-10 years to full autocracy. Not sudden, making it harder to recognize in real time |
| Why did Hungary succeed and Poland (partially) fail? | Hungary: Orbán got 2/3 constitutional majority; Polish courts and civil society stronger; EU pressure more effective in Poland |
| What is Mounk's "undemocratic liberalism"? | Technocratic institutions (EU, IMF, central banks) making consequential decisions without democratic accountability — creates populist backlash |
| What keeps democracies alive? | Strong courts, independent media, unified opposition, robust civil society, and crucially: norm-upholding factions within the ruling party |

## Common Confusion Points

**Backsliding is not the same as bad governance**: A country can have poor economic policy or corrupt officials and still have a functioning democracy (Italy, Greece). Backsliding specifically means democratic institutions themselves are being dismantled.

**"Populism" is not the cause**: Populism is a style of politics (people vs. elite); it comes in left (Chávez, Sanders) and right (Trump, Orbán) versions. Populism can be pro-democratic (democratizing established oligarchies) or anti-democratic (using popular mandate to dismantle constraints). The style doesn't determine the outcome.

**Legal cover is the point**: What makes 21st-century autocratization distinctive is that each step can be defended as legal. The Constitutional Tribunal in Hungary was packed legally. Polish judicial reform was passed through parliament legally. This means international observers can't call it a coup; domestic opposition has to fight in courts that are being captured.

**Reversibility**: South Korea 2016, Peru 2000, Romania 2012 show backsliding can be reversed. Poland 2023 shows even partial reversal is difficult once courts are packed. The window for reversal narrows quickly once the judiciary is captured.
