## Bretton Woods Architecture (1944)

The Bretton Woods Conference (July 1944, New Hampshire) designed the postwar international monetary system while WWII was still ongoing. The designers knew what they were doing: prevent the competitive devaluations and protectionism of the 1930s that contributed to the Great Depression and political radicalization.

```
BRETTON WOODS — DESIGNED AGAINST 1930s FAILURES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  1930s FAILURE              BRETTON WOODS SOLUTION
  ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Competitive currency        Fixed exchange rates:
  devaluations (each          all currencies pegged to
  country trying to gain      dollar; dollar pegged to
  export advantage)           gold at $35/oz

  Sudden capital flows        IMF: lender of last resort
  destabilizing balance       for countries with temporary
  of payments crises          BOP deficits — avoids
                              forced deflation

  No development finance      World Bank: long-term
  for reconstruction          capital for reconstruction
  and development             and development

  Trade protectionism         GATT (1947): multilateral
  (Smoot-Hawley tariff        tariff reduction through
  triggered retaliation       negotiating rounds;
  spiral)                     MFN principle

  Germany strangled by        Marshall Plan (1948-52):
  reparations → political     $13 billion grant (not loan)
  radicalization              for European recovery
```

### Bretton Woods Collapse (1971)

The fixed exchange rate system broke down under the strains of US fiscal expansion (Vietnam War + Great Society spending), European economic recovery (eroding US trade surplus), and the growing dollar overhang (more dollars held abroad than US gold could back).

**Nixon Shock (August 1971)**: Nixon unilaterally closed the gold window — no longer would the US exchange gold for dollars at $35/oz. The Bretton Woods system of fixed rates ended.

Result: floating exchange rate system (1973-present). IMF's mandate evolved from managing fixed rates to surveillance and crisis lending.

## Ikenberry's Constitutional Order Theory

G. John Ikenberry (*After Victory: Institutions, Strategic Restraint, and the Rebuilding of Order After Major Wars*, 2001) offers the most sophisticated theoretical account.

**Core thesis**: After major wars, winning states face a "problem of power." They can exploit their dominance opportunistically, but this triggers balancing coalitions. Alternatively, they can lock in their advantages through institutions — self-binding their own future behavior in exchange for other states' acceptance of the order.

```
IKENBERRY'S LOGIC
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  POWER ASYMMETRY          INSTITUTIONAL SOLUTION
  (US dominant 1945)
       │
       ▼
  Options:
  A. Unilateral exploitation → triggers balancing → order fails
  B. Institutional self-binding → other states accept order

  B mechanism:
  • US agrees to rule-based constraints on its own behavior
    (won't use force unilaterally, will operate through UNSC)
  • Other states accept US leadership
  • Order becomes "constitutional" — rights and procedures
    constrain even the dominant power

  WHY IT WORKED:
  • US domestic politics (Congress) actually constrained
    executive, making self-binding credible
  • Shared values: liberal democracies (Western Europe, Japan)
    trusted US more than they'd trust any alternative
  • Information environment: institutions created transparency,
    reducing transaction costs of cooperation

  WHY IT PARTIALLY FAILED:
  • US regularly violated its own rules (Vietnam, Iraq)
  • Non-Western states never fully integrated as equals
  • Economic order served US financial sector disproportionately
```

## NATO as Security Order

NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization, April 1949) is the institutional anchor of the US-European security relationship.

```
NATO — CORE ARCHITECTURE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Article 5: Collective defense —
  "An attack against one is an attack against all"

  WHAT ARTICLE 5 ACTUALLY MEANS:
  "each of them... will assist the Party or Parties so attacked
  by taking... such action as it deems necessary, including
  the use of armed force"
  ↑
  Note: "as it deems necessary" — not automatic war declaration
  Each member decides its own response

  Article 5 invoked ONCE: After 9/11 (2001)

  NATO ENLARGEMENT:
  1949: 12 founding members (US, UK, France, Italy, Belgium,
        Netherlands, Luxembourg, Portugal, Canada, Denmark,
        Iceland, Norway)
  1952: Greece, Turkey
  1955: West Germany (triggers Warsaw Pact)
  1999: Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic (post-Cold War)
  2004: Baltic states, Romania, Bulgaria, Slovakia, Slovenia
  2009: Albania, Croatia
  2017-2024: Montenegro, North Macedonia, Finland, Sweden
             (31-32 members; Finland/Sweden joined after
             Russia's Ukraine invasion)
```

The Russia-NATO-Ukraine relationship is the central security dispute of contemporary Europe. Russia's objection: NATO enlargement into former Soviet space violates a (disputed) 1990 promise not to expand "one inch eastward" (Baker to Gorbachev). Western position: no such binding commitment was made; sovereign states can choose their alliances.

## European Integration as Peace Project

The European project originated from a specific political theory: France and Germany had fought three wars in 70 years (1870, 1914, 1939). Reconciliation through economic integration — starting with coal and steel (the material of war) — would make another war not just undesirable but structurally impossible.

```
EUROPEAN INTEGRATION LOGIC
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  THEORY: Functionalist integration —
  Start with specific economic sectors,
  create shared institutions, build habits of cooperation,
  expand to new areas (spillover)

  TIMELINE:
  1951: ECSC (European Coal and Steel Community)
        France + Germany + Italy + Benelux
  1957: Treaties of Rome → EEC (common market)
  1985: Schengen Agreement (open borders)
  1986: Single European Act (single market program)
  1992: Maastricht Treaty → European Union
  1999: Euro launched (single currency)
  2004: Eastern enlargement (8 post-communist states)
  2016: Brexit referendum → UK leaves (2020)
  2024: 27 member states; EU still expanding (Ukraine candidate)

  WHAT INTEGRATION ACHIEVED:
  • No war between EU member states (unprecedented for Europe)
  • Largest single market in world
  • Soft power: EU membership incentivized democratic reform
    in post-communist and Mediterranean states

  WHAT IT DIDN'T ACHIEVE:
  • Political union: foreign policy still national
  • Fiscal union: eurozone crisis revealed asymmetric adjustment
  • Strategic autonomy: security still depends on NATO/US
```

## The American Century and Hegemonic Stability Theory

Charles Kindleberger (*The World in Depression*, 1973) developed hegemonic stability theory: an open, stable international economy requires a single dominant power to provide public goods — lender of last resort, open markets, stable reserve currency, security guarantees.

When Britain was too weak (post-WWI) and the US was too isolationist, no one provided these public goods → competitive protectionism, currency instability, depression, war.

After 1945, the US provided them all.

```
HEGEMONIC PUBLIC GOODS (Kindleberger/Ikenberry)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  PUBLIC GOOD              US PROVISION            MECHANISM
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Open markets             US market open to       GATT/WTO rules +
                           allies even when        US unilateral
                           they maintained         openness
                           barriers

  Stable reserve           Dollar as global        Bretton Woods →
  currency                 reserve currency        post-1971 petrodollar
                                                   dollar standard

  Lender of last           Dollar swap lines       Federal Reserve acts
  resort                   in crises               globally, not just
                                                   domestically

  Security provision       US military presence    NATO, Pacific alliances,
                           globally; nuclear       carrier battle groups,
                           umbrella for allies     forward basing

  Rules/adjudication       Dispute settlement      WTO DSM, ICSID,
                           mechanisms              ICJ
```

**The burden-sharing problem**: If the US provides these goods regardless of allied behavior, allies can free ride — spend less on defense, maintain trade barriers, challenge US positions, yet still receive security guarantees. The "burden sharing" debate in NATO is this problem.

## The Washington Consensus and Its Discontents

The "Washington Consensus" (John Williamson, 1989) described the standard IMF/World Bank policy package for developing countries:

1. Fiscal discipline (reduce deficits)
2. Reorient public expenditure (away from subsidies toward education, health)
3. Tax reform (broaden base, reduce marginal rates)
4. Interest rate liberalization (market-determined)
5. Competitive exchange rates
6. Trade liberalization
7. Liberalization of foreign direct investment
8. Privatization
9. Deregulation
10. Property rights protection

```
WASHINGTON CONSENSUS — RECORD
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  WHERE IT APPEARED TO WORK:
  • Latin America stabilization (end of hyperinflation,
    1980s-90s) — fiscal discipline was necessary
  • Eastern European transition to market economy

  WHERE IT FAILED:
  • Sub-Saharan Africa: structural adjustment
    conditionality devastated public services,
    increased poverty in many cases
  • East Asia: countries that followed Washington
    Consensus LEAST (Korea, Taiwan — industrial policy,
    capital controls) grew fastest
  • Russia: rapid privatization created oligarchs,
    not efficient markets

  CRITIQUE (Stiglitz, Rodrik):
  • One-size-fits-all ignored institutional prerequisites
  • Sequencing mattered: financial liberalization before
    regulatory capacity = crisis
  • Political economy: policies that worked in theory
    required political conditions rarely present
```

## Fukuyama, Huntington, and the 1990s Debate

**Fukuyama's "End of History"** (1989 article, 1992 book): Liberal democracy and market capitalism have won the ideological competition. History — understood as the struggle between competing visions of the good polity — has ended. What remains is administration, economic development, and perhaps boredom.

**Huntington's "Clash of Civilizations"** (1993 article, 1996 book): The next conflicts will be between civilizational blocs — Western, Confucian, Islamic, Orthodox, Hindu, Latin American, African — not between ideologies or nation-states.

## 2008 Financial Crisis and Order Stress

The 2007-2009 global financial crisis was the first major stress test of the post-Cold War liberal order:

- Originated in the United States (financial deregulation + housing bubble)
- Spread globally through securitized financial instruments and banking contagion
- Exposed the asymmetric costs: Global South suffered from commodity price collapse and credit tightening without having caused the crisis
- IMF/World Bank seen as inadequate; G20 elevated as response forum
- China's stimulus ($586 billion) helped stabilize global demand; Chinese model gained prestige

**Consequence for the order**: The US model of financial deregulation was discredited. Chinese state capitalism seemed to manage the crisis better. "End of history" narrative became untenable.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is the Bretton Woods system? | Fixed exchange rates (currencies pegged to dollar, dollar to gold); IMF for BOP crises; World Bank for development; killed competitive devaluations of 1930s |
| Why did Bretton Woods collapse? | US fiscal expansion (Vietnam + Great Society) + European recovery eroded US surplus + dollar overhang exceeded gold backing → Nixon closed gold window 1971 |

## Common Confusion Points

**Bretton Woods ≠ current system**: Current floating exchange rates are a different system from Bretton Woods. The IMF and World Bank survive, but the core fixed rate mechanism is gone. Dollar remains dominant as reserve currency but for different reasons (network effects, depth of US financial markets, not gold convertibility).

**Marshall Plan scale**: $13.3 billion in 1948 dollars = ~$150 billion in 2020 dollars. Represented ~5% of US GDP at the time. For reference, 2009 US stimulus was ~$800 billion (~5.5% of GDP). Not trivial, but also not the only reason European recovery happened — the European productive base was intact, unlike WWII's Soviet case.

**"Liberal" in liberal order**: "Liberal" means rule-based, open markets, non-coercive great power relations — not socially liberal. Saudi Arabia and Pinochet's Chile were part of the liberal order. The "liberal" is about how great powers relate to each other and the rules of international trade, not about domestic governance.

**Article 5 is not automatic war**: NATO's collective defense clause says members will assist "as they deem necessary." It does not require any specific military response. The credibility of Article 5 depends on reputation and political will, not legal automaticity.
