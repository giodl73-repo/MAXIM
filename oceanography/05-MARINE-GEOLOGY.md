## Ocean Floor Types and Crustal Structure

```
OCEANIC CRUST (two types):

NORMAL OCEANIC CRUST (MORB-type):
  Layer 1: Sediment                    0–3 km thick (increases with age)
  Layer 2A: Pillow basalts / sheeted dikes  ~0.5 km
  Layer 2B: Sheeted dike complex            ~1.5 km
  Layer 3: Gabbro (coarse-grained basalt)   ~5 km
  Total: ~7 km thick
  Vp (seismic velocity): 1.5 → 6.5 → 7.0 km/s (layers 1→2→3)
  Density: ~3000–3100 kg/m³ (denser than continental crust at 2700–2900 kg/m³)

CONTINENTAL CRUST:
  ~35 km thick (up to 70 km under Himalayas)
  Granitic/andesitic composition (lighter, lower density)
  → continental crust CANNOT subduct (too buoyant)
  → only oceanic crust subducts
```

## Mid-Ocean Ridges

```
GLOBAL MID-OCEAN RIDGE SYSTEM:
  ~60,000 km total length — longest mountain chain on Earth
  All tectonic plates being formed at ridges
  Generates ~75 km³ of new ocean crust per year

RIDGE CLASSIFICATION BY SPREADING RATE:
                                        Half-rate    Morphology
  Mid-Atlantic Ridge (MAR)               10–25 mm/yr  SLOW — deep rift valley, rugged
  Juan de Fuca Ridge (Pacific NW)        25–45 mm/yr  INTERMEDIATE — shallow valley
  East Pacific Rise (EPR)                50–80 mm/yr  FAST — smooth dome, no rift valley
  Southwest Indian Ridge                  8–12 mm/yr  ULTRA-SLOW — sparse magmatism

SLOW RIDGE STRUCTURE (Mid-Atlantic):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │        Axial Valley (1–3 km deep, 10–30 km wide)         │
  │  Rift flanks rise 1000–3000 m above valley floor         │
  │  Serpentinized peridotite exposed (mantle at surface)     │
  │  Episodic: long quiescence → magmatic pulse → rapid build│
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

FAST RIDGE STRUCTURE (East Pacific Rise):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Axial Summit Trough (AST): narrow graben, <100 m wide   │
  │  No major rift valley — continuous magma supply           │
  │  Axial magma chamber (AMC): thin melt lens at ~2 km depth│
  │  Smooth, broad dome morphology                            │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Thermal Subsidence — Depth-Age Relationship

```
COOLING OCEANIC PLATE:
  Ridge: hot asthenosphere → plate ~ 0 km depth above sea level? No —
  Ridge crest at ~2500 m depth (already below sea level)

  As plate moves away from ridge:
    Cools conductively, contracts, becomes denser
    Subsides thermally:

  PARSONS-SCLATER LAW:
    d = 2500 + 350 √t     (depth in meters, t in Myr, for young crust)
    Valid for t < ~70 Ma

  Example: crust at 0 Ma → 2500 m, at 9 Ma → 3550 m, at 64 Ma → 5300 m
  (Most abyssal plains: 100+ Ma → ~5000–5500 m depth)

  WHY? Like cooling a metal bar: thermal contraction increases density.
  The √t law comes from the 1D heat diffusion equation (thermal diffusivity κ).
```

## Transform Faults and Fracture Zones

```
RIDGE SEGMENTS OFFSET BY TRANSFORM FAULTS:
  Ridge crest is not a continuous straight line
  Offset by transform faults: perpendicular to ridge, subparallel to spreading
  Active seismicity: ONLY between ridge offsets (plates sliding past each other)
  Beyond the ridge: FRACTURE ZONE (fossil transform, no longer active, no earthquakes)

  WHY: Euler pole for rotation is not the same as the geographic pole.
  The plate boundary must curve to follow small circles around the Euler pole.
  Transforms accommodate the geometric mismatch.

MID-ATLANTIC RIDGE TRANSFORMS:
  Every 50–100 km, ridge is offset
  Fracture zones extend across entire ocean basin (visible in bathymetry)
  Major ones (Romanche, Equatorial, Vema Fracture Zone) breach
  sills allowing AABW to circulate between sub-basins
```

## Subduction Zones and Trenches

```
TRENCH FORMATION:
  Old, cold, dense oceanic crust → dense enough to sink into mantle
  Slab bends → trench forms at bend point
  Arc volcanism: slab dehydrates at ~80–100 km depth
    → water lowers peridotite melting point
    → arc magmas rise → volcanic arc above subduction zone

TRENCH GEOMETRY:
  Mariana Trench (W. Pacific): deepest at ~10,935 m (Challenger Deep)
    → formed by old (~170 Ma) Pacific plate subducting under Mariana arc
  Japan Trench: ~9000 m; 2011 Tohoku earthquake rupture zone
  Peru-Chile Trench: 8065 m; Nazca plate under S. America
  Puerto Rico Trench: 8376 m; deepest Atlantic point

SEDIMENT IN TRENCHES:
  Turbidites accumulate in trench axis
  Subducted sediment can be scraped off → accretionary wedge
  Or entire sediment package subducted → sediment recycling into mantle
  Pacific margins: mostly non-accreting (steep subduction)
  Barbados, Cascadia: large accretionary prisms

HADAL FAUNA (see also 06-DEEP-SEA.md):
  Hadal zone: >6000 m (only in trenches)
  Pressure: 60–110 MPa
  Trench axis acts as sediment trap → food-rich relative to surrounding abyssal plain
```

## Seamounts and Oceanic Islands

```
SEAMOUNT FORMATION:
  Intraplate hotspot: mantle plume penetrates lithosphere
  → Shield volcanism at surface
  → Plate moves over hotspot → chain of extinct volcanic edifices

HOTSPOT CHAINS:
  Hawaiian chain: 5600 km, ~40 seamounts/islands
    Kauai (5.1 Ma) → Oahu (3.7 Ma) → Maui (1.3 Ma) → Big Island (active)
    Rate: ~10 cm/yr → consistent with Pacific plate velocity
    Emperor Seamount Chain: older extension, ~90°bend at 47 Ma
    (bend = plate motion direction change, not hotspot motion)

  Louisville chain (S. Pacific): similar
  Tristan da Cunha, Iceland, Reunion hotspots: current active expressions

SEAMOUNT STATISTICS:
  ~100,000+ seamounts >1000 m tall in global ocean
  Most poorly mapped (large gaps in multibeam coverage)
  Biological hotspots: seamounts concentrate zooplankton, fish, marine mammals

GUYOTS (flat-topped seamounts):
  Volcanic island at surface → wave erosion flattens summit to sea level
  Plate moves → island subsides below sea level → guyot
  Preserved flat top as underwater "fossil island"
  ~30 m/Myr average island subsidence rate in Pacific
```

## Ocean Sediments

### Sediment Types and Distribution

```
SEDIMENT CLASSIFICATION:

BIOGENIC PELAGIC OOZES:
  Calcareous ooze:  CaCO₃ shells (foraminifera, coccolithophores, pteropods)
    Covers ~50% of ocean floor (mainly <4500 m)
    CCD (Carbonate Compensation Depth): ~4000–4500 m in Atlantic, ~3000–4000 m in Pacific
    Below CCD: carbonate dissolves → no calcareous ooze
    → abyssal plains below CCD: red clay or siliceous ooze

  Siliceous ooze: SiO₂ (biogenic opal) from diatoms, radiolaria
    Beneath high-productivity areas: Southern Ocean diatom ooze, tropical Pacific radiolarian ooze
    Siliceous ooze is not affected by CCD (silica doesn't dissolve like carbonate at pressure)

TERRIGENOUS CLAYS:
  Lithogenic material: continental weathering transported by rivers, wind, ice
  Red/brown deep-sea clay: distal, low-productivity areas
  Accumulation rate: 1–5 mm per 1000 years (slowest sediment type)

TURBIDITES:
  Turbidity currents: submarine avalanches of sediment-laden water
  Triggered by: earthquakes, oversteepening, river floods
  Deposit: graded bed (coarse→fine upward = Bouma sequence)
  Accumulation: fast (mm/yr) but episodic
  Distribution: continental margins, canyon fans, trench axes

ICE-RAFTED DEBRIS (IRD):
  Glacially eroded material transported by icebergs
  Scattered coarse particles in fine pelagic sediment → marks ice presence
  Heinrich events identified by IRD layers in N. Atlantic cores
```

### Carbonate Compensation Depth (CCD)

```
CCD MECHANISM:
  CaCO₃ dissolution rate increases with:
    Depth (pressure increases CO₂ solubility → carbonate dissolves)
    Cold temperature (increases CO₂ solubility)
    CO₂ concentration (corrosive water)

  CCD = depth where production rate = dissolution rate
  (net zero carbonate preservation)

  Atlantic CCD: ~4500 m (NADW fresher, less corrosive)
  Pacific CCD: ~3500–4000 m (old water, more CO₂ accumulated → more corrosive)

  Lysocline: depth where dissolution begins (shallower than CCD)
    Individual foram shells start to show dissolution below lysocline
    Saturation horizon (Ω=1): thermodynamic CaCO₃ saturation boundary
    Lysocline ~ saturation horizon in kinetically slow dissolution
    CCD ~ 1000 m below lysocline (where preservation essentially nil)

CLIMATE SIGNAL IN CARBONATE PRESERVATION:
  Ice ages: lower sea level → rivers deliver less freshwater → Atlantic saltier
  → CCD deepens in Atlantic during glacials → more carbonate preserved
  Glacial/interglacial CCD shifts: ~500 m in some basins
  Also: ocean acidification (anthropogenic CO₂) is shoaling the lysocline
```

## Seismic Stratigraphy

```
USING SEISMIC REFLECTION TO READ SEDIMENT ARCHIVES:

  Seismic reflection profiles image sediment layers in cross-section
  Each reflector = density/velocity contrast at layer boundary
  Same physics as radar (EM waves) but using sound waves (P-waves, ~200 Hz)

  TWO-WAY TRAVEL TIME (TWT): time for sound to go down and reflect back up
  Conversion to depth: velocity = 1500 m/s in water, ~1600–2000 m/s in sediment

  SEQUENCE STRATIGRAPHY:
    Packages of sediment bounded by unconformities (erosion surfaces)
    Sea level changes → base level change → progradation/retrogradation sequences
    "Type-1 unconformity" = sequence boundary (major sea level fall)
    Parasequence: shallowing-upward unit (one regression cycle)

  DRILL CORE VALIDATION (DSDP / ODP / IODP):
    Deep Sea Drilling Project 1968–1983
    Ocean Drilling Program 1985–2003
    Integrated Ocean Drilling Program 2003–2013
    International Ocean Discovery Program 2013–present
    Drilled >4000 holes, recovered sediment to >2 km below seafloor
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why are mid-ocean ridges elevated? | Hot asthenosphere upwelling beneath → thermal expansion; subsides as plate cools with √t law |
| Why no deep rift valley on fast-spreading ridges? | Continuous magma supply keeps crust hot and buoyant; slow ridges are cooler, weaker, rifted |
| What is the CCD? | Depth where carbonate production rate = dissolution rate; below this, no calcareous ooze |
| Why is the Pacific CCD shallower than Atlantic? | Pacific deep water older, more CO₂-rich → more acidic → dissolves carbonate faster |
| What is a fracture zone? | Fossil transform fault; seismically inactive zone extending across basin |
| What creates arc volcanoes? | Slab dehydration at ~80–100 km depth releases water → lowers melting point → arc magmatism |
| What is a guyot? | Flat-topped seamount: volcanic island wave-planed then subsided below sea level |
| How is turbidite identified? | Graded bedding (coarse to fine upward) — Bouma sequence |
| What is IRD? | Ice-rafted debris: coarse particles dropped from melting icebergs; marks paleo-ice extent |

## Common Confusion Points

**Oceanic plate age vs. sediment thickness**: Young crust (near ridge) has thin or no sediment. Very old crust (>100 Ma) has 1–3 km of sediment. But below the CCD, even old crust may have no calcareous ooze — just red clay. Age correlates with sediment thickness only broadly.

**Transform fault vs. fracture zone**: Transforms are ACTIVE — seismicity and relative plate motion between ridge segments. Fracture zones are INACTIVE extensions of transforms beyond the ridge tips. Same topographic feature (scarp, valley), completely different seismicity. The 1906 San Francisco earthquake was on a transform fault (San Andreas), not a fracture zone.

**CCD vs. saturation horizon vs. lysocline**: Three separate concepts that are often confused. Saturation horizon (Ω=1) = CaCO₃ neither dissolves nor precipitates. Lysocline = depth where dissolution becomes visible in samples. CCD = depth of zero net preservation. The order is: saturation horizon < lysocline < CCD (increasing depth = more dissolution).

**MORB vs. OIB (ocean island basalt)**: Mid-ocean ridge basalt (MORB) forms from depleted mantle (lithosphere recycled through subduction). Ocean island basalt (OIB) comes from mantle plumes that tap the deeper, undepleted mantle. Different trace element and isotope signatures. Hawaii basalts are OIB; seamounts along a chain progressively change from OIB → becoming more MORB-like as the magmatic system evolves.

**Thermal subsidence vs. isostasy**: Thermal subsidence happens as the plate cools and contracts. Isostasy means the plate floats on the mantle at depth proportional to its density. Both are happening simultaneously — the net result is the √t depth-age relationship. Faster-spreading ridges have the same depth vs. age relationship as slow spreaders; it's about crustal age, not spreading rate.
