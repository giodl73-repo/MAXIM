# Thermohaline Circulation — Deep Water Formation, AMOC/NADW/AABW, Conveyor Belt Timescales

## The Big Picture

```
+============================================================================+
|               GLOBAL THERMOHALINE CIRCULATION (THC)                        |
|         "The Conveyor Belt" — Wally Broecker's 1991 conceptualization      |
+============================================================================+
|                                                                            |
|  NORTH ATLANTIC                                                            |
|  ──────────────                                                            |
|  Gulf Stream carries warm, salty surface water northward                  |
|  → releases heat to atmosphere (Europe warms)                              |
|  → water cools, becomes dense enough to sink                               |
|  → NADW forms (North Atlantic Deep Water)                                  |
|  → flows south along western Atlantic at depth                             |
|                                                                            |
|  SOUTHERN OCEAN                                                            |
|  ─────────────                                                             |
|  Antarctic bottom freezing → brine rejection → AABW                       |
|  AABW is densest water in ocean, fills abyssal basins                      |
|  ACC (Antarctic Circumpolar Current) disperses water globally              |
|                                                                            |
|  UPWELLING & RETURN                                                        |
|  ──────────────────                                                        |
|  Deep water slowly upwells in Indian/Pacific (turbulent diffusion)         |
|  Returns to surface → flows back to North Atlantic → repeat                |
|  Complete cycle: ~1000–2000 years                                          |
|                                                                            |
+============================================================================+
```

```
SIMPLIFIED CONVEYOR SCHEMATIC (Broecker 1991):

  N. Atlantic                                    Indian & Pacific
     SINK                                           UPWELLING
      │  WARM SHALLOW RETURN FLOW ←─────────────────────┐
      │                                                  │
      ▼  NADW / DEEP FLOW ──────────────────────────────►│
      │                                                  │
  [Antarctic]                                           │
   AABW sink                                           │
      └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

Stommel's (1961) two-box model shows the THC has two stable equilibria with a hysteresis loop between them — mathematically a saddle-node bifurcation, physically the same as a Schmitt trigger or any bistable dynamical system. The mechanism: temperature restores quickly (days to months via air-sea heat flux), salinity restores slowly (centuries via the water cycle). This asymmetric restoring rate creates a regime where freshwater forcing can flip the system from the "on" state to the "off" state without the return path crossing the same threshold — hysteresis. The Younger Dryas is the paleoclimate record of this flip being triggered by meltwater, and the current concern is whether GIS melt is pushing the freshwater forcing parameter toward the bifurcation boundary.

## Deep Water Formation — The Two Main Sources

### North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW)

```
FORMATION MECHANISM:

  Gulf Stream + North Atlantic Current bring warm salty water to ~60°N

  Atmospheric cooling in winter:
    Labrador Sea: air temp -20°C, sea temp 6°C → 300 W/m² heat loss
    Norwegian Sea: similar but stronger cooling

  Cooling increases density until water column becomes unstable:
    ∂ρ/∂z becomes positive (denser water on top)
    → deep convection: chimneys 20–50 km wide, plunging to 1000–3000 m

  Labrador Sea Water (LSW):   σθ ~ 27.74–27.80, T~3-4°C, S~34.88
  Northeast Atlantic Deep Water (NEADW): via overflow across Iceland-Scotland Ridge
  Denmark Strait Overflow Water (DSOW): densest overflow, T~0°C

NADW PROPERTIES (composite):
  T = 2–4°C, S = 34.9–35.0 PSU
  Depth range: 1500–4000 m in Atlantic
  Transport: ~17 Sv (Sverdrup) at 26°N
  (1 Sv = 10⁶ m³/s = ~10× total flow of all world's rivers combined)
```

### Antarctic Bottom Water (AABW)

```
FORMATION MECHANISM:

  Sea ice formation in Weddell Sea and Ross Sea

  Salt rejected from growing sea ice stays in liquid below ice
  (sea ice is ~90% fresh — salt cannot fit in ice crystal lattice)

  Dense salty brine sinks along continental shelf
  → mixes with Circumpolar Deep Water (CDW)
  → AABW formed, densest water in ocean

  Weddell Sea Bottom Water (WSBW):
    T < -0.8°C, S ~ 34.65 PSU, σ₄ ~ 46.06 (referenced to 4000 dbar)

  Ross Sea Bottom Water: second major source

AABW PROPERTIES:
  T = -0.4 to 1.5°C (near or below surface freezing point)
  S = 34.64–34.72 PSU
  Spreads through all ocean basins at bottom
  ~40 year formation-to-arrival time in North Atlantic abyssal plain
```

## AMOC — Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation

```
AMOC STRUCTURE (cross-section at 26°N):

  SURFACE LAYER (0–1000 m)
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  NORTHWARD FLOW: ~18 Sv                                    │
  │  Warm (15–25°C), saline (36–37 PSU)                        │
  │  Gulf Stream, Antilles Current, Florida Current            │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  UPPER NADW (1000–3000 m)
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SOUTHWARD FLOW: ~8–10 Sv                                  │
  │  T ≈ 3–4°C, S ≈ 34.95 PSU                                  │
  │  Labrador Sea Water, Overflow Water                        │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  LOWER NADW (3000–5000 m)
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SOUTHWARD FLOW: ~5–7 Sv                                   │
  │  T ≈ 2–3°C, S ≈ 34.9 PSU                                   │
  │  Denmark Strait Overflow Water                             │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ABYSSAL LAYER (>5000 m)
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  NORTHWARD FLOW: ~2–4 Sv                                   │
  │  AABW entering from south                                  │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

NET AMOC TRANSPORT: ~17–18 Sv at 26°N (RAPID array, 2004–present)
```

### AMOC and European Climate

```
HEAT TRANSPORT BY AMOC:
  At 26°N: ~1.3 PW (petawatt = 10¹⁵ W) poleward
  (~25% of total Atlantic meridional heat transport)

  This maintains N. Europe ~5–10°C warmer than equivalent latitudes
  in the North Pacific (London ~51°N = same latitude as Calgary,
  but London avg Jan: 5°C vs Calgary: -7°C).

  NOT entirely the Gulf Stream — also westerly winds, land distribution.
  But AMOC is a significant contributor.

AMOC WEAKENING (ongoing, IPCC concern):
  Freshwater from Greenland Ice Sheet melting → freshens N. Atlantic
  → reduces surface density → reduces sinking → slows AMOC

  RAPID array observations: AMOC declined ~15% since 2004
  (but there is debate — high natural variability makes trend detection hard)

  AMOC collapse (tipping point at ~2°C warming, uncertain):
    Would cool N. Europe 5–10°C, shift ITCZ, disrupt monsoons
    Not imminent but nonzero probability under high-emission scenarios
```

## Water Mass Pathways and Timescales

```
DEEP CIRCULATION PATHWAYS:

North Atlantic:
  NADW formed → flows south → crosses equator → enters Indian Ocean
  → enters Pacific via deep passages → slowly upwells
  → returns as surface/thermocline water → back to N. Atlantic
  TOTAL TRANSIT: ~1000–2000 years (radiocarbon constraints)

Antarctic:
  AABW formed → fills Atlantic abyss from south → fills Indian, Pacific
  → upwells in Southern Ocean and via diapycnal mixing
  TOTAL TRANSIT: ~100–500 years for bottom water renewal

Pacific deep water:
  Pacific is the "oldest" deep water — radiocarbon ages >1500 yr
  No significant deep water formation in N. Pacific
  (N. Pacific is too fresh — precipitation > evaporation)
  Deep Pacific water is NADW + AABW that has aged for millennia
```

```
TRACERS USED TO TRACK WATER MASSES:

  ¹⁴C (radiocarbon): age dating; deep Pacific ~1500 yr old
  ³He, ⁴He:          hydrothermal venting signatures
  CFCs (Freon):       anthropogenic transient tracer from ~1950
                      → maps modern ventilation pathways
  SF₆:               synthetic tracer injections for eddy tracking
  O₂:                oxygen decreases with water age (biological consumption)
  Nutrients:          NO₃, PO₄, Si accumulate with water age
  Salinity:           conservative tracer, set at surface
```

## Convection and Preconditioning

```
DEEP CONVECTION (e.g., Labrador Sea):

  PRECONDITIONING (fall):
    Dome of weakly stratified water (isopycnals cup upward → "chimney region")
    Geographic: cyclonic circulation in subpolar gyre domes isopycnals

  VIOLENT MIXING (winter):
    Strong air-sea heat fluxes (-200 to -400 W/m²)
    Plumes: ~1 km wide, vertical velocities ~5–10 cm/s
    Upward plume: ~0.1 cm/s (1 m/h) entrains stratified water
    Downward plume: ~5 cm/s, penetrates to 1000–3000 m

  RESTRATIFICATION (spring):
    Lateral intrusions of stratified water from eddy field
    Submesoscale eddies (10–50 km) restratify the convective patch
    New Labrador Sea Water spreads laterally into Atlantic

  INTERANNUAL VARIABILITY:
    1988–1994 and 2010–2015 strong convection (NAO+ years)
    Weak NAO → shallow convection → weaker AMOC signal
```

## Feedback Loops and Stability

```
STOMMEL'S THERMOHALINE LOOP MODEL (1961):
  Two-box model: equatorial box vs. polar box
  Shows that THC has two stable equilibria:
    1. "On" state: saline surface water flows poleward, sinks, returns deep
    2. "Off" state: no overturning — completely different climate

  The key: temperature and salinity have opposite effects on density,
  and each restores differently to perturbation.
  Temperature restores fast (air-sea heat fluxes, days-months).
  Salinity restores slowly (water cycle, centuries).

  This asymmetry creates bistability.
  Perturbation with enough freshwater → flip to "off" state
  (Younger Dryas: meltwater pulse from Laurentide Ice Sheet ~12,900 yr BP)

POSITIVE FEEDBACK (freshwater forcing):
  Freshwater input → AMOC slows → less northward salt transport
  → N. Atlantic freshens further → AMOC slows more
  This is the salt-advection feedback (Rahmstorf mechanism)

STABILITY INDICATOR (Stommel fingerprint):
  AMOC strength can be inferred from S difference between Atlantic and Pacific
  Strong AMOC → Atlantic saltier than Pacific (observed: ~1 PSU difference)
  Weakening AMOC → Atlantic-Pacific S contrast shrinks
```

## Paleoclimate — AMOC Switches

```
DANSGAARD-OESCHGER EVENTS (last glacial, 70,000–20,000 BP):
  Rapid warming events in Greenland ice cores: 10–15°C in decades
  Stadial (cold) → Interstadial (warm) transitions
  Mechanism: AMOC OFF → ON switch → North Atlantic heat pump reactivates
  ~25 events in last glaciation, ~1500 yr spacing (debated)

HEINRICH EVENTS:
  Massive iceberg armadas from Laurentide → freshwater flood → AMOC OFF
  → Greenland stadial (cold) → subsequent AMOC restart → rapid warming

YOUNGER DRYAS (12,900–11,700 BP):
  Last deglaciation interrupted by sudden cooling
  Candidate: Lake Agassiz overflow → N. Atlantic freshwater pulse
  → AMOC weakening → 1200 years of cold
  Then rapid (~50 yr) AMOC restart → Holocene warming
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Conveyor belt" is a myth (sort of)**: Broecker's 1991 Science article introduced the conveyor metaphor for public communication. The real THC is not a steady pipe — it's turbulent, eddy-rich, has multiple pathways, and the return flow in the Indian/Pacific involves complex mixing processes over centuries, not a simple surface flow.

**AMOC ≠ Gulf Stream**: The Gulf Stream is driven primarily by wind (subtropical gyre) and continues even if AMOC weakens. AMOC is the thermohaline component of N. Atlantic circulation — the overturning cell. The Gulf Stream transports ~30 Sv; AMOC's thermohaline component is ~17 Sv. They overlap in geography but have different physics.

**Cold = denser is not always true**: At temperatures below ~4°C, freshwater reaches maximum density at 4°C and is LESS dense when cooler. In the deep ocean, this is less relevant because salinity dominates. But in freshwater lakes, cold surface water floats on denser 4°C water — inverse stratification under ice. In the open ocean, where salinity is 35 PSU, density increases monotonically as temperature decreases (no freshwater density maximum effect).

**Sverdrup unit (Sv)**: 1 Sv = 10⁶ m³/s. The entire Amazon River ~0.2 Sv. Total global river discharge ~1 Sv. AMOC at 26°N = ~17 Sv. Just making sure the scale registers: AMOC moves 17× all the world's rivers combined.

**AMOC "collapse" vs. AMOC "weakening"**: The current observed trend is modest weakening (~15% over 60 years per RAPID array). Tipping-point collapse (Stommel bistability) requires crossing a threshold of freshwater forcing that is not yet reached. The two scenarios — gradual weakening vs. abrupt collapse — have very different climate implications.

## Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW)

AAIW is the third major water mass by volume, formed at the Antarctic Convergence (Subantarctic Front, ~50°S) where cold, fresh Antarctic surface water subducts beneath warmer subtropical surface water. It fills the 700–1,000 m depth range throughout the Southern Hemisphere and into parts of the North Atlantic and North Pacific.

```
AAIW PROPERTIES:
  T = 3–7°C, S = 33.8–34.5 PSU (freshest intermediate water)
  Characteristic salinity minimum visible in vertical profiles at ~800 m
  Formation: northward Ekman drift + subduction at Subantarctic Front
  Volume transport: ~10–15 Sv globally

  PATH: forms at surface → subducts at Antarctic Convergence → spreads
  equatorward at ~1000 m depth → identifiable as far as ~20°N in Atlantic
  → key ventilation pathway for O₂ and CO₂ exchange to intermediate depths

WHY IT MATTERS:
  AAIW carries oxygen to mid-depth ocean — limits extent of oxygen minimum zones
  Carries anthropogenic CO₂ to ~1000 m depth (significant portion of ocean uptake)
  Its formation rate responds to Southern Ocean winds and buoyancy fluxes
  → sensitive to Southern Annular Mode variability and climate change
```
