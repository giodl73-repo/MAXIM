# Coastal Systems — Estuaries, Deltas, Barrier Islands, Mangroves, Salt Marshes, Coastal Erosion

## The Big Picture

```
+===========================================================================+
|                  COASTAL ZONE TAXONOMY                                    |
|     Interface between terrestrial, freshwater, and marine systems         |
+===========================================================================+
|                                                                           |
|  ENERGY HIGH                              ENERGY LOW                      |
|  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────                    |
|  Rocky cliffs     Beaches      Barriers    Estuaries    Wetlands          |
|  Sea stacks       Dunes        Lagoons     Deltas       Mangroves         |

|  platforms        transport    islands                  Peatlands         |

|  TIDAL RANGE:                                                             |
|  Microtidal: <2 m  → wave-dominated (beaches, barriers)                  |

|  Macrotidal: >4 m  → tide-dominated (estuaries, tidal flats, channels)   |
|                                                                           |
+===========================================================================+
```

---

Estuary mixing is a two-fluid stratified flow problem from fluid dynamics: the salt wedge is a stable density-stratified two-layer flow (heavier salt water below lighter fresh water), with the Richardson number determining whether the interface is stable or goes turbulent. The transition from salt wedge → partially mixed → well-mixed estuary maps directly to increasing Reynolds/Richardson regime mixing. The sediment budget (inputs − outputs = net accumulation) is a mass balance equation — the same conservation-of-mass framework used in chemical engineering process design. The Bruun rule (shoreline retreat = SLR × [profile width / vertical range]) is a geometric scaling law derived by assuming the active profile shape is conserved: it's a first-order dimensional analysis result that predicts ~50–100 m of shoreline retreat per meter of sea level rise.

## Estuaries — Mixing Zones

```
DEFINITION: Semi-enclosed coastal body where freshwater from rivers
  meets and mixes with seawater from the ocean

PHYSICAL CLASSIFICATION:
  Salt wedge estuary (high river flow, low tidal mixing):
    River water slides over denser seawater
    Sharp halocline separates two layers
    Example: Mississippi River mouth
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  FRESHWATER (S=0)  ───────────────────  │
    │ ─────────────────────────────────────── │  sea
    │     SALTWATER (S=35) ──────────────► │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Partially mixed estuary (moderate tidal mixing):
    Turbulent mixing at the salt-fresh interface
    Vertical gradient present but diffuse
    Example: Chesapeake Bay, Delaware Bay

  Well-mixed estuary (strong tidal mixing, small river flow):
    Vertically uniform salinity at each cross-section
    Salinity increases seaward
    Example: Thames Estuary

  Fjord (glacially deepened, sill):
    Deep water trapped behind shallow sill
    Very stratified, limited renewal of deep water
    Anoxia in deep fjord basins (Black fjords of Norway)
    Example: Norwegian fjords, Chilean fjords, BC fjords

ESTUARINE CIRCULATION:
  Even in well-mixed estuary, net two-layer circulation driven by density gradient:
    Net seaward near surface (freshwater input)
    Net landward at bottom (entrained salt water)
  This drives NUTRIENT PUMP: brings marine nutrients into estuary
```

## Estuarine Mixing and the Turbidity Maximum

```
TURBIDITY MAXIMUM:
  Zone of maximum suspended sediment concentration near the limit of saltwater intrusion
  MECHANISM:
    Estuarine circulation:
      Bottom inflowing seawater → carries fine sediment landward
      Surface outflowing freshwater → carries suspended particles seaward
    These converge at the estuary head → sediment concentrates
    Flocculation: clay particles aggregate in the presence of electrolytes (salt)
    → larger aggregates settle faster → sediment trap

  Turbidity maximum: [SSC] can reach 10,000 mg/L (vs. 10–100 mg/L in open estuary)
  Important for:
    Benthic deposition rates (estuarine sediment archive)
    Nutrient cycling (large surface area of fine particles)
    Fisheries (spawning and nursery habitat in turbid estuaries)

RESIDENCE TIME:
  Time for complete water exchange in estuary
  Critical for: pollution management, nutrient dynamics, recovery time
  Range: days (small, tide-flushed) to months (large fjord)
  Chesapeake Bay: ~1 year mean residence → slow recovery from nutrient loading
```

## Deltas

```
DELTA FORMATION:
  River brings sediment to coast → deposits where velocity drops → delta

  DELTA CLASSIFICATION (Galloway triangle):

                     FLUVIAL DOMINATED
                           │
                   Mississippi
                    (birdfoot)
               /                   \
    WAVE DOMINATED                TIDE DOMINATED
    Nile, São Francisco           Ganges-Brahmaputra
    (cuspate, arcuate)            (funnel-shaped)

  FLUVIAL DOMINATED (low wave/tidal energy):
    Distributary channels extend into sea (Mississippi birdfoot)
    Rapid progradation seaward
    Unstable — channels avulse when gradient advantage switches

  WAVE DOMINATED (high wave energy):
    Waves rework river mouth → smooth arcuate shoreline
    Longshore drift distributes sediment
    Example: Nile Delta

  TIDE DOMINATED (large tidal range):
    Tidal currents remobilize sediment → finger-like sand ridges
    Example: Ganges-Brahmaputra, Irrawaddy (Myanmar)

DELTA SUBSIDENCE:
  Natural: compaction of sediment + tectonic subsidence
  Anthropogenic: groundwater extraction, reduced sediment supply (upstream dams)
  Mississippi Delta: sinking 1–2 cm/yr (natural) + reduced sediment supply (dams trap ~90%)
  → Louisiana land loss: ~2600 km² since 1930
  Mekong, Nile, Yangtze deltas: all losing area rapidly
  Bangladesh: at extreme risk — >10 million people on floodplain at sea level
```

## Barrier Islands and Barrier Coasts

```
BARRIER ISLAND FORMATION:
  Multiple hypotheses (likely all contribute):
  1. Longshore drift building spits that detach
  2. Coastal dune submergence during sea level rise
  3. Offshore bar migration onshore
  4. Wave-built shoals at river mouths

  STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS:
  Ocean → Surf zone → Beach berm → Foredune → Swale → Backdune → Marsh → Lagoon → Mainland

  US East Coast barriers: 295 islands, 3000 km, from Maine to Texas
  Outer Banks (NC): classic elongated barriers, inlet migration
  Florida Keys: biogenic (coral/limestone), not classic clastic barrier
  Padre Island (TX): longest barrier island in the world, ~182 km

BARRIER ISLAND DYNAMICS:
  ROLLOVER MIGRATION:
    During storms, waves overwash barrier crest
    Sediment deposited on back side → island migrates LANDWARD over time
    Rate: 1–5 m/yr typical; up to 20 m/yr during active storm periods

  INLETS:
    Storm breaches create new inlets
    Natural inlet migration: ~1–5 m/yr lateral drift
    Tidal prism controls inlet stability: large tidal prism → inlet stays open
    Small tidal prism → inlet fills with longshore sediment

  BARRIER ISLAND VULNERABILITY:
    Fixed barriers (seawalls, buildings) → stop natural rollover
    → Island "drowns" — cannot migrate fast enough
    Sea level rise + developed barriers = long-term disappearance
    Many small uninhabited barriers: will migrate and survive
    Developed barriers (Miami Beach, Ocean City): major future adaptation challenge
```

## Mangroves

```
DISTRIBUTION:
  Tropical/subtropical coasts (25°N–25°S approximately)
  ~146,000 km² globally (2020 estimate; down from ~200,000 km² in 1980)
  Three independent origins: Indo-West Pacific (~60 species), W. Africa (~9 species),
  New World (~12 species)
  Highest diversity: Malay Archipelago (Indo-Pacific center)

PHYSIOLOGICAL ADAPTATIONS TO INTERTIDAL:
  Salinity:
    Salt exclusion: roots act as reverse osmosis membranes (Rhizophora)
    Salt secretion: glands on leaves excrete salt (Avicennia, Aegiceras)
    Salt accumulation: concentrate salt in old leaves then shed them

  Anoxic sediment:
    Pneumatophores: vertical breathing roots protruding above sediment (Avicennia)
    Prop roots / stilt roots: aerial root systems (Rhizophora)
    Aerenchyma: air channels in roots deliver O₂ to buried tissues

  Vivipary (Rhizophora):
    Seeds germinate ON the parent tree (propagule)
    When dropped: arrow-like propagule drives into sediment
    Can also float → long-distance dispersal via ocean currents

ECOSYSTEM SERVICES:
  Carbon sequestration: 6–8 tonnes C/ha/yr (among highest of any ecosystem)
    "Blue carbon" — mangrove peat builds up over centuries to meters thick
  Storm protection: buffer 50–90% of wave energy; reduce storm surge
  Fisheries nursery: 50–80% of tropical fisheries spend juvenile stage in mangroves
  Shoreline stabilization: prop roots trap sediment, build land
  Loss rate: ~2% per year historically → largely due to shrimp aquaculture,
    urbanization, wood harvest for charcoal
```

## Salt Marshes

```
DISTRIBUTION: Temperate estuaries and protected coasts (higher latitudes than mangroves)
  Northern North America, Europe (NW coast), temperate S. Hemisphere
  ~55,000 km² globally

VEGETATION ZONATION:
  UPLAND
  ─────────────────────── High marsh (flooded only by spring tides)
  Spartina patens (cordgrass), Salicornia (glasswort), Juncus
  ─────────────────────── Mid marsh (flooded ~50% of tides)
  Spartina alterniflora (dominant N. American), Puccinellia (Europe)
  ─────────────────────── Low marsh (flooded most tides)
  Bare mud, algal mat, Spartina alterniflora (tall form)
  ─────────────────────── Tidal flat (always submerged except low spring tide)
  SUBTIDAL

MARSH ACCRETION:
  Marsh must keep pace with sea level rise to survive
  Accretion mechanisms:
    Inorganic: suspended sediment deposited during flood tides
    Organic: belowground root/rhizome production (dominant in mature marshes)
  Rate: 2–10 mm/yr
  Threshold: if SLR > accretion rate → marsh drowns → open water
  Historical SLR (0.3–1.5 mm/yr): marshes keeping pace
  Projected SLR (3–10 mm/yr): many marshes will drown by 2100

BLUE CARBON:
  Anoxic waterlogged soil: slow decomposition → organic carbon accumulates
  Peat depths: up to 4 m in old marshes (centuries of accumulation)
  Carbon density: 2–5 times more C/area than terrestrial forests
  Drainage or disturbance → rapid aerobic decomposition → CO₂ release

ECOSYSTEM SERVICES:
  Filter nutrients (N, P) from agricultural runoff → coastal water quality
  Fisheries habitat: ~75% of Atlantic coast commercial fish use marsh
  Wave attenuation: up to 72% reduction in wave height over 100 m of marsh
  Shoreline accretion: trap sediment → coast building
```

## Coastal Erosion

### Processes and Controls

```
EROSION DRIVERS:
  Waves: wave energy ∝ H² (wave height squared)
    Storm waves: 10–20 m heights during severe events
    Persistent swell: slowly erodes cliffs at sea level
  Currents: longshore currents transport sediment
  Sea level rise: shifts erosion zone landward
  Storminess: increased storminess → more erosion events
  Sediment supply: reduced by dams, seawalls → sediment-starved coast

COASTAL EROSION TYPES:
  Rocky coast erosion:
    Hydraulic action: wave compression in cracks → rock fracture
    Abrasion: sand/gravel carried by waves sandpapers rock face
    Solution: carbonate rocks (chalk, limestone) dissolve in seawater
    Slumping: wave erosion undercuts base → mass failure of cliff

  Sandy coast erosion:
    Beach sediment budget: sources (rivers, cliffs) minus sinks (offshore, longshore)
    Seasonal: summer (calm) → accretion; winter (storms) → erosion
    LONG TERM erosion: net loss if sinks > sources

SHORELINE SEDIMENT BUDGET:
  INPUTS:
    River sediment → longshore drift
    Cliff erosion → beach sand supply
    Dune supply (blowouts)
    Biogenic production (shells, coral fragments)

  OUTPUTS:
    Offshore transport (below wave base, permanent loss)
    Longshore drift out of coastal cell
    Overwash to back-barrier
    Dune building
    Sand trapping by engineering structures (jetties, groins)
```

## Coastal Engineering and Management

```
HARD ENGINEERING:
  Seawall: vertical or curved concrete barrier
    Protection: immediate
    Drawback: wave scour at base accelerates beach erosion
    Adjacent beaches: "flanking" — sediment bypasses seawall, adjacent shores erode faster
    "End effect": soft coast next to seawall erodes rapidly at the interface

  Groin: structure perpendicular to shore, traps longshore drift sediment
    Builds beach updrift, starves beach downdrift
    Groin field: multiple groins → each starves the next

  Jetty: protects harbor entrance, maintains navigable inlet
    Same side effects as groins on longshore transport

  Breakwater: offshore parallel structure, reduces wave energy behind
    Tombolo formation: sand builds up behind breakwater, connects to shore

SOFT ENGINEERING / MANAGED RETREAT:
  Beach nourishment: dredge offshore sand, pump to beach
    Expensive ($5–50M per km), temporary (~5 yr lifespan)
    Can buy time for managed adaptation
  Living shorelines: restoration of marshes, reefs, oyster beds
    Self-maintaining, accrete with sea level, protect shore
  Managed retreat: abandon vulnerable areas, allow natural landward migration
    Politically difficult, economically complex, but often most cost-effective

SETBACK LINE MANAGEMENT:
  Bruun rule: beach erodes landward by R = L × (Δh / (B + h))
    R = shoreline retreat, L = active profile width,
    Δh = sea level rise, B = berm height, h = closure depth
    Simplified: ~50–100× horizontal retreat per 1 m of SLR
    (1 m SLR → typical beach retreats 50–100 m landward)
  Used to set coastal construction setback lines (e.g., 30-year or 60-year setback)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Estuary classification is not strict**: Many estuaries are intermediate or change character seasonally. Chesapeake Bay is partially mixed in spring (high river discharge) but nearly vertically homogeneous in summer droughts. Same estuary, different regimes at different times.

**Delta vs. estuary**: Deltas form where river deposition outpaces sea level rise and erosion (river dominates). Estuaries form where sea level rise inundates the river valley (sea dominates). Drowned river valleys → estuaries (Chesapeake, Thames). Prograding river mouths → deltas (Nile, Mississippi).

**Barrier islands vs. spits**: Barrier islands are separated from the mainland by a lagoon. Spits are attached to the mainland at one end. Both form by longshore drift but have different morphology and connectivity.

**Seawall erosion paradox**: Seawalls protect the property immediately behind them but accelerate erosion of the beach in front and the coast on either side. If everyone builds seawalls, beaches disappear (terminal groyne effect). The coast can't migrate landward → beach drowned between seawall and rising sea level.

**Mangrove carbon is conditional**: Mangroves are among the highest carbon-storing ecosystems per unit area. BUT: destroying mangroves releases the stored peat carbon RAPIDLY (aerobic decomposition in drained soil). A cleared mangrove plantation that grows shrimp for 20 years may emit more CO₂ than it ever sequestered. The carbon stock is a debt: the longer the mangrove stood, the larger the penalty for clearing it.

## Coral Reef Geomorphology

Coral reefs are coastal structures built by biological calcification. Darwin's (1842) subsidence theory correctly predicted the fringing → barrier reef → atoll progression before confirmation by drilling.

```
REEF TYPE PROGRESSION (Darwin's Subsidence Theory):

  FRINGING REEF:
    Coral grows directly adjacent to shoreline
    No significant lagoon; reef flat exposed at low tide
    Young volcanic island, low subsidence rate
    Example: Hawaii nearshore reefs, Red Sea fringing reefs

  BARRIER REEF:
    Island has subsided; lagoon separates reef from shore
    Lagoon 5–75 m deep, 10–100 km wide
    Example: Great Barrier Reef (2300 km), Belize Barrier Reef (300 km)
    Darwin predicted: same island that once had fringing reef, now subsided

  ATOLL:
    Volcanic island fully submerged; only reef ring remains
    Lagoon enclosed (or mostly enclosed) by reef
    Low, flat coral islands (motu) on reef crest at most
    Example: Maldives, Marshall Islands, Tuamotus
    Confirmed by Bikini Atoll drilling (1952): basalt basement at depth
    below thick coral carbonate → confirmed Darwin's prediction

NET CARBONATE BUDGET:
  Reef growth = calcification - dissolution - physical erosion (bioerosion + abrasion)

  HEALTHY REEF: net accretion ~1–10 mm/yr
    Calcification: corals, coralline algae, mollusks
    Bioerosion: parrotfish (scrape ~0.1 mm/yr from hard substrate),
      sea urchins, sponges, boring mollusks, boring worms
    Physical: wave abrasion, storm breakage

  DEGRADED REEF: net erosion
    Bleaching → coral death → bioerosion exceeds calcification
    Acidification reduces calcification rates
    "Carbonate budget" modeled at reef scale to predict reef future

  REEF ACCRETION RATE vs. SEA LEVEL RISE:
    Maximum accretion rate: ~10 mm/yr (healthy, warm, shallow)
    Current SLR: ~4 mm/yr and accelerating
    Many reefs: accretion 1–3 mm/yr → falling behind SLR already
    Degraded reefs: net erosion → no longer growing toward sea level
    → Reef islands (atolls) lose structural foundation

HOLOCENE REEF RECORDS:
  Reef growth since last glacial maximum (~20 ka BP):
  Sea level rose ~120 m in ~10,000 years (~12 mm/yr average)
  Reefs "kept up" during some phases, "drowned" during rapid SLR pulses
  Paleoclimate record shows reefs are sensitive sea level change indicators
```
