## Surface Gravity Wave Mechanics

### Dispersion Relation

For linear surface gravity waves on water of depth h:

```
ω² = gk tanh(kh)

where:
  ω = angular frequency (rad/s)
  k = wavenumber = 2π/λ (rad/m)
  g = 9.81 m/s²
  h = water depth (m)

This is the fundamental dispersion relation — waves are DISPERSIVE
(different frequencies travel at different speeds).
```

### Two Limiting Cases

```
DEEP WATER (kh >> 1, i.e., h >> λ/4π):
  tanh(kh) → 1
  ω² = gk
  Phase speed: c_p = ω/k = √(g/k) = √(gλ/2π)
  Group speed: c_g = dω/dk = c_p/2  ← HALF of phase speed

  Example: λ = 100 m → c_p = √(9.81×100/2π) ≈ 12.5 m/s (27 knots)

SHALLOW WATER (kh << 1, i.e., h << λ/4π):
  tanh(kh) → kh
  ω² = gk²h
  Phase speed: c_p = √(gh)
  Group speed: c_g = c_p  ← NON-DISPERSIVE (all frequencies same speed)

  Example: h = 4000 m (open ocean) → c_p = √(9.81×4000) ≈ 200 m/s
  (This is why tsunamis are fast: λ >> depth → shallow-water waves)
```

### Wave Energy and Group Velocity

```
ENERGY DENSITY:
  E = (1/2)ρg a²   [J/m²]

  where a = amplitude, ρ = water density

  For a 2 m amplitude wave: E ≈ ½ × 1025 × 9.81 × 4 ≈ 20,000 J/m²

ENERGY PROPAGATION:
  Energy travels at GROUP VELOCITY c_g, not phase velocity c_p

  For deep-water waves: c_g = c_p/2
    → Individual crests move THROUGH the wave group
    → Watch a wave group from above: crests appear at back, travel forward,
       vanish at front — like a "Mexican wave" at a stadium
    → Energy envelope travels at half the crest speed

WAVE TRAINS AND SWELL:
  Wind generates broadband spectrum of waves (many frequencies)
  Dispersion separates them: longer wavelengths travel faster
  → Distant swell arrives as nearly monochromatic waves
     (long waves arrived first, short ones are still en route)
  Surfers track Southern Ocean storms by swell period:
    20 s period swell → arrived from far away → long deep-ocean swell
    8 s period → local or regional generation
```

## Wave Breaking and Surf Zone

```
DEEP WATER WAVE SHOALING:

  As waves approach shallow water (h decreasing):
    c_p ∝ √h → slows down
    Energy conservation: E × c_g = const (no dissipation)
    As c_g decreases, E must increase → amplitude increases (shoaling)

  Wave steepness: ε = a/λ
    Breaking occurs when ε ~ 1/7 (empirical criterion)
    or when wave height ≥ 0.78 × water depth (shallow water)

BREAKER TYPES:
  Spilling breaker:   gentle slope, small ε → foam from crest downward
  Plunging breaker:   steeper slope → overturning crest ("barrel")
  Surging breaker:    very steep slope → wave runs up without breaking

SURF ZONE DYNAMICS:
  Breaking waves → radiation stress gradient → drives longshore current
  (current parallel to beach, in direction of wave approach)
  Longshore current → longshore sediment transport → coastal morphology
```

For full treatment see 01-OCEAN-PHYSICS.md. Key addition here:

INTERNAL SOLITARY WAVES (solitons):
  Nonlinear internal waves generated by tides over topography
  Single large-amplitude (10–200 m) disturbances
  Wavelengths 1–10 km, speeds 0.5–2 m/s
  Visible in SAR satellite imagery as surface roughness modulation
  Strong turbulence when they break on shelves
  South China Sea internal waves among the largest known (~200 m amplitude)

## Tsunamis

```
TSUNAMI PHYSICS:

  Seismic source (usually subduction zone megathrust) displaces seafloor
  → vertical displacement of water column
  → Very long wavelength wave (100–1000 km) compared to ocean depth
  → SHALLOW WATER WAVE regardless of ocean depth

  Open ocean:
    λ ~ 500 km, h ~ 4 km
    c = √(gh) = √(9.81 × 4000) ≈ 200 m/s ≈ 720 km/h (jet airliner speed)
    Amplitude: ~0.1–1 m (barely detectable at sea surface)
    Period: 10 min to 2 hr

  Approaching shore:
    Depth decreases → c decreases → wave slows and amplifies
    Shoaling: amplitude ∝ h^(-1/4)
    Runup: can reach 30–40 m above sea level (2011 Tohoku: 40 m in places)

  DART BUOY NETWORK:
    Bottom pressure sensors detect tsunami (~1 Pa change at 6000 m depth)
    Transmit to surface buoy → satellite → warning centers
    Warning time: 0 min (local) to ~8 hr (trans-Pacific)
```

## Tides — Equilibrium Theory

### Tidal Forcing

```
TIDAL FORCE = differential gravitational pull

  Moon's gravity on near-side ocean > gravity at Earth center > far-side ocean
  Net: two tidal bulges — one toward Moon, one away

  LUNAR TIDAL FORCE MAGNITUDE:
    F_tidal ∝ M_Moon × R_Earth / D³
    Maximum: ~1.1×10⁻⁷ g (11 millionths of Earth gravity)
    Small, but acts over the entire ocean → resonance amplification

  SUN'S CONTRIBUTION:
    Sun is ~390× farther away, 27 million× more massive
    F_sun / F_moon ≈ 0.46  (Sun's tidal force ~46% of Moon's)
    → Spring tides (Moon+Sun aligned): ≈ 1.46× lunar alone
    → Neap tides (Moon-Sun at 90°): ≈ 0.54× lunar alone
```

### Tidal Constituents (Harmonic Analysis)

```
TIDAL HARMONIC DECOMPOSITION:
  Real tidal signal = sum of discrete sinusoidal constituents

  PRINCIPAL CONSTITUENTS:
  Symbol  Period      Description
  ──────  ──────      ───────────
  M2      12.42 hr    Principal lunar semi-diurnal (largest globally)
  S2      12.00 hr    Principal solar semi-diurnal
  N2      12.66 hr    Larger lunar elliptic semi-diurnal
  K1      23.93 hr    Lunisolar diurnal
  O1      25.82 hr    Principal lunar diurnal
  P1      24.07 hr    Principal solar diurnal
  M4      6.21 hr     Shallow-water harmonic (1/4 diurnal)

  Spring tide: M2 + S2 in phase (every ~14.77 days)
  Perigean spring tide: M2 amplified when Moon at perigee

  The ~37 major constituents account for >99% of tidal variance at any station.
```

## Dynamic Tides — Real Ocean Response

The equilibrium tide (two bulges following the Moon) is almost entirely wrong for the actual ocean because:
1. Ocean basins have boundaries (continents)
2. Basin dimensions create resonance
3. Earth rotation (Coriolis) deflects tidal flows

```
TIDAL RESONANCE:
  If ocean basin length L = (2n-1) × quarter wavelength of M2:
    c = √(gh), T = 12.42 hr → λ = c × T
    h = 4000 m → c = 200 m/s → λ = 200 × 44,712 s = 8,942 km
    Quarter wavelength = 2,235 km

  Bay of Fundy: L ≈ 270 km, h ≈ 80 m → T_resonance ≈ 13.3 hr (close to M2!)
    → Fundy tides: up to 16 m range (highest on Earth)

  North Sea: L ≈ 600 km, h ≈ 60 m → resonance near M2 → 5–7 m tides
```

## Amphidromic Points

```
AMPHIDROMIC SYSTEM:

  Coriolis effect + basin geometry → tidal wave becomes a ROTATING WAVE
  (Kelvin wave rotating around a basin)

  AMPHIDROMIC POINT: node where tidal amplitude = zero
  Cotidal lines radiate outward from amphidromic point
  Co-range lines form closed curves around amphidromic point

  In Northern Hemisphere:
    Tidal wave rotates COUNTER-CLOCKWISE around amphidromic point
    (Coriolis deflects to the right → Kelvin wave hugs right-hand coast)

  In Southern Hemisphere: rotates CLOCKWISE

  North Atlantic M2 tidal system:
    ~3 amphidromic points
    Tidal range: 0 m at amphidromic point, up to 8+ m at resonant coasts

ASCII MAP OF N. ATLANTIC TIDAL SYSTEM (schematic):
                                     High tidal range: English Channel, Bay of Fundy
                      ↗ cotidal line
    ·Amphidromic point  ← near-zero range at center
  ↙↗ cotidal lines rotate counterclockwise
                                     ↘ cotidal line toward S. America
```

## Tidal Power and Dissipation

```
TOTAL TIDAL ENERGY DISSIPATION: ~3.7 TW

WHERE IT GOES:
  ~2.5 TW  Deep-sea internal tides (wave generation over ridge topography)
  ~0.7 TW  Shallow-water dissipation (bottom friction, turbulence)
  ~0.5 TW  Internal seiches in enclosed seas

  Deep-ocean internal tide mixing (from ~2.5 TW):
    This drives ~half of the diapycnal mixing required to maintain
    the thermohaline circulation — critical link!
    Without tidal mixing, deep water wouldn't upwell efficiently.

TIDAL BRAKING:
  Tidal dissipation transfers angular momentum from Earth's rotation to Moon
  → Earth's rotation slowing: +1.4 ms/century to day length
  → Moon receding: +3.8 cm/yr from Earth
  (Measured by lunar laser ranging since 1969 Apollo retroreflectors)
```

## Kelvin Waves and Coastal Trapping

```
KELVIN WAVE:
  Rotating wave that propagates along a coastline or equator
  Cannot exist in open ocean — requires boundary to prevent Coriolis deflection

  COASTAL KELVIN WAVE:
    Propagates with coast on its RIGHT (N. Hemisphere)
    Decays exponentially away from coast with e-folding scale = Rossby radius
    Phase speed: c = √(gh) (same as shallow-water gravity wave)
    Key role: tidal propagation along coasts, wind-driven coastal surges

  EQUATORIAL KELVIN WAVE:
    Propagates eastward along equator (equator acts as "waveguide")
    Period: weeks to months
    Phase speed: ~2.8 m/s for baroclinic mode
    KEY ROLE IN EL NIÑO:
      Anomalous westerly wind burst in central Pacific
      → downwelling equatorial Kelvin wave propagates east at ~2.8 m/s
      → reaches South American coast in ~2 months
      → depresses thermocline off Peru
      → warm water anomaly → El Niño SST signal
```

## Storm Surge

```
STORM SURGE MECHANISM:
  Two effects:
  1. Inverse barometer: 1 hPa pressure drop → ~1 cm sea level rise
     Major hurricane: central pressure ~940 hPa = 70 hPa drop → ~70 cm surge
  2. Wind stress: sustained onshore winds pile water against coast
     η = τ × L / (ρ g h)
     where τ = wind stress, L = fetch, h = water depth
     Can produce 3–6 m surges in shallow shelf seas

  CATASTROPHIC SURGES:
    Bangladesh 1970 Bhola cyclone: ~6 m surge, ~500,000 deaths
    Hurricane Katrina 2005: ~8–9 m surge over low-lying New Orleans
    2013 Typhoon Hainan (Haiyan): ~6 m surge, central Philippines

  STORM SURGE vs. TSUNAMI:
    Both are long waves → shallow-water wave physics
    Storm surge: slow rise over hours (synoptic timescale forcing)
    Tsunami: arrive as discrete waves, period 10 min–1 hr
    Storm surge can be superimposed on astronomical tide → compound event
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Wavelength vs. period**: Swell is characterized by its PERIOD (not wavelength) because period is conserved during propagation while wavelength changes as depth changes. A 15-second swell in deep water has λ~350 m; as it shoals, λ shrinks while T stays at 15 s.

**Phase velocity vs. group velocity**: In deep water, the phase velocity (crest speed) is TWICE the group velocity (energy propagation). Watch a wave group: crests appear at the rear, move forward through the group, disappear at the front. The group itself moves at half the crest speed. This is what you see when you throw a rock in a pond — the ring of crests expands faster than the ring of energy.

**Semi-diurnal vs. diurnal tides**: Some locations have two high tides per day (semi-diurnal — UK, US East Coast), others one per day (diurnal — Gulf of Mexico, SE Asia), others mixed. Explained by location relative to amphidromic systems and latitude (diurnal constituents K1, O1 depend strongly on latitude). The M2 (semi-diurnal) dominates globally, but basin-specific resonance and amphidromic node placement can make diurnal constituents dominant locally.

**Tidal range and tidal amplitude**: Range is the height difference between high and low water (= 2 × amplitude). Bay of Fundy range of 16 m → amplitude of 8 m. Bay of Fundy tides are extreme due to near-resonance, NOT because the Bay is close to the Moon.

**Storm surge ≠ wave height**: Storm surge is the quasi-static sea level elevation. Waves ride on top of the surge. Total water level during a storm = tide + surge + wave setup + individual wave height. Katrina's 8 m storm surge still had 10 m waves on top in some areas.
