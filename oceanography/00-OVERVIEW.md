MORPHOLOGICAL FEATURES:
  Continental shelf    <200 m, <0.1° slope — geologically part of continent
  Continental slope    200–2000 m, 1–4° slope, cut by submarine canyons
  Continental rise     sediment apron at base of slope (turbidite lobes)
  Abyssal plain        flattest terrain on Earth, 4–6 km depth
  Mid-ocean ridge      60,000 km global chain, seafloor spreading center
  Trench               subduction margins, deepest zones up to 11 km
  Seamount             isolated volcanic peak >1000 m above seafloor
  Guyot                flat-topped seamount (wave-planed when at surface)
  Fracture zone        transform fault scar offsetting ridge segments

## Computational and Engineering Bridges

The ocean is a geophysical fluid dynamics problem on a rotating sphere. If you know Navier-Stokes, the governing equations are the same — plus salinity as a second scalar, plus buoyancy-driven convection from thermohaline forcing. Geostrophic balance (Coriolis ≈ pressure gradient) is the large-scale force balance, analogous to the hydrostatic approximation in atmospheric dynamics. Stratification is simply a stable density gradient resisting vertical mixing — the same stability analysis as in control theory (Brunt-Väisälä frequency is the natural frequency of the linearized perturbation).

Signal processing maps directly: sonar is active acoustic sensing (matched filtering, Doppler processing for current velocity via ADCP), the SOFAR channel is a waveguide (acoustic normal modes), and ocean noise spectra are analyzed with the same FFT/spectral estimation tools as any engineering signal. Thermodynamics drives stratification: the T-S diagram is a phase diagram for seawater, with density as the state variable. Heat transport is solved as a diffusion-advection PDE. The thermohaline circulation is buoyancy-driven convection at planetary scale — the same physics as a convective heat exchanger, but the fluid is saltwater and the domain is the global ocean basin.

## Common Confusion Points

**Ocean vs. sea**: Seas are smaller, partially enclosed by land (Mediterranean, South China Sea). The five oceans are open bodies. The distinction matters legally (UNCLOS exclusive economic zones) and oceanographically (limited exchange affects salinity and circulation).

**Salinity units — PSU vs. ppt vs. g/kg**: PSU (Practical Salinity Unit) is dimensionally unitless — it's a ratio derived from conductivity. Writing "35 PSU" is conventional. Modern practice sometimes writes just "35" or uses the Absolute Salinity scale in g/kg (TEOS-10). Don't confuse with ppm (mg/kg): 35 PSU ≈ 35,000 ppm.

**In situ temperature vs. potential temperature**: In situ temperature increases adiabatically under pressure as you go deeper. Potential temperature (θ) corrects for adiabatic compression — it's the temperature a water parcel would have if raised to the surface without heat exchange. Always use θ for water mass identification and T-S diagrams.

**Epipelagic ≠ photic zone exactly**: The photic (euphotic) zone is defined optically (~1% of surface irradiance penetrating). In clear open ocean water this extends to ~200 m, matching the epipelagic boundary. But in turbid coastal water it may be only 20 m. Same depth cutoff, different physical definitions.

**Pacific vs. Atlantic deep water formation**: The North Pacific has NO equivalent of NADW formation. The Pacific is wider, receives more precipitation relative to evaporation (lower salinity), and the sill geometry differs. Deep water forms primarily in the North Atlantic and around Antarctica (AABW). The North Pacific is a water mass "dead end" — it ages and accumulates nutrient load without being refreshed from above.

**Upwelling productivity paradox**: The most productive ocean regions (eastern boundary upwelling systems — California, Benguela, Peru-Humboldt, Canary, Somalia) are found adjacent to deserts, not tropical forests. Cold, nutrient-rich water from depth supports massive phytoplankton blooms and anchovy-sardine-tuna food chains.
