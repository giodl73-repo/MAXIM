# Silesia & the Central European Borderlands

## Why This Region Is the CK2 Heartland

Central European micro-territories — Silesia, Pomerania, Brandenburg, Lusatia, Meissen, Thuringia, the Palatinates, the Alpine lands — are where feudal mechanics play out in maximum density. Every rule about inheritance, sub-infeudation, title transfer, dynastic extinction, and Habsburg accumulation has its best example here.

```
CENTRAL EUROPEAN BORDERLANDS c. 1200:

  POMERANIA         POLAND
  (coastal duchy) ←──────────────+
       |                         |
  NORDMARK → MARGRAVIATE     SILESIA
  OF BRANDENBURG               (Piast dukes)
       |                         |
  LAUSITZ             MEISSEN ←──+
  (Lusatia)          (Wettin)
       |
  THURINGIA (Landgrave)
       |
  FRANCONIA ← BAMBERG, WÜRZBURG (bishoprics)
       |
  PALATINATE ←──────────────────────────────+
  (Rhine + Upper — separated!)              |
       |                                    |
  BAVARIA ← TYROL ← CARINTHIA ← STYRIA ← AUSTRIA
                                         (Habsburg core)
       |
  CARNIOLA → MORAVIA → BOHEMIA
  (all will be Habsburg)
```

## Silesia — The Territory That Changed Hands Six Times

Silesia is the perfect case study in how a territory's ownership traces through dynastic history.

```
SILESIA OWNERSHIP TIMELINE:
  ~966: Part of Piast Poland (Mieszko I)
  1138: Poland fragments; Silesia becomes semi-independent Piast duchy
  1241: Mongol invasion devastates Silesia (Battle of Legnica)
  1335: Treaty of Trentschin: Bohemia gains suzerainty over Silesia
        (Piast dukes become vassals of King of Bohemia)
  1526: Bohemia → Habsburgs (Ferdinand I elected King of Bohemia)
        Silesia becomes Habsburg territory
  1742: Frederick the Great of Prussia seizes Silesia
        War of Austrian Succession; Maria Theresa keeps rest but loses Silesia
  1945: Potsdam Conference; Silesia given to Poland; Germans expelled
        → Current state

THE PIAST PROBLEM (1138):
  Bolesław III Wrymouth divided Poland among his four sons
  (gavelkind inheritance applied to a kingdom — catastrophic)
  → Poland fragmented into multiple duchies
  → Each Piast branch: Silesia, Greater Poland, Masovia, Kuyavia, etc.
  → Silesian Piasts became increasingly germanized (German colonists)
  → They felt more attachment to Bohemia than to Poland by 1300

MONGOL IMPACT (1241):
  Two Mongol columns invaded
  Henry II of Silesia ("the Pious") assembled Polish-German-Teutonic force
  Battle of Legnica (Liegnitz) April 9, 1241:
    Henry II killed; Mongols won
    But Mongols withdrew (Great Khan Ögedei died — succession crisis)
    Silesia spared occupation but not devastation
  → Depopulation enabled massive German colonization (Ostsiedlung)
  → Silesian towns founded/refounded under German Magdeburg law
```

## Pomerania — The Coastal Fragmentation

Pomerania (from Slavic "Po more" — by the sea) was the Baltic coastal territory between the German-settled west and Polish-settled east.

```
POMERANIAN DYNASTIES:
  House of Pomerania (Greifen/Griffins): ruled Pomerania from ~1170
  Constantly subdivided:
    Pomerania-Stettin
    Pomerania-Wolgast
    Pomerania-Rügen (with island of Rügen, important Baltic port)

ACQUISITION BY BRANDENBURG:
  Hohenzollern Margrave of Brandenburg: coveted Pomerania for decades
  Treaty of Pyritz 1493: mutual inheritance rights established
  Ducal line kept dying out and then continuing...
  Finally: Last Pomeranian duke Bogislaw XIV died 1637
  → Brandenburg claimed Pomerania per the treaty
  Problem: Sweden had occupied most of Pomerania during 30 Years War
  Westphalia 1648: Western Pomerania → Sweden; Eastern Pomerania → Brandenburg
  Sweden's Pomerania: held until 1815 (Napoleonic reshuffling)
  → Prussia gets all of Pomerania by 1815
```

## Brandenburg — From Frontier to Kingdom

The most important of the HRE's eastern territories for modern European history.

```
NORDMARK (Northern March):
  Created by Otto I ~960 as a frontier against Slavic tribes
  Initial territory: modern Berlin area + surroundings
  Markgraf (Margrave) = commander of the northern frontier

ASCANIAN DYNASTY:
  Albert the Bear (r. 1134–1170): first Margrave of Brandenburg
  Conquered the Havelland from the Slavic Hevelli tribe
  Germans colonized the territory → German character established
  Ascanians also held: Anhalt, Saxony-Anhalt
  Brandenburg became electoral (Golden Bull 1356): Margrave of Brandenburg
  is an Elector

CRISIS: Ascanian line dies out 1320
  → Brandenburg passed through: Wittelsbach (1324-1373),
    Luxemburg (1373-1415 — under Emperor Sigismund)

HOHENZOLLERN ACQUISITION (1415):
  Emperor Sigismund: needs money (always) and loyalty
  Frederick I of Hohenzollern (Burgrave of Nuremberg) pays 400,000 gold guilden
  and provides crucial service at Constance (Council 1414-1418)
  → Frederick I becomes Elector of Brandenburg
  THIS IS THE FOUNDATION of the eventual Prussian kingdom

HOHENZOLLERN PROGRESSION:
  Elector of Brandenburg (1415)
  + Duke of Prussia (1618 — Polish fief, later independent)
  = Brandenburg-Prussia under Frederick William the Great Elector (1640-1688)
  + Kingdom of Prussia (1701 — Frederick I crowned at Königsberg)
  → Frederick the Great (1740-1786) — dominant European power
```

## Lusatia (Lausitz)

Two territories: Upper Lusatia and Lower Lusatia, inhabited by Sorbs (West Slavic people).

```
OWNERSHIP HISTORY:
  Early medieval: Sorbian tribes
  ~960s: Otto I conquers; becomes HRE borderland
  1018: Treaty of Bautzen: Bolesław I of Poland gets it
  1031: Back to HRE (German)
  1158: Emperor Frederick Barbarossa — reorganized
  ~1300: Under Bohemian Crown lands (along with Silesia, Moravia)
  1526: → Habsburg (with Bohemia)
  1635: Treaty of Prague — Lusatia → Electorate of Saxony
  1815: Congress of Vienna — divided: most to Prussia, part to Saxony
  Sorbian minority exists in modern Germany (Brandenburg/Saxony)
  The point: every border zone had this sort of back-and-forth ownership
```

## Meissen and the Wettin Dynasty

Meissen (the March of Meissen) is a key HRE micro-territory — and its ruling dynasty, the Wettins, became one of the most prolific in European history.

```
MARCH OF MEISSEN:
  Created ~960 by Otto I as eastern frontier command
  Egbert the One-Eyed: early margrave
  Captured by Poland under Bolesław Chrobry 1002; recovered 1031

WETTIN DYNASTY:
  Acquired Meissen 1089
  Over time accumulated: Meissen + Thuringia + Saxony
  Ernest (1464): divided the lands between two sons
  → ERNESTINE line: got Electoral Saxony (Wittenberg)
  → ALBERTINE line: got Meissen and other lands

  CRUCIAL SPLIT:
  Ernestine Wettins: Electoral Saxony → supported Luther!
  Frederick III "the Wise" (Ernestine) protected Luther at Wartburg
  Albertine Wettins: eventually got Electoral dignity 1547
  (after Schmalkaldic War — Protestant princes lost)

  LATER DISPERSAL:
  Wettin branches spread across Europe:
  Saxe-Coburg and Gotha → became the British royal family (1917: renamed Windsor)
  Kingdom of Belgium (Leopold I, 1831)
  Kingdom of Portugal (House of Braganza-Saxe-Coburg)
  Kingdom of Bulgaria (1887)
  → One dynasty branching into half of European monarchies
```

## Thuringia and the Wartburg

The Thuringian Landgraviate was one of the most valuable central German territories.

```
LANDGRAVIATES OF THURINGIA:
  Landgrave = count with authority over the "land" broadly
  Ludowingian dynasty: ruled 1131–1247
  Famous for: Wartburg Castle (near Eisenach)
    → St. Elizabeth of Hungary (lived here; canonized 1235)
    → Martin Luther hid here 1521-1522 (translated New Testament)
    → Wagner set Tannhäuser here
    → German nationalist symbol (Wartburg Festival 1817)

  Landgraviate of Thuringia → Wettin (1247, inherited by Wettin
    through Jutta of Thuringia's marriage)
  → Eventually becomes part of Saxony-Weimar, Saxony-Gotha, etc.
  The fragmentation here is extreme: Thuringia split into
  ~8 sub-duchies and landgraviates by the 16th century
```

## The Rhine Palatinate and Upper Palatinate — The Split Problem

This is the most confusing aspect of Palatinate geography.

```
PALATINATE = TWO SEPARATED TERRITORIES:
  Rhine Palatinate (Kurpfalz):
    On the Rhine near Heidelberg
    This is where the Count Palatine lived
    Contains: Heidelberg (university 1386 — oldest in HRE)
    Electoral dignity: this territory's count was an Elector

  Upper Palatinate (Oberpfalz):
    Far to the southeast, near Bavaria/Bohemia border
    (modern NE Bavaria, Amberg area)
    Same count ruled both — but they weren't adjacent

WHY IT'S SPLIT:
  The Wittelsbachs held both Bavaria AND the Palatinate
  1329: Treaty of Pavia split the Wittelsbach lands
  Rhineland Palatinate: to Rudolph's line
  Bavaria: to Ludwig's line
  Upper Palatinate: split between both — complicated further
  The separation of the Rhine and Upper Palatinates was permanent
  until 1777 when the Wittelsbach lines reunited

30 YEARS WAR CONSEQUENCE:
  Elector Palatine Frederick V accepted Bohemian crown 1619
  → Triggered 30 Years War
  → Frederick V lost both: Bohemia + his Electoral dignity
  → Electoral dignity transferred to Bavaria (Catholic Wittelsbach)
  → Frederick V became "the Winter King" (ruled Bohemia one winter)
  → His wife Elizabeth Stuart (daughter of James I of England)
     → their granddaughter Sophia of Hanover
     → became the Protestant heir to British throne
     → Act of Settlement 1701: her son became George I of Britain
```

## The Alpine Territories — Habsburg Title Accumulation

The Alpine lands are where Habsburg power was built, piece by piece.

```
AUSTRIA (Osterreich — "Eastern Reich"):
  Babenberg dynasty: rulers of Austria from ~976
  Babenbergs gained: Austria proper + Styria
  Last Babenberg: Frederick II "the Warlike" died 1246 (no heirs)
  Interregnum: Ottokar II of Bohemia occupied Austria 1251

  RUDOLF OF HABSBURG (elected emperor 1273):
  Declared Ottokar's occupation invalid
  Battle of Marchfeld/Dürnkrut 1278: Rudolf defeats and kills Ottokar II
  → Austria granted to Rudolf's sons
  → FOUNDATION of Habsburg Austria

STYRIA (Steiermark):
  Originally: Babenberg fief; passed with Austria to Habsburgs

CARINTHIA (Kärnten):
  Originally: separate duchy; passed to Habsburgs 1335

CARNIOLA (Krain):
  Slovenian core; into Habsburg hands 1335 with Carinthia

TYROL (Tirol):
  Alpine territory controlling key passes (Brenner Pass)
  County of Tyrol: Habsburg 1363 (Margaret "Maultasch" died without heirs)
  Critical: Brenner Pass = main route between Germany and Italy
  Whoever holds Tyrol controls HRE-Italy communication

VORARLBERG:
  Westernmost Austrian territory
  Gradually acquired in pieces; completed ~1375

HABSBURG RESULT BY 1386:
  Austria + Styria + Carinthia + Carniola + Tyrol + Vorarlberg
  = "Hereditary lands" (Erblande) — the Habsburg core
  Then: Burgundian Netherlands 1477, Bohemia 1526, Hungary 1526,
        Spain + New World 1516 → Charles V's empire
```

## The Teutonic Order in Prussia — A Military-Ecclesiastical State

```
TEUTONIC KNIGHTS:
  Military religious order founded during Third Crusade (Acre, 1190)
  Initially: field hospital → military order → landholders in Holy Land
  Failed in Holy Land; invited to:
    Transylvania (Hungary) 1211 — expelled 1225 (became too powerful)
    Prussia 1226 (by Konrad I of Masovia — wanted help vs pagan Prussians)

CONQUEST OF PRUSSIA (1230–1283):
  Brutal systematic conquest of Baltic Prussians (non-Slavic Baltic tribe)
  Converted by the sword; survivors assimilated or enslaved
  German colonists settled the Baltic coast
  Cities: Königsberg (1255), Danzig, Thorn, Marienburg

STATE OF THE TEUTONIC ORDER:
  The "Ordensstaat" — unique feudal entity
  Not a dynasty (Order = celibate knights)
  Ruled by Grand Master
  Headquarters: Malbork Castle (Marienburg) — largest brick castle in world

DECLINE:
  Battle of Grunwald (Tannenberg) 1410:
    Polish-Lithuanian-Tatar army defeats Teutonic Order
    Grand Master Ulrich von Jungingen killed
    Order never fully recovers
  Second Peace of Thorn 1466:
    West Prussia (including Danzig) → Poland
    East Prussia (incl. Königsberg): Teutonic Order continues
      but as Polish vassal
  1525: Grand Master Albert of Brandenburg-Ansbach (Hohenzollern!)
    Converts to Lutheranism; dissolves the Order in Prussia
    Becomes: secular Duchy of Prussia under Polish suzerainty
    → Eventually merges with Brandenburg to create Brandenburg-Prussia
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Silesia" is both a region and multiple polities**
At various points "Silesia" refers to: a single Piast duchy, multiple competing Piast duchies, a collection of Bohemian fiefs, a Habsburg crownland, a Prussian province. Context determines meaning.

**The HRE's "circles" cut across these micro-territories**
The 1512 imperial circles grouped territories for administrative purposes. Many of these micro-territories ended up in one circle while their ruler also held land in another. Cross-circle holdings were normal.

**Wettins ruling both Protestant AND Catholic territories**
After the Reformation split, Wettin branches ended up on both sides. Ernest Wettins (Ernestine) supported Luther; Albert Wettins (Albertine) eventually shifted. One family, two confessions, decades of potential war.

**"Palatine" doesn't always mean the Palatinate**
"Palatine" rights could be granted elsewhere. The Bishop of Durham had palatine jurisdiction in England (County Palatine of Durham). Chester was a county palatine. "Palatine" = quasi-royal jurisdictional privilege, not necessarily connected to the Rhine Palatinate.
