## China's Dynastic Cycle — The Conceptual Framework

### The Mandate of Heaven (Tianming 天命)

The foundational Chinese political concept — and the legitimation mechanism for every dynasty change.

```
THE MANDATE OF HEAVEN:
  Heaven (Tian) sanctions a ruler/dynasty
  Signs of Heaven's favor: good harvests, peace, floods controlled,
    borders secure, people content
  Signs of Heaven's withdrawal:
    Natural disasters (floods, droughts, famines, earthquakes)
    Military defeats
    Popular rebellions
    → These prove the dynasty has lost the Mandate
    → Rebellion becomes not just legal but mandatory
    → The successful rebel PROVES he has the Mandate (circular, but powerful)

  WHO HOLDS IT: the emperor (Son of Heaven — Tianzi)
  THE PROBLEM: only one Son of Heaven → multiple claimants = civil war
               China has no elective mechanism; succession disputes + weakness
               → periodic collapse → new dynasty

DYNASTIC CYCLE (simplified):
  Founding:     Strong founder; conquers; reforms; capable government
       ↓
  Consolidation: Second/third rulers; administrative refinement
       ↓
  Peak:          Cultural + economic flowering; expansion
       ↓
  Decay:         Weak emperors; court factions; eunuch power;
                 land concentration → dispossession of peasants
                 → revenue decline → military weakness
       ↓
  Crisis:        Natural disasters + military failures + rebellions
       ↓
  Collapse:      Warlords compete; dynasty falls
       ↓
  Interregnum:   Multiple competing states (worst period)
       ↓
  New dynasty:   Strongest claimant wins; claims Mandate; cycle restarts
```

## Tang Dynasty (618–907) — China's Cosmopolitan Peak

```
TANG FOUNDED: Li Yuan (Emperor Gaozu) overthrew Sui Dynasty
  Li Shimin (Emperor Taizong, r. 626–649): the great consolidator
    Xuanwu Gate Incident: killed his brothers; forced father to abdicate
    → Celebrated as China's greatest emperor anyway
    Capable: Confucian governance; military expansion; ruled by discussion
    Territorial: Tang at peak controlled: China + Central Asia to the Pamirs
                 + Korea (briefly) + Vietnam (north) + Tibet (contested)

COSMOPOLITAN TANG:
  Chang'an (Xi'an): capital; ~1 million population; most international city
  Merchants, monks, envoys from: Arabia, Persia, India, Japan, Korea,
    Southeast Asia, Central Asian Silk Road states
  Nestorian Christianity, Zoroastrianism, Islam, Manichaeism:
    all had temples in Chang'an
  Tang court dress: absorbed Central Asian fashions
  Music: Central Asian instruments, rhythms
  Tang poetry: Li Bai, Du Fu — peak of classical Chinese poetry

EMPRESS WU ZETIAN (r. 690–705):
  Only woman to rule China as emperor in her own name and title
  Capable administrator; expanded civil service examination system
  Ruthless: executed critics and rivals including her own family members
  Legitimized her rule partly by patronizing Buddhism

TANG COLLAPSE:
  An Lushan Rebellion 755–763: devastating civil war
  Dynasty survived but permanently weakened
  Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms 907–960: fragmentation
```

### Song Dynasty (960–1279)

```
NORTHERN SONG (960–1127):
  Wang Anshi reforms (1069–1076): attempted fiscal reform
    New Laws: low-interest government loans, militia service
    vs. old landowner-scholar gentry interests
    Classic reform-vs-conservative factional battle
  Paper money: Song China invented it (jiaozi, then huizi)
  Naval power: Song had the largest navy in the world
  Gunpowder weapons: bombs, fire arrows, early rockets in military use
  Printing: moveable type (Bi Sheng ~1040) — 400 years before Gutenberg

LOSS OF NORTH (1127):
  Jurchen Jin Dynasty invaded; captured Emperor Huizong + son Qinzong
  → "Jingkang Incident" — humiliation comparable to Hattin
  → Southern Song: court fled south; capital at Hangzhou (Lin'an)

SOUTHERN SONG (1127–1279):
  Economically sophisticated; one of the world's richest societies
  Rice cultivation revolution; commercial economy; urban middle class
  Hangzhou: ~2 million population (Marco Polo: "greatest city in the world")
  Neo-Confucianism: Zhu Xi systematized Confucian philosophy
  Faced Mongols: most expensive conquest Kublai Khan undertook
  Battle of Yamen 1279: last Song fleet defeated; last emperor drowned
  → Yuan Dynasty (Mongol) rules all China
```

## Yuan Dynasty (1271–1368) and Ming Restoration

```
YUAN CHINA:
  See 11-MONGOL-EMPIRE.md for full Yuan coverage
  Relevant point: Yuan collapse set pattern for Ming

MING DYNASTY (1368–1644):
  Founded by: Zhu Yuanzhang (Hongwu Emperor) — peasant origin, Buddhist monk
  REACTION AGAINST MONGOLS: closed borders; reduced foreign contact
  Rebuilt the Great Wall (most of what survives is Ming-era)
  Capital: Nanjing initially → Beijing (Yongle Emperor moved it 1421)
           Forbidden City built 1406–1420 (900+ buildings; still standing)

ZHENG HE'S VOYAGES (1405–1433):
  Yongle Emperor: sent eunuch admiral Zheng He on seven voyages
  Fleet: 300+ ships; "treasure ships" up to 130m long
  Reached: Southeast Asia, India, Persian Gulf, East Africa (Kenya coast)
  Purpose: diplomatic tribute collection; demonstrate Ming greatness
  THEN: Xuande Emperor's court turned inward; Confucian scholars
        opposed "wasteful" voyages; fleet burned/left to rot
  → China withdrew from maritime expansion at the exact moment
    Europe was beginning its Age of Exploration
  Counterfactual: if China had continued, the world might be very different

MING DECLINE:
  Wanli Emperor (r. 1572–1620): withdrew from governance entirely
  Eunuch factions; court paralysis
  Wokou (pirates/smugglers): Japanese-origin pirates + Chinese participants
  Manchu threat: Nurhaci unified Jurchen tribes from ~1599
  Li Zicheng: peasant rebel took Beijing 1644
  Ming Emperor Chongzhen hanged himself in the Forbidden City
  Manchu Qing: exploited the chaos; entered China
  → Qing Dynasty (1644–1912)
```

## Japan — From Imperial Court to Shogunate

### The Imperial System and Its Erosion

```
JAPANESE EMPEROR SYSTEM:
  Tennō (天皇) = Heavenly Sovereign
  Divine origin: descended from sun goddess Amaterasu
  Never dethroned (the same dynasty for 1,400+ years — possibly oldest
  continuous dynasty in the world)
  BUT: largely ceremonial after ~800 CE; real power held elsewhere

NARA PERIOD (710–794): capital at Nara
  Buddhism imported from China via Korea
  Chinese administrative model adopted
  Kojiki (712) + Nihon Shoki (720): written histories establishing
    imperial mythology

HEIAN PERIOD (794–1185): capital at Heian-kyō (Kyoto)
  Most culturally refined period of Japanese history
  THE COURT ARISTOCRACY: art, poetry, romance
  The Tale of Genji (Murasaki Shikibu, ~1000): first psychological novel
    in world literature; written by a court lady
  Pillow Book (Sei Shōnagon, ~1000): brilliant observations of court life

FUJIWARA REGENCY:
  Fujiwara clan dominated court politics
  Method: marry daughters to emperors; become regent (sesshō) for minor emperor
    or kampaku (chancellor) for adult emperor
  Fujiwara no Michinaga (r. effective 995–1027): peak
    "This world is mine, entire — as the full moon fills the sky,
    without the slightest lack" — his poem (not modest)
  INSEI (cloistered rule):
    Emperors abdicated to become "retired emperors" (jōkō)
    Retired emperor held more freedom than actual emperor
    → "Cloistered government" (insei) from ~1086
    → Counter to Fujiwara regency
```

### Samurai Rise and the Kamakura Shogunate (1185–1333)

```
SAMURAI ORIGINS:
  Provincial warrior class developed to:
  - Protect estates (shōen — private tax-exempt estates)
  - Fight for provincial governors
  - Extend aristocratic families' interests beyond Kyoto
  Two dominant samurai clans: TAIRA (Heike) and MINAMOTO (Genji)

GENPEI WAR (1180–1185):
  Taira Kiyomori: effective ruler; based in western Japan (Kyoto)
  Minamoto no Yoritomo: exiled; rebuilt in eastern Japan (Kanto)
  Yoritomo's brother Yoshitsune: military genius; won the battles
  Battle of Dan-no-ura 1185 (naval): Taira annihilated
    → Child emperor Antoku drowned with the Taira (Imperial Regalia — the
       sword — lost with him)

YORITOMO'S INNOVATION:
  Did NOT go to Kyoto or seek court titles in the normal way
  Instead: stayed at Kamakura (eastern Japan)
  Created parallel government:
    Samurai-dokoro: Board of retainers
    Monchujo: Board of inquiry (legal)
    Mandokoro: administrative board
  1192: Emperor granted title Sei-i Taishōgun
    ("Commander for the Suppression of Barbarians")
    → SHOGUN: military dictator of Japan; emperor keeps ceremonies

BAKUFU (shogunate = "tent government"):
  Yorimoto's system: shugo (military governors per province) +
                     jitō (estate stewards collecting taxes)
  → Parallel governance to Kyoto court; in practice superseded it

HOJO REGENCY (1199–1333):
  Yoritomo died; his sons were weak
  Hojo Masako (his wife, "nun shogun"): maneuvered effectively
  Hojo family became "regents to the shogun" — 3rd layer of power
  Emperor → (ceremonial) Shogun → Hojo Regent (real power)
  Japan had: an emperor, a shogun, and a regent all simultaneously
```

### Mongol Invasions (1274, 1281)

```
KUBLAI KHAN'S INVASIONS OF JAPAN:
  First invasion 1274: ~30,000 troops; reached Hakata Bay (Kyushu)
    Repelled partly by storms, partly by Japanese samurai
  Second invasion 1281: ~140,000 troops (largest overseas invasion in
    medieval history)
    Japanese coastal defenses had been built; held off the fleet
    KAMIKAZE: both fleets destroyed by typhoons
    "Divine Wind" (kamikaze): credited as divine protection
    → Invincibility myth + samurai over-confidence
    → Also: horde of samurai expected individual rewards for fighting
            Defending isn't glamorous; who do you reward for typhoon help?
            Bakufu financial crisis from reward demands → instability
```

### Ashikaga Shogunate and Sengoku (1336–1615)

```
KENMU RESTORATION + ITS FAILURE:
  Emperor Go-Daigo: restored imperial power 1333 (fell to discontented
    samurai under Ashikaga Takauji)
  Takauji: defeated Go-Daigo; established Ashikaga Shogunate 1336
  Go-Daigo fled: set up Southern Court (Yoshino)
  Northern Court: Ashikaga-backed rival emperor in Kyoto
  Nanbokuchō period (1336–1392): two courts simultaneously
  Resolved 1392: Southern Court submitted; Northern Court lineage continues

ASHIKAGA SHOGUNATE (1336–1573):
  Never as centralized as Kamakura
  Yoshimitsu (r. 1368–1394): peak; built Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)
    Officially submitted to Ming China as "King of Japan" to get trade
  Ōnin War (1467–1477): succession dispute → decade of warfare in Kyoto itself
    → Triggered the Sengoku (Warring States) period

SENGOKU JIDAI (1467–1615):
  Central authority collapsed; daimyo (domain lords) competed for survival
  KEY DAIMYO:
    Oda Nobunaga: used firearms (acquired from Portuguese 1543) systematically
      Battle of Nagashino 1575: massed arquebusiers defeated cavalry
      Unified most of central Japan; killed in coup by Akechi Mitsuhide 1582
    Toyotomi Hideyoshi: Nobunaga's successor; completed unification 1590
      Peasant origin → achieved highest power; social mobility peak
      Invaded Korea twice (1592–1598): failed; fought Joseon + Ming
    Tokugawa Ieyasu: patient operator; Battle of Sekigahara 1600
      → Tokugawa Shogunate (Edo Period 1603–1868) — 265 years of peace
      Han system: ~250 domains (han); daimyo controlled locally
      Sankin-kōtai: daimyo required to alternate between Edo and domain
                    → families kept in Edo as hostages; kept daimyo poor
      SAKOKU: "closed country" policy; no Japanese could leave; no foreigners
              except Dutch (at Dejima, Nagasaki) + Chinese + Koreans
```

## Korea — Three Kingdoms to Joseon

```
THREE KINGDOMS (c. 57 BCE – 668 CE):
  Goguryeo (north): militarized; resisted Tang China repeatedly
  Baekje (southwest): close ties to Japan; Buddhism to Japan via here
  Silla (southeast): allied with Tang; unified Korea 668

UNIFIED SILLA (668–935):
  Capital: Gyeongju; Buddhist culture; tributary to Tang
  Hwaeom (Avatamsaka) Buddhism; spectacular temples (Bulguksa)
  Collapsed: bone rank system rigidity; revolts

GORYEO (918–1392):
  Wang Geon founded it; "Goryeo" → "Korea" in English
  Buddhist state: Tripitaka Koreana (~80,000 carved woodblocks of
    Buddhist canon — the world's most complete; still intact)
  Civil service exam system (less rigid class restriction than Silla)
  Mongol invasion: 1231, 1232, 1235, 1247, 1253 — repeated invasions
    Goryeo court retreated to Ganghwa Island
    1259: Goryeo submitted as Mongol tributary
    Mongol brides for Goryeo kings; Yuan influence deep
    Anti-Mongol coup 1356 (King Gongmin): threw off Yuan

JOSEON DYNASTY (1392–1910):
  Yi Seonggye: general who overthrew last Goryeo king
  NEO-CONFUCIANISM: adopted as official ideology (replacing Buddhism)
    Strictly hierarchical; status by education + birth
    Four classes: scholars (yangban) → farmers → artisans → merchants
    (+ "outside": slaves + performers + shamans)

  SEJONG THE GREAT (r. 1418–1450):
    Created HANGUL (Korean alphabet) 1443
    Designed for phonetic representation of Korean sounds
    One of the most scientific, practical alphabets ever designed
    Purpose: literacy for commoners (classical Chinese too hard)
    Also: astronomical instruments, rain gauge, music notation, calendar
    Japanese invasions 1592–1598 (Imjin War):
    Toyotomi Hideyoshi's Korean campaigns devastated the peninsula
    Admiral Yi Sun-sin: turtle ships (ironclad?); destroyed Japanese supply fleets
    China (Ming) sent armies to help Joseon; Japan eventually withdrew
    → Korea shattered; recovery took generations
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Shogun ≠ Emperor**
The Emperor was a religious/ceremonial figurehead; the Shogun was the real military and administrative ruler. This dual-power structure (with actual power often wielded by a third party — the regent) was a consistent Japanese pattern for ~700 years.

**Goryeo ≠ modern Korea (geographically)**
Goryeo controlled roughly the same territory as modern Korea, but the concept of "Korea" as a fixed territorial entity is modern. Goguryeo had extended into modern Manchuria; Goryeo was more compact.

**Song China wasn't "weak" — it was differently focused**
Song is often characterized as weak because it couldn't hold the north against Jurchen Jin. But Southern Song (1127–1279) survived for 150 years against the most aggressive military force in Asian history, had the world's most advanced economy, and fell only to the Mongols who were simultaneously conquering most of Eurasia.

**Sakoku wasn't total isolation**
The Tokugawa "closed country" policy allowed controlled trade with: the Dutch East India Company (at Dejima), Chinese merchants, and Koreans. Japan wasn't hermetically sealed — it was selectively engaged. Dutch books (Rangaku = "Dutch learning") kept Japan informed about European scientific developments.
