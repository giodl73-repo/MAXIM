# Feudal Mechanics — How the System Actually Worked

## The Core Contract

Feudalism is not an ideology — it's a **security and resource allocation system** that emerged from the collapse of centralized Roman administration. When you can't pay a professional army and a bureaucracy, you substitute: land for cash, personal loyalty for institutional law.

```
                    THE FEUDAL BARGAIN
+------------------------+     +------------------------+
|       LORD             |     |       VASSAL           |
|                        |     |                        |
|  Grants: fief (land or | --> |  Receives: fief        |
|    office, revenue,    |     |    (livelihood,        |
|    rights)             |     |     status, income)    |
|                        |     |                        |
|  Owes: protection,     | <-- |  Owes: knight service, |
|    justice, good faith |     |    counsel, aid, suit  |
|                        |     |    of court, fealty    |
+------------------------+     +------------------------+

Both parties can breach. Breach = war.
```

## Ceremony: Homage and Fealty

These are distinct rituals, often confused.

```
HOMAGE (homagium):
  Vassal kneels, places hands between lord's hands (immixtio manuum)
  Vassal says: "I become your man for [fief], to bear faith and loyalty to you
               for life and limb and earthly honor..."
  Lord raises vassal — contract complete
  → Homage creates the lord-vassal bond
  → Can only be done once per fief (usually)
  → Problem: what if you're vassal to multiple lords? → liege homage

FEALTY (fidelitas):
  Vassal swears on relics/Gospel: will not harm lord, will reveal threats, etc.
  → Fealty is the oath of loyalty
  → Can be renewed, can be sworn to multiple lords
  → Bishops swear fealty but sometimes not homage (ecclesiastical independence)

LIEGE HOMAGE:
  When a vassal holds fiefs from multiple lords, one lord is "liege" —
  primary loyalty. In conflict, vassal fights for liege lord first.
  → Philip II of France used this to pull Norman barons away from English king
```

## Military Obligation — The Point of the Whole System

### Knight Service (Servitium Debitum)

```
King
 ├── grants barony to Baron X
 │     Baron X owes: 20 knights for 40 days per year
 │     Baron X subinfeudates to 5 knights
 │       Each knight owes: 4 knights for 40 days
 │         (actually rides himself + 3 mounted men-at-arms)
 │
 ├── grants barony to Baron Y
 │     Baron Y owes: 15 knights for 40 days per year
 │     Baron Y runs own household knights (enfeoffed to no one)
```

**The 40-day rule:** Knight service owed per year was typically 40 days.
This was sufficient for a campaign season but NOT for garrisoning a castle indefinitely.
Result: kings needed **mercenaries** (paid soldiers) for sustained warfare from the start.

### Scutage (Shield Money)

```
Knight says: "I don't want to fight. I'll pay instead."
King (or lord) says: "Fine. Pay scutage — shield money."

  Amount: typically 1-3 marks per knight's fee
  Lords: collect scutage from vassals, pay lump sum up to king,
          keep any surplus
  Kings: use cash to hire mercenaries who fight all year

EFFECT: By 1200s, scutage undermined knight service almost entirely.
        Professional armies replaced feudal levy.
        Magna Carta (1215) restricted English king's scutage powers.
```

### Castle Guard

Knights owed time at lord's castle. Usually rotational. Important for:
- Defending the lord's stronghold
- Garrisoning captured fortifications
- Showing up matters even if you do nothing

## Taxation and Revenue

Medieval rulers had multiple revenue streams, none systematic.

```
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| SOURCE           | WHAT IT IS                       | WHO PAYS         |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Demesne income   | Revenue from lord's own lands    | Peasants/serfs   |
| Tallage          | Arbitrary tax on serfs/towns     | Serfs, burgesses |
| Tithe            | 1/10 of agricultural produce     | Everyone (Church)|
| Feudal aids      | Money payments on 4 occasions    | Vassals          |
|   (see below)    |                                  |                  |
| Relief           | Inheritance payment to take fief | Heir of vassal   |
| Wardship         | Revenue from minor's fief        | Lord manages it  |
| Marriage fine    | Fee to approve vassal's marriage | Vassal/heiress   |
| Customs/tolls    | Duties on trade, road/bridge use | Merchants        |
| Profits of       | Fines, fees from court cases     | Litigants        |
|   justice        |                                  |                  |
| Scutage          | Shield money instead of service  | Vassals          |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
```

### The Four Feudal Aids

These were the only times a lord could demand cash from vassals as of right:

1. **Ransom the lord** — Lord captured in battle; vassals help pay ransom
2. **Knighting the lord's eldest son** — Ceremony costs money
3. **Dowry for the lord's eldest daughter** — First marriage only
4. **Crusade** — Lord taking the Cross

Magna Carta 1215 codified this list for England. Lords extorting beyond these occasions was a key grievance.

## High Justice vs Low Justice

```
HIGH JUSTICE (haute justice):
  Lord can try capital cases: murder, treason, serious felony
  Can impose: death, mutilation, forfeiture of land
  Held by: only the most powerful lords, or by royal grant
  France: only the king and highest nobles
  HRE: more widely distributed → "Blutgericht" (blood court)

LOW JUSTICE (basse justice):
  Minor civil disputes: trespass, small debts, breach of contract
  Minor criminal: petty theft, assault without death
  Penalties: fines, corporal punishment (not death)
  Held by: most lords of a manor
  This is what gave lords direct power over peasants daily
```

**The Palatinate exception:** Some lords (Count Palatine of the Rhine, Bishop of Durham) held near-royal jurisdictional powers including high justice, coin, and military command. Effectively mini-kings within the larger kingdom.

## The Manorial System

The lowest level of the pyramid — where 90% of the population lived.

```
THE MANOR (basic economic unit):
+--------------------------------------------------+
|  LORD'S DEMESNE (lord farms directly, ~1/3)      |
|  Open fields farmed in strips by peasants        |
|  Common pasture + woodland (shared use rights)   |
|  Village + church + mill (lord usually owns mill)|
+--------------------------------------------------+

PEASANT CATEGORIES:
  Free peasants (freeholders):
    - Hold land in exchange for rent (cash or kind)
    - Can leave, can sue in royal courts
    - Military service possible

  Villeins (serfs, bondsmen):
    - Bound to the land — cannot leave without permission
    - Owe labor services (week-work: 3 days/week on demesne)
    - Owe boon works (extra harvest labor)
    - Cannot marry without lord's permission (merchet fine)
    - Cannot sue lord in royal court (only manorial court)
    - Lord can tallage them at will

  Cotters/cottars:
    - Smallest landholders
    - More labor, less land
    - Near-subsistence
```

**Black Death effect:** Labor shortages post-1347 destroyed the economic basis of serfdom. Peasants could demand wages. Villeinage collapsed in Western Europe over 1350–1500.

## Inheritance Law — The Succession Question

This is the engine of medieval history. Every war traces to a disputed succession.

```
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| SYSTEM           | RULE                             | WHERE USED       |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Primogeniture    | Eldest son inherits whole fief   | England (from    |
| (strict)         | Prevents fragmentation           | Norman Conquest),|
|                  | Sister inherits if no sons       | France gradually |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Gavelkind        | All sons share equally           | Kent (England),  |
|                  | Fragments landholding each gen.  | Wales, Ireland   |
|                  |                                  | (pre-Norman)     |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Tanistry         | The strongest/worthiest of the   | Celtic Ireland,  |
|                  | kin-group is chosen (by election | Scotland (early) |
|                  | among eligible males)            |                  |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Agnatic          | Male-line only. No women inherit,| France (Salic),  |
| seniority        | no transmission through women.   | HRE, many German |
| (Salic law)      | Nearest agnate male inherits     | territories      |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Cognatic         | Both male and female lines count.| England, many    |
|                  | Women can inherit; can transmit  | others — women   |
|                  | claim through their children     | ruled or passed  |
|                  |                                  | claims to husbands|
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Rotation / Rota  | Kievan Rus system: throne rotates| Kievan Rus       |
|                  | among princes by seniority in    | (disastrously)   |
|                  | Rurik dynasty — works in theory  |                  |
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| Elective         | Rulers chosen by designated      | HRE, Poland-     |
|                  | electors or nobility             | Lithuania, Papacy|
+------------------+----------------------------------+------------------+
```

**Why Salic Law mattered for France:**
Philip IV of France died 1314 leaving three sons — all died childless.
If cognatic: his daughter Isabella (wife of Edward II of England) transmits the claim to her son Edward III.
If Salic: nearest agnatic heir is Philip of Valois → Philip VI.
France ruled Salic. England ruled cognatic. Edward III claimed the French throne through his mother.
→ Hundred Years War (1337–1453).

## The Investiture Controversy (1076–1122)

The deepest structural crisis of the medieval Church-state system.

```
THE PROBLEM:
  Bishops hold land as feudal lords → owe military service to king
  Bishops lead the Church → appointed by and answerable to Pope

  KING: "I invest bishops. I give them the ring and staff."
        (Ring = spiritual authority, Staff = temporal authority)
  POPE: "Only I invest bishops. Lay investiture is heresy."

  RESULT: If Pope controls appointments:
    - King loses control of 1/3 to 1/2 of his military resources
    - King loses ecclesiastical courts
    - King loses a key tool of royal patronage

  If King controls appointments:
    - Pope loses control of Church in every kingdom
    - Simony (selling church offices) runs rampant
    - Church reform (Gregorian Reform) fails

HENRY IV vs GREGORY VII (1076):
  Gregory: excommunicates Henry IV
  Henry: walks to Canossa barefoot in snow (January 1077)
         does penance, gets absolution
         → Gregory must lift excommunication
         → Henry has survived — not really defeated
  Henry: later deposes Gregory, installs antipope
  Result: stalemate

CONCORDAT OF WORMS (1122):
  Compromise: Bishop receives two investitures
    1. Spiritual investiture (ring + staff) from Pope
    2. Temporal investiture (scepter = land, rights) from King
  Sequence matters: spiritual first in Germany,
                    temporal first elsewhere
  Neither side won cleanly. The ambiguity lasted.
```

## Crusades as Feudal Enterprise

The Crusades were not just religious wars — they were **feudal expansion with Papal authorization**.

```
WHY LORDS WENT:
  + Papal indulgence (sins forgiven) → massive draw
  + Land grants in the Levant if successful
  + Younger sons with no inheritance got fiefs
  + Adventure + prestige + escape from debt
  - Expense: typical crusade cost 3-5x annual income
  - Mortality: most who went died (disease >> battle)
  - Estates: had to be mortgaged or managed by proxy

FEUDAL STRUCTURE IN OUTREMER:
  King of Jerusalem
    ├── Prince of Antioch (barely subordinate)
    ├── Count of Tripoli
    ├── County of Edessa (lost 1144 — triggered 2nd Crusade)
    └── Barons of Jerusalem
          ├── Ibelin family (most powerful)
          ├── Montfort
          ├── Military Orders (Hospitallers, Templars)
          │     → Hold land, owe knight service, answer to Pope
          │     → Best-equipped troops, but pursue own agendas
          └── Italian merchant communes (Venice, Genoa, Pisa)
                → Hold quarters in cities, owe no service
```

## The HRE as the Extreme Case of Elective Monarchy

The Holy Roman Empire is feudalism pushed to its logical limit of fragmentation.

```
ELECTED EMPEROR (not hereditary in theory):
  Seven Electors (after Golden Bull 1356):
    - Archbishop of Mainz
    - Archbishop of Cologne
    - Archbishop of Trier
    - King of Bohemia
    - Count Palatine of the Rhine
    - Duke of Saxony (Electorate of Saxony)
    - Margrave of Brandenburg

  Emperor needed majority — bribery, deal-making, dynastic pressure standard

WHAT EMPEROR COULD NOT DO (unlike French/English king):
  - Tax the princes directly
  - Raise an imperial army (had to ask for Römermonat contributions)
  - Legislate without Reichstag approval
  - Override imperial estates' internal affairs

WHAT EMPEROR COULD DO:
  - Grant titles and confirm privileges
  - Call Reichstag
  - Imperial justice (Reichskammergericht after 1495)
  - Lead imperial campaigns (when princes cooperated)
  - Negotiate with foreign powers
```

Result: 300+ semi-sovereign entities by the 17th century. The "empire" was a legal fiction preserving ancient claims while princes built real states.

## Decision Cheat Sheet — Transfer Mechanisms

| Situation | Mechanism | Who gains | Risk |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|------|
| Vassal dies leaving son | Primogeniture | Son (pays relief) | Son might be a minor — wardship |
| Vassal dies, daughter only | Depends on law | Daughter or her husband | Cognatic: yes; Salic: no |
| Vassal rebels | Forfeiture/attainder | Lord reclaims fief | Enforcing it requires military success |
| Heiress marries | Jure uxoris | Husband manages fief | Wife's children inherit on husband's death |
| Two heirs claim same title | War / arbitration | Stronger claimant | Generation of warfare |
| Territory purchased | Treaty | Buyer, if he can pay | Money scarce; pledges common |
| Pope grants crusade territory | Conquest + Papal bull | Crusade leader | Only if conquest succeeds |
| Lord dies, no heirs | Escheat | Overlord | Lord must enforce it militarily |

## Common Confusion Points

**Homage ≠ allegiance to a nation**
A French duke could do homage to the English king for his English lands while remaining a French peer. He was not a traitor to France — he was meeting his contractual obligations for each fief.

**Feudalism was not universal**
Large parts of Europe — particularly in the East — never had the full knight-fief system. Russia had Rurikid patrimony (votchina), not French-style feudalism. The Ottomans had timar grants. The Mongols had the ulus system. These were all "feudal-ish" in the loose sense of land-for-service but operated very differently.

**Manorialism ≠ feudalism**
Feudalism is the political/military relationship between lords. Manorialism is the economic/agricultural relationship between lord and peasants. They overlapped but aren't identical — a bishop could run manors with serfs without any military obligation to anyone.

**"Feudalism" as a term was invented later**
Medieval people didn't call it feudalism. The word derives from "feodum" (fief) and was coined by legal scholars in the 17th century. The system was understood as custom, obligation, and right — not as a named ideology.
