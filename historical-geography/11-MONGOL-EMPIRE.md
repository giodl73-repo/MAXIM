## Genghis Khan — The Unification

### The Steppe World Before Genghis

The Mongolian steppe was divided among competing tribal confederations: Mongols, Tatars, Keraits, Naimans, Merkits. Constant warfare for pasture, horses, and prestige. No permanent political structure. Children were regularly captured by enemy tribes.

Temüjin's early life: father poisoned by Tatars; family abandoned by his father's tribe; captured by Merkits; escaped. He built alliances through loyalty, cunning, and generosity to followers — and ruthlessness to enemies.

```
TEMÜJIN'S RISE:
  Married Börte (wife kidnapped by Merkits → recovered with ally's help)
  Alliance with Toghrul (Kerait Khan) → "anda" (blood brother) of Jamukha
  Battle of Dalan Balzhut: fought Jamukha → lost, but survived
  Gradually built coalition; won Battle of Qalaqaljid Sands
  Defeated Tatars (who killed his father) — systematic annihilation
  Defeated Naimans; defeated Merkits; unified all steppe peoples
  1206: KURULTAI at the Onon River headwaters
        Proclaimed Genghis Khagan — "universal ruler"
```

### What Made the Mongol Army Different

```
ORGANIZATION: Decimal system
  10 = arban (squad)
  100 = zuun (company)
  1,000 = mingghan (regiment)
  10,000 = tümen (division)

  Commanders appointed by merit, not kinship (initially)
  Each man responsible for equipment; army self-supplied on campaign
  Communication: yam (postal relay system) — could transmit orders
    across the empire faster than any contemporary system

TACTICS:
  Feigned retreat (quriltai tactic): draw enemy cavalry into pursuit,
    then turn; flanking units encircle the pursuers
  Mangudai: "suicide cavalry" that charged enemy and pretended to rout
  Massed archery: composite bow with 300-yard range while riding
  Strategic deception: send envoys; if refused, destroy; offer surrender
    terms; if accepted, usually honored; if not, annihilate

SIEGE WARFARE:
  Initially: weak at sieges (steppe nomads don't do fortifications)
  Learned rapidly: captured Chinese engineers; later Persian engineers
  Used catapults, gunpowder weapons, and most importantly:
  PATIENCE — starved out cities when walls were too strong

INTELLIGENCE:
  Extensive use of merchants as spies
  Yam system: information traveled faster than armies
  Reconnoitred territory years before invasion
  (The 1241 invasion of Poland + Hungary was planned years in advance
   using intelligence gathered by Mongol agents)
```

## The Conquests — Speed and Scale

```
CONQUEST TIMELINE:
  1209–1215: Xi Xia and Jin Dynasty (northern China) — war begins
  1219–1221: Khwarazmian Empire (modern Uzbekistan/Iran/Afghanistan)
    Shah Muhammad II refused to negotiate after Mongol merchants
    were killed. Response: total destruction.
    Samarkand, Bukhara, Merv, Nishapur — great cities annihilated
    Merv: reported ~700,000 killed (probably exaggerated; still catastrophic)
  1223: Battle of the Kalka River — first contact with Rus
  1227: Genghis dies (campaign against Xi Xia; reason unclear)
  1230–1234: Final conquest of Jin (northern China)
  1236–1241: Batu Khan's western campaign
    1237: Ryazan, Vladimir (Russia)
    1240: Kyiv sacked
    1241: Battle of Legnica (Poland), Battle of Mohi (Hungary)
    Withdrew: Ögedei died; succession required return
  1253–1258: Hulagu's campaign west
    1258: Baghdad sacked; Abbasid caliphate ended
    1260: Ain Jalut — stopped by Mamluks

SCALE:
  At maximum (~1279): ~24 million km² — largest contiguous land empire ever
  Population under Mongol control: ~100 million
  Estimated deaths from Mongol conquests: 40 million (some say more)
  (This is approximately 10% of the world's population at the time)
  China's population dropped from ~120 million to ~80 million
```

## The Pax Mongolica — What the Empire Made Possible

```
TRADE NETWORK:
  Silk Road (really: multiple silk roads) was safest in centuries
  Merchants could travel from China to Eastern Europe
  with a valid pass (gerege/paiza — gold tablet of safe conduct)
  This was genuine. Merchants used it.
  Marco Polo (1271–1295): traveled to Kublai Khan's court; worked there
  Giovanni da Pian del Carpine (1245): Papal envoy to Karakorum
  William of Rubruck (1253–1255): Franciscan friar to Karakorum
  → We know more about Mongol culture from Western observers than
    almost any other aspect of medieval Central Asian history

CULTURAL TRANSFER:
  Chinese gunpowder → West via Mongol empire
  Chinese printing → West (slower)
  Chinese paper money → attempted in Persia (failed there)
  Persian astronomy/mathematics ← → Chinese astronomy
  Islamic medicine ← → Chinese medicine
  Bubonic plague: almost certainly spread via Mongol trade routes
    (1347 outbreak = Mongol siege of Caffa → Genoese → Mediterranean)

THE YAM (POSTAL RELAY):
  Stations every ~25-30 miles; fresh horses; riders
  A message could cross the empire in 2-3 weeks
  (Comparable to telegraph era speed for official communications)
  Genghis Khan required safe passage for all envoys
  Violation of envoy safety: the most serious offense
  He destroyed the Khwarazmian Empire in part because its Shah
  had killed Mongol merchants (treated as envoys)
```

## The Four Khanates — Divergent Paths

### Yuan Dynasty (1271–1368) — China

**Kublai Khan** (r. 1260–1294): Genghis's grandson; the last "Great Khan" with any real authority over the other khanates.

```
SINICIZATION OF THE YUAN:
  Kublai moved capital to Khanbaliq (modern Beijing)
  Adopted Chinese administrative systems (partially)
  Chinese title: Emperor of the Yuan dynasty
  Language: maintained Mongolian at court; Chinese administration
  Religion: patronized Buddhism (Tibetan Buddhism especially)
  Marco Polo served in his court for ~17 years

WHAT THEY FAILED TO CONQUER:
  Japan: Typhoon destroyed invasion fleets 1274, 1281
         (The "kamikaze" — divine wind — typhoons)
         Japan was one of the few states that refused and survived
  Vietnam (Đại Việt): Three invasions; all repelled
  Java: Invasion reached; failed; withdrew
  These failures showed limits of Mongol land warfare vs defended coasts

FALL:
  Chinese resentment; natural disasters (floods, famines); peasant rebellions
  Red Turban Rebellion: major uprising 1351
  Zhu Yuanzhang (peasant origin): led rebellion; founded Ming Dynasty 1368
  Yuan expelled to Mongolia
```

### Golden Horde (c. 1241–1502) — Russia's Overlord

**Batu Khan** (r. 1227–1255): Founded the Golden Horde. Capital: Sarai (on Volga).

```
GOLDEN HORDE AND RUSSIA:
  Russian princes traveled to Sarai for yarlik (confirmation of rule)
  Tribute collected; census taken for tax purposes
  Religion: initially tolerant; Orthodox Church exempt from tribute
  Conversion to Islam: officially under Özbeg Khan 1313
    → Islamized Mongol-Tatar state
  Russian princes exploited: played off competing Horde factions
    Moscow particularly good at this (Ivan I Kalita strategy)

FRAGMENTATION:
  Late 14th century: "Great Troubles" (Velikaya Zamyatnya)
  ~25 khans in 20 years
  Timur (Tamerlane) defeats and humiliates Horde 1391, 1395
  Final split into successor khanates:
    Khanate of Kazan (falls to Ivan IV 1552)
    Khanate of Astrakhan (falls to Ivan IV 1556)
    Crimean Khanate (persists under Ottoman protection until 1783)
    Khanate of Sibir (falls to Cossack expansion ~1582)
    Great Horde (last remnant; destroyed 1502)
```

### Ilkhanate (1256–1335) — Persia

**Hulagu Khan** (r. 1256–1265): Destroyed the Abbasid Caliphate (1258). His wife was Nestorian Christian; Hulagu himself was interested in multiple religions (Buddhist, tolerant of Christians and Jews).

```
ILKHANATE ISLAMIZATION:
  Ghazan Khan (r. 1295–1304): converted to Islam
  → Ilkhanate officially Muslim
  → Reversed Mongol tolerance of other religions somewhat
  Ghazan: significant administrator; reformed finances

  ILKHANATE AND THE CRUSADERS:
  Franco-Mongol alliance discussions: multiple attempts by Crusaders
  to ally with Mongols against Mamluks (common enemy)
  Never materialized at decisive scale
  (Hulagu was fighting Mamluks anyway — Ain Jalut 1260)

  FALL:
  Ilkhanate fragmented after Abu Said (last strong khan) died 1335
  Multiple competing khans; dissolved into regional dynasties
  → Replaced by: Jalayirids, Muzaffarids, then TIMURIDS
```

### Chagatai Khanate (c. 1220–1370s) — Central Asia

The most turbulent successor state. Named for Chagatai (Genghis's second son, who held Central Asia). Constant civil war between factions supporting different candidates.

The Chagatai Khanate eventually split: eastern (Moghulistan) and western portions. Both fragmented and were replaced by Timurid rule.

## Timur / Tamerlane (r. 1370–1405)

```
TIMUR "THE LAME" (Tamerlane = Timur-i-lang):
  Born near Samarkand; Barlas Turkic clan (not Mongol but claimed legitimacy)
  Never called himself "Great Khan" — used title "amir" + son-in-law of
  Chagatai Khan (rule through legitimate Chingisid puppet)
  Built empire: Central Asia, Persia, Iraq, parts of India, Anatolia
  SCALE OF DESTRUCTION:
    Delhi Sultanate sacked 1398: ~100,000 prisoners massacred before battle
    Ottoman Sultan Bayezid I captured at Battle of Ankara 1402
    → Ottomans thrown into crisis; Crusaders missed their chance
  Towers of skulls: his trademark
  SAMARKAND: his capital; brilliant architectural patronage
    Registan Square; magnificent mosques; center of learning
    The Timurid Renaissance: Persian poetry, miniature painting, astronomy
    Ulugh Beg (his grandson): built observatory; star catalogue

  DIED 1405: on campaign toward China
  TIMURID DYNASTY:
    Successors: Shahrukh (son), Ulugh Beg (grandson — murdered by son)
    Babur (descendant): failed in Central Asia → conquered India 1526
    → Mughal Empire (Moghul = Mongol)
```

## Why the Empire Fragmented

```
STRUCTURAL REASONS:
  1. No clear succession law:
     Mongol tradition: kurultai (assembly) elects Great Khan
     In practice: whoever had the strongest army won
     Every death → civil war among brothers/cousins

  2. Vast distances + diverse subjects:
     Communication limit of the yam system
     Each khanate needed local administrators → local interests
     Chinese subjects required Chinese methods
     Muslim subjects required Islamic legitimacy
     Eventually: local identity > Mongol identity

  3. Islamization as divergence:
     Golden Horde + Ilkhanate both Islamized
     → Could not fight each other easily (fellow Muslims)
     → But fought each other anyway for political reasons
     Yuan (China): Buddhist/syncretic
     Chagatai: late Islamization
     No shared religious framework to bind the empire

  4. The plague:
     Black Death traveled the Mongol trade network
     1347 outbreak: devastating across all four khanates
     Population losses disrupted the tribute/military systems
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Tatars ≠ Mongols**
The Tatars were one of the tribes that Genghis Khan conquered (and massacred extensively — they had killed his father). "Tatar" became a generic Western/Russian term for all Mongols and their successors. The Golden Horde's populations were mixed Turkic/Mongol. The Crimean Tatars (who survived until modern times) were predominantly Turkic-speaking and Islamized.

**Ain Jalut was significant but not uniquely decisive**
The Battle of Ain Jalut (1260) is often called "the battle that stopped the Mongols." But the Mongols were already stretched thin, depleted by campaigns, and facing the need to deal with succession politics. They might not have pushed further anyway. The battle was significant; it was not necessarily the only thing that stopped them.

**Pax Mongolica ≠ peace**
The "Mongol Peace" meant safety for trade on major routes — not that warfare ceased. The Mongols were constantly fighting each other (Kublai vs Ariq Böke; Ilkhanate vs Golden Horde; etc.) and expanding against new territories. For the people being conquered, there was nothing peaceful about it.

**Timur was not restoring the Mongol Empire**
Timur (Tamerlane) built a new empire using Mongol legitimacy claims, but it was ethnically Turkic, linguistically Chagatai Turkish, and culturally Persian. It was not a revival of the Genghisid empire — it was a successor state using the Mongol brand.
