## France: The Capetian Consolidation Arc

### The Starting Position (987 CE)

The Carolingian line in West Francia died out. The great lords elected Hugh Capet as king — they expected him to be a puppet. He wasn't strong enough to dominate, but he was clever enough to survive.

**Hugh Capet's actual territory:** Roughly the Île-de-France — Paris, Orléans, some surrounding districts. The "King of France" controlled less territory than a dozen of his own vassals.

### The Great Fiefs and Their Dynasties

```
DUCHY OF NORMANDY:
  Created: 911 by Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte
  First duke: Rollo (Viking, Christianized)
  Dynasty: Rollonids → William the Conqueror
  Key moment: William conquers England 1066
               → Duke of Normandy now ALSO King of England
               → French king's most dangerous vassal is also
                  a foreign king with his own resources
  Absorbed by France: Philip II takes Normandy 1204 from King John

DUCHY OF AQUITAINE:
  Enormous territory: Gascony + Poitou + Limousin + more
  Inherited by: Eleanor of Aquitaine (queen consort of France,
                then of England — see below)
  Key transfer: Eleanor married Louis VII of France 1137
                Louis divorces Eleanor 1152 (no male heirs, grounds claimed)
                Eleanor immediately marries Henry Plantagenet
                → Henry becomes Henry II of England 1154
                → Brings Aquitaine INTO English hands
                → Angevin Empire: England + Normandy + Anjou + Aquitaine
  Absorbed: Hundred Years War; English lose Aquitaine 1453

COUNTY OF FLANDERS:
  The richest territory in northwest Europe
  Wool industry: English raw wool → Flemish cloth → European markets
  Counts of Flanders: often more powerful than French king in resources
  Key tension: economically tied to England (wool), politically to France
  Black Death + peasant revolts (Bruges/Ghent) destabilize late 14th century
  Eventually into Burgundian Valois orbit

DUCHY OF BURGUNDY:
  First Capetian appanage: granted to Robert (son of Robert II) 1032
  Problem: Capetian Burgundy line dies out 1361
  Solution: King John II of France grants it to his youngest son Philip the Bold
  → Valois Duchy of Burgundy begins
  Valois dukes: Philip the Bold → John the Fearless → Philip the Good →
               Charles the Bold (dies 1477 fighting Swiss/Lorraine)
  → On Charles's death: daughter Mary of Burgundy marries Maximilian Habsburg
  → Burgundian Netherlands pass to Habsburgs
  → France absorbs the Duchy itself (Salic law: Mary can't inherit French territory)

COUNTY OF TOULOUSE:
  The "other France" — Languedoc, distinct language (Occitan), culture
  Counts of Toulouse: Raymond family; enormously powerful
  Cathars (Albigensians): heretical Christian sect flourished here
  → Pope Innocent III calls Albigensian Crusade 1209
  → Simon de Montfort (the Elder) leads crusade, massacres towns
  → Treaty of Paris 1229: Toulouse humiliated, county to pass to French crown
  Absorbed: 1271, last count dies without male heirs
```

### Philip II Augustus (r. 1180–1223) — The Consolidator

```
BEFORE Philip II: French crown controls ~10% of France
AFTER Philip II: French crown controls ~40% of France

KEY MOVES:
1. Takes Normandy from King John 1204 (John forfeited fief by murdering nephew)
2. Takes Anjou, Maine, Touraine (the heart of Angevin Empire)
3. Battle of Bouvines 1214: defeats coalition of England + HRE + Flanders
   → Decisive — English threat ended for a generation
   → John forced to sign Magna Carta 1215 due to weakened position
4. Builds Paris walls, paves streets, founds University of Paris
```

### The Hundred Years War (1337–1453)

Not one war — a series of campaigns over 116 years between England and France.

```
CAUSE:
  Edward III claims French throne through mother Isabella
  France rules Salic law → Philip VI (Valois) is king
  Edward III: "I am the rightful king of France"

PHASE 1 — ENGLISH HIGH TIDE (1337–1360):
  Crécy 1346: English longbows destroy French cavalry
  Poitiers 1356: Edward the Black Prince captures John II of France
  Treaty of Brétigny 1360: England gets 1/3 of France in full sovereignty

PHASE 2 — FRENCH RECOVERY (1360–1415):
  Charles V of France + Bertrand du Guesclin
  Guerrilla / chevauchée tactics avoided pitched battles
  English territorial gains reversed

PHASE 3 — ENGLISH RETURN (1415–1429):
  Henry V: Agincourt 1415 (again, English longbows)
  Treaty of Troyes 1420: Henry V made heir to French throne
  Henry V dies 1422; infant Henry VI king of England AND France (in English claim)

PHASE 4 — JOAN OF ARC + FRENCH VICTORY (1429–1453):
  Joan of Arc: relieves Orléans 1429
  Charles VII crowned at Reims 1429
  Joan captured by Burgundians 1430, sold to English, burned 1431
  But the momentum was with France
  Castillon 1453: last major battle; English lose Gascony
  → England retains only Calais (until 1558)
```

## Iberia: The Reconquista (711–1492)

### The Starting Map

In 711 CE, the Umayyad Caliphate invaded from North Africa. Within 7 years, almost the entire Iberian Peninsula was under Muslim control. The Reconquista is the 780-year process of Christian kingdoms pushing south.

```
IBERIA c. 800 CE:
+-----------------------------------------------+
| KINGDOM OF ASTURIAS (northwest mountain core) |
+-----------------------------------------------+
|         [AL-ANDALUS — Umayyad Islamic Spain]  |
|                                               |
| Córdoba (Umayyad Emirate, then Caliphate)     |
| Seville, Toledo, Granada, Valencia, Zaragoza  |
+-----------------------------------------------+

IBERIA c. 1000 CE:
+-----------+----------+--------+
| Galicia   | León     | Castile| ← merged and split repeatedly
+-----------+----------+--------+
| Navarre | ARAGON    | BARCELONA (Count of Barcelona = Catalonia)
+---------+-----------+-----------+
| PORTUGAL (emerging 12th century)|
+---------------------------------+
|   TAIFA KINGDOMS (after 1031):  |
|   Caliphate of Córdoba collapsed |
|   → 30+ small taifa kingdoms    |
|   Toledo / Seville / Granada    |
|   / Valencia / Zaragoza / etc.  |
+---------------------------------+
| ALMORAVIDS (Berber dynasty      |
|  from Morocco, called in 1086)  |
+---------------------------------+
```

### Key Christian Kingdoms

**León and Castile:**
Leon was the dominant early kingdom. Castile began as a frontier county — "land of castles" (fortifications against raids). They merged, split, merged again under various inheritances.

Ferdinand I (r. 1037–1065): Unified León and Castile under one rule. Then divided it again among his sons — which triggered wars.

Alfonso VI (r. 1072–1109): Reunified; took Toledo 1085 — the first major taifa capital to fall. This triggered the Almoravid invasion.

Ferdinand III (r. 1217–1252): The Saint. Unified León-Castile permanently. Took Córdoba 1236, Seville 1248.

**Crown of Aragon:**
Aragon was a mountain kingdom in the Pyrenean foothills. The County of Barcelona (Catalonia) united with Aragon by marriage 1137. This created the Crown of Aragon — two distinct entities under one ruler.

James I the Conqueror (r. 1213–1276): Took Valencia 1238, Majorca 1229. The Crown of Aragon became a Mediterranean empire: Sicily (1282), Sardinia (1323), Naples eventually.

**Portugal:**
The County of Portugal was carved from León and given to Henry of Burgundy (a crusader) as a reward. His son Afonso Henriques (r. 1128–1185) declared himself king, won Battle of Ourique 1139, got Papal recognition 1143. Portugal reached its modern borders by ~1250 — the earliest stable national boundary in Europe.

**Navarre:**
The Pyrenean kingdom between France and Spain. Declined in relative power as Castile and Aragon grew. Eventually absorbed by Aragon (1512) except the northern Basse-Navarre (→ France 1589 via Henry IV).

### The Taifa Collapse and Religious Interventions

```
ALMORAVID DYNASTY (1086–1147):
  Called in by taifa kings after Alfonso VI takes Toledo
  Berber dynasty from Maghreb (modern Morocco/Mauritania)
  Strict Maliki Sunni Islam — cracked down on the sophisticated
  tolerant culture of Andalusia
  Reunified al-Andalus; stopped Christian advance temporarily
  Alfonso VI's offensive halted; El Cid (Rodrigo Díaz) operates
  as a freelance warlord in this period, takes Valencia 1094

ALMOHAD DYNASTY (1147–1269):
  Reformed Berber movement from Atlas Mountains
  More austere than Almoravids
  Defeated Almoravids, took North Africa + al-Andalus
  Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa 1212: decisive Almohad defeat
  → Christian kingdoms could now push south rapidly

FINAL ACT:
  Emirate of Granada: Last Muslim state in Iberia
  Founded by Muhammad I ibn Nasr 1238
  Survived as a tributary kingdom under Castile
  The Alhambra palace built here (1238–1358)
  Ferdinand and Isabella: Granada surrenders Jan 2, 1492
  → Reconquista complete; Columbus sails 3 months later
```

## England: From Heptarchy to Tudor Monarchy

### Before 1066

The Anglo-Saxon kingdoms (Heptarchy) — Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Wessex, Sussex, Essex, Kent — consolidated under Wessex dominance.

**Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899): King of Wessex. Stopped the Viking (Great Heathen Army) conquest. Danelaw established — north and east England under Norse control. Alfred's descendants gradually recovered it.

**Edgar the Peaceful** (r. 959–975): First king recognized as king of all England.

**Cnut** (r. 1016–1035): Danish king; conquered England; ruled Denmark + Norway + England as a North Sea empire.

### The Norman Conquest (1066)

```
EVENTS:
  Edward the Confessor dies Jan 5, 1066 — no direct heir
  Three claimants:
    1. Harold Godwinson: Earl of Wessex; English noble; elected by Witan
    2. Harald Hardrada (Norway): claimed via Cnut succession
    3. William, Duke of Normandy: claims Edward promised him the throne
       + Harold swore fealty to William (contested circumstances)

  Harald Hardrada invades north → Harold Godwinson marches north
  Battle of Stamford Bridge Sept 25, 1066: Harold destroys Norwegian army
  William lands at Pevensey Sept 28, 1066
  Harold force-marches south (bad decision)
  Battle of Hastings Oct 14, 1066: Harold killed (eye/arrow — probably myth)

CONSEQUENCES:
  Norman aristocracy replaces Anglo-Saxon (within a generation)
  Domesday Book 1086: complete survey of England
  French vocabulary layered onto English (beef/cow, pork/pig, etc.)
  English church reorganized (Norman bishops)
  Castle building: 500+ motte-and-bailey castles
  Common law begins developing under William and successors
```

### The Angevin Empire

```
HENRY II (r. 1154–1189) held:
  Kingdom of England
  Duchy of Normandy (inherited from mother Matilda)
  County of Anjou (inherited from father Geoffrey Plantagenet)
  County of Maine
  County of Touraine
  Duchy of Aquitaine (through Eleanor — his wife)
  [+ nominal overlordship of Scotland, Wales, Ireland]

COMBINED TERRITORY: larger than France by area

PROBLEMS:
  Thomas Becket: Henry's Archbishop of Canterbury; murdered 1170
                 by knights who misheard Henry's frustration
                 → penance, humiliation, but survived politically
  Sons rebel: Great Revolt 1173-74 — Henry's sons + Eleanor rebel
              (Eleanor imprisoned for 15 years)
  Richard I (1189-1199): mostly crusading; England barely managed
  John (1199-1216): loses Normandy, Anjou to Philip II 1204
                    Magna Carta 1215 (barons revolt)
                    → Angevin Empire collapses
```

### Wars of the Roses (1455–1485)

```
HOUSES:
  Lancaster (red rose): descended from John of Gaunt, 4th son of Edward III
  York (white rose): descended from Edmund of York + through Lionel of Antwerp
                     (actually had a STRONGER claim in some lines)

ROOT CAUSE: Henry VI (Lancaster) — weak, mentally ill king
            Richard, Duke of York becomes protector
            → conflict over who governs → open war

KEY BATTLES:
  1st St Albans 1455 (Yorkist win)
  Wakefield 1460 (Lancastrian win — Richard of York killed)
  Towton 1461 (largest battle on English soil — Yorkist win)
    → Edward IV crowned
  Barnet 1471, Tewkesbury 1471 (Edward IV defeats Lancaster)
  Bosworth Field 1485: Richard III (Yorkist) vs Henry Tudor (Lancaster)
    → Henry Tudor wins; Richard III killed
    → Henry VII: marries Elizabeth of York (white + red = Tudor rose)
    → Tudor dynasty begins

RESULT: Noble families decimated. Tudor monarchy vastly more powerful.
        Attainder used aggressively to seize noble estates.
```

## Italy: The Fragmented Peninsula

Italy in this period is NOT a unified state — it's a collection of competing entities.

```
NORTHERN ITALY (Po Valley, Lombardy):
  Nominally part of HRE
  Actually: self-governing city-states (comuni)
  Lombard League formed 1167 to resist Barbarossa
    → Battle of Legnano 1176: cities defeat emperor
  Key cities:
    Milan: Visconti dynasty (dukes from 1395) → Sforza 1450
    Venice: Republic; elected Doge; maritime empire
    Genoa: Republic; rival of Venice; overseas colonies
    Florence: Republic (nominally); banking families
               Medici family dominant from 1430s
               Lorenzo the Magnificent (r. 1469–1492)
    Bologna, Ferrara, Mantua, Parma, etc.

CENTRAL ITALY:
  Papal States: strip from Rome north to Bologna
  Pope is temporal ruler of this territory + spiritual ruler of all Christians
  Constant tension: Guelphs (pro-Pope) vs Ghibellines (pro-Emperor) in every city

SOUTHERN ITALY / KINGDOM OF SICILY:
  Normans conquer Sicily + south Italy 1060–1090
  Kingdom of Sicily (capital: Palermo): most sophisticated court in 12th century
  Roger II: multicultural court; Arabic, Greek, Latin
  Frederick II (HRE emperor + King of Sicily):
    built Castel del Monte; spoke 6 languages; wrote poetry; fought papacy
    Died 1250 → Hohenstaufen line fought out
  Anjou (French): Charles of Anjou takes Sicily 1266
    Sicilian Vespers 1282: massacre of French in Sicily
    → Sicily → Kingdom of Aragon; mainland → Kingdom of Naples (Anjou)
  The division: Kingdom of Sicily = Sicily + Naples (contested name for 200 years)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Plantagenet" ≠ always used by contemporaries**
The family called themselves "Angevins" for the Anjou connection. "Plantagenet" (broom plant = planta genista, supposedly Geoffrey of Anjou's badge) was used occasionally but not as a formal dynastic name until Richard Duke of York used it in the 15th century to bolster his claim.

**"France" vs the Kingdom of France**
The French crown's actual power grew enormously over 1000–1500. In 987, the "kingdom" was a hollow claim over powerful vassals. By 1500, France was the most powerful centralized monarchy in Europe.

**Eleanor of Aquitaine's two marriages**
She was queen consort of France (as wife of Louis VII) and queen consort of England (as wife of Henry II). As Duchess of Aquitaine in her own right, she was the most powerful woman in Europe. Her divorce from Louis was technically an annulment on grounds of consanguinity — but everyone knew it was about her failure to produce male heirs and her difficult personality.

**The Sicilian Vespers**
Not just a rebellion — a mass coordinated massacre of French (Angevin) soldiers and settlers in Sicily, starting at evening prayer (vespers) on March 30, 1282. The Sicilians invited Peter III of Aragon (married to Manfred's daughter — Hohenstaufen heir) to take the throne. This permanently split the Kingdom of Sicily.
