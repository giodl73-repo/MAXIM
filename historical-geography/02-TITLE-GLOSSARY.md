# Title Glossary — Every Rank, Territory, and Concept

## The Title Hierarchy (Western European)

```
IMPERIAL / ROYAL TIER:
  Emperor / Empress
  King / Queen
  Archduke / Archduchess  (Austria only, post-1453)

SOVEREIGN PRINCELY TIER:
  Duke / Duchess
  Prince / Princess  (sovereign, not royal family member)
  Marquis / Margrave / Marchioness
  Landgrave / Landgravine
  Count Palatine / Countess Palatine
  Elector / Electress  (HRE only)

UPPER NOBILITY:
  Count / Earl / Countess
  Viscount / Viscountess  (France: vicomte; England: viscount)
  Baron / Baroness

LOWER NOBILITY:
  Banneret (knight commanding own banner)
  Knight / Dame  (Sir / Lady)
  Esquire / Squire  (armiger — entitled to bear arms)
  Gentleman / Gentlewoman  (lowest recognized rank)

NON-NOBLE:
  Yeoman (free peasant with own land — England)
  Villein / Serf  (unfree)
  Burgess  (recognized townsman)
```

## Individual Title Entries

### Emperor (Latin: Imperator; German: Kaiser; Russian: Tsar/Czar; Byzantine: Basileus)

The highest secular title in the Western/Christian world — theoretically.
In practice, "emperor" meant different things in different contexts:

```
HOLY ROMAN EMPEROR:
  Source: Charlemagne 800 CE (Pope Leo III coronation)
  Claim: successor to Roman Empire
  Reality: elected by princes; limited actual power
  Territory: Germany, Italy (nominal), Burgundy (nominal)
  Language: Kaiser (from Caesar)

BYZANTINE EMPEROR (Basileus):
  Source: continuous from Roman Empire, 4th century
  Claim: THE Roman Emperor — no "holy" needed
  Reality: absolute monarch in theory; often controlled by
           powerful families, generals, eunuch bureaucrats
  Survived until 1453

TSAR / CZAR (Slavic form of Caesar):
  Adopted by: Bulgarian tsars (10th century), Serbian tsar (14th),
              Ivan III of Moscow (informal), Ivan IV (formal, 1547)
  Ivan III: married Sophia Palaiologina (Byzantine princess)
            → claimed succession to Byzantine Empire
            → Third Rome doctrine
  Not recognized as equivalent to Kaiser/Emperor by Western courts
```

### King (Latin: Rex; French: Roi; German: König; Arabic: Malik)

Sovereign lord of a kingdom. Below emperor, above everyone else within the realm.

Key distinctions:
- **Rex Christianissimus** — "Most Christian King" — title of French kings, granted by Pope
- **Fidei Defensor** — "Defender of the Faith" — English kings (ironic: granted to Henry VIII by Pope, kept after break with Rome)
- **Catholic Monarchs** (Reyes Católicos) — Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain
- **Rex Romanorum** — "King of the Romans" — HRE title before imperial coronation

### Archduke (Erzherzog)

Created specifically for the Habsburg dynasty, Austria, 1453.
The Privilegium Maius (forged document, accepted anyway) made the Duke of Austria an Archduke — ranking above all other dukes.
Significance: all Habsburg family members used this title; it became their house title.

---

### Duke (Latin: Dux; French: Duc; German: Herzog; Italian: Duca)

```
ORIGIN: Roman "dux" = military commander
MEDIEVAL USE:
  - Governor of a major territory
  - The most powerful vassals of a king
  - Typically commanded armies, held high justice

GREAT FIEFS OF FRANCE (c. 1000):
  Duchy of Normandy      → Vikings become French lords (911)
  Duchy of Burgundy      → Capetian cadet branch; later Valois
  Duchy of Aquitaine     → Eleanor's inheritance → English claim
  Duchy of Brittany      → Celtic holdout; technically a duchy 1297
  Duchy of Gascony       → Part of Aquitaine complex
  County of Flanders     → Sometimes "duchy"; hugely wealthy
  County of Champagne    → Fair-trading capital of medieval Europe
  Duchy of Anjou         → Plantagenet origin
  County of Toulouse     → Languedoc; Albigensian Crusade 1209

DUCHY ≠ CONTIGUOUS:
  Duke of Lorraine might hold lands scattered across multiple regions
  "The duchy" = all lands held by the duke, wherever they are
```

### Prince / Princess (Latin: Princeps; German: Fürst)

Two entirely different uses:
1. **Royal prince/princess**: member of ruling family; holds title by birth, not territory
2. **Sovereign prince**: rules a principality as its lord; not necessarily royal

The HRE had hundreds of "princes of the empire" (Reichsfürsten) — ecclesiastical and secular lords who answered directly to the emperor, not to intermediate lords.

---

### Marquis / Margrave (German: Markgraf; French: Marquis; Italian: Marchese)

```
ORIGIN: "march" = frontier zone requiring military administration
DUTY: defend the frontier; often given extraordinary powers
      because they couldn't wait for orders from the interior

FAMOUS MARGRAVES:
  Margrave of Brandenburg
    → Started as Nordmark (frontier vs Slavs)
    → Became Electorate of Brandenburg (Golden Bull 1356)
    → House of Hohenzollern takes over 1415
    → Eventually becomes Kingdom of Prussia

  Margrave of Baden
  Margrave of Meissen → became Electorate of Saxony
  Margraviate of Moravia
  March of Tuscany → eventually Florence absorbs it

  In Iberia:
    March of Catalonia (created by Charlemagne vs Moors)
    → County of Barcelona → Crown of Aragon
```

---

### Landgrave (German: Landgraf)

A count with jurisdiction over a "land" — a large territorial unit — rather than a single county. Imperial immediacy (answers to emperor, not to intermediate lord).

**Landgraviate of Thuringia**: The most contested central German territory — where dynastic succession, religious conflict, and strategic geography converged. Passed through the Ludowingians, then to Wettin (the same family as Saxony). Famous as the place Luther would eventually be sheltered (Wartburg Castle).

**Landgraviate of Hesse**: Split from Thuringia 1264. Becomes important in Reformation era.

**Landgrave of Alsace**: The territory that will eventually be disputed between France and Germany for centuries.

---

### Count Palatine (Latin: Comes Palatinus; German: Pfalzgraf)

"Palatine" from "Palatium" — the Palatine Hill, seat of Roman imperial administration. A count exercising royal powers within his territory.

```
COUNT PALATINE OF THE RHINE (Pfalzgraf bei Rhein):
  One of the seven electors (Golden Bull 1356)
  Held: two separated territories — Rhine Palatinate + Upper Palatinate
  The split caused constant problems (see 06-SILESIA-BORDERLANDS.md)

COUNTY PALATINE OF CHESTER (England):
  Bishop of Durham: near-royal powers in northern England
  Count Palatine of Chester: similar in northwest
  These "palatinates" were necessary border defenses vs. Wales/Scotland

KINGDOM OF SICILY as palatinate:
  Roger II of Sicily (Norman) ran it palatine-style
  Frederick II (HRE + King of Sicily) made it the most administratively
  sophisticated state in 13th century Europe
```

---

### Elector (German: Kurfürst)

One of the princes entitled to elect the Holy Roman Emperor.

```
BEFORE GOLDEN BULL 1356:
  Number and identity disputed — various princes claimed electoral status

AFTER GOLDEN BULL 1356 — SEVEN ELECTORS:
  ECCLESIASTICAL:
    Archbishop of Mainz     → Archchancellor of Germany
    Archbishop of Cologne   → Archchancellor of Italy
    Archbishop of Trier     → Archchancellor of Burgundy

  SECULAR:
    King of Bohemia         → Archcupbearer
    Count Palatine of Rhine → Archduke (seneschal)
    Duke of Saxony          → Archmarshall
    Margrave of Brandenburg → Archchamberlain

LATER ADDITIONS:
  Duke of Bavaria (1623 — Palatinate's electoral vote transferred)
  Duke of Hanover (1708)
  (Eventually more added — but by then HRE was dying)

PROCESS: Electors met at Frankfurt to elect; coronation at Aachen
REALITY: Habsburgs held the title almost continuously 1438–1806
          (with one brief interruption: Charles VII, Wittelsbach, 1742–45)
```

### Non-Western Titles

### Huangdi (皇帝 — Emperor of China)

The supreme title of Chinese imperial rule, combining cosmic, administrative, and ritual authority. Adopted by Qin Shi Huang in 221 BCE upon unifying the Warring States — a deliberate break from the Zhou "Wang" system. The character "huang" (august/brilliant) plus "di" (divine ruler/ancestor) signaled a new political ontology: the emperor as son of Heaven (tianzi), mediating between the cosmic order and the human realm.

```
IMPERIAL AUTHORITY STRUCTURE:
  Heaven (Tian) — cosmic order
       ↓ Mandate of Heaven (Tianming)
  Emperor (Huangdi) — son of Heaven, ruler of All under Heaven
       ↓ Bureaucratic delegation
  Ministries → Prefectures → Counties
  (no feudal intermediaries in the mature system)

KEY CONTRAST WITH WESTERN MODEL:
  Western: feudal pyramid, personal loyalty chains
  Chinese: bureaucratic meritocracy (in theory)
           emperor as cosmic pivot, not just military chief
  Examination system (605 CE onward under Sui/Tang):
  Officials recruited by test, not birth → different legitimacy base
```

### Tenno (天皇 — Heavenly Sovereign, Japan)

Japan's imperial title, introduced ~late 7th century CE under Emperor Tenmu to assert parity with China's Huangdi. Literally "heavenly sovereign" — the emperor as earthly manifestation of Amaterasu (the sun goddess). Unique feature: the imperial line never formally changed dynasty, maintaining continuity (in name if not always in power) from legendary antiquity to the present.

```
JAPANESE DUAL-POWER STRUCTURE:
  Tenno (Emperor): sacred legitimacy, ritual functions
       ↓ appointed (increasingly ceremonial)
  Shogun (general): actual military/administrative power
       ↓ from 1192 (Minamoto Yoritomo at Kamakura)
  Daimyo: regional lords → samurai retinues

CONTRAST: The emperor never lost title even when powerless
  Cf. Byzantine emperor: deposed frequently by generals
  Japanese emperor: undeposed, but real power delegated upward
```

### Wang (王 — King, China and Korea)

Below Huangdi in the Chinese system. Princes of the imperial family received Wang titles. Vassal rulers (Korea's Goryeo kings, Vietnamese rulers) used Wang when acknowledging Chinese suzerainty — a deliberate subordination signal. When a ruler declared himself Huangdi rather than Wang, it was an assertion of independence from Chinese suzerainty (e.g., Vietnamese rulers internally styled themselves emperor while acknowledging Chinese Wang status diplomatically).

### Sultan (Arabic: سلطان)

Power, authority. A ruler with practical sovereign power — distinct from religious legitimacy (caliph). The Seljuk Turks adopted sultan c. 1055 when they became protectors of the Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad. The title implied worldly governance; the caliph retained nominal religious authority.

### Caliph (Arabic: Khalīfah — Successor/Deputy)

Successor to the Prophet Muhammad. Combined religious authority (Imam of all Muslims) with political rule. After the Abbasid caliphate collapsed under the Mongols (1258), the title's real meaning dissolved — the Ottomans claimed it after 1517 but this was disputed and mostly symbolic.

### Khan / Khagan (Mongolian/Turkic)

```
Khan:    Ruler of a tribe or territory
Khagan:  Khan of Khans — overarching ruler
         Genghis Khan's formal title: Genghis Khagan
         His successors: Great Khan (the paramount position)

Four ulus (successor khanates):
  Yuan: Great Khan → became Emperor of China
  Chagatai: Khan of Central Asia
  Ilkhanate: Khan of Persia/Middle East
  Golden Horde: Khan of the Eurasian steppe (Russia's overlords)
```

### Emir (Arabic: أمير — Commander, Prince)

A military commander, prince, or governor. Used for:
- Governors of provinces under a caliph
- Independent rulers of small principalities (the **emirates**)
- The petty kings of Iberia after Umayyad fragmentation: **taifa** kingdoms

### Doge (Italian: from Latin Dux)

The elected head of the Venetian and Genoese republics. Powerful in title, increasingly ceremonial in practice (elaborate constraints prevented tyranny). Venice's Doge was elected by an extraordinarily complex multi-round, lottery-and-election process designed to prevent any family from seizing control.

### Voivode (Slavic: Войвода — War Leader)

Prince/governor in Eastern Europe. Used in:
- Wallachia and Moldavia (Romanian principalities)
- Poland: governor of a province (voivodeship)
- Transylvania: Voivode of Transylvania (subordinate to Hungarian crown)

### Jarl (Old Norse)

The top tier of Norse nobility below the king. Regional governors, military commanders. The word becomes "earl" in English (via Scandinavian influence on Anglo-Saxon). By the 11th century in Norway/Denmark, the jarls were being replaced by royal administrators as kings centralized.

### Thegn (Old English)

The Anglo-Saxon equivalent of a knight — military retainer, minor nobleman. Below an ealdorman (who becomes the earl). Holding five hides of land (roughly 600 acres) conferred thegn status.

## Relational Concepts

### Appanage

Land given to younger sons of a royal family — not a separate kingdom, but enough to live on. The appanage holder owes allegiance to his brother the king. Problem: appanages often became de facto hereditary and the holders sought independence.

France: Burgundy as Valois appanage became the greatest threat to the French crown (Charles the Bold, 1460s-70s). The solution after Charles the Bold died (1477) was to absorb the appanage back into the crown — which the French crown could do because Salic law prevented his daughter Mary from inheriting the French portion of Burgundy.

### Demesne (Domain)

The land a lord directly administers and profits from — as opposed to land subinfeudated to vassals. The royal demesne = the king's own estates, providing direct income.

```
Royal revenue sources ranked by reliability:
1. Royal demesne income (most reliable — direct control)
2. Feudal aids (occasional, regulated)
3. Scutage (negotiated)
4. Taxation (requires consent — parliament, estates general)
5. Extraordinary levies (dangerous — rebellion risk)
```

### Fief (Latin: Feodum, Feudum)

Any grant of land, office, revenue, or rights given in exchange for homage and service. A fief is NOT the land itself — it's the bundle of rights over the land. You can have a fief consisting of: a castle, the revenues from three villages, the right to collect tolls on a road, and the obligation to provide two knights.

### Allodium (Allodial Land)

Land held with full ownership and no overlord. No homage owed. No service owed. A true owner. Increasingly rare as feudal sub-infeudation spread. The Swiss Confederation maintained considerable allodial landholding. Norwegian odal (odel) rights were similar. After the French Revolution, all land in France became allodial in principle.

### Suzerainty

The relationship of a suzerain (overlord) to a vassal state or lord. The vassal may run its own affairs internally but owes homage, may not conduct independent foreign policy, and must provide military support.

### Escheat

The return of a fief to the lord when the vassal dies without heirs or commits treason (attainder). In theory, automatic. In practice, had to be enforced militarily. English kings used attainder extensively against noble families (particularly after the Wars of the Roses) to confiscate estates and redistribute them to loyalists.

### Sub-infeudation vs Substitution

```
SUB-INFEUDATION (before Quia Emptores 1290):
  A holds from King
  A grants sub-fief to B
  B is A's vassal; King has no direct claim on B
  B can grant sub-sub-fief to C
  → Chains of obligation dilute royal authority

SUBSTITUTION (after Quia Emptores 1290, England only):
  A holds from King
  A sells/grants to B
  B now holds DIRECTLY from King, in A's place
  A drops out of the chain
  → Prevented further fragmentation in England
  → Not adopted in France/HRE → continued fragmentation
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Earl = Count**
In England, the title count never caught on — the Anglo-Scandinavian "earl" persisted. The wife of an earl is a "countess" (from Latin comitissa). The earl's county is his "earldom." The word "earl" and "jarl" are the same word.

**Duke of Normandy ≠ King of England**
William the Conqueror was Duke of Normandy (vassal of France) AND King of England (sovereign). He did homage to the French king for Normandy. He owed the French king nothing for England. This created endless complications.

**Elector ≠ just a voter**
The seven electors were the most powerful princes in the HRE — their territories had special privileges, their persons were protected by the Golden Bull, and their inheritance rules were codified to prevent fragmentation. Being an elector was the highest secular rank below emperor.

**Margrave = Marquis = March Count**
Same title, different language. English "marquess" from French "marquis" from Frankish "marchio" from "marca" (march/frontier). All mean the same thing.

**"Prince" in medieval context**
Be careful: a 13th-century document calling someone a "prince" might mean: (1) a royal son, (2) a sovereign ruler, (3) any great noble (general usage). Context determines meaning.
