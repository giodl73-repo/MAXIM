# The Byzantine Empire — Eastern Rome, 330–1453

## Self-Understanding and Modern Misconceptions

The Byzantines did not call themselves Byzantine. They called themselves:
- **Rhomaioi** (Ῥωμαῖοι) — Romans
- Their empire: **Βασιλεία τῶν Ῥωμαίων** (Basileia tōn Rhomaiōn) — Kingdom of the Romans
- Their capital: Constantinople = New Rome

"Byzantine" is a historiographic term coined in 1557 by Hieronymus Wolf, using the old name for Constantinople (Byzantion/Byzantium). The Byzantines would find it bizarre.

```
THE CONTINUITY ARGUMENT:
Roman Empire (27 BCE)
       ↓
Tetrarchy (3rd century)
       ↓
Constantine moves capital to Constantinople (330 CE)
       ↓
Western Empire "falls" 476 CE — but the Eastern half
continues unbroken. From Constantinople's perspective:
Rome fell; we did not. We ARE Rome.
       ↓
Byzantine Empire (the Eastern Roman Empire)
continues until 1453 CE
TOTAL DURATION: ~1,123 years from Constantine, ~1,480 from Augustus
```

## The Administrative System — Themes

The theme system (θέματα, themata) was the key Byzantine military-administrative innovation.

```
EARLIER SYSTEM (Justinian era):
  Separate: military commanders (dukes) and civilian governors (praefects)
  Problem: barbarian invasions required coordinated military-civilian response
           The separation was too slow

THEME SYSTEM (7th century under Heraclius → systematized under Constans II):
  Theme = large military district
  Strategos (general): commands BOTH military AND civilian administration
  Local soldiers: settled in the theme, receive land grants,
                  provide military service → self-sustaining
  No feudal sub-infeudation: soldiers are imperial soldiers,
                              not the general's private vassals

THEMES AT PEAK (~900 CE):
  Anatolikon (central Anatolia)
  Armeniakon (northeast Anatolia)
  Thrakesion (western Anatolia)
  Opsikion (northwest Anatolia, near Constantinople)
  Macedonian (northern Balkans/Thrace)
  Hellas (Greece)
  Peloponnesos
  ... and more subdivisions as territory grew/shrank

EVOLUTION: Themes were subdivided over time.
  Large themes → smaller themes → new smaller unit (kleisoura, then tourma)
  By the 11th century: dozens of small themes + large military commands
  System broke down with Seljuk conquest of Anatolia (1071)
  → Lost the theme lands that provided soldiers
  → Had to rely on mercenaries → expensive → fiscal crisis
```

## The Dynasties and Their Arcs

### Heraclian Dynasty (610–711)

**Heraclius** (r. 610–641): One of the most consequential emperors.
- Took power in a coup; empire on the verge of collapse (Avars in Balkans, Persians at the Bosphorus)
- Reorganized the army on theme model
- Launched Persian wars: brilliant campaign 622–628; destroyed Persian heartland; recovered Holy Cross from Jerusalem
- Then: Islamic Arab explosion. Heraclius lost Syria, Palestine, Egypt in his own lifetime.
- This was the permanent loss — those territories never returned.

### Isaurian Dynasty (717–802)

**Leo III** (r. 717–741): Defended Constantinople against Arab siege 717–718. Launched Iconoclasm — destruction of religious images. This triggered 120 years of theological war within the empire and alienated Rome permanently.

### Macedonian Dynasty (867–1056) — The Golden Age

```
THE MACEDONIAN GOLDEN AGE:
  Basil I (867–886): peasant from Macedonia; murdered his way to power
  Leo VI "the Wise" (886–912): prolific legislator; Basilika (legal code)
  Constantine VII Porphyrogennetos (913–959): scholar-emperor;
    wrote De Administrando Imperio (how to run the empire) —
    invaluable historical source
  Nikephoros II Phokas (963–969): military reconquest (Cilicia, Cyprus)
  John I Tzimiskes (969–976): military emperor; reconquered northern Syria
  Basil II "Bulgaroktonos" (Bulgar-slayer) (976–1025):
    THE peak. Destroyed Bulgarian Empire; blinded 15,000 Bulgarian soldiers
    (kept one eye per hundred men so they could find their way home)
    Tsar Samuel died of shock seeing his blinded army return
    Empire at maximum territorial extent under Basil II

CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS:
  Cyril and Methodius (missionaries sent to Moravia 863):
    Created Glagolitic script → Cyrillic alphabet for Slavic languages
    Converted Moravia, Bulgaria, eventually Russia to Orthodox Christianity
    The Byzantine cultural orbit (second empire of sorts)

  Byzantine art: mosaic tradition, icon painting
  Universities: Magnaura school in Constantinople
  Greek fire: naval secret weapon (exact composition lost)
```

### The Doukid/Komnenian Collapse and Recovery

**Battle of Manzikert, 1071:** The hinge of Byzantine history.

```
BEFORE MANZIKERT:
  Byzantine Anatolia = heartland of the empire
  Provides most of the soldiers, most of the agricultural revenue
  Theme system sustained by Anatolian peasant-soldiers

MANZIKERT:
  Romanos IV Diogenes (emperor) vs Alp Arslan (Seljuk Sultan)
  Near Lake Van in eastern Anatolia
  Romanos captured — unprecedented
  Terms: humiliating ransom; cession of frontier cities
  BUT: while Romanos was in captivity, his enemies crowned a rival emperor
  Romanos released → civil war → killed
  CONSEQUENCE:
  Seljuk Turks flood into Anatolia
  Within 10 years: most of Anatolia lost
  Theme system collapse → lost most soldier-land
  Constantinople now a coastal city surrounded by Seljuk territory

KOMNENIAN RECOVERY:
  Alexios I Komnenos (1081–1118):
    First Komnenian emperor; stabilized the collapse
    Appealed to Pope Urban II for mercenary knights → First Crusade (1095)
    (He wanted mercenaries; he got an independent crusading army instead)
    Made territorial gains against Seljuks with crusader help

  John II Komnenos (1118–1143): consistent military campaigner
  Manuel I Komnenos (1143–1180): brilliant but over-extended
    Attempted to reconquer Italy; failed at Myriokephalon 1176 (vs Seljuks)
    Made too many concessions to Venetians (commercial privileges)
```

### The Angelid Collapse and Latin Empire

**Isaac II Angelos and Alexios III Angelos:** Two disastrous emperors.

**Fourth Crusade and the Latin Empire (1204):**

```
FOURTH CRUSADE PLAN: Attack Egypt (most valuable Islamic territory)
ACTUAL RESULT: Sack of Constantinople

HOW IT HAPPENED:
  Crusaders owed Venice enormous debt for transport ships
  Could not pay
  Venice (Doge Enrico Dandolo, blind, 90+ years old) offered deal:
    Help us retake Zara (Christian city in Dalmatia) → payment deferred
  Pope Innocent III was furious: crusaders sacked a Christian city
  Then: Alexios (Byzantine prince) offered: help me retake throne
    → I'll pay your debt, reunite Churches, supply Crusade

  Crusaders installed Alexios IV: he couldn't pay
  Greeks overthrew him → new emperor (Alexios V Doukas "Mourtzouphlos")
  Crusaders: "We've been cheated. Let's take Constantinople."
  April 12–13, 1204: Constantinople sacked by the Latin Crusade
  Three-day sack; priceless treasures looted
  Venice got the best of the loot (Four Horses of St. Mark → Venice)

LATIN EMPIRE (1204–1261):
  Baldwin of Flanders: first Latin Emperor
  Venice got Crete + key trading posts
  Other crusaders divided Greece into fiefdoms (Achaea, Athens, etc.)
  Rump Byzantine successor states:
    Empire of Nicaea (strongest — eventually reconquered Constantinople)
    Empire of Trebizond (Black Sea coast)
    Despotate of Epirus (northwest Greece)
```

### Palaiologan Twilight (1261–1453)

**Michael VIII Palaiologos:** Reconquered Constantinople 1261. Founded last dynasty.

```
PALAIOLOGAN DECLINE:
  Territory: reduced to Constantinople + Thrace + parts of Greece
  Economy: Venetians and Genoese controlled all trade
  Civil wars: continuous dynastic conflict sapped remaining resources
  John VI Kantakouzenos (usurper): invited Ottoman forces as allies 1345
    → Ottomans got their first European foothold (Gallipoli)
    → A decision that condemned Constantinople

  Manuel II Palaiologos: his lecture tour of Western Europe 1400-1403
    → begged for help; received sympathy, no armies
    → wrote theological dialogues about Islam that Pope Benedict XVI
       quoted controversially in 2006

FALL OF CONSTANTINOPLE (May 29, 1453):
  Mehmed II (Ottoman) vs Constantine XI Palaiologos (last emperor)
  75,000+ Ottoman army vs ~7,000 defenders
  Mehmed had huge cannon (Urban's bombard) that breached the walls
  Final assault: Genoese captain Giovanni Giustiniani wounded, retreated
                 Morale collapse; Ottomans broke through
  Constantine XI died fighting — body never found with certainty
  Mehmed converted Hagia Sophia to mosque (now museum/mosque again)
  END OF THE ROMAN EMPIRE

SUCCESSOR STATES (post-1261):
  Despotate of the Morea (Peloponnese): Palaiologan princes; fell 1460
  Empire of Trebizond: on Black Sea; fell 1461
  These were the last Byzantine successor states
```

## The Legacy

```
BYZANTINE LEGACY:

ORTHODOX CHRISTIANITY:
  Eastern Orthodox Church = Byzantine cultural export
  Russia, Bulgaria, Serbia, Romania, Greece, Armenia
  All use Cyrillic alphabet or Orthodox liturgy (Byzantine products)

LEGAL TRADITION:
  Corpus Juris Civilis (Justinian's code, 529–534):
    The greatest legal achievement of the ancient world
    Survived through Constantinople; spread West (Bologna law school 11th c.)
    Basis of civil law tradition (most of Europe, Latin America, Quebec)
    COMPARE TO COMMON LAW:
    Corpus Juris: judge interprets statute/code
    Common Law (English): judge follows precedent
    This split traces to Byzantine survival of Roman law

ART AND ARCHITECTURE:
  Hagia Sophia (built 532–537): engineering marvel; dome on pendentives
  Icon painting tradition → Russian, Greek, Serbian art
  Mosaic tradition → Ravenna, Venice, Palermo

THIRD ROME:
  Moscow declared Third Rome after 1453
  Ivan III married Sophia Palaiologina (niece of last Byzantine emperor)
  → Russia claimed Byzantine imperial legacy
  → Tsar (Caesar) → Russian Orthodox Church claims Patriarchal status

SCIENTIFIC PRESERVATION:
  Classical Greek texts preserved and copied in Constantinople
  Exodus of Greek scholars after 1453 → Italian Renaissance fed by
  Byzantine manuscript traditions
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Byzantine" bureaucracy isn't just pejorative**
The Byzantine civil service was genuinely sophisticated — professional, literate, record-keeping. Much more advanced than Western feudal courts. The "Byzantine = complex/devious" usage is unfair — they were administering a major empire for 1,100 years, which requires complexity.

**The Crusades were partly invited**
Alexios I asked the Pope for military help against the Seljuks. He got something far more dangerous: independent crusading armies that plundered their way through his territory, carved out their own states, and eventually sacked his capital. He was not grateful.

**Hagia Sophia's engineering**
The dome sits on pendentives (triangular concave sections) rather than a drum — this was the innovation that allowed a circular dome on a square base. The dome is smaller than the Pantheon's but far more structurally complex. It stood for 916 years without a major collapse (minor collapses and reconstructions).

**The "Great Schism" of 1054 was not sudden**
The formal split between Catholic and Orthodox Christianity was preceded by centuries of theological and political drift. The 1054 exchange of excommunications was the formal crystallization of a divide that had been building since at least the Iconoclasm controversy. The Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople (1204) did far more to make reunion psychologically impossible.
