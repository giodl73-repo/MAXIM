## Pre-State Scandinavia — The Thing System

Before kings consolidated power, Norse society ran on the **þing** (thing) — an assembly.

```
THING HIERARCHY:

  Fjordung-þing   (local assembly — settlement level)
       ↑
  Fylki-þing      (regional assembly — "fylki" = province)
       ↑
  Løgþing / Althing  (the supreme assembly for a country or territory)

WHAT THINGS DID:
  - Resolved disputes (no professional judges — respected men judged)
  - Declared law (oral tradition, later written)
  - Decided outlawry (exile from the community — serious punishment)
  - Could elect/approve kings (legitimation function)
  - Divided inheritances
  - Declared feuds

THING VENUE:
  Usually at a sacred/traditional site
  All free men could attend
  Armed men attended (kept swords sheathed)
  Lasted several days; market fair often attached
```

**Iceland's Althing (930 CE):** The oldest parliament in continuous (though interrupted) existence. All Iceland's free landowners met at Þingvellir annually. The Lawspeaker (lögsögumaður) recited law from memory — no written code until 1117. The Althing settled disputes, declared law, and managed the Commonwealth.

## Norse Society and Titles

```
JARLS (earls):
  The top tier of Norse nobility
  Regional governors/warlords
  Word becomes "earl" in English (Scandinavian influence)
  Great jarls: Earls of Lade (Norway); Earls of Orkney; Earls of Northumbria

KARLS:
  Free farmers — the backbone of Norse society
  Armed, voting at the þing, owed no labor service
  Unlike Western serfs: Norse farmers were free

THRALLS:
  Slaves — captured in raids or born into slavery
  Could be freed (become "freedman" — leysingi)
  Norse slavery was widespread but not the plantation model

SKALD:
  Court poet — highly valued; composed intricate verse forms
  (dróttkvætt — complex meter with internal rhyme, alliteration)
  Preserved histories as oral tradition

BERSERKER:
  Elite warriors who entered a battle trance
  "Berserk" = bear shirt (wore bear/wolf skins?)
  Whether this involved mushrooms, psychology, or training: debated
```

## The Three Kingdoms

### Norway

**Unification:** Harald Fairhair (r. ~872–930) won Battle of Hafrsfjord (~872) — traditionally the unification of Norway. Opponents fled: many went to Iceland, Orkney, Faroe Islands.

**Key Jarls of Lade:** After Harald, Norway was often effectively ruled by the jarls of Lade (in Trondheim) while kings were nominal or contested.

**Olaf Haraldsson (St. Olaf, r. 1015–1028):** Consolidated Norway, completed Christianization. Killed at Battle of Stiklestad 1028. Venerated as Norway's patron saint almost immediately — political martyrdom and genuine popular devotion.

**Cnut of Denmark** conquered Norway 1028; Olaf died fighting him.

**Harald Hardrada (Hard-ruler, r. 1046–1066):** Last great Viking king. Fought at Stamford Bridge 1066 — killed by Harold Godwinson. His defeat marks the conventional end of the Viking age.

### Denmark

The most organized of the three kingdoms. Control of the Jutland Peninsula + the strait passages (Øresund) between the North Sea and Baltic gave Denmark enormous strategic and economic leverage.

**Harald Bluetooth** (r. ~958–986): Christianized Denmark. The Bluetooth wireless standard is named for him (his rune initials form the Bluetooth logo).

**Sweyn Forkbeard** (r. 986–1014): Conquered England 1013; first Danish king of England. Died 1014 after a 5-week reign.

**Cnut the Great** (r. 1016–1035):
```
CNUT'S REALM:
  King of England: 1016–1035
  King of Denmark: 1018–1035
  King of Norway: 1028–1035
  Overlord of parts of Sweden

GOVERNING ENGLAND:
  Kept Scandinavian elite but adapted to English institutions
  Divided England into earldoms (Godwin of Wessex; Siward of Northumbria)
  Respected the Church; went to Rome 1027
  Issued law codes in the English tradition

THE CANUTE/CNUT TIDE STORY:
  Apocryphal — he commanded the tide to stop to demonstrate to
  flatterers that no king can command nature.
  Original point: his piety. Later re-told as arrogance.

SUCCESSION CRISIS:
  Cnut's sons Harald Harefoot and Harthacnut die without heirs
  → Aethelred's son Edward the Confessor restored (1042)
  → Sets up the 1066 succession crisis
```

### Sweden

Sweden unified more slowly than Norway and Denmark. Regional kings competed in Svealand (Uppsala area) and Götaland (south).

**Eric IX the Saint** (r. ~1156–1160): Patron saint of Sweden. Led crusade into Finland — beginning Swedish expansion east.

**Birger Jarl** (~1248–1266): The real power behind the throne; founded Stockholm; began Swedish consolidation. His son became King Valdemar.

The **Folkunga dynasty** ruled Sweden 1250–1363, consolidating royal power and Swedish identity.

## The Orkney Earldom — Norse Persistence in Britain

The Earldom of Orkney is a textbook case of Norse institutional persistence outlasting political control — Norse law, language, and landholding survived transfer of sovereignty by centuries.

```
HISTORY:
  Harald Fairhair (Norway) claimed Orkney ~875 — suppressed piracy base
  Jarls of Orkney: Norwegian vassals nominally
  Key earls: Sigurd the Stout (died Clontarf 1014); Thorfinn the Mighty

  NORWEGIAN SOVEREIGNTY → SCOTTISH:
  1468: Christian I of Norway (also Denmark — Kalmar Union) pledged
        Orkney as security for dowry of Margaret of Denmark
        (who married James III of Scotland)
  The dowry was never paid.
  James III annexed Orkney + Shetland 1472.
  → Scotland keeps them.

  Norse dialect (Norn) spoken in Orkney until ~18th century.
  Place names still overwhelmingly Norse (Kirkwall, Stromness, etc.)
```

## Norman Conquest as Viking Legacy

The Normans who conquered England in 1066 were the descendants of Vikings (Northmen → Norman). Rollo's descendants had:
- Adopted French language (completely, within a generation or two)
- Adopted Christianity
- Adopted feudal structure
- But retained some administrative habits and martial culture

```
ROLLO (Viking) → Robert I of Normandy → William the Conqueror
(Treaty of St-Clair-sur-Epte 911)

WHAT THE NORMANS SPREAD:
  England (1066): William the Conqueror
  Southern Italy / Sicily (1060s-1090s): Robert Guiscard + Roger I
  Antioch (1098): Bohemond (Robert Guiscard's son) — Crusader state
  Ireland (1169): Anglo-Norman lords under Henry II

Norman architecture: round arches, massive piers, fortress-cathedrals
  Durham Cathedral, Caen churches, Palermo's Cappella Palatina
```

## Iceland's Commonwealth (930–1262)

The most complete medieval experiment in stateless governance.

```
CONSTITUTION (Grágás — "Grey Goose" law code):
  No executive: no king, no jarl, no standing army
  Althing: legislative + judicial + dispute resolution
  Lawspeaker: recites law; chairs proceedings
  Goðar (singular: goði): chieftains with attached households
    → Not territorial lords — people chose which goði to follow
    → Could switch goðar (a form of political freedom)

DISPUTES:
  Resolved by outlawry (útlægr) or settlement (agreement + silver)
  Full outlawry = anyone can kill you without penalty
  Partial outlawry = exile for 3 years
  Feuds: blood feud was normal and expected; honor culture
  The sagas document these feuds in extraordinary detail

COLLAPSE:
  13th century: great families (Sturlungar, Haukdælar, etc.)
  accumulated power; goðar became territorial lords de facto
  Sturlurekar (Sturlung Age): violent civil conflict 1220s-1262
  1262: Iceland submits to Haakon IV of Norway
  → End of the Commonwealth
```

## Kalmar Union (1397–1523)

All three Scandinavian kingdoms under one crown. The attempt to make the Norse world permanent — and why it failed.

```
BACKGROUND:
  Denmark dominant; Norway already under Danish influence
  Sweden contested but drawn in
  Margaret I of Denmark: the architect

MARGARET I (r. effectively 1375–1412):
  Never formally queen (women couldn't hold Scandinavian thrones)
  Regent for her son Olaf (died 1387), then for her great-nephew Eric
  De facto ruler of all three kingdoms
  Coronation at Kalmar 1397: Eric of Pomerania crowned king of all three

KALMAR UNION STRUCTURE:
  One king ruling Denmark, Norway, Sweden
  Each kingdom kept separate law, nobility, institutions
  Foreign policy and war coordinated
  → Not a unification — a personal union

SWEDISH RESISTANCE:
  Swedish nobility repeatedly rebelled against Danish domination
  Engelbrekt Engelbrektsson rebellion 1434–1436 (peasant-backed)
  Sten Sture the Elder: "regent" of Sweden, effectively independent 1471
  Battle of Brunkeberg 1471: Swedish forces beat Danish army outside Stockholm

BREAKUP:
  Stockholm Bloodbath 1520: Christian II of Denmark massacred
  Swedish nobility + clergy who had resisted (80+ people)
  Gustav Vasa: led Swedish rebellion; elected king 1523
  → Kalmar Union dissolved; Sweden independent
  Denmark-Norway union continued until 1814
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Viking" ≠ a people**
"Viking" describes an activity: going on a sea-raid (víking). Not all Norsemen were Vikings; not all raids were Vikings. Most Norsemen were farmers. The raiding/trading/settling populations are properly called Norse or Scandinavian.

**Varangians = Eastern Vikings**
The eastern traders/settlers are usually called Varangians (derived from the word for sworn companions). These are primarily Swedes, though other Norse joined. They are the founders of Kievan Rus — see 10-RUSSIA-RUTHENIAN.md.

**Norse religion wasn't "Viking religion"**
Ásatrú (the Norse gods: Odin, Thor, Freyr, etc.) was the religion of Germanic peoples broadly — Anglo-Saxons, Goths, etc. had related pantheons. Christianization happened at different times (Norway ~1000; Iceland at Althing 1000 by compromise; Sweden slower).

**Danelaw**
The Danelaw was not a separate kingdom — it was a region of England where Danish customs, law, and land tenure prevailed after settlement. It included roughly everything north and east of the line Watling Street. York (Jorvik) was its major city. Absorbing the Danelaw into a unified England took Alfred's dynasty a century.
