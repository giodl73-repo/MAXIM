# Holy Roman Empire — The Bewildering Fragmentation

## The Famous Quip and What It Actually Means

Voltaire: "This agglomeration which was called and which still calls itself the Holy Roman Empire was neither holy, nor Roman, nor an empire."

```
NOT HOLY:
  The Pope crowned the emperor, but popes and emperors spent
  centuries fighting each other (Investiture Controversy, Guelphs/
  Ghibellines, Frederick II vs multiple popes).
  "Holy" referred to the concept of a divinely sanctioned
  Christian universal monarchy — the emperor as secular protector
  of Christendom. In practice: anything but harmonious.

NOT ROMAN:
  It was German. Based in German-speaking lands.
  The "Roman" claim derived from Charlemagne's coronation 800 CE
  by Pope Leo III — who was trying to create a Western counterpart
  to the Byzantine (actual Roman) Empire.
  Emperors went to Rome for coronation, but ruled from Frankfurt,
  Aachen, Prague, Vienna, etc.

NOT AN EMPIRE:
  An empire implies a single sovereign power over a territory.
  The HRE was a confederation of princes who elected a chairman.
  The emperor couldn't tax them directly, couldn't legislate
  without their consent, couldn't raise an army without their
  cooperation.

WHAT IT ACTUALLY WAS:
  A legal framework preserving the fiction of universal Christian
  monarchy, within which hundreds of entities maintained autonomy
  while occasionally cooperating against external threats.
```

## The Dynasties

### Ottonian Dynasty (919–1024)

**Henry I the Fowler** (r. 919–936): First Saxon king. Established the Ottonian dynasty. Defended against Magyar (Hungarian) raids by building fortified towns (Burgwarden).

**Otto I the Great** (r. 936–973): The key figure. Won Battle of Lechfeld 955 against Magyars — ended the Hungarian raids that had plagued Europe for 60 years. Intervened in Italy. **Pope John XII crowned him emperor on Feb 2, 962 CE** — this is the conventional founding date of the HRE.

Otto I established the **Ottonian system**: appointing bishops and abbots as royal administrators. Clergy couldn't pass fiefs to children (celibacy), so ecclesiastical lords' territories reverted to royal control for re-appointment. This gave the king a powerful tool — and planted the seed of the Investiture Crisis.

**Otto III** (r. 996–1002): Dream of restoring Rome as imperial capital. Made Gerbert of Aurillac (greatest scholar of the era) Pope Sylvester II. Died at 21 before the project could materialize.

### Salian Dynasty (1024–1125)

**Henry III** (r. 1039–1056): Strongest Salian emperor. Appointed three popes in succession. The imperial papacy concept at its peak.

**Henry IV** (r. 1056–1106): The Investiture Controversy.

```
TIMELINE OF HENRY IV vs GREGORY VII:

  1073: Gregory VII becomes Pope; launches Gregorian Reform
  1075: Gregory bans lay investiture (emperor appointing bishops)
  1076: Henry IV convenes council at Worms; "deposes" Gregory
        Gregory excommunicates Henry; releases his vassals from oaths
  1077: Walk to Canossa
        Henry stands barefoot in snow for 3 days outside the castle
        Gregory must lift excommunication (canonical obligation)
        Henry survives; vassals return
        → Gregory had not really won
  1080: Henry IV drives Gregory out of Rome; Gregory dies in exile
  1122: Concordat of Worms (under Henry V): the compromise
        (see 01-FEUDAL-MECHANICS.md for details)
```

### Hohenstaufen Dynasty (1138–1254)

The most dramatic dynasty — brilliant emperors, catastrophic finale.

**Frederick I Barbarossa** (r. 1155–1190):
- Crowned emperor by Pope Adrian IV
- Repeatedly invaded Italy to assert imperial control over Lombard cities
- Lombard League (formed 1167) defeated him at Legnano 1176
- Peace of Constance 1183: cities get autonomy; swear nominal fealty
- Drowned crossing a river in Anatolia on the Third Crusade

**Henry VI** (r. 1190–1197):
- Married Constance, heiress of Sicily — combined empire with Sicily
- Held Richard I of England for ransom (Richard's ransom = 150,000 marks)
- Died at 32; left infant son Frederick

**Frederick II** (r. 1220–1250): "Stupor Mundi" — Wonder of the World
```
WHAT MADE HIM EXTRAORDINARY:
  - Born in Sicily; raised there; native Arabic, German, French, Latin, Italian, Greek
  - Built Castel del Monte (Sicily) — geometric perfection, no clear military purpose
  - Wrote poetry in Italian (predates Dante)
  - Wrote scientific treatise on falconry (De Arte Venandi cum Avibus)
  - Negotiated Jerusalem via diplomacy (Sixth Crusade 1229):
    Treaty with al-Kamil (Ayyubid sultan) — Christians get Jerusalem
    without fighting — Pope furious (it was too rational)
  - Fought three popes (Gregory IX twice, Innocent IV)
  - Excommunicated multiple times
  - Created the most sophisticated royal bureaucracy in 13th century Europe
    in Sicily (centralized, salaried officials, no feudal intermediaries)

HOHENSTAUFEN COLLAPSE:
  Frederick II died 1250
  Son Conrad IV died 1254
  Grandson Conradin executed 1268 by Charles of Anjou
  → Hohenstaufen extinct in male line
  → The Great Interregnum (1254-1273): no effective emperor
  → Princes used the vacuum to grab powers permanently
```

## The Golden Bull (1356) — The Constitutional Settlement

After the interregnum, Habsburgs, Luxembourgs, and Wittelsbachs competed for the imperial title. The Golden Bull (so named for its golden seal) imposed order on the chaos.

**Issued by:** Charles IV (Luxembourg), King of Bohemia and Holy Roman Emperor.

**Core provisions:**

```
1. SEVEN ELECTORS FIXED:
   Archbishop of Mainz, Archbishop of Cologne, Archbishop of Trier
   King of Bohemia, Count Palatine of Rhine, Duke of Saxony,
   Margrave of Brandenburg
   → Their territories and titles were indivisible and passed by primogeniture
   → Protected against fragmentation that was destroying other territories

2. ELECTION PROCESS:
   After emperor's death, Archbishop of Mainz summons electors
   They meet at Frankfurt within one month
   They have 30 days to elect; if they fail, they fast on bread and water
   Simple majority suffices (ended requirement for unanimity)

3. ELECTORAL TERRITORIES PRIVILEGED:
   Electors could not have their territories seized for debts
   Their children had precedence at imperial court
   First-born son inherits entire electorate (no splitting)
   Electors could levy tolls, coin money, hold Jewish communities

4. PAPAL ROLE: ELIMINATED
   The Golden Bull made no mention of Papal confirmation
   → Election alone made a king-emperor
   → Emperor did not need coronation in Rome (though it continued)
```

## The Habsburg Accumulation

The Habsburgs turned the elective empire into a de facto hereditary one through strategic marriages and outlasting competitors.

```
HABSBURG TITLE ACCUMULATION:

  Rudolf of Habsburg elected emperor 1273
  → Defeats Ottokar II of Bohemia 1278; takes AUSTRIA
  (Austria = the foundation of all future Habsburg power)

  Albert II: CARINTHIA, CARNIOLA, STYRIA added
  (Alpine territories — defensible, wealthy)

  Rudolf IV the Founder (1358–1365):
    Forges Privilegium Maius — fake document granting Austria
    special status, new title "Archduke"
    → Charles IV (emperor) called it a forgery
    → Frederick III (emperor, himself a Habsburg) authenticated it 1453
    → Habsburgs are Archdukes from that point

  MAXIMILIAN I (r. 1493–1519):
    Marriage policy par excellence:
    "Bella gerant alii, tu felix Austria nube"
    ("Let others wage war; thou, happy Austria, marry")

    Maximilian marries Mary of Burgundy (1477):
    → Burgundian Netherlands pass to Habsburgs
    (Charles the Bold died leaving only a daughter)

    Maximilian arranges marriages for grandchildren:
    → Philip the Handsome + Juana of Castile/Aragon
    → Their son becomes Charles V:
       King of Spain (incl. New World) 1516
       Holy Roman Emperor 1519
       Duke of Burgundy + Netherlands
       Archduke of Austria
       = largest realm in European history to that point
```

## The Fragmentation Map — Why It Looks Like That

```
HRE ENTITIES COUNT (approximate):
  ~1200: ~300 entities with varying autonomy
  ~1500: ~350 entities
  ~1800: ~300 entities (Westphalia rationalized some)
         (then Napoleon dissolved it 1806)

CATEGORIES:
  Ecclesiastical principalities:
    3 electoral archbishoprics: Mainz, Cologne, Trier
    ~50 prince-bishoprics: Bamberg, Würzburg, Speyer, Worms...
    ~70 imperial abbeys: Fulda, Reichenau, Corvey...

  Secular principalities:
    Electoral: Brandenburg, Saxony, Palatinate, Bohemia, Bavaria
    Non-electoral duchies: dozens (Austria, Lorraine, etc.)
    Landgraviates: Thuringia, Hesse, Alsace
    Counties: hundreds (Tyrol, Nassau, Württemberg, Flanders...)
    Lordships: even smaller

  Imperial Cities (Reichsstädte):
    ~65 cities with direct imperial relationship
    Frankfurt, Augsburg, Nuremberg, Hamburg, Lübeck, Cologne, etc.
    Self-governing; their own laws; no feudal lord between them and emperor

WHY SO FRAGMENTED:
  1. No centralized enforcement → princes kept what they had
  2. Partible inheritance (gavelkind) in many regions
  3. Every generation of lords could and did sub-divide
  4. Church territories couldn't consolidate (celibacy)
  5. No common law (unlike England's common law)
  6. Elective emperors couldn't build up hereditary royal demesne
```

## The Imperial Circles (Reichskreise) — 1500 Reform

Maximilian I's reform: 10 regional circles for defense and taxation coordination.

```
IMPERIAL CIRCLES (1512 organization):
  Austrian Circle        → Habsburg core lands
  Burgundian Circle      → Low Countries (Habsburg)
  Electoral Rhine Circle → Electoral territories
  Franconian Circle      → Nuremberg, Bamberg, Ansbach
  Bavarian Circle        → Bavaria, Salzburg
  Swabian Circle         → Württemberg, many cities
  Upper Rhine Circle     → Alsace, Baden, Palatinate area
  Lower Saxon Circle     → Brunswick, Mecklenburg
  Westphalian Circle     → Cologne, Minden, Münster
  Upper Saxon Circle     → Saxony, Brandenburg, Pomerania

  NOT IN ANY CIRCLE: Bohemia, the Austrian-Habsburg domains
                     (too important to be just one part of something)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Germany" didn't exist**
The HRE was not Germany. It included German-speaking lands, yes, but also: the Kingdom of Bohemia (Czech), the Kingdom of Burgundy (French-speaking), northern Italy, the Low Countries, and at times much more. "Germany" as a concept emerged slowly; as a state, it dates to 1871.

**The emperor wasn't the HRE's "head"**
The emperor was more like a president with very limited powers. The Reichstag (Diet) of princes, counts, and cities had to consent to major decisions. The emperor could influence but not command.

**The Guelphs and Ghibellines**
Originally: Welf (Guelph) = German dynasty supporting papal authority; Waiblingen (Ghibelline) = Hohenstaufen dynasty supporting imperial authority. These became faction labels in Italian cities where pro-Pope vs pro-Emperor mapped onto local power struggles — long after the original families' fights had ended. In Florence, Dante was exiled by the Guelph faction.

**The HRE and modern Germany**
The HRE's eastern expansion (Ostsiedlung — colonization of Slavic lands) created the German-speaking presence in Silesia, Pomerania, Prussia, and the Baltic. The borders of these expansions shaped conflicts that lasted until 1945.
