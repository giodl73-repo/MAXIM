## The Aztec Triple Alliance — Mesoamerican Empire

### Foundation: Mexica Origins

```
MEXICA (AZTEC) ORIGIN STORY:
  From Aztlan (mythical northern homeland — location debated)
  Wandered for generations; directed by god Huitzilopochtli
  Arrived in Valley of Mexico ~1250 CE
  Problem: all good land taken by established city-states (Tepanec,
    Acolhua, Culhua, etc.)
  Mexica: mercenary soldiers for hire; took the worst land
  1325 (traditional): founded Tenochtitlan on an island in Lake Texcoco
    WHERE THEY SAW THE OMEN: eagle on a cactus eating a snake
    (Now on the Mexican flag)
  Island: brilliant defensive position; expandable via chinampas

CHINAMPA AGRICULTURE:
  "Floating gardens" — not actually floating
  Built by layering lake mud, vegetation, and soil
  Criss-crossed with canals; hugely productive
  Two to three crops per year
  Fed a city that grew to 200,000–300,000 people
  (Larger than any European city of the period)
```

### The Triple Alliance (1428)

```
TRIPLE ALLIANCE STRUCTURE:
  Tenochtitlan (Mexica) — DOMINANT partner
  Texcoco (Acolhua) — senior scholarly/judicial partner
  Tlacopan (Tepanec faction) — junior partner

  TRIBUTE SPLIT:
    Tenochtitlan: 2/5
    Texcoco: 2/5
    Tlacopan: 1/5

  NOT AN EMPIRE IN ROMAN SENSE:
    Did not impose administration on conquered territories
    Installed loyal local rulers (or kept existing ones who submitted)
    DEMANDED: tribute (fixed lists of goods, materials, people)
               military access (pass-through, soldiers when needed)
    LEFT ALONE: local religion, law, governance, language
    RESULT: a hegemonic extraction empire, not a colonial integration empire
    Compare to Aztec vs Roman Empire:
    Romans: built roads, imposed law, trained local elites in Latin
    Aztec: took what was owed, left the rest alone
```

### Tribute System and Flower Wars

```
TRIBUTE ROLLS:
  Codex Mendoza: documented tribute from each province
  Typical annual tribute from one province:
    400 loads of cotton cloaks
    200 jade beads
    1 jaguar skin, 1 eagle warrior costume
    200 pots of honey
    24,000 bundles of copal (incense)
    Cacao beans in the tens of thousands

  POCHTECA (long-distance merchants):
    Professional merchant class; ran long-distance trade
    Also served as intelligence agents
    Could cross into enemy territory under merchant protection
    Had own god (Yacatecuhtli), own neighborhoods, own courts

FLOWER WARS (Xochiyaoyotl):
  Ritual warfare for CAPTIVES, not conquest
  Major purpose: supply prisoners for sacrifice
  Opponents: Tlaxcalans, Huexotzincans, Cholulans
  BOTH SIDES AGREED: this was a ritual conflict; both sides benefited
    (Enemy warriors became "jaguar food for the gods")
  Also: kept warrior class sharp; maintained war economy
  KEY PROBLEM IN HINDSIGHT:
    Tlaxcalans never submitted → kept their hatred fresh
    → became Cortés's primary allies 1519–1521
    → Tlaxcala contributed more to the conquest than Spanish did
```

### Human Sacrifice at Scale

```
SCALE AND PURPOSE:
  Aztec sacrifice: the most institutionalized large-scale human sacrifice
  in documented history
  Numbers are disputed: Spanish sources exaggerated; modern archaeology
  gives lower but still enormous figures
  Templo Mayor (main temple at Tenochtitlan): dual pyramid
    One side: Huitzilopochtli (war/sun god)
    Other side: Tlaloc (rain/agriculture god)
  Heart excision: sacrificial victim laid on stone; priest cut out heart;
    held it up to the sun
  Skull rack (tzompantli): archaeological excavations found ~650 skulls
    in one rack; larger numbers documented by Spanish

  COSMOLOGICAL NECESSITY (not sadism):
  Aztec cosmology: we live in the Fifth Sun; it was created by gods'
    self-sacrifice at Teotihuacan
  The Fifth Sun requires blood to keep moving; without sacrifice the
    sun stops and the world ends
  Sacrifice = cosmic maintenance; warriors captured for sacrifice died
    honorably and went to the sun
  THIS was the Aztec worldview, not modern cruelty: institutionalized cosmic contract

SPANISH CONQUEST 1519–1521:
  Hernán Cortés: 500 Spaniards + ~200,000 Tlaxcalan allies
  Tenochtitlan fell August 13, 1521
  Disease (smallpox): killed ~50% of population before siege ended
  Moctezuma II: died in Spanish custody (killed by Aztecs angry at his
    accommodation? killed by Spanish? disputed)
  Cuauhtémoc (last tlatoani): tortured for gold, executed 1525
```

## The Inca Tawantinsuyu — Largest Empire in the Americas

### Foundation and Expansion

```
TAWANTINSUYU = "Land of the Four Quarters" (Quechua)
  Four suyus (quarters):
    Chinchaysuyu (northwest: Ecuador + Colombia)
    Antisuyu (northeast: Amazon slopes)
    Collasuyu (southeast: Bolivia + Argentina + Chile)
    Cuntisuyu (southwest: Peru coast)
  Capital: Cusco (Qusqu) — navel of the world in Inca understanding

PACHACUTI (r. 1438–1471): the founder of the empire
  Before Pachacuti: Inca were one of several competing Andean kingdoms
  Pachacuti: defeated the Chanka confederacy (who were threatening Cusco)
  when his father fled (Pachacuti held — possibly apocryphal)
  Then: systematic conquest in all directions
  Built: Machu Picchu (royal estate / ritual site, ~1450)
  Extended: to Lake Titicaca; northern Peru

TOPA INCA (r. 1471–1493): expansion continued
  Conquered: coastal Peru, Ecuador, Bolivia, northwest Argentina, Chile
  Naval expedition into the Pacific (reached Pacific islands? — debated)

HUAYNA CAPAC (r. 1493–1527): maximum extent
  Died: possibly from smallpox (European disease outrunning the Europeans)
  His death triggered civil war between sons Huáscar and Atahualpa
  → This civil war enabled Pizarro's conquest
```

### Administrative System

```
INCA STATE INFRASTRUCTURE:
  No writing system (developed writing was absent)
  BUT: sophisticated information management via KHIPU

KHIPU (QUIPU):
  Knotted string recording device
  Knot type, color, position, string color: encoding information
  What they recorded: census data, tribute records, calendar,
    military records, histories (oral + khipu combined)
  Khipu camayoc: trained specialists who read/wrote khipu
  Most were destroyed by Spanish; ~600 survive; partially decoded
  Recent research: some may have encoded narrative (not just numbers)

ROAD SYSTEM (Qhapaq Ñan):
  ~40,000 km of roads — more than the Roman road network
  Traversed: Andes mountains, coastal desert, jungle edges
  Suspension bridges: over gorges (braided fiber; some still maintained
    by local communities)
  Tambos: rest houses/storehouses every ~15-20 km
  Chasquis: relay runners; carried messages (~250 km/day)
  → Fresh fish from the Pacific coast could reach Cusco (inland, 3,700m)
    in ~2 days

ECONOMIC SYSTEM — NO MONEY:
  Mit'a: labor tax; every adult owed work to the state
    Types: army service, road building, mining, temple construction, farming
    State provided: food, tools, clothing DURING the labor
    No cash economy; redistribution rather than market exchange
  Ayllu: the basic social unit
    Extended kinship group; held land collectively
    Communal labor obligations to each other (not just to the state)
  State redistributed: massive storage system at tambos
    Freeze-dried potato (chuño): stored for years; famine insurance
    Dried meat (charqui — becomes "jerky" in English)
    Textiles, weapons, tools: all stored and redistributed

SPANISH CONQUEST 1532:
  Francisco Pizarro: 168 Spaniards
  Found: Huáscar vs Atahualpa civil war just ended (Atahualpa won)
  Battle of Cajamarca: surprise attack; captured Atahualpa
  RANSOM: Atahualpa offered to fill a room (6m × 5m × 2.5m) with gold
    + two smaller rooms with silver
    → Fulfilled; Pizarro had him strangled anyway (charged with idolatry)
  Disease + alliance with anti-Inca people + steel weapons + horses
  + psychological shock of capture of the Sapa Inca
  → Entire empire collapsed within decades
```

## The Maya — City-States and Classic Collapse

### Classic Period Maya (250–900 CE)

```
MAYA CIVILIZATION:
  NOT an empire — independent city-states competing + occasionally allying
  Major centers:
    Tikal (Guatemala): perhaps 100,000 people
    Palenque (Mexico): Temple of Inscriptions; ruler Pakal
    Copan (Honduras): the "Athens of the Maya" — sculpture, astronomy
    Calakmul: Tikal's great rival; Calakmul-Tikal wars for dominance

MAYA ACHIEVEMENTS:
  Writing: only fully developed indigenous writing system in pre-Columbian Americas
    ~800 glyphs; logographic + syllabic
    Decoded progressively from 1950s–1990s; now largely readable
    Dresden, Madrid, Paris codices (books): survived Spanish burning
  Calendar:
    Tzolkin: 260-day ritual calendar
    Haab: 365-day solar calendar
    Calendar Round: 52-year cycle (when both align)
    Long Count: absolute dating from 3114 BCE (base date)
    Predicted solar eclipses accurately
  Astronomy:
    Venus cycle tracked to extraordinary accuracy
    Eclipse tables in Dresden Codex
  Mathematics:
    Zero: independently invented (before Europe)
    Vigesimal (base-20) number system
    Positional notation

CLASSIC COLLAPSE (~800–900 CE):
  ~50 major southern lowland sites abandoned within ~100 years
  Population collapse: 90%+ in some regions
  CAUSES (debated; probably multiple):
    Prolonged droughts (paleoclimate data confirms)
    Overpopulation → deforestation → soil erosion
    Interstate warfare (escalating; captured labor → less farming)
    Elite competition → monument building diverting labor
    Trade network disruption
  → Most compelling theory: climate shift + internal political failure
  NORTHERN YUCATAN: survived the Classic collapse
```

### Postclassic Maya

```
CHICHEN ITZA (c. 600–1200):
  Mixed Maya-Toltec culture (debated)
  El Castillo pyramid: 365 steps; equinox shadow effect
  Ball court: largest in Mesoamerica
  Cenote Sagrado: jade, gold, human remains deposited

LEAGUE OF MAYAPAN (c. 1200–1441):
  Three-city confederation: Chichen Itza, Uxmal, Mayapan
  Collapsed when Cocom family of Mayapan imprisoned the Xiu ruler
  → Revenge: Xiu destroyed Mayapan 1441
  → Maya fragmented into ~16 competing polities
  THESE FRAGMENTED MAYA met the Spanish
  Yucatan conquest took ~170 years (1527–1697) — far longer than Mexico
  Because: no central authority to decapitate
```

## North America — Cahokia, Haudenosaunee, Pacific Northwest

### Cahokia (c. 700–1400 CE)

```
LOCATION: Near modern St. Louis, Illinois
MONKS MOUND: largest pre-Columbian earthwork north of Mexico
  Base larger than the Great Pyramid of Giza by footprint
  Built over ~250 years (10th–12th centuries)
  Total earthwork volume in Cahokia: larger than Great Pyramid

POPULATION PEAK (~1100 CE): ~10,000–20,000 people
  Larger than contemporary London
  Planned urban grid around the central plaza

ECONOMY:
  Agricultural surplus (corn) → supports non-farming specialists
  Long-distance trade: copper from Great Lakes, shells from Gulf of Mexico,
    mica from Appalachians
  Ceremonial center for broader region

COLLAPSE:
  ~1200–1350: population declined; mounds abandoned
  Reasons: climate shift; soil depletion; flooding? political failure?
  No surviving written records; incomplete picture

CHUNKEY: Cahokia's sport/gambling game
  Stone disk rolled; players threw spears to predict where it stopped
  Evidence of high-stakes gambling, probably including lives
```

### Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy)

```
FIVE NATIONS (later Six):
  Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca
  Tuscarora added 1722 → Six Nations
  League founded: traditional date 1451 or 1570 (debated; pre-European)
  DEGANAWIDA (the Peacemaker) and HIAWATHA:
    Founding figures; brought warring nations together under Great Law of Peace

GREAT LAW OF PEACE (Gayanashagowa):
  Constitution of the Confederacy
  Written in wampum (shell beads as mnemonic device)
  KEY FEATURES:
    Grand Council: 50 sachems (chiefs) representing clans
    Consensus decision-making required for war
    Women (clan mothers) held critical power:
      Nominated and deposed sachems
      Could veto war decisions
    Individual nations retained internal autonomy
    Common foreign policy and war decisions by consensus only

  INFLUENCE ON US CONSTITUTION? (contested):
    Benjamin Franklin was familiar with the Iroquois Confederacy
    Some scholars: Great Law influenced Constitutional Convention (1787)
    Others: influence was indirect at most; primary sources were
            classical Greece/Rome and English constitutional tradition
    The debate continues; both things can be partly true
```

### Pacific Northwest Potlatch

```
POTLATCH:
  Ceremonial feast tradition of Pacific Northwest peoples
  (Chinook, Haida, Kwakwaka'wakw, Tlingit, Tsimshian, etc.)
  HOST gives away wealth to demonstrate status
  MECHANISM:
    Chief hosts feast; distributes massive quantities of goods
    More you give away, higher your status
    Rivals must counter with equal or greater distribution
    → Competitive generosity rather than competitive accumulation
    → Fundamentally inverts the Western "more = higher status" logic
  FUNCTION:
    Wealth redistribution within the community
    Alliance building and renewal
    Rank confirmation and dispute resolution
    Celebration of rites of passage (birth, death, naming, marriage)
  POTLATCH AND COLONIALISM:
    Canada banned the potlatch 1885–1951
    Reason: "wasteful" by Protestant work ethic standards
    The ban was deeply destructive to Northwest cultures
    Since 1951: legal again; major cultural revival
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Pre-Columbian" doesn't mean static or primitive**
The Americas had been continuously developing for 15,000+ years before European contact. The civilizations at contact were at various stages of their own historical trajectories — not frozen primitives waiting to be discovered. The Aztec empire was expanding; the Inca had just finished a civil war; the Maya had already had a major collapse-and-recovery cycle.

**The Aztec sacrifice debate**
Numbers given by Spanish conquistadors are likely exaggerated (political justification for conquest). The true scale was large but probably 20,000–80,000 per year empire-wide — still massive by any standard, but not the hundreds of thousands claimed by some Spanish sources. Recent archaeology (tzompantli excavation 2015–2020) confirms scale but not the highest estimates.

**Machu Picchu's purpose**
Machu Picchu is not Cusco and was not the Inca capital. It was almost certainly a royal estate of Pachacuti — a seasonal retreat and ceremonial site. Its location at 2,430m in the Andes (high, but not the highest Inca site) made it defensively excellent and scenically dramatic.

**Disease as the primary conquest mechanism**
European conquest of the Americas was primarily a demographic catastrophe via disease. Indigenous populations had no immunity to smallpox, measles, typhus, influenza. Estimates: 50–90% of pre-contact population died within 100 years of contact. This is the largest demographic collapse in recorded human history. Conquest with 168 men (Pizarro vs. the Inca) is only possible because the society was already collapsing from epidemic disease.
