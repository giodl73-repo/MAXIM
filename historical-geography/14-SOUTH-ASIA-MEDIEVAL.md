## Post-Gupta Fragmentation (550–1000 CE)

### Harsha's Empire (606–647)

**Harsha of Kanauj** (r. 606–647): Last significant ruler to briefly unify northern India before the Delhi Sultanate. Extended control over most of the Gangetic plain and beyond. Patronized Buddhism and Hinduism simultaneously.

The Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang (Hsuang-tsang) visited his court — a primary source for this period. Harsha died without heirs; his empire fragmented immediately.

### The Tripartite Struggle (c. 750–1000)

```
THREE-WAY COMPETITION FOR KANAUJ:
  Kanauj = prestige capital of north India
  "Whoever holds Kanauj holds north India" (simplified but directionally true)

  GURJARA-PRATIHARA (Rajput federation, northwest + Gangetic plain):
    Nagabhata I: stopped Arab advance into India from Sind (~730)
    Mihira Bhoja I (r. 836–885): greatest Pratihara ruler; controlled Kanauj
    Declined after Rashtrakuta raids

  RASHTRAKUTA (Deccan):
    Dantidurga: founded dynasty ~735 by defeating Chalukyas
    Dhruva, Govinda III: raided north repeatedly; took Kanauj temporarily
    Amoghavarsha I (r. 814–878): scholar-ruler; wrote Kannada poetry
    Declined after internal rebellions

  PALA DYNASTY (Bengal/Bihar):
    Buddhist dynasty; major patrons of Nalanda university
    Dharmapala (r. ~770–810): rebuilt Vikramashila monastery
    Nalanda: the greatest university in Asia at this period
      10,000+ students; monks from China, Tibet, Java, Korea
      Burned by Muhammad Khilji 1203 — catastrophic cultural loss
    Pala: declined under pressure from Senas
```

## The Regional Powerhouses

### Chola Empire (c. 850–1279)

The most significant South Indian dynasty — and one with reach far beyond the peninsula.

```
THE CHOLA TERRITORY:
  Core: Tamil Nadu (southeastern India)
  Extended: most of South India under Rajaraja and Rajendra

  RAJARAJA I (r. 985–1014):
    Military genius; systematized Chola administration
    Built: Brihadeeswarar Temple, Thanjavur
    (Stone tower 66m tall; still standing; UNESCO World Heritage)
    Conquered: Pandyas, Cheras, parts of Sri Lanka, Maldives

  RAJENDRA I (r. 1014–1044):
    Extended north: reached Ganga River (symbolic); earned title
    "Gangaikonda" (He who took the Ganga)
    Built: new capital Gangaikondacholapuram
    NAVAL EXPEDITION TO SOUTHEAST ASIA (~1025):
    Sent fleet attacking Srivijaya (Sri Lanka → Malay Peninsula → Java)
    Purpose: breaking Srivijaya's monopoly on Southeast Asian trade
    → One of the most ambitious naval expeditions in medieval Asian history
    Result: temporarily disrupted Srivijayan trade dominance
    Indian cultural influence on Southeast Asia was deep and lasting
    (Angkor Wat built ~1100s — Hindu/Buddhist; Sanskrit used at court)

CHOLA ADMINISTRATION:
  Sophisticated revenue system: detailed cadastral surveys
  Village assemblies: remarkable local self-governance
  Irrigation infrastructure: tanks, channels across Tamil Nadu
  Temple as economic institution: temples held land, organized labor,
    provided banking functions

CHOLA DECLINE:
  Hoysalas (Karnataka), Pandyas (south Tamil), and eventually
  Delhi Sultanate pressure
  Last Chola king ~1279
```

### The Rajputs

```
RAJPUTS = "Sons of Kings" — warrior clans claiming Kshatriya status
  Origins contested: some genuinely ancient; some Central Asian immigrants
  (Hunas/Gurjaras) who adopted Hindu identity over generations
  Controlled: Rajasthan + parts of central and western India
  Fragmented into many competing clans (no unified "Rajput state")

KEY CLANS:
  Chahamana (Chauhan): Prithviraj Chauhan III — most famous
  Guhilot (Mewar/Sisodiya): Chittorgarh fortress; longest-resisting clan
  Paramaras: Malwa region; King Bhoja (1010–1055) — great patron of learning
  Chandellas: Khajuraho temples (~950–1050 CE) — famous erotic sculptures
    (actually ~10% erotic; 90% religious; erotic panels may have
     had tantric/didactic purpose — debated)

PRITHVIRAJ CHAUHAN III (r. ~1177–1192):
  First Battle of Tarain 1191: Prithviraj defeats Muhammad of Ghor
  Second Battle of Tarain 1192: Muhammad of Ghor returns; defeats and
    captures Prithviraj
  → End of Hindu political dominance of north India
  → Beginning of the Delhi Sultanate

RAJPUT RESISTANCE:
  Rajputs fought the Delhi Sultans and Mughals for centuries
  Chittorgarh (Mewar): sacked multiple times; rebuilt; became symbol
    of Rajput resistance (Rani Padmini legends, probably mythological)
  Akbar's policy: marriage alliances with Rajputs
    → Many Rajput clans became Mughal allies
    → Mariam-uz-Zamani (Rajput princess): mother of Emperor Jahangir
  Aurangzeb reversed Akbar's policy: alienated Rajputs again
```

## The Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526) — Five Dynasties

```
FIVE DYNASTIES:
  1. Mamluk/Slave Dynasty (1206–1290)
  2. Khilji Dynasty (1290–1320)
  3. Tughlaq Dynasty (1320–1414)
  4. Sayyid Dynasty (1414–1451)
  5. Lodi Dynasty (1451–1526)
```

### Slave (Mamluk) Dynasty (1206–1290)

**Qutb al-Din Aibak**: Muhammad of Ghor's Turkish slave general. Founded the sultanate when his master died. Built the Qutb Minar in Delhi (minaret; 72.5m; started ~1193).

**Iltutmish** (r. 1211–1236): Consolidated the sultanate. Received recognition from the Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad — legitimizing his rule. Blocked Mongol advance (first contact).

**Razia Sultana** (r. 1236–1240): His daughter. First female sultan of Delhi. Rode elephants, held court unveiled, commanded armies. Overthrown by male nobles who couldn't tolerate a ruling woman. Killed in battle.

**Balban** (r. 1266–1287): Ruled effectively, established court ceremonialism. Repelled multiple Mongol invasions (Mongols raided regularly; Balban kept them out).

### Khilji Dynasty (1290–1320)

**Alauddin Khilji** (r. 1296–1316): The most powerful Delhi Sultan.

```
ALAUDDIN'S ACHIEVEMENTS:
  Military:
    Repelled multiple Mongol invasions (5+ major invasions stopped)
    Battle of Kili 1299: defeated Mongol force of 200,000(?)
    Conquered: Deccan (Devagiri 1296, 1307), Warangal, Hoysala, Pandya
    First sultan to bring all of India south of the Vindhyas to submission
    (Malik Kafur, his general, led the southern campaigns)

  Economic:
    Market price controls: set fixed maximum prices for grain, cloth, cattle
    Surveillance system: spies in every market
    PURPOSE: pay large army cheaply (price controls → controlled expenses)
    Modern analysis: probably worked in short term; economically distortive
    Revoked after his death → markets normalized

  NEVER CONVERTED TO CALIPHATE/ULAMA OVERSIGHT:
    When asked if his policies were Islamic, reportedly said:
    "I do not know whether it is lawful or unlawful; whatever I think
    is beneficial to my country and whatever is expedient at the time,
    that I decree."
    — Practical autocracy over religious law
```

### Tughlaq Dynasty (1320–1414)

**Muhammad bin Tughlaq** (r. 1325–1351): The paradox sultan.

```
BRILLIANT AND DISASTROUS:
  1. Moved capital from Delhi to Daulatabad (Deccan):
     Forced the entire Delhi population (~500,000) to march 700 miles
     Many died. He then moved the capital back.
     Reason: better centralized India from the Deccan? Poor planning?
     Both, probably.

  2. Token currency experiment:
     Introduced copper/brass tokens as equivalent to gold/silver
     No enforcement mechanism for counterfeiting
     Result: everyone made their own "coins"; token system collapsed
     Had to buy them back at face value; treasury drained

  3. Expanded further than any sultan: briefly controlled most of India
  4. Rebellions: ~22 rebellions during his reign
     Most ended with him defeated or negotiating
     His cousin Ibn Battuta called him "combines courage with cruelty"
     — accurate

FIRUZ SHAH TUGHLAQ (r. 1351–1388):
  More conservative, stable; built Delhi's infrastructure
  But: withdrew from Deccan; local sultanates there became permanent

TIMUR'S SACK OF DELHI 1398:
  Timur (Tamerlane) invaded India
  Firuz Shah was dead; Delhi sultanate weak
  Delhi sacked; ~100,000 prisoners massacred before the battle
  (Timur said: they were making his army "sluggish and difficult to move")
  Delhi didn't recover economically for a century
```

### Lodi Dynasty and Babur (1451–1526)

**Ibrahim Lodi** (r. 1517–1526): Last Delhi Sultan. Alienated nobles; ruled incompetently.

**Battle of Panipat (First), April 21, 1526:**
```
BABUR (Zahir ud-Din Muhammad Babur):
  Timurid prince from Fergana (modern Uzbekistan)
  Lost his ancestral territory; failed multiple times to take Samarkand
  Conquered Kabul as consolation prize
  Invited by Ibrahim's disaffected nobles to invade

  BABUR'S ADVANTAGES:
  Firearms: matchlock muskets + artillery (rare in India at this time)
  Tactics: "tulughma" — flanking movement learned from Uzbek cavalry methods
  Experience: decades of campaigning

  BATTLE:
  Babur's artillery terrified Ibrahim's war elephants
  Tulughma encirclement trapped Ibrahim's larger army
  Ibrahim Lodi killed; Delhi taken
  → MUGHAL EMPIRE (Moghul = Mongol) begins
```

## The Mughal Empire (1526–1857)

### Babur to Akbar

**Babur** (r. 1526–1530): Won India; didn't like it. His memoirs (Baburnama) are remarkably candid — he missed the melons and grapes of Central Asia. Died in India; buried in Kabul by request.

**Humayun** (r. 1530–1540, 1555–1556): Lost the empire to Sher Shah Suri (Afghan general); spent 15 years in Persian exile. Persian court gave him cultural influences that shaped later Mughal art. Won India back; fell down a staircase 6 months later.

**Akbar** (r. 1556–1605): The architect of the Mughal state.

```
AKBAR'S ADMINISTRATIVE SYSTEM:
  Mansabdari system:
    All nobles ranked on a scale (zat: personal rank; sawar: cavalry command)
    Mansabdar (rank holder): paid salary; provided soldiers
    NOT hereditary — rank must be re-earned; no aristocratic consolidation
    Prevented the kind of feudal sub-infeudation that plagued Europe
    Transferred regularly: prevented local power buildup
    Genius: separated the military command structure from territorial ownership

  Revenue: Todar Mal's survey (1580s)
    Detailed land survey; production estimates; standardized taxation
    Revenue assessed in cash (not kind) → monetized peasant economy
    Result: most stable revenue system in Mughal history

  Din-i-Ilahi ("Religion of God"):
    Akbar's syncretic personal faith — drew from Islam, Hinduism,
    Zoroastrianism, Jainism, Christianity
    NOT a mass movement; tiny circle of court nobles
    Expression of his genuine intellectual curiosity
    Ulema (Muslim religious scholars) were furious

  RAJPUT POLICY:
    Married Rajput princesses; gave Rajput nobles high mansab ranks
    Rajput generals commanded Mughal armies
    → Genuine partnership; Mughal empire partly ran on Rajput military capacity
```

**Jahangir, Shah Jahan** (r. 1605–1658): Art and architecture patronage. Shah Jahan built the Taj Mahal (1632–1653 — tomb for his wife Mumtaz Mahal).

### Aurangzeb and the Beginning of Decline (r. 1658–1707)

```
AURANGZEB'S REVERSALS:
  Imprisoned his father Shah Jahan (Agra Fort — Shah Jahan could
    see the Taj Mahal from his prison window)
  Executed his brother Dara Shikoh (most liberal contender)

  RELIGIOUS POLICY:
    Reimposed jizya (tax on non-Muslims) — abolished by Akbar
    Demolished some Hindu temples (how many: disputed; certainly some)
    Banned music at court; many arts suppressed
    Alienated: Rajputs, Marathas, Sikhs, Jats
    Sikh Guru Tegh Bahadur: executed 1675
    → Sikhs militarized permanently; Khalsa founded 1699

  DECCAN CAMPAIGNS (1681–1707):
    Aurangzeb spent last 26 years in the Deccan trying to conquer Bijapur +
    Golconda (the Deccan Sultanates) + defeat the Marathas
    Won the sultanates; could not defeat the Marathas
    The Deccan was a strategic overextension:
    While Aurangzeb was in the south, northern territories loosened
    Treasury drained; army exhausted; nobles demoralized
    He died in the Deccan; Mughal empire never fully recovered
```

## Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646)

```
FOUNDED: Harihara and Bukka Raya I (~1336) in the Deccan
CAPITAL: Hampi (on the Tungabhadra River) — stunning ruins survive

PURPOSE: explicitly as Hindu bulwark against the Delhi Sultanate
  and the Deccan Sultanates (Bahmani, later Bijapur, Golconda etc.)
  "We are a great kingdom in the midst of hostile kings"

KRISHNADEVARAYA (r. 1509–1529): the greatest Vijayanagara ruler
  Military: expelled Bijapur; held Raichur; won everywhere
  Patron: Tamil and Telugu literature; wrote Telugu poetry himself
  Economy: Portuguese described Vijayanagara as richest city in the world
  Population: 500,000+ (larger than contemporary London)

BATTLE OF TALIKOTA 1565:
  Four Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur + Golconda + Ahmednagar + Bidar) allied
  against Vijayanagara
  Vijayanagara's Muslim officers defected mid-battle
  King Aliya Rama Raya captured and beheaded
  Capital Hampi sacked and burned — never resettled
  Empire limped on in fragments until ~1646
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Mughal" ≠ Mongol by culture**
The Mughals claimed Timurid (and through them, Mongol Genghisid) descent. But their language was Chagatai/Persian, their culture was Persian, their art was Persian-influenced. The Mongol connection was a legitimacy claim, not a cultural identity. Babur's memoirs are in Chagatai Turkish; the Mughal court ran in Persian.

**Delhi Sultanate was not monolithic**
Five different dynasties over 320 years, each with different origins, policies, and territorial control. The Slave Dynasty's Iltutmish barely controlled the Gangetic plain. Alauddin Khilji controlled most of India. Muhammad bin Tughlaq tried to control all of it. They were as different from each other as the HRE's dynasties.

**Akbar didn't abolish Islam**
Din-i-Ilahi was not a state religion; it had fewer than 20 adherents. Akbar's empire was administered through Muslim legal norms; he was personally curious and heterodox but didn't impose that on his subjects. The idea that he tried to create a new religion is an exaggeration of his court syncretism.

**The Maratha Confederacy (briefly)**
By the 18th century the Marathas (centered in Maharashtra) under Shivaji (1627–1680) and his successors had become the dominant military power in India. The Maratha Confederacy (Peshwa-led after 1713) controlled more of India than the Mughals by 1750. The Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–1818) ended this — replaced by British paramountcy.
