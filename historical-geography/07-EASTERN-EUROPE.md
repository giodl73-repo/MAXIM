## Poland — Piast, Fragmentation, Reunion, Jagiellonian

### The Piast Dynasty (966–1370)

**Mieszko I** (~966): Converted Poland to Christianity — this was strategic, not just religious. Accepting Roman Christianity put Poland under Papal protection and gave it diplomatic legitimacy in the European system.

**Bolesław I "Chrobry" (the Brave)** (r. 992–1025): First Polish king (crowned 1025). Expanded into: Bohemia, Moravia, Lusatia, Silesia, Red Ruthenia. A brief pan-Slavic empire — didn't last past his death.

```
THE CATASTROPHE — FRAGMENTATION 1138:
Bolesław III Wrymouth: divided Poland among four sons
  Władysław II (Silesia)
  Bolesław IV (Masovia)
  Mieszko III (Greater Poland)
  Henryk (Sandomierz/Łęczyca)

Added: seniority principle — eldest brother holds Kraków + is "senior duke"
This was theoretical — the brothers immediately fought

CONSEQUENCE: Poland spent 180 years fragmented into competing
  principalities (like Germany but without even the HRE fiction)
  Polish fragmentation enabled:
  - Teutonic Order conquest of Prussia (1230–1283)
  - Mongol devastation (1241 — Battle of Legnica)
  - German colonization of Silesia, Pomerania, other territories
  - Loss of Silesia to Bohemia permanently (by 1335)
```

**Władysław I the Elbow-High** (r. 1320–1333): Reunified core Poland. Crowned at Kraków — the symbolic return of unified kingship.

**Casimir III "the Great"** (r. 1333–1370): Last Piast king. "Found Poland built of wood, left it built of stone." Reformed law (Statutes of Wiślica/Piotrków), built castles, encouraged Jewish settlement (gave Jews significant autonomy). University of Kraków founded 1364.

Died without legitimate male heirs → Piast dynasty ends.

### The Jagiellonian Dynasty (1386–1596)

The Jagiellonians created the largest state in Europe through the Union of Poland and Lithuania.

**Jadwiga of Poland** + **Władysław II Jagiełło** (1386):
- Jadwiga: female heir of Polish throne (cognatic inheritance)
- Jagiełło: Grand Duke of Lithuania (pagan)
- Marriage + conversion = Union of Krewo 1385/1386
- Jagiełło converted to Christianity, was baptized, married Jadwiga
- Became King of Poland (as Władysław II Jagiełło)

```
BATTLE OF GRUNWALD (TANNENBERG) 1410:
  Polish-Lithuanian + Tatar force vs Teutonic Knights
  One of the largest battles in medieval Europe
  Grand Master Ulrich von Jungingen killed
  Teutonic Order devastated — never fully recovered
  Territorial gain: limited (Peace of Thorn 1411 — favorable but not crushing)
  Second Peace of Thorn 1466: West Prussia + Pomerelia → Poland

JAGIELLONIAN AT PEAK:
  Poland + Lithuania + (nominal): Bohemia + Hungary
  Vladislaus II Jagiellonian: King of Bohemia AND Hungary (1490)
  → For a moment: one dynasty controlled from Baltic to Adriatic

UNION OF LUBLIN 1569:
  Poland + Lithuania formalized as Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth
  (Rzeczpospolita Obojga Narodów)
  Shared king (elected!), parliament (Sejm), foreign policy
  Separate: armies, treasuries, legal systems
  LIBERUM VETO: any noble could veto any Sejm decision with "nie pozwalam"
  → In theory: democratic; in practice: paralysis (exploited by foreign powers)
  → Commonwealth declined throughout 17th century
  → Partitioned by Prussia, Austria, Russia (1772, 1793, 1795 — Poland disappears)
```

## Bohemia — Přemyslid, Luxembourg, Habsburg

Bohemia is the western-most Slavic kingdom — geographically situated as the HRE's crown jewel and later as the trigger of the Thirty Years War.

### The Přemyslid Dynasty (to 1306)

**Wenceslas I** (Good King Wenceslas, r. 921–935): Bohemian duke who accepted German overlordship and Christianity. Murdered by his pagan brother Boleslav I. Immediately venerated — patron saint of Bohemia.

**Přemysl Otakar II** (r. 1253–1278): "The Iron and Golden King." At his peak held: Bohemia + Moravia + Austria + Styria + Carinthia + Carniola. The most powerful ruler in Central Europe — he should have been elected emperor in 1273. Instead: Rudolf of Habsburg was elected (the princes feared Otakar's power). Rudolf then declared Otakar's Austrian lands forfeit. Battle of Marchfeld/Dürnkrut 1278: Otakar II killed. Habsburg gets Austria permanently.

```
THE PŘEMYSLID LINE ENDS 1306:
  Wenceslas III (last Přemyslid) assassinated at Olomouc
  No male heir
  → Who gets Bohemia?
  Henry of Carinthia: elected, lasted briefly
  John of Luxembourg: elected 1310 (married Přemyslid princess)
  → Luxembourg dynasty begins
```

### The Luxembourg Dynasty (1310–1437)

**John of Luxembourg** (r. 1310–1346): Never really governed Bohemia — always off fighting somewhere. Died at Crécy 1346 (allied with France; his horse was led into battle because he was blind). His crest — three ostrich feathers — was taken by the Black Prince as his own after Crécy. The Prince of Wales still uses it.

**Charles IV** (r. 1346–1378): The greatest Bohemian king. Made Prague the imperial capital. Founded Charles University 1348 (oldest in Central Europe). Built Charles Bridge. Issued the Golden Bull 1356 fixing HRE electoral rules. Prague Cathedral begun.

```
THE HUSSITE CRISIS (1415–1436):
  Jan Hus: Czech theologian, influenced by John Wycliffe
    Criticized Church corruption, sale of indulgences, papal authority
    Council of Constance 1414: Hus given safe conduct
    Hus goes; is tried; condemned; burned July 6, 1415
    → Bohemia erupts

  HUSSITE WARS:
    Two Hussite factions:
    Utraquists (moderate): communion in both kinds (bread + wine) for laity
    Taborites (radical): apocalyptic; denied transubstantiation; social reform
    Against: five crusades called by Pope and HRE
    Bohemian armies won — Jan Žižka (Taborite commander) never lost a battle
      even after going blind
    Battle of Lipany 1434: Utraquists + Catholics defeat Taborites
    Compacts of Basel 1436: Utraquists accepted; Bohemia somewhat reconciled

  SIGNIFICANCE:
    Pre-Reformation: a century before Luther, Bohemia achieved
    religious autonomy from Rome
    Jan Hus = Czech national martyr
```

**Sigismund of Luxembourg** (HRE emperor + King of Bohemia): Had Jan Hus burned (violated the safe conduct). Spent 13 years trying to regain Bohemia from the Hussites.

**Bohemia → Habsburg 1526:** Louis II of Bohemia (Jagiellonian) killed at Battle of Mohács. Ferdinand I (Habsburg) elected King of Bohemia — beginning Habsburg Bohemia that lasted until 1918.

## Hungary — Árpád, Golden Bull, Ottoman Partition

### The Árpád Dynasty (895–1301)

The Magyars (Hungarians) arrived in the Carpathian Basin ~895 CE under Árpád — nomadic steppe people originally from east of the Urals.

**Stephen I (István I, r. 1000–1038):** First Christian King of Hungary (crowned Christmas Day 1000, crown sent by Pope Sylvester II). Organized Hungary on Western feudal model. Patron saint of Hungary.

```
HUNGARIAN GOLDEN BULL 1222:
  Issued by Andrew II under noble pressure
  (Magna Carta's contemporary — same year)
  Key provisions:
  - Nobles exempt from taxation
  - Nobles can resist king by legal means if king violates their rights
    (ius resistendi — right of resistance)
  - Nobles can appeal to palatine (not just king)
  - Regular diet (parliament) to be held annually
  - Foreigners cannot hold office
  COMPARISON TO MAGNA CARTA:
    Magna Carta: King + barons + clergy + some towns
    Golden Bull: King + nobles exclusively — no towns, no clergy
    Golden Bull ius resistendi was more explicit but less legally developed
```

**Béla IV** (r. 1235–1270): Rebuilt Hungary after the Mongol invasion of 1241–1242.

```
MONGOL INVASION 1241:
  Batu Khan + Subutai: two-column invasion
  Battle of Mohi (River Sajó) April 11, 1241:
    Mongols destroy Hungarian army; Béla IV flees to Adriatic coast
  Hungary devastated: ~20-30% population killed or fled
  Mongols withdraw 1242 (Great Khan Ögedei died)
  Béla IV returns; spends reign rebuilding; encourages immigration
  → Kumans (Turkic steppe people) settle in Hungary as border guards
```

### The Later Dynasties

After the Árpáds ended (1301), Bohemia, Anjou, Luxembourg, and Jagiellon dynasties fought over Hungary.

**Matthias Corvinus** (r. 1458–1490): The greatest Hungarian king. Won control of: Bohemia (disputed), Austria (briefly), Moravia, Silesia, Lusatia. Patron of the Renaissance in Hungary. Royal library (Corvina) — second largest in Europe after Vatican. Never had legitimate sons; succession disputed.

```
BATTLE OF MOHÁCS 1526:
  Suleiman I (Ottoman) vs Louis II of Hungary-Bohemia (Jagiellonian)
  Hungarian army destroyed; Louis II drowned fleeing
  Suleiman occupied Buda
  CONSEQUENCE — THREE-WAY PARTITION OF HUNGARY:

  Royal Hungary: Habsburg-controlled strip (Slovakia, Croatia, western Hungary)
    Ferdinand I (Habsburg) elected king — claims all Hungary
    Capital: Bratislava (Pozsony / Pressburg)

  Ottoman Hungary: central plains (Buda, Pest, most agricultural area)
    Direct Ottoman administration as eyalets
    Stayed under Ottoman control until 1683-1699

  Principality of Transylvania: semi-independent under Ottoman suzerainty
    Hungarian princes rule it
    Notable: John II Sigismund (son of Zápolya), then Báthory family
    Relative religious tolerance: Catholic/Protestant/Orthodox/Jewish
    Under Stephen Báthory: became King of Poland (1576) — personal union
```

## The Baltic — Teutonic Knights, Hanseatic League, Lithuania

### The Baltic Political Map

```
BALTIC REGION c. 1300:
  KINGDOM OF DENMARK: controls coasts + Øresund toll
  TEUTONIC ORDER: Prussia (conquered 1230-1283) + Livonia (1237+)
  HANSEATIC LEAGUE: Lübeck + 200 cities; trade network
  GRAND DUCHY OF LITHUANIA: emerging; pagan; expanding east and south
  KINGDOM OF SWEDEN: Baltic access
  FREE CITY OF RIGA: bishop's city; contested control
  ESTONIA: Danish overlords in north; Teutonic Order in south
  LATVIA: split between Teutonic Order + archbishopric of Riga
  KINGDOM OF POLAND: wanted Baltic access (Danzig/Gdańsk)
```

### The Hanseatic League

Not a state — a trading network of merchant cities sharing interests and a legal framework.

```
HANSEATIC LEAGUE (Hanse):
  Hanse = "convoy, company" in Middle Low German
  Core cities: Lübeck (the dominant one), Hamburg, Bremen
  At peak: ~200 cities
  Extent: London to Novgorod; Bergen to Kraków

  WHAT THEY SHARED:
    Commercial privileges in foreign cities (Kontore — trading posts)
    London Steelyard, Bergen Bryggen, Bruges, Novgorod
    Shared legal norms (Lübeck Law used by ~100 cities)
    Mutual defense against pirates and hostile rulers
    Boycott: could exclude a city (Verhansung)

  WHAT THEY DIDN'T SHARE:
    A government (no permanent central body)
    An army (raised ad hoc)
    A currency (each city kept its own)

  DECLINE:
    English and Dutch shipping competition (better ships, lower costs)
    Territorial states (Brandenburg, Denmark) bypassed the Hanse
    Last Diet 1669
```

### The Grand Duchy of Lithuania

```
GRAND DUCHY OF LITHUANIA:
  Mindaugas: first (briefly) Christian Lithuanian ruler, murdered 1263
  Gediminas (r. 1316–1341): expanded east; founded Vilnius
  Algirdas (r. 1345–1377): expanded south; Battle of Blue Waters 1362
    → Took Kyiv and much of Ukraine from weakened Golden Horde
  Vytautas the Great (r. 1392–1430): peak of power
    After Grunwald 1410: largest state in Europe
    Territory: Baltic to Black Sea
    Population: mostly Orthodox Christians + significant Jewish community
    Language of administration: Ruthenian (Old Belarusian/Ukrainian)

  CHRISTIANIZATION 1387:
    Forced by the union with Poland (Jadwiga-Jagiełło marriage)
    Last pagan state to convert in Europe
    Lithuania's paganism had been a useful diplomatic tool
    (Could play Catholic and Orthodox sides against each other)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Bohemia" is not ethnic Czech**
Bohemia (Čechy) = the western part of the modern Czech Republic. Moravia = the eastern part. Both were part of the Bohemian crown. Silesia was also a Bohemian crownland. "Crown of Bohemia" = all three.

**Hungary's borders are not modern Hungary**
Medieval Hungary included modern Slovakia, Croatia, Transylvania (modern Romania), and parts of Serbia and Ukraine. The Treaty of Trianon (1920) reduced Hungary to roughly its ethnic core — a massive shock to Hungarian national identity.

**Lithuania was enormous**
The Grand Duchy of Lithuania at its peak (Vytautas, c. 1400) stretched from the Baltic Sea to the Black Sea. Modern Lithuania is a tiny remnant. The historical Grand Duchy covered most of what is now Belarus, Ukraine, and parts of Russia.

**The Jagiellonians were everywhere**
At peak, one family ruled: Poland, Lithuania, Bohemia, Hungary. Four of the largest kingdoms of East-Central Europe under one dynasty. Had Mohács gone differently, the Habsburgs might never have dominated Central Europe.
