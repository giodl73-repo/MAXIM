## Background: Why the Crusades Were Called

### The Seljuk Disruption

In 1071, the Seljuk Turks defeated Byzantium at Manzikert (see 08-BYZANTINE-EMPIRE.md). Within a decade, they occupied most of Anatolia — including the routes pilgrims used to reach Jerusalem. Jerusalem itself passed from the relatively tolerant Fatimid Egyptians to the Seljuks (1071–1098), who were less hospitable to Christian pilgrims.

### Alexios I's Appeal

Emperor Alexios I Komnenos sent envoys to Pope Urban II at the Council of Piacenza (1095) asking for mercenary reinforcements to help retake Anatolia. What he got was far bigger and far less controllable.

### Urban II at Clermont (1095)

```
URBAN II'S SPEECH (November 27, 1095):
  [No verbatim record — we have 4 different accounts]
  Core message:
  - Eastern Christians are being persecuted by Turks
  - The Holy Land is desecrated
  - Knights who fight in Christ's service receive plenary indulgence
    (sins forgiven — enormous spiritual incentive)
  - "God wills it!" (Deus lo volt / Deus vult) — crowd response

  AUDIENCE:
  French knights — the most land-hungry, sword-happy class in Europe
  Younger sons with no inheritance
  Indebted lords looking for new revenue
  Genuinely pious Christians who wanted to secure their salvation

  RESPONSE:
  Overwhelming. Much larger than Urban anticipated.
  Multiple separate forces organized; departed 1096
```

## First Crusade (1096–1099)

### People's Crusade

Before the organized forces departed, Peter the Hermit led a mass of poorly equipped peasants and minor knights toward Constantinople. The Byzantine emperor Alexios sent them across the Bosphorus immediately. The Seljuks annihilated them at Civetot.

### The Main Crusade

Four major armies converged on Constantinople:
- Godfrey of Bouillon (Duke of Lower Lorraine)
- Bohemond of Taranto (Norman Italian) + nephew Tancred
- Raymond IV of Toulouse (richest, most experienced)
- Hugh of Vermandois (brother of French king)

```
KEY EVENTS:
  Nicaea: surrendered to Byzantines (not the crusaders — Alexios seized it)
          → First friction between crusaders and Byzantines
  Dorylaeum 1097: Crusaders defeated Seljuks in field
  Antioch: siege Oct 1097 – June 1098
    Found via traitor (Firouz) just before Kerbogha's relieving army arrived
    Crusaders entered; then besieged inside by Kerbogha
    Holy Lance "discovered" (Antioch lancé — morale boost)
    Sortie defeated Kerbogha
    Bohemond kept Antioch — refused to hand to Byzantines (betrayal of oath)
  Siege of Jerusalem: June–July 1099
    Wall breached July 15, 1099
    MASSACRE: most of Jerusalem's Muslim and Jewish population killed
    "Through the streets and squares of the city, nothing was to be
    seen save the heaps of heads and hands and feet" — Fulcher of Chartres

GODFREY OF BOUILLON:
  Refused title "King of Jerusalem" — "no king should reign where Christ wore a crown of thorns"
  Title: Advocatus Sancti Sepulchri (Advocate/Defender of the Holy Sepulchre)
  His brother Baldwin became first actual King of Jerusalem 1100
```

## The Four Crusader States — Structure and Governance

### The Kingdom of Jerusalem

The most elaborate feudal constitution in Christendom — the Assizes of Jerusalem.

```
KINGDOM OF JERUSALEM'S CONSTITUTION:
  The Haute Cour (High Court):
    All crown vassals: barons, bishops, military orders
    Had to consent to legislation, major decisions
    King could NOT legislate unilaterally
    Even more restrictive than Magna Carta England
    (The barons of Jerusalem had real constitutional limits on kingship
     from early on — born of necessity: king needed baronial military support)

  VASSALS:
    Great lordships: Galilee, Sidon, Caesarea, Beirut, Jaffa, Hebron
    Each lord had a court, high justice, knight service obligation
    Military orders: held vast castles; owed service; hard to control
                     (they answered to the Pope, not the king)

  POPULATION:
    Franks (Western settlers): warrior class; landowners; urban merchants
    Local Christians (Syrian, Armenian, Greek Orthodox): artisans, farmers
    Muslims: the majority! Mostly rural peasants; not expelled initially
    Jews: smaller communities; somewhat protected
    Relation was complicated — not simple apartheid
```

### County of Edessa

The first Crusader state founded (Baldwin of Boulogne, 1098) and the first to fall.

Armenian Christians dominated the local population; Baldwin initially allied with them. The Armenians welcomed the Crusaders as protectors against Seljuk pressure. When Imad al-Din Zengi of Mosul captured Edessa in December 1144, he killed or enslaved the Frankish population but generally spared the local Armenian Christians.

Loss of Edessa triggered the **Second Crusade** (1147–1149): disastrous. Failed to retake Edessa; attacked Damascus (a potential ally against Zengi's line) instead. Left the Crusader position worse than before.

### Principality of Antioch

Bohemond I (son of Robert Guiscard — Norman Italian) seized Antioch and kept it over Byzantine objections. The Principality was more Norman-Italian in character than the Kingdom of Jerusalem. It maintained closer ties to the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia (to the north) than to Jerusalem.

Key princes: Bohemond I, Tancred (regent), Bohemond III.

### County of Tripoli

Founded by Raymond IV of Toulouse (who died in 1105 during a failed siege of Tripoli). His heirs held it after Tripoli finally fell (1109). The County was the geographic link between Antioch and Jerusalem; it included Krak des Chevaliers (held by Hospitallers) — the greatest Crusader castle.

## The Military Orders

Three military-religious orders became the most effective fighting forces in Outremer — and the most autonomous political actors.

```
KNIGHTS HOSPITALLER (Order of St. John):
  Founded: pilgrim hospital in Jerusalem ~1080 (pre-Crusade)
  Military role: added to hospital mission ~1120s
  Master: elected for life; answered to Pope
  Held: Krak des Chevaliers, Margat, and many other castles
  Revenue: donations from all over Europe; commanderies in every kingdom
  Key distinction: when Templars dissolved 1312 → Hospitallers got
                   their properties

KNIGHTS TEMPLAR (Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ):
  Founded: ~1119 by Hugh de Payens + 8 knights in Jerusalem
  Mission: protect pilgrims on road from Jaffa to Jerusalem
  Papal endorsement: Council of Troyes 1129; Bernard of Clairvaux wrote Rule
  Exempt from all local authority: only Pope
  Could not retreat unless outnumbered more than 3:1 (Rule)
  Banking: invented early international banking (letters of credit)
           Pilgrims deposited money in Europe; withdrew in Jerusalem
  At peak: 900+ commanderies/estates across Europe
  DISSOLUTION: Philip IV of France (deeply in debt to them) + Pope Clement V
               1307: mass arrest; torture + confessions extracted
               1312: Pope dissolved the order; assets to Hospitallers
               Jacques de Molay (last Grand Master): burned 1314

TEUTONIC KNIGHTS:
  Founded: Acre 1190 during Third Crusade; field hospital → military order
  German character: German leadership, German recruits
  Focus shifted to Baltic theater (Prussia, Livonia) after 1226
  See 06-SILESIA-BORDERLANDS.md for their Baltic role
```

## Baldwin IV "the Leper King" (r. 1174–1185)

One of the most remarkable rulers in medieval history.

```
DIAGNOSIS: Leprosy — confirmed by childhood tutors (William of Tyre noticed
           he felt no pain in his right arm)
POLITICAL SITUATION: Kingdom exhausted; Saladin unifying Egypt + Syria

MILITARY ACHIEVEMENT:
  Battle of Montgisard 1177:
  Saladin invaded with ~26,000 men
  Baldwin IV, 16 years old, paralyzed on one side, personally led the charge
  with ~500 knights + Templar reinforcements
  Crusader cavalry broke Saladin's army; Saladin fled on a camel
  Saladin's greatest defeat

CONSTITUTIONAL CRISIS:
  Baldwin unmarried (no one would marry a leper)
  His sister Sibylla's marriages: first husband (William of Montferrat) died
  Second: Guy of Lusignan — Baldwin came to distrust him
  Baldwin tried to annul the marriage; couldn't
  Died 1185 at ~24 years old
  → Sibylla + Guy of Lusignan became queen/king
  → Guy's incompetence led to Hattin
```

## Hattin and Saladin's Reconquest (1187)

```
BATTLE OF HATTIN, JULY 4, 1187:
  BACKGROUND: Guy of Lusignan (king) vs Raymond of Tripoli (regent faction)
  Raymond had truce with Saladin; Guy desperate to prove himself
  TRAP:
  Saladin offered Guy a choice: defend a town or defend the water at
  the Springs of Saffuriyah — predictable ground
  Guy marched the entire Crusader army toward Tiberias in July heat
  Saladin's forces harassed; water denied; Crusaders parched
  Attacked at dawn on Hattin plateau (the "Horns of Hattin" hill)
  RESULT: Crusader army annihilated
  Guy of Lusignan captured; ransomed
  Reynald of Châtillon captured: Saladin killed him personally
    (Reynald had repeatedly violated truces, attacked pilgrimage caravans)
  Templars and Hospitallers captured: all executed
  TRUE CROSS: captured (the relic — not the entire object)

AFTERMATH:
  Jerusalem fell October 2, 1187
  Saladin: no massacre (deliberately contrasted with 1099)
           Christians allowed to ransom themselves; most did
           Those who couldn't pay were enslaved (thousands)
  Within months: almost entire kingdom lost
  Only TYRE held: Conrad of Montferrat (arrived by ship just in time)
                  fortified it; Saladin couldn't take it
```

### Saladin's Character

```
SALADIN (Salah al-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub, 1137–1193):
  Kurdish origin; career under Zengid Nur al-Din
  STRATEGY: "Jerusalem must be Islamic AND unified Islamic world must
             be reunified before it can hold Jerusalem"
             Spent years unifying Egypt + Syria + northern Mesopotamia
             first, THEN attacked Crusaders
  CHIVALRY: genuine by contemporary standards
  - Released Richard I's messengers
  - Sent fruit and ice to Richard when Richard was ill
  - Provided a horse when Richard's was killed
  This was also politics (reputation for mercy encouraged surrender)
  but consistent with his personal conduct throughout his career
  DIED 1193: left almost nothing in his treasury — had given it away
```

## Third Crusade (1189–1192)

```
PARTICIPANTS:
  Richard I of England ("Lionheart")
  Philip II of France (rival; left early)
  Frederick Barbarossa (HRE) — drowned in Anatolia; his army dispersed
  → Richard and Philip carried the actual crusade

SIEGE OF ACRE (1189–1191):
  Conrad of Montferrat + Guy of Lusignan fighting each other
  Richard arrived with fleet; Philip arrived separately
  Acre surrendered July 1191
  Richard's famous act: massacred 2,700 Muslim prisoners at Acre
  (context: ransom negotiations failed; Richard needed to march south)

ARSUF 1191: Saladin attacked Richard's march south
  Richard held discipline; Hospitallers broke formation at the end
  Richard salvaged it; won a tactical victory

JAFFA: retaken

JERUSALEM: Richard marched to within sight of Jerusalem twice
           Decided not to assault: couldn't hold it without large garrison
           → No siege of Jerusalem

TREATY OF JAFFA 1192:
  Christians: keep coastal strip (Acre to Jaffa)
  Jerusalem: Christian pilgrims can visit unarmed
  3-year truce
```

## The Later Crusades — Diminishing Returns

```
FOURTH CRUSADE (1202–1204): Sacked Constantinople
  Intended: Egypt. Achieved: destruction of the Byzantine Empire.
  (See 08-BYZANTINE-EMPIRE.md for full account)

FIFTH CRUSADE (1217–1221): Egypt
  Captured Damietta (Nile delta city) 1219
  Marched on Cairo; trapped by Nile floods; surrendered
  Damietta given back for safe passage

SIXTH CRUSADE (1228–1229): Frederick II's diplomatic crusade
  Frederick II (HRE) — excommunicated, went anyway
  Negotiated Treaty of Jaffa 1229 with al-Kamil (Ayyubid)
  Christians got: Jerusalem, Bethlehem, Nazareth, a corridor to coast
  10-year truce
  Pope Innocent IV was furious (Frederick was excommunicated)
  Patriarch of Jerusalem refused to crown Frederick; Frederick crowned himself
  Muslims were angry at al-Kamil for conceding Jerusalem
  Jerusalem lost again 1244 (Khwarazmian Turks — fleeing Mongols)

SEVENTH + EIGHTH CRUSADES (Louis IX of France):
  Seventh 1248–1254: Egypt. Took Damietta again.
    Battle of Al Mansurah: Crusaders walked into trap; Louis captured
    Massive ransom paid; Damietta returned; campaign failed
  Eighth 1270: Louis IX died in Tunis (disease; possibly typhus)
    Before reaching the Holy Land
    This is where the formal Crusade tradition effectively ended

FALL OF ACRE 1291:
  Mamluk Sultan al-Ashraf Khalil besieged Acre
  May 18, 1291: Acre fell after 43-day siege
  Tyre, Sidon, Beirut: abandoned by Crusaders
  → Entire Crusader presence in the mainland Levant ended
  → Hospitallers: moved to Cyprus → Rhodes (1310) → Malta (1530)
  → Templars: moved to Cyprus → dissolved 1312
```

## Frankish Greece — The Crusade's Other Legacy

After the Fourth Crusade, Crusader states appeared across Greece.

```
LATIN EMPIRE OF CONSTANTINOPLE (1204–1261):
  See 08-BYZANTINE-EMPIRE.md

KINGDOM OF THESSALONIKI: collapsed quickly (1224)

PRINCIPALITY OF ACHAEA (Morea):
  Geoffrey of Villehardouin family; ruled Peloponnese
  French culture transplanted to Greece
  Chivalric courts; troubadour poetry in the Aegean
  Lasted until Ottoman conquest ~1430s-1460

DUCHY OF ATHENS:
  De la Roche family; held Athens + Thebes
  Then: Catalan Company (mercenaries) seized it 1311
  Then: Florentine Acciaiuoli family 1388
  Ottoman 1458

DUCHY OF THE ARCHIPELAGO:
  Venetian Marco Sanudo; many Aegean islands
  Lasted in fragments well into Ottoman period

KINGDOM OF CYPRUS:
  Guy of Lusignan (failed King of Jerusalem) given Cyprus 1192
  Richard I had conquered it on the way to the Holy Land
  Lusignan Cyprus: lasted until Venetian takeover 1489
  Then Ottoman conquest 1571
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Crusade" applied to non-Holy Land expeditions**
The Church called Crusades against: Spanish Muslims (Reconquista), Balts and Slavs (Northern Crusades, including Teutonic Knights), Albigensian heretics (Southern France), and even political enemies of the Pope (Frederick II). "Crusade" = papally authorized holy war with indulgences, not specifically Jerusalem-bound.

**Military orders ≠ national armies**
The Templars and Hospitallers were international corporations answering only to the Pope. They had their own foreign policies, made their own truces, and sometimes worked at cross-purposes with the Kingdom of Jerusalem's kings. They were the Crusader states' most reliable troops and most uncontrollable allies simultaneously.

**Saladin's "chivalry" was strategic AND genuine**
Saladin's relative mercy toward non-combatants was real — compared to the 1099 massacre. It was also strategically effective (cities surrendered more readily). Both things were true. He was not a saint — he executed Templars and Hospitallers after Hattin because they were the most dangerous fighters and would never stop fighting.

**The True Cross**
The True Cross (relic believed to be part of the actual crucifixion cross) was carried into battle by the Kingdom of Jerusalem as their most sacred military standard. Losing it at Hattin was spiritually devastating. Despite many claims, no authenticated piece has ever been confirmed.
