## The Trans-Saharan Trade Network — The Structural Driver

Everything in West African medieval history orbits the trans-Saharan trade.

```
COMMODITIES:
  SOUTH → NORTH:
    Gold: from Ghana/Mali/Songhai to North Africa
          (Bambuk and Buré goldfields, upper Senegal/Niger)
    Salt: from Saharan mines (Taghaza, Timbuktu salt trade)
          (Actually: north sends salt south; south sends gold north)
    Wait — the salt trade goes BOTH ways:
          Salt from Taghaza (central Sahara) → south
          Gold from West African fields → north
    Ivory: sub-Saharan Africa → Mediterranean and beyond
    Enslaved people: significant component of the trade in all periods
    Kola nuts: stimulant; important in Islamic West Africa (no alcohol)

  NORTH → SOUTH:
    Salt (crucial — sub-Saharan populations needed it)
    Copper + brass (valued for ornaments, currency)
    Horses (for cavalry — vital military advantage in savanna)
    Textiles, glass beads, luxury goods
    Islamic manuscripts, education

INFRASTRUCTURE:
  Camel domestication (~3rd century BCE in Sahara): enables long-haul desert trade
  Berber caravan merchants: the middlemen across the Sahara
  Oasis towns: Sijilmasa, Timbuktu, Gao, Kano — trans-shipment points
  Routes: ~3 weeks crossing minimum; water management crucial

ISLAM AS TRADE VECTOR:
  Arabic literacy = commercial literacy (contracts, letters, accounting)
  Muslim merchants: common commercial culture from Morocco to India
  Islamic law: enforceable across political boundaries
  Rulers converted: commercial advantage + literacy + diplomatic access
  → Islam spread along trade routes, not primarily by conquest in West Africa
```

## West Africa — The Savanna Empires

### Ghana Empire (~700–1240)

Not modern Ghana (which adopted the name at independence for historical inspiration — they are geographically distinct).

```
LOCATION: Modern Mauritania + western Mali
          (Wagadu region; capital probably Kumbi Saleh)

STRUCTURE:
  Ghana = king's title (Ghanaian king was called Ghana or Kaya Maghan)
  Soninke people: rulers + trading class
  Revenue model:
    Tax on every donkey load passing through the kingdom
    Monopoly on gold nuggets (dust was for trade; nuggets for king)
    "The king of Ghana can put 200,000 warriors in the field,
     more than 40,000 of whom are bowmen" — al-Bakri (1068)

ALMORAVID IMPACT (1076):
  Almoravid forces (Berber reformers from Maghreb) sacked Kumbi Saleh
  Ghana never fully recovered from this blow
  Decline: also internal fragmentation + Sosso Kingdom (rival)
  Final: Battle of Kirina 1235 — Sosso Kingdom defeated by Sundiata Keita (Mali)
```

### Mali Empire (1235–c. 1400)

Founded by **Sundiata Keita** (r. ~1235–1255) — the Lion King (Sunjata in Mande epic tradition).

```
SUNDIATA KEITA:
  Oral epic: Sundiata (D.T. Niane's recording of the oral tradition)
  Historical figure: defeated Sumanguru Kante (Sosso king) at Battle of Kirina 1235
  Mande (Mandinka/Malinke) people; Keita clan
  Founded Mali Empire centered on Niani (upper Niger River)

STRUCTURE:
  Mansa = emperor/ruler (title of Malian kings)
  Three provinces of original Mande territory + expanding conquest
  Gold fields of Bambuk and Buré under direct control
  Revenue: gold + salt trade taxation

MANSA MUSA (r. 1312–1337):
  The defining figure of Mali's global fame

  THE HAJJ OF 1324:
  Mansa Musa pilgrimaged to Mecca — standard pious act for Muslim rulers
  SCALE: estimated 60,000–80,000 people in his entourage
         500+ servants each carrying 4-lb gold staffs
         80–100 camels each carrying 300 lbs of gold dust
  HE CRASHED THE MEDITERRANEAN GOLD MARKET:
    Spent gold so lavishly in Cairo and across North Africa
    that gold's purchasing power fell by ~25%
    Cairo's gold market didn't recover for ~12 years
  His wealth was noticed: European maps (Catalan Atlas 1375)
    showed him seated on a throne, holding a golden orb
    "This Negro lord is called Mansa Musa... so abundant is
    the gold which is found in his country that he is the
    richest and most noble king in all the land"

  BUILT: Great Mosque of Djenné (later rebuilt); mosques in Timbuktu
  Timbuktu under Musa: major center of Islamic learning
  Sankore mosque/university: ~25,000 students at peak

DECLINE:
  Sons less capable; regional breakaway (Songhai, Tuareg)
  Tuareg nomads sacked Timbuktu 1433
  By ~1430s: Mali contracted to Mande heartland
```

### Songhai Empire (c. 1375–1591)

```
ORIGIN: Songhai people on bend of Niger River (Gao area)
  Had been Ghana's then Mali's tributaries
  Sunni Ali (r. 1464–1492): military genius; expanded massively
    Took Timbuktu 1468; Djenné 1473; controlled entire Niger Bend
    Famous for harsh treatment of Muslim scholars
    (He was Muslim but not observant; scholars disliked him)
  Askia Muhammad (r. 1493–1528):
    Overthrew Sunni Ali's son; more pious Muslim ruler
    Went on Hajj 1497 (as famous as Mansa Musa's but less disruptive)
    Organized bureaucracy; legal reforms; expanded empire
    Songhai at maximum extent: largest West African empire

TIMBUKTU UNDER SONGHAI:
  ~180 Quranic schools; al-Zawiya (study groups) throughout city
  Sankore and other mosques: 25,000+ manuscripts in private libraries
  Manuscript tradition: over 700,000 manuscripts survive from this era
  (Ahmed Baba library; Timbuktu Manuscripts Project)

FALL 1591:
  Moroccan Saadi dynasty sent expedition across Sahara
  El Mansur (Moroccan sultan): wanted Songhai's gold
  Spanish muskets and cannons vs Songhai cavalry + archers
  Battle of Tondibi 1591: Songhai cavalry broke in face of muskets
  Songhai fell — gold mines never fully delivered (much further south)
  → Morocco couldn't rule effectively; Songhai fractured into petty states
  → End of the great Sahelian empires
```

### Kanem-Bornu Empire

The longest-lasting West African state — straddling Lake Chad for ~1,000 years.

```
KANEM (c. 700–1380): centered east of Lake Chad
  Sefuwa dynasty: ruled almost continuously from ~700 to 1846
  (claimed descent from a supernatural ancestor — Dugu)
  At peak: controlled trans-Saharan route to Libya (Fezzan)
  Converted to Islam ~1000–1100

BORNU (1380–1893): moved west of Lake Chad after Bulala attacks
  Idris Alooma (r. 1571–1603): the greatest Bornu sultan
    Military reforms; Ottoman-trained musketeers
    Diplomatic relations with Ottoman Empire and Morocco
    Legal reforms; Islamic law more fully implemented

LONGEVITY:
  Sefuwa dynasty: ~800 years of rule — one of the longest dynasties in history
  Kanem-Bornu survived: Saharan shifts, slave trade disruption,
    Hausa competition, regional wars
  Fell finally to Rabih az-Zubayr's conquest 1893
```

### Hausa City-States (Hausaland)

```
SEVEN HAUSA STATES (Hausa Bakwai):
  Biram, Daura, Kano, Rano, Gobir, Zazzau (Zaria), Katsina
  Each: an independent city-state with surrounding territory
  NOT an empire — competing states, sometimes at war

  Kano: largest and most commercially important
    Chronicle of Kano: important historical source
    Textile production (blue cloth); leather goods
    Under Kano: 30,000+ weavers at peak

  Islam: spread gradually 14th–15th centuries via merchants
  Fulani jihad 1804 (Usman dan Fodio): overthrew Hausa rulers
  → Created Sokoto Caliphate
  → Hausa territory under Fulani-Islamic rule
```

## East Africa — The Swahili Coast

### The Indian Ocean Trade Network

```
MONSOON TRADE SYSTEM:
  Northeast monsoon (Nov–March): blows ships FROM Arabia/India TO Africa
  Southwest monsoon (April–October): blows ships FROM Africa TO Arabia/India
  Dhow: lateen-rigged sailing vessel; perfectly suited to monsoon routes
  Arabic/Persian sailors: knew these routes since antiquity
  Chinese ships (junks): reached East Africa (Zheng He's voyages, 1405–1433)

COMMODITIES:
  EAST AFRICA → Arabia/India/China:
    Gold (from Zimbabwe plateau via coastal ports)
    Ivory, iron, tortoise shell, ambergris
    Enslaved people
    Animal exotica (leopards, ostriches, even a giraffe to China)

  Arabia/India → East Africa:
    Textiles (cotton, silk)
    Ceramics (Chinese porcelain found in ruins)
    Glass beads
    Dates, grain
```

### Swahili City-States

```
SWAHILI COAST SETTLEMENTS (c. 1000–1500 peak):
  Kilwa (Tanzania): the dominant commercial city
    Ibn Battuta (1331): "one of the most beautiful and well-constructed
    towns in the world... its inhabitants are engaged in a
    jihad for it is surrounded by unbelievers"
    Gold transit from Zimbabwe plateau: shipped through Kilwa
    Kilwa Sultanate: Muslim rulers; sophisticated governance
  Mombasa (Kenya): major northern port
  Malindi: Vasco da Gama stopped here 1498; got pilot
  Zanzibar: island trading hub
  Sofala: southernmost major port; Zimbabwe gold gateway
  Mozambique, Pate, Lamu: additional nodes

SWAHILI CULTURE:
  Swahili (Kiswahili): Bantu base language with heavy Arabic influence
  "Swahili" = Arabic for "coastal" (from sahil = coast)
  Stone Town architecture: coral stone buildings; carved wooden doors
  Islam: widespread by ~1000 CE; mosques, Islamic law, Arabic literacy
  POPULATION: mixed Bantu African + Arab + Indian merchants
              not purely Arab (common misconception)
```

### The Ethiopian Solomonid Dynasty

```
AXUM (ancient predecessor, ~100–700 CE):
  Aksumite Kingdom: Red Sea port; trade empire
  Converted to Christianity (Coptic/Ethiopian Tewahedo) ~340 CE
  First Christian state in sub-Saharan Africa
  Declined due to: Islamic expansion blocking Red Sea trade,
                   ecological degradation, power shift inland

ZAGWE DYNASTY (~900–1270):
  Non-Solomonid interregnum
  Built: Lalibela rock-hewn churches (carved into living rock ~1200)
  St. Yimrehane Kristos, Bete Giyorgis — engineering achievements

SOLOMONID RESTORATION (1270–):
  Yekuno Amlak overthrew last Zagwe king
  Claimed descent from Solomon + Queen of Sheba via Menelik I
  (The Kebra Nagast — Glory of Kings — is the documentary "proof")
  This claim persisted: Haile Selassie used it in 20th century

  Amda Seyon I (r. 1314–1344): military expansion; defeated Muslim
    sultanates in the east; Ethiopian peak before 16th century
  Zara Yaqob (r. 1434–1468): theological reformer; suppressed old
    practices; codified Ethiopian Christianity

  THE PRESTER JOHN LEGEND:
  Europeans thought a powerful Christian king existed in the East
  The legend attached to Ethiopia by ~1200
  This led to Ethiopian-European diplomatic contacts (15th century)
  Portuguese sent envoys to Ethiopia; Ethiopian monks visited Lisbon
```

## Central and Southern Africa

### Great Zimbabwe (c. 1100–1450)

```
GREAT ZIMBABWE:
  Location: Zimbabwe plateau, southern Africa
  "Zimbabwe" = dzimba dza mabwe = "great stone houses" in Shona
  CONSTRUCTION: dry-stone walling (no mortar); walls up to 11m high
               The largest pre-colonial stone structure in sub-Saharan Africa
  No written records from the builders themselves

  RULING PEOPLE: Shona-speaking ancestors (Gokomere/Gumanye culture)
  ECONOMY: cattle herding + gold trade
    Gold from the plateau → Sofala on coast → Indian Ocean trade
    Great Zimbabwe's rulers controlled this gold transit
  ZIMBABWE BIRDS: 8 carved soapstone bird statues found
    Now national symbols of Zimbabwe; on flag

  DECLINE c. 1450:
  Possibly: soil exhaustion from overgrazing
  Trade routes shifted (Khami, then Torwa Kingdom)
  → Successor: Mutapa (Monomotapa) state further north

MUTAPA (c. 1430–1759):
  Nyatsimba Mutota: founder; moved north toward Zambezi
  Controlled gold trade; Portuguese dealt with Mutapa rulers
  Eventually fragmented under Portuguese pressure + internal division
```

### Kingdom of Kongo (c. 1390–1857)

```
LOCATION: Modern DRC, Republic of Congo, Angola, Gabon
PEOPLE: Bakongo; Kikongo language
CAPITAL: Mbanza Kongo (São Salvador after Portuguese contact)

STRUCTURE:
  Manikongo = king; elected by council of great nobles
  Six provinces: Soyo, Mbata, Nsundi, Mpangu, Mbamba, Mbata
  Well-administered; taxation; provincial governors
  Road network; market system; currency (nzimbu shells)

PORTUGUESE CONTACT (1483):
  Diogo Cão sailed up Congo River
  Manikongo Nzinga a Ntinu: welcomed Portuguese
  AResult: Christianization of ruling class
  Afonso I (r. 1509–1542): the "apostle of Kongo"
    Enthusiastically adopted Christianity; built churches; sent sons to Lisbon
    Literacy in Portuguese + Christian theology among Kongolese elite
    PROBLEM: Portuguese slave trade
    Afonso's letters to King João III of Portugal:
    "It is our will that in these kingdoms there should not be any trade
    in slaves nor outlet for them... because it is our will that this
    Kingdom not be a country of trade in our subjects"
    His protests were ignored

  KONGO AND THE SLAVE TRADE:
  Portuguese demands: enslaved people
  Kongo: became increasingly enmeshed in the trade
  Raids on neighbors; internal destabilization
  Battle of Mbwila 1665: Portuguese-backed forces defeated Kongo
  → Kingdom fragmented; never reunified at full strength
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Dark Continent" was always wrong**
Medieval Africa had sophisticated urban societies, long-distance trade networks, written literatures (Arabic, Ge'ez), monumental architecture, and complex governance. The "dark continent" framing is 19th-century European ignorance/ideology, not historical reality. Timbuktu's universities were contemporary with Oxford.

**Swahili ≠ Arab**
The Swahili coast was culturally hybrid from the start — Bantu African population, Arab and later Indian merchants, mixed through centuries of interaction. The Arab merchants married locally; the culture that resulted was distinctly Swahili, not imported Arab culture. Arabic was used for religion and trade; Kiswahili was the vernacular.

**Great Zimbabwe wasn't built by "lost civilizations"**
19th-century European colonizers refused to believe Africans built Great Zimbabwe — claiming Phoenicians, Arabs, or others. This was racist nonsense refuted by archaeology. The builders were ancestors of the Shona people who still live in Zimbabwe.

**Mansa Musa's wealth comparisons are unverifiable**
"Richest person in history" calculations applied to Mansa Musa are modern estimations working backward from historical records. The hajj's gold-dumping effect is historically attested (al-Umari, writing in Cairo 12 years later, still described the gold market disruption). The specific wealth numbers are modern estimates, not medieval counts.
