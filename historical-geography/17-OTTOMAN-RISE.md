# Ottoman Rise — From Anatolian Beylik to World Empire

## The Big Picture

```
OTTOMAN TERRITORIAL EXPANSION:

  1299: Osman I — small beylik in northwest Anatolia
        (~600 km² ; one of many)
  1326: Bursa captured → first real capital
  1361: Edirne (Adrianople) → European foothold
  1389: Battle of Kosovo → Serbia tributary
  1396: Battle of Nicopolis → Balkans secured
  1402: Timur defeats Bayezid I at Ankara — near collapse
  1453: Constantinople falls — empire confirmed
  1473: Ottomans defeat Karamanids → Anatolia unified
  1516: Mamluk Egypt conquered → Syria, Egypt, holy cities
  1517: Caliphate claim (disputed but maintained)
  1520-66: Suleiman the Magnificent — maximum classical extent

  GEOGRAPHIC REACH AT PEAK (Suleiman's death 1566):
  +─────────────────────────────────────────────────────+
  | Hungary (1526)  → Vienna siege 1529 (failed)        |
  | Wallachia/Moldavia (tributaries)                    |
  | Crimean Khanate (vassal)                            |
  | Anatolia + Constantinople                           |
  | Syria + Palestine + Egypt                           |
  | Arabia (Mecca, Medina, Yemen)                       |
  | Tripolitania + Algeria (nominal)                    |
  | Iraq (intermittently)                               |
  +─────────────────────────────────────────────────────+
  THREE SEAS: Mediterranean, Red Sea, Indian Ocean (briefly)
  TWO CONTINENTS: Europe + Asia + Africa
```

## From Beylik to Empire — The Founding Dynasty

### Anatolian Context: The Beylik System

After the Mongols defeated the Seljuks of Rum at the Battle of Köse Dağ (1243), Anatolia fragmented into a dozen competing Turkish principalities (beylik = principality of a bey).

```
MAJOR ANATOLIAN BEYLIKS c. 1300:
  +─────────────────────────────────────+
  | Kastamonu (Jandarids)               |
  | Sinop | Trabzon (Pontic Greeks)     |
  |──────────────────────────────────── |
  | GERMIYAN (Kütahya area — powerful) |
  | KARAMAN (Konya area — most         |
  |   prestigious: former Seljuk core) |
  | Eretna (central Anatolia)          |
  | KARAMANIDS: considered themselves  |
  |   rightful Seljuk heirs            |
  |──────────────────────────────────── |
  | OTTOMAN (northwest corner — small) |
  | Adjacent to Byzantine territory    |
  | Position: on the ghazi frontier    |
  +─────────────────────────────────────+

  Ghazi = frontier warrior for Islam
  The northwest border had the most Christian territory to raid
  → attracted fighters and glory-seekers → grew the Ottoman beylik
```

**Why the Ottomans specifically?** Their location was decisive: on the Byzantine frontier, where raids were possible, where land was available, where Byzantine internal weakness meant constant opportunities. Other beyliks were surrounded by other Turkish states — nothing to raid without fighting fellow Muslims (legitimacy problem).

## The Founders

### Osman I (r. 1299–1324/26)

The dynasty's founder. "Ottoman" = Osmanli = "of/descended from Osman."

```
OSMAN'S POLITICAL GENIUS:
  Allied with: Byzantine frontier lords (sometimes); Turkic settlers
  Married into: Şeyh Edebali's family (Sufi religious legitimacy)
  The dream narrative: Osman dreamed a tree grew from his chest
    and covered the earth; prophesied empire
    (Written down later — standard founder mythology)
  First major victory: Battle of Bapheus 1302
    Defeated Byzantine force; established Ottoman as serious beylik
  Died before Bursa fell; buried there (Osman's tomb still in Bursa)
```

### Orhan (r. 1324/26–1362) — First Institutional Builder

```
ORHAN'S INNOVATIONS:
  1. First standing infantry: Yaya (infantry) — paid; not tribal
  2. Captured Bursa (first major city) 1326: became capital
  3. First Ottoman coinage (akçe)
  4. First madrasas (Islamic colleges) in Ottoman territory
  5. Treaty with Byzantium: married Byzantine princess Theodora
     (daughter of John VI Kantakouzenos — the usurper)
     → Ottoman troops helped Byzantine CIVIL WAR 1345
     → Byzantine weakness is structural: they kept inviting Ottomans in

  GALLIPOLI 1354:
    Earthquake damaged Byzantine fortifications at Gallipoli
    Ottoman forces occupied it
    → FIRST PERMANENT EUROPEAN FOOTHOLD
    → The Balkans are now accessible

  ORGANIZATION:
    Orhan created the distinctive dual administration pattern:
    Military-administrative structure emerging
    NOT yet bureaucratized — still heavily personal rule
```

## Into Europe — Murad I and the Balkans

### Murad I (r. 1362–1389) — First Sultan

Murad I is the first Ottoman ruler to use the title **Sultan** formally. He also founded the two key institutions that made the Ottoman Empire unique.

```
INSTITUTION 1: JANISSARIES (Yeniçeri = "New Troops")
  Created: under Murad I, ~1363
  Original system: devshirme (tribute of boys)
    Christian Balkan families: required to give one son to the state
    Boys: converted to Islam; educated; trained as soldiers
    Janissaries: the sultan's personal infantry; slaves of the sultan
    NOT feudal: no land; paid salary; fed and housed by state
    Could not marry (initially); no family loyalties
    → Theoretically: total loyalty to sultan (no competing family ties)
    → In practice: eventually became a hereditary force that made and broke sultans

  WHY THIS WORKED:
    Genghis Khan's decimal system was one model
    More directly: Mamluk slave-soldier system was the template
    The Ottoman innovation: systematic, mass, state-managed
    Result: best disciplined professional infantry in the world for ~200 years

INSTITUTION 2: TIMAR SYSTEM
  Timar = military land grant
  Sipahi (cavalry): received timar (revenue grant from land)
    Sipahi collected revenue; in return provided himself + mounted men for campaigns
    NOT hereditary (unlike Western feudal fief) — service-conditional
    State reassigned timars; prevented permanent aristocracy from forming
  Hierarchy:
    Timar (~3,000–20,000 akçe revenue)
    Zeamet (20,000–100,000 akçe)
    Has (100,000+ akçe — usually governors, high officials)
  This was the backbone of the Ottoman military machine until gunpowder changed warfare
```

### Battle of Kosovo (June 15, 1389)

```
THE BATTLE:
  Serbian-led coalition (Lazar of Serbia, Bosnian forces, Wallachian detachment)
  vs. Murad I's Ottoman army
  On the Field of Blackbirds (Kosovo Polje) in modern Kosovo

  During or after the battle:
  Murad I killed — by a Serbian knight (Miloš Obilić — possibly mythological)
    who pretended to defect and stabbed him in his tent
  Serbian Prince Lazar captured and executed
  Ottoman victory: Serbia became Ottoman tributary

  SIGNIFICANCE:
    Serbia was never the same — enters long Ottoman tributary/vassal relationship
    Kosovo became the Serbian national martyrology site
    The Battle of Kosovo in Serbian epic poetry: tragic heroism; self-sacrifice
    → "Kosovo" still resonates in Serbian national identity (to this day)

  OTTOMAN SUCCESSION:
    Murad's son Bayezid I took command mid-battle
    His first act as sultan: execute his brother (fratricide as policy begins)
```

### Bayezid I "the Thunderbolt" (r. 1389–1402)

```
RAPID EXPANSION:
  Conquered: most of Anatolia (absorbed rival beyliks)
  Balkans: tightened Ottoman control
  Battle of Nicopolis 1396: crusade (French, Burgundian, Hungarian, Wallachian)
    → Ottomans annihilated the crusading force
    → Last major Western Crusade to the Balkans

  SIEGE OF CONSTANTINOPLE:
    Bayezid besieged Constantinople 1394–1402
    First major Ottoman siege of the city
    Byzantine emperor Manuel II went to Western Europe begging for help
    The siege was lifted not by Western aid but by:

BATTLE OF ANKARA 1402 — THE NEAR COLLAPSE:
  Timur (Tamerlane) invaded Anatolia
  Timur had his own reasons: Bayezid had sheltered Timur's enemies
    (Turkic rulers Timur had driven away)
  Ottoman army met Timur at Ankara
  THE KEY: Timur had promised the Anatolian beys (conquered by Bayezid)
    their territories back. Their contingents DESERTED mid-battle.
  Bayezid captured — died in captivity 1403
  → Ottomans controlled no Anatolian beyliks; Balkans only
  → The "Ottoman Interregnum" 1402–1413: Bayezid's sons fight each other

  HOW OTTOMAN SURVIVED THIS:
  Mehmed I won the civil war (Battle of Çamurlu 1413)
  The Balkans had remained loyal throughout — core territory preserved
  Byzantine Emperor HELPED Mehmed — because a divided Ottoman state
    was better for Byzantium than a united one. A miscalculation:
    Mehmed reunified the empire.
```

## Mehmed II — The Conquest of Constantinople

### Murad II and the Run-Up (r. 1421–1444, 1446–1451)

```
MURAD II: consolidated Anatolia; fought Crusades
  Battle of Varna 1444: Crusade led by Władysław III of Poland-Hungary
  → Ottomans annihilated the Crusade
  → Last serious Western attempt to push Ottomans from Europe

  SECOND REIGN: abdicated (unusual!) in favor of son Mehmed II
  Son lost control; Janissary revolt; Murad returned
  Murad died 1451 → Mehmed II again, now 19

  MURAD'S CONTRIBUTION: Regularized devshirme; expanded Janissaries;
  kept administrative records; professional bureaucracy developing
```

### Mehmed II "Fatih" (the Conqueror) (r. 1444–46, 1451–1481)

```
THE FALL OF CONSTANTINOPLE (May 29, 1453):

  MEHMED'S PREPARATION:
  Built Rumeli Hisarı (fortress on European shore of Bosphorus, 1452)
    → Controlled Bosphorus shipping; strangled Constantinople
  Contracted Urban (Hungarian engineer) to build massive cannon
    The bombard: could hurl 272-kg stone balls
    Fired 7x per day; weakened the Theodosian Walls

  BYZANTINE DEFENDERS:
    ~7,000 defenders (including ~2,000 Genoese mercenaries)
    Giovanni Giustiniani Longo: commander; critical

  OTTOMAN ARMY:
    ~50,000–80,000 men
    Naval fleet: ~150+ ships

  THE CHAIN PROBLEM:
    Byzantines stretched a chain across the Golden Horn
    Ottoman fleet couldn't enter from the north
    MEHMED'S SOLUTION: built a road over the hills north of Galata
    Dragged 70+ ships overland (greased rollers) into the Golden Horn
    → Psychological shock + opened second front

  FINAL ASSAULT May 29, 1453:
    Giustiniani wounded; evacuated (against orders)
    His departure caused morale collapse at critical sector
    Ottomans broke through the Kerkoporta gate (possibly left unlocked)
    Constantine XI died fighting — body never definitively identified

  AFTERMATH:
    3 days of looting (per negotiated agreement with troops)
    Mehmed: rode to Hagia Sophia; had it converted to mosque
    Said to have wept in the ruins of the palace:
    "What a city we have given over to plunder and destruction"
    Population: sold into slavery or ransomed; some Greek community preserved

  WHY THIS MATTERED:
    End of the Byzantine/Roman Empire (1,123 years from Constantinople's founding)
    Trade routes to Asia disrupted → pressure to find sea routes
      (Christopher Columbus [1492] and Vasco da Gama [1498] both
       partly motivated by finding routes around Ottoman-controlled lands)
    Greek scholars fled to Italy → fed the Renaissance
    Mehmed: spoke Greek, studied philosophy, had his portrait painted by Gentile Bellini
```

## Selim I — Caliphate and the East (r. 1512–1520)

**The most successful conqueror per year in Ottoman history** — doubled the empire's size in 8 years.

```
SELIM'S CAMPAIGNS:
  1514: Battle of Chaldiran vs Safavid Persia (Shah Ismail I)
    Significance: Safavids = Shia; Ottomans = Sunni
    Ottoman cannons and Janissaries vs Safavid cavalry
    Decisive Ottoman victory; took Tabriz temporarily
    → Established Ottoman-Safavid rivalry that lasted centuries
    → Anatolia's Shia population (Kizilbash) massacred — 40,000+ killed
    → This sectarian conflict drove much of Middle Eastern history 1500–present

  1516–1517: Mamluk Egypt conquered
    Battle of Marj Dabiq 1516 (Syria): Mamluks annihilated
    Battle of Ridaniyya 1517 (near Cairo): Egypt taken
    MAMLUK SULTAN TOMANBAY: captured; hanged at Cairo's gate

  WHAT SELIM GAINED:
    Egypt: the richest territory in the Islamic world
    Syria + Palestine: holy cities route
    Hejaz: Mecca and Medina — guardianship of the holy cities
    The last nominal Abbasid "caliph" (in Cairo, under Mamluks):
      Selim received the symbols of caliphate
      Whether this was a formal transfer or Ottoman construction: debated
      BUT: Selim and successors used title "Caliph of all Muslims"
           This added spiritual legitimacy to temporal power
```

## Suleiman I "the Magnificent" / "the Lawgiver" (r. 1520–1566)

```
THE PEAK OF THE CLASSICAL EMPIRE:

MILITARY EXPANSION:
  Belgrade: captured 1521 (blocking road to Europe for decades)
  Battle of Mohács 1526 (see 07-EASTERN-EUROPE.md):
    Louis II of Hungary-Bohemia killed; Hungary partitioned
    Royal Hungary → Habsburgs; Central Hungary → Ottomans;
    Transylvania → semi-independent under Ottoman suzerainty
  First Siege of Vienna 1529:
    Failed — supply lines too long; weather; strong defense
    → Vienna = the high-water mark of Ottoman advance in Europe
    → Every subsequent European-Ottoman confrontation referenced this
  Mediterranean: naval dominance under Hayreddin Barbarossa
    Battle of Preveza 1538: Ottoman fleet defeated Holy League
    → Ottoman Mediterranean naval supremacy for ~30 years
  Iraq: Baghdad taken 1534, 1555 (contested with Safavids)
  Yemen, Algeria, Tripolitania: extended reach

"KANUNI" (LAWGIVER) — THE ADMINISTRATIVE ACHIEVEMENT:
  Ibrahim Pasha (Grand Vizier): reorganized central administration
  Kanun (secular law): Suleiman issued massive codification
    of secular law (separate from and complementary to Islamic sharia)
    Tax laws, land tenure, criminal law, administrative procedure
    This was the "Lawgiver" reputation — more important in Ottoman
    eyes than the "Magnificent" title (European focus)

MILLET SYSTEM:
  Each recognized religious community (millet) governed internally
    by its own religious law and leaders for personal status matters:
    Orthodox Christian millet (under Patriarch of Constantinople)
    Armenian Christian millet
    Jewish millet (under Chief Rabbi of Constantinople)
    Muslim community: governed by sharia + kanun
  Taxation paid; religious freedom within the millet
  THIS was why the Ottoman Empire lasted 600 years with such ethnic diversity:
    It didn't try to create a uniform population
    Compare to: Spanish Inquisition, French expulsion of Huguenots
    The Ottoman approach was more stable (if not equal)

SULEIMAN AND IBRAHIM PASHA:
  Suleiman's closest friend and Grand Vizier: Ibrahim Pasha (devshirme)
  Ibrahim: brilliant administrator; perhaps too ambitious
  Executed 1536 on Suleiman's order (Hürrem Sultan's influence? jealousy?)
  Pattern: great favorites eventually executed when they became threats

DEATH (1566):
  Suleiman died on campaign in Hungary at ~71
  His death was concealed until the army returned safely
  (Janissary mutiny risk if news spread too early)
  His son Selim II inherited — "Selim the Sot" (drinker; less capable)
  → Beginning of the "Sultanate of Women" + institutional decline
```

## Why the Ottomans Lasted 600 Years

```
STRUCTURAL DURABILITY FACTORS:

1. DEVSHIRME + JANISSARIES:
   The ruling class was recruited, not born
   Talented people from any background could rise to Grand Vizier
   (Ibrahim Pasha: son of a Greek shepherd → ruler of an empire)
   Prevented a hereditary aristocracy from forming that could challenge the sultan

2. TIMAR SYSTEM:
   Distributed military costs across the empire
   No standing army to maintain centrally (initially — before Janissaries expanded)
   Sipahi cavalry self-financed via land revenues

3. MILLET SYSTEM:
   Each group governed itself internally
   Reduced resistance to Ottoman rule
   Provided skilled administrators (Greek Phanariots; Jewish merchants)
   Dramatic tolerance compared to contemporary Europe

4. GEOGRAPHIC POSITION:
   Controlled critical chokepoints:
   Bosphorus (Europe-Asia trade)
   Eastern Mediterranean (routes to Asia)
   Arabian holy cities (Islamic legitimacy)

5. RELIGIOUS LEGITIMACY:
   Sunni caliph (from 1517, somewhat disputed)
   Guardian of Mecca and Medina
   Defender of Sunni Islam vs. Safavid Shia
   → Wars with Safavids were both geopolitical AND religiously framed

6. FLEXIBILITY:
   Absorbed conquered peoples' techniques: Byzantine administration,
   Arabic scholarly tradition, Persian court culture
   Ottoman court culture = synthesis of all of the above
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Sublime Porte" ≠ just a poetic name**
The Sublime Porte (Bab-ı Âli — High Gate) refers to the Ottoman government, specifically the office of the Grand Vizier. "Gate" is literal: the gate of the Topkapı Palace complex where petitioners and diplomats were received. Over time it became metonymy for the government itself. Western powers addressed correspondence to "the Sublime Porte."

**Fratricide was policy, not accident**
Ottoman sultans routinely executed their brothers on accession. Mehmed II formalized it in law (Kanunname of Mehmed II): "It is lawful for whichever of my sons inherits the sultanate to execute his brothers for the order of the world." This was chilling but effective: prevented civil wars over succession. Later modified to cage confinement (kafes system) — princes confined to the harem, preventing both execution and political experience.

**"Magnificent" vs "Lawgiver"**
Europeans called Suleiman "the Magnificent" for his wealth and power display. Ottomans called him "Kanuni" — the Lawgiver — for his legal codifications. The Ottoman name reflects what they actually valued about his reign.

**Ottoman decline was very slow**
The Ottoman Empire is often treated as "declining" after Suleiman. It had serious crises from the 1580s onward (Janissary degeneration, inflation, succession problems). But it survived until 1922 — 356 more years after Suleiman's death. "Decline" for the Ottomans meant a 350-year process. By European standards they were functioning — and dominating — through the 17th century.
