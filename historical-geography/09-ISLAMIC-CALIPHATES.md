## The Succession Crisis — Seeds of Sunni-Shia Split

This is the most consequential religious-political schism in Islamic history. It happened within 30 years of Muhammad's death.

```
MUHAMMAD DIES 632 CE without designating a clear successor.

TWO VIEWS:
  SUNNI VIEW: The community (umma) elects/chooses
    Abu Bakr (Muhammad's father-in-law): first caliph
    Umar ibn al-Khattab: second caliph (brilliant administrator)
    Uthman ibn Affan (Umayyad family): third caliph — assassinated
    Ali ibn Abi Talib (Muhammad's cousin + son-in-law): fourth caliph

  SHIA VIEW (Shiat Ali = Party of Ali):
    Leadership should have passed to Ali immediately
    Ali was the rightful first caliph
    The first three caliphs were usurpers
    Religious authority passes through Ali's bloodline (imams)

FIRST FITNA (First Civil War, 656–661):
  Ali vs Aisha (Muhammad's wife) + Muawiyah (Umayyad)
  Battle of the Camel (656): Ali beats Aisha's faction
  Battle of Siffin (657): Ali vs Muawiyah → arbitration
  Arbitration outcome: disputed; Kharijites split from Ali
  Ali assassinated 661 by Kharijite
  → Muawiyah I becomes caliph → Umayyad dynasty begins

SECOND FITNA + KARBALA (680):
  Husayn ibn Ali (Ali's son): refuses to recognize Yazid I (Umayyad)
  Marches toward Kufa with small party (~70 fighters)
  Battle of Karbala: Husayn + companions massacred
  → Karbala = the foundational tragedy of Shia Islam
  → Martyrdom of Husayn commemorated annually (Ashura)

THE PERMANENT SPLIT:
  Sunni: caliphate = political leadership; any qualified Muslim
  Twelver Shia: imam = religious AND political; must be from Ali's line
                (12 imams; last went into occultation 874 — will return)
  Ismaili Shia: accepted 7th imam (Ismail); Fatimid caliphs claimed this
```

## Rashidun Caliphate (632–661)

The "Rightly Guided" caliphs (from Sunni perspective). The founding generation.

```
EXPANSION UNDER RASHIDUN:
  Abu Bakr (632–634): unified Arabia; suppressed Ridda (apostasy) wars
  Umar I (634–644): military genius; conquered Syria, Mesopotamia, Egypt
    Battle of Yarmouk 636: decisive defeat of Byzantine Syria
    Battle of al-Qadisiyyah 636: decisive defeat of Sasanian Persia
    Jerusalem surrendered 638: Umar received the keys personally
  Uthman (644–656): expanded east (Persia fully conquered); standardized Quran
  Ali (656–661): civil war absorbed his reign

WHAT THEY BUILT:
  Administration: kept existing Byzantine/Persian bureaucracies initially
  Tax system: jizya (non-Muslim poll tax); kharaj (land tax)
  Military settlements: misr (garrison cities) — Basra, Kufa, Fustat
  No permanent conversion pressure initially (tax revenue from non-Muslims)
```

## Umayyad Caliphate (661–750) — The Arab Empire

The Umayyads created what was functionally an Arab ethnic empire, with Arabs privileged over converts.

```
UMAYYAD EXPANSION AT MAXIMUM EXTENT:
  WEST: Conquest of North Africa (~700), then Iberia (711)
  NORTHWEST: Battle of Tours/Poitiers 732 — stopped by Charles Martel
              (Carolingian mayor of palace; grandfather of Charlemagne)
              Significance of Tours: debated. Probably not decisive —
              Arabs were raiding more than settling by this point
  EAST: Transoxiana (modern Uzbekistan/Tajikistan); edge of India
  NORTHEAST: Battle of Talas 751 — Arab-Abbasid forces + Tibetan allies
              defeat Tang Chinese army
              → Chinese expansion into Central Asia stopped
              → Arab/Islamic cultural sphere in Central Asia
              (Also: Chinese paper-making technology captured → spreads west)

ADMINISTRATION:
  Damascus as capital: reflects Umayyad Syrian power base
  Arabic made the administrative language
  Arab Muslims: privileged military class, exempt from some taxes
  Mawali (non-Arab converts): initially second-class → major grievance
  Dhimmis (Christians, Jews, Zoroastrians): protected but taxed + restricted

IBERIAN CONQUEST (al-Andalus):
  711: Tariq ibn Ziyad (Berber general) crosses from Morocco
       (Gibraltar = Jabal al-Tariq — "mountain of Tariq")
  Battle of Guadalete: Visigothic King Rodrigo defeated
  Within 7 years: almost all Iberia under Islamic control
  Córdoba: Umayyad capital in Iberia
  The culture: sophisticated, multilingual, relatively tolerant
  Umayyad Córdoba vs Christian kingdoms: see 03-WESTERN-EUROPE.md
```

### Umayyad Collapse (750)

The Abbasid Revolution overthrew the Umayyads. Key grievances: Arab privilege over non-Arab Muslims; Umayyad perceived impiety.

One Umayyad prince escaped: Abd al-Rahman I fled to Iberia, established an **Emirate of Córdoba** — independent of the Abbasid caliphate. His descendants eventually proclaimed themselves caliphs (929 CE). The Umayyad line survived in Iberia until 1031.

## Abbasid Caliphate (750–1258) — The Islamic Golden Age

```
ABBASID SHIFT:
  Capital moved: Damascus → Baghdad (new city, built 762)
  Character: cosmopolitan, Persian-influenced, multi-ethnic
  Persians and other non-Arabs included in administration

HARUN AL-RASHID (r. 786–809):
  The caliphate of the 1001 Nights
  Diplomatic exchanges with Charlemagne
  Height of Abbasid power and cultural flourishing
  Bayt al-Hikma (House of Wisdom): translation movement
    Greek philosophy, science, medicine → Arabic
    Preserved Aristotle, Euclid, Galen
    Original contributions: algebra (al-Khwarizmi — algorithm/algebra),
    optics (Ibn al-Haytham), medicine (Ibn Sina/Avicenna)

AL-MAMUN (r. 813–833):
  Son of Harun al-Rashid; won civil war against brother
  Mutazilite theology: rationalist; tried to impose it
  Major scientific patronage
  Astronomical observations; measurement of Earth's circumference

THE BUYID ECLIPSE (945–1055):
  Buyids (Iranian Shia dynasty) from the Caspian
  Entered Baghdad as "protectors" 945
  Caliph kept as religious figurehead; Buyids held real power
  Buyid Amir al-Umara = effective ruler
  Caliph: ceremonial; religious legitimacy only

THE SELJUK TURKS (1055–):
  Replaced Buyids as military power in Baghdad
  Great Seljuk Sultan Tughril: entered Baghdad 1055
  Also "protectors" of the caliph — but Sunni
  Malik-Shah (1072–1092): Seljuk empire at maximum extent
  Manzikert 1071: Seljuks defeat Byzantines → Anatolia falls
  Seljuks fragmented after Malik-Shah's death
```

## Fatimid Caliphate (909–1171)

The Ismaili Shia counter-caliphate — a direct challenge to Abbasid legitimacy.

```
FATIMID EXPANSION:
  Founded in Tunisia by Ubayd Allah al-Mahdi (claimed Fatimid descent)
  Conquered Egypt 969; founded Cairo (al-Qahira = "The Triumphant")
  al-Azhar mosque (972) → eventually became al-Azhar University
    (world's oldest continuously operating university, some argue)
  Peak: controlled North Africa, Egypt, Palestine, Syria, western Arabia
        (including Mecca and Medina briefly)

FATIMID JERUSALEM:
  1099: Crusaders captured Jerusalem one year after Fatimids had
        retaken it from the Seljuks
  Fatimids had expelled Seljuks and offered the Crusaders a deal
  The Crusaders rejected it and took Jerusalem anyway

SALADIN ABOLISHES FATIMIDS (1171):
  Saladin (Salah al-Din) Yusuf ibn Ayyub: Kurdish general
  Sent to Egypt to help Fatimids against Crusaders
  Became vizier; last Fatimid caliph died 1171
  Saladin simply stopped praying in the Fatimid caliph's name
  → Fatimid caliphate ended without confrontation
  → Saladin then united Egypt + Syria under Sunni Ayyubid rule
```

## Regional Sultanates — The Fractured Islamic World

After the Abbasid caliphate became nominal, real power fragmented into regional sultanates.

```
MAJOR REGIONAL POWERS (~900–1300):

SAMANID DYNASTY (819–999, Persia/Central Asia):
  Persian renaissance; Persian language (Farsi) revival
  Patronized Ferdowsi (Shahnameh epic); Ibn Sina

GHAZNAVID SULTANATE (975–1186, Afghanistan/Punjab):
  Mahmud of Ghazni: 17 raids into India (1000–1025)
  Took enormous plunder; began Islamization of northern India

AYYUBID DYNASTY (1171–1260, Egypt/Syria):
  Founded by Saladin (Kurdish)
  SALADIN (r. 1174–1193):
    Reunified Egypt and Syria
    Battle of Hattin 1187: destroyed Crusader army
    Recaptured Jerusalem 1187 (treated captives relatively well)
    Defeated Richard I at Acre area; negotiated with him
    Treaty of Jaffa 1192: Christians keep coastal strip; access to Jerusalem

MAMLUK SULTANATE (1250–1517):
  Mamluks = slave soldiers (originally Turkic/Circassian military slaves)
  Revolt against Ayyubids 1250; took power
  BATTLE OF AIN JALUT 1260:
    Mongols (Hulagu Khan's army) vs Mamluks
    Near Nazareth in Palestine
    FIRST major Mongol defeat — Mongols stopped
    Significance: Mamluks prevented Mongol conquest of Egypt/North Africa
  Mamluks kept a nominal Abbasid caliph in Cairo (after Mongols killed the real one)
  → Religious legitimacy theater until Ottomans absorbed Egypt 1517
```

## The Abbasid Caliphate's End (1258)

```
MONGOL INVASION OF IRAQ:
  Hulagu Khan (grandson of Genghis, brother of Kublai and Möngke)
  Ordered to pacify the Middle East
  Siege of Baghdad January–February 1258
  Al-Musta'sim (last Abbasid caliph): refused to surrender; refused to
    mobilize; apparently paralyzed by indecision
  Baghdad sacked: al-Musta'sim wrapped in a carpet, trampled by horses
    (Mongol reluctance to spill royal blood directly)
  THE HOUSE OF WISDOM destroyed: books thrown into the Tigris River
    (river ran black with ink, then red with blood, allegedly)
  Hundreds of thousands killed

SIGNIFICANCE:
  The caliphate as a genuine political institution ended permanently
  The Abbasid "caliph" in Cairo under the Mamluks was a figurehead
  "Caliph of all Muslims" became a contested, largely symbolic claim
  Ottoman sultans used it; Saudi state used it; it was never uncontested

  The Mongol sack also destroyed agricultural infrastructure:
  Irrigation networks of Iraq — built over millennia —
  were never fully repaired
  Iraqi agricultural capacity permanently reduced
```

## The Almoravids and Almohads (Iberia and Maghreb)

Two Berber reform dynasties that shaped the later Reconquista period.

```
ALMORAVID DYNASTY (1040s–1147):
  Origin: reform movement among Sanhaja Berbers in Mauritania
  Ibna Yasin: religious reformer
  Yusuf ibn Tashfin: military leader; founded Marrakesh
  Called to Iberia 1086 by taifa kings after Toledo fell
  Battle of Sagrajas 1086: defeated Alfonso VI of Castile
  → Reunified al-Andalus under Almoravid rule
  → Ended relative tolerance of taifa period
  Decline: unable to stop Christian advance long-term; internal rebellions

ALMOHAD DYNASTY (1121–1269):
  Ibn Tumart: Berber religious reformer; declared himself Mahdi
  Abd al-Mumin: military leader; conquered Maghreb + Iberia
  More theologically strict than Almoravids
  Persecuted Jews and Christians more aggressively than predecessors
  Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa 1212: decisive defeat vs unified Christian kingdoms
  → Almohad power broken; al-Andalus fragmented again
  → Christian advance could not be stopped
  → Only Granada survived (under Nasrid dynasty) until 1492
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Sunni vs Shia is not just political**
The split has theological dimensions (nature of religious authority, infallibility of imams in Twelver Shia) as well as political origins. But the political-territorial aspect has been primary in most historical conflicts: Fatimid (Shia) vs Abbasid (Sunni) was a geopolitical rivalry using religion as its idiom.

**"Caliph" changed meaning over time**
Early Rashidun: successor to Muhammad, combined political and religious authority. Abbasid peak: elaborate court, cosmopolitan ruler, religious legitimation. Late Abbasid: figurehead for actual Buyid/Seljuk rulers. Mamluk period: symbolic legitimation tool. Ottoman: contested claim. The word carries centuries of semantic drift.

**Saladin was not Arab**
Saladin was Kurdish. His family (Ayyubids) were from Tikrit (in modern Iraq). He built his career in service to Nur al-Din (Zengid), a Turkic ruler. The most famous defender of Arab Islam against the Crusaders was neither Arab nor Turkish.

**"Islamic Golden Age" survived the caliphate**
The cultural flowering associated with Baghdad continued in various forms in the successor states. Persian literature (Ferdowsi, Rumi, Omar Khayyam), mathematics, astronomy, medicine — these flourished under various sultanates and local rulers, not necessarily under the Abbasids.
