# Flowers and Reproduction — Pollination, Double Fertilization, and Seed Dispersal

## The Big Picture

Flowers are the evolutionary innovation that drove the Cretaceous angiosperm radiation (~130 Mya). By co-evolving with animal pollinators, flowering plants achieved directed mating across large distances — far more efficient than wind scattering pollen randomly. The result: ~300,000 angiosperm species vs. ~1,000 gymnosperm species, and virtually all human food crops.

```
ANGIOSPERM REPRODUCTIVE CYCLE OVERVIEW
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FLOWER → POLLINATION → FERTILIZATION → SEED → DISPERSAL → GERMINATION

         Attracts         Pollen tube    Embryo +     Animal/wind/    Seedling
         pollinator       grows to       endosperm    water/
         (or wind)        ovule          formed       explosive
                                         (double
                                         fertilization)

vs. GYMNOSPERM:
  No flower, no fruit, no double fertilization
  Wind pollination only (primarily)
  "Naked seed" on cone scale, not enclosed in ovary
  → less efficient pollination, less efficient dispersal
```

## Flower Anatomy

```
COMPLETE FLOWER ANATOMY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    PISTIL (female)
                    ┌──────────────────┐
                    │ Stigma           │ ← Receives pollen; sticky surface
                    │ Style            │ ← Pollen tube grows through this
                    │ Ovary            │ ← Contains ovules (future seeds)
                    └──────────────────┘

   STAMEN (male)
   ┌───────────────┐
   │ Anther        │ ← Pollen production; 2 × 2 pollen sacs (microsporangia)
   │ Filament      │ ← Stalk supporting anther
   └───────────────┘

PETALS (corolla):   Attract pollinators; color, shape, UV patterns
SEPALS (calyx):     Protect flower bud; often green, leaf-like
RECEPTACLE:         Flower base; becomes fruit tissue in pomes (apple)
NECTARIES:          Secretes nectar (reward for pollinator)

TERMINOLOGY:
  Complete flower:  has all 4 parts (sepal, petal, stamen, pistil)
  Perfect flower:   has both stamen AND pistil (bisexual)
  Imperfect flower: only stamen OR pistil (unisexual)
    - Monoecious: both on same plant (corn: tassel ♂, silk ♀)
    - Dioecious: on different plants (holly, ginkgo, date palm, marijuana)
  Actinomorphic:    radially symmetric (rose, buttercup)
  Zygomorphic:      bilaterally symmetric (orchid, snapdragon, pea)
```

## Pollen and Pollen Tube Growth

```
POLLEN DEVELOPMENT AND GERMINATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MICROSPOROGENESIS (in anther):
  Diploid microsporocyte (2n) → meiosis → 4 microspores (n)
  Each microspore → mitosis → pollen grain:
    Tube cell nucleus (grows the tube)
    Generative cell → divides into 2 sperm cells

POLLEN GRAIN WALL:
  Exine: sporopollenin (most durable biological polymer known)
    → survives millions of years in sediment → palynology
    Species-specific patterns used for forensic identification
  Intine: cellulose inner wall

POLLEN TUBE GROWTH after landing on stigma:
  Recognition reaction (self-incompatibility check)
  Exine ruptures → tube cell nucleus controls tube growth
  Tube grows through style (cm to dm depending on species)
  Speed: up to 1 cm/hour in fastest species (maize silks: 30cm!)
  Generative cell divides → 2 sperm cells follow in tube

Longest pollen tube: some palms — tube must grow ~30cm to reach ovule
```

## Double Fertilization: The Angiosperm Innovation

Double fertilization is unique to angiosperms and is arguably the key innovation that enabled their ecological dominance.

```
DOUBLE FERTILIZATION MECHANISM
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
POLLEN TUBE arrives at OVULE:
  Ovule contains EMBRYO SAC (8 cells, all haploid n):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Egg cell (n)                                                    │
  │  2 Synergids (n): guide pollen tube (chemotropism signals)       │
  │  2 Polar nuclei (n + n = 2n) — central cell                     │
  │  3 Antipodal cells (n): role unclear                             │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

FERTILIZATION (simultaneous):
  Sperm 1 + Egg cell → ZYGOTE (2n) → EMBRYO
  Sperm 2 + 2 Polar nuclei → ENDOSPERM (3n = TRIPLOID)

WHY DOUBLE FERTILIZATION IS BRILLIANT:
  Endosperm only develops when an egg is also fertilized.
  In gymnosperms: nutritive tissue forms regardless of fertilization
    → wasted investment if egg wasn't fertilized
  In angiosperms: no fertilization → no endosperm → no wasted resource
  This makes angiosperms more metabolically efficient at seed production.

ENDOSPERM in food:
  Corn: seed = mostly endosperm (starchy white part)
  Wheat: 80% endosperm (white flour)
  Coconut: liquid and solid endosperm
  Coffee: endosperm is the "bean"
  Rice: grain = embryo + endosperm
```

### Pollination Biology Economics

The flower is offering a bribe (nectar, pollen as food) to a pollinator that accidentally carries pollen to the next flower. The relationship is mutualistic but each party is exploiting the other.

```
MUTUALISM ECONOMICS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Flower perspective: get pollen to correct species' stigma
  Adaptations: species-specific morphology, scent, UV patterns
  Ensure same-species transfer: guide marks, floral tubes, rewards

Pollinator perspective: get food (nectar, pollen) efficiently
  Adaptations: floral constancy (bees prefer one species at a time)
  Memory: remember profitable flower types → efficient foraging

CHEATING:
  Plant side: Rewardless flowers (many orchids, ~8,000 species)
    Orchids that mimic: bee females (sexual deception), nectar sources,
    fungal mushrooms, other flower species
    Bee attempts to mate → picks up orchid pollen (pollinium) → moves to next
  Pollinator side: Nectar robbing (bees that bite through base of flower
    to access nectar without touching stamens/pistils — bypasses the deal)
```

## Seed Dispersal Mechanisms

```
SEED DISPERSAL STRATEGIES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ANEMOCHORY (wind dispersal):
  Samaras: winged fruits spin as they fall (maple, ash)
    Engineering: autorotating flight, maximizes drift time
  Plumes/pappus: dandelion, thistle, willowherb
    Fine silk hairs → parachute effect
  Dust seeds: orchids (<1mg), some parasites
    Essentially float in air currents

HYDROCHORY (water dispersal):
  Coconut: buoyant husk + waterproof seed coat → months at sea
  Mangrove propagules: some germinate on tree, fall into water
  Alder catkins: float in streams
  Lotus: fruit pod floats, seeds viable for 1,000+ years

ZOOCHORY — EXTERNAL (epizoochory):
  Hooks, spines, burrs cling to fur/clothing
  Arctium (burdock): hooked bracts → velcro (Mestral's 1941 inspiration)
  Galium (cleavers): tiny hooks on entire plant
  Grass seeds: barbed awns work into soil + fur

ZOOCHORY — INTERNAL (endozoochory):
  Fleshy fruit → animal eats → seed passes through gut
  Seeds often scarified by digestive acids → enhanced germination
  Seed dispersal requires: fruit color (visible to specific animal),
                           timing (ripe = digestible + seed mature),
                           size (match target animal gape size)
  Dodo-avocado hypothesis: avocado's huge seed was adapted for gut
    of giant Pleistocene ground sloths/horses now extinct
    → avocado fruit is "ecological anachronism"

AUTOCHORY (self-dispersal/explosive):
  Squirting cucumber (Ecballium): ~10m range
  Witch hazel, touch-me-not (Impatiens): spring-loaded pod
  Sandbox tree (Hura crepitans): explosive capsule, >60m range, seeds at >70 m/s
  Hazel catapult: catapult mechanism via hygroscopic tissue
```

## Apomixis and Asexual Seed Production

Some plants produce seeds without fertilization — a remarkable bypass of the entire sexual process.

```
APOMIXIS (asexual seed production)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Diplospory: embryo sac forms from unreduced cell (2n)
            → embryo is 2n (genetically identical to mother)
Apospory: somatic cells replace normal meiosis in embryo sac

Examples:
  Citrus (common): seeds contain multiple embryos
    One sexual embryo + several apomictic (clonal) embryos
    → commercial nucellar seedlings = clones (uniform rootstocks)
  Dandelion (Taraxacum): obligate apomict in many species
    → each dandelion = genetically distinct clone of its mother
  Hawkweed (Hieracium): Mendel's "difficult" plant — apomictic,
    didn't follow his rules → contributed to his doubts near career end
  Some grasses, roses, blackberries, mangoes

AGRICULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  Apomixis research: if we could engineer apomixis into F1 hybrids,
  farmers could save seeds that breed true (same yield as parent)
  → would eliminate farmer seed dependency on corporations
  → active research area (CGIAR, various universities)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is double fertilization? | Two sperm cells: one fertilizes egg (→ 2n embryo), one fertilizes polar nuclei (→ 3n endosperm) |
| What is endosperm? | Triploid nutritive tissue surrounding embryo; wheat flour, corn, rice are mostly endosperm |
| Why are orchids evolutionarily unique in pollination? | ~8,000 species use deceptive pollination (no reward); most mimic other flowers, insects, or food sources |
| What is anemochory? | Wind dispersal; samara/pappus/dust seeds |
| What is the dodo-avocado hypothesis? | Avocado's large seed adapted for gut of extinct Pleistocene megafauna; now has few effective dispersers |
| What is apomixis? | Asexual seed production without fertilization; offspring genetically identical to mother |
| How is burdock related to velcro? | George de Mestral observed burdock hooks on his dog's fur (1941) → invented velcro |

## Common Confusion Points

**Gymnosperms are not "primitive" — they're just differently specialized.** Conifers dominate boreal forests globally because their adaptations (needle leaves, drought/frost tolerance, mycorrhizal networks) are supremely effective in cold climates. Evolution doesn't have a direction.

**Not all flowers are showy.** Wind-pollinated flowers (grasses, oaks, birches) are tiny, greenish, petal-less, and produce enormous pollen quantities. About 10% of flowering plant species are wind-pollinated.

**Self-incompatibility prevents inbreeding.** Many plants have molecular systems that reject self-pollen (or pollen from close relatives). This is why you often need two apple cultivars to get good fruit set — the trees can't pollinate themselves.

**"Fruit" is not what you think it is.** A pea pod is a fruit (seed-bearing structure from ovary). A strawberry is not (receptacle tissue with seeds on surface). A pine cone is neither (gymnosperm — no ovary enclosing seeds). The botanical definition is strict; the culinary definition is loose.
