# Trees and Forests — Structure, Succession, and Old Growth

## The Big Picture

A forest is not a collection of trees — it is a self-organizing, multi-trophic ecosystem with physical structure that spans vertical space from canopy to forest floor. The emergent properties of a mature forest (microclimate buffering, carbon storage, water regulation, biodiversity) cannot be recovered for centuries after disturbance. Understanding forests requires understanding both individual tree biology and ecosystem-level dynamics.

## Forest Stratification and Light

Light attenuation through the canopy follows an approximate Beer-Lambert relationship:

```
LIGHT ATTENUATION (approximate)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Above canopy:         100% full sunlight
Top of canopy:        ~50% (reflection, self-shading)
Mid-canopy:           ~10-20%
Below canopy:         ~1-5% (temperate deciduous)
                      ~0.1-1% (tropical rainforest)

Forest floor of temperate forest: ~1-5% PAR (photosynthetically active radiation)
Forest floor of rainforest: ~0.5-1% PAR

Shade plants: light saturation point at 25-50 μmol/m²/s
Sun plants: light saturation at 500-2,000 μmol/m²/s
Full midday summer sun: ~2,000 μmol/m²/s

Spring ephemeral strategy: wildflowers (trout lily, bloodroot, trillium)
  flower March-April before canopy leafout
  Complete entire annual cycle in 6-8 weeks of available light
  Store energy in bulbs/corms during rest of year
```

## Forest Succession

### Primary Succession (bare rock → forest)

```
PRIMARY SUCCESSION (approximately, temperate)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bare rock/glacial scour/volcanic ash
    │ lichen and mosses
    │ weather rock to soil, accumulate organic matter
    ▼ (decades)
Pioneer herbs and annuals
    │ fireweed, annual grasses, nitrogen-fixing herbs
    │ begin soil building
    ▼ (5-20 years)
Perennial grasses and forbs
    │ taller vegetation shades out pioneers
    ▼ (10-30 years)
Shrubs
    │ willow, alder, shrubby cinquefoil
    │ alder: nitrogen-fixing → accelerates succession
    ▼ (20-50 years)
Pioneer trees
    │ birch, aspen, poplar, pine → fast-growing, sun-demanding
    │ create shade below them → enable next stage
    ▼ (50-100 years)
Shade-tolerant trees begin
    │ (maple, beech, hemlock, spruce depending on region)
    │ grow in understory of pioneer trees
    ▼ (50-200 years)
"Climax" / late-successional forest
    │ steady state (not static — disturbance creates internal dynamics)
```

### Secondary Succession (disturbed forest → forest)

Much faster because soil is intact, seed banks remain, and mycorrhizal networks often survive:

```
SECONDARY SUCCESSION (abandoned farm → forest, ~150 years)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Year 1-3:     Annual weeds (crabgrass, ragweed, goldenrod)
Year 3-10:    Perennial grasses + shrubs (blackberry, elderberry)
Year 10-25:   Pioneer trees (gray birch, aspen, pin cherry, eastern red cedar)
Year 25-75:   Early-successional forest (young canopy, dense)
Year 75-150:  Mid-successional forest
Year 150+:    Late-successional (depending on region, climate, disturbance)
```

**The "climax community" concept:** F.E. Clements (~1920) proposed that succession ends in a single stable "climax" community determined by climate. Now known to be wrong — forests are not in equilibrium; disturbance (fire, wind, disease, drought) continually resets patches. The landscape is a mosaic of different successional stages.

## Old-Growth Forest: What It Is and Why It Matters

### Definition

Old-growth forest is **not defined by tree age alone**. It is a complex ecosystem with specific structural attributes that take centuries to develop.

```
OLD-GROWTH CHARACTERISTICS (temperate forest)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LIVE STRUCTURE:
  Multiple canopy layers (not uniform height)
  Large-diameter trees (species and site dependent: often 60cm+ DBH)
  Trees of multiple ages (uneven-aged structure)
  Gap dynamics (individual trees die → small gaps → regeneration patches)

DEAD WOOD:
  Standing dead trees (snags): wildlife habitat (cavity nesters, insects)
  Large-diameter snags: secondary cavity nesters (woodpeckers create, others reuse)
  Fallen logs (coarse woody debris): salamanders, fungi, invertebrates, seedbed
  Multi-decay stage logs: each stage supports different species assemblages

SOIL:
  Deep, complex soil food web
  Extensive mycorrhizal networks (old trees have largest networks)
  Buried wood (soil carbon)
  Accumulated organic layers

MICROCLIMATE:
  Temperature buffering: 3-8°C cooler in summer, warmer in winter
  Humidity maintenance: old-growth interior keeps higher RH
  Wind moderation: multilayer structure reduces wind speed

BIOLOGY:
  Species dependent on structural complexity:
  Northern spotted owl (old-growth obligate in Pacific NW)
  Pacific giant salamander (needs large stream-covering logs)
  Marbled murrelet (nests only on large-diameter mossy canopy branches)
```

### Old-Growth Forest Minimums

| Region | Approximate minimum age |
|--------|------------------------|
| Temperate deciduous (eastern US) | 150-200 years |
| Pacific Northwest conifer | 200-500 years |
| European beech | 200-400 years |
| Tropical rainforest | Centuries to millennia for full complexity |
| Boreal forest | 100-200 years |

**Old-growth extent:** <5% of original US old-growth forests remain. Most old-growth globally was logged 18th-20th century. Second-growth forests (even 100-year-old) lack the structural complexity — snags, fallen logs, multi-layer canopy — of true old-growth.

## Disturbance Ecology

### The Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis

Joseph Connell (1978): **Maximum biodiversity occurs at intermediate disturbance levels.**

```
INTERMEDIATE DISTURBANCE HYPOTHESIS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LOW DISTURBANCE:                     HIGH DISTURBANCE:
  Late-successional species           Only pioneer/weedy species survive
  dominate; exclude earlier           Low diversity — only stress-tolerant
  colonists; low diversity            species persist

             ↓    INTERMEDIATE DISTURBANCE    ↓
  Mix of early and late successional species coexist
  No single species dominates long enough to exclude others
  Maximum species diversity

Examples of intermediate disturbance:
  Fire (periodic): longleaf pine savanna, chaparral, boreal
  Flood: riparian forests, river banks
  Wind throw: temperate forest gaps
  Large herbivores: savanna grassland/tree balance
```

### Fire-Adapted Ecosystems

```
FIRE-ADAPTED ECOSYSTEMS AND THEIR FIRE REGIMES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LONGLEAF PINE (Pinus palustris), SE USA:
  Fire return interval: 1-3 years (ground fire)
  Fire adaptations: thick bark (insulates cambium), self-pruning,
                    grass stage (seedling grows huge root before stem)
  Without fire: shade-tolerant oaks suppress longleaf pine
  Original range: 90M acres → now <3% remains
  Ecosystem: ~500 vertebrate species, ~900 plant species

CHAPARRAL (California):
  Fire return interval: 25-150 years
  Adaptations: lignotuber (fire-sprouting rootcrown), heat-activated seeds
  Serotinous cones: Knobcone pine — heat opens cones, releases seeds
  After fire: explosive wildflower display (decades of seeds germinate)

BOREAL FOREST:
  Fire return interval: 50-150 years (varies by region)
  Fire cycle: key driver of boreal landscape mosaic
  Post-fire: fireweed → aspen/birch → spruce/fir regeneration
  Jack pine: serotinous cones (need fire heat to open)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What % of light reaches tropical rainforest floor? | ~0.5-1% |
| What is the intermediate disturbance hypothesis? | Max biodiversity at intermediate disturbance levels; too little = late-successional dominance, too much = only pioneers |
| What defines old-growth beyond tree age? | Structural complexity: dead wood (snags, logs), multiple canopy layers, deep soil web |
| What are serotinous cones? | Cones sealed with resin, only opened by heat; release seeds after fire (jack pine, lodgepole pine) |
| What is Pando? | ~80,000 quaking aspen stems sharing one root system; largest single organism by mass |
| What was wrong with Clements' climax theory? | Forests are not static equilibria — disturbance continuously resets patches; no single endpoint |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Virgin forest" ≠ "old-growth."** Virgin = never logged (human definition). Old-growth = specific structural complexity (ecosystem definition). Some virgin forest is young (naturally burned/windthrown recently). Some "logged" old-growth patches have been recovering for centuries.

**Shade tolerance ≠ inability to grow in sun.** Shade-tolerant trees (maple, hemlock, beech) grow fine in sun — they just can also survive in shade. Shade-intolerant trees (birch, aspen, cherry) grow fast in sun and die in shade.

**Succession doesn't "want" to reach forest.** In grasslands maintained by fire and grazing (great plains, African savanna), forest never establishes — this is not arrested succession, it's an alternative stable state. Suppressing fire converts these to shrubland/forest — often with biodiversity loss.

**Old-growth isn't necessarily the highest carbon storage per acre.** Middle-aged forests (50-150 years) may accumulate carbon faster than old-growth (which is near equilibrium). Old-growth is highest in total carbon stored. Different metrics, different conclusions.
