# Plant Cell Physiology — Cell Wall, Chloroplast, and Water

## The Big Picture

Plant cells differ from animal cells in three major ways: a rigid cell wall, a chloroplast for photosynthesis, and a large central vacuole. These three features determine everything about how plants grow, move water, respond to stress, and defend themselves.

```
PLANT CELL ANATOMY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  OUTER CELL WALL
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Primary wall: cellulose microfibrils in pectin/hemicellulose gel    │
  │ Secondary wall (when present): S1+S2+S3 cellulose + LIGNIN         │
  └──────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┘
                             │ (connected to next cell via Plasmodesmata)
  PLASMA MEMBRANE            │
  ┌──────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CYTOPLASM                                                           │
  │                                                                      │
  │  ┌─────────────────┐    ┌───────────────────────────────────────┐   │
  │  │   CHLOROPLAST   │    │              VACUOLE                  │   │
  │  │ (photosynthesis)│    │   (up to 90% of cell volume)         │   │
  │  │ outer membrane  │    │   tonoplast membrane                 │   │
  │  │ inner membrane  │    │   water, pigments, secondary         │   │
  │  │ thylakoid stack │    │   metabolites, enzymes               │   │
  │  │ (grana)         │    │   TURGOR PRESSURE source             │   │
  │  │ stroma          │    └───────────────────────────────────────┘   │
  │  └─────────────────┘                                                │
  │                                                                      │
  │  + Nucleus, ER, Golgi, Mitochondria (same as animal cells)         │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Cell Wall

### Composition and Layering

```
PRIMARY CELL WALL (all living plant cells)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Component        % dry weight    Function
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cellulose        20-30%          Structural backbone; glucose polymer
                                  in β-1,4 linkage → unbranched, crystalline
                                  microfibrils (humans cannot digest β-1,4)
Hemicellulose    20-30%          Cross-links cellulose microfibrils
                                  (xyloglucan, arabinoxylan — diverse)
Pectin           30-35%          Gel-like matrix; Ca²⁺ cross-linked
                                  Cement between cells (middle lamella)
                                  Jam is heated pectin + sugar
Structural       ~10%            Extensin (glycoprotein — stiffening)
proteins
Water            ~80-90%         Primary wall is mostly water
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECONDARY CELL WALL (added inward after cell stops growing)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
S1 layer: crossed cellulose microfibrils
S2 layer: ~80% of wall thickness; cellulose at low angle to fiber axis
S3 layer: thin, helical
+ LIGNIN impregnates all layers → waterproof, rigid, incompressible
  Lignin chemistry: aromatic polymer from phenylpropanoid precursors
  Lignin = wood. Can't be digested by most organisms.
  White rot fungi are the primary lignin decomposers.
```

### Plasmodesmata

Cytoplasmic channels (30-60 nm diameter) running through cell walls, connecting adjacent cells. The plasma membrane and ER (as desmotubule) are continuous through plasmodesmata.

```
TRANSPORT PATHWAYS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Symplastic:  Through plasmodesmata — cell cytoplasm
             is connected network. Proteins, mRNA,
             viruses, sugars can move symplastically.

Apoplastic:  Through cell walls and intercellular
             spaces. Water and dissolved minerals
             move apoplastically.

Casparian    Strip of suberin in root endodermis.
Strip:       Blocks apoplastic pathway → forces
             all water/solutes through symplast.
             Quality control filter for soil → xylem.
```

## The Chloroplast

Chloroplasts are endosymbiotic cyanobacteria (Margulis endosymbiosis hypothesis; confirmed by genome evidence). They retain their own circular DNA, divide by binary fission, and have two membranes.

### Anatomy

```
CHLOROPLAST CROSS-SECTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Outer membrane (permeable to small molecules)
Inner membrane (selective; protein-import machinery)
  │
  └─ STROMA (fluid interior)
       Location of: Calvin cycle enzymes (RuBisCO, etc.)
                    Chloroplast DNA (circular)
                    Ribosomes (prokaryotic 70S type)
                    Starch granules
       │
       └─ THYLAKOID MEMBRANE SYSTEM
            Stack of thylakoids = GRANUM (pl. grana)
            Connected by stroma lamellae
            Thylakoid membrane contains:
              Photosystem II (P680): water splitting, O₂ release
              Photosystem I (P700): NADPH production
              Cytochrome b6f complex: proton gradient
              ATP synthase (CF₀CF₁): ATP production
            Thylakoid lumen: H⁺ accumulation (drives ATP synthesis)
```

### Light Reactions (in thylakoid membrane)

```
LIGHT REACTIONS FLOW
H₂O → [PSII] → [Cyt b6f] → [PSI] → NADPH
                    │
                    ▼ (proton gradient)
                   ATP

Inputs:  Light, H₂O
Outputs: ATP, NADPH, O₂ (released)
Purpose: Generate energy currency for Calvin cycle
```

### Calvin Cycle (in stroma)

RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) is the enzyme that fixes CO₂ into organic carbon. It is the most abundant protein on Earth (~0.7 billion tonnes globally).

```
CALVIN CYCLE (simplified)
CO₂ + RuBP → [RuBisCO] → 2× PGA (3-carbon)
PGA → G3P (using ATP + NADPH from light reactions)
G3P → sugar outputs + RuBP regeneration
```

RuBisCO's oxygenase problem: see `04-LEAVES-PHOTOSYNTHESIS.md` for full treatment.

## The Central Vacuole

In mature plant cells, the central vacuole can occupy 80-90% of cell volume. The tonoplast (vacuolar membrane) maintains this compartment.

### Functions

| Function | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Turgor pressure** | Water influx by osmosis → vacuole expands → pushes against rigid wall |
| **Storage** | Sugars, organic acids, amino acids, secondary metabolites, pigments |
| **Defense** | Toxic compounds (alkaloids, glucosinolates) sequestered away from cytoplasm |
| **Protein degradation** | Vacuolar proteases; plant "lysosome" equivalent |
| **pH regulation** | Acidic interior (~5.5 pH) |
| **Pigmentation** | Anthocyanins in vacuoles → red/blue/purple flowers/fruits |

## Water Potential and Turgor

Plant water movement is governed entirely by water potential — water moves from high ψ to low ψ.

```
WATER POTENTIAL EQUATION
ψ = ψs + ψp

ψ  = total water potential (MPa)
ψs = solute potential (always negative; solutes lower water potential)
ψp = pressure potential (positive in turgid cells, negative in xylem)

Pure water at atmospheric pressure: ψ = 0
Wilted cell (no turgor):           ψp ≈ 0, ψ = ψs (negative)
Turgid cell:                       ψp > 0 (wall pressure counteracts ψs)
Xylem during transpiration:        ψ = -1 to -5 MPa (negative pressure!)
```

### Cohesion-Tension Mechanism (water moving up trees)

```
HOW WATER REACHES A 116m REDWOOD

Leaf stomata open → water vapor diffuses out → evaporation from mesophyll
     │
     ▼ (transpiration pull)
Water column in xylem under tension (negative pressure)
     │
     ▼ (cohesion)
Water molecules hydrogen-bond to each other → continuous column
     │
     ▼ (adhesion)
Water molecules bond to xylem wall → prevents column separation
     │
     ▼
Water absorbed from soil by osmosis at root

Maximum measured xylem tension: -5 MPa (50 atm negative pressure)
This works because of water's unique cohesion properties (hydrogen bonds)
```

**Breaking point:** Air bubble (embolism) breaks the column. Trees vulnerable in drought. Some species (oak) refill embolized vessels in spring; others (conifers) cannot and must grow new vessels.

## Phloem Transport

Phloem moves sugars (mainly sucrose) from sources (leaves making it) to sinks (roots, fruits, meristems using it). Pressure-flow hypothesis (Ernst Münch, 1930):

```
PRESSURE-FLOW MODEL
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SOURCE (leaf)                        SINK (root/fruit)
──────────────                       ─────────────────
Photosynthesis makes sucrose         Sucrose unloaded, metabolized/stored
Active loading into sieve tube       Active unloading from sieve tube
     │                                     │
     │ ← High osmotic concentration        │ ← Low osmotic concentration
     │ ← Water enters by osmosis           │ ← Water exits by osmosis
     │ ← HIGH PRESSURE (8-25 bar)          │ ← LOW PRESSURE
     │                                     │
     └─────────────────────────────────────┘
              Bulk flow: high → low pressure
              Sucrose moves at ~0.5-2 m/hour

Sieve tube elements: alive but no nucleus (degraded at maturity)
Companion cells: metabolically active, support sieve tubes
```

## Hormones and Cell Growth

Plants coordinate growth via chemical signals. The full set comprises nine major hormones; the auxin:cytokinin ratio is the master switch between growth modes.

| Hormone | Source | Key Functions | Engineering note |
|---------|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Auxin (IAA)** | Shoot apex | Cell elongation; phototropism; apical dominance | High auxin:cytokinin → root formation; low ratio → shoot development |
| **Cytokinins** | Root tips | Cell division; delay senescence | Counter-signal to auxin; promotes branching when apex removed |
| **Gibberellins (GA)** | Various | Stem elongation; seed germination | Green Revolution: Rht dwarfing gene desensitizes wheat to GA → shorter, harvest-stable stems |
| **Abscisic acid (ABA)** | Wilting tissues | Stomata closure; seed dormancy; drought response | Rapid production within minutes of water deficit; systemic stress signal |
| **Ethylene** | Ripening fruits | Fruit ripening; leaf abscission; stress response | Autocatalytic: ethylene → more ethylene → sigmoid ripening curve |
| **Brassinosteroids** | Shoots/leaves | Cell expansion; vascular differentiation; stress tolerance | Steroid hormones; brassinosteroid-insensitive mutants are dwarf with dark-green leaves |
| **Jasmonic acid (JA)** | Wound sites | Herbivory defense; trichome induction; reproductive development | Biosynthesized from linolenic acid via lipoxygenase; systemic wound signal via vascular transport |
| **Salicylic acid (SA)** | Infected tissue | Systemic acquired resistance (SAR); pathogen defense | Precursor to aspirin; SA wave propagates ahead of pathogen front (broadcast defense alert) |
| **Strigolactones** | Root tips | Suppress axillary bud outgrowth; stimulate mycorrhizal symbiosis | Exuded into soil as symbiosis signal; parasitic plant *Striga* germinates on strigolactone detection |

**Hormone interaction logic:** Auxin:cytokinin ratio is a two-variable control signal — tissue culture exploits this directly (high auxin → callus grows roots; high cytokinin → callus grows shoots). JA and SA act antagonistically: SA-mediated pathogen resistance suppresses JA-mediated herbivory resistance, creating a tradeoff that some pathogens exploit by inducing SA signaling in the host.

## Common Confusion Points

**Cell wall ≠ cell membrane.** Wall is outside the plasma membrane, rigid, made of cellulose. Membrane is a lipid bilayer, selectively permeable. Plants have both; animal cells only have the membrane.

**Chloroplasts ≠ mitochondria.** Both have double membranes and their own DNA. Chloroplasts capture energy (photosynthesis). Mitochondria release energy (respiration). Plants have both — they respire all the time and photosynthesize when light is available.

**Phloem flow direction ≠ one-way downward.** Phloem moves from source to sink. In spring, when roots release stored sugar to growing shoots, phloem flows upward. In summer, photosynthesizing leaves send sugar down to roots. Direction is context-dependent.

**Turgor ≠ wilting-prevention only.** Turgor is also growth force. Cell expansion is driven by loosening the wall (expansins) + turgor pressure. Stop turgor → stop growth. Young seedlings grow by turgor, not cell division.
