# Stems and Wood — Primary Growth, Secondary Growth, and Dendrochronology

## The Big Picture

The stem is the plant's scaffold and distribution system — conducting water and sugar between root and leaf, and in woody plants, accumulating structural support that makes century-spanning growth possible. The transition from herbaceous to woody growth involves a lateral meristem (cambium) that is absent in grasses but present in all trees.

```
STEM TYPES AND GROWTH MODES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HERBACEOUS                          WOODY (trees, shrubs)
──────────────                      ─────────────────────
Primary growth only                 Primary + secondary growth
No cambium (monocots) or            Vascular cambium (continuous ring)
  limited cambium (annual dicots)   Cork cambium (periderm)
Soft, flexible, green               Hard, rigid, bark-covered
Annual or perennial with            Perennial; can grow for centuries
  underground storage               Unlimited height potential
Grasses, dandelions, tomatoes       Oaks, pines, palms (monocot exception)

EXCEPTION: Palms are monocots with no vascular cambium
           but achieve tree form via primary thickening meristem
           → very different wood anatomy (palm "wood" = vascular bundles
              dispersed throughout, not rings)
```

## Primary Growth: From Meristem to Stem

The apical meristem (shoot apex) produces all primary tissues. This is indeterminate growth — the meristem does not consume itself.

```
PRIMARY STEM ANATOMY (young dicot stem — cross section)

Outside:
  Epidermis (single layer, cuticle, stomata, trichomes)
      │
  Cortex (parenchyma, large cells, sometimes collenchyma)
      │
  Endodermis / pericycle (less defined than in root)
      │
  VASCULAR BUNDLES (arranged in ring in most dicots)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Each vascular bundle:                              │
  │  [Phloem — outer] ← sieve tubes + companion cells   │
  │  [Cambium — middle] ← divides to produce both       │
  │  [Xylem — inner] ← vessels + tracheids              │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
      │
  Pith (central parenchyma, often hollow in older stems)

MONOCOT STEM: vascular bundles scattered throughout (no ring)
              no cambium between bundles
              cannot do secondary growth
```

## Secondary Growth: Building Wood

Secondary growth is what makes a tree. The vascular cambium (a one-cell-thick lateral meristem) divides repeatedly, pushing new cells inward (→ wood) and outward (→ phloem).

```
SECONDARY GROWTH TIMELINE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YEAR 1: Procambium between primary xylem and phloem connects
        → forms continuous vascular cambium ring

EACH GROWING SEASON:
  Vascular cambium (1 cell layer) divides:
  ├── Inward: new secondary xylem (wood) cells
  └── Outward: new secondary phloem cells
  Cambium shifts outward as wood accumulates inside it

OVER TIME:
                         ←────────────────────────────┐
                         (outward)                    │
  Bark  | Phloem | Cambium |  Xylem (wood)             │
        growing                 growing               │
        outward                 inward                │
                                                      │
  Result: bark expands (or cracks off and replaces via cork cambium)
          wood core grows every year
          the oldest wood is at the center
```

### Cork Cambium (Phellogen)

When secondary growth begins, the original epidermis can no longer cover the expanding circumference. The cork cambium (phellogen) forms in the cortex and produces:
- **Cork (phellem)**: dead cells impregnated with suberin (hydrophobic). The outer bark.
- **Phelloderm**: living cells inward from cork cambium

Cork oak (*Quercus suber*): exceptionally thick cork layer. Harvested every 9-12 years without killing the tree (Portugal/Spain cork industry). Wine bottle corks, insulation, cork tiles.

## Wood Anatomy

### Gymnosperm Wood (Softwood)

```
GYMNOSPERM (pine, spruce, fir, larch) WOOD
Simple anatomy:
  TRACHEIDS: long, tapered dead cells
             both water conduction AND support
             connect via bordered pits (circular pits with membrane)
             ~90-95% of wood volume
  RAY PARENCHYMA: living cells running radially
                  resin channels (in pine, fir)
                  horizontal transport

No vessels (except Gnetophytes)
No true wood fibers

Relatively uniform structure → consistent properties
Easier to work
Makes up most structural timber (dimensional lumber, framing)
```

### Angiosperm Wood (Hardwood)

```
ANGIOSPERM (oak, maple, cherry, mahogany) WOOD
Complex anatomy:
  VESSELS: wide, short cells stacked end-to-end
           perforations between cells → water highway
           Only function: water conduction
  WOOD FIBERS: long, thick-walled, dead
               Only function: support
               "Hardwood" name comes from high fiber content
  TRACHEIDS: less important than in gymnosperms
  RAYS: wider, more prominent than in gymnosperms

TWO VESSEL ARRANGEMENTS:
  Ring-porous: vessels concentrated in earlywood (oak, ash, elm)
               → visible pore ring in cross section
  Diffuse-porous: vessels scattered throughout (maple, birch, cherry)
                  → uniform appearance
```

### Heartwood vs Sapwood

```
HEARTWOOD/SAPWOOD TRANSITION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  BARK                                                           CENTER
  ─────── Phloem ─── Cambium ───────────────────────────────────────────
                                SAPWOOD              HEARTWOOD
                                ────────────────      ──────────────────
                                Living parenchyma     Dead cells
                                Active water          No longer conducts
                                  conduction          water
                                Light colored         Dark colored
                                New wood              Old wood (core)
                                Stores starch         Contains:
                                  (autumn) →            tannins
                                  consumed              oils
                                  in spring             resins
                                Attacked by           Generally resistant
                                  fungi/insects         to rot

Heartwood formation: programmed cell death, tyloses block vessels,
secondary metabolites deposited. Creates the durable core of the tree.

Examples:
Teak heartwood: tectoquinone (natural oil) → extremely rot-resistant
Black walnut: juglone → allelopathic (suppresses nearby plants)
Cedar: thujaplicin → moth-repellent (cedar chests)
```

## Annual Rings and Dendrochronology

### Ring Formation

```
ANNUAL RING FORMATION (temperate climate)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SPRING (cambium reactivates):
  EARLYWOOD: large-diameter vessels/tracheids
             thin walls
             low density
             Light-colored band

SUMMER/FALL:
  LATEWOOD: small-diameter vessels/tracheids
            thick walls
            high density
            Dark-colored band

WINTER: cambium dormant → sharp boundary visible

ONE YEAR = one earlywood + one latewood band = one annual ring

Width variation:
  Wide ring = good growing season (moisture + warmth)
  Narrow ring = drought/cold/damage/competition
  Missing ring = severe stress year (common in very old trees)
```

**Tropics:** Trees growing in aseasonal tropics (no winter dormancy) may lack clear annual rings, or form rings based on wet/dry seasons rather than temperature seasons. Dendrochronology most reliable in temperate/boreal regions.

### Dendrochronology

Andrew Ellicott Douglass (1867–1962): astronomer who developed dendrochronology ~1901-1929 while looking for solar cycle evidence in tree rings.

```
CROSSDATING METHOD
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Problem: Individual trees have different lifespans
         No single tree covers thousands of years

CROSSDATING:
Living tree (known dates)                 Overlapping ring patterns
      ┌──────────────────┐
      │ 1900 — present   │── ring pattern ──┐
      └──────────────────┘                  │
                                  Dead log (from old building)
                              ┌──────────────────┐
                              │ ~1700 — 1920     │── ring pattern ──┐
                              └──────────────────┘                  │
                                                            Ancient timber
                                                        ┌──────────────────┐
                                                        │ ~1000 — 1750 CE  │
                                                        └──────────────────┘
                                                                   │
                                                           Subfossil wood
                                                       ┌──────────────────┐
                                                       │ 8,000+ BP        │
                                                       └──────────────────┘

Longest continuous chronologies:
  German oak/pine: ~12,500 years (extending to Bronze Age)
  Bristlecone pine (ITRDB): ~9,000+ years
  Irish oak (Belfast): ~7,000+ years
```

### Applications

| Application | Principle |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Dating archaeological wood** | Match ring pattern to master chronology → calendar year |
| **Calibrating radiocarbon dating** | Known ring years → known ¹⁴C values → calibration curve |
| **Climate reconstruction** | Ring width/density → past temperature/precipitation |
| **Volcanic eruption dating** | Narrow "frost rings" from volcanic aerosol cooling |
| **Forest history** | Growth patterns reveal drought events, fire years |

## Wood Properties and Timber

```
WOOD PROPERTIES THAT MATTER IN USE

JANKA HARDNESS (resistance to indentation, lbf)
  Balsa:              70     (softest commercial timber)
  Eastern white pine: 380
  Douglas fir:        620    (most-used construction timber)
  Hard maple:         1,450  (flooring, butcher block)
  White oak:          1,360
  Teak:               1,070  (marine, outdoor — rot resistance)
  Brazilian teak:     3,540
  Lignum vitae:       4,500  (hardest commercial timber — ships' bearings)

DENSITY AND MECHANICAL PROPERTIES:
  Douglas fir: strong, stiff, moderate density → structural lumber
  Spruce: lightweight, resonant → musical instruments (violin top)
  Oak: ring-porous, high strength, rot-resistant → barrels, flooring
  Balsa: very light, weak → model aircraft, flotation

MOISTURE MOVEMENT:
  Wood is hygroscopic — absorbs/releases moisture → swells/shrinks
  Primarily tangential (across ring) > radial > longitudinal
  This is why solid wood furniture/floors must acclimate to humidity
  Engineered wood products (plywood, CLT) partially solve this
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What produces wood? | Vascular cambium (lateral meristem) dividing inward |
| Why can't grasses grow into trees? | No vascular cambium → no secondary growth (palms are exception via primary thickening) |
| Heartwood vs sapwood function? | Sapwood = active water conduction; heartwood = dead core with secondary metabolites |
| What is the Janka hardness of Douglas fir? | 620 lbf — main structural timber |
| What is crossdating? | Matching ring patterns across overlapping tree lifespans to build master chronology |
| How old is the longest tree ring chronology? | ~12,500 years (German oak/pine master chronology) |
| Why is ring-porous vs diffuse-porous wood different to work? | Ring-porous (oak) has visible open pores → needs filling before finishing; diffuse-porous more uniform |

## Common Confusion Points

**Softwood ≠ soft, hardwood ≠ hard.** Balsa is a hardwood (angiosperm). Yew is a softwood (gymnosperm) that is harder than many hardwoods. Soft/hard refers to the presence/absence of vessels and fibers in wood anatomy, not physical hardness.

**Annual rings ≠ always annual.** Tropical trees may form rings based on wet/dry seasons (2+ per year) or have no rings. Some very old bristlecone pines skip rings in severe drought years. Rings must be validated by crossdating, not just counted.

**"Old growth" ≠ just big trees.** Old-growth forest is defined by structural complexity: multiple canopy layers, standing dead trees (snags), fallen logs, and the fungal/soil ecosystem that comes with centuries of undisturbed succession. A big tree in a second-growth forest is not old-growth.

**Cambium ≠ cambium layer visible on outside.** The vascular cambium is one cell thick, invisible to the naked eye, located between bark (phloem) and wood (xylem). The thin greenish layer under bark that smells like pine = sapwood + phloem, not the cambium itself.
