# Economic Botany — Timber, Fiber, Medicinals, Rubber, and Dyes

## The Big Picture

Economic botany is the study of plants used by humans. Before the 20th century, plants were the primary source of materials, medicines, fuels, and dyes. Industrial chemistry replaced many botanical sources, but key commodities (natural rubber, cotton, plant-derived pharmaceuticals, timber) remain irreplaceable or competitively superior to synthetic alternatives.

```
ECONOMIC BOTANY CATEGORIES AND SCALE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TIMBER AND WOOD PRODUCTS:
  Global roundwood production: ~3.8 billion m³/year (2021)
  Most harvested natural material by volume globally

FIBER CROPS:
  Cotton: ~25M tonnes/year (most important natural textile)
  Flax, hemp, jute: smaller volumes but significant

MEDICINAL PLANTS:
  ~25% of all prescription drugs: plant-derived or plant-inspired
  ~80% of global population: plant-based primary healthcare (WHO est.)
  Botanical pharmaceutical market: ~$150B+ globally

RUBBER:
  Natural rubber: ~14M tonnes/year
  No synthetic substitute for demanding applications (aircraft tires, surgery gloves)

DYES AND COLORANTS:
  Mostly synthetic now, but natural dyes: growing market (~$6B)
  Saffron, indigo, madder, carmine — niche high-value

OILS AND RESINS:
  Essential oils: $7B market
  Resins: frankincense, myrrh, copal, naval stores (turpentine, rosin)
```

## Timber

### Softwood vs Hardwood

```
TIMBER TAXONOMY (economic classification)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SOFTWOOD (gymnosperm = conifer):
  ~80% of global lumber production
  Faster growing → plantation forestry viable
  Key species:
    Douglas fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii): most important US structural timber
      Janka: 620 lbf; stiff, strong; Pacific Northwest and mountain West
    Sitka spruce (Picea sitchensis): highest strength/weight of any timber
      Musical instruments (Stradivarius violins, guitar tops)
    Scots pine (Pinus sylvestris): European construction timber
    Loblolly pine (P. taeda): SE USA plantation; paper pulp + lumber
    Southern yellow pine (multiple species): Janka: 1,225 lbf; harder than many hardwoods
    Radiata pine (Pinus radiata): NZ/Chile plantation; fast growing; paper + construction

HARDWOOD (angiosperm):
  Higher density, slower growth, premium price
  Furniture, flooring, millwork
  Key species:
    Red oak (Quercus rubra): Janka: 1,290 lbf; most common US hardwood flooring
    White oak (Q. alba): Janka: 1,360; tighter pores → whiskey/wine barrel
    Sugar maple (Acer saccharum): Janka: 1,450; flooring, butcher block, bowling alleys
    Black walnut (Juglans nigra): Janka: 1,010; premium furniture, gunstocks
    Teak (Tectona grandis): Janka: 1,070; natural oils → rot/marine resistance
    Mahogany (Swietenia macrophylla): Janka: 900; fine furniture, boats; now CITES listed
    Lignum vitae (Guaiacum spp.): Janka: 4,500; hardest commercial timber
      Self-lubricating (resin); ship propeller shaft bearings; croquet balls
    Balsa (Ochroma pyramidale): Janka: 70; lightest commercial timber; model making
      Hardwood (angiosperm) but exceptionally low density
```

### Wood Properties and Use

```
WOOD PROPERTIES GUIDE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRUCTURAL:
  Modulus of elasticity (stiffness): Spruce > pine > oak
  Compressive strength: parallel to grain; Douglas fir 38 MPa (psi equiv ~5,500)
  Shear strength: perpendicular to grain (weak point of wood)
  Ratio of strength to weight: Sitka spruce = highest

DURABILITY (natural rot resistance via heartwood chemistry):
  Excellent:    Teak (tectoquinone), Black locust, White oak, Osage orange
  Good:         Red cedar (thujaplicin), Douglas fir, redwood
  Poor:         Most sapwood, many tropical hardwoods
  None:         Pine sapwood, birch, ash

DIMENSIONAL STABILITY:
  Wood shrinks/swells perpendicular to grain (tangential > radial)
  Quartersawn lumber: grain at 60-90° to face → stable (radial movement)
  Flatsawn lumber: growth rings parallel to face → more movement
  Engineered wood: plywood/CLT/LVL/oriented strand board → much more stable

ACOUSTIC PROPERTIES:
  Guitar tops: Sitka spruce or cedar (high stiffness/density ratio)
  Violin (Stradivarius): spruce top + maple back/sides
  Marimba bars: rosewood or padauk (specific density + damping)
```

## Fiber Crops

### Cotton (*Gossypium* spp.)

```
COTTON BOTANY AND PRODUCTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Species:
  G. hirsutum (Upland cotton): ~90% of world production; Americas origin
  G. barbadense (Pima/Egyptian cotton): long-staple, luxury; Americas origin
  G. arboreum, G. herbaceum: Old World species; minor today

Fiber biology:
  Seed hair (trichome): single epidermal cell elongates from seed coat
  Grows up to 35mm (long staple = premium)
  Primary cell wall: cellulose + pectins
  Secondary cell wall: ~90% pure cellulose

  Fiber quality parameters:
    Length (staple): short (<25mm), medium (25-32mm), long (32-36mm), extra long (>36mm)
    Fineness (micronaire): diameter; finer = softer = higher value
    Strength: more stress per unit area
    Color: off-white → cream → light-spotted

PROCESSING:
  Cotton gin (Eli Whitney, 1793): separates seed from fiber mechanically
    Before gin: 1 pound clean cotton/day by hand
    After gin: 50 pounds/day → cotton boom → plantation expansion → slavery expansion
  Ginning: gin separates lint from seed
  Baling: press to 227kg bales
  Ginning byproducts: cottonseed oil (salad oil, cooking), cottonseed meal (livestock)

ENVIRONMENTAL ISSUES:
  Water use: ~10,000 L per kg cotton (high but depends on rainfall vs irrigation)
  Aral Sea disaster: Soviet irrigation for Central Asian cotton → sea desiccated
  Pesticide use: historically high (10% of world insecticides on 2.5% land)
  Bt cotton: dramatically reduced insecticide use in adopting regions
  Organic cotton: ~1% of global production; higher price
```

### Flax (*Linum usitatissimum*)

```
FLAX (LINEN) AND LINSEED OIL
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Two uses from one plant:
  Fiber variety (long-stemmed): bast fiber → linen
  Oilseed variety (branched): seed oil → linseed oil

FIBER PROCESSING (retting):
  Dew retting: lay cut stems in field → dew + bacteria → pectin degradation
  Water retting (fastest, most consistent): immerse in water → bacteria digest pectin
  Retting separates bast fibers from woody core (hurds)
  Heckling: comb fibers into parallel → hackled flax → spinning

LINEN PROPERTIES:
  Strongest natural fiber (stronger than cotton)
  Cool to touch: high thermal conductivity → draws heat away
  Low elasticity: "linen wrinkles" is structural reality (no stretch to recover)
  Moisture-wicking, breathable
  Gets softer with washing (pectin residue softens)

OLDEST TEXTILE:
  Flax fiber: 34,000-year-old twisted fibers found (Dzudzuana cave, Georgia)
  Egyptian mummy wrappings: linen
  Renaissance paintings on linen canvas
```

### Hemp (*Cannabis sativa*)

```
HEMP BOTANY AND ECONOMICS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cannabis sativa varieties:
  Drug cannabis: high THC (>0.3%), low-fiber types
  Industrial hemp: low THC (<0.3% dry weight), high-fiber types
  Legal distinction in US: >0.3% THC = Schedule I; <0.3% = legal (2018 Farm Bill)

FIBER PROPERTIES:
  Longest natural bast fiber (up to 2m per plant)
  Stronger than cotton and flax
  Low water use (no irrigation typically needed)
  Fast-growing (120 days to harvest)
  Pest-resistant (naturally low pest pressure)

USES:
  Traditional: rope, canvas, paper (US Declaration of Independence: hemp paper)
  Modern: building insulation (hempcrete), composite materials, textiles,
          paper, bioplastics, CBD extraction (not fiber use)

HISTORICAL NOTE:
  "Canvas" = derived from "cannabis" (Latin/Greek)
  Sails of sailing ships: hemp canvas
  US Prohibition of cannabis 1937: Marijuana Tax Act
  Hemp also criminalized → US hemp production eliminated
  1960-2018: no legal US hemp production
  2018: Farm Bill restoration → explosive growth
```

## Medicinal Plants

### The Aspirin Story

```
ASPIRIN — DISCOVERY AND SYNTHESIS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional knowledge:
  Willow bark (Salix spp.): used for pain/fever since ancient Egypt, Hippocrates
  Active compound: salicin (glycoside)

1828: Henri Leroux isolates salicin (active component)
1838: Raffaele Piria synthesizes salicylic acid from salicin
1853: Charles Gerhardt synthesizes acetylsalicylic acid (but discards it)
1897: Felix Hoffmann (Bayer): re-synthesizes, purifies → ASA
1899: Bayer patents and markets as "Aspirin"
  A = acetyl; spirsäure = German for salicylic acid (from Spiraea plant)

MECHANISM (not understood until 1970s):
  John Vane (Nobel 1982): aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX-1/COX-2
  → inhibits prostaglandin synthesis
  → anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, analgesic, antiplatelet (COX-1)

Aspirin as antiplatelet:
  Low-dose aspirin (81mg): irreversibly inhibits platelet COX-1
  → decreased thromboxane A₂ → decreased platelet aggregation
  → reduced cardiovascular event risk in high-risk patients
```

### Quinine

```
QUININE — FROM CINCHONA BARK TO MALARIA TREATMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Source: Cinchona officinalis, C. calisaya (Rubiaceae), Andean bark
  Discovered by Quechua peoples of Peru → transmitted to Spanish (~1633)
  "Jesuit's bark" → controversial in Protestant England (religious medicine)

1820: Pierre-Joseph Pelletier + Joseph Bienaimé Caventou isolate quinine
  → standardized dosing for malaria treatment
  → British Empire expansion into tropical Africa/India became possible
  → "Gin and tonic": quinine in tonic water (bitter) + gin (palatable) + lime

MECHANISM:
  Malaria parasite (Plasmodium) digests hemoglobin → toxic heme released
  Parasite normally polymerizes heme into non-toxic hemozoin
  Quinine: inhibits heme polymerization → toxic heme accumulates → parasite dies
  Selective toxicity: human cells don't digest hemoglobin

1944: Woodward + Doering: total synthesis of quinine (elegant, but too expensive)
  Still extracted from Cinchona bark today
  ~700 tonnes quinine/year (primarily from Congo, Indonesia)

Modern malaria treatment: artemisinin (from Artemisia annua) has largely replaced
  quinine as first-line treatment, but quinine still used for P. vivax + drug-resistant
```

### Taxol (Paclitaxel)

```
TAXOL — FROM PACIFIC YEW TO CANCER TREATMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Discovery:
  1962: NCI (National Cancer Institute) plant screening program
        Bark samples from Pacific yew (Taxus brevifolia) submitted by USDA
  1964: Jonathan Hartwell's group: cytotoxic activity in extract
  1967: Monroe Wall + Mansukh Wani: isolate taxol
  1979: Susan Horwitz: mechanism of action (microtubule stabilization)
  1984: First human clinical trial begins
  1992: FDA approval for ovarian cancer
  1994: Commercial launch (Bristol-Myers Squibb)

MECHANISM:
  Most cancer drugs: prevent spindle formation (vincristine) or inhibit DNA replication
  Taxol: OPPOSITE — stabilizes microtubules (prevents depolymerization)
  → spindle cannot segregate chromosomes → mitosis arrest → apoptosis
  (Note: this mechanism was not previously known; Taxol helped discover microtubule dynamics)

SUPPLY PROBLEM:
  1992: 3 Pacific yew trees (mature) needed per patient per year
  Pacific yew: very slow-growing, old-growth forest understory
  Old-growth harvest + cancer demand = unsustainable

  SOLUTION (1994): Semi-synthesis from Taxus brevifolia NEEDLES
    Needles contain: 10-deacetylbaccatin III (precursor)
    Chemical synthesis: 2 steps from precursor → taxol
    Needles harvestable without killing tree
    Now also: Taxus baccata + T. media plantation grown

CURRENT PRODUCTION:
  Fermentation: Taxomyces andreanae (endophytic fungus in yew bark)
    Produces taxol directly → no harvest needed in research quantities
    Commercial scale still problematic
  Plant cell culture: commercial production from Taxus cell culture
```

## Rubber (*Hevea brasiliensis*)

```
NATURAL RUBBER — BOTANY AND GEOPOLITICS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SOURCE: Hevea brasiliensis (Euphorbiaceae); tropical tree; ~25 years productive life

BIOLOGY:
  Latex: milky fluid in laticifers (canal cells) throughout plant
  Contains polyisoprene (cis-1,4-polyisoprene): natural rubber polymer
  Also: proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, inorganic salts

TAPPING:
  Diagonal incision in bark: cut through phloem, not cambium (heals)
  Latex drips into cup: 30-60 mL per tapping / 1 tree
  Tapped every 2-3 days; yields for 25-30 years

COLONIAL TRANSFER (Henry Wickham, 1876):
  Amazon: rubber tree native; indigenous Brazilians used rubber for generations
  1876: Henry Wickham collects 70,000 Hevea seeds → Kew Gardens, London
  ~2,800 seedlings survive → sent to Singapore, Malaya, Ceylon
  1900-1920: Malayan plantation rubber establishes global dominance
  1913: Malayan rubber exceeds Amazon wild rubber production
  Amazon rubber boom collapse (~1912): cannot compete with plantation efficiency

VULCANIZATION (Charles Goodyear, 1844):
  Natural rubber: sticky when hot, brittle when cold; temperature-sensitive
  Goodyear: heated rubber + sulfur → sulfur cross-links between polymer chains
  → stable over temperature range; elastic, tough, durable
  Mechanism: sulfide bonds cross-link cis-polyisoprene chains; prevent chain slippage

SYNTHETIC RUBBER:
  Developed WWII when Japan cut off Malayan rubber supply
  SBR (styrene-butadiene rubber): ~60% of synthetic; tires, shoes
  NBR (nitrile rubber): oil-resistant; O-rings, fuel hoses
  EPDM: weather-resistant; roofing, weatherstripping
  Silicone: extreme temperature range; medical devices

WHY NATURAL RUBBER PERSISTS (~40% of all rubber):
  Flex-fatigue resistance: aircraft tires, truck tires
  Surgery gloves: protein-rubber combination
  Natural latex medical devices: cannot be fully replicated by synthetic
  Condoms: ~70% still natural rubber
```

## Dyes from Plants

```
HISTORICAL PLANT DYES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INDIGO (Indigofera tinctoria, I. suffruticosa):
  Blue dye; Indian subcontinent origin
  Active compound: indigotin (reduced form: indigo white/leuco-indigo)
  Vat dyeing: reduce indigo (sodium hydrosulfite) → soluble leuco form
  Fiber absorbs leuco-indigo → air oxidizes → indigotin precipitates in fiber
  Resist dyeing: batik, tie-dye — wax/binding prevents dye access
  1895-1904: BASF synthesizes indigo chemically → devastates Indian indigo trade
  Modern: ~80% synthetic; "natural indigo" revival in premium denim/natural textiles

WOAD (Isatis tinctoria):
  European indigo substitute (same molecule, lower concentration)
  Celtic/Pict warriors: blue body paint (likely, though evidence thin)
  Medieval European blue dye before Indian indigo trade
  German Woad towns: Erfurt, Gotha — woad trade = enormous wealth ~1100-1600

MADDER (Rubia tinctorum):
  Red dye; Middle East origin
  Active compounds: alizarin, purpurin (anthraquinones)
  "Turkey red" technique: oil + alum mordant + madder → brilliant red
  British Army "redcoats": dyed with madder
  1869: Graebe + Liebermann synthesize alizarin (first synthetic dyestuff from coal tar)
  → destroys madder trade globally within decades

WELD (Reseda luteola): yellow dye; alum mordant
WELD + WOAD = GREEN (additive color mixing on fiber)
WELD + MADDER = ORANGE

SAFFRON (Crocus sativus): yellow dye (too expensive except for luxury)
TURMERIC (Curcuma longa): yellow dye (fugitive — fades); still important in South Asian textiles

RESINS AND OTHER:
  Henna (Lawsonia inermis): red-brown; lawsone molecule; skin/hair
  Logwood (Haematoxylum campechianum): black/purple; haematoxylin; still used in histology staining
  Cochineal (not plant — scale insect): carmine red; currently major red food colorant
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Hardwood" and "softwood" describe plant type, not wood hardness.** Balsa is a hardwood (angiosperm). Southern yellow pine is a softwood (gymnosperm) but harder than most hardwoods by Janka measure.

**Natural rubber ≠ synthetic rubber in all applications.** Synthetic rubber (SBR, NBR, EPDM) is excellent for most uses but fails in high-flex applications (aircraft tires) and medical uses. The flex-fatigue property of natural rubber is not fully understood or replicable. ~40% of global rubber is still natural for this reason.

**"Plant-based" medicine ≠ ineffective.** ~25% of modern pharmaceutical drugs are derived from or inspired by plant compounds. Morphine, aspirin, taxol, quinine, artemisinin, vincristine, digoxin — all major pharmaceuticals from plants. The active compound is isolated and used, not the whole plant.

**Synthetic indigo and natural indigo are chemically identical.** The molecule indigotin is indigotin whether from Indigofera or BASF. "Natural indigo" premium is about farming system, not molecular difference. This doesn't make natural indigo worthless (sustainable farming, heritage craft) but the chemistry is identical.
