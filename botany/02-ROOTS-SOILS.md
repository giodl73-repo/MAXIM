# Roots and Soils — Underground Architecture

## The Big Picture

The root system is the plant's interface with the soil ecosystem — extracting water, minerals, and forming partnerships with fungi and bacteria that extend its reach far beyond what roots alone can access. This underground economy is as complex as anything above ground.

```
ROOT SYSTEM AND SOIL INTERACTIONS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SOIL SURFACE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    ROOT SYSTEM
    ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  TAPROOT system (dicots):      FIBROUS system (monocots): │
    │  ┌─────┐                       ─────────────────────────  │
    │  │     │ primary root          ─────────────────────────  │
    │  │     │ dominates             ─────────────────────────  │
    │  │  ───┤                       ─────────────────────────  │
    │  │  ───┤ laterals              Adventitious roots from    │
    │  │  ───┤                       stem base, spread wide     │
    │  │  ───┤                       not deep                   │
    │  │     │                                                   │
    └──┴─────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Root hairs: maximum absorption zone, 1-4mm behind root tip
    Mycorrhizal hyphae: extend 10-100x beyond root hair zone
    Rhizosphere: 1-3mm zone of intense microbial activity
    Bulk soil: comparatively sterile
```

## Root Architecture

### Taproot vs Fibrous

| Feature | Taproot (dicots) | Fibrous (monocots) |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Primary root fate | Persists, dominates | Degenerates, replaced |
| Origin of laterals | Primary root | Stem (adventitious) |
| Depth | Can be deep (carrot: 3m+) | Usually shallow (<1m) |
| Water access | Deep water table | Wide shallow moisture |
| Examples | Dandelion, carrot, oak | Grass, wheat, corn, rice |

### Root Anatomy (cross section)

```
YOUNG DICOT ROOT — CROSS SECTION (inside out)

  Epidermis (single cell layer, root hairs)
      │
  Cortex (parenchyma, large cells, air spaces)
  [apoplastic pathway for water until...]
      │
  ENDODERMIS (one cell layer)
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CASPARIAN STRIP: band of suberin in cell walls      │
  │  Blocks apoplastic pathway                          │
  │  All water/solutes must pass THROUGH endodermal cells│
  │  = quality control checkpoint                       │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
      │
  Pericycle (meristematic, source of lateral roots)
      │
  VASCULAR CYLINDER (stele)
  ├── Xylem (star-shaped in cross section)
  └── Phloem (alternates with xylem arms)
```

**The Casparian strip's significance:** It prevents soil contaminants from entering the xylem by passive diffusion. Heavy metals (lead, cadmium) in soil are partially blocked here. But once a cell is damaged, the barrier fails — hence why hyperaccumulator plants can take up metals.

```
      │

```

## Root Hairs

Root hairs are the primary interface between plant and soil solution. They are single-cell extensions of epidermal cells — not separate cells.

```
ROOT HAIR STATISTICS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Length:           0.1-1.5 mm
Diameter:         ~10 μm
Lifetime:         days to weeks (constantly replaced as root grows)
Surface area:     ~600× increase over bare root surface
Density:          50-2,000 per mm² of root surface

Absorption mechanism:
H⁺ ATPase pumps H⁺ out → membrane potential (−100 to −200 mV)
    → K⁺, NH₄⁺ enter down electrochemical gradient
    → NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, H₂PO₄⁻ via H⁺-cotransport
Water: by osmosis (solute potential difference)
```

### Phosphate Limitation

Phosphorus (P) is often the limiting nutrient. H₂PO₄⁻ moves extremely slowly in soil (diffusion coefficient ~10⁻¹³ m²/s vs. ~10⁻⁹ for water). Root hairs deplete a ~1mm zone around them. This is why mycorrhizal networks are essential for P nutrition.

## Mycorrhizal Networks

~90% of land plant species form mycorrhizal associations. This is the norm, not a specialized adaptation.

### Two Main Types

```
ECTOMYCORRHIZAE                      ARBUSCULAR MYCORRHIZAE (AM)
────────────────────                  ────────────────────────────────
Fungi: Basidiomycetes/Ascomycetes     Fungi: Glomeromycota (ancient group)
  (many familiar forest mushrooms)    ~80% of all land plant species
Hosts: mostly trees (oak, pine,       Hosts: grasses, most herbs, most
  beech, birch, eucalyptus)             crops (wheat, corn, potato, tomato)
Structure: mantle around root surface  Structure: fungi ENTER root cells
           Hartig net between cells     Arbuscules: branched structures inside
           Hyphae extend into soil       cortex cells (~100 μm)
No cell entry                         Vesicles: lipid storage structures

Trade: plant → sugar (20-40% photosynthate!)
       fungus → P, N, water, micronutrients
```

### The "Wood Wide Web"

The concept (Suzanne Simard, 1997 *Nature*): trees exchange carbon via mycorrhizal networks. Older "mother trees" may subsidize seedlings.

```
WHAT IS ESTABLISHED:
  ✓ Carbon transfer between plants via mycorrhizal networks is real
  ✓ Labeled ¹³C experiments show inter-plant carbon flow
  ✓ Larger trees have larger fungal networks
  ✓ Seedlings in established forests have better fungal associations
  ✓ Cutting old trees affects overall fungal network health

WHAT IS CONTESTED:
  ? Whether transfer is "cooperative" vs. passive leakage
  ? Whether "mother tree" subsidization is adaptive/deliberate
  ? Scale of the effect on forest dynamics vs. other factors

CONSENSUS: Real phenomenon; ecological significance still being quantified.
  The popular press has overstated intentionality.
```

## Rhizosphere Chemistry

The rhizosphere — the soil zone directly influenced by root exudates — is a metabolic hotspot. 5-21% of all photosynthetically fixed carbon is released by roots into the soil.

```
ROOT EXUDATES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Low molecular weight:               High molecular weight:
  Sugars (glucose, fructose)         Mucilage (polysaccharides)
  Amino acids                        Proteins (enzymes)
  Organic acids (citrate, malate,
    oxalate — mobilize P, Fe, Al)
  Phenolics
  Volatile compounds (CO₂, H₂)

EFFECTS:
  Feed rhizosphere bacteria (10-1,000× more bacteria than bulk soil)
  Mobilize mineral nutrients (organic acids dissolve phosphate minerals)
  Attract Rhizobium and mycorrhizal fungi
  Suppress pathogens (some exudates are antimicrobial)
  Allelochemicals (suppress competitor plants — black walnut juglone)
```

## Nitrogen Fixation (Detailed)

See `food-plants/02-LEGUMES.md` for full treatment. Supplement:

```
BIOLOGICAL NITROGEN FIXATION — GLOBAL CONTEXT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Natural BNF (before agriculture):    ~140 Tg N/year (lightning adds ~5 Tg)
Agricultural BNF (legumes):          ~40 Tg N/year
Haber-Bosch (industrial):            ~120 Tg N/year (uses ~1% of world energy)

Without BNF: Earth's soils would be rapidly nitrogen-depleted.
The evolution of nitrogen fixation (Archean era, ~2 Bya) was a planetary
transformation comparable to oxygenic photosynthesis.
```

**Azospirillum and free-living fixers:** Some nitrogen-fixing bacteria live in soil without legume symbiosis (*Azobacter*, *Clostridium*, cyanobacteria). They fix ~10-50 kg N/ha/year — significant but less than root nodule symbiosis.

## Soil Food Web

Soil is not passive substrate — it is an ecosystem.

```
SIMPLIFIED SOIL FOOD WEB
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ENERGY INPUT:    Plant roots + litter (organic matter)
                         │
         ┌───────────────┼──────────────────┐
         ▼               ▼                  ▼
    BACTERIA         FUNGI               Other decomposers
    (bacteria-        (fungal          (soil algae, actinomycetes)
    vores: proto-     ivores:
    zoa, nematodes)   nematodes,
         │            mites)             │
         └────────────────────────────────┘
                         │
                    SECONDARY PREDATORS
                    (mites, springtails, centipedes)
                         │
                    TERTIARY PREDATORS
                    (beetles, spiders)
                         │
              Nutrient mineralization (N, P, S released)
                         │
              Plant-available nutrients
```

**One teaspoon of healthy soil contains:**
- ~1 billion bacteria (100-200 species typically active)
- Several yards of fungal hyphae
- 1,000+ species of protozoa
- Dozens of nematode species

Tillage disrupts this web — breaks fungal networks, disrupts water channels, oxidizes organic matter. No-till agriculture attempts to preserve soil structure.

## Common Confusion Points

**Root hairs are NOT separate cells.** They are outgrowths (extensions) of epidermal cells. One epidermal cell can have one root hair. Easily confused because they look like separate structures.

**Taproot ≠ always edible carrot.** Dandelion taproot is fibrous and bitter. Carrot taproot is enlarged storage organ. The term refers to root architecture (one dominant primary root), not edibility.

**Mycorrhizae need living plants.** When you remove a forest tree, the mycorrhizal network associated with it degrades. Clearing old-growth forest removes not just trees but the entire fungal infrastructure that made the forest possible. Replanting in cleared area has minimal mycorrhizal support.

**Not all soil microbes are beneficial.** Pathogens (*Phytophthora*, Fusarium, etc.) also live in soil. The balance between beneficial and pathogenic microbes depends on soil organic matter, moisture, crop rotation. Monocultures shift the balance toward pathogens.
