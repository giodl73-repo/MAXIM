# Biomes — Tropical, Temperate, Boreal, Mediterranean, Grassland, Desert

## The Big Picture

A biome is a major ecological community defined primarily by climate. The same climate conditions anywhere on Earth produce the same suite of adaptations — convergent evolution drives plants toward similar solutions under similar selective pressures.

## Tropical Rainforest

### Climate

```
TROPICAL RAINFOREST CLIMATE PARAMETERS
Latitude:     ±10° of equator (Amazon, Congo, SE Asia/Borneo/New Guinea)
Temperature:  Mean 25-30°C; variation <5°C annually (no seasons)
Rainfall:     >2,000 mm/year; often >3,000 mm; no dry season (Af)
              or very short dry season (<3 months)
Soil:         OXISOL/ULTISOL: highly weathered, nutrient-poor, acidic
              Red/orange from iron/aluminum oxides
Light:        Intense year-round at canopy; ~0.5-1% at forest floor
```

### Key Features

```
TROPICAL RAINFOREST ECOLOGY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
BIODIVERSITY:
  ~50% of all species on ~7% of land area
  Amazon: ~10% of all species on Earth
  A single hectare of Amazon: 200-300 tree species (vs. ~30 in temperate forest)
  Explanation: stable climate → no mass extinction events; long evolution time;
               high energy → supports more species at each trophic level

NUTRIENT CYCLING:
  PARADOX: Most nutrient-poor soil, but most productive ecosystem
  EXPLANATION: Nutrients cycle in the living biomass, not the soil
  Dead leaf/animal → decomposers (fungi, bacteria, termites) → almost immediate
    uptake by roots + mycorrhizae
  Slash-and-burn agriculture: burns vegetation → brief nutrient pulse in ash
    → 2-3 years of crops → soil depleted → abandon → second-growth takes over

VERTICAL STRUCTURE:
  Emergent layer (45-60+ m): tallest trees; full sun, high wind, temperature stress
  Canopy (30-45 m): dense, continuous; >95% of light captured here
  Understory (5-20 m): shade-tolerant palms, Philodendrons, tree ferns
  Shrub/herb layer (0-5 m): very low light; deep shade adaptations
  Epiphytes: ~30,000 species of epiphytes (plants growing on other plants)
    orchids, bromeliads, ferns — not parasites, use branches as substrate

DEFORESTATION:
  ~17% of Amazon deforested since 1970
  Tipping point hypothesis (Thomas Lovejoy/Carlos Nobre):
    20-25% deforestation may trigger "savannification" (forest → savanna)
  2019-2023: accelerated deforestation under Brazilian political shifts
  Current ~20% deforested; near tipping point threshold
```

## Temperate Deciduous Forest

```
TEMPERATE DECIDUOUS FOREST PARAMETERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Latitude:    30-60° N/S
Temperature: 4 distinct seasons; mean -10°C to +20°C annual range
             Frost occurs; winter temperatures well below 0°C
Rainfall:    750-1,500 mm/year; relatively evenly distributed
             Or winter-wet/summer moderate (Eastern N. America, Europe, E. Asia)
Soil:        Alfisol/Mollisol: moderate weathering; mineral-rich; neutral pH
             Brown forest soils; good agricultural potential

DECIDUOUS STRATEGY:
  Why lose leaves? Winter = frozen water → no uptake → hydraulic failure
  Frost at -10°C → cell contents freeze → ice crystals puncture membranes
  Cold + no water → leaf = liability (transpiration continues, no replacement)
  Solution: senescence (orderly cell death) + abscission (separation layer)
  Before abscission: chlorophyll breaks down → anthocyanins/carotenoids revealed
  → autumn color (red/orange/yellow)

KEY CANOPY SPECIES:
  Eastern North America: red/white/pin oak, sugar maple, American beech,
                         American chestnut (destroyed by blight 1904-1940),
                         basswood, tulip poplar
  Europe: pedunculate oak, European beech, hornbeam, ash, lime
  East Asia: mixed oak, maple, ash, hornbeam (highest diversity)

SPRING EPHEMERALS:
  Trillium, bloodroot, trout lily, spring beauty, Virginia bluebells
  Flower March-May before canopy leafout
  Complete above-ground cycle in 6-8 weeks
```

## Boreal Forest (Taiga)

```
BOREAL FOREST (TAIGA) PARAMETERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Latitude:    50-70° N (largest biome by land area)
             Southern hemisphere: equivalent is too small (more ocean)
Temperature: Mean -5°C to +5°C; extreme range (-40°C to +35°C)
             Growing season: 50-100 days (trees only ~100 days photosynthesis)
Rainfall:    250-500 mm/year — surprisingly low (much as light snow)
Permafrost:  Present in much of northern boreal (seasonally thawed active layer)
Soil:        Spodosol/Histosol: podzolized (acidic, bleached horizon);
             peat bogs (anaerobic decomposition = carbon accumulation)

DOMINANTS:
  North America: black spruce (most widespread), white spruce, balsam fir,
                 jack pine, tamarack/larch (deciduous conifer), trembling aspen
  Eurasia: Siberian larch (largest forest by area), Scots pine, Norway spruce,
           Siberian fir

TAIGA CARBON:
  Boreal forests + peat bogs = world's largest terrestrial carbon reservoir
  Peat: organic matter accumulating for 10,000+ years without decomposing
  (anaerobic = no decomposer access)
  Northern peat bogs: ~500 Gt C stored
  Global warming → permafrost thaw → aerobic decomposition → CO₂/CH₄ release
  → Potential tipping point (carbon release accelerates warming)
  Boreal forest carbon accounting: critical uncertainty in climate models

FIRE ECOLOGY:
  Fire return interval: 50-200 years
  Black spruce cone bank: seeds in serotinous cones
  Jack pine: heat-opened cones release seeds after fire
  Post-fire: fireweed → aspen/birch → spruce regeneration (~100 years)
```

## Mediterranean Shrubland

```
MEDITERRANEAN SHRUBLAND PARAMETERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FIVE LOCATIONS (total ~5 Mediterranean-climate regions globally)
  California (chaparral)
  Mediterranean basin (maquis, garrigue, macchia)
  Chile (matorral)
  SW Australia (kwongan/mallee)
  South Africa (fynbos/Cape region)

DEFINING CLIMATE: Winter-wet / Summer-dry
  Köppen Cs (Csa = hot summer, Csb = warm summer)
  Rainfall: 250-900 mm/year; falls October-April
  Summer drought: 3-6 months of very low precipitation
  Temperatures: mild (no extreme cold or heat)

SCLEROPHYLLY (hardleaf adaptation):
  Sclerophyllous leaves: thick, waxy, small, often evergreen
  Low surface area to volume ratio → lower transpiration
  High lignin + cuticle → resist desiccation
  Example: olive, oaks, manzanita, banksia, protea

FIRE ADAPTATION:
  Fire return interval: 25-150 years
  Most plants fire-adapted in one of three ways:
  1. Resprouters: burn to ground → resprout from lignotuber/roots
     (California ceanothus, SA Proteaceae, chaparral shrubs)
  2. Seeders: killed by fire; heat or smoke triggers seed germination
     (lodgepole pine in fire-adapted variant, many annuals)
  3. Both: obligate seeders that also resprout under some conditions

CAPE FLORISTIC REGION (South Africa):
  Highest plant diversity in world per unit area
  ~9,600 plant species in 90,000 km² (vs ~20,000 in Amazon per million km²)
  Fynbos: proteaceae + restios (reed-like) + ericaceae dominants
  Extraordinary speciation: ~68% of species endemic
```

## Grassland and Savanna

```
GRASSLAND/SAVANNA PARAMETERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
GRASSLAND (temperate):
  Rainfall: 250-750 mm/year (insufficient for forest, too much for desert)
  North American prairie: short-grass (west, drier) + tall-grass (east, wetter)
  Eurasian steppe: world's largest temperate grassland (~8M km²)
  Maintained by: fire + grazing (without both → trees invade)
  Soil: Mollisol (black, deep, organic-rich — best agricultural soil)
    → 90% of North American grassland converted to agriculture

SAVANNA (tropical):
  Rainfall: 500-1,300 mm/year; strongly seasonal (wet + dry season)
  Africa (Serengeti): ~2M large mammals; wildebeest/zebra/gazelle migration
  Fire + megaherbivore grazing maintains grass/tree balance
  African savanna: controlled burn → grass regrowth → seasonal grazing migration

GRASS ADAPTATION TO GRAZING:
  Meristems at base (not tip) → grazing removes leaf, meristem survives
  vs. trees/forbs: growing point at tip → grazing = death
  Deep fibrous roots: access deep soil moisture + fire survival
  C4 photosynthesis common in tropical/warm-season grasses (see botany/04)

TIPPING POINT: savanna ↔ forest
  Rainfall at ~1,000-1,200 mm = bistable zone (either can exist)
  Fire keeps it savanna; fire suppression → forest
  Grazing keeps it savanna; remove grazing → succession to woodland
```

## Desert

```
DESERT PARAMETERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rainfall:    <250 mm/year; <125 mm = hyperarid
Temperature: Variable — hot desert (Sahara: >50°C day, 0°C night) or
             cold desert (Gobi: -40°C winter, +45°C summer)
Soil:        Aridisol: saline, alkaline, minimal organic matter; caliche
             (calcium carbonate hardpan, impermeable at depth)

HOT DESERT ADAPTATIONS:
  Water storage (succulents):
    Cactus: stem succulents (North America) → CAM photosynthesis
    Euphorbia: stem succulents (Africa) — convergent evolution, not related
    Aloe, Agave: leaf succulents
    WAX COATING: cuticle can be 10× thicker than mesic plants

  Water access (phreatophytes):
    Mesquite: taproot >50m deep → access permanent water table
    Tamarisk: deep + salt-tolerant → invasive in US riparian

  Drought avoidance (annuals):
    Complete life cycle in 2-6 weeks after rare rain
    Seed bank persists for decades
    Mojave superbloom: triggered by specific rainfall + temperature

  Root competition:
    Saguaro cactus: 50m lateral roots just below surface (rain harvest)
    No large taproot — all water capture at surface after rain

COLD DESERT:
  Gobi, Great Basin: cold + dry; no freezing prevention possible
  Dominants: sagebrush (Artemisia tridentata) = most characteristic
             Basin: salt-tolerant shrubs, bunchgrasses
             Snow as primary moisture (spring melt)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Why is tropical rainforest soil so poor? | Nutrients cycle in living biomass, not soil; rapid decomposition + uptake |
| Why do trees drop leaves in temperate zones? | Winter = frozen soil = no water uptake; leaves = liability in drought |
| What stores more carbon: boreal or tropical forest? | Boreal (peat bogs + permafrost = ~500 Gt C); tropical stores more in living biomass |
| Why are Mediterranean regions so diverse? | Stable mild climate + fire disturbance + Mediterranean basin: 3 continents meet |
| What maintains grassland vs forest? | Fire + large herbivore grazing; suppress either → trees invade |
| What is the Amazon tipping point? | ~20-25% deforestation may trigger savannification (self-reinforcing dieback) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Biomes are not defined by tree species.** They're defined by climate. The same temperature/rainfall produces convergent plant forms across disconnected continents (cactus in North America, euphorbia in Africa — unrelated but morphologically similar).

**"Tropical" ≠ "rainforest."** Tropical dry forests (Mexico, India), tropical grasslands (African savanna), and tropical deserts (Atacama) are all in tropical latitudes but very different biomes.

**Tundra ≠ just cold.** Arctic tundra = treeless due to permafrost + short growing season + wind. Alpine tundra = treeless due to altitude + wind. Both have similar appearances; different ecological constraints.

**Desert ≠ sand.** Most of the Sahara is rocky (reg/hamada) not sand dune (erg). The sand dune image comes from a small fraction. Cold deserts (Gobi, Great Basin) are rocky, gravelly, shrubby — not sand.
