# Sedimentary Rocks — Stratigraphy, Steno, Depositional Environments

## The Big Picture

Sedimentary rocks cover ~75% of Earth's land surface but are only ~5% of the crust by volume (a thin veneer). They matter disproportionately because they contain: the fossil record, petroleum systems, groundwater aquifers, most economically mined coal, and the chemical evidence of past climates.


## Weathering — Breaking Down Source Rock

### Physical (Mechanical) Weathering
- **Freeze-thaw** — water in cracks expands 9% on freezing → fractures rock
- **Pressure unloading (exfoliation)** — granite batholiths exposed by erosion → confining pressure removed → sheets spall off (Half Dome, Yosemite)
- **Abrasion** — river/glacier grinding; produces increasingly rounded, sorted particles
- **Thermal expansion** — day/night cycles crack desert rocks

### Chemical Weathering
```
PROCESS          REACTION                              VULNERABLE MINERALS
--------------   ------------------------------------   ---------------------
Hydrolysis       Feldspar + H₂O + CO₂ →               Feldspar, Pyroxene,
(dominant)       Clay minerals + dissolved ions         Olivine

Oxidation        Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ (rust)                   Iron-bearing minerals
                 2FeS₂ + 7O₂ + 2H₂O → 2FeSO₄ + 2H₂SO₄  (pyrite → acid)

Dissolution      CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻  Carbonates (limestone,
(carbonation)                                          marble) → karst

Hydration        CaSO₄ + 2H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O           Anhydrite → Gypsum
```

**Weathering stability (field observation rule):** Bowen's series reversed = weathering resistance sequence. Quartz (last to crystallize) is most stable; olivine (first to crystallize) weathers first.

## Clastic Sedimentary Rocks — Grain Size Classification

**Wentworth scale** — particle size:

```
NAME         DIAMETER     SORTING     ROCK NAME
-----------  -----------  ----------  ------------------
Boulders     >256 mm      —           Conglomerate (rounded)
Cobbles      64–256 mm                Breccia (angular)
Pebbles      4–64 mm                  (gravel → conglomerate)
Granules     2–4 mm
Sand         0.063–2 mm   Good        Sandstone
             (0.25–0.5mm              (quartz dominant in mature)
              = medium)
Silt         0.004–0.063  Moderate    Siltstone / Loess
Clay         <0.004 mm    Any         Mudstone / Shale
                                      (most common sedimentary rock)
```

**Rounding and sorting as transport indicators:**
- Angular, poorly sorted → short transport (glacial till, rockfall)
- Rounded, well-sorted → long transport (eolian dune sand, beach sand)
- Rounding of quartz requires much more energy than rounding of feldspar

## Chemical and Biogenic Sedimentary Rocks

| Rock | Origin | Composition | Environment |
|------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Limestone | Biochemical + chemical | CaCO₃ | Shallow warm marine |
| Chalk | Biogenic (coccoliths) | CaCO₃ | Pelagic (open ocean) |
| Dolomite | Diagenetic replacement | CaMg(CO₃)₂ | Restricted marine/burial |
| Chert | Biochemical (radiolaria/diatoms) | SiO₂ | Deep ocean, or hot spring |
| Rock salt (halite) | Evaporite | NaCl | Restricted basin evaporation |
| Gypsum | Evaporite | CaSO₄·2H₂O | Evaporite sequence |
| Coal | Biochemical | Organic carbon | Swamp/peat environment |
| Iron formation (BIF) | Chemical | Fe₂O₃/SiO₂ | Pre-2.5 Ga ocean (Proterozoic) |
| Phosphorite | Biochemical | Ca₅(PO₄)₃ | Upwelling zones |

**Evaporite sequence** — precipitation order as seawater evaporates (decreasing solubility):
CaCO₃ (calcite) → CaSO₄ (gypsum) → NaCl (halite) → KCl → MgSO₄

## Steno's Principles — The Foundation of Stratigraphy

Nicholas Steno (1669) established the logical basis for reading rock sequences:

```
PRINCIPLE                STATEMENT                      APPLICATION
--------------------     --------------------------     -------------------
Superposition           Younger rocks overlay          Bottom = oldest,
                        older rocks (undisturbed)       top = youngest

Original Horizontality  Sediments deposit              Tilted strata =
                        horizontally                    later deformation

Lateral Continuity      Strata extend in all           Correlate across
                        directions until thinning       canyons/outcrops
                        or hitting a barrier

Cross-cutting           Intruding feature is           Dikes, faults are
Relationships           younger than what it cuts      younger than cut rock

Inclusions              Fragments are older            Clast = older than
                        than their host                 the rock hosting it

Faunal Succession       (William Smith, 1799)          Index fossils define
(add-on to Steno)       Each layer has unique          time zones globally
                        fossil assemblage
```

**Facies** = a body of rock with distinct characteristics (lithology + fossils) deposited in a specific environment. Facies change laterally (facies belts) and vertically (facies sequences reflecting sea-level change).

## Diagenesis — From Sediment to Rock

```
BURIAL DEPTH    PROCESS              RESULT
------------    ------------------   ----------------------------
0–1 km          Compaction           Clay porosity: 80% → 30%
                Dewatering           Pore water expelled
                                     Soft sediment deformation

1–3 km          Cementation          Pore space filled by
                                     precipitated minerals
                                     (calcite, silica, iron oxide)
                                     Porosity: 30% → 5–15%

3–5 km          Dissolution          Pressure solution; grains
                                     dissolve at contacts

>5 km           Begins to            Transition to diagenesis →
                approach             low-grade metamorphism
                metamorphic          (zeolite facies)
                conditions
```

**Common cements and their implications:**
- **Calcite cement** — formed from marine pore water; limestones, some sandstones
- **Silica cement** — produces very hard, tough quartzite-like sandstones
- **Iron oxide cement** — red/brown staining in "red beds" (oxidizing conditions)
- **Pyrite framboids** — reducing (anoxic) burial conditions

## Petroleum Systems — Sedimentary Rocks as Energy Archive

```
PETROLEUM SYSTEM ELEMENTS

SOURCE ROCK:     Organic-rich shale, buried to "oil window"
                 Thermal maturity: Ro 0.6–1.3% (oil); 1.3–3% (gas)
                 Kerogen types: I (lacustrine), II (marine), III (terrestrial)

MIGRATION:       Primary: from source into carrier bed
                 Secondary: along carrier beds to trap

RESERVOIR:       Porous/permeable rock (sandstone, carbonate)
                 Key metrics: porosity (%), permeability (mD)

TRAP:            Structural: anticline, fault, salt dome
                 Stratigraphic: pinch-out, unconformity, reef

SEAL:            Low-permeability cap rock (shale, evaporite)

TIMING:          All must coincide — generation + migration +
                 trap formation + seal integrity
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Sample in hand | Identification |
|----------------|----------------|
| Gritty, quartz grains, medium-grained | Sandstone |
| Fine-grained, fissile (splits in layers), dark | Shale |
| Fine-grained, fizzes with HCl | Limestone |
| Coarse angular fragments in fine matrix | Breccia (close-source, angular) |
| Coarse rounded fragments in fine matrix | Conglomerate (transported) |
| White, powdery, very fine-grained, fizzes | Chalk |
| Very hard, glassy, chipping like flint | Chert |
| Laminated, salty taste | Rock salt (halite) |
| Black, combustible, organic | Coal |

## Common Confusion Points

**Shale vs mudstone vs slate** — Shale is fissile (splits into thin sheets), mudstone is blocky non-fissile fine-grained rock. Both are clay-rich. Slate is metamorphosed shale (foliation develops). "Shale" in petroleum context often means any fine-grained source rock including mudrocks.

**Limestone vs dolomite** — Both are grey/white carbonates. HCl test: limestone fizzes vigorously (CaCO₃), dolomite weakly (CaMg(CO₃)₂) or only when powdered. Most dolomite is secondary — formed by replacement of limestone by Mg-rich fluids.

**Facies and time** — A facies is an environment indicator, not a time indicator. The same facies (e.g., beach sand) exists simultaneously at many ages. Fossils (biostratigraphy) constrain time; facies constrain environment.

**Coal rank** — Not all coal is equal. Peat → Lignite → Bituminous → Anthracite reflects increasing burial and temperature (increasing carbon %, decreasing moisture and volatiles). Anthracite is nearly pure carbon; peat is wet, brown, low-energy.
