## The Big Picture

Sedimentary rocks cover ~75% of Earth's land surface but are only ~5% of the crust by volume (a thin veneer). They matter disproportionately because they contain: the fossil record, petroleum systems, groundwater aquifers, most economically mined coal, and the chemical evidence of past climates.

## Weathering ‚ÄĒ Breaking Down Source Rock

### Physical (Mechanical) Weathering
- **Freeze-thaw** ‚ÄĒ water in cracks expands 9% on freezing ‚Üí fractures rock
- **Pressure unloading (exfoliation)** ‚ÄĒ granite batholiths exposed by erosion ‚Üí confining pressure removed ‚Üí sheets spall off (Half Dome, Yosemite)
- **Abrasion** ‚ÄĒ river/glacier grinding; produces increasingly rounded, sorted particles
- **Thermal expansion** ‚ÄĒ day/night cycles crack desert rocks

### Chemical Weathering
```
PROCESS          REACTION                              VULNERABLE MINERALS
--------------   ------------------------------------   ---------------------
Hydrolysis       Feldspar + H‚āāO + CO‚āā ‚Üí               Feldspar, Pyroxene,
(dominant)       Clay minerals + dissolved ions         Olivine

**Weathering stability (field observation rule):** Bowen's series reversed = weathering resistance sequence. Quartz (last to crystallize) is most stable; olivine (first to crystallize) weathers first.

## Clastic Sedimentary Rocks ‚ÄĒ Grain Size Classification

**Wentworth scale** ‚ÄĒ particle size:

```
NAME         DIAMETER     SORTING     ROCK NAME
-----------  -----------  ----------  ------------------
Boulders     >256 mm      ‚ÄĒ           Conglomerate (rounded)
Cobbles      64‚Äď256 mm                Breccia (angular)
Pebbles      4‚Äď64 mm                  (gravel ‚Üí conglomerate)
Granules     2‚Äď4 mm
Sand         0.063‚Äď2 mm   Good        Sandstone
             (0.25‚Äď0.5mm              (quartz dominant in mature)
              = medium)
Silt         0.004‚Äď0.063  Moderate    Siltstone / Loess
Clay         <0.004 mm    Any         Mudstone / Shale
                                      (most common sedimentary rock)
```

**Rounding and sorting as transport indicators:**
- Angular, poorly sorted ‚Üí short transport (glacial till, rockfall)
- Rounded, well-sorted ‚Üí long transport (eolian dune sand, beach sand)
- Rounding of quartz requires much more energy than rounding of feldspar

## Steno's Principles ‚ÄĒ The Foundation of Stratigraphy

Nicholas Steno (1669) established the logical basis for reading rock sequences:

```
PRINCIPLE                STATEMENT                      APPLICATION
--------------------     --------------------------     -------------------
Superposition           Younger rocks overlay          Bottom = oldest,
                        older rocks (undisturbed)       top = youngest

Original Horizontality  Sediments deposit              Tilted strata =
                        horizontally                    later deformation

Lateral Continuity      Strata extend in all           Correlate across
                        directions until thinning       canyons/outcrops
                        or hitting a barrier

Cross-cutting           Intruding feature is           Dikes, faults are
Relationships           younger than what it cuts      younger than cut rock

Inclusions              Fragments are older            Clast = older than
                        than their host                 the rock hosting it

Faunal Succession       (William Smith, 1799)          Index fossils define
(add-on to Steno)       Each layer has unique          time zones globally
                        fossil assemblage
```

**Facies** = a body of rock with distinct characteristics (lithology + fossils) deposited in a specific environment. Facies change laterally (facies belts) and vertically (facies sequences reflecting sea-level change).

## Diagenesis ‚ÄĒ From Sediment to Rock

```
BURIAL DEPTH    PROCESS              RESULT
------------    ------------------   ----------------------------
0‚Äď1 km          Compaction           Clay porosity: 80% ‚Üí 30%
                Dewatering           Pore water expelled
                                     Soft sediment deformation

1‚Äď3 km          Cementation          Pore space filled by
                                     precipitated minerals
                                     (calcite, silica, iron oxide)
                                     Porosity: 30% ‚Üí 5‚Äď15%

3‚Äď5 km          Dissolution          Pressure solution; grains
                                     dissolve at contacts

>5 km           Begins to            Transition to diagenesis ‚Üí
                approach             low-grade metamorphism
                metamorphic          (zeolite facies)
                conditions
```

**Common cements and their implications:**
- **Calcite cement** ‚ÄĒ formed from marine pore water; limestones, some sandstones
- **Silica cement** ‚ÄĒ produces very hard, tough quartzite-like sandstones
- **Iron oxide cement** ‚ÄĒ red/brown staining in "red beds" (oxidizing conditions)
- **Pyrite framboids** ‚ÄĒ reducing (anoxic) burial conditions

## Petroleum Systems ‚ÄĒ Sedimentary Rocks as Energy Archive

```
PETROLEUM SYSTEM ELEMENTS

SOURCE ROCK:     Organic-rich shale, buried to "oil window"
                 Thermal maturity: Ro 0.6‚Äď1.3% (oil); 1.3‚Äď3% (gas)

                 Secondary: along carrier beds to trap

                 Key metrics: porosity (%), permeability (mD)

TRAP:            Structural: anticline, fault, salt dome
                 Stratigraphic: pinch-out, unconformity, reef

                 trap formation + seal integrity

## Common Confusion Points

**Shale vs mudstone vs slate** ‚ÄĒ Shale is fissile (splits into thin sheets), mudstone is blocky non-fissile fine-grained rock. Both are clay-rich. Slate is metamorphosed shale (foliation develops). "Shale" in petroleum context often means any fine-grained source rock including mudrocks.

**Limestone vs dolomite** ‚ÄĒ Both are grey/white carbonates. HCl test: limestone fizzes vigorously (CaCO‚āÉ), dolomite weakly (CaMg(CO‚āÉ)‚āā) or only when powdered. Most dolomite is secondary ‚ÄĒ formed by replacement of limestone by Mg-rich fluids.

**Facies and time** ‚ÄĒ A facies is an environment indicator, not a time indicator. The same facies (e.g., beach sand) exists simultaneously at many ages. Fossils (biostratigraphy) constrain time; facies constrain environment.

**Coal rank** ‚ÄĒ Not all coal is equal. Peat ‚Üí Lignite ‚Üí Bituminous ‚Üí Anthracite reflects increasing burial and temperature (increasing carbon %, decreasing moisture and volatiles). Anthracite is nearly pure carbon; peat is wet, brown, low-energy.
