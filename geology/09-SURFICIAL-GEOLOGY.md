# Surficial Geology — Glacial, Fluvial, Karst, Coastal, Pedogenesis

**Bridge — surficial geology as the rendering layer over tectonic infrastructure:** Plate tectonics is the backend: it sets topographic relief, rock type distribution, and the substrate. Surficial processes are the rendering layer: ice, water, wind, and gravity continuously reshape the interface that humans actually experience. The backend creates the raw geometry (mountains via collision, valleys via rifting); the frontend optimizes it toward equilibrium (erosion reducing relief, deposition filling basins). This is full-stack geology — the backend processes operate on 10⁶–10⁸ year timescales; surficial processes render on 10²–10⁵ year timescales, fast enough to be observable in a human lifetime. Climate is the runtime environment that determines which surficial processes dominate: glacial at high latitude/altitude, fluvial in temperate zones, eolian in arid zones, chemical (karst) where carbonate rock and rainfall intersect. Change the climate, change the active process set — but the tectonic substrate persists.

## The Big Picture

Surficial geology covers processes that shape Earth's surface by moving material without involving plate tectonics directly. The agents — ice, water, wind, gravity — operate at human-perceivable timescales and produce the landscapes we actually live in.

## Glacial Geology

### Glacial Types

```
ICE SHEET          ICE CAP           VALLEY GLACIER    PIEDMONT GLACIER
(continental)      (polar highland)  (alpine)          (spreading at
                                                         mountain base)
Greenland, AIS     Iceland           Alps, Rockies,    Malaspina (Alaska)
~3 km thick        ice caps          Andes, Himalayas
```

**Glacial budget:** Accumulation zone (snowfall) vs Ablation zone (melting/calving). Mass balance determines advance/retreat.

### Glacial Erosion Landforms

```

```

**Glacial erosion mechanisms:**
- **Abrasion** — rock fragments frozen in ice grind bedrock (striations + polish)
- **Plucking/quarrying** — ice freezes to bedrock, pulls away blocks on downstream side
- **Meltwater** — subglacial streams erode channels (P-forms, Nye channels)

### Glacial Depositional Landforms

```
MORAINE TYPES:          ORIENTATION/FORMATION
--------------------    --------------------------------------------
Terminal moraine        Ridge at glacier maximum extent (ice front)
Recessional moraine     Ridge deposited as glacier retreats (episodic)
Ground moraine          Flat sheet of till under/behind glacier
Medial moraine          Ridge within glacier (two glaciers merge)
Lateral moraine         Ridge along glacier sides (rock from valley walls)

DRUMLINS:               Streamlined hills of till; elongate parallel to
                        ice flow; formed under active ice (still debated)
                        Like inverted spoons pointing downstream

ESKERS:                 Sinuous ridges of sand/gravel = infilled subglacial
                        stream tunnels; excellent aquifer materials

KETTLES:                Depressions from buried ice blocks that melted
                        (kettle lakes in Minnesota, Wisconsin)

VARVES:                 Annual laminations in glacial lake sediments:
                        summer = coarse (spring melt), winter = fine
                        Each couplet = one year; count varves = date
```

**Till vs outwash** — Till is unsorted, unstratified debris deposited directly by ice (diamicton). Outwash is sorted, stratified sand/gravel deposited by meltwater streams (stratified drift). Outwash plains = sandur (Iceland term).

  |             .  Transport    .

  |              ....Dep. .....    settles)

     Clay  Silt  Sand  Gravel  Cobbles
```

Clay paradox: very fine clay is harder to erode than sand because clay particles cohere (electrostatic forces). Once in suspension, clay stays suspended at very low velocities.

### River Systems

```
HEADWATER ZONE        TRANSFER ZONE         DEPOSITION ZONE
(mountain streams)    (middle course)        (lowland, delta)

High gradient         Moderate gradient      Low gradient
Bedload dominant      Mixed load             Suspended load
Waterfalls, pools     Meanders begin         Meanders, oxbows
Rapids, gorges        Floodplain develops    Floodplain, delta

CHANNEL TYPES:
Braided: multiple channels, coarse bedload, steep gradient
Meandering: single sinuous channel, fine-grained, low gradient
Anastomosing: multiple stable channels with vegetated islands
```

**Meander mechanics:**
- Thalweg (fastest flow) hits outer bank → erosion → undercut cliff (cut bank)
- Inner bank = deposition → point bar
- Over time, meander loop tightens → oxbow lake when cut off
- Meander wavelength ≈ 10–14× channel width (independent of scale)

### Alluvial Fans and Deltas

| Feature | Where Formed | Grain Size | Structure |
|---------|-------------|------------|-----------|
| Alluvial fan | Mountain front enters lowland | Coarse to fine radially | Radiating channels |
| Bajada | Coalescing fans along mountain front | Mixed | Continuous apron |
| Delta | River enters standing water (sea, lake) | Fine sand, silt, clay | Topset/foreset/bottomset |
| Floodplain | Adjacent to river channel | Fine silt, clay | Horizontal overbank deposits |

## Karst Geomorphology

Karst develops where carbonate (limestone, dolomite) or evaporite (gypsum) rock is dissolved by slightly acidic groundwater:

```
DISSOLUTION REACTION:
CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻

(Rainwater absorbs CO₂ → carbonic acid; dissolves limestone;
 Ca²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ carried away in solution)

SURFACE FEATURES:
Sinkhole (doline): depression from collapse/dissolution
Polje: large flat-floored depression
Karren: small-scale grooves on bare limestone surface
Disappearing streams: surface water enters underground at swallet

SUBSURFACE FEATURES:
Caves (speleological system)
Stalactites: hang from ceiling (drip water)
Stalagmites: grow from floor up
Columns: where they meet
Flowstone: sheet deposit on cave floor/walls
```

**Sinkhole hazard** — Cover-collapse sinkholes: dissolution of limestone under a cover of sand/clay. No surface warning until roof collapse. Common in Florida, central Texas, Yucatan.

**Karst aquifer** — Very productive but also vulnerable: rapid water movement through conduit networks means little filtering → pollution travels fast.

### Loess — Wind-Deposited Silt

Loess is glacially-ground silt (rock flour) transported by wind and deposited as thick, unstratified blankets:

```
PROPERTIES:
  Grain size: 20–50 μm (silt; too fine for dune, too coarse for dust)
  Structure: vertical jointing; stands in near-vertical bluffs
  Composition: quartz + feldspar + carbonates (not heavily weathered)
  Thickness: up to 300m (Loess Plateau, China)

GLOBAL DISTRIBUTION:
  China Loess Plateau: 640,000 km²; 100–300m thick; source = Gobi Desert
  US Great Plains: source = Pleistocene outwash from ice sheets
  Pampas (Argentina): Southern Hemisphere equivalent
  Rhine Valley, Ukraine: European loess from periglacial outwash

AGRICULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE:
  Among most fertile soils on Earth (Mollisols developed on loess)
  US Midwest, Ukraine, North China = breadbaskets built on loess
  High porosity + vertical jointing = excellent water retention
  Vulnerability: gully erosion when disturbed (vertical jointing means
  slopes fail suddenly along joint planes)
```

## Pedogenesis — Soil Formation

Soil = weathered regolith + organic matter + organisms. The O-A-B-C horizon sequence:

```
HORIZON  NAME           DESCRIPTION
-------  -------------  ----------------------------------------
O        Organic        Litter, humus (only in forested soils)
A        Topsoil        Mixed mineral + organic; darkest; highest
                        biological activity (root zone)
B        Subsoil        Illuviation (accumulated clays, oxides,
         (illuvial)     carbonates leached from A); iron-rich
C        Parent         Partially weathered bedrock fragments;
         material       little soil structure
R        Bedrock        Unweathered parent rock
```

**CLORPT factors** (Jenny 1941) — Soil = f(Climate, Organisms, Relief, Parent material, Time):

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Climate | Temperature + precipitation drive weathering rate and leaching; most important at global scale |
| Organisms | Vegetation type determines organic matter input; earthworms mix; mycorrhizae facilitate |
| Relief (topography) | Slope angle/aspect affects water drainage; north-facing slopes cooler in N hemisphere |
| Parent material | Starting composition; granite vs basalt vs limestone → very different soils |
| Time | More developed profile = older soil |

**Major soil orders (USDA Soil Taxonomy):**

| Order | Climate/Setting | Key Feature | Agricultural relevance |
|-------|----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Mollisol | Temperate grassland | Deep, organic, A horizon; base-rich | Most productive (US Midwest, Ukraine) |
| Oxisol | Humid tropical | Deep weathering; Fe/Al oxides; nutrient-poor | Rainforest soils; leached |
| Aridisol | Desert | Evaporite accumulation; caliche layer | Low productivity; irrigation needed |
| Inceptisol | Young soils | Weakly developed; forests, mountains | Moderate |
| Ultisol | Subtropical humid | Leached; red from Fe oxides | SE US; moderate |
| Entisol | Very young | Minimal profile; alluvium, sand | Fertile if fresh alluvium |
| Spodosol | Boreal forest | Bleached E horizon; spodic B | Acidic; conifer forests |

## Mass Wasting — Gravity-Driven Slope Processes

Mass wasting moves material downslope under gravity, without a fluid transport medium (distinguishing it from fluvial/eolian transport). Classification hinges on material type, water content, and velocity:

                  fine soil      water                  rainfall

                                                        gravity

                                  melt)                  active layer
                                                        (periglacial)
```

**Slope stability factors:**
- Angle: failure occurs when shear stress (driven by gravity component along slope) exceeds shear strength (cohesion + friction × normal stress)
- Water: raises pore pressure → reduces effective normal stress → reduces friction → reduces shear strength; also adds weight
- Clay minerals: low friction angle (~15°), expand/contract with moisture → plastic failure
- Vegetation: root reinforcement stabilizes slopes; removal (logging, fire) dramatically increases failure probability

**Notable events:**
- Frank Slide (Alberta, 1903): rockslide from Turtle Mountain; 70 million tonnes, 90 dead, 3 km run-out in 100 seconds
- Vargas (Venezuela, 1999): debris flows + mudflows on steep coastal slopes after extreme rainfall; ~30,000 dead
- Oso (Washington, 2014): slow-moving earthflow that accelerated; 43 dead; site had prior movement history

## Common Confusion Points

**Glacial vs periglacial** — Glacial processes occur under/within the ice. Periglacial processes occur in cold regions near glaciers but without ice coverage — freeze-thaw, permafrost, solifluction. Much of Alaska, Canada, Siberia is periglacial, not glacial.

**Stalagmite vs stalactite** — Stalactites hang from ceiling (c for ceiling). Stalagmites grow from the ground up (g for ground). Both are speleothems deposited by CaCO₃ precipitation from drip water.

**Alluvial fan vs delta** — Both are depositional fans. Alluvial fans form on land where streams decelerate at mountain fronts. Deltas form where streams enter standing water (sea, lake). Delta sediments are much finer and have distinct topset/foreset/bottomset structure.

**Soil horizon B vs C** — B horizon is where illuviated minerals accumulate (clays, Fe oxides, carbonates) — it's a product of soil-forming processes. C horizon is partially weathered parent material — above R (bedrock), below the actively soil-forming zone. C is not part of the "true soil."
