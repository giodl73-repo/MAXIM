# Planetary Geology — Comparative Planetology, Moon, Mars, Venus

## The Big Picture

Planetary geology applies terrestrial geologic principles to other bodies. The key comparative framework: each planet's geology is determined by its **size** (internal heat budget), **distance from Sun** (solar energy, volatile inventory), and **history** (impact flux, differentiation). Earth is unusual — it has active plate tectonics. Most other bodies are "one-plate planets."

## Impact Cratering — Universal Process

Impact cratering is the dominant geologic process on airless or atmosphere-thin bodies. Understanding crater morphology reveals surface age and internal structure.

```
CRATER FORMATION STAGES:
1. CONTACT + COMPRESSION (milliseconds)
   Projectile contacts surface; shock wave propagates
   into target and back into projectile

2. EXCAVATION (seconds to minutes)
   Excavation cavity grows; material ejected
   upward and outward as ejecta curtain

3. MODIFICATION (minutes to hours)
   Crater walls collapse; peak ring forms (large craters)
   Final crater form depends on size and gravity

CRATER MORPHOLOGY vs DIAMETER (on Moon):
< 15 km:  Simple (bowl-shaped, continuous ejecta blanket)
15–300 km: Complex (central peak, terraced walls, flat floor)
> 300 km:  Multi-ring basin (Orientale, Chicxulub)
```

**Crater scaling** — Diameter increases with ~0.3 power of impactor kinetic energy. A 10 km impactor (K-Pg Chicxulub) produces a ~180 km diameter crater. Gravity-dependent: same impactor makes larger crater on Moon (lower gravity) than Earth.

**Crater counting as age clock** — More craters = older surface. Calibrated using Apollo sample ages from known crater populations:
- Lunar highlands: ~4.0 Ga (heavily cratered, ancient)
- Lunar maria: ~3.0–3.5 Ga (younger, fewer craters)
- Mars Tharsis region: perhaps <100 Ma in places

## The Moon — Earth's Geological Mirror

The Moon is a differentiated body: metallic core + mantle + crust. Formed ~4.5 Ga from giant impact (Theia-scale body hitting proto-Earth → debris disk → accretion).

```
LUNAR GEOLOGY HIGHLIGHTS:

HIGHLANDS (terrae):
- Light-colored, densely cratered
- Ancient (4.0–4.5 Ga)
- Composition: anorthosite (Ca-plagioclase)
- Formed as lunar "magma ocean" crystallized
- Plagioclase floated → formed anorthositic crust

MARIA (mare):
- Dark, smoother, fewer craters
- Younger (3.0–3.8 Ga) — volcanism filled basins
- Basaltic composition (low-viscosity flows)
- Named (Mare Tranquillitatis, Oceanus Procellarum)
- Contain Fe and Ti-rich basalts (titanium indicator)

RILLES:
- Linear: graben (extensional tectonics)
- Sinuous: collapsed lava tubes or ancient channels

MASCONS: Mass concentrations beneath maria
- Positive gravity anomalies
- Impact basins filled with dense basalt

LUNAR METEORITES:
- Some crater ejecta launches off Moon (no atmosphere)
- Found as meteorites on Earth (basaltic + anorthositic types)
```

**Lunar stratigraphy** (Pre-Nectarian → Nectarian → Early/Middle/Late Imbrian → Eratosthenian → Copernican):

The Late Heavy Bombardment (~3.9 Ga) — spike in impact flux inferred from lunar sample ages. Controversial: may be sampling bias from a few basin-forming events rather than a true global spike.

## Mars — A Planet That Had It All

Mars preserves a 4.5 Ga geological record because, unlike Earth, it has had no plate recycling (one-plate planet). The geological history:

```
NOACHIAN (4.1–3.7 Ga):
- Heavily cratered terrain
- Valley networks (fluvial erosion by water!)
- Clay minerals (phyllosilicates) formed in neutral water
- Warm and wet? OR cold and icy with episodic melt?
- Theis = "Noah's time" analogy

HESPERIAN (3.7–3.0 Ga):
- Massive volcanic outpourings
- Catastrophic floods (outflow channels: Valles Marineris)
- Sulfate mineral formation (acidic water)
- Permanent ice caps forming

AMAZONIAN (3.0 Ga–present):
- Cold, dry, thin CO₂ atmosphere
- Slow eolian (wind) erosion
- Periglacial features (permafrost, thermokarst)
- Recent gullies, possible transient liquid water?
- Possible low-level volcanic activity (InSight seismometer
  detected marsquakes)
```

### Major Martian Features

| Feature | Size | Type | Notes |
|---------|------|------|-------|
| Olympus Mons | 22 km high, 600 km wide | Shield volcano | Largest volcano in Solar System |
| Tharsis Bulge | 5000 km across, 10 km high | Volcanic plateau | Tharsis volcanoes distort Mars's shape |
| Valles Marineris | 4000 km long, 7 km deep | Tectonic canyon + erosion | 10× longer, 4× deeper than Grand Canyon |
| Hellas Basin | 2300 km diameter | Impact basin | Deepest point on Mars (-8.2 km) |
| Amazonis Planitia | Large smooth plain | Volcanic/sedimentary | Low elevation |
| North Polar Cap | ~1000 km diameter | Ice (H₂O + CO₂) | Seasonal CO₂ frost |

**Mars water history** — Liquid water existed early Mars (valley networks, delta deposits in Jezero Crater). The water went: into the subsurface (permafrost + possible deep groundwater), into space (atmospheric loss — Mars's weak gravity + no magnetic field → solar wind stripped the atmosphere), and as polar ice caps.

## Venus — Earth's Twisted Twin

Venus and Earth are near-identical in size/density but geologically opposite:

```
EARTH                          VENUS
--------------------------     --------------------------
Plate tectonics                Stagnant lid (probably)
Abundant liquid water          No liquid water
Mean surface T: 15°C           Mean surface T: 465°C
Atmospheric P: 1 bar           Atmospheric P: 92 bar
CO₂: 0.04% of atmosphere       CO₂: 96.5% of atmosphere
Active volcanism (at ridges)   Possibly active volcanism?
Thin, young ocean floor        Highly deformed; no subduction

VENUSIAN UNIQUE FEATURES:
Tessera terrain: Highly deformed "pancake" highland plateaus
              (equivalent of ancient continental crust?)
Coronae: Circular/oval features (100–2600 km);
         upwelling mantle plume → volcanic construction + collapse
Volcanos: Large shield volcanoes (Maat Mons, 8 km high)
         1600+ volcanoes mapped
Arachnoids: Spider-web pattern of fractures around circular center
           (mantle plume tectonics)
```

**Venus resurfacing hypothesis** — Venus's surface is uniformly young (~500–800 Ma) and shows few impact craters. Two models:
1. **Episodic resurfacing** — Venus periodically undergoes catastrophic overturn event, resurfacing the entire planet, then returns to stagnant lid
2. **Continuous equilibrium** — volcanic activity continuously resurfaces at steady rate

**No plate tectonics on Venus — why?** Probably because Venus has little or no water. Water weakens rocks (lubricates faults), hydrates the oceanic crust (making it denser → subduction), and lowers melting points. Without water: crust too strong to subduct, no plate tectonics.

## Io and Europa — Active Geology in Outer Solar System

```
IO (Jupiter's innermost large moon):
- Most volcanically active body in Solar System
- Hundreds of active volcanoes; sulfur-covered surface
- No impact craters (surface renewed continuously)
- Heat source: TIDAL FLEXING from Jupiter's gravity
- Volcanic temperatures: up to 1600°C (ultramafic eruptions)
- No water: too close to Jupiter, too hot

EUROPA:
- Covered by cracked ice shell (~10–30 km thick)
- Subsurface ocean (~100 km deep) confirmed
- Heat: tidal flexing
- Candidates for life: liquid water + chemistry + energy
- Chaos terrain: ice blocks rafted in liquid (ancient ocean
  exposure or thin ice melting)
```

## Impact Geology on Earth

Most Earth craters are heavily eroded, buried, or subducted. Only ~190 confirmed impact craters on Earth:

```
NOTABLE EARTH IMPACT STRUCTURES:
Chicxulub (Mexico)    ~66 Ma    ~180 km   K-Pg extinction event
Sudbury (Canada)      ~1.85 Ga  ~130 km   Hosts major Ni-Cu-PGE ore deposits
Vredefort (S. Africa) ~2.02 Ga  ~160 km   Largest confirmed on Earth
Manicouagan (Canada)  ~214 Ma   ~100 km   Visible from space as ring lake
Barringer Crater (AZ) ~50 ka    1.2 km    Best-preserved young crater
```

**Impact signatures (shocked quartz, suevite, planar deformation features)** — Diagnostic evidence for impacts: shocked quartz (planar deformation features at specific angles), impactites (suevite breccia), shatter cones (striated conical rocks from pressure waves), iridium anomaly.

## Common Confusion Points

**Moon's dark vs light regions** — Highlands = light, old (~4 Ga), anorthosite. Maria = dark, younger (~3–3.8 Ga), basalt. The "man in the moon" face is maria. The far side is almost entirely highlands (less impact basin flooding).

**Mars has water now** — Mars has substantial water: polar ice caps (water ice + CO₂ ice), subsurface permafrost, trace atmospheric water vapor, and possibly seasonal brines. What it doesn't have: stable liquid water at the surface today (too cold, too low pressure — water sublimates directly to vapor).

**Venus retrograde rotation** — Venus rotates backwards (retrograde) relative to its orbital motion, with a Venusian day longer than its year. This is puzzling; possibly a large impact or resonance with Earth. Unrelated to its geologic stagnant lid state but adds to Venus's "wrongness."

**Planetary volcanoes vs Earth's** — Earth's largest volcano (Mauna Kea) is ~10 km from sea floor. Olympus Mons on Mars is 22 km above datum and 600 km across. Mars volcanism grows so large because: one-plate planet (no conveyor belt moving crust away from plume), lower gravity (erupted material piles up rather than spreading under its own weight).
