## 1. Treaties and International Agreements

### Treaty Formation and Effect

```
TREATY FORMATION (Vienna Convention on Law of Treaties 1969):
  Agreement between states in written form governed by international law
  Negotiation → Adoption → Authentication → Consent to be bound → Entry into force

  Signature: adopted text; may or may not bind state pending ratification
  Ratification: state's formal consent to be bound (US: 2/3 Senate advice and consent)
  Accession: joining a treaty after it's already in force
  Reservation: state excludes/modifies certain provisions upon ratification
    Permitted unless: treaty prohibits, or reservation incompatible with object/purpose

US CONSTITUTION + TREATIES:
  Article II: President negotiates; Senate provides advice and consent (2/3 supermajority)
  Treaties = supreme law of the land (Supremacy Clause) → above state law
  Self-executing vs non-self-executing:
    Self-executing: enforceable in US courts without implementing legislation (Medellin)
    Non-self-executing: requires Act of Congress to have domestic legal effect
  Executive agreements: binding international commitments without Senate ratification
    (Presidents use extensively; may not override prior statutes)

TREATY INTERPRETATION:
  Vienna Convention Art. 31: ordinary meaning; context; object and purpose
  Travaux préparatoires: drafting history (supplementary means; less weight than US statutory)
  Subsequent practice: states' practice in applying treaty → evidence of interpretation

TERMINATION:
  By terms of treaty; mutual consent; material breach by other party (Vienna Conv. Art. 60);
  Supervening impossibility; fundamental change of circumstances (rebus sic stantibus)
  US unilateral withdrawal: president can withdraw; congressional role contested
```

### Key International Conventions

```
CISG (Convention on Int'l Sale of Goods):
  Governs contracts for sale of goods between parties from different CISG member states
  Applies by default unless parties opt out ("This agreement shall not be governed by CISG")
  80+ member states (US joined 1988; China, Germany, France, Canada, etc.)
  Differences from UCC: no statute of frauds; "perfect tender" doesn't apply;
    broader force majeure; buyer/seller rights structured differently
  Note: standard US commercial contract language often opts out

New York Convention (Convention on Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958):
  160+ parties; near-universal
  Obligates courts of member states to recognize and enforce foreign arbitral awards
  Narrow grounds to refuse enforcement: incapacity, invalid agreement, no notice,
    award outside scope, procedural irregularity, not yet binding, public policy
  Public policy exception: narrowly construed; domestic law violations ≠ international public policy
  → Makes arbitration clauses + awards globally enforceable; key reason arbitration preferred

Rome Convention / Rome I (EU): choice of law for contracts
  Rome II (EU): choice of law for torts/delict
  Hague Convention on Choice of Court Agreements: mutual enforcement of exclusive choice-of-court

UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration:
  Template for national arbitration laws; adopted in 70+ jurisdictions (not US)
  FAA (Federal Arbitration Act) governs US international arbitration
```

## 2. WTO and International Trade

```
WTO (World Trade Organization):
  Founded 1995; successor to GATT (1947)
  164 member states; administers trade agreements; dispute settlement; trade policy review
  Headquarters: Geneva

CORE PRINCIPLES:
  Most Favored Nation (MFN): treat all WTO members equally (no better terms for one than another)
    Exceptions: FTAs (GATT Art. XXIV); developing countries (GSP); regional preferences
  National Treatment: treat imported goods ≥ domestic goods after they've entered market
    (cannot discriminate against foreign goods after the tariff has been paid)

KEY AGREEMENTS:
  GATT (goods): tariff bindings; anti-dumping; subsidies; safeguards
  GATS (services): market access; national treatment (by sector, by mode of supply)
  TRIPS (intellectual property): minimum IP protection standards (patents 20yr, copyright life+50yr minimum)
  SPS Agreement: sanitary/phytosanitary measures (food safety, animal/plant health)
    Must be based on scientific principles; risk assessment required
  TBT Agreement: technical barriers to trade; product standards/regulations
    Must not be more trade-restrictive than necessary

DUMPING: exporting below cost/home market price
  WTO Anti-Dumping Agreement: countries can impose anti-dumping duties if:
    (1) Dumping exists
    (2) Material injury (or threat) to domestic industry
    (3) Causal link
  Calculated as: normal value − export price = dumping margin

SUBSIDIES AND COUNTERVAILING MEASURES (SCM Agreement):
  Prohibited subsidies: export subsidies, import substitution subsidies
  Actionable subsidies: cause adverse effects → countervailing duties
  Non-actionable: no longer formally protected

DISPUTE SETTLEMENT UNDERSTANDING (DSU):
  Binding; two-stage: panels + Appellate Body
  Appellate Body: 7 members; 2024: only 1 member (US blocking appointments since 2017)
  <!-- @editor[content/P2]: WTO Appellate Body status stamped 2024 — verify whether appointments have been made or MPIA has become more established by 2026 -->
    → Dispute settlement in crisis; Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arrangement (MPIA) used by others
  Remedies: prospective only (no back-payment); suspension of concessions if non-compliance

FTAs (Free Trade Agreements):
  Preferential tariffs between parties; GATT Art. XXIV exception to MFN
  US FTAs: USMCA (US-Mexico-Canada, successor to NAFTA), US-Korea, US-Japan, CAFTA, etc.
  CPTPP (Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership): US withdrew 2017
```

## 3. Sovereign Immunity

```
SOVEREIGN IMMUNITY: doctrine that foreign states cannot be sued in domestic courts
  Absolute immunity (historical): foreign state completely immune from suit
  Restrictive immunity (modern majority): immunity only for sovereign/public acts
    Commercial activities: no immunity (jure gestionis)
    Governmental acts: immunity preserved (jure imperii)

US LAW: Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA) 1976:
  Foreign states presumed immune UNLESS one of the exceptions applies:
  (1) Commercial activity exception: foreign state carries on commercial activity in US,
      or act performed in connection with commercial activity has DIRECT effect in US
  (2) Waiver: explicit waiver; or activity in US that implies consent to suit
  (3) Expropriation: property taken in violation of international law
  (4) Noncommercial torts: personal injury/death in US (embassy car accidents)
  (5) FSIA 2008 Amendment: terrorism-designated state sponsors + US nationals

  Commercial activity test: "nature" not "purpose" of act
    Foreign state issuing government bonds: commercial (bond market activity)
    Foreign state regulating its own officials: governmental (sovereign)
    Central bank operations: generally immune (separate FSIA provision)

DIPLOMATIC IMMUNITY:
  Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (1961):
    Full immunity: diplomatic agents (career diplomats); cannot be arrested or prosecuted
    Administrative/technical staff: limited criminal immunity
    Service staff: no immunity
  Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (1961): narrower immunity
  Persona non grata: host state can declare diplomat persona non grata → expelled
  Waiver: sending state can waive immunity (rarely done)

ACT OF STATE DOCTRINE:
  US courts will not examine the validity of sovereign acts of foreign governments
    done within their own territory
  Banco Nacional de Cuba v Sabbatino (1964): courts defer to foreign expropriations
  Not jurisdictional; judge-made rule of decision-avoidance
  Commercial activities exception (limited): if activity is commercial, doctrine may not apply
```

## 4. Cross-Border Enforcement of Judgments

```
COMITY:
  Principle: courts extend recognition to foreign judgments as a matter of good will/reciprocity
  Not required by US Constitution (unlike full faith and credit for sister-state judgments)
  Uniform Foreign Money Judgments Recognition Act (UFMJRA): majority of US states
    Grounds for non-recognition:
      (1) Due process not provided (notice and opportunity to be heard)
      (2) Court lacked personal/subject matter jurisdiction
      (3) Fraud in obtaining judgment
      (4) Judgment inconsistent with US public policy
      (5) Judgment conflicting with another final US judgment

ENFORCING US JUDGMENTS ABROAD:
  No global convention on enforcement of court judgments (unlike NY Convention for arbitration)
  Must obtain new judgment in foreign country (re-litigate)
  Many countries enforce US money judgments if: jurisdiction, finality, due process
  Civil law countries: most willing if jurisdiction not exorbitant
  China: limited enforcement track record of US judgments; enforcement depends on specific treaty

WHY ARBITRATION WINS:
  NY Convention: 160+ countries → arbitral awards enforceable
  Court judgments: patchwork, no global convention → expensive re-litigation
  → Cross-border commercial contracts almost always include international arbitration clause
```

## 5. International Commercial Arbitration

```
ARBITRATION vs LITIGATION for international disputes:

  Arbitration advantages:
    Enforceability: NY Convention → 160+ countries
    Neutrality: neither party's home court
    Confidentiality: private proceedings (litigation = public record)
    Expertise: choose arbitrators with technical/industry background
    Finality: limited grounds to challenge award (no appeal on merits)
    Efficiency: single forum; no parallel proceedings; no interlocutory appeals

  Arbitration disadvantages:
    Cost: arbitrator fees are substantial (3-arbitrator tribunal = 3 expensive professionals)
    No discovery: limited vs US litigation; depends on institution rules
    Slow: complex arbitrations take 2-4 years easily
    Limited remedies: no injunctions easily; class arbitration disfavored

ARBITRATION INSTITUTIONS:
  ICC (International Chamber of Commerce): Paris; most prestigious; global enforcement
  AAA/ICDR: American Arbitration Association; international rules (ICDR)
  LCIA (London Court of International Arbitration): popular for UK/European disputes
  SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre): dominant for Asia-Pacific
  ICSID (International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes): investor-state only

ARBITRATION AGREEMENT:
  Must be in writing; separability doctrine: arbitration clause survives if main contract void
  Kompetenz-Kompetenz: arbitral tribunal decides its own jurisdiction (unless parties agree otherwise)
  Arbitrability: some disputes not arbitrable (some consumer claims, employment in some states,
    antitrust — though courts have broadly construed arbitrability)

SEAT OF ARBITRATION (lex arbitri):
  Law of the seat governs the arbitral procedure and court supervision
  Popular seats: Paris (ICC), London (LCIA), Singapore (SIAC), Geneva, New York
  Choosing New York: FAA applies; federal courts enforce arbitration agreements broadly

ARBITRAL AWARD:
  Final and binding; limited grounds to set aside at seat court
    UNCITRAL/FAA: evident partiality, arbitrator misconduct, exceeded authority, fraud
    NO appeal on the merits in most systems
  Enforcement: file in local court under NY Convention; narrow defenses to resist enforcement
```

## 6. Investment Arbitration and BITs

```
BILATERAL INVESTMENT TREATIES (BITs):
  Agreements between two states protecting investors of each state in the other's territory
  3,000+ BITs worldwide; US has ~40 BITs + investment chapters in FTAs (NAFTA/USMCA)

STANDARD INVESTOR PROTECTIONS:
  Fair and equitable treatment (FET): vague; high-impact
    Legitimate expectations: investor reasonably expected stable regulatory environment
    Denial of justice: denial of access to courts
    Transparency: changes in law/regulation without notice
  Full protection and security: physical protection of investment
  National treatment + MFN treatment
  No unlawful expropriation: direct (taking of property) or indirect (regulatory taking)
    Indirect: regulation that substantially interferes with investment value
      Compensation required if indirect expropriation found
  Free transfer of funds: capital and returns must be freely transferable

ICSID ARBITRATION:
  World Bank; investor-state disputes where both states parties to ICSID Convention
  Self-contained: annulment mechanism (not national courts) to challenge awards
  Enforcement: signatory states must recognize awards as domestic court judgments

ISDS (Investor-State Dispute Settlement):
  Mechanism where investor can sue sovereign state directly in arbitration
  Bypasses local courts entirely
  Criticized: multinationals suing governments over public policy measures (Philip Morris v Australia;
    Vattenfall v Germany re nuclear phase-out; numerous tobacco, environmental cases)
  EU terminated many intra-EU BITs (Achmea decision); USMCA removed most ISDS provisions
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Scenario | Analysis |
|----------|----------|
| Cross-border commercial contract: where to litigate disputes | International arbitration (ICC/AAA) — NY Convention enforcement in 160+ countries |
| Drafting international sales contract | Specify CISG opt-out if you want UCC/domestic law to apply |
| Foreign state breaches contract with US company | FSIA commercial activity exception; likely can sue in US court |
| Diplomat's car hits someone in US | Vienna Convention immunity for diplomatic agent; civil suit barred; sending state can waive |
| US company wins arbitration; need to enforce in Brazil | File petition to enforce NY Convention award in Brazilian court; narrow defenses |
| Investment in foreign country nationalized | BIT claim for expropriation → ICSID or ad hoc arbitration; FET violation |
| Chinese company owes money under US court judgment | Re-litigate in China; no bilateral enforcement treaty; arbitration would have been easier |

## Common Confusion Points

**Public vs private international law:** "International law" usually means public international law — treaties, state-to-state obligations, UN Charter, ICJ. Private international law (conflict of laws) governs which nation's domestic law applies to cross-border private disputes. Two completely different bodies of doctrine.

**Sovereign immunity is about the defendant, not the subject matter:** A foreign government can waive immunity (explicitly or implicitly by entering into commercial activity). A US court can have jurisdiction over a foreign state if an FSIA exception applies — even for acts taken abroad.

**Arbitration award ≠ arbitration judgment:** An arbitral award is the arbitrator's decision. To actually collect money, you need to enforce it in a court (file a petition to confirm the award). The NY Convention makes this much easier than enforcing a foreign court judgment, but you still need court action.

**CISG opt-out is standard US practice:** Most US commercial contracts include something like "This agreement shall be governed by the laws of [State], excluding the UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods." Otherwise CISG applies automatically when both parties are from different CISG member states. Different rules than UCC.

**WTO Appellate Body crisis:** The US has been blocking appointment of new Appellate Body members since 2017 (claiming pro-defendant bias). As of 2024, the Body is non-functional (needs minimum 3 members). Disputes still proceed at panel level, but appeals are stuck. The multilateral trading system faces structural crisis.

**Act of state ≠ sovereign immunity:** Sovereign immunity is a defense to jurisdiction (court can't hear the case). Act of state doctrine is a rule of decision (court can hear the case but won't second-guess the foreign sovereign's act). They often overlap but are analytically distinct.
