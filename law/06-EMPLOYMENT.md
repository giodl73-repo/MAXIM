## 1. At-Will Employment

```
DEFAULT RULE (US, except Montana): employer or employee may terminate the relationship
  at any time for any reason or no reason — without liability

EXCEPTIONS TO AT-WILL:
  Statutory exceptions: cannot fire FOR protected characteristic (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, etc.)
  Public policy exception: cannot fire for:
    Refusing to commit illegal act (most states)
    Filing a workers' comp claim
    Jury service
    Whistleblowing (varying scope by state)
  Implied contract exception: employee handbook, policy manuals, oral promises may
    create an implied contract limiting termination rights
    Disclaimer: "Nothing in this handbook creates a contract of employment" → defeats implication
  Implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing: minority of states only
    California: applies to some employment relationships; limits bad faith termination

CONTRACT EMPLOYEES:
  Fixed-term employment contract: can only be terminated for cause during the term
  "Cause" typically defined: material breach of duties, criminal conduct, willful misconduct
  Severance agreements: contractual limits on termination rights + severance payment

WARN ACT (Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification):
  60-day notice required for plant closings or mass layoffs (100+ employees, 50+ affected)
  Exceptions: unforeseeable business circumstances, natural disaster, faltering company
  Penalty: up to 60 days back pay and benefits per affected employee
  State mini-WARN acts: California (100+; longer notice), NY, NJ — stricter thresholds
```

## 2. Employment Discrimination

### Federal Statutes

```
TITLE VII (Civil Rights Act of 1964):
  Prohibits discrimination based on: race, color, religion, sex, national origin
  Applies to employers with ≥15 employees
  Protected against: hiring, firing, pay, job assignments, promotions, training,
    any term/condition/privilege of employment
  Sex includes: pregnancy (PDA), sexual orientation (Bostock v Clayton County 2020),
    gender identity (Bostock)
  Enforcement: EEOC → Right to Sue Letter → federal court

DISPARATE TREATMENT (intentional discrimination):
  McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting framework (most cases):
    (1) Plaintiff establishes prima facie case (member of protected class; qualified;
        adverse action; similarly situated employees outside class treated differently)
    (2) Burden shifts to employer: articulate legitimate non-discriminatory reason
    (3) Plaintiff must show reason is pretext (inconsistency, timing, comparators)
  Mixed-motive: discriminatory + legitimate motives; employer must show same result

DISPARATE IMPACT (neutral policy with discriminatory effect):
  Griggs v Duke Power: intelligence tests that screened out Black applicants → illegal
  Plaintiff shows statistical disparity → burden shifts to employer to show business necessity
  Plaintiff can show less discriminatory alternative
  EEOC's 4/5 rule: selection rate for protected class < 4/5 of majority = adverse impact

SEXUAL HARASSMENT:
  Quid pro quo: tangible employment action tied to sexual demands
    Employer strictly liable when supervisor takes tangible action
  Hostile work environment: severe or pervasive unwelcome sexual conduct
    Faragher-Ellerth defense: employer exercised reasonable care to prevent/correct +
      employee unreasonably failed to use preventive/corrective opportunities
    (Defense not available if supervisor took tangible employment action)

RETALIATION (§704):
  Employer cannot retaliate against employee who: opposed discriminatory practice,
    filed charge, testified, assisted in Title VII proceeding
  Burlington Northern: materially adverse action (could dissuade reasonable worker)
    includes negative references, schedule changes, not just firing/demotion
```

### Other Discrimination Statutes

```
ADEA (Age Discrimination in Employment Act):
  Prohibits discrimination based on age ≥40
  Applies to employers ≥20 employees
  No disparate impact theory under ADEA for federal age discrimination (Gross v FBL)
  Adverse employment action must be "because of" age (but-for causation, not same mixed-motive)
  Waiver of ADEA claims: OWBPA requirements — 21 days to consider (45 if group layoff);
    7-day revocation period; reference to §626(f) rights; written agreement

ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act):
  Prohibits discrimination against qualified individual with disability
  Disability: physical/mental impairment substantially limiting major life activity;
    record of such; regarded as having such
  Major life activities: walking, breathing, caring for oneself, working, concentrating, etc.
  Qualified: can perform essential functions with or without reasonable accommodation
  Reasonable accommodation: modification that enables disabled employee to do the job
    unless it imposes undue hardship on employer (significant difficulty or expense)
  Interactive process: employer and employee must engage to identify accommodation
  Direct threat defense: significant risk of substantial harm to self or others, not reducible
    by accommodation

SECTION 1981 (42 USC §1981):
  Prohibits racial discrimination in contracts; broader than Title VII
  No employee minimum; no cap on compensatory/punitive damages; longer statute of limitations
  Does not cover sex, national origin, religion, age, disability

GINA (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act):
  Cannot use genetic information in employment decisions
  Cannot request genetic information from employees (exceptions: voluntary wellness programs)
  Overlaps with ADA for predisposition to conditions
```

## 3. Wage and Hour Law

```
FLSA (Fair Labor Standards Act):
  Minimum wage: $7.25/hour federal (many states/cities higher; California $16, NYC $16+)
  Overtime: 1.5× regular rate for hours worked >40/week
  Child labor: restricts hours/hazardous occupations for under-18

EXEMPT vs NON-EXEMPT (overtime eligibility):
  White-collar exemptions: salary basis test + duties test
    Executive: primary duty = management; directs 2+ employees; authority to hire/fire
    Administrative: primary duty = office work; exercise of independent judgment and discretion
    Professional: primary duty = advanced knowledge in science/learning or creative
    Computer: specific IT roles at $27.63/hour or salary basis; design/development/etc.
    Salary threshold (2024): $684/week ($35,568/year) → proposed raise to $1,128/week blocked by courts
  Outside sales: paid on commission; primarily away from employer's place of business

MISCLASSIFICATION ISSUES:
  Independent contractor vs employee: major enforcement focus
    IRS 20-factor test; ABC test (California AB5/DLSE); economic realities test (DOL)
    California AB5: workers presumed employees unless: A) free from control;
      B) perform work outside usual course of business; C) customarily in independent trade
    Consequences: back taxes (FICA/FUTA), back wages, overtime, benefits, class actions

REGULAR RATE CALCULATION:
  All remuneration for employment ÷ hours worked = regular rate
  Includes: non-discretionary bonuses, commissions, shift differentials
  Excludes: discretionary bonuses, overtime premium itself, gifts, expense reimbursements
  1.5× the REGULAR RATE for all hours over 40 (not just 1.5× minimum wage)

JOINT EMPLOYER LIABILITY:
  Two companies can both be employers of same worker → both liable for FLSA violations
  Staffing agencies: both agency and client company may be joint employers
  Common in gig economy, franchise, supply chain contexts
```

## 4. Leave Laws

```
FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act):
  Applies to employers with ≥50 employees (within 75 miles)
  Eligible employees: 12 months tenure; 1,250 hours in last 12 months
  Entitlement: 12 weeks unpaid leave per year for:
    Birth/adoption/foster placement
    Serious health condition of employee or immediate family member
    Qualifying military exigency
    Caregiver for injured servicemember (26 weeks)
  Job protection: return to same or equivalent position
  Benefits: employer must maintain group health insurance during leave
  Notice: 30 days if foreseeable; as soon as practicable if not
  Employer can require concurrent use of PTO

STATE LEAVE LAWS (often more generous):
  California CFRA: similar to FMLA; covers employers with ≥5 employees; domestic partners
  CTFMLA, NY PFL, NJ FLI: paid family leave programs
  Pregnancy disability leave: California PDL (4 months); many other states
  Bereavement, sick leave: many states mandate paid sick leave

FEDERAL LEAVE OBLIGATIONS (tech company context):
  ADA interactive process: disability-related leave as reasonable accommodation
  USERRA: military leave; reinstatement rights; prohibits adverse action based on service
```

## 5. Non-Competes and Employee Mobility

```
NON-COMPETE ENFORCEABILITY BY STATE:

  CALIFORNIA: void and unenforceable (Business & Professions Code §16600)
    "Every contract by which anyone is restrained from engaging in a lawful profession..."
    Exception: sale of business (seller can be restricted from competing)
    Exception: dissolution of partnership
    Non-solicitation of employees/customers: also largely unenforceable in California
    No NCA with California employees regardless of governing law clause
    SB 699 (2024): employers cannot enforce NCA signed out-of-state against California employees;
      filing/threatening suit violates California law; violators owe attorney fees

  MAJOR STATES (with caveats):
    New York: enforceable if reasonable; garden leave required (pay during restriction) — trend
    Illinois: banned for workers earning <$75K/year (2022)
    Minnesota: banned for all employees (2023)
    Florida: very employer-friendly; statute presumes enforceability if meets requirements
    Texas: enforceable if ancillary to otherwise enforceable agreement; reasonable scope

<!-- @editor[content/P2]: FTC non-compete rule status dated 2024 — verify whether rule was revived, re-proposed, or permanently blocked; also check state-level changes in 2025-2026 -->
  FTC RULE (2024): nationwide ban on most employee non-competes
    Final rule issued April 2024; effective September 2024
    Blocked by federal courts (Fifth Circuit Aug 2024); invalidated nationwide
    Status: uncertain; enforcement not proceeding; potential appeal/reconsideration

REASONABLENESS ANALYSIS (where non-competes enforced):
  Legitimate business interest: protectable interests include customer relationships,
    confidential business info, specialized training
  Reasonable in time: 6-12 months standard; 2 years increasingly scrutinized
  Reasonable in geography: where employee actually worked/had influence
  Reasonable in scope of restricted activities
  Blue-penciling: courts may modify (narrow) unreasonable provisions vs. void entirely
    Depends on state policy

GARDEN LEAVE: pay employee's full salary during restriction period (UK standard)
  Few US states require; NY increasingly requiring; shows employer's legitimate interest

NON-SOLICITATION AGREEMENTS (separate from non-competes):
  Customer non-solicitation: more likely enforceable (customer relationships = legitimate interest)
  Employee non-solicitation: used to prevent raiding; enforceability varies by state
  California 2024: broadly bans employee non-solicits unless protection of trade secrets
```

## 6. Employee IP Assignments

```
WORK FOR HIRE (Copyright Act §101):
  Employee works: employer automatically owns copyright if within scope of employment
  Contractor works: NOT automatically work for hire for most types (see IP module)
  → Contractors need written IP assignment agreement

INVENTION ASSIGNMENT AGREEMENTS:
  Most tech companies require employees to assign all inventions to company
  Standard scope: inventions made during employment period using company resources
    OR related to company's business

  CALIFORNIA LABOR CODE §2870-2872 (similar in Delaware, others):
    Employer CANNOT claim inventions that:
    (a) Employee develops entirely on own time
    (b) Without using employer's equipment/supplies/facilities/trade secrets
    (c) AND does not relate to employer's current or reasonably anticipated business
    (d) AND does not result from work performed for employer
    All four elements must be met for employee to keep invention
    → Employers often claim everything; employees who side-project need to be careful

PRIOR INVENTIONS LIST:
  Disclosure at hire of prior inventions employee wants to retain
  Prevents company from later claiming pre-existing work
  Important for developers who have open source projects or side work

DEVELOPER BEST PRACTICES:
  Keep scrupulous records of when personal projects were worked on
  Ensure no company equipment/network/resources used for personal projects
  No consulting on "related to employer's business" topics without explicit permission
  Check your assignment agreement carefully for what you're giving up
```

## Common Confusion Points

**At-will doesn't mean fire for anything:** At-will means no required reason — but you cannot fire for an ILLEGAL reason (protected characteristics, retaliation for protected activity, public policy violations). Managers often think "at-will = I can fire anyone anytime for anything." Wrong.

**Salary ≠ exempt:** Many employers misclassify salaried employees as exempt from overtime. The white-collar exemptions require BOTH: (1) salary basis above threshold AND (2) primary duties test. A salaried computer programmer who mostly does data entry is probably non-exempt.

**California AB5 and gig economy:** The ABC test presumes employment. The B prong (work outside usual course of business) is particularly broad — Uber drivers were found to be employees because transporting people is Uber's core business. Many companies restructured; some sectors got specific exemptions.

**Non-solicitation ≠ non-compete:** Non-solicitation agreements (don't call our customers after you leave) are often more enforceable than full non-competes because they're narrower. In California, even these are largely void after 2024 unless protecting trade secrets. Understand which you're signing.

**ADEA waiver formalities are strict:** The OWBPA requirements for waiving ADEA claims are mandatory and non-waivable. If you give a 40+ employee a severance agreement and it doesn't include the 21-day consideration period (45 days for group layoffs) and 7-day revocation period, the ADEA waiver is void — even if employee signed it.

**Harassment liability and the Faragher-Ellerth defense:** Employers are strictly liable when a supervisor takes a tangible employment action (firing, demotion, pay cut) against a harassed employee. The affirmative defense (we had a good policy; employee didn't report) is only available when no tangible action was taken. Run your harassment reporting procedures correctly — they're your actual legal protection.
