## 1. Common Law vs Civil Law — Deep Difference

### Common Law: Precedent-Driven

```
Stare decisis: "stand by things decided"
  Binding precedent: same jurisdiction, higher court
  Persuasive precedent: other jurisdictions, lower courts, law review articles

Ratio decidendi: the legal reasoning essential to the decision (binding)
Obiter dicta: remarks made "by the way" (not binding, persuasive)

Distinguishing: court avoids following precedent by showing material differences
Overruling: higher court explicitly rejects prior precedent
  UK: only Supreme Court (formerly House of Lords) can overrule itself
  US: SCOTUS can overrule itself (Dobbs overruled Roe)

Equity: parallel system of law (now merged in US courts)
  Origin: Court of Chancery → Lord Chancellor
  Remedies: injunctions, specific performance, constructive trusts
  "Equity follows the law but doesn't always follow it rigidly"
  Equitable doctrines: estoppel, laches, fiduciary duty, unconscionability
```

### Civil Law: Code-Driven

```
Comprehensive codes cover entire domains:
  France: Code Civil (1804) — Napoleon; still in force
  Germany: BGB (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, 1900)
  Spain, Italy, Japan, Louisiana (US): civil law influenced

Method: abstract general rules → apply deductively to facts
No stare decisis formally; courts cite cases for persuasion, not as binding
Legal scholars (doctrine) have greater authority than in common law

Mixed: Quebec (civil law for private law; common for public), South Africa, Scotland
```

## 2. US Court Structure

```
FEDERAL COURTS:
  U.S. Supreme Court          ← 9 justices; cert petition (4 votes to grant)
       ↑                         ~60–80 cases per year; discretionary review
  U.S. Courts of Appeals       ← 13 circuits (11 numbered + DC + Federal)
  (Circuit Courts)                3-judge panels (en banc = full circuit)
       ↑
  U.S. District Courts         ← 94 districts; trial courts
  (+ Specialized: Bankruptcy, Tax, Court of Federal Claims, Court of International Trade)

CIRCUIT SPLIT: circuit courts disagree → SCOTUS more likely to grant cert

STATE COURTS:
  State Supreme Court          ← final authority on state law
       ↑
  Intermediate Appellate Courts
       ↑
  Trial Courts (various names: Superior Court, Circuit Court, District Court)

JURISDICTION:
  Federal question: "arises under" Constitution, federal statute, treaty
  Diversity: citizens of different states + amount in controversy >$75,000
  Removal: defendant can remove state case to federal court if federal jurisdiction exists
  Erie doctrine: federal court in diversity case applies state substantive law,
                 federal procedural law
```

## 3. Legal Reasoning Methods

```
ISSUE — RULE — APPLICATION — CONCLUSION (IRAC: standard legal analysis)
  Issue: what question of law must be resolved?
  Rule: what legal standard governs?
  Application: how does the rule apply to these facts?
  Conclusion: what result follows?

STATUTORY INTERPRETATION:
  Textualism: plain meaning of the text; nothing beyond the words (Scalia)
  Intentionalism: legislative intent; use legislative history (committee reports, floor debate)
  Purposivism: what purpose did the statute serve? interpret to advance it
  Dynamic interpretation: meaning evolves with context (living constitution)

CONSTITUTIONAL INTERPRETATION:
  Originalism: original public meaning at time of ratification
  Living constitutionalism: Constitution evolves with changing norms
  Structural argument: inferred from constitutional structure/design
  Precedent argument: stare decisis (conservative force in constitutional adjudication)

CANONS OF CONSTRUCTION:
  Expressio unius: expression of one thing excludes others
  Ejusdem generis: general term following specifics = limited to that class
  Noscitur a sociis: word is known by its associates
  Rule against surplusage: every word has meaning (drafters don't use surplus words)
  Avoid constitutional doubt: construe statute to avoid constitutional questions
```

## 4. Key Legal Concepts

### Standing, Ripeness, Mootness

```
ARTICLE III STANDING (constitutional minimum):
  Injury in fact: concrete, particularized, actual/imminent (not speculative)
  Causation: fairly traceable to defendant's conduct
  Redressability: favorable decision likely to redress the injury

PRUDENTIAL STANDING (judge-made, can be waived by statute):
  Third-party standing: generally can't assert others' rights
  Zone of interests: plaintiff within zone of interests protected by statute
  Generalized grievances: not adequate (everyone equally affected)

RIPENESS: claim must be mature; no advisory opinions on hypotheticals
MOOTNESS: live controversy required; voluntary cessation exception
          class actions: named plaintiff's mootness doesn't moot entire class
```

### Standard of Review

```
De novo: appellate court decides fresh (law questions, constitutional questions)
Clear error: factual findings of trial court reversed only if clearly erroneous
Abuse of discretion: discretionary calls (evidentiary rulings, sanctions)

Constitutional review standards:
  Strict scrutiny: fundamental rights or suspect classes (race, national origin)
    → law must be narrowly tailored to serve a compelling government interest
    → rarely survives; "strict in theory, fatal in fact"
  Intermediate scrutiny: quasi-suspect classes (sex, illegitimacy), commercial speech
    → substantially related to an important government interest
  Rational basis: all other cases
    → rationally related to legitimate government interest
    → very deferential; almost always upheld
```

### Civil vs Criminal

```
CRIMINAL:
  State/federal government brings action against defendant
  Beyond reasonable doubt standard (highest)
  Punishment: incarceration, fines, death penalty
  Constitutional protections: 4th, 5th, 6th, 8th Amendments

CIVIL:
  Plaintiff (private) vs defendant
  Preponderance of evidence ("more likely than not" — >50%)
  Clear and convincing evidence (some matters: fraud, punitive damages)
  Remedies: compensatory damages, punitive damages, injunction, specific performance

OJ Simpson: acquitted criminally (beyond reasonable doubt)
            found liable civilly (preponderance) → different standards
```

## 5. Litigation Process

```
PRE-SUIT:
  Demand letter → negotiation → potential resolution

COMPLAINT FILED:
  Service of process on defendant
  Answer (admit/deny/affirmative defenses) within 21 days (FRCP)

MOTION TO DISMISS (12(b)(6)):
  Failure to state a claim → accept all factual allegations as true; do legal sufficiency matter?
  Twombly/Iqbal plausibility pleading standard (2007/2009)

DISCOVERY:
  Interrogatories (written questions)
  Depositions (sworn oral testimony)
  Document requests (ESI — electronically stored information is massive in big cases)
  Requests for admission
  Expert witness disclosure (Rule 26 reports)
  Privilege: attorney-client privilege; work product doctrine

SUMMARY JUDGMENT (Rule 56):
  No genuine dispute of material fact; moving party entitled to judgment as a matter of law
  ~70% of federal civil cases resolve by motion practice or settlement (never trial)

TRIAL:
  Jury selection (voir dire); strikes for cause + peremptory challenges (limited)
  Opening statements → plaintiff's case → defendant's case → closing
  Directed verdict / JMOL (judgment as a matter of law)

POST-TRIAL:
  JNOV, new trial motions
  Appeal: only final judgments ordinarily appealable (final judgment rule)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Is a prior court decision binding on this court? | Only if same/higher court in same jurisdiction; ratio decidendi (not dicta) |
| What standard to overturn a factual finding on appeal? | Clear error (deferential) |
| What to win on motion to dismiss? | Show complaint plausibly alleges all elements (Twombly/Iqbal) |
| Who wins if statute and common law conflict? | Statute wins (unless unconstitutional) |
| Who wins if federal and state law conflict? | Federal law wins (Supremacy Clause) |
| What constitutional review applies to racial classification? | Strict scrutiny |
| Civil burden of proof? | Preponderance (>50%); clear and convincing for some claims |
| Where to sue a corporation? | State of incorporation, principal place of business, or where cause of action arose |

## Common Confusion Points

**Stare decisis is not absolute:** SCOTUS can and does overrule itself (Brown overruled Plessy; Dobbs overruled Roe). Lower courts cannot overrule higher court precedent — they must follow it or distinguish it on the facts.

**Common law is not unwritten:** It's written in judicial opinions (case reporters). "Unwritten" is a historical misnomer — it means not legislatively enacted, not that it's oral tradition.

**Civil law ≠ civil (as opposed to criminal) law:** "Civil law" as a legal system (France, Germany) is completely different from "civil law" as opposed to criminal law. Context is everything.

**Federal courts are not superior to state courts on state law questions:** Erie doctrine. Federal court in diversity applies state substantive law. The Texas Supreme Court is the final word on Texas contract law, not the 5th Circuit.

**Regulations have the force of law:** Administrative rules (CFR) are not suggestions. They are binding law, enforceable like statutes, issued by agencies under delegated statutory authority (Chevron deference historically; post-Loper Bright, courts interpret statutes de novo).

**Punitive damages are civil, not criminal:** They can be enormous (BMW v. Gore established proportionality limits), but they're civil damages for outrageous conduct, not criminal punishment. No double jeopardy issue with both criminal conviction and civil punitive damages.
