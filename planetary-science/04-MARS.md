# Mars: Geology, Climate, and Habitability

## The Big Picture

Mars is the most studied planet besides Earth, yet also the most surprising: overwhelming evidence for ancient liquid water on a planet that is now frozen, thin-aired, and irradiated. The story of Mars is the story of planetary climate collapse — and the search for whether life arose during the wet phase.

## Geological Timescale

Mars has no confirmed rock record with absolute ages (no sample return yet). Ages are estimated from crater densities tied to the lunar calibration curve.

```
MARS GEOLOGICAL PERIODS
========================

  PERIOD      AGE (Ga)     KEY EVENTS                  CLIMATE
  ------      --------     ----------                  -------
  Noachian    4.1–3.7 Ga   Heavy bombardment           Warmer, wetter?
                           Valley networks form         Liquid water on surface
                           Phyllosilicates form         pH-neutral; habitable?
                           Magnetic dynamo active

  Hesperian   3.7–3.0 Ga   Tharsis volcanism peaks     Transitional; drying
                           Outflow channels: floods     Brief wet episodes
                           Sulfates deposited           More acidic conditions
                           Magnetic dynamo dies ~3.9 Ga

  Amazonian   3.0–0 Ga     Cold, dry, icy               Cold / glacial
                           Low erosion rates            Modern Mars climate
                           Residual polar caps form     Obliquity-driven ice ages
                           Dust storms dominate
```

### The Key Problem: Was Ancient Mars Warm?

```
THE FAINT YOUNG SUN PARADOX (MARS VERSION)
===========================================

  4 Ga ago: Sun was ~75% current luminosity
  Mars at 1.52 AU received ~0.32× current Earth solar flux
  → Mean surface temperature should have been well below -50°C

  YET: Valley networks, lake deposits, phyllosilicates → liquid water

  SOLUTIONS PROPOSED:
  1. High CO₂ + H₂ greenhouse (Ramirez et al.): H₂ is a potent
     collision-induced absorber; volcanic outgassing of H₂ + CO₂
     could maintain >273 K
  2. Impact-driven warming: large impacts vaporize ice → temporary wet periods
  3. Obliquity variations: Mars at high obliquity (~40°) → poles melt → wetter
  4. Geothermal heating: subsurface hydrothermal systems; surface water
     from below (not precipitation-driven)

  NO CONSENSUS — the ancient Mars climate problem is unsolved
```

## Water Evidence: Ancient and Modern

```
EVIDENCE FOR ANCIENT LIQUID WATER
===================================

  LANDFORMS:
  Valley networks    Noachian highlands; tributary structure; ~3.7-4.1 Ga
                     (precipitation or groundwater sapping — debated)
  Outflow channels   Catastrophic floods; up to 100 km wide channels
                     (e.g., Ares Vallis, Kasei Vallis; Hesperian)
  Paleolakes         Crater lakes; delta deposits (Jezero, Gale)
                     Fan-delta in Jezero = Perseverance landing site
  Shorelines?        Putative ocean shorelines at northern lowlands (contested)

  MINERALOGY:
  Phyllosilicates    Clays (smectite, chlorite) → formed in pH-neutral water
  (Noachian aged)    Detected from orbit by CRISM/OMEGA spectrometers
  Sulfates           Jarosite, gypsum → acidic water (Hesperian)
  (Hesperian aged)   Opportunity rover: Meridiani sulfate deposits
  Carbonates         Trace amounts → CO₂-rich water
  Perchlorates       ClO₄⁻; soluble; may form brine at low T; Phoenix lander

  IN SITU (ROVERS):
  Curiosity (Gale Crater): mudstone layers → ancient lake; habitable conditions
  Opportunity (Meridiani):  hematite "blueberries" → water percolation
  Perseverance (Jezero):    river delta; organic compound detection
```

### Current Water

```
CURRENT WATER ON MARS
======================

  POLAR ICE CAPS:
  North pole:  Water ice + CO₂ ice (seasonal CO₂ cap)
               Permanent water ice: ~1.6 × 10⁶ km³
  South pole:  Primarily CO₂ ice; water ice cap underneath

  SUBSURFACE ICE:
  Mid-latitudes: Ice confirmed by SHARAD/MARSIS radar (0.5-5 km thick sheets)
                 Phoenix lander scraped up subsurface ice (68°N)
                 Massive buried glaciers at 35-55° latitude

  LIQUID WATER CANDIDATES:
  MARSIS radar: Subglacial brine detected under south polar cap? (~2018 report)
                Depth: ~1.5 km under ice
                Requires: high salt (perchlorate?), geothermal heat, low T
                Contested: may be CO₂ clathrates or conductive layer mimicking signal

  RECURRENT SLOPE LINEAE (RSL):
  Dark streaks on steep slopes; form in summer, fade in winter
  Initially: liquid brine? Now: dry granular flows likely
  Disappointing resolution: probably not liquid water (dryness confirmed by MRO)

  ATMOSPHERE:
  Water vapor: ~10 precipitable microns (global average)
  Small amount; varies seasonally and with obliquity cycles
```

## Atmosphere: Structure and Dynamics

```
MARS ATMOSPHERIC STRUCTURE
===========================

  100+ km     Upper atmosphere; photodissociation; escape to space
  80-100 km   Mesosphere
  40-80 km    Lower atmosphere; CO₂ ice clouds (polar winter)
  0-40 km     Troposphere; dust + water ice clouds; weather

  SCALE HEIGHT: H = kT/(mg) = (1.38×10⁻²³ × 210)/(7.3×10⁻²⁶ × 3.72) ≈ 11 km
  (larger than Earth's 8.5 km because g is lower; extends higher per unit mass)

  SURFACE PRESSURE CYCLE:
  Varies ±25% annually as CO₂ condenses at the winter pole
  In winter: CO₂ freezes out → atmospheric mass moves to pole
  In summer: sublimes back → pressure returns

  DUST STORMS:
  Regional: common; dust-to-orbit season
  Global: every few Mars years (2+ year cycles)
  2018 global storm: lasted ~3 months; reduced solar power for Opportunity
  Dust devils: very common; electrostatic + thermal

  ESCAPE RATE:
  ~100 g/s of atmosphere lost to space today (MAVEN measurements)
  Sputtering by solar wind + photochemical escape + thermal escape
  Dominant ion escape: O⁺ from upper atmosphere photodissociation
```

## Habitability Assessment

```
HABITABILITY FRAMEWORK FOR MARS
=================================

  REQUIREMENT      NOACHIAN MARS    HESPERIAN MARS   TODAY
  -----------      -------------    --------------   -----
  Liquid water     YES (surface)    Episodic          Possible (brine)
  Energy source    Solar + impact   Geothermal        Geothermal (weak)
  + geothermal
  CHNOPS elements  YES              YES               YES
  Redox couples    YES (abundant)   YES               YES
  Habitable T      Intermittent     Cold              No (surface)
  Radiation shield Partial (dyn.)   Weakening         None (global)
  Stability        Unknown          Declining         Low

  VERDICT:
  Noachian Mars: potentially habitable (liquid water + chemistry + energy)
  Hesperian Mars: transitional; hydrothermal systems most promising
  Modern Mars: surface uninhabitable; deep subsurface uncertain

  BEST CURRENT HOPE FOR LIFE:
  Deep subsurface brine (>2 km depth): shielded from radiation;
  possible geothermal energy; perchlorate brine stable at -70°C
  cf. Earth: life found in Atacama, Antarctic dry valleys, 3+ km depth mines
```

## Tharsis and Volcanism

```
THARSIS VOLCANIC PROVINCE
==========================

  Location: Western hemisphere, centered ~245°E, ~5°N
  Size: 5000 km across; ~10 km high bulge in crust
  Mass: 3×10²³ kg (~6% of Mars total)

  SHIELD VOLCANOES:
  Olympus Mons: 21 km height, 600 km base diameter — largest volcano in solar system
  Arsia, Pavonis, Ascraeus Montes: Tharsis chain; ~15-17 km elevation
  Alba Patera: largest volcano by area; caldera complex

  TIMING:
  Tharsis began ~4 Ga; peak activity: Noachian-Hesperian (~3.5-2 Ga)
  Continued activity into Amazonian (some flows <100 Ma?)
  Recent: putative eruptions within last few million years

  EFFECTS ON CLIMATE:
  SO₂ outgassing → sulfates (Hesperian deposits)
  CO₂ + H₂O outgassing → temporarily warmer, wetter periods?
  Tidal deformation of planet: may have driven polar wander

  VALLES MARINERIS (nearby):
  Not a river canyon — a rift valley + tectonic graben system
  4000 km long, 200 km wide, 7 km deep
  Comparable: East African Rift, but 10× the scale
```

## Sample Return: Mars Sample Return (MSR) Program

```
MARS SAMPLE RETURN ARCHITECTURE
=================================

  PHASE 1: Sample Caching (NOW)
    Perseverance rover: ~30 titanium sample tubes
    Jezero Crater: deltaic sediments, igneous rock, regolith
    Target: Noachian-Hesperian boundary materials; biosignature potential

  PHASE 2: Sample Retrieval (architecture under revision as of 2024–25)
    Original architecture: ESA Earth Return Orbiter + NASA Sample Retrieval
    Lander with Mars Ascent Vehicle (MAV); rendezvous in Mars orbit
    STATUS: NASA restructured MSR in 2024 due to cost overruns (~$10B+ estimate)
    and schedule slip (original ~2033 Earth return now pushed well beyond 2035).
    Multiple alternative architectures under study; program may involve commercial
    partners or a simplified "fetch rover only" approach. ESA's Earth Return
    Orbiter development continues. Launch dates were not confirmed as of early 2025.

  PHASE 3: Return to Earth (date TBD — post-2035 likely under revised plans)
    Sample containment: Biosafety Level 4 protocols required (precautionary)
    Earth Entry Vehicle delivers samples to Utah Test and Training Range
    Curation facility (similar to Apollo lunar samples, JSC)

  WHY SAMPLES MATTER:
    Radiometric age dating requires <1 mg material — impossible from orbit
    Biosignature detection at ppb sensitivity → impossible in situ
    Mineral geochemistry at sub-micron scale → impossible in situ
    Ground truth for all remote sensing (spectrometer calibration)
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Was Mars warm and wet? | Definitive geological evidence for liquid water (valley networks, lake deltas, clays); unclear if warm was global or localized/episodic |
| Why did Mars lose its atmosphere? | Dynamo cessation ~3.9 Ga → no magnetic shield → solar wind stripping; also small gravity (5 km/s escape velocity) |
| What are the crustal magnetic stripes? | Remnant magnetism from the Noachian dynamo; patterns resemble seafloor spreading on Earth but may have different origin |
| Why is the northern hemisphere so different? | Hemispheric dichotomy: northern lowlands are thin-crusted, low, smooth — possibly from a single giant oblique impact |
| What is the best evidence for past habitability? | Jezero Crater fan delta (ancient lake system); Gale Crater mudstones with CHNOPS chemistry; Opportunity jarosite (liquid water) |
| What is InSight's most important result? | Core radius ~1830 km (larger than models predicted); crust 20-70 km thick; mantle unexpectedly cold; seismically active planet |
| How does Mars's obliquity affect climate? | High obliquity (~40°) shifts polar ice to mid-latitudes; snowball episodes; obliquity has varied 15°-35° over millions of years |

## Common Confusion Points

**Valles Marineris is not a river canyon**: It's a tectonic rift, formed by the Tharsis bulge stretching and pulling apart the crust. It's the scale analog of the East African Rift, not the Grand Canyon.

**Mars is not a dead planet**: InSight detected 1,300+ marsquakes; the largest was magnitude 5. Volcanic activity may have occurred within the last few million years. Mars is geologically quiescent but not dead.

**The RSL mystery is mostly resolved**: Recurrent slope lineae were thought to be briny water seeps. Current consensus is they're dry granular flows triggered by seasonal temperature changes — not liquid water.

**Perchlorates are ambiguous**: Mars soil is rich in perchlorates (ClO₄⁻) which are toxic to life as we know it but also a potential energy source for some chemolithotrophs. They complicate organic detection because they combust organics when heated (as in Viking GC/MS).

**Mars "habitable zone" doesn't mean hospitable**: Habitability means the conditions allow liquid water and basic chemistry. Modern Mars is technically inside the Sun's habitable zone but utterly hostile at the surface due to UV, cold, and dryness.
