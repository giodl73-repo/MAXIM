# 07 — Cumin, Coriander & Fennel

## The Apiaceae Seed Spices — Ancient Mediterranean Origins, Anethole Chemistry

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Apiaceae (Umbelliferae) family: the carrot/parsley family; umbrella-shaped flower clusters (*umbels*); produces aromatic seeds (technically split fruits, *schizocarps*) with characteristic chemical profiles; includes cumin, coriander, fennel, caraway, dill, anise, celery, parsley, carrot
- **Cumin** (*Cuminum cyminum*): native to Mediterranean/Middle East (probably Levant/Egypt); one of the world's oldest spices; found in Tutankhamun's tomb; mentioned in Bible; cuminaldehyde (4-isopropylbenzaldehyde) as primary flavor compound; p-cymene, γ-terpinene
- Cumin in trade: major Roman spice; stored on tables like salt and pepper; cumin-based rent payment documented; cumin in medieval European cooking as common as pepper; fall from fashion in European cooking but remains central to Indian (jeera), Middle Eastern, Mexican, and North African cuisines
- Black cumin (*Nigella sativa*, Ranunculaceae) — completely different plant, different family; thymoquinone as active compound; Middle Eastern and South Asian use; "the seed that cures everything except death" (hadith attribution); not related to cumin
- **Coriander** (*Coriandrum sativum*): the seed and the leaf (cilantro) are from the same plant but taste completely different; seed flavor from linalool (floral, citrus); leaf flavor from aldehydes — (E)-2-alkenal compounds (2-decenal, 2-dodecenal) — described as "soapy" by ~4–14% of people due to genetic variant near olfactory receptor gene *OR6A2*
- Coriander in antiquity: seeds found in 8,000-year-old Nahal Hemar cave (Israel); Egyptian papyri; Sanskrit texts; Bronze Age Mediterranean trade; coriander seed = mildly sweet, citrusy; widely used in European, Middle Eastern, South Asian, Latin American cooking; cilantro — Southeast Asian, Mexican, Indian (fresh herb use)
- **Fennel** (*Foeniculum vulgare*): Mediterranean native; anethole (trans-anethole, ~80% essential oil) — the defining compound shared with anise, star anise, and licorice (convergent chemistry, completely different plants); Florence fennel (bulb vegetable) vs wild fennel (seeds and fronds); fennel pollen as premium spice
- Anethole chemistry: *trans*-anethole (phenylpropanoid, safe); *cis*-anethole (minor isomer, estrogenic effects at high doses — concerns about herbal supplements); metabolism to anisaldehyde and anisketone
- **Caraway** (*Carum carvi*): carvone (*R*-enantiomer — caraway/dill; *S*-enantiomer — spearmint; same molecule, opposite chirality, completely different smell — one of the most striking examples of olfactory chirality); Northern/Central European spice; rye bread, sauerkraut, aquavit; *Dill* (*Anethum graveolens*) — similar compounds (carvone + limonene); dill seed vs fresh dill weed
- **Anise** (*Pimpinella anisum*): Mediterranean; anethole-dominant; distinction from star anise (different plant, same key compound); green Chartreuse, absinthe, pastis, ouzo, arak, sambuca, pernod all use anise/star anise
