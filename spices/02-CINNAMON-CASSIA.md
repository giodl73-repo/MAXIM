# 02 — Cinnamon & Cassia

## The Bark Spices — Ceylon vs Cassia, Colonial Monopoly, Coumarin Chemistry

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Botany: both are dried inner bark of *Cinnamomum* trees; Ceylon/true cinnamon (*C. verum*, Sri Lanka) vs cassia (*C. aromaticum*, southern China/Vietnam; *C. cassia*, *C. burmanii* = Indonesian cinnamon, *C. loureiroi* = Vietnamese/Saigon cinnamon); most "cinnamon" sold in North America is cassia
- How to distinguish: Ceylon — thin, papery, multiple layers, quill rolls from both sides → tight scroll; cassia — thick, single-layer, rough, curls one side only, darker reddish-brown
- Key compounds: cinnamaldehyde (the primary flavor/aroma compound, ~90% of essential oil in both) — responsible for TRPV1 activation (slight warmth); eugenol (higher in Ceylon); coumarin — present in cassia at ~2,100 ppm vs Ceylon ~250 ppm; coumarin hepatotoxic at high doses → EU limits on cassia in foods
- Sri Lanka dominance: Ceylon cinnamon indigenous to wet zone of Sri Lanka; Dutch VOC seized Portuguese Ceylon (1658) to control cinnamon; Dutch cinnamon gardens, punishing smugglers with death; British takeover (1796) and eventual loss of monopoly as cultivation spread to Java, Seychelles, Brazil
- Cinnamon in antiquity: mentioned in Chinese writing 2700 BCE; Egyptian embalming; Hebrew Bible; mystery of ancient cinnamon sources (possibly East African/Arabian trade routes for cassia confused with Ceylon)
- Ancient cinnamon birds myth: Herodotus and Aristotle claimed cinnamon was brought by giant birds to build nests — Arab traders propagated this to protect source locations
- Modern production: Sri Lanka still top producer of Ceylon; China/Indonesia/Vietnam dominate cassia; cinnamon essential oil in food industry (flavoring, antimicrobial properties — cinnamaldehyde active against many bacteria/fungi)
- Culinary geography: Ceylon preferred in Mexico, Sri Lanka, UK; cassia dominant in North America and China; Vietnamese/Saigon cassia highest cinnamaldehyde content (~5%) → most intense flavor
