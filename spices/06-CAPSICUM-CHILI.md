# 06 — Capsicum & Chili

## The New World's Hottest Export — Columbian Exchange, TRPV1, and Global Adoption

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Botany: genus *Capsicum* (Solanaceae family, related to tomatoes, potatoes, eggplant); five domesticated species — *C. annuum* (most common: bell pepper, jalapeño, cayenne, paprika), *C. chinense* (habanero, Scotch bonnet, Carolina Reaper), *C. frutescens* (Tabasco), *C. baccatum* (aji amarillo), *C. pubescens* (rocoto); wild ancestor: *C. annuum* var. *glabriusculum* (bird pepper, Mexico/Caribbean)
- Domestication: independent domestication in at least 3 locations in Mexico/South America by ~6,000 BCE; evidence from Huaca Prieta, Peru; capsaicin likely evolved as seed dispersal mechanism — birds (which disperse seeds) insensitive to capsaicin, mammals (which crush seeds) deterred
- Columbian Exchange: Columbus 1492 — brought chilies to Spain (confused with black pepper, called "pepper"); Portuguese maritime spread was fastest in history for a food plant: reached India, West Africa, Southeast Asia all before 1550; within 50 years, chilies integrated into cuisines as if indigenous everywhere
- Why adoption was so rapid: cheap, easy to grow in tropics, shelf-stable dried, could replace expensive black pepper flavor profile; nutritional bonus (high vitamin C — critical for scurvy prevention); spread along Portuguese trade routes, then independently
- Capsaicin chemistry: vanillylamide alkaloid; produced in placenta (white membrane) not the flesh; birds completely insensitive (no functional TRPV1 binding for capsaicin); 8-methyl-N-vanillyl-6-nonenamide; dihydrocapsaicin (second most abundant); capsaicinoids collectively determine heat
- TRPV1 receptor: Transient Receptor Potential Vanilloid 1 (discovered by David Julius, Nobel 2021); thermosensor that fires above 43°C ("nociceptive" temperature); capsaicin binds and opens channel at room temperature → brain experiences "burning heat" that isn't actually hot; topical capsaicin creams deplete substance P → pain relief
- Scoville scale: Wilbur Scoville's 1912 organoleptic test (panel of tasters — subjective); modern measurement is HPLC (high-performance liquid chromatography) of capsaicinoid content, converted to Scoville Heat Units (SHU); bell pepper = 0, jalapeño = 2,500–8,000, habanero = 100,000–350,000, Carolina Reaper = 1.4–2.2M SHU; pure capsaicin = 16M
- Geographic spread outcomes: Indian cuisine redefined (no vindaloo, no most curries without chili); Sichuan mala (numbing-hot combination with Sichuan pepper); Korean kimchi and gochujang; Hungarian paprika (Bell pepper dried = sweet paprika, keystone of goulash); West African piri-piri; Thai cuisine; all post-1500
- Paprika: *C. annuum* cultivars specifically bred for low capsaicin + high color (carotenoids — capsanthin, capsorubin give red color); Hungary and Spain as PDO production centers; smoked paprika (pimentón de La Vera)
