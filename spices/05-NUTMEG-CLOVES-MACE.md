# 05 — Nutmeg, Cloves & Mace

## The Banda Islands — The Original Spice Islands and the Birth of Corporate Capitalism

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Nutmeg** (*Myristica fragrans*): a single fruit yields both nutmeg (the seed) and mace (the red aril surrounding the seed); native exclusively to Banda Islands (10 small volcanic islands, Maluku, Indonesia); this geographic specificity is unique among major spices
- Nutmeg botany: large evergreen tree; dioecious (separate male/female trees — farmers kept few males, mostly females for fruit production); 20-year wait to first fruit; productive for 70+ years; fruit resembles apricot, splits when ripe
- Nutmeg chemistry: myristicin (3–4% essential oil) — psychoactive at large doses (monoamine oxidase inhibitor, metabolizes to MMDA-like compound); elemicin; safrol; safrole; eugenol; nutmeg is technically a hallucinogen in quantity (Malcolm X prison memoir); normal culinary use poses no risk
- Mace: same tree, different part (dried aril); similar flavor to nutmeg but more delicate and floral; separately traded; higher price per unit weight
- **Cloves** (*Syzygium aromaticum*): dried flower buds; native to Ternate and Tidore (North Maluku); eugenol ~72–90% of essential oil — analgesic properties (clove oil for toothache — real mechanism, eugenol is TRPV1 agonist and local anesthetic)
- **The Banda Islands story**: only place on Earth where nutmeg grew until 18th century; Arab traders, then Portuguese (Afonso de Albuquerque seized Malacca 1511; established Fort in Banda 1512); Dutch VOC arrival and the transformation of trading to monopoly by force
- VOC Banda massacre (1621): Jan Pieterszoon Coen organized systematic extermination of Bandanese population (~15,000 people killed or enslaved, 1,000 survivors) to enforce nutmeg monopoly; replaced with Dutch-run plantation system (*perken*) using slave labor; one of the earliest examples of corporate-driven genocide
- Run Island (Pulau Run): tiny Banda island held by English; Anglo-Dutch Treaty of Breda (1667) — English traded Run Island to Dutch in exchange for New Amsterdam (Manhattan); nutmeg was considered the more valuable asset at the time
- Breaking the monopoly: French botanist Pierre Poivre (literally "Peter Pepper") smuggled nutmeg and clove seedlings from Banda to Mauritius (1770); eventually propagated to Zanzibar (now world's top clove producer), Grenada (nutmeg — on the national flag), Indonesia
- Modern production: Indonesia still top producer; Grenada = "Island of Spice"; Zanzibar cloves; clove cigarettes (*kretek*) — Indonesian cultural product consuming ~95% of Indonesia's clove production
