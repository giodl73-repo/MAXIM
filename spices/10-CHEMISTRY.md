# 10 — Spice Chemistry

## Alkaloids, Phenylpropanoids, Terpenes — Why Plants Make These Compounds

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Why plants make "spice" compounds: secondary metabolites (not primary metabolic machinery); hypotheses — herbivore deterrence, pathogen resistance, seed dispersal manipulation (capsaicin deters mammals/attracts birds), UV protection (flavonoids), allelopathy; many have co-evolved with specific animal interactions
- **Pungency compounds** (heat/burn):
  - Capsaicin (vanilloid alkaloid): binds TRPV1 at Tyr511/Ser512 in transmembrane domain; same receptor that fires at >43°C; explains "burning" sensation at room temperature; 8-methyl-N-vanillyl-6-nonenamide structure; biosynthesized from phenylalanine (via phenylpropanoid pathway) + valine
  - Piperine (alkaloid): different structural class (piperidine alkaloid); different binding mechanism from capsaicin; inhibits CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein → increases bioavailability of many compounds (drug interaction concern)
  - Gingerols/shogaols (phenylpropanoid-derived): structural similarity to capsaicin explains shared TRPV1 activity; fresh vs dried transformation
  - Allyl isothiocyanate (mustard/horseradish/wasabi): not pre-formed — produced by myrosinase cleaving sinigrin when plant tissue damaged; activates TRPA1 (not TRPV1) → different quality of burn (sinus-clearing vs mouth/tongue)
- **Phenylpropanoid pathway**: major biosynthetic route for spice compounds; phenylalanine → cinnamic acid (PAL enzyme) → p-coumaric acid → diverse downstream products including: cinnamates (cinnamon), eugenol (clove/bay/nutmeg), safrole, chavicol, anethole, capsaicin backbone; pathway shared across plants, explaining convergent chemical profiles
- **Terpenes and terpenoids**: built from 5-carbon isoprene (C₅) units via MVA or MEP pathway; monoterpenes (C₁₀): thymol, carvacrol, menthol, camphor, limonene, 1,8-cineole (eucalyptol), linalool, α-pinene; sesquiterpenes (C₁₅): bisabolene, zingiberene (ginger); responsible for most "aroma" without pungency; chirality determines character (R-carvone = caraway, S-carvone = spearmint)
- **Alkaloids** in spices: piperine, capsaicin-related vanillamides, caffeine (coffee/tea/cacao), theobromine (cacao); nitrogen-containing secondary metabolites; often bitter; historically the "medicines" in spices
- Olfactory vs gustatory: aroma compounds detected by olfactory epithelium (volatile, airborne); taste compounds detected by tongue receptors (non-volatile, in solution); retronasal olfaction (breathing out while eating) → most of "flavor" is actually smell; explains why capsaicin burns (trigeminal nerve, not taste)
- Chirality and flavor: R/S enantiomers of carvone (caraway vs spearmint), limonene (orange vs turpentine), terpinen-4-ol; olfactory receptors are chiral — they are proteins (inherently asymmetric) → can distinguish mirror images; explains why same molecular formula smells completely different
- Volatility and preservation: essential oil volatility (vapor pressure at room temperature) determines aroma quality; heat accelerates loss; light oxidizes terpenes (limonene → carvone → off-flavors); whole spices >> ground (surface area × exposure); dark airtight storage; refrigeration for fresh herbs
- Industrial extraction: steam distillation (most essential oils), cold pressing (citrus peel), solvent extraction → oleoresin (less volatile compounds retained), supercritical CO₂ extraction (best quality, no solvent residue)
- Synergy and quenching: flavor compounds interact; capsaicin + fat (dairy casein binds capsaicin, explains why milk relieves chili burn better than water); bread/starch as mechanical dilution; certain combinations perceived as more or less than sum of parts
