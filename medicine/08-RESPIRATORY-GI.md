## 1. RESPIRATORY: β2-Agonists

```
MECHANISM:
  β2-receptor (Gs-coupled GPCR) → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP ↑ → PKA activation
  PKA phosphorylates: myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) → inactivation → smooth muscle relaxation
  Also: mast cell stabilization; mucociliary clearance ↑
  Selectivity: β2 >> β1 (cardiac); at high doses → β1 effects (tachycardia)

SABA — SHORT-ACTING β2-AGONISTS (rescue):
  Salbutamol (albuterol): onset 5 min; duration 4-6 hrs; racemic (R-isomer active)
  Terbutaline: similar; also used for premature labor (tocolytic)
  Indication: acute bronchospasm (asthma/COPD exacerbation); exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB)
  Adverse: tachycardia, tremor, HYPOKALEMIA (K+ shifted intracellularly by cAMP); dysrhythmia at high doses
  Monitoring: K+ in high-dose nebulization (e.g., status asthmaticus)

LABA — LONG-ACTING β2-AGONISTS (maintenance):
  Salmeterol: onset 30 min; duration 12 hrs; lipophilic → anchors in membrane → slow release
  Formoterol: onset 5 min (faster than salmeterol); duration 12 hrs; can be used as rescue in some regimens
  Indacaterol: once-daily; 24-hr duration; for COPD
  CRITICAL: LABA monotherapy is CONTRAINDICATED in asthma (SMART trial: increased asthma mortality)
    Always combine with ICS in asthma (LABA+ICS fixed-dose inhaler)
    Rationale: ICS treats underlying inflammation; LABA controls symptoms; LABA alone masks worsening
  In COPD: LABA monotherapy acceptable (no ICS mandate); LABA+LAMA = standard dual bronchodilator

MECHANISM OF TOLERANCE:
  β-receptor downregulation with chronic SABA overuse → reduced rescue effect
  Clinical significance: overuse of SABA (>2 canisters/month) = marker of poor control
```

## 2. RESPIRATORY: Anticholinergics (SAMA/LAMA)

```
MECHANISM:
  Muscarinic M3 receptors: activation → Gq → IP3/DAG → smooth muscle contraction + mucus secretion
  Anticholinergics block M3 → reduce bronchoconstriction and mucus hypersecretion
  β2-agonists more potent bronchodilators acutely; anticholinergics better for mucus/COPD
  Synergy: LABA+LAMA combination more effective than either alone (different mechanisms)

SAMA — SHORT-ACTING MUSCARINIC ANTAGONIST:
  Ipratropium: onset 15-30 min; duration 6-8 hrs; quaternary ammonium → poor GI absorption → few systemic effects
  Indicated: COPD first-line; acute asthma when inadequate β2 response (add-on)
  Combination: salbutamol + ipratropium (Duovent/Combivent) — synergistic in acute COPD exacerbation

LAMA — LONG-ACTING MUSCARINIC ANTAGONIST:
  Tiotropium (Spiriva): once-daily; kinetic selectivity M1/M3 over M2 (M2 presynaptic → inhibits ACh release; blocking M2 would paradoxically increase ACh)
    Dissociation rate: very slow from M1/M3 but fast from M2 → effective selectivity
  Umeclidinium, aclidinium, glycopyrronium: once-daily alternatives; similar profile
  Adverse: urinary retention (M3 in detrusor), constipation, dry mouth, blurred vision (angle-closure glaucoma risk)
  Contraindication: narrow-angle glaucoma (use with caution; ensure not acute)
  Clinical: tiotropium reduces COPD exacerbation rate, hospitalizations; slows FEV1 decline

GINA / GOLD STEP-UP:
  Asthma: ICS → ICS+LABA → ICS+LABA+LAMA (add tiotropium at Step 4-5)
  COPD: LAMA or LABA monotherapy → LAMA+LABA → LAMA+LABA+ICS (if eosinophils/FEV1 <50%)
```

## 3. RESPIRATORY: Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)

```
MECHANISM:
  GCS (glucocorticoid receptor) → nuclear translocation → transcription factor modulation:
    Transactivation: anti-inflammatory genes (IL-10, annexin-1)
    Transrepression: inhibit NF-κB → ↓ IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, eotaxin, TNF-α
  Results: reduced eosinophil infiltration, mast cell degranulation, mucus production, edema
  Inhaled delivery: local lung effect; minimizes systemic exposure vs oral steroids

AGENTS:
  Fluticasone propionate (FP): high potency; high first-pass effect → low oral bioavailability
  Budesonide: moderate; swallowed fraction inactivated by hepatic first-pass; used in nebulized form (croup)
  Beclomethasone (BDP): prodrug → active beclomethasone-17-monopropionate in lungs
  Mometasone: high receptor affinity; once-daily; intranasal + inhaled
  Ciclesonide: prodrug activated in airways; lower oropharyngeal deposition

ADVERSE EFFECTS:
  Local: oropharyngeal candidiasis (preventable with spacer + rinse/gargle after use)
  Local: dysphonia (hoarseness) — muscle effect on vocal cords; often reversible
  Systemic (high-dose chronic): HPA suppression (rare at standard doses; significant at fluticasone >1000 mcg/day)
  Systemic: reduced bone density (long-term high-dose); fracture risk; monitor DEXA
  Pediatric growth: some reduction at high doses; outweighed by asthma control benefit
  Not the same as systemic corticosteroids: much lower systemic exposure at therapeutic doses

ICS DOSE CATEGORIES (fluticasone propionate equivalent):
  Low: ≤250 mcg/day; Medium: 251-500 mcg/day; High: >500 mcg/day
```

## 4. RESPIRATORY: Methylxanthines and Biologics

```
METHYLXANTHINES:
  Theophylline: narrow therapeutic index (TI); PDE (phosphodiesterase) inhibitor → cAMP ↑
    Also: adenosine receptor antagonist (central stimulant); anti-inflammatory at low concentrations
  Mechanism: non-selective PDE inhibitor; weak bronchodilator; histone deacetylase activation (anti-inflam)
  Pharmacokinetics: oral; hepatic CYP1A2 + CYP3A4 metabolism; many drug interactions
    Inducers: smoking, rifampicin, carbamazepine → lower levels → inadequate effect
    Inhibitors: ciprofloxacin, erythromycin, cimetidine → elevated levels → toxicity
  Therapeutic range: 5-15 mg/L; toxicity: 20+ mg/L (seizures, arrhythmias, nausea, vomiting)
  Monitoring: serum levels; ECG; status of interacting drugs
  Role: largely replaced by safer LABA/ICS combinations; still used in LMIC and some COPD

BIOLOGICS FOR SEVERE ASTHMA:
  Anti-IgE — Omalizumab (Xolair):
    IgE mediated: Type 1 hypersensitivity → mast cell degranulation; IgE binds FcεRI
    Omalizumab: anti-IgE mAb; binds free IgE → prevents FcεRI binding; reduces mast cell priming
    Indication: allergic asthma, serum IgE 30-1500 IU/mL, sensitized to perennial aeroallergen
    Effect: reduces exacerbations ~25-50%; allows ICS dose reduction
    Dosing: SC every 2-4 weeks; dose based on IgE level and body weight

  Anti-IL-5 (eosinophil-targeted):
    IL-5: key eosinophil survival/differentiation cytokine
    Mepolizumab: anti-IL-5 mAb; reduces blood/sputum eosinophils
    Benralizumab: anti-IL-5Rα; direct eosinophil apoptosis via ADCC
    Indication: severe eosinophilic asthma (blood eos ≥ 300/µL); reduces exacerbations 50%+
    Also used: eosinophilic COPD (mepolizumab), EGPA (eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis)

  Anti-TSLP — Tezepelumab (Tezspire):
    TSLP: upstream epithelial alarmin; triggers Th2 cascade, ILC2 activation
    Broadest mechanism: works for eosinophilic AND non-eosinophilic severe asthma
    Reduces exacerbations regardless of blood eosinophil count
    Most recent approval (FDA 2021); premium pricing

  Anti-IL-4Rα — Dupilumab (Dupixent):
    Blocks IL-4 + IL-13 receptor → reduces Th2 inflammation
    Approved: severe asthma + atopic dermatitis + chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps (triple indication)
    FeNO as biomarker: high FeNO (>25 ppb) predicts dupilumab response

ANTIFIBROTICS (IPF — Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis):
  Pirfenidone: anti-TGF-β; reduces fibroblast proliferation; slows FVC decline; GI side effects
  Nintedanib: multi-kinase inhibitor (PDGFR, VEGFR, FGFR, TGF-βR); also GI side effects
  Both slow decline; no cure; lung transplant for end-stage
```

## 5. GI: Acid Suppression

```
PROTON PUMP INHIBITORS (PPIs):
  Mechanism: irreversibly inhibit H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump) in gastric parietal cells
    PPIs are prodrugs: absorbed in small intestine → activated by acid in parietal cell canaliculi
    Active form: sulfenamide; covalently binds cysteines on H+/K+-ATPase
    Irreversible: recovery requires new pump synthesis (~18-24 hrs for full effect)
  Acid suppression: pH > 4 in ~80% of day on once-daily dosing
  Timing: take 30-60 min BEFORE meals (pump must be active for PPI to bind)

  Common agents:
    Omeprazole (original PPI), esomeprazole (S-isomer; slightly better bioavailability)
    Lansoprazole, pantoprazole (IV available), rabeprazole, dexlansoprazole

  Indications:
    GERD: gold standard; heals erosive esophagitis
    Peptic ulcer disease (PUD): H. pylori eradication + PPI × 4-8 weeks
    H. pylori triple therapy: PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin × 14 days (or quadruple)
    Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: gastrinoma → massive acid hypersecretion; high-dose PPI
    NSAID-induced ulcer prevention: PPI co-prescription with NSAIDs in high-risk patients
    Stress ulcer prophylaxis: ICU patients on mechanical ventilation

  Long-term adverse effects (increasing recognition):
    Hypomagnesemia: impairs Mg2+ absorption (especially with diuretics, digoxin → arrhythmia)
    C. difficile infection: reduced acid → bacterial overgrowth; ~2× increased C. diff risk
    Fractures: osteoporosis/fracture risk with long-term (reduced Ca2+ absorption)
    Renal: acute interstitial nephritis (rare); CKD association (controversial causality)
    Vitamin B12 malabsorption: acid needed for pepsin activation → B12 release
    Community-acquired pneumonia: slightly increased risk (acid barrier to organisms)

H2-RECEPTOR ANTAGONISTS (H2 blockers):
  Mechanism: competitive antagonist at histamine H2 receptor on parietal cells
    H2 receptor → Gs → cAMP ↑ → PKA → H+/K+-ATPase activation → acid secretion
    Block H2 → reduce stimulated acid secretion (less complete than PPIs)
  Agents: famotidine (q12h; most potent), ranitidine (withdrawn — NDMA contamination 2019), cimetidine (multiple drug interactions via CYP450)
  Tolerance: tachyphylaxis within days — receptor upregulation
  Less effective than PPIs for erosive disease; OK for GERD symptom relief
  IV famotidine: used in ICU stress ulcer prophylaxis when oral not available
  Useful: nocturnal acid suppression added to PPI (some refractory GERD benefit)
```

## 6. GI: Motility and Antiemetics

```
PROKINETICS:
  Metoclopramide:
    Mechanism: D2 antagonist (CNS + peripheral) + 5-HT4 agonist
    D2 block → reduces tonic dopaminergic inhibition of motility → increased LES tone + gastric emptying
    5-HT4 → directly stimulates gastric/intestinal motility
    Indications: gastroparesis, GERD, CINV (chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting), opiate-induced constipation
    Adverse: extrapyramidal side effects (akathisia, tardive dyskinesia with long-term → irreversible!)
      Maximum 12 weeks per FDA; highest EPS risk of GI prokinetics
    CNS effects: sedation, fatigue; crosses BBB (unlike domperidone)
    Hyperprolactinemia: blocks D2 in anterior pituitary → prolactin ↑ → galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia

  Domperidone:
    D2 antagonist (peripheral; poor CNS penetration — less EPS risk)
    Better GI prokinetic profile; fewer CNS effects
    Not FDA approved in US (cardiac risk concern: QT prolongation); widely used in Europe/Canada/Australia
    QT: caution with other QT-prolonging drugs; potassium should be normal

  Erythromycin (low-dose):
    Motilin receptor agonist → triggers migrating motor complex (MMC) → powerful gastric emptying
    IV 1-3 mg/kg: very effective for acute gastroparesis
    Oral: tachyphylaxis develops within days; limited long-term utility
    Antibiotic effects at low prokinetic doses: not significant (selective for motilin)

ANTIEMETICS — 5-HT3 ANTAGONISTS (first-line for CINV):
  Ondansetron (Zofran), granisetron, palonosetron (longer half-life; better for delayed CINV)
  Mechanism: block 5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferents and CTZ (chemoreceptor trigger zone)
  Serotonin released by enterochromaffin cells → activates vagal 5-HT3 → emetic reflex
  Very effective: acute CINV, post-op nausea (PONV), radiotherapy-induced
  Adverse: constipation, headache; QT prolongation (high doses, IV ondansetron > 32mg — FDA restriction)
  Combined with dexamethasone ± NK1 antagonist for highly emetogenic chemo

  NK1 ANTAGONISTS:
  Aprepitant (oral), fosaprepitant (IV prodrug): block substance P at NK1 receptor in vomiting center
  Third component of antiemetic triple therapy (5-HT3 + dex + NK1) for highly emetogenic cisplatin
  CYP3A4 interactions: aprepitant both inhibits and induces CYP3A4 → multiple drug interactions
  Rolapitant, netupitant: longer-acting NK1 antagonists

ANTIEMETICS — DOPAMINE ANTAGONISTS:
  Prochlorperazine: D2 block in CTZ + AP; used for nausea/vomiting from various causes
  Promethazine: H1 + D2 + muscarinic block; sedating; phenothiazine class; IM/IV/oral
    IV administration: extravasation risk (IV formulation can cause severe tissue damage → prefer IM/oral)
  Haloperidol: D2; used in palliative care for intractable nausea

ANTIEMETICS — OTHER:
  Dexamethasone: effective antiemetic via unclear mechanism (possibly PGE2 suppression, BBB penetration)
    Single dose pre-chemo; also used PONV
  Dronabinol (cannabis-derived cannabinoid): CB1 agonist; approved for CINV refractory to other antiemetics
  Scopolamine patch (transdermal): muscarinic antagonist; effective for motion sickness; PONV
```

## 7. GI: Lower GI — Laxatives and Antidiarrheals

```
LAXATIVES:
  Osmotic:
    Lactulose: non-absorbable disaccharide → bacterial fermentation → osmotic load + acidification
      Also: hepatic encephalopathy (acidic pH traps NH3 as NH4+ → fecal excretion)
    Polyethylene glycol (PEG, Miralax): non-absorbable; draws water into colon; gentler
    Magnesium salts (Mg hydroxide, Mg citrate): osmotic; rapid onset; used for bowel prep
    Sodium phosphate: powerful; avoid in renal failure (phosphate retention → acute phosphate nephropathy)

  Stimulant:
    Bisacodyl: direct colonic stimulation + electrolyte secretion; 6-8 hrs oral; 15-60 min rectal (PR)
    Senna (sennosides): anthraquinone prodrug; colonic bacteria activate → stimulant effect; 8-12 hrs
    Risk: melanosis coli (brown pigmentation; harmless); cathartic colon with chronic overuse (controversial)

  Bulk-forming:
    Psyllium, methylcellulose: absorb water → increase stool bulk → stimulate peristalsis
    Safest long-term; must take with adequate water (obstruction risk if dehydrated)
    Benefit: also reduce LDL (binds bile acids)

  Secretagogues/Channel activators:
    Lubiprostone: ClC-2 Cl- channel activator → fluid secretion into intestine; for chronic idiopathic constipation, opioid-induced
    Linaclotide, plecanatide: GC-C (guanylate cyclase-C) agonist → cGMP → CFTR activation → fluid secretion
      Also reduce visceral pain (cGMP activates sensory neurons via phosphodiesterases) → IBS-C

ANTIDIARRHEALS:
  Loperamide (Imodium): μ-opioid receptor agonist (peripheral only; P-glycoprotein at BBB prevents CNS entry)
    Reduces peristalsis + increases anal sphincter tone; does not cause CNS opioid effects at normal doses
    Indications: acute/traveler's diarrhea; ileostomy high output; chronic diarrhea
    Contraindication: bloody diarrhea/infectious colitis with fever (may precipitate toxic megacolon)
    Overdose: QT prolongation + cardiac arrhythmia (high doses; cases in opioid misuse)

  Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol): anti-secretory + anti-microbial
    Active against H. pylori (bismuth quadruple therapy); traveler's diarrhea prophylaxis
    Converts to bismuth + salicylate in GI; avoid in aspirin allergy, Reye's syndrome risk in children

  Cholestyramine: bile acid sequestrant; indicated for bile acid malabsorption diarrhea (post-cholecystectomy, Crohn's terminal ileum resection)

IBD DRUGS:
  5-ASA (mesalazine/mesalamine): topical anti-inflammatory; releases 5-aminosalicylic acid in colon
    NF-κB inhibition + prostaglandin reduction in colonic mucosa
    Enema/suppository for distal disease; oral for extensive disease
    Used: ulcerative colitis (highly effective); minimal Crohn's benefit
    Adverse: nephrotoxicity (rare; check renal function); generally well tolerated

  Azathioprine/6-mercaptopurine (6-MP): thiopurine immunosuppressants (see 07-IMMUNOMODULATORS)
  Biologics: anti-TNF (infliximab, adalimumab), anti-integrin (vedolizumab), anti-IL-12/23 (ustekinumab) — see 07
```

## 8. Decision Cheat Sheet

| Condition / Situation | Drug / Approach | Key Notes |
|----------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Asthma rescue | SABA (albuterol/salbutamol) | Onset 5 min; 4-6 hr duration; use PRN, not scheduled |
| Asthma maintenance (persistent) | ICS ± LABA | LABA alone in asthma = CONTRAINDICATED (SMART trial: ↑ mortality without ICS) |
| COPD maintenance | LAMA (tiotropium) or LABA; dual LABA+LAMA | LAMA preferred initial single-agent; dual preferred for symptomatic or exacerbation prevention |
| Severe asthma (add-on biologic) | Anti-IL-5 (mepolizumab) if eosinophilic; anti-TSLP (tezepelumab) for any phenotype | Tezepelumab works regardless of eosinophil count — broadest coverage |
| Acid suppression (GERD, PUD) | PPI 30-60 min BEFORE meals | Pump must be active (acid secretion occurring) when PPI arrives to get activated |
| CINV (chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting) | 5-HT3 antagonist (ondansetron) + dexamethasone ± NK1 antagonist (aprepitant) | Add aprepitant for highly emetogenic regimens (cisplatin, AC) |
| H. pylori eradication | Triple therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin × 14d | Check local clarithromycin resistance; bismuth quadruple if high resistance area |
| Diarrhea (traveler's, non-infectious) | Loperamide (μ-opioid peripheral) | Avoid in bloody diarrhea or fever (risk toxic megacolon in C. diff / invasive infection) |
| IBD — ulcerative colitis | 5-ASA (mesalazine) for mild-moderate; corticosteroids for flare; biologics for moderate-severe | 5-ASA has minimal benefit in Crohn's disease |
| IBD — Crohn's disease | Corticosteroids (flare); anti-TNF (infliximab, adalimumab); vedolizumab; ustekinumab | Aminosalicylates not effective; "top-down" biologics increasingly preferred |
| Prokinetic (gastroparesis) | Metoclopramide ≤ 12 weeks (FDA) | Tardive dyskinesia risk irreversible with long-term use |
| ICS local side effects prevention | Spacer device + mouth rinse/gargle after each use | Prevents oropharyngeal candidiasis and dysphonia |
| Constipation (CIC or IBS-C) | Linaclotide (GC-C agonist) or lubiprostone (ClC-2 activator) | Secretagogues: both increase luminal fluid; linaclotide also has visceral analgesic effect |
| Theophylline monitoring | Target 5-15 mg/L; narrow TI | Toxicity (seizures, arrhythmias) at >20 mg/L; multiple CYP interactions |

## Common Confusion Points

**SABA tachyphylaxis vs LABA tolerance:** Regular use of SABAs → β2-receptor downregulation → reduced rescue effectiveness. This is a clinical sign of poor asthma control (not a reason to use more SABA). LABAs have different kinetics (salmeterol anchors in membrane); less tachyphylaxis but still require ICS co-prescription in asthma.

**PPIs as prodrugs — why timing matters:** PPIs must be activated in the acidic parietal cell canaliculus. Taking PPI with a meal ensures the parietal cell is actively secreting acid when PPI arrives. Taking PPI at night (when pumps are dormant) = much less activation = poor acid control. Bedtime PPI is the classic pharmacy error.

**Metoclopramide CNS vs domperidone:** Both are D2 antagonists. Metoclopramide crosses the BBB (causes akathisia, sedation, EPS, hyperprolactinemia). Domperidone is excluded by P-glycoprotein at BBB — fewer CNS effects, same peripheral prokinetic effect. In the US, domperidone was never approved (cardiac concerns). In Europe, domperidone is first-line.

**Ipratropium (SAMA) for asthma vs COPD:** Ipratropium is second-line for acute severe asthma (add to β2 if inadequate response); it is first-line bronchodilator for COPD. This reflects different pathophysiology: asthma is primarily β2-mediated (epithelial/mast cell); COPD is primarily cholinergic bronchomotor tone + mucus hypersecretion.

**Lactulose for hepatic encephalopathy:** Lactulose works here not as an osmotic laxative per se, but because colonic bacteria ferment it to organic acids → acidification → NH3 (from protein breakdown) is trapped as NH4+ (non-absorbable) → excreted in stool. Target 2-4 loose stools/day in hepatic encephalopathy. This is the primary mechanism in that indication.
