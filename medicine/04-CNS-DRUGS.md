## Big Picture: CNS Drug Landscape

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     CNS DRUG TARGETS                                     │
├─────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ MONOAMINE SYSTEM    │ SSRIs/SNRIs: block SERT/NET reuptake               │
│ (5-HT/NE/DA)        │ TCAs: block SERT + NET + H1 + muscarinic + α1     │
│                     │ MAOIs: block MAO-A/B → ↑ all monoamines            │
│                     │ Bupropion: DA + NE reuptake inhibitor              │
├─────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ DOPAMINE SYSTEM     │ D2 antagonists: antipsychotics (all classes)       │
│                     │ D2 partial agonists: aripiprazole, brexpiprazole   │
│                     │ VMAT2 inhibitors: tetrabenazine, valbenazine       │
├─────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ GABA SYSTEM         │ Benzodiazepines: GABA-A PAM (↑ Cl⁻ frequency)     │
│                     │ Barbiturates: GABA-A PAM (↑ Cl⁻ duration)         │
│                     │ Valproate: ↑ GABA synthesis/release                │
│                     │ Vigabatrin: GABA-T inhibitor → ↑ GABA             │
├─────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ GLUTAMATE SYSTEM    │ Memantine, ketamine, esketamine: NMDA antagonists  │

│                     │   firing, also ↓ glutamate release indirectly)     │
├─────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ OPIOID RECEPTORS    │ μ (MOR): analgesia, euphoria, respiratory dep.     │
│                     │ κ (KOR): analgesia, dysphoria, sedation            │
│                     │ δ (DOR): mood, analgesia                          │
└─────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## 1. Antidepressants

### SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)

**Mechanism:** Block SERT (serotonin transporter) → ↑ synaptic 5-HT. 2–4 week onset for clinical effect because:
1. Initial: 5-HT1A autoreceptors (raphe nucleus) activated by ↑ synaptic 5-HT → negative feedback (↓ neuron firing)
2. Weeks 2–4: 5-HT1A autoreceptors desensitize → ↑ tonic 5-HT release → downstream neuroplasticity
3. Downstream: ↑ BDNF → hippocampal synaptogenesis (neuroplasticity model)

| Drug | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| Fluoxetine | Long t½ (7 days for norfluoxetine) → safest if doses missed; less discontinuation syndrome; CYP2D6 inhibitor |
| Sertraline | Generally preferred (minimal drug interactions, broad evidence base) |
| Escitalopram | S-enantiomer of citalopram; very clean receptor profile; minimal interactions; preferred in elderly |
| Citalopram | Dose-dependent QTc prolongation → max 40mg (20mg if >60yr/hepatic/CYP2C19 inhibitor) |
| Paroxetine | Most anticholinergic SSRI; short t½ → worst discontinuation syndrome; CYP2D6 inhibitor; teratogenic (cardiac?) → avoid in pregnancy |
| Fluvoxamine | OCD; CYP1A2 + CYP3A4 inhibitor → major drug interactions |

**SSRIs class adverse effects:**
- **Sexual dysfunction** (delayed ejaculation, anorgasmia, ↓ libido): 30–40%; a major reason for non-adherence
- **GI:** Nausea (dose-related, transient), diarrhea (especially sertraline)
- **Insomnia** (activating SSRIs: fluoxetine, sertraline)
- **Weight gain** (modest, especially paroxetine and citalopram)
- **Hyponatremia:** SIADH mechanism; elderly, diuretics → ↑ risk
- **Serotonin syndrome** (see below)
- **Discontinuation syndrome** (especially short t½): brain zaps, dizziness, flu-like, anxiety; taper when stopping

**Serotonin syndrome triad:** Altered mental status + autonomic instability + neuromuscular abnormalities (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor)
- Causes: SSRI + MAOI, SSRI + tramadol, SSRI + linezolid (MAOI properties), SSRI + fentanyl/meperidine
- Severe: hyperthermia, seizures, rhabdomyolysis → cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist) + cooling + BZDs
- Distinguish from NMS (neuroleptic malignant syndrome): NMS = dopamine depletion from antipsychotic; slower onset, rigidity > clonus; bromocriptine/dantrolene

### SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors)

**Drugs:** Venlafaxine, duloxetine, desvenlafaxine, levomilnacipran, milnacipran (fibromyalgia only)

**Added NE reuptake inhibition** → additional benefit for:
- Pain (neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia — duloxetine FDA-approved for DPN, GAD, fibromyalgia, MSK pain)
- Fatigue, concentration (NE + DA involvement)
- Possibly more effective than SSRIs in severe depression (meta-analysis suggests)

**Adverse:** Similar to SSRIs + ↑ BP (NE effect → caution in uncontrolled HTN), urinary retention
**Venlafaxine** high dose > 225mg: significant NE effect. Low dose: SSRI-like profile only.
**Discontinuation:** Similar to paroxetine (short t½ preparations); extended-release reduces this.

### TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)

**Mechanism:** Block SERT + NET + muscarinic + H1 + α1 receptors

```
Therapeutic effects: SERT (5-HT↑) + NET (NE↑)
Side effects from "off-target" receptor blockade:
  M1 (muscarinic): dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, constipation, tachycardia, confusion
  H1 (histamine): sedation, weight gain
  α1 (α1-adrenergic): orthostatic hypotension
```

**Cardiotoxicity:** Fast Na⁺ channel block → QRS widening, QTc prolongation, arrhythmias. In overdose: VT, VF.
- TCA overdose: sodium bicarbonate (IV) → alkalinize blood → ↑ protein binding of TCA + ↑ Na gradient → narrow QRS

**Uses:** Depression (2nd line), neuropathic pain (amitriptyline: low dose 10–25mg), migraine prophylaxis, enuresis (imipramine), OCD (clomipramine — most serotonergic TCA).

**Key drugs:** Amitriptyline (most sedating), nortriptyline (metabolite of amitriptyline; less anticholinergic), imipramine, clomipramine, desipramine (most NE-selective, least anticholinergic).

### MAOIs

**Mechanism:** Irreversibly inhibit MAO-A (degrades 5-HT, NE) and/or MAO-B (degrades DA). ↑ all monoamines.

**Selective:**
- **Selegiline** (MAO-B selective at low dose): Parkinson's (adjunct); transdermal patch for depression bypasses first-pass MAO-A gut inhibition; at high doses → non-selective
- **Phenelzine, tranylcypromine, isocarboxazid:** Non-selective, irreversible; used for treatment-resistant depression, atypical depression, panic disorder

**Drug interactions (life-threatening):**
- **Tyramine hypertensive crisis ("cheese reaction"):** Foods high in tyramine (aged cheese, wine, cured meats, soy sauce, fermented products) → normally metabolized by gut/liver MAO-A → MAOI blocks this → tyramine enters bloodstream → displaces NE from sympathetic terminals → hypertensive crisis (HA, diaphoresis, ↑↑ BP, death)
- **Serotonin syndrome:** MAOI + any serotonergic drug (SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, meperidine, tramadol, dextromethorphan) → lethal
- Washout: 2 weeks before starting SSRI after MAOI; 5 weeks (fluoxetine) before starting MAOI after SSRI

### Atypical Antidepressants

| Drug | Mechanism | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Bupropion | Weak DA + NE reuptake inhibitor (NDI); no SERT activity | No sexual dysfunction; activating; weight neutral/loss; ↓ seizure threshold (CI in eating disorders, seizure Hx); smoking cessation (Zyban); ADHD off-label |
| Mirtazapine | α2 antagonist (↑ NE/5-HT release) + 5-HT2/5-HT3 antagonist + H1 antagonist | Sedating (H1); weight gain; used in depression + insomnia/anorexia; no sexual dysfunction; ↑ appetite useful in elderly/cancer patients |
| Vortioxetine | Multimodal: SERT block + 5-HT1A partial agonist + 5-HT3/7 antagonist | Cognitive benefit claim; GI side effects; minimal sexual dysfunction |
| Trazodone | SERT block + 5-HT2 antagonist + H1 + α1 block | Primarily used as sleep aid (H1+α1 sedation); low doses; priapism (rare) |

## 2. Antipsychotics

### First Generation (Typical) — D2 Blockade

**All typical antipsychotics:** High-affinity D2 antagonists → block mesolimbic DA hyperactivity (positive symptoms) but also block mesocortical and nigrostriatal pathways.

**EPS (Extrapyramidal Symptoms) — dopamine pathway-specific:**
```
NIGROSTRIATAL blockade → EPS:
  Acute dystonia: sustained muscle contractions (hours-days of starting); Rx: diphenhydramine/benztropine IV
  Akathisia: motor restlessness, inability to sit still (most distressing); Rx: β-blockers, BZDs, mirtazapine
  Drug-induced parkinsonism: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (weeks-months); Rx: benztropine, dose reduce
  Tardive dyskinesia (TD): involuntary orofacial movements after months-years; D2 receptor upregulation
    TD Rx: VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine, deutetrabenazine); clonazepam; switch to lower-EPS drug

TUBEROINFUNDIBULAR blockade → ↑ prolactin:
  Galactorrhea, amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction, gynecomastia
```

**NMS (Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome):**
- Rare but life-threatening; all antipsychotics (typical >> atypical)
- "Lead pipe" rigidity + hyperthermia + altered consciousness + autonomic instability
- Labs: ↑ CK, leukocytosis
- Treatment: stop antipsychotic, supportive care, bromocriptine (DA agonist), dantrolene (muscle relaxant)
- Distinguish from serotonin syndrome: NMS = rigidity > clonus; slower onset; background of antipsychotic

**High-potency typicals:** Haloperidol, fluphenazine — ↑ EPS, ↓ anticholinergic/sedation; useful for acute psychosis (Haldol IM)
**Low-potency typicals:** Chlorpromazine, thioridazine — ↑ sedation, ↑ anticholinergic, ↓ EPS

### Second Generation (Atypical) — D2 + 5-HT2A Blockade

**5-HT2A antagonism:**
- Disinhibits mesocortical DA release → modest improvement in negative symptoms
- Modulates nigrostriatal pathway → ↓ EPS risk
- Fast D2 dissociation ("hit and run" kinetics for some — clozapine/quetiapine) → less sustained D2 blockade → ↓ EPS

**Metabolic adverse effects (clozapine/olanzapine worst):**
- Weight gain (H1 + 5-HT2C antagonism → ↑ appetite), dyslipidemia, insulin resistance
- Metabolic syndrome; monitor waist circumference, lipids, glucose

| Drug | EPS | Metabolic | Special |
|------|-----|-----------|---------|
| Clozapine | Very low | ↑↑↑ | Only for treatment-resistant (≥2 failed trials); agranulocytosis (1–2%) → REMS/ANC monitoring weekly; lowers seizure threshold; myocarditis (first month); hypersalivation |
| Olanzapine | Low | ↑↑↑ | Most weight gain; useful IM for acute agitation |
| Risperidone | Moderate (dose-dependent) | ↑↑ | ↑ prolactin (most among atypicals — tight D2 binding); depot formulation available |
| Quetiapine | Very low | ↑↑ | Sedating (H1); used off-label for insomnia/anxiety; cataracts (lens monitoring) |
| Ziprasidone | Low | Low | QTc prolongation (take with food); weight neutral |
| Aripiprazole | Very low | Low | D2 partial agonist + 5-HT2A antagonist + 5-HT1A partial agonist; activating; akathisia; impulse control issues |
| Lurasidone | Low | Low | Take with food (>350 cal); modest weight effect; approved in bipolar depression |

### Third Generation — Partial D2 Agonists

**Aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, cariprazine:** Partial agonists at D2/D3 → stabilize dopamine tone
- Where DA is high (mesolimbic): partial agonist acts as functional antagonist → ↓ positive symptoms
- Where DA is low (mesocortical, nigrostriatal): partial agonist increases activity → ↓ negative symptoms, ↓ EPS
- Cariprazine: preferential D3 affinity → possibly better for negative symptoms

## 3. Mood Stabilizers

### Lithium

```
Mechanism (multiple, incompletely understood):
  Mimics Na⁺ → enters cells via Na⁺ channels/transporters
  Inhibits GSK-3β (glycogen synthase kinase-3β) → neuroprotection
  Inhibits inositol monophosphatase → ↓ phosphoinositide signaling
  ↑ BDNF → neuroprotection, neurotrophic effects

Indication: bipolar I (acute mania + maintenance prevention of recurrence); also suicidality reduction
  Only mood stabilizer with proven anti-suicidal effect in bipolar

PK: renal clearance (100% unchanged); Na⁺ wasting conditions → ↑ lithium retention → toxicity
  t½ ~24h; steady state in 5 days

Therapeutic window: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L (acute mania: upper end; maintenance: lower end)
Narrow TI: monitor levels regularly

Adverse effects:
  Fine tremor (very common; ↓ with propranolol or dose reduction)
  Polyuria/polydipsia (nephrogenic DI — ADH resistance); amiloride for NDI
  Hypothyroidism (interferes with iodine uptake + thyroid hormone synthesis): check TFTs
  Weight gain, acne, worsens psoriasis
  Teratogenic: Ebstein anomaly (tricuspid valve → right heart) — relative CI in 1st trimester

Lithium toxicity (levels >1.5 mEq/L):
  Mild: coarse tremor, confusion, GI
  Moderate: ataxia, slurred speech, EEG changes
  Severe: seizures, coma, arrhythmias (levels >2.5)
  Triggers: dehydration, NSAIDs (↓ renal Li clearance), diuretics (thiazides especially), ACEi/ARBs
  Treatment: hold lithium, IVF → Na+ loading, hemodialysis if severe
```

### Valproate (Valproic Acid / Divalproex)

**Mechanisms:** ↑ GABA (↑ synthesis, ↓ catabolism), Na⁺ channel block, T-type Ca²⁺ channel block, histone deacetylase inhibition.

**Uses:** Bipolar I (acute mania + maintenance), epilepsy (broad spectrum), migraine prophylaxis.

**Adverse:**
- GI (take with food); weight gain; hair loss (telogen effluvium, transient)
- Tremor (dose-related)
- **Hepatotoxicity:** Rare but severe; mostly children <2 years on polytherapy; check LFTs
- **Polycystic ovary syndrome:** ↑ androgen levels
- **Teratogenic:** Neural tube defects (1–2% with NTD risk; highest teratogenic risk of any AED); cognitive effects; spina bifida → use only if no alternatives in reproductive-age women; supplemental folate required
- Thrombocytopenia, pancreatitis (rare)
- **Drug interactions:** Inhibits CYP2C9/UGT → ↑ lamotrigine levels (↓ lamotrigine dose when adding VPA)

### Lamotrigine

**Mechanism:** Na⁺ channel (voltage-gated) block; stabilizes neuronal membranes. Also blocks Ca²⁺ channels and reduces glutamate release.

**Uses:** Bipolar II (maintenance, especially depression phase); bipolar I maintenance; epilepsy (broad spectrum including LGS, partial, primary generalized); cannot be used for acute mania.

**Critical adverse effect — Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)/TEN:**
- Severe mucocutaneous reaction; potentially fatal
- Risk: rapid titration (must titrate slowly over 6+ weeks)
- Risk: co-administration with valproate (↑ lamotrigine levels 2× → use half dose with VPA)
- HLA-B*1502 (Asian populations) → ↑ SJS risk (also carbamazepine)
- Any rash during initiation → stop immediately

**Pregnancy:** Safest mood stabilizer in pregnancy; levels ↑ in pregnancy (↑ glucuronidation), dose may need adjustment.

## 4. Anxiolytics and Sedatives

### Benzodiazepines

```
Mechanism: GABA-A receptor (ligand-gated Cl⁻ channel)
  BZD binding site: distinct from GABA binding site (on γ-subunit)
  Positive allosteric modulator (PAM): ↑ FREQUENCY of Cl⁻ channel opening
  (Barbiturates ↑ DURATION of channel opening — more dangerous, lower TI)
  ↑ Cl⁻ influx → hyperpolarization → ↓ neuronal excitability

Clinical effects (dose-dependent):
  Anxiolytic → sedative → hypnotic → anesthesia (high dose)

Individual drugs (by duration):
  Ultra-short: triazolam (sleep induction only)
  Short: lorazepam, oxazepam, temazepam
    No active metabolites → safe in liver disease, elderly
    Oxazepam, lorazepam: directly glucuronidated (Phase II only → no CYP interaction)
  Long-acting: diazepam, chlordiazepoxide, clonazepam
    Active metabolites (desmethyldiazepam) → long duration; useful for alcohol withdrawal

Tolerance, dependence, withdrawal:
  Tolerance develops (especially anxiolytic, euphoric effects); less to anticonvulsant
  Physical dependence: withdrawal = life-threatening (like alcohol withdrawal)
    Seizures, autonomic instability, delirium within 24–72h (long-acting BZDs: later onset)
  Treat withdrawal with same class (CIWA protocol) → taper

Antidote: FLUMAZENIL (competitive antagonist at BZD site)
  Short t½ (1h) → re-sedation possible; use in monitored setting
  Contraindicated in chronic BZD users (precipitates withdrawal/seizures)

Beers Criteria: avoid in elderly (↑ fall risk, fractures, cognitive impairment, paradoxical agitation)
```

### Buspirone

**Mechanism:** 5-HT1A partial agonist (presynaptic → ↓ serotonin release; postsynaptic → partial activation). No GABAergic activity.

**Advantages:** No dependence, no tolerance, no withdrawal, no abuse potential. Safe in elderly.

**Disadvantages:** Slow onset (2–4 weeks for anxiolysis — similar to antidepressants); does NOT work acutely. If patient previously on BZD, doesn't provide cross-tolerance (start BZD taper separately).

**Indication:** GAD (generalized anxiety disorder), especially if BZD dependence concern.

### Z-Drugs (Non-BZD Hypnotics)

**Zolpidem, zaleplon, eszopiclone:** Act at GABA-A BZD site (same mechanism) but preferentially bind α1-subunit containing receptors → sedation >> anxiolytic/muscle relaxant/anticonvulsant.

Zolpidem half-life: ~2.5h; zolpidem CR: extended-release for sleep maintenance. Lower dose in women and elderly (↓ metabolism).

**Still have abuse/dependence potential (DEA Schedule IV)**; complex sleep behaviors (sleepwalking, sleep-driving) → take only when ready to sleep 7-8h.

## 5. Opioids

### Receptor Pharmacology

```
μ (MOR, OPRM1): primary analgesia receptor; euphoria; respiratory depression;
  constipation (enteric MOR); physical dependence; miosis
κ (KOR, OPRK1): spinal analgesia; dysphoria; sedation; hallucinogenic at high doses
δ (DOR, OPRD1): mood modulation; supraspinal analgesia; less respiratory depression

ALL opioid receptors: Gi-coupled → ↓ cAMP → ↓ protein kinase A → ↑ K⁺ conductance
  (hyperpolarization) + ↓ Ca²⁺ conductance → ↓ neurotransmitter release → analgesia

Full agonists: morphine, oxycodone, hydrocodone, fentanyl, hydromorphone, meperidine, methadone
Partial agonist: buprenorphine (μ; ceiling effect on respiratory depression but not full analgesia)
Mixed agonist-antagonist: butorphanol, pentazocine, nalbuphine (κ agonist, μ antagonist)
Antagonists: naloxone (IV, short t½, reversal), naltrexone (PO, long t½, OUD/AUD)
```

### Key Individual Opioids

| Drug | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Morphine | Prototype; active metabolite M6G (renally cleared → accumulates in CKD → sedation) |
| Oxycodone | PO; similar potency to morphine; OxyContin (abuse-deterrent extended release); CYP2D6 activation |
| Hydromorphone | ~5× more potent than morphine; IV or PCA use; less histamine than morphine |
| Fentanyl | 80–100× morphine; very lipophilic → rapid onset; transdermal patch (72h), IV, buccal |
| Tramadol | Weak μ-agonist + SNRI (weak 5-HT + NE reuptake inhibition); CYP2D6 activation to O-desmethyltramadol (active); lowers seizure threshold; serotonin syndrome risk with SSRIs; not interchangeable with pure opioids in opioid-tolerant patients |
| Meperidine | Avoid: normeperidine (toxic metabolite) → seizures (CKD accumulation); serotonin syndrome with MAOIs; tachycardia (antimuscarinic) |
| Methadone | Very long t½ (24–36h, variable 8–59h); NMDA antagonism; QTc prolongation; multiple drug interactions (CYP3A4/2D6/2C19); OUD MAT (clinic dispensed) |
| Buprenorphine | Partial μ agonist + κ antagonist + NOP receptor activation; ceiling on respiratory depression; high receptor affinity (displaced by naloxone only at high naloxone dose); Suboxone (+ naloxone) for OUD outpatient; injectable (Brixelle) monthly; SUBLOCADE SQ monthly |

**Opioid adverse effects:**
- **Respiratory depression:** Primary safety concern; dose-dependent; naloxone reversal
- **Constipation:** Does NOT develop tolerance (opioid-induced constipation = OIC); treat with osmotic laxatives, methylnaltrexone/naloxegol (peripherally-restricted antagonists)
- **Nausea/vomiting:** Chemoreceptor trigger zone (D2/5-HT3 activation); tolerance usually develops
- **Miosis:** Pontine constriction → miosis; no tolerance
- **Pruritus:** Histamine release (morphine > fentanyl); treat with low-dose naloxone infusion or antihistamines
- **Urinary retention:** ↑ bladder sphincter tone, ↓ detrusor
- **Tolerance and physical dependence:** Expected pharmacological adaptations
- **Hyperalgesia (OIH):** Paradoxical ↑ pain with escalating opioids in some patients

**Naloxone:** μ antagonist; IV (0.4–2mg), IM, intranasal; onset minutes; t½ ~1h (shorter than most opioids → re-narcotization risk if long-acting opioid → repeat dosing); Narcan nasal spray for home/community use.

## 6. Anticonvulsants

See 08-NEUROLOGICAL-PSYCHIATRIC.md for clinical epilepsy context. Here: mechanisms only.

```
Na⁺ CHANNEL BLOCK (use-dependent — more selective for rapidly firing neurons):
  Phenytoin / fosphenytoin: Na⁺ channel; also Ca²⁺ channel; CNS toxicity (nystagmus, ataxia, diplopia),
    CYP2C9/3A4 induction, gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, teratogenic (fetal hydantoin syndrome),
    zero-order kinetics (dose increases → disproportionate level ↑), hypertension with IV infusion
  Carbamazepine: Na⁺ channel; also ↑ GABA; used for bipolar, neuropathic pain, trigeminal neuralgia;
    induces own metabolism (autoinduction), agranulocytosis (rare), SIADH (hyponatremia),
    SJS (HLA-B*1502 screening in Asians), teratogenic (NTD)
  Oxcarbazepine: carbam analog; less enzyme induction, less SJS risk, ↑ hyponatremia
  Lamotrigine: Na⁺ channel; see mood stabilizers section
  Lacosamide: Na⁺ channel (slow inactivation state); IV/PO; PR interval prolongation

GABA ENHANCEMENT:
  Phenobarbital: GABA-A (↑ duration of Cl⁻ channel opening); broad spectrum; sedating, tolerance;
    CYP inducer; neonatal seizures; pregnancy category D (NTD, cardiac)
  Valproate: ↑ GABA + Na⁺ channel + T-type Ca²⁺; see mood stabilizers
  Vigabatrin: irreversible GABA-T inhibitor → ↑ GABA; infantile spasms, refractory CPS
    Irreversible peripheral visual field loss (20–40% patients) → limits use; REMS program
  Tiagabine: GABA reuptake inhibitor (GAT1 blocker)
  Gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin): α2δ subunit of VDCC; ↓ Ca²⁺ influx → ↓ excitatory NT release
    Not technically GABA-ergic despite name; gabapentin: neuropathic pain, restless legs, anxiety (off-label)
    Pregabalin: GAD (FDA-approved), neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia; controlled substance (V)

T-TYPE Ca²⁺ CHANNEL BLOCK:
  Ethosuximide: absence seizures ONLY; first-line for childhood absence; GI side effects

SV2A LIGAND (synaptic vesicle protein 2A):
  Levetiracetam: broad spectrum; first-line for many; minimal drug interactions; behavioral effects
    (irritability, hostility "keppra rage"); IV formulation
  Brivaracetam: similar; may have fewer behavioral effects; higher SV2A affinity

AMPA ANTAGONISM:
  Perampanel: adjunctive for partial seizures; behavioral adverse effects (aggression/hostility)

CARBONIC ANHYDRASE INHIBITOR:
  Topiramate: Na⁺ channel + GABA + AMPA + CA inhibitor; weight loss (useful side effect, used for obesity);
    cognitive slowing ("dopamax"), kidney stones, metabolic acidosis, teratogenic; migraine prophylaxis FDA
  Acetazolamide: mild anticonvulsant; altitude sickness prophylaxis
  Zonisamide: similar to topiramate; weight loss
```

|-----------|----------------|-------|

| MDD + pain | SNRI (duloxetine) | DPN, fibromyalgia, MSK indications |
| MDD + insomnia/anorexia (elderly) | Mirtazapine | Sedating, appetite-stimulating, minimal interactions |

## Common Confusion Points

**SSRI 2-4 week onset:** 5-HT autoreceptor desensitization is the key step, not just increased 5-HT. This explains why "treatment works the day you start" is incorrect and why "give it time" is clinically important.

**Serotonin syndrome vs NMS:**
SS: caused by serotonergic drug excess; rapid onset; clonus + hyperreflexia dominant; hyperthermia; treat with cyproheptadine.
NMS: caused by DA blockade (antipsychotic); slow onset (days); "lead-pipe" rigidity dominant; hyperthermia; treat with bromocriptine + dantrolene.

**BZD vs barbiturate GABA-A mechanism:**
BZD: ↑ FREQUENCY of Cl⁻ channel opening (requires GABA to be present).
Barbiturates: ↑ DURATION of Cl⁻ channel opening (can directly open channel at high doses → more dangerous respiratory depression → lower TI).

**Lithium and NSAIDs/diuretics:** NSAIDs ↓ renal prostaglandins → ↑ Li reabsorption → ↑ Li levels → toxicity. Thiazides → ↑ proximal tubule Na reabsorption (compensatory) → ↑ Li co-absorption (proximal tubule handles Li like Na) → toxicity. Monitor lithium levels if these drugs added.

**Tramadol dangers:** Often perceived as "mild opioid" but has serotonergic activity → fatal serotonin syndrome with MAOIs. Lowers seizure threshold. CYP2D6 UMs → excess active metabolite → opioid toxicity. Schedule IV in US.

**Atypical antipsychotic metabolic risk:** Clozapine = olanzapine >> quetiapine > risperidone > ziprasidone/aripiprazole. Mechanism: H1 blockade → ↑ appetite; 5-HT2C blockade → further metabolic disruption; direct effect on insulin signaling? Monitor all atypicals: weight, waist circumference, fasting glucose/lipids, BP.
