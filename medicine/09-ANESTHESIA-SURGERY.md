## Big Picture: The Anesthesia Triad

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│               ANESTHESIA: THREE COMPONENTS (TRIAD)                      │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                         │
│  UNCONSCIOUSNESS / HYPNOSIS                                             │
│   Propofol, etomidate, ketamine, volatile agents (sevoflurane, etc.)   │
│   Mechanism: GABA-A potentiation (most) or NMDA antagonism (ketamine)  │
│                                                                         │
│  ANALGESIA (pain control)                                               │
│   Opioids: fentanyl, remifentanil, morphine                            │
│   Regional: neuraxial/nerve blocks; eliminate surgical pain completely  │
│   NSAIDs + paracetamol: multimodal analgesia; reduce opioid requirement│
│                                                                         │
│  MUSCLE RELAXATION                                                      │
│   Depolarizing: succinylcholine (suxamethonium) — rapid but short-lived│
│   Non-depolarizing: rocuronium, vecuronium, cisatracurium               │
│   Reversal: neostigmine (anticholinesterase) or sugammadex (rocuronium) │
│                                                                         │
│  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│  │  GENERAL:  all three components; intubated; mechanical ventilation│  │
│  │  REGIONAL: analgesia/motor block without unconsciousness         │  │
│  │  LOCAL:    targeted nerve blockade; patient awake                │  │
│  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## 1. Induction Agents

```
PROPOFOL (2,6-diisopropylphenol):
  Mechanism: positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A (Cl- channel → hyperpolarization)
    Also: activates glycine receptors; attenuates NMDA receptor
  Formulation: lipid emulsion (intralipid) — soybean oil/egg lecithin; white milky appearance
    Supports bacterial growth → use within 12 hrs; dispose unused portions
  Pharmacokinetics: extremely lipophilic → rapid distribution to brain → fast induction
    Redistribution: effect offset by redistribution to peripheral compartments (not metabolism)
    Elimination: hepatic + extrahepatic glucuronidation; CYP2B6 minor
    Context-sensitive half-time: predictable even for prolonged infusions → ICU sedation

  Adverse:
    PAIN ON INJECTION: burning in small veins; inject into large vein or add lignocaine
    HYPOTENSION: most clinically significant → vasodilation + negative inotropy; fluid/vasopressor ready
    Apnea: brief apnea on induction → airway management essential immediately
    Propofol Infusion Syndrome (PRIS): rare; high-dose prolonged infusion → metabolic acidosis + rhabdomyolysis + cardiac failure; limit <4 mg/kg/hr for >48hrs; avoid in critically ill children
    Not an analgesic: anti-emetic effects; reduce PONV

KETAMINE:
  Mechanism: NMDA receptor antagonist (non-competitive; channels stays open but K+ blocks)
    Also: μ-opioid agonist (weak); blocks monoamine reuptake (catecholamines)
  Unique: DISSOCIATIVE anesthetic — analgesia + amnesia without full loss of consciousness
  Airway: maintains pharyngeal reflexes and airway tone (does NOT remove risk of aspiration)
  Hemodynamics: sympathomimetic → ↑ HR, BP, CO → useful in trauma/hemodynamic compromise
  Bronchodilation: useful in reactive airway disease / asthmatic intubation
  Analgesic properties: sub-anesthetic ketamine infusion for chronic pain, opioid-sparing
  NMDA role in pain: central sensitization; ketamine reduces opioid tolerance/hyperalgesia

  Adverse:
    Emergence phenomena: hallucinations, vivid dreams, delirium on emergence
      Attenuated by co-administration of benzodiazepines (midazolam) or low-dose propofol
    Tachycardia/hypertension: relative contraindication in uncontrolled hypertension, ICP elevation
    ICP: classically taught as raising ICP → now controversial; safe in TBI with controlled ventilation

ETOMIDATE:
  Mechanism: GABA-A potentiation (imidazole ring binds at distinct site from propofol)
  Hemodynamic stability: minimal effect on BP/HR → preferred in hemodynamically unstable patients
  ADRENOCORTICAL SUPPRESSION: blocks 11-β-hydroxylase → cortisol synthesis ↓ for 4-8 hrs
    Single induction dose: transient suppression (clinically significant in septic shock?)
    Controversy: avoid in adrenal insufficiency; caution in critical illness; some anesthesiologists avoid
  Myoclonus: involuntary movements on induction; does not indicate seizure
  No analgesia; anti-emetic effect less than propofol

THIOPENTAL (sodium thiopentone):
  Historical: barbiturate; first IV induction agent
  Still used: ICP management (cerebral metabolic suppression); RSI in obstetric anesthesia historically
  Tissue necrosis: intra-arterial injection → catastrophic; must confirm IV placement
  Context-sensitive accumulation: prolonged infusion → very long emergence
  Largely replaced by propofol in routine use
```

## 2. Volatile Anesthetic Agents

```
MECHANISM:
  Volatile agents: halogenated ethers; "immobilizing" via spinal cord (not brain); hypnosis from brain
  GABA-A potentiation (primary); NMDA antagonism; glycine modulation
  MAC (Minimum Alveolar Concentration): alveolar concentration preventing movement in 50% subjects to incision
    Index of potency; inversely related to oil:gas coefficient (lipid solubility)
    Additive: 0.5 MAC volatile + 0.5 MAC N2O = 1 MAC combined

MAC VALUES:
  Sevoflurane:  MAC = 2.05%;  blood:gas λ = 0.65 (low → fast on/off)
  Desflurane:   MAC = 6.0%;   blood:gas λ = 0.42 (lowest → fastest, but airway irritant)
  Isoflurane:   MAC = 1.17%;  blood:gas λ = 1.4  (slower; cheapest; still used)
  Halothane:    MAC = 0.77%;  blood:gas λ = 2.5  (historic; higher solubility → slow)
  Nitrous oxide: MAC = 104%;  blood:gas λ = 0.47 (cannot achieve MAC in air alone!)

BLOOD:GAS COEFFICIENT:
  Low coefficient → less drug dissolves in blood → faster equilibrium → fast induction/recovery
  Desflurane fastest emergence (useful for outpatient/ambulatory surgery)
  Sevoflurane: sweet smell → good for inhalational induction in children; most used worldwide

MALIGNANT HYPERTHERMIA (MH):
  Triggered by: all volatile anesthetic agents + succinylcholine
  Genetic: mutations in RYR1 (ryanodine receptor, skeletal muscle SR Ca2+ channel)
  Mechanism: uncontrolled skeletal muscle Ca2+ release → sustained contraction + metabolic crisis
  Signs: rapid temperature rise, CO2 production ↑, rigidity, metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis
    LATE sign: temperature rise; EARLY: masseter spasm, unexplained ETCO2 rise, tachycardia
  Treatment: DANTROLENE (inhibits RYR1 → stops Ca2+ release); 2.5 mg/kg IV STAT; repeat prn
    Also: stop volatile agent, 100% O2, cool patient, treat acidosis, treat hyperkalemia
  Susceptibility testing: caffeine-halothane contracture test (CHCT) on muscle biopsy
  MH-safe anesthesia: propofol + opioid + non-depolarizing NMB + avoid succinylcholine

HEPATOTOXICITY:
  Halothane hepatitis: rare but serious; cross-sensitization with other fluorinated volatiles
  Mechanism: oxidative biotransformation produces trifluoroacetyl (TFA) chloride → protein adducts → immune response
  Sevoflurane/desflurane: much lower hepatotoxic potential; inorganic fluoride (nephrotoxicity only at very high doses/low-flow anesthesia)

NITROUS OXIDE:
  MAC 104% → cannot produce full anesthesia in air (max ~70% N2O in clinical use)
  Use: adjunct; analgesic ("laughing gas" for dental); second gas effect with volatile agent
  Mechanism: NMDA antagonism (like ketamine) + opioid modulation
  Vitamin B12 oxidation: irreversible inactivation of B12-dependent methionine synthase
    Homocysteine accumulates; methionine synthesis impaired; myelopathy with prolonged exposure
    AVOID: in vitamin B12 deficiency, MTHFR polymorphism, bowel obstruction (gas expansion)
  Bone marrow: megaloblastic changes with prolonged surgical exposure; monitor in prolonged cases
  Environmental: global warming potential; anesthetic gas scavenging systems required
```

## 3. Muscle Relaxants (Neuromuscular Blocking Agents, NMBAs)

```
NEUROMUSCULAR JUNCTION:
  Motor nerve releases ACh → binds nicotinic ACh receptor (nAChR) at motor end plate
  nAChR: ligand-gated Na+/K+ channel → end plate potential → action potential → contraction
  Depolarizing NMBAs mimic ACh; non-depolarizing NMBAs competitively block nAChR

SUCCINYLCHOLINE (suxamethonium) — DEPOLARIZING:
  Structure: two ACh molecules joined; activates nAChR → initial fasciculations then paralysis
  Phase I block: sustained depolarization → receptor desensitization → flaccid paralysis
  Phase II block: prolonged exposure → receptor changes resemble non-depolarizing (variable)

  Duration: 5-10 minutes (pseudocholinesterase/plasma ChE hydrolyzes in plasma)
    Pseudocholinesterase deficiency: prolonged apnea (familial; genetic variants; dibucaine number)
  Onset: 30-60 seconds → fastest available → gold standard for RSI (rapid sequence induction)

  ADVERSE EFFECTS:
    Hyperkalemia: K+ released during fasciculations; safe normally (~0.5 mEq/L rise)
      DANGEROUS hyperkalemia (5-10 mEq/L rise) in:
        Burns (>24hr post-burn; upregulation of extrajunctional nAChRs for ~6 months)
        Crush injuries, prolonged immobilization
        Denervation injuries (spinal cord injury, stroke, UMN lesion)
        Severe muscle disease (Duchenne: rhabdomyolysis risk)
    Masseter spasm: after succinylcholine → may indicate MH susceptibility
    Bradycardia: muscarinic stimulation (especially with second dose in children)
    Increased ICP, IOP, intragastric pressure: theoretical; not proven clinically significant
    Cannot be reversed: must wait for metabolism; rocuronium+sugammadex preferred if reversal needed immediately

NON-DEPOLARIZING NMBAs:
  Rocuronium: fast onset (1.2 mg/kg = RSI dose; onset 60-90 sec); intermediate duration 30-60 min
    Benzylisoquinolinium (cisatracurium) vs aminosteroid (rocuronium, vecuronium)
    Sugammadex (encapsulates rocuronium → rapid complete reversal within minutes — preferred over neostigmine)
  Vecuronium: similar to rocuronium; hepatic elimination; intermediate duration
  Cisatracurium: Hofmann degradation (non-enzymatic at physiologic pH/temperature) → organ-independent
    Preferred in hepatic or renal failure; ICU sedation (avoid residual block)
  Pancuronium: long-acting; renal elimination; tachycardia (vagolytic)

REVERSAL:
  Neostigmine (anticholinesterase):
    Inhibits AChE → ACh accumulates → competes for nAChR → reverses non-depolarizing block
    Must give with atropine or glycopyrrolate (muscarinic block) to prevent bradycardia/secretions
    Limitation: cannot reliably reverse deep block; fails if TOF < 10%
  Sugammadex:
    Modified cyclodextrin; encapsulates rocuronium/vecuronium in plasma → removes from NMJ
    Works at ANY depth of block; 16 mg/kg reverses profound succinylcholine-like block within 3 minutes
    No muscarinic effects → no atropine needed
    Limitations: expensive; rocuronium/vecuronium only; not aminosteroidal competition — encapsulation
    Reversal of severe anaphylaxis to rocuronium: sugammadex works (fascinating mechanism)

MONITORING NEUROMUSCULAR BLOCK:
  Train-of-four (TOF): 4 supramaximal stimuli at 2 Hz; count twitches and fade
  TOF ratio: 4th/1st twitch amplitude; ≥ 0.9 = adequate recovery for extubation
  Post-tetanic count (PTC): deep block assessment; count twitches after 50 Hz tetanic stimulus
```

## 4. Regional Anesthesia

```
NEURAXIAL ANESTHESIA:
  Spinal (subarachnoid block):
    Lumbar puncture L3/4 or L4/5; needle into CSF; inject local anesthetic (LA) ± opioid
    Hyperbaric (bupivacaine + dextrose): patient position controls level spread
    Onset: fast (2-5 min); COMPLETE reliable block; fixed duration (bupivacaine ~2.5-4 hrs)
    Adverse:
      Hypotension: sympathetic block → vasodilation (fluid preload ± vasopressor)
      PDPH (Post-Dural Puncture Headache): CSF leak → traction on meninges; positional; treat: caffeine, EBP (epidural blood patch)
      High spinal: LA rises too high → respiratory muscle paralysis; immediate management required
    Indications: C-section, lower limb surgery, urological, total hip/knee replacement

  Epidural:
    Epidural space (not CSF); catheter allows titration; incrementally dose
    Onset: 15-30 min; dose for C-section: ~20 mL 0.5% bupivacaine
    Indications: labor analgesia (excellent), thoracic surgery, major abdominal, post-op pain
    Advantages vs spinal: titratable; continuous infusion possible; less hypotension
    Complications: epidural hematoma (anticoagulation risk — ASRA guidelines for timing)
      Epidural abscess, accidental dural puncture, inadequate block

  Intrathecal opioids: morphine (delayed respiratory depression up to 24 hrs), fentanyl (short)

PERIPHERAL NERVE BLOCKS:
  Ultrasound guidance: visualize nerve and needle in real time; gold standard
  Examples:
    Interscalene block: brachial plexus (C5/6/7); shoulder/upper arm surgery
      Always causes ipsilateral phrenic nerve block → 25% diaphragm paralysis; avoid in contralateral phrenic palsy or severe respiratory disease
    Femoral nerve block (or adductor canal): knee surgery; quadriceps weakness (falls risk)
    Popliteal sciatic block: foot/ankle surgery; complete foot block
    TAP (Transversus Abdominis Plane) block: abdominal wall analgesia; laparotomy, C-section, lap hernia
    PECS block, serratus block: chest wall for breast surgery/rib fractures

  ERAS integration: regional blocks → avoid opioids → earlier mobilization, shorter stay
```

## 5. Local Anesthetics

```
MECHANISM:
  Block voltage-gated Na+ channels → stabilize inactivated state → prevent action potential propagation
  Frequency-dependent (use-dependent): more active neurons block more readily (important for pain selectivity)
  Differential block: smaller myelinated Aδ (sharp pain, temp) > unmyelinated C fibres > motor (Aα) > proprioception
    Therefore: at appropriate concentration, sensory block before motor block (epidural regional)

AMIDES vs ESTERS:
  Amides: contain "-i- in the" name (lidocaine, bupivacaine, ropivacaine, levobupivacaine, prilocaine)
    Metabolized: hepatic CYP enzymes
  Esters: cocaine, procaine, chloroprocaine, tetracaine
    Metabolized: plasma pseudocholinesterase → PABA (para-aminobenzoic acid) → allergic reactions
  True LA allergy: rare; almost always to PABA from ester metabolism; amides rarely cause true allergy
    "Allergy to lidocaine": usually anxiety response or vasovagal; real allergy very uncommon

KEY AGENTS:
  Lidocaine (lignocaine): 1.5-4 hr duration; max dose 3 mg/kg (7 mg/kg with epinephrine)
    Rapid onset; IV lidocaine: antiarrhythmic (Class IB); perioperative systemic infusion analgesic
  Bupivacaine: 4-12 hr duration; more cardiac toxic → cardiotoxicity if IV bolus (binds Na+ channel at cardiac)
    Max dose 2 mg/kg (3 mg/kg with epi); levobupivacaine (S-isomer): less cardiotoxic
  Ropivacaine: 6-12 hr; less cardiotoxic than bupivacaine; slightly less potent; preferred in modern regional
  Chloroprocaine: ester; very rapid hydrolysis → very short duration; useful for spinal in ambulatory
  EMLA cream: eutectic mixture 2.5% lidocaine + 2.5% prilocaine; topical skin analgesia 45-60 min

VASOCONSTRICTORS:
  Epinephrine 1:200,000 (5 mcg/mL): added to extend duration + reduce systemic absorption
    Reduces peak plasma LA concentration by 50% → allows higher total dose
    CONTRAINDICATED: digital blocks (fingers/toes), penis, nose, ear (terminal circulation → ischemia)
    Cardiac risk: epinephrine in venous injection → arrhythmia; always aspirate before injection

LOCAL ANESTHETIC TOXICITY (LAST):
  CNS symptoms first (lower threshold): metallic taste, tinnitus, circumoral numbness, dizziness → seizures
  Cardiac (higher dose): conduction abnormalities, VF, cardiac arrest
  Treatment:
    STOP INJECTION; secure airway; control seizures (midazolam/propofol)
    INTRALIPID 20% (Lipid Emulsion Therapy): 1.5 mL/kg IV bolus → partitions LA from plasma into lipid sink
      Mechanism: "lipid sink" + direct cardiac effect; most dramatic for bupivacaine toxicity
    CPR if cardiac arrest (may need prolonged CPR until lipid works); ECMO if available
  Prevention: aspirate before injection; incremental dosing; use US guidance; have lipid rescue available
```

## 6. Perioperative Care

```
PREOPERATIVE ASSESSMENT:

ASA PHYSICAL STATUS:
  I:   Healthy; no organic disease
  II:  Mild systemic disease (controlled HTN, mild DM, BMI 30-40)
  III: Severe systemic disease (uncontrolled HTN, poorly controlled DM, NYHA III)
  IV:  Severe systemic disease, constant threat to life (NYHA IV, ESRD, ARDS)
  V:   Moribund; unlikely to survive without operation
  VI:  Brain-dead; organ donation
  E: emergency modifier (ASA IIIE, IVE)
  Mortality correlation: ASA I ~0.1%; ASA V ~50-80%

CARDIAC RISK STRATIFICATION:
  Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI/Lee Index): 6 factors; 1 per:
    High-risk surgery, ischemic heart disease, CHF, cerebrovascular disease, insulin-dependent DM, Cr >2.0
    ≥3 factors: major cardiac event risk ~10%
  ACC/AHA guidelines: stepwise approach (functional capacity, risk factors, surgical urgency)
  Perioperative beta-blockers: continue if on chronically; do NOT newly start on day of surgery

FASTING GUIDELINES (ASA 2017 — "2-4-6-8"):
  Clear liquids: 2 hours before induction
  Breast milk: 4 hours
  Non-human milk / light meal: 6 hours
  Full meal / fatty food: 8 hours
  Rationale: minimize gastric content → reduce aspiration risk
  Rapid sequence induction (RSI): modified technique for full stomach (cricoid pressure + no mask ventilation between induction and intubation)

VTE PROPHYLAXIS:
  LMWH: enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily; renal adjustment (anti-Xa monitoring)
  Mechanical: sequential compression devices (SCDs) for all patients; TED stockings
  Caprini score: risk stratify perioperative VTE; high risk → extended LMWH prophylaxis
  Timing around neuraxial: ASRA guidelines: hold LMWH 12-24 hrs before epidural; restart 4-8 hrs after needle

ERAS (ENHANCED RECOVERY AFTER SURGERY):
  Multimodal bundle: reduced fasting, carbohydrate loading, regional anesthesia, multimodal analgesia,
    early mobilization, early oral intake, PONV prophylaxis, goal-directed fluid therapy
  Impact: reduced length of stay ~30-40%; reduced complications; faster functional recovery
  Applied to: colorectal, hepato-pancreato-biliary, cardiac, orthopedic, OB/GYN, thoracic surgery
  Key elements: avoid opioids → use multimodal (paracetamol/NSAID/regional/ketamine/gabapentinoids)

PERIOPERATIVE MEDICATIONS:
  CONTINUE: antihypertensives (except ACEi/ARBs on day of surgery — refractory hypotension), beta-blockers, statins, SSRIs
  HOLD (day of surgery): ACEi/ARBs (vasodilation + renal sparing → hypotension; restart post-op)
  HOLD (before surgery): anticoagulants (bridge per procedural bleed risk)
  METFORMIN: hold day of surgery (contrast nephropathy risk if imaging; also lactic acidosis if perioperative hypoperfusion)
  INSULIN: reduce dose of basal insulin; careful glucose monitoring; tight glycemic control (140-180 mg/dL ICU target)

SURGICAL SITE INFECTION PREVENTION:
  Prophylactic antibiotics: within 60 min before incision; cefazolin 2g (3g if >120 kg) most procedures
  Re-dose if surgery >4 hrs or large blood loss (>1.5L)
  Hair removal: clippers (not shaving — micro-abrasions increase infection)
  Normothermia: warming blanket; hypothermia → impaired neutrophil function → SSI risk
  Glucose control: hyperglycemia → impaired WBC function → SSI
  Skin prep: chlorhexidine/alcohol > povidone-iodine (more persistent antimicrobial activity)
```

## 7. Decision Cheat Sheet

| Condition / Situation | Drug / Approach | Key Notes |
|----------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Induction: hemodynamic instability | Etomidate or ketamine | Etomidate: minimal BP change (watch adrenal suppression); ketamine: sympathomimetic (↑ BP/HR) |
| Induction: standard/routine | Propofol | Lipid emulsion — discard unused portion within 12 hrs (bacterial growth risk) |
| Malignant hyperthermia | Dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV STAT | Stop all volatile agents and succinylcholine; active cooling; treat hyperkalemia/acidosis |
| Succinylcholine hyperkalemia risk | Avoid in: burns (>24h), denervation (SCI/stroke), crush, prolonged immobility, severe myopathy | Risk is extra-junctional nAChR upregulation — not baseline K⁺ level; ordinary hyperkalemia is not a contraindication |
| NMB reversal (routine) | Sugammadex (rocuronium/vecuronium) or neostigmine + atropine (non-steroidal NMBAs) | Sugammadex works at any block depth; neostigmine needs moderate block; always verify reversal with quantitative TOF ≥0.9 |
| Fastest anesthetic offset (outpatient) | Desflurane (λ = 0.42) or sevoflurane (λ = 0.65) | Sevoflurane preferred in outpatient (desflurane is airway irritant, requires vaporizer heating) |
| Post-dural puncture headache | Conservative: caffeine, hydration; definitive: epidural blood patch | PDPH from CSF leak at dural puncture site; EBP seals the leak with autologous blood |
| Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) | Stop injection; seizure control (BZD); Intralipid 20% 1.5 mL/kg IV bolus | "Lipid sink" — Intralipid sequesters free LA from plasma; call for ACLS if cardiac arrest |
| Full stomach / aspiration risk | RSI: pre-oxygenate → induction agent → succinylcholine or high-dose rocuronium → intubate immediately | No bag-mask ventilation between induction and intubation; no waiting |
| LA true allergy | Use amide (vs ester) if ester allergy | True allergy almost always to PABA (ester hydrolysis product); amides lack PABA moiety |
| Hold medications pre-op | ACEi/ARBs: hold morning of surgery | Risk refractory intraoperative hypotension; restart post-op when hemodynamically stable |
| Nitrous oxide contraindication | Avoid in B12 deficiency or prolonged/repeated exposure | Oxidizes B12 → inactivates methionine synthase → homocysteinemia + neurotoxicity |
| Pre-op fasting — clear liquids | 2 hours | Standard ASA guideline; solids: 6–8 hours depending on fat content |
| ASA 3 physical status examples | Uncontrolled HTN, poorly controlled DM, NYHA III CHF, BMI > 40, active hepatitis | Severe systemic disease, compensated but limiting; anesthetic risk ↑ but not prohibitive |

## Common Confusion Points

**Ketamine maintains airway reflexes but NOT safe from aspiration:** Pharyngeal reflexes are preserved (unlike propofol), making ketamine useful for emergency intubation in the uncontrolled patient. BUT ketamine does NOT prevent aspiration — the protective reflexes are present but the patient can still aspirate. RSI protocols still apply in full-stomach situations.

**Succinylcholine is safe in most hyperkalemic patients:** Many people avoid suxamethonium reflexively in "hyperkalemia" but the risk is specific to UPREGULATED EXTRA-JUNCTIONAL nAChRs (burns >24hrs, denervation, crush). In ordinary hyperkalemia (e.g., renal failure, K+=5.5), suxamethonium adds only ~0.5 mEq/L. The contraindication is about the number of receptors, not baseline potassium.

**MAC is additive but not simply so for all combinations:** Nitrous oxide (MAC 104%) contributes ~0.5 MAC when used at 50%, and combines additively with volatile agents. But opioids reduce MAC without being inhalational agents themselves — "MAC-sparing" effect. The adjuncts (propofol, midazolam, ketamine) also reduce volatile agent requirements, enabling lower doses of each.

**Spinal vs epidural hypotension:** Both cause sympathetic block and vasodilation. Spinal is faster (minutes) and often more profound (large sympathectomy immediately). Epidural is slower and titratable — you can stop adding dose. Both require: prehydration (though "preload" evidence is limited), vasopressor availability (ephedrine for mixed α/β; phenylephrine for pure vasopressor in obstetrics), and left uterine displacement in pregnancy.

**Non-depolarizing NMBAs require adequate reversal before extubation:** Residual neuromuscular blockade (TOF ratio <0.9) is a leading cause of post-op pulmonary complications. Subjective clinical assessment (grip strength, head lift, tongue depressor) is unreliable — use quantitative TOF monitoring. Sugammadex > neostigmine for reliable complete reversal.
