# Air Masses and Fronts — Classification, Frontal Theory, Norwegian Cyclone Model

## The Big Picture

An air mass is a large body of air with horizontally uniform temperature and humidity, acquired by sitting over a source region. Fronts are the narrow boundaries between contrasting air masses. The Norwegian cyclone model (Bjerknes et al., 1918–1920) describes how extratropical cyclones develop along the polar front — still the conceptual foundation of synoptic forecasting.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              AIR MASS AND FRONTAL FRAMEWORK                       |
|                                                                   |
|  SOURCE REGIONS  →  AIR MASSES  →  FRONTS  →  CYCLONES          |
|                                                                   |
|  Arctic Ocean/     cA, cP        Cold Front   Extratropical      |
|  Siberia/Canada    (cold, dry)   Warm Front   cyclone develops   |
|                                  Stationary   along polar front  |
|  Gulf/Caribbean    mT            Occluded                        |
|  (warm, moist)                                                   |
|                                                                   |
|  Pacific           mP            Front = baroclinic zone =       |
|  (maritime polar)  (cool, moist) strong horizontal T gradient   |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Air Mass Classification

Two axes: **Source latitude** (temperature) × **Surface type** (moisture):

```
SYMBOL   NAME                    TEMPERATURE   MOISTURE   SOURCE REGION
------   ---------------------   -----------   --------   ------------------
cA       Continental Arctic      Very cold     Very dry   Arctic ice, Siberia
cP       Continental Polar       Cold          Dry        Canada, central Asia
mP       Maritime Polar          Cool          Moist      N. Pacific, N. Atlantic
mT       Maritime Tropical       Warm          Moist      Gulf of Mexico,
                                                          Caribbean, W. Pacific
cT       Continental Tropical    Hot           Very dry   Sahara, N. Mexico,
                                                          SW US (summer)
mE       Maritime Equatorial     Very warm     Very moist Equatorial oceanic
```

**Modification** — air masses change as they move. A cP air mass moving south over the unfrozen Great Lakes picks up moisture and heat → lake-effect snow. An mT air mass moving north cools → fog if cooled below dewpoint.

**North American air mass interactions:**
- Winter: cA/cP southward → clash with mT from Gulf → severe weather belt
- Summer: mT dominates eastern half; cT dominates Southwest
- cP from Pacific modified by Rockies → less extreme than continental interior

## Fronts — Boundaries Between Air Masses

A front = sloping surface separating air masses with different temperature (and usually moisture). Frontal slope is shallow: ~1:100 to 1:300 (1 km vertical rise per 100–300 km horizontal).

### Cold Front

```
COLD FRONT (vertical cross-section):
West / Northwest                              East / Southeast

    COLD AIR                 WARM AIR
    cP/cA                    mT
                            .
                           . .
    \\\\  Front       . . . . (warm air pushed
     \\\\ surface     . . . .  upward by advancing
      \\\\ (slopes)   . . .    cold air)
       \\\\          .
        \\\\ ←←←  COLD advancing
    ///  \\\\
   (cold front = cold air advancing, warm air retreating)

WEATHER AT COLD FRONT:
- Narrow squall line or broken line of convection
- Sudden wind shift (often backing then veering)
- Temperature drop, pressure rise after passage
- Cumulonimbus (Cb) clouds; heavy but brief precipitation
- Symbol on map: blue line with triangular points in direction of motion
```

### Warm Front

```
WARM FRONT (vertical cross-section):
West                                          East

                            WARM AIR
                           mT advancing
                           (overriding cold air)
              . . . . . . . ← warm air glides up
          . . . . . . . . .
      . . . . . . . . . .
    . . . . . . . . .
     COLD AIR (retreating)   ← ahead of front
         ← warm front advancing slowly

WEATHER AT WARM FRONT:
- Wide precipitation shield (300–500 km ahead of surface front)
- Sequence (from far ahead to frontal passage):
  Ci → Cs (halo) → As → Ns → drizzle/rain → passage
- Gradual temperature and dewpoint rise
- Symbol: red line with semicircular "bumps" in direction of motion
```

### Stationary and Occluded Fronts

**Stationary front** — nearly zero motion; often produces prolonged precipitation along or just north of the front. Symbol: alternating cold/warm front symbols on each side of the line.

**Occluded front** — cold front catches warm front (cold front moves faster); warm sector is "occluded" (lifted off the surface):

```
COLD OCCLUSION:          WARM OCCLUSION:
Advancing cold front     Advancing cold front
colder than retreating   warmer than retreating
cold air ahead           cold air ahead
(common in NH)           (less common)

Symbol: purple line with both triangles and semicircles
```

## Norwegian Cyclone Model — Lifecycle of an Extratropical Cyclone

Bjerknes and Solberg (1918-1922) described the complete lifecycle:

```
STAGE 1: STATIONARY FRONT
~~~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~   (stationary front; cold to north,
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~ ~~~~~~~~   warm to south; not much happening)

STAGE 2: WAVE DEVELOPS (frontal wave)
         ~~~~L~~~~
        /         \
   Cold~             ~Warm
        \           /
         ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~
(small low develops along front; wave amplitude grows)

STAGE 3: MATURE CYCLONE (open wave)
              L
         _____|_____
        /           \
  Cold  |  Warm      |  Cold
   Air  |  Sector    |  Air
  front →            ← front
        |____________|
         Cold front (south)
         Warm front (east of L)

STAGE 4: OCCLUSION (cold front catches warm front)
              L
              |
         ~ ~ ~ ~ ~
          (occluded;
           warm sector
           lifted off
           surface)

STAGE 5: DISSIPATION
         Small, weakening L
         surrounded by cold air
         (warm sector fully occluded)
```

**Typical lifecycle timeline:** 2–7 days from wave development to dissolution.

**Typical track (NH):** Cyclones develop along stationary fronts, often in "cyclogenesis regions" (lee of Rockies, Gulf coast, North Atlantic/Pacific tracks), then move northeast, intensifying, then occluding.

## Ana-Front vs Kata-Front

```
ANA-FRONT: Warm air actively rises along frontal surface
           (ascending ana-cold front, ana-warm front)
           → More vigorous precipitation and thunderstorms

KATA-FRONT: Warm air descends along frontal surface
            (subsiding warmer air)
            → Less precipitation; stratus rather than convection
```

Ana-cold fronts produce classic vigorous squall lines. Kata-cold fronts produce more stratiform precipitation with broken cloudiness.

## Dryline — The Moisture Boundary

A quasi-stationary boundary between moist Gulf air and dry continental air over the western Great Plains. Not a temperature front — it's a humidity boundary:

```
DRYLINE:
        | MOIST          | DRY
        | (mT from Gulf) | (cT/cP from SW)
        | Td 20°C        | Td -5°C
        | T 35°C         | T 38°C
        ← dryline (nearly no temperature difference!)

SIGNIFICANCE: Convergence along dryline initiates convection
              Spring severe weather in TX/OK/KS often triggered by
              dryline in afternoon as it "bulges" eastward
```

## Frontal Passage Summary

| Front Type | Before Passage | During Passage | After Passage |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| Cold | Warm, S/SW winds; pressure falling | Squall, T drops, wind shifts W/NW | Cool, clear; pressure rising; NW winds |
| Warm | Cool, SE winds; pressure falling; stratiform precip | T rises, wind shifts S/SW | Warm, humid; steady S winds; possibly foggy |
| Occluded | Various | Complex; depends on type | Generally cooler |
| Stationary | Variable | Persistent precip | Little change |

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Weather observation | Frontal/Air Mass inference |
|--------------------|---------------------------|
| Temperature drop + wind shift NW + pressure rise | Cold front just passed |
| Gradual increase in high clouds (Ci→Cs→As) + pressure falling | Warm front approaching |
| Persistent rain for 12+ hours, no temperature change | Stationary front or slow warm front |
| Very warm, moist, hazy; dewpoints 20°C+ | mT air mass |
| Clear, cold, low humidity, pressure 1030+ mb | cP air mass behind cold front |
| Humidity jump with no temperature change (Great Plains) | Dryline |

## Common Confusion Points

**Cold front temperature drop: how much?** — Varies enormously. A mild spring cold front might drop temperatures 5°C. A strong Arctic outbreak (cA air mass) can drop temperatures 20–30°C in hours. The strength depends on the temperature contrast between the two air masses.

**Warm front precipitation area** — The stratiform precipitation associated with a warm front extends 300–500 km ahead of the surface front. The rain/snow falls from the elevated warm air as it glides up the frontal slope. You can be experiencing precipitation from a warm front well before it arrives.

**Occlusion direction** — Cold fronts move faster than warm fronts because cold dense air displaces warm air more efficiently at the surface. Eventually the cold front catches the warm front → occlusion. This process also acts to weaken the cyclone (disrupts the supply of warm, moist air feeding the system).

**Fronts on surface maps vs upper levels** — Surface fronts are ground-level intersections of sloping frontal surfaces. Upper-level fronts (jet stream level, ~200–300 mb) are different features. A surface cold front may have a distinct upper-level "jet front" (strong temperature gradient aloft) that doesn't align exactly.
