# 03 — Shelter

---

```
         SHELTER
         ───────

         /\  /\  /\
        /  \/  \/  \     ← roof: keeps rain and wind out
       /____________\
      |              |   ← walls: keep cold out
      |   you here   |
      |______________|
      ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓   ← floor: keep cold ground away from body

  Cold + wet + wind = fast death.
  You can die from exposure in hours.
  Shelter is not a comfort — it is survival.
```

---

## Why shelter is urgent

Your body must stay near its normal temperature to work.

When you get cold and wet, your body works very hard to stay warm.
If it cannot keep up, you lose the ability to think clearly.
Then you lose the ability to move. Then you die.

This happens faster than most people expect — sometimes in a few hours
in cold rain, even when the temperature is not freezing.

Do not wait until you are cold to build shelter.
Build shelter first, then find water, then find food.

---

## Choosing where to build

Where you build matters as much as what you build.

```
  GOOD LOCATIONS:

  ✓  Natural windbreak — a rocky outcrop, a hill, a dense stand
     of trees. Something that blocks wind from at least one side.

  ✓  South-facing slope (if you are in the northern half of the
     world). Faces the sun. Stays warmer and drier.

  ✓  Near water, but NOT at the water's edge.
     Build above the flood line — look for debris on tree trunks
     to see how high water has reached before.

  ✓  Flat, dry ground. Test it: press your hand down. Dry? Good.

  ✓  Near materials you need — fallen branches, leaves, bark.


  BAD LOCATIONS:

  ✗  Flood plains or low areas that fill with water when it rains.

  ✗  Under dead trees or dead branches. They fall without warning.

  ✗  In the middle of an animal trail.
     (Look for worn paths, droppings, tracks before building.)

  ✗  At the very top of an exposed hill.
     Wind is strongest there. Lightning strikes there.

  ✗  At the very bottom of a valley.
     Cold air sinks and pools there. Often the coldest spot nearby.

  ✗  In a dry streambed. Flash floods come without rain overhead.
```

## Reading the weather

Learn to read the sky before you commit to a location.

```
  SKY SIGNALS:

  Red sky at night (west, at sunset)  →  likely clear tomorrow
  Red sky in the morning (east)       →  storm likely coming

  High thin clouds like torn cloth    →  change coming in 1–2 days
  (cirrus clouds)

  Towering clouds building upward     →  storm coming now or soon
  like an anvil or cauliflower        →  get under cover

  Clouds moving fast                  →  wind is building

  Smoke rising straight up            →  high pressure, settled weather
  Smoke swirling and hugging ground   →  low pressure, rain coming

  Sudden drop in wind before a storm  →  storm very close
  (the world goes quiet)
```

## The lean-to — build in about one hour, alone

The lean-to is the simplest shelter. It blocks wind and rain from
one side. Good for one or two people.

```
  WHAT IT LOOKS LIKE:

        ridge pole
     ──────────────────
    /  /  /  /  /  /  /   ← branches laid at angle
   /  /  /  /  /  /  /    ← covered with leaves/bark
  ────────────────────     (like scales, each overlapping
                            the one below)

  Open front faces AWAY from the wind.
  You sleep here, feet toward fire.
```

**How to build it:**

```
  STEP 1: Find your ridge pole.
    A straight branch, dry if possible.
    Length: about 2 arm-spans.
    Thickness: as wide as your wrist.

  STEP 2: Support it at chest height.
    Rest it on two forked branches stuck in the ground,
    or lash it between two standing trees about 2 arm-spans apart.

  STEP 3: Lean branches from the ridge pole to the ground.
    Place them close together — a hand-width apart or less.
    The angle should be steep enough to shed rain.
    (Think 45°, like the corner of a room.)

  STEP 4: Layer your roofing from the bottom up.
    Start at the bottom and work upward.
    Each layer overlaps the one below — like fish scales or roof tiles.
    Use: large leaves, bark, bracken fern, pine boughs, long grass.
    The more layers, the drier you stay.
    Arm-length of debris on top = mostly waterproof.

  STEP 5: Insulate the floor.
    This is critical. Cold ground pulls heat from your body faster
    than cold air does.
    Pile dry leaves, dry grass, or bracken fern on the ground inside.
    Minimum depth: you should sink in up to your elbow when you press.
    More is better.

  STEP 6: Optional side panels.
    Lean branches at an angle on both sides and cover with debris.
    This helps with cold nights.
```

## The debris hut — warmer, takes longer, needs help

The debris hut traps your own body heat inside a small space.
In cold weather it can keep you alive with no fire.
It requires more work and is better with two people.

```
  WHAT IT LOOKS LIKE (from the side):

   entrance
      ───→  ───────────────────────────────────┐
            ≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋≋ ≋≋≋│ ← debris arm-length thick
            ─────── ridgepole ──────────────────┘
            └─ rests on low support
```

**Key principle:** Make it just big enough for your body. No bigger.
A small space heats up faster and stays warm from your breath alone.

**How to build:**

```
  1. Find a ridge pole — a straight branch, at least your body length
     plus one arm-span.

  2. Rest one end on a low support (forked branch or rock) about
     waist-height. The other end rests on the ground.

  3. Lean branches (ribs) along both sides, close together.

  4. Pile debris over the ribs: leaves, pine needles, bracken,
     grass, bark. The pile should be arm-length thick on all sides.
     Thicker in cold weather.

  5. Pack more debris inside as your mattress.

  6. Crawl in. Pull a bundle of debris in behind you as a door plug.

  The hut will be dark and tight. That is correct.
  Your body and breath will warm it within minutes.
```

## Ground insulation — the most important thing

This cannot be said strongly enough:

**Cold ground kills faster than cold air.**

When you sleep on cold ground, it pulls heat from your body all night.
Even on a warm night, cold damp ground can cause hypothermia.

```
  MINIMUM: pile of dry leaves as deep as your elbow when you press
  BETTER: arm-length thick
  BEST: multiple layers — grass, then leaves, then more grass

  Test: press your bare hand on the ground for 30 seconds.
  If it feels cold: add more insulation.
  If it feels neutral or warm: you have enough.
```

## Signs that someone is dangerously cold

```
  EARLY (act soon — get them warm):
    Shivering uncontrollably
    Pale skin
    Stumbling

  SERIOUS (act now):
    Stopped shivering (this is worse, not better — body is failing)
    Confused or speaking strangely
    Clumsy hands, cannot do simple tasks
    Tired and wants to sleep

  EMERGENCY (life-threatening):
    Barely responsive
    Skin cold to the touch everywhere
    Very slow heartbeat

  TO WARM SOMEONE:
    Get them out of wind and rain.
    Remove wet clothing.
    Cover with dry material — leaves, dry cloth, anything.
    Skin-to-skin warmth from another person is very effective.
    Warm (not hot) liquids if they can swallow safely.
    Do not rub their limbs hard — this can cause problems.
    Let them warm slowly.
```

---

## Simple summary

```
  1. Build shelter before you are cold. Don't wait.
  2. Choose location carefully: windbreak, dry, above flood line.
  3. Read the sky: red morning = storm coming.
  4. Lean-to: fast and simple, blocks wind from one side.
  5. Debris hut: warmer, more work, traps body heat.
  6. Floor insulation is as important as the roof.
     Cold ground kills. Pile leaves until you can't feel cold below.
  7. If someone stops shivering but is still cold: emergency.
```
