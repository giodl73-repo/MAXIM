## Why fire matters

Fire does many things:

- **Warmth** — cold and wet kill fast. Fire keeps you alive.
- **Cooking** — raw meat and many plants carry things that make you sick. Cooking kills them.
- **Boiling water** — the safest way to make water safe to drink.
- **Light** — darkness is dangerous. Light means safety and work.
- **Signal** — three fires in a triangle is understood worldwide as a call for help.
- **Morale** — fire changes how you feel. Do not underestimate this.

---

## What fire needs

Fire needs three things. Remove any one and fire goes out.

```
             HEAT
              /\
             /  \
            / 🔥 \
           /______\
        FUEL       AIR

  Fuel: something that burns — wood, dry grass, bark
  Air: oxygen — fire needs airflow to breathe
  Heat: enough heat to start the chain reaction

  To start fire: add heat.
  To put fire out: remove any one side of the triangle.
```

## Making fire without tools — the bow drill

This is the most reliable way to make fire with nothing but wood and
cord. It takes practice. Do not wait until you are desperate to try it.
Practice now while you are not cold.

**What you need:**

```
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  HEARTH BOARD    flat piece of soft dry wood          │
  │                  willow · basswood · cottonwood ·     │
  │                  mullein stalk · cedar                │
  │                  (soft enough to dent with your nail) │
  │                                                       │
  │  SPINDLE         straight round stick, dry            │
  │                  slightly harder than the hearth      │
  │                  about arm-length long                │
  │                  ends carved to a dull point          │
  │                                                       │
  │  BOW             curved branch, roughly arm-length    │
  │                  with a cord tied loosely from        │
  │                  one end to the other                 │
  │                  (shoelace · plant cord · rawhide)    │
  │                                                       │
  │  HANDHOLD        flat rock or piece of hardwood       │
  │                  with a small socket for the top      │
  │                  of the spindle                       │
  │                                                       │
  │  TINDER BUNDLE   very dry, fluffy material            │
  │                  dry grass · cattail fluff ·          │
  │                  shredded bark · milkweed · moss      │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Setup:**

```
  HANDHOLD (press down)
       │
       ↓
  ══════════ spindle (spinning)
       │
  ─────────────────────  ← hearth board (on ground)
        ○  ← burn hole   \  ← notch cut to the side
                          \  ← piece of bark under notch
                              to catch the ember

  BOW: loop cord once around the spindle, draw back and forth
```

**Step by step:**

```
  1.  Cut a shallow socket in the hearth board.
      Burn in the spindle tip to char it — this helps.

  2.  Cut a notch from the socket to the edge of the board.
      The notch should be about 1/8 of the full circle.
      (Like a pie slice, but small.)

  3.  Place a small piece of bark under the notch.
      This is where the ember will fall.

  4.  Prepare your tinder bundle — a loose bird-nest shape.

  5.  Loop the bowstring once around the spindle.
      The spindle should sit between the string and the bow.

  6.  Put the spindle in the socket.
      Press down firmly with the handhold.
      Kneel with one foot on the hearth board.

  7.  Draw the bow back and forth in smooth, long strokes.
      Not too fast — you need even pressure and rhythm.
      The spindle spins and creates friction.

  8.  After 20–40 strokes you will see smoke.
      Keep going until there is steady smoke from the notch.

  9.  Stop. Gently lift the hearth board off the bark.
      You should see a small black ember on the bark.

  10. Let it sit a moment — do not rush.
      Tap the board gently to dislodge any last material.

  11. Slide the ember into the center of your tinder bundle.
      Fold the bundle around it loosely — like a nest around an egg.

  12. Hold the bundle up and blow gently, steadily into it.
      Aim your breath at the ember. Not too hard.
      Keep blowing until it smokes, then flames.

  13. Place the burning bundle under your kindling.
```

If nothing happens: the wood is too wet, the cord is too loose, or
the pressure is not enough. Keep trying. This takes practice.

## Building the fire

```
  START WITH THE SMALLEST MATERIAL FIRST.

  TINDER            dry grass, fluff, fine bark — thumbnail size
     ↓
  KINDLING          dry twigs, thin sticks — pencil-thin
     ↓
  SMALL FUEL        finger-thick dry sticks
     ↓
  LARGER FUEL       wrist-thick logs

  Never add large fuel to a small flame. It smothers it.
  Build up slowly.
```

**The teepee structure** — best for starting:

```
         /\
        /  \
       / 🔥 \       lean sticks in a cone around the tinder
      /______\      leaves space for air underneath
                    easy to light, burns quickly
```

**The log cabin structure** — burns longer, makes good coals:

```
  ═══════════
  ║  tinder ║     stack logs in alternating layers
  ═══════════     like the walls of a small house
                  fire burns down into the coals
```

## Keeping fire alive

Once you have fire, protect it.

```
  ○ Feed it before it goes low — do not let it burn down to nothing.
  ○ Keep larger fuel nearby and dry.
  ○ Wind kills fire — shield it with your body or rocks.
  ○ Rain kills fire — cover it or find shelter for it.

  TO BANK FIRE FOR THE NIGHT (keep coals until morning):
    Push all embers together in a pile.
    Cover completely with ash.
    In the morning: blow ash away, feed small tinder onto the coals.
    Often the coals survive 6–8 hours this way.
```

## Signaling for rescue

If you want to be found:

```
  THREE FIRES IN A TRIANGLE = international distress signal

          🔥

        🔥   🔥

  Space them about 50 paces apart if you can.
  Use green leaves or wet wood on a fire to make white smoke.
  White smoke is visible in daylight for many kilometers.
  Three of anything (three whistle blasts, three shots, three flashes)
  means the same thing in many traditions: HELP.
```

## Fire safety

Fire that escapes becomes your enemy.

```
  BEFORE LIGHTING:
    Clear dead leaves and dry material from 2 arm-spans around the fire.
    Have water or a pile of dirt nearby to smother it.
    Never build fire against a tree — the roots can catch.
    Never build fire in a cave — smoke has nowhere to go.

  NEVER BURN:
    Plastic or synthetic materials (toxic fumes, can be fatal)
    Poison ivy, poison oak, poison sumac — the smoke is toxic
    Green or very wet wood — mostly smoke, little heat, wastes effort
    Treated or painted wood — toxic chemicals released

  LEAVING A FIRE:
    Drown it with water, or smother with dirt.
    Stir the ash and feel it with your hand.
    If it is still warm: add more water. Stir again.
    Cold ash = safe to leave.
    Hot ash = fire is not out.
```

## Simple summary

```
  1. Fire needs fuel + air + heat. All three, always.
  2. Bow drill is the most reliable no-tool method.
     It takes practice — practice before you need it.
  3. Build small to large: tinder, kindling, fuel.
  4. Protect it from wind and rain.
  5. Bank it with ash to keep coals overnight.
  6. Three fires in a triangle = ask for help.
  7. Never leave a fire unquenched.
  8. Never burn plastic, treated wood, or toxic plants.
```
