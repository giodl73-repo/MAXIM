## Before you touch anyone

**Wash your hands first.**

Dirty hands carry invisible things that can turn a small wound into a
fatal infection. This is the most important rule in this chapter.

```
  CLEAN HANDS:
    Wash with soap and water if you have it.
    If not: wash with plain water for as long as it takes to count to 30.
    If water is limited: use a small amount, rub thoroughly, rinse.
    Use boiled-then-cooled water to wash a wound.
```

## Wounds

Most wounds can be managed if you treat them correctly and early.

### Stop the bleeding

```
  1. Press firmly on the wound with a clean cloth.
     Do not peek. Do not remove the cloth for 10–15 minutes.
     If blood soaks through: add more cloth on top. Do not remove.

  2. Elevate the wounded part above the level of the heart.
     If it is an arm or leg, raise it.

  3. Hold pressure until bleeding stops.
     Most wounds stop within 10–15 minutes of firm pressure.

  4. If an arm or leg is bleeding severely and pressure is not enough:
     Tourniquet — tie a strip of cloth tightly around the limb
     ABOVE the wound (between the wound and the heart).
     Tighten until the bleeding stops.
     Write the time you applied it somewhere visible.
     A tourniquet saves lives but cuts off blood. Loosen after 2 hours.
```

### Clean the wound

```
  1. Get boiled water. Let it cool until it is warm but not burning.

  2. Pour or drip the water into the wound to flush it out.
     This is the most important step. Use a lot of water.
     Flush until you can see no dirt or debris.

  3. Remove any visible debris gently with clean fingers or cloth.
     Do not dig — only remove what is visible.

  4. If you have honey: apply a thin layer. Honey is antimicrobial.
     If you have garlic: crush a clove and apply the juice.
     If you have clean cloth moistened with salt water: apply.
```

### Close and cover

```
  Small wounds (can pull edges together): close with strips of
  clean cloth pulled across the wound, not along it.

          ─────                 ─────
  wound: ───────  strips: ─|─ ─|─ ─|─   hold edges together
          ─────

  Do NOT close: wounds that are very dirty, very deep, or already
  showing signs of infection. Let those drain.

  Cover with a clean dry cloth. Hold in place with strips of cloth.
  Change the dressing every day.
```

### Watch for infection

Check the wound every day.

```
  SIGNS OF INFECTION — act quickly:

  ! Red spreading away from the wound edges
    (not just at the wound, but spreading outward in the skin)

  ! Wound feels hot to the touch

  ! Swelling and pain increasing each day instead of decreasing

  ! Pus: thick yellow, green, or cloudy liquid

  ! The person develops a fever

  ! Red streaks spreading up the limb from the wound
    → THIS IS DANGEROUS. The infection is entering the blood.
    → Lie the person down. Do not let them walk on it.
    → Open the wound to let it drain. Apply honey.
    → Seek any other knowledge or help immediately.
```

## Fever

Fever is the body fighting infection. It is the body working, not broken.
But very high fever can cause brain damage and death.

```
  TEST FOR FEVER:
    Place the back of your hand on the person's forehead.
    Then place it on your own forehead.
    If they feel clearly hotter than you: they have fever.
    (Normal body temperature: like a warm but not hot cup of tea.)
```

### Managing fever

```
  1. Cool the person.
     Wet cloths on the forehead, armpits, and groin.
     These are where blood vessels run close to the skin.
     Change cloths when they warm up.

  2. Give water. Small sips, frequently.
     Fever uses water fast. Dehydration makes fever dangerous.

  3. Willow bark tea — natural aspirin.
     (See: Plant medicines below.)

  4. Rest in the shade or a cool space.

  5. If the person shivers: they are trying to raise their temperature.
     Cover them briefly, then cool again when shivering stops.
```

### When fever is dangerous

```
  GET HELP OR ACT URGENTLY IF:

  → Temperature feels much hotter than a warm cup of tea — very high
  → Seizure (shaking, loss of consciousness)
  → Confusion — person does not know where they are or who you are
  → Cannot be woken from sleep
  → Stiff neck AND fever AND headache together
    (possible meningitis — very serious)
  → Fever lasting more than 3 days without improvement
```

## Broken bones

```
  HOW TO TELL:
    Heard or felt a snap
    Cannot move that part normally
    Severe pain when touched
    Swelling and bruising
    Looks bent in the wrong direction

  WHAT NOT TO DO:
    Do not try to straighten a badly bent break.
    Do not press on the broken area.
    Do not let the person use the broken part.
```

### Splinting

A splint holds the bone still so it can begin to heal.

```
  YOU NEED:
    A straight, rigid stick — longer than the bone
    Clean cloth strips for padding and binding

  HOW TO SPLINT:
  1. Pad the area with cloth first.
     No padding directly on bone = pressure sores later.

  2. Place the stick alongside the limb.
     The splint must extend past the joint ABOVE and BELOW the break.
     (If the bone in the lower arm is broken: splint from elbow to hand.)

  3. Bind with cloth strips. Firm but not tight.
     Test: slide one finger under the binding. If you can't: too tight.
     Too tight cuts circulation.

  4. Check circulation every hour:
     Can the person feel their fingers or toes below the splint?
     Are the fingers or toes warm?
     Can they wiggle them?
     If no: loosen the binding immediately.

  5. Keep the limb elevated — above heart level if possible.
     This reduces swelling.
```

## Plant medicines

Nature provides medicines in almost every landscape.
Learn these plants. They work.

---

**Willow bark** — pain and fever

```
  LOOKS LIKE:  Narrow leaves, flexible branches, often near water.
               Many species, all work similarly.
  HOW TO USE:  Tear or cut bark from young branches.
               Simmer 2 handfuls in 2 cups of water for 15 minutes.
               Let cool. Drink up to 2–3 cups per day.
  WORKS FOR:   Pain (headache, joint pain, wound pain), fever.
  NOTE:        Contains salicin — the original aspirin.
               Do not give to children with fever (same caution as aspirin).
```

**Honey** — wounds and infection

```
  WORKS BECAUSE: Draws water out of bacteria, killing them.
                 Almost never spoils. Lasts indefinitely.
  HOW TO USE:    Apply directly to a clean wound before covering.
                 Change every day.
  ALSO:          A spoon of honey in warm water soothes sore throat.
```

**Garlic** — infection

```
  HOW TO USE:  Crush a fresh clove. Apply the juice to a wound.
               Eat 2–3 raw cloves per day for internal infection
               (severe stomach or respiratory illness).
  NOTE:        Smells strong. Burns slightly on wounds. Works anyway.
```

**Charcoal** — poisoning

```
  HOW TO USE:  Grind charcoal from fire into powder (not ash — the
               black material, not the grey powder).
               Mix 2–3 tablespoons into a cup of water.
               Drink. Follow with lots of plain water.
  WORKS FOR:   Many types of poisoning and food sickness.
               Binds toxins in the gut before they are absorbed.
  NOTE:        Use when someone may have eaten something poisonous.
               Also causes vomiting — that is acceptable.
```

**Plantain** *(common lawn weed with parallel veins in leaves)*

```
  LOOKS LIKE:   Low rosette of oval leaves with strong parallel ribs.
                Found almost everywhere in disturbed ground, lawns.
  HOW TO USE:   Chew a leaf and apply the paste to:
                insect stings, minor cuts, splinters, rashes.
                Reduces swelling and draws out poisons.
  ALSO:         Boil leaves in water and drink for cough.
```

**Ginger** — nausea and stomach pain

```
  HOW TO USE:  Slice fresh root. Boil in water. Drink.
               Or chew small pieces of raw root.
  WORKS FOR:   Nausea, vomiting, stomach cramps, motion sickness.
```

**Mint** — stomach and airways

```
  HOW TO USE:  Crush leaves. Smell (opens airways).
               Boil in water and drink (stomach cramps, nausea).
  ALSO:        Strong mint rubbed on skin repels some insects.
```

## Childbirth

Childbirth is normal. It has happened in difficult circumstances
throughout human history. Most births go smoothly.

```
  BEFORE THE BIRTH:
    Wash hands and keep them clean throughout.
    Prepare clean cloths — as many as possible.
    Prepare warm water.
    Prepare something to tie and cut the cord:
      — Two strips of clean cloth, long enough to tie firmly
      — A clean sharp blade

  DURING LABOR:
    The mother should move, walk, or change position as feels right.
    Labor often takes many hours. This is normal.
    The mother should drink water.
    Encourage and stay calm.

  WHEN THE BABY IS COMING:
    The mother can be on hands and knees, or semi-reclined.
    Do not pull on the baby. Let the body do the work.
    Guide the head gently if it comes quickly — don't grab.
    If the cord is around the neck: loop it over the head gently.

  IMMEDIATELY AFTER BIRTH:
    Clear the baby's mouth and nose if there is fluid.
    The baby should cry or breathe within a minute.
    If not: rub the back firmly, blow small gentle puffs into mouth+nose.
    Wrap the baby in dry cloth immediately. Warmth is critical.
    Place the baby on the mother's chest — skin to skin.

  THE CORD:
    Wait until the cord stops pulsing before cutting (it will stop
    within 5–10 minutes). The cord carries blood to the baby.
    Tie the cord tightly with a strip of clean cloth:
      — one tie 2 hand-widths from the baby's belly
      — one tie 3 hand-widths from the baby's belly
    Cut between the two ties with a clean blade.

  THE PLACENTA:
    Delivers 10–30 minutes after the baby.
    Do not pull. Let it come on its own.
    If the mother is bleeding heavily after delivery:
      — Firmly massage the lower belly in a circular motion
      — Have the baby nurse immediately (this causes uterus to contract)
      — Bleeding should reduce within minutes
    Heavy ongoing bleeding is serious. Keep massaging.
```

## Mental health

Fear, grief, anger, despair, and numbness are normal responses to
disaster, loss, and hardship.

They are not weakness. They are the mind processing what happened.

```
  WHAT HELPS:

  ○ Name it. "I am afraid." "I am grieving." "I am exhausted."
    Naming a feeling takes some of its power.

  ○ Find tasks. Small, concrete actions settle the mind.
    Make the fire. Build the shelter. Collect water.
    The doing-of-things helps when the thinking-of-things overwhelms.

  ○ Sleep. Sleep is medicine.
    A person who cannot sleep for days is in real danger.
    A safe, warm, sheltered place makes sleep possible.

  ○ Find people. Isolation is its own kind of slow death.
    Even one other person changes everything.

  ○ Allow grief its time. You do not have to be strong right now.
    You have to be alive. Grief and survival can coexist.

  ○ Plan one day at a time. The distance of months or years is
    overwhelming. The distance of tomorrow is manageable.
```

## Simple summary

```
  1. Wash hands before touching any wound.
  2. Stop bleeding: press hard for 15 minutes without peeking.
  3. Clean wounds: flood with boiled water, flush out debris.
  4. Watch for infection: spreading red, warmth, pus, fever.
  5. Red streaks up a limb = emergency. Open, drain, act.
  6. Fever: cool the body, hydrate, willow bark tea.
  7. Broken bones: splint past the joint above and below.
     Check that fingers and toes can feel and move.
  8. Learn: willow (pain/fever) · honey (wounds) · garlic (infection)
             charcoal (poisoning) · plantain (stings/cuts)
  9. Childbirth: clean hands, warmth for the baby, do not pull.
  10. Fear and grief are real. Name them. Find tasks. Find people.
```
