# 10 — Copying These Books

---

```
         COPYING
         ───────

  These books will not last forever.
  Paper decays. Ink fades. Flood, fire, and time destroy.

  The most important thing you can do with these books
  is make more copies of them.

  Every copy made is a backup.
  Every person who has read them is a living copy.

  This chapter gives you the paths — from the simplest
  (pen and hand) to the fastest (photography and print).

  Start with what you have. Work toward what you can build.
```

## The hierarchy of copying

Some methods are slow but need nothing. Some are fast but need tools.

```
  SPEED  ───────────────────────────────────────→  FAST
  NEED   ────────────────────────────────────────→  MORE TOOLS

  1. Hand copy            slow · needs pen + paper only
  2. Rubbing              medium · needs paper + charcoal
  3. Woodblock printing   faster · needs carved blocks + ink
  4. Hectograph           fast · needs gelatin + dye ink
  5. Photography          fastest · needs chemistry + darkroom

  All of these can be done without modern industry.
  Work your way up the list as your community grows.
```

## Step 1 — Making writing materials

Before you can copy anything, you need something to write on and
something to write with.

---

### Making paper

Paper can be made from almost any plant fiber.

**Best source plants:** stinging nettle, cattail leaves, flax, hemp,
iris leaves, dried grass, bark (inner bark of mulberry, elm, cedar).

```
  THE PROCESS:

  1. PREPARE FIBERS:
     Soak the plant material in water for 2–3 days.
     Pound it with a smooth stone until completely shredded into pulp.
     The goal: a wet mass of fibers too small to see individually.

  2. MAKE A MOLD:
     A flat wooden frame with woven material stretched across it.
     Stretched cloth, woven split branches, or reeds work.
     The mesh must be fine enough to catch fibers, loose enough to drain.

  3. FORM THE SHEET:
     Suspend the pulp in a large container of water.
     Stir until evenly distributed.
     Dip the mold flat into the water, then lift it up through the pulp.
     A thin mat of fibers will settle on the mold surface.
     Lift slowly and evenly.

  4. DRY:
     Set the mold in sun and wind to drain and dry.
     When damp but no longer wet: transfer to a flat surface.
     Press flat with a heavy stone. Let dry completely.
     Peel from mold.

  5. SIZE (optional — makes ink-resistant paper):
     Brush the dry sheet with diluted starch water (boil grain or root,
     strain, use the liquid).
     Dry again.

  RESULT: rough paper. Acceptable for writing. Lasts decades if kept dry.
  Practice makes better paper. First sheets will be uneven. Keep going.
```

**Also usable as writing surfaces:**
- Animal skin (vellum): cleaned, scraped thin, stretched flat while drying
- Birch bark: inner white surface takes ink readily
- Clay tablets: impress marks while wet, dry in sun or fire
- Prepared smooth wood: sand flat, seal with oil or varnish, write with ink

### Making ink

**Soot ink (most reliable, lasts centuries):**
```
  1. Collect soot from the inside of a pot or lamp chimney.
     Or hold a plate over a candle flame and collect the deposit.
     This soot is carbon black — the same pigment used in
     printing for thousands of years.

  2. Mix soot with a binder:
     Plant gum (gum arabic if available) dissolved in water, OR
     Boiled animal bones or skin (small amount of gelatin water), OR
     Diluted tree resin.
     Mix: about 1 part gum solution to 2 parts soot.
     The ink should be thick enough to flow without spreading.

  3. Add a little water if too thick. Add more binder if too runny.
  4. Bottle in a sealed clay vessel.

  RESULT: black ink. Writes clearly. Permanent when dry.
```

**Oak gall ink (iron gall — used in Europe for 1400 years):**
```
  1. Collect oak galls (round growth on oak trees — wasp eggs inside).
     Crush 10 galls. Boil in water for 30 minutes.
     Strain and keep the dark liquid.

  2. Dissolve iron (rusted iron filings, or iron nail in vinegar
     for several days — the vinegar turns dark green = iron solution).

  3. Mix the oak gall liquid with the iron solution.
     Result: dark greenish-black ink that darkens as it dries.
     Very permanent — survives centuries.

  4. Add a little gum for flow.
```

### Making a pen

```
  QUILL (from a large bird feather):
  1. Take a primary flight feather from a goose, turkey, or large bird.
  2. Scrape off the barbs from the lower end of the shaft.
  3. Harden the quill: soak in water, then bury in hot sand for
     an hour (or heat gently), or soak in acid (weak vinegar).
  4. Cut the tip at an angle with a sharp blade.
  5. Cut a small slit up the center of the nib — this controls ink flow.
  6. The nib can be recut many times as it wears.

  REED PEN (simpler):
  Cut a hollow reed at an angle at one end.
  Cut a small slit. Works immediately.

  BRUSH:
  Bind a bundle of fine animal hairs or plant fibers to a stick.
  Works for painting ink onto surfaces.
```

## Step 2 — Hand copying

The oldest and most reliable method. No tools beyond pen and paper.
Anyone who can write can copy a page.

```
  A person writing carefully can copy about 1 page per hour.
  One book of 300 pages = 300 hours.
  10 people each copying 30 pages = one copy in 30 hours of work.
  Divide the work among many people.

  RULES FOR COPYING:
  ○ Copy exactly. Do not correct or improve the original.
    If you think something is wrong: mark it in the margin.
    Future readers can judge. Your "improvement" might be an error.

  ○ Copy in bright light. Work slowly. Check each line.

  ○ Mark the source: "Copied from [original title], [year]."

  ○ Have two people check each copied section against the original.
    Errors compound: a copy of a copy of a copy drifts from the truth.

  ○ Copy the most important chapters first:
    Read This First (this book) → water, fire, food, healing →
    the overview files of each section → then the full volumes.
```

## Step 3 — Rubbing and transfer

A faster method for short texts, diagrams, and important pages.

**Rubbing:**
```
  Place thin paper over a page with raised text or an incised surface.
  (You can carve the text in reverse into a flat piece of hardwood first.)
  Rub gently with charcoal, chalk, or soft colored stone.
  The marks transfer the pattern to the paper.

  Useful for:
  — Copying diagram pages exactly
  — Making copies of carved stone inscriptions
  — Quick rough copies when precision matters less
```

**Transfer pressing:**
```
  Brush a page with a thin coat of oil (linseed, nut oil).
  Press a clean sheet firmly against it.
  The oil transfers the carbon ink lightly to the new sheet.
  Only works once before the original is too faint.
  Use only on pages you are ready to sacrifice OR make a wet copy
  (dampen the blank sheet, press to a freshly inked page, lift).
  Results are reversed — good for making a printing plate.
```

## Step 4 — Woodblock printing

Described in chapter 09. With woodblocks, one carved block can print
hundreds of copies of the same page.

```
  FOR COPYING BOOKS:
  Carve each page as a mirror image into hardwood.
  One block per page.
  Apply ink. Press paper. Lift. Repeat.

  SPEED: 1 carved block = 200–500 prints before significant wear.
         Carving 1 page: 2–10 hours depending on complexity.
         Printing 1 page: 2–3 minutes per copy.

  For a 300-page book:
  Carving: 300 pages × 5 hours = 1500 hours total carving.
  But once carved: 100 copies × 300 pages = 30,000 prints.
  And carving can be shared among many people.

  A library of carved blocks can print copies indefinitely.
  Store the blocks flat, dry, and wrapped. They last for generations.
```

## Step 5 — The hectograph (gelatin duplicator)

The hectograph is a forgotten technology that was used in offices and
schools in the early 1900s. It requires only gelatin and dye ink.
One master copy makes 20–50 duplicates in minutes.

```
  MATERIALS:
    Gelatin (from boiling animal bones, hooves, or skin for many hours —
             or from seaweed — the thick sticky result is gelatin)
    Glycerin (from the byproduct of soap-making — see below)
    Aniline dye or strong dark plant dye (concentrated oak gall ink,
    walnut hull ink, elderberry — any very dark, strong dye)

  MAKE THE GELATIN PAD:
  1. Dissolve gelatin in hot water (1 part gelatin : 5 parts water).
  2. Add equal volume of glycerin. Mix thoroughly.
  3. Pour into a flat baking dish or wooden frame.
     Depth: about 1 thumb-width.
  4. Let cool and set. The pad is rubbery and slightly sticky on top.

  MAKE THE MASTER COPY:
  1. Write or draw on paper using the strong dye ink.
     Write clearly and darkly — the dye transfers.

  2. Press the written side of the master face-down onto the gelatin pad.
     Press gently and evenly. Wait 2 minutes.
  3. Peel the master off. The dye image is now on the gel surface.

  PRINT COPIES:
  1. Lay a clean sheet of paper onto the gel surface.
     Smooth it gently. Wait 30 seconds.
  2. Peel off. The image transfers in mirror — it reads correctly from
     the front of the paper.
  3. Repeat: each sheet takes a little dye from the gelatin.
     First copies are darkest. After 30–50 copies the image fades.

  RESET:
  Warm the gelatin surface gently (set in sun or near fire).
  The image dissolves. Let reset. Ready for a new master.

  GLYCERIN FROM SOAP-MAKING:
  When you make soap (fat + wood ash lye, boiled and cured),
  glycerin is a natural byproduct — it separates during cooling.
  Skim it off the top. Filter through cloth. Pale yellow liquid.
```

## Step 6 — Photography

Photography captures an exact image of a page in seconds. A photograph
of a book page is as readable as the original.

This path requires chemistry, but the chemistry can be built from
materials found in the natural world. It is the fastest path to making
accurate copies at scale.

---

### First: the camera obscura (no chemistry needed)

Before making photographic copies, understand how a camera works.

```
  THE PINHOLE CAMERA (camera obscura):

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  dark box, any size                                 │
  │                                                     │
  │  ·  ← pinhole (1mm or less) in one end              │
  │                                         ┌────────┐  │
  │  light enters →  (image forms here) →  │ image  │  │
  │                                         │ screen │  │
  │                                         └────────┘  │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  HOW IT WORKS:
  Light from the outside scene passes through the tiny hole.
  Each point of light travels in a straight line.
  On the opposite wall, the scene forms as a real image —
  upside-down and reversed.

  TO USE IT:
  Build a box about the size of a book.
  Punch a very small, round hole in one side.
  On the opposite inner wall: place your light-sensitive material.
  Point the pinhole at the page you want to photograph.
  Open the hole: light enters. Close: exposure ends.

  A larger hole = more light but blurry image.
  A smaller hole = sharp image but long exposure needed.
  Best pinhole: about the size of a pin point.
```

### Cyanotype — the blueprint process

The cyanotype process uses iron chemistry instead of silver.
It is simpler and safer than silver photography. It produces
blue-and-white prints on paper or cloth.

```
  WHAT YOU NEED:
    Ferric ammonium citrate  (iron + ammonia + citric acid)
    Potassium ferricyanide   (potassium + iron compound)
    Clean paper or cloth
    Sunlight or UV light source

  MAKING THE CHEMISTRY:
  Ferric ammonium citrate:
    Citric acid = juice of lemons or sour fruits
    Dissolve iron filings or iron powder in diluted citric acid.
    Add ammonia (from aged urine, or wood ash in water).
    The result: a pale yellow-green solution.

  Potassium ferricyanide:
    This is more complex without industrial chemistry.
    Alternative: ferric chloride (iron dissolved in hydrochloric acid,
    which can be made from salt + sulfuric acid, or sourced from
    volcanic areas, or found as a mineral).

  If you cannot make these: skip to the silver process below,
  or focus on woodblock printing for book reproduction.

  THE CYANOTYPE PROCESS:
  1. Mix equal parts of the two solutions in dim light (not bright sun).
  2. Coat paper or cloth evenly with the mixture.
  3. Let dry in a dark place.
  4. Place the page you want to copy flat on the coated paper.
     It must be in direct contact — no gap.
     (The original page must be somewhat transparent, or use a glass
     plate copy as a negative.)
  5. Expose to sunlight: 5–20 minutes depending on sun strength.
     The coated area not under the original turns blue-green.
     The area under the text stays pale.
  6. Wash in plain water. The image fixes — it will not fade in light.

  RESULT: blue paper with white text — a readable copy.
           Very stable: cyanotypes from the 1840s still exist.
```

### Silver photography — the most complete path

Silver photography captures the finest detail and produces the most
readable copies of pages. It was the first practical photography (1839).

```
  WHAT YOU NEED:
    Silver (from coins, silver ore, melted jewelry, or tableware)
    Nitric acid (complex — see below)
    Table salt (sodium chloride) or potassium iodide
    Clean paper
    A darkroom (windowless room or tent)

  MAKING SILVER NITRATE (the key ingredient):
    Dissolve silver metal in nitric acid.
    Silver + nitric acid → silver nitrate (in water solution)

    MAKING NITRIC ACID WITHOUT INDUSTRY:
    From potassium nitrate (saltpeter) + sulfuric acid.
    Saltpeter forms naturally in soil rich in animal waste and
    dried manure, especially in sheltered dry areas (soil + urine + air + time).
    Scrape the white efflorescence from old stable walls or aged
    compost heaps. Dissolve in water, filter, evaporate = saltpeter.
    Sulfuric acid: from pyrite (iron sulfide, "fool's gold") heated in air,
    or from volcanic sulfur deposits.
    This is difficult but has been done by people with no industry for
    centuries — gunpowder required saltpeter before firearms did.

  SALTED PAPER PROCESS (simplest silver photography):
  1. Brush clean paper with strong salt water. Dry.
  2. In dim red light: brush the salted side with silver nitrate solution.
     (Salt + silver nitrate → silver chloride forms in the paper.)
  3. You now have light-sensitive paper. Keep it in the dark.
  4. Place the original page flat on the sensitive paper.
     Expose to sunlight: the exposed areas turn dark brown/black.
     The area under the text stays pale (the text protected it).
  5. Wash in water.
  6. FIX (make permanent): wash in strong salt water or thiosulfate.
     (Thiosulfate = sodium hyposulfite — forms from certain mineral
     springs or can be made from sulfur compounds.)
     Without fixing: image continues to darken in light.
     With fixing: image is stable for centuries.

  RESULT: brown-and-white image of the page. Readable.
           Each print takes about 5 minutes.
           One person can produce 50–100 page copies per day.
```

### The darkroom

```
  A darkroom is any space with NO white light.
  Red light is acceptable (silver chemistry is not sensitive to red).
  A tent, a cave, a room with all gaps sealed will work.

  WORKING IN THE DARKROOM:
  Use the faint red light of a fire through a red cloth,
  OR work entirely by touch (after practice, this is possible).

  Keep all light-sensitive materials wrapped in dark cloth
  until you are inside the darkroom.

  VENTILATION: chemistry produces fumes. Allow some airflow
  without admitting white light. A small cloth-covered vent works.
```

## Storing copies to last

A copy only survives if it is stored properly.

```
  PAPER:
  The enemy of paper: moisture, light, and acid.
  Store in a dry, dark, cool place.
  Wrap in clean cloth, then seal in clay or wood containers.
  Paper kept dry can last centuries.

  INK:
  Oil-based and carbon inks are most permanent.
  Avoid berry juice and other fruit-based inks — they fade.
  Use iron gall ink or soot/carbon ink for anything intended to last.

  STORAGE RULES:
  ○ Dark — no light, especially no sunlight
  ○ Dry — away from ground, away from any water source
  ○ Elevated — not directly on soil or stone (moisture wicks up)
  ○ Sealed — cloth-wrapped inside a sealed container
  ○ Duplicated — the best protection is multiple copies in different places

  THE DISTRIBUTION RULE:
  Never keep all copies in the same place.
  A fire, a flood, or bad luck can destroy a single location.
  Make at least three copies and store them in three different places.
  This is how knowledge survived for thousands of years.
```
