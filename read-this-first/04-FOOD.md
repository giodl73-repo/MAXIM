## The timeline

Know what kills in what order:

```
  Hours:     Exposure (cold + wet + wind)
  Days:      Thirst (3 days without water)
  Weeks:     Hunger (about 3 weeks without food)

  Do not risk your life searching for food before you have water
  and shelter secured.
  Calm hunger is manageable. Wet and cold can kill tonight.
```

---

## A rule about eating plants you do not know

**Never eat a plant you do not recognize without testing it first.**

Many plants that look edible are poisonous. Some can kill quickly.
This section gives you a test to use, and a list of plants that are
safe in most of the world without the test.

---

## The Universal Edibility Test

Use this when you must eat something you do not recognize and no other
food is available. The test takes about 24 hours. It tests one plant
part at a time.

**Important rules before you begin:**

```
  ○ Test only ONE plant at a time.
  ○ Test only ONE part of the plant at a time.
     (Root, stem, leaf, and seed may differ — test each separately.)
  ○ Eat nothing else during the 8-hour waiting period.
  ○ If you feel sick at any point: stop, induce vomiting with
     a tablespoon of salt dissolved in water, and discard that plant.
```

**The test:**

```
  STEP 1 — SMELL
    Crush a small piece. Smell it.
    If it smells like: bitter almonds · rotten · chemical · harsh
    → DISCARD. Do not eat.

  STEP 2 — SKIN CONTACT (15 minutes)
    Rub crushed plant on the inside of your forearm or wrist.
    Wait 15 minutes.
    If skin becomes: red · itchy · burning · blistered
    → DISCARD. Do not eat.

  STEP 3 — LIP CONTACT (3 minutes)
    Touch a small piece to your lower lip.
    Wait 3 minutes.
    If you feel: burning · tingling · numbness
    → DISCARD. Do not eat.

  STEP 4 — TONGUE CONTACT (15 minutes)
    Place a small piece on your tongue.
    Do NOT swallow.
    Wait 15 minutes.
    Any burning, tingling, or numbing → DISCARD. Spit it out.

  STEP 5 — CHEW, DO NOT SWALLOW (15 minutes)
    Chew a small piece.
    Do NOT swallow.
    Wait 15 minutes.
    Any reaction → DISCARD. Spit it out, rinse mouth.

  STEP 6 — SWALLOW A SMALL AMOUNT (8 hours)
    Swallow a thumbnail-sized piece.
    Eat nothing else.
    Wait 8 hours.
    If you feel sick: induce vomiting, drink water, discard plant.

  STEP 7 — EAT A SMALL AMOUNT (8 more hours)
    If no reaction, eat a small handful.
    Wait 8 more hours.
    If no reaction: this plant part is probably safe in this amount.

  The full test takes about 24 hours. In that time, do not eat
  anything else during the waiting periods.
```

## Plants that are safe in most parts of the world

These plants are found almost everywhere. They do not need the full
test. Learn to recognize them — they will feed you without testing.

**Cattail** *(tall reed with brown sausage-shaped top, grows in water)*
```
         ○ ← brown cylindrical seed head
         |
         | ← tall flat leaves
    ≈≈≈≈≈|≈≈≈≈≈  ← water's edge
         | ← roots in mud

  ALL PARTS ARE EDIBLE:
  ○ Young shoots (spring): eat raw or boil, like asparagus
  ○ Pollen (summer): yellow, shake into container, use as flour
  ○ Roots (any time): wash, roast or boil, starchy like potato
  ○ Found at edges of ponds, streams, and marshes worldwide
```

**Dandelion** *(yellow flower, jagged leaves, white puff seed head)*
```
  ○ ← yellow flower (eat)
  |
  ))) ← jagged leaves pointing like teeth (eat)
  |
  ○ ← root (eat)

  ○ Leaves: eat raw or boil (bitter raw — boiling helps)
  ○ Flowers: eat raw
  ○ Root: roast and eat, or roast and grind as coffee substitute
  ○ Found in fields, roadsides, disturbed ground worldwide
```

**Pine and most conifer trees** *(needle-leaved trees with cones)*
```
  /\  /\  /\
 /  \/  \/  \   ← tree shape

  ○ Inner bark (cambium): scrape from under the rough outer bark.
    White or greenish layer. Eat raw or dry and grind to flour.
    Best in spring when sap runs.
  ○ Young needles: make tea (vitamin C — prevents scurvy).
    Boil a handful in water, drink the liquid.
  ○ Seeds in cones: edible, small but nutritious.
  ○ Found widely in temperate and cold climates worldwide.
```

**Acorns** *(from oak trees — rounded leaves or lobed leaves, round nuts)*
```
  Bitter raw — must be prepared:
  1. Shell the acorns.
  2. Crush or grind the inner nut.
  3. Soak the crushed meal in running water for several hours,
     or change the water 3–4 times.
  4. Taste: if still very bitter, soak more.
  5. When mild or tasteless: safe to cook.
  ○ Grind into flour, make flat cakes cooked on hot rock.
  ○ A reliable, storable, high-calorie food where oaks grow.
```

## Warning signs — plants to avoid without careful testing

```
  HIGH RISK — test very carefully or avoid:

  ✗  White milky sap when stem is broken
     (many highly toxic plants have this)

  ✗  Umbrella-shaped clusters of small white or yellow flowers
     (hemlock family — some of the most poisonous plants known)
     These look like wild carrot but can be fatal in small amounts.

  ✗  Shiny leaves in groups of three
     ("leaves of three, let it be" — poison ivy/oak/sumac)
     Causes severe skin rash; smoke from burning it causes lung damage.

  ✗  Bitter almond smell when crushed
     (indicates cyanide compounds)

  ✗  Colorful berries on unknown plants
     (most white, yellow, and red berries are toxic or risky)
     Exception: some are fine. Test carefully. Do not assume.

  ✗  Bulbs and roots that smell strongly onion-like
     UNLESS you are certain it is a true onion or garlic.
     If it doesn't smell like onion: it might be death camas (fatal).
```

## Animals

Almost all animals are edible if properly cooked.

```
  SAFE WITH COOKING:
    All mammals
    All birds and their eggs
    Fish and shellfish (cook shellfish thoroughly)
    Insects and grubs (remove wings/legs if large, cook)
    Worms (boil or roast)
    Frogs and salamanders (avoid very brightly colored ones)
    Snails and slugs (boil thoroughly)

  AVOID OR BE VERY CAREFUL:
    Brightly colored frogs (many are toxic — bright color is a warning)
    Brightly colored insects (same principle)
    Liver of large predators (toxic vitamin A concentration)
    Fish that puff up or have spines (some are toxic — be careful)
    Any animal that was already dead when you found it (disease risk)
```

Always cook animal food if at all possible. Raw meat carries parasites
and bacteria. A few minutes over fire makes it safe.

## Simple trapping

**The snare** — best for rabbits and small animals on trails:

```
  Find an animal trail (worn path, droppings, footprints).

  Make a loop of strong cord or wire:

        ○ ← loop, about the size of your fist
       / \
      /   \
  ───/─────\─── ← cord attached to stick
                  stuck firmly in the ground

  Set the loop:
    — over the trail
    — one hand above the ground
    — loop must be free to close when animal runs through

  Check snares every few hours — morning and evening.
  Animals in snares suffer. Do not leave them long.
```

**The figure-4 deadfall** — for larger animals or where cord is scarce:

```
  Three sticks arranged to trigger a heavy flat rock:

      ┌──────────────────────┐
      │      heavy rock      │  ← will fall and trap animal
      └────────────┬─────────┘
                   │  \
                   │   \ ← diagonal brace
                   │    \
             ──────┘      └── ← bait stick
             vertical post     (bait on the end)
                              animal touches bait,
                              support collapses, rock falls

  Practice the balance of this before setting it with bait.
  The trigger is delicate — that is the point.
```

## Simple fishing

**Gorge hook:**
```
  Take a thin straight thorn or small straight stick.
  Tie your line to the CENTER of the stick.
  Bait the whole stick. The fish swallows it lengthwise.
  When you pull, it turns sideways and catches.
  No curved hook needed.

       ────── line ──────→ ←
              |
         ─────┼─────  ← bait-covered stick or thorn
```

**Simple weir (for streams):**
```
  Build a fence of sticks across most of the stream.
  Leave a gap in the middle.
  Place a basket or trap at the gap.
  Fish swim into the barrier and collect at the gap.

  ──────┤ ├──────  ← stick fence
        ▽          ← gap with trap
```

## Starting a garden

Once you are stable, a garden changes everything.
One person can grow enough food for five with a small garden.

**The most important plants to grow:**

```
  Potato      — easy, underground, stores well, filling
  Bean        — fixes nitrogen in soil, protein-rich
  Squash      — easy, large harvest, stores for months
  Corn/Grain  — makes flour, stores for years
  Leafy green — fast-growing, vitamins (kale, spinach)

  If you can only grow three: potato, bean, squash.
  These three planted together is called the Three Sisters.
  They help each other grow (see below).
```

**Three Sisters planting:**
```
  Corn grows tall first — a pole for beans.
  Beans climb the corn and fix nitrogen in the soil.
  Squash spreads wide on the ground — shades weeds, keeps moisture.

     🌽
    /|\ ← bean climbing
   / | \
  ○  |  ○  ← squash leaves on ground
```

**Timing:**
```
  Plant after the last frost.
  Sign: oak leaves are the size of a mouse's ear.
  Or: you can sleep outside without shelter without getting dangerously cold.

  Harvest before first frost (sign: morning ice on puddles or grass).
```

**Saving seeds:**
```
  Let one of every plant go to full seed — do not harvest it.
  Wait until the seed head is fully dry and brown.
  Remove the seeds. Dry them for two weeks in a dry, ventilated spot.
  Store in a dry, dark, cool place.
  Properly dried seeds last 2–5 years.
  This is how you grow food next year.
```

## Preserving food

Food that lasts lets you survive winter, drought, and bad harvests.

```
  SMOKING:
    Hot smoke for 2–4 hours.
    Thin strips of meat dry faster and smoke better.
    Smoke kills bacteria. Dried smoked meat lasts weeks.
    Hang strips in the smoke above a low fire.

  SUN DRYING:
    Thin slices of meat or fish, laid on rocks or hung on cord.
    Full sun for 2–3 days until completely dry and stiff.
    Must be stored in a dry place. Lasts weeks to months.

  SALTING:
    Pack heavily in salt. Draws out moisture. Bacteria cannot grow.
    Rinse before eating.
    Salt is found in dry lake beds, seawater (evaporate to get salt),
    mineral salt deposits (grey/pink rocky salt in ground).

  ROOT CELLAR:
    Dig a hole below the frost line — below where the ground freezes.
    Line with rocks or wood.
    Root vegetables (potato, turnip, carrot) stored here stay cool
    and last through the winter.
    Temperature underground stays near 5–10°C even in cold climates.
```

## Simple summary

```
  1. Water, shelter, fire before food. You have 3 weeks.
  2. Never eat an unknown plant without the full edibility test.
  3. Safe plants everywhere: cattail, dandelion, pine, acorns.
  4. Avoid: milky sap, umbrella white flowers, shiny three-part leaves.
  5. Cook all animal food.
  6. Set snares on animal trails. Check twice a day.
  7. Grow potato + bean + squash when stable.
  8. Save seeds. Your next year depends on this.
  9. Preserve with smoke, sun drying, or salt.
```
