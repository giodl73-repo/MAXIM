# 12 — Warnings

*What will kill you that doesn't look like it will*

---

```
          WARNINGS
          ────────

  Most deaths in survival situations are not dramatic.
  They are quiet. They come from mistakes that felt safe.

  This chapter covers the famous ones —
  the things that look harmless, feel fine,
  and then kill you or someone you care about.

  Read this chapter before you need it.
  The time to learn these is not while it is happening.
```

---

## 1. Fire in a closed space — carbon monoxide

This kills more people than almost any other fire-related mistake.
It is silent. It has no smell. You fall asleep and do not wake up.

**What it is:**

When anything burns — wood, charcoal, candles, oil — it consumes
oxygen and produces carbon monoxide gas. In open air this disperses
harmlessly. In an enclosed space, it builds up.

Carbon monoxide replaces oxygen in your blood without you feeling it.
Your brain does not send a "suffocating" alarm. You simply get sleepy,
then confused, then unconscious, then dead.

A small charcoal fire in a sealed tent or cave can kill everyone
inside within two hours. A fireplace in a room with a blocked chimney
can kill a family overnight.

**Signs that carbon monoxide is building:**
```
  Headache that gets worse over time
  Feeling unusually tired or drowsy
  Dizziness or confusion
  Nausea without obvious cause
  Everyone in the space feels bad at the same time

  KEY SIGN: you feel better when you go outside.
  If you feel better in fresh air: it is the air inside that is wrong.
```

**Rules:**

```
  NEVER burn anything in an enclosed space without ventilation.
  A gap at the top AND a gap at the bottom — air must flow through.
  The gap at the bottom brings fresh air in.
  The gap at the top lets poisoned air out.

  NEVER sleep in a sealed shelter with a fire or coals inside.
  NEVER bring burning charcoal into any enclosed space.
  NEVER block a chimney or flue completely.
  NEVER burn anything under a tarp pulled fully down to the ground.

  If someone cannot be woken:
    Carry them into fresh air immediately.
    Lay them on their side.
    They may recover within minutes in fresh air.
    If they do not breathe: begin rescue breathing (see below).
```

## 2. Mushrooms — the edibility test does not work

Chapter 04 gives a universal edibility test for plants.
**That test does not work for mushrooms.**

This is the most important exception in this book.

**Why mushrooms are different:**

The most deadly mushrooms in the world — particularly the death cap
(*Amanita phalloides*) and its relatives — cause no reaction during
the edibility test. None. You can eat a small piece, wait 8 hours,
feel perfectly fine, and conclude it is safe. Then, 24 to 48 hours
later, your liver and kidneys begin to fail.

By the time you feel sick, the damage is done. There is no antidote.
A single cap is enough to kill an adult. Half a cap has killed children.

The death cap looks like several edible mushrooms. It smells mild.
It tastes mild. It gives no warning.

```
  THE RULE FOR MUSHROOMS:

  Do not eat any mushroom you cannot identify with certainty.
  Not "probably right." Not "looks similar." Certainty.

  If you are not 100% certain of the species:
    Do not eat it.
    Not a small amount.
    Not after testing.
    Not after cooking it.
    Just do not eat it.

  There is no safe way to test an unknown mushroom.
  The edibility test does not apply.
```

**Learning mushrooms safely:**

```
  Start with species that have NO dangerous lookalikes:
    Giant puffball (pure white inside when cut, no gills, no cap)
    Chicken of the woods (bright orange shelf fungus on trees)
    Morels (honeycomb cap, hollow when cut — but see false morel)

  Learn from someone who already knows, not from books alone.
  A photograph in a book does not capture all the features.
  If you are learning from a book:
    Verify every single identification feature.
    Spore print color. Gill attachment. Smell. Habitat. Season.
    All must match. Not most — all.

  When in doubt: throw it out.
  No mushroom meal is worth dying for.
```

## 3. Lightning

Lightning kills without warning. But the conditions that create it
are predictable, and the risks can be greatly reduced.

**When lightning is a threat:**
```
  Any time you see a cumulonimbus cloud — a tall, rapidly building
  cloud that looks like a rising tower or an anvil shape at the top.

  Any time you hear thunder. If you can hear it, you are already
  within range to be struck. Sound travels about 1 km every 3 seconds.
  Count the seconds between flash and thunder:
  3 seconds = 1 km away. 9 seconds = 3 km away.
  If less than 30 seconds: take shelter now.

  Lightning can strike up to 15 km from the storm.
  "But there are no clouds here" is not protection.
```

**Where NOT to be:**
```
  ✗ Under the tallest tree in an open area
    (lightning seeks the highest point — you become part of the tree)

  ✗ In open fields, on hilltops, on ridges
    (you become the highest point)

  ✗ In or near water: rivers, lakes, swimming
    (water conducts electricity across its surface)

  ✗ In a small open shelter (three-sided lean-to in open ground)
    (partial protection is sometimes worse than none)

  ✗ Holding metal objects upright: tools, poles, fishing rods

  ✗ Near tall isolated trees anywhere
```

**Where to shelter:**
```
  ✓ Inside a substantial building, away from windows and water pipes.

  ✓ In a dense, low forest — among many trees of similar height
    is safer than being near one tall isolated tree.

  ✓ In a cave — but move away from the entrance and away from
    walls and ceiling. Electricity can flow along rock surfaces.
    Crouch in the middle of the space, not touching the walls.

  ✓ In a low area: a ravine, a ditch, a depression in the ground.
    Not a flooded one.

  IF CAUGHT IN THE OPEN with no shelter:
    Do not lie flat (ground current travels horizontally).
    Do not stand upright.
    Crouch on the balls of your feet. Feet together.
    Hands over ears (the thunder is loud enough to damage hearing).
    Do not touch the ground with your hands.
    Move away from other people — spread the group out by 15 paces.
    Multiple people standing together create a larger target.
```

## 4. Tetanus — the wound that looks fine

Tetanus is caused by bacteria that live in soil, dust, and animal
waste. They enter through wounds — especially deep puncture wounds,
wounds contaminated with soil, rusty metal cuts, and animal bites.

The wound often looks minor. It may not even become infected in the
usual sense. Then, days later, the muscles begin to seize.

Tetanus causes the muscles to lock. It starts in the jaw — called
"lockjaw" — and spreads. In severe cases, the whole body spasms.
Breathing stops. It killed a large proportion of the wounded in every
war before modern medicine.

**High-risk wounds:**
```
  ✗ Deep puncture wounds from nails, thorns, splinters, animal teeth
  ✗ Any wound contaminated with soil or manure
  ✗ Animal bites — especially from horses, cattle, dogs
  ✗ Wounds that were not cleaned thoroughly
  ✗ Wounds that closed over quickly without deep cleaning
    (bacteria sealed inside = higher risk)
```

**Prevention:**
```
  The most important thing: CLEAN WOUNDS DEEPLY.
  Flush with boiling water. Get to the bottom of the wound.
  A wound that looks clean on the surface but has debris inside
  is the most dangerous kind.

  For puncture wounds specifically:
    Squeeze gently to encourage bleeding (bleeding flushes the wound).
    Pour boiling water directly into the hole.
    Do not seal puncture wounds closed — they need to drain.
    Apply honey or garlic juice deep inside if possible.
    Watch carefully for the first 2 weeks.
```

**Signs of tetanus:**
```
  Days 3–21 after the wound (often 7–10 days):
    Stiffness or cramping in the jaw
    Difficulty opening the mouth or swallowing
    Neck stiffness
    Painful muscle spasms that spread through the body
    Spasms triggered by noise, touch, or light

  Tetanus is very serious. There is no simple treatment.
  Keep the person calm, in a quiet dark space.
  Prevent injury during spasms (pad the area around them).
  Give willow bark for pain.
  The body may recover over several weeks if the person survives
  the initial period and is kept comfortable, fed, and hydrated.
```

## 5. Animal bites — rabies

Most animal bites are not dangerous beyond the wound itself.
But some animals carry rabies — a virus that attacks the brain.
Once symptoms appear, rabies is fatal. Always.

**Animals that can carry rabies:**
Bats, foxes, raccoons, skunks, wild dogs, wolves, jackals, and many
other mammals. In some regions: monkeys.

**The risk:**
```
  An animal that approaches you without fear is suspicious.
  Healthy wild animals avoid humans.
  A wild animal that seems friendly, confused, or aggressive in an
  unusual way may be sick. Do not approach it.

  Bats are particularly important: a bat found on the ground,
  unable to fly, may be rabid. Do not handle it.
  A bat bite may be too small to feel. If you wake and find a bat
  in the room where you were sleeping: treat as a possible bite.
```

**After a bite from a suspicious animal:**
```
  1. Wash the wound immediately and thoroughly.
     Scrub with soap or ash-water for at least 15 minutes.
     Flush with large amounts of clean water.
     This is the single most effective action — do it immediately.
     Washing within minutes can significantly reduce risk.

  2. Apply alcohol, or iodine if available, or strong salt water.

  3. Do not close the wound. Let it drain.

  4. Watch the animal if possible.
     An animal that dies within 10 days of biting you had rabies.

  5. Symptoms of rabies in a human:
     2–12 weeks after bite: fever, headache, general illness.
     Then: confusion, agitation, fear of water, foaming.
     Once these appear: no treatment is effective.
     This is why the wound cleaning in step 1 is so critical.
```

## 6. Raw kidney beans — and undercooked legumes

If you are growing or eating kidney beans (chapter 04), know this:

**Raw or undercooked kidney beans are toxic.**

They contain a compound called lectin (phytohaemagglutinin) at very
high levels. Eating as few as 4–5 raw kidney beans causes severe
vomiting and diarrhea within 1–3 hours. Enough can kill.

```
  THE RULE:
    Soak dried kidney beans for at least 5 hours in cold water.
    Discard the soaking water.
    Boil vigorously for at least 10 minutes.
    (Slow cooking at low temperature does NOT destroy the toxin —
    it may even concentrate it. Must reach a full rolling boil.)
    Then simmer until fully soft.

  OTHER BEANS WITH SIMILAR RISK:
    Broad beans (fava beans): toxic raw. Must be cooked.
    Butter beans (lima beans): contain cyanide compounds when raw.
    Boil all dried beans before eating.

  SAFE RAW: peas, green beans (french beans), and lentils carry
  much lower levels and are safer, though cooking is still better.
```

## 7. Frostbite — the rewarming trap

Frostbite is frozen tissue. It happens when skin and the flesh
underneath freeze solid, usually in the fingers, toes, ears, and nose.

The flesh looks white or grey, feels hard, and has no feeling.

**The trap:**
```
  DO NOT rewarm frostbitten tissue unless you are certain
  it will not freeze again.

  Tissue that thaws and then refreezes suffers catastrophic damage —
  far worse than staying frozen.

  Frostbitten feet can be walked on while still frozen —
  but not after thawing.

  If you must walk to safety: keep frostbitten feet frozen
  and walk on them. Painful, but survivable.
  Thaw them only when you have reached permanent warmth.
```

**Rewarming correctly:**
```
  Rewarm in warm water — about the temperature of a comfortable bath.
  (Test with your elbow: comfortably warm, not hot.)
  Do NOT use fire, hot water, heating stones, or rubbing.
  All of these damage the fragile thawing tissue.

  Rewarming is very painful as sensation returns. This is normal.
  It means the tissue is surviving.
  No pain during rewarming means deeper damage.

  After rewarming:
  Blisters will form. Do not pop them. They protect the healing tissue.
  Keep the area clean and covered.
  Do not allow pressure on the tissue.
```

## 8. Alcohol in cold weather

Alcohol feels warm. It is not.

Alcohol causes blood vessels near the skin to widen. This brings
warm blood to the surface, creating a flush of warmth. But that
same surface warmth means heat is escaping your body faster.
Your core temperature drops while you feel comfortable.

```
  DO NOT drink alcohol to stay warm. It does the opposite.

  In cold and wet conditions:
  Alcohol accelerates hypothermia.
  It impairs judgment (you stop noticing danger signs).
  It causes sleepiness (you may fall asleep in a dangerous place).
  It dehydrates you (worsening cold stress on the body).

  If alcohol is available for disinfecting wounds: use it for that.
  Not for warmth. Not in cold weather.
```

## 9. Moving an injured person — spinal injury

If someone has fallen from a height, been in a serious accident, or
taken a blow to the head or neck — do not move them carelessly.

The spine is a column of small bones protecting the spinal cord.
If those bones are cracked, moving the person can cause the broken
pieces to damage the cord — turning a survivable injury into permanent
paralysis or death.

```
  SIGNS OF POSSIBLE SPINAL INJURY:
    Fall from height greater than standing height
    Head-first impact
    Being struck by something heavy
    Complaining of neck or back pain after an accident
    Numbness or tingling in hands or feet after a fall
    Any loss of consciousness after a head blow

  IF SPINAL INJURY IS POSSIBLE:
    Do not move the person unless they are in immediate danger
    (fire, flood, structural collapse).

    If they must be moved:
    Keep the head, neck, and spine aligned in one straight line.
    Move them as a single rigid unit — this requires several people.
    One person holds the head still. Others move the body.
    The head must not turn, tilt, or move independently.

    A makeshift stretcher:
    Two straight poles + jackets, cloth, or rope woven between them.
    Slide onto it without bending the spine.
```

## 10. Giving food or water to an unconscious person

It seems like kindness. It can kill.

An unconscious person has no control over their swallowing reflex.
Food or liquid poured into the mouth of an unconscious person
goes into the lungs, not the stomach. This causes choking, lung
infection, or suffocation.

```
  NEVER give anything by mouth to an unconscious person.

  If an unconscious person needs fluids (severe dehydration,
  blood loss): there is no safe oral method.
  Keep them on their side so fluid drains out rather than in.
  Do not pour anything into their mouth.

  Only give water to someone who is conscious, alert,
  and able to swallow intentionally.
```

## 11. Stopping CPR too soon

If someone's heart has stopped and they are not breathing,
the only thing that keeps blood moving to the brain is you.

CPR — pressing hard on the center of the chest — is exhausting.
After a few minutes most people want to stop. Don't.

```
  BASIC CPR:
    Lay the person on their back on a firm surface.
    Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest.
    Place your other hand on top.
    Press straight down about 5–6 cm (roughly 2 thumb-widths).
    Press hard and fast: about 100–120 times per minute.
    (Faster than once per second. The rhythm of a fast marching step.)

    Let the chest fully rise between each compression.
    Do not lean on the chest.

    If you can do rescue breathing:
    Every 30 compressions: tilt the head back, lift the chin,
    pinch the nose, cover the mouth with yours,
    blow two steady breaths until you see the chest rise.
    Then back to 30 compressions.

    If you cannot or will not do rescue breathing:
    Continuous chest compressions alone are still valuable.

  KEEP GOING:
    Until the person breathes on their own.
    Until someone else takes over.
    Until you physically cannot continue.

  Signs of recovery:
    Gasping breath.
    Color returning to lips and face.
    Eye movement or response.
```

## 12. The "lookalike" plants — know these pairs

Several common edible plants have dangerous lookalikes.
These are the most frequently confused.

```
  WILD CARROT (edible) vs POISON HEMLOCK (fatal)

  Wild carrot:
    Hairy stem
    Purple flower at center of white flower cluster
    Smells like carrot when crushed
    Root smells like carrot

  Poison hemlock:
    Smooth stem with purple blotches or spots
    No central purple flower — all white
    Smells musty or unpleasant when crushed
    Found near water, roadsides, disturbed ground

  RULE: If in doubt about any white-flowered plant in the carrot
        family — do not eat it. The risk is not worth it.
        Hemlock has killed people who were certain they had the
        right plant.


  WILD ONION / GARLIC (edible) vs DEATH CAMAS (fatal)

  Wild onion:
    Smells strongly of onion or garlic when crushed
    The smell is unmistakable

  Death camas:
    Looks similar — same grassy leaves, same bulb
    Does NOT smell like onion
    Has a faint unpleasant smell or no smell

  RULE: If it doesn't smell like onion: it is not an onion.
        Do not eat it.
        The smell test is the entire test for this family.


  ELDERBERRY (edible, cooked) vs WATER HEMLOCK (fatal)

  Elderberry:
    Shrub or small tree
    Clusters of dark purple/black berries
    Berries hang in flat-topped clusters
    Leaves have a slightly unpleasant smell

  Water hemlock:
    White-flowered, near water
    The ROOT looks like a cluster of small white tubers
    Smells faintly of parsnip or carrot
    One root can kill a person

  NOTE: Elderberries must be cooked before eating in quantity.
        Raw elderberries and all other parts of the elder plant
        (leaves, bark, unripe berries) cause nausea and vomiting.
        Cook the berries. Discard everything else.


  YEW TREE (all parts toxic except the red flesh around the seed)

  The yew is a common tree with red berries.
  The seeds inside the red flesh are highly toxic.
  The leaves, bark, and wood are toxic.
  Even small children have died from eating yew berries.
  The bright red flesh itself is sweet and edible — but
  the seed inside must not be chewed or swallowed.

  RULE: Do not eat yew berries unless you are certain of the plant
        and can remove all seeds. Easier rule: don't eat them.
```

## 13. Heatstroke — when cooling fails

Heat exhaustion and heatstroke look similar but require different
responses. Treating one as the other can kill.

```
  HEAT EXHAUSTION (body struggling to cool down):
    Heavy sweating
    Pale, cool, clammy skin
    Weak, rapid pulse
    Nausea
    Dizziness and fainting
    Still conscious and confused

    TREATMENT: Move to shade. Lie down. Loosen clothing.
               Cool with wet cloths. Drink water in small sips.
               Rest. Most people recover within 30 minutes.

  HEATSTROKE (the cooling system has failed — emergency):
    Stopped sweating even though extremely hot
    Skin is hot, red, and DRY
    Rapid strong pulse
    Confusion, strange behavior, or unconsciousness
    Body temperature very high (skin feels like a hot stone)

    TREATMENT: Emergency cooling — immediately.
               Immerse in cold water if possible.
               Wet the whole body and fan aggressively.
               Apply cold water to neck, armpits, and groin.
               This is life-threatening. Cool first, everything else second.

  KEY DIFFERENCE: Sweating = heat exhaustion (serious but manageable).
                  NOT sweating, hot dry skin = heatstroke (emergency).
```

## 14. Flash floods

Flash floods can arrive in a narrow canyon or valley when there has
been no rain at your location. A storm many kilometers away can send
a wall of water down a dry streambed in minutes.

```
  HIGH RISK SITUATIONS:
    Camping in narrow canyons or gorges
    Camping in a dry streambed (looks safe — is not)
    After a period of heavy rain anywhere upstream
    When you hear a roaring or rumbling from upstream before you
    see any water
    When the water in a stream suddenly turns brown and rises fast

  RULES:
    Never camp in the bottom of a canyon or in a dry wash.
    If you hear rumbling from upstream: move immediately to high ground.
    Do not wait to see what it is.
    A flash flood wave travels faster than you can run.
    Get to high ground first — be curious about what it was after.

  IF CAUGHT:
    Do not try to outrun the flood downstream.
    Move immediately perpendicular to the flow — toward the canyon wall.
    Climb. Get as high as you can as fast as you can.
    A flood that fills a canyon 3 meters deep can pass in 20 minutes.
    Wait until the water fully recedes before descending.
```

## 15. Entering an enclosed space — oxygen depletion

Old wells, pits, storage cellars, caves, mines, grain silos, and
sealed containers can have dangerously low oxygen — even with no
obvious fire or fumes.

Oxygen is consumed by:
- Rusting metal
- Rotting organic material
- Certain soils
- Previous occupants (human or animal)

```
  TEST BEFORE ENTERING:
    Lower a lit candle into the space on a rope.
    If the flame goes out or turns blue before the candle reaches
    the bottom: the air there is dangerous.
    Do not enter.

    A person who enters a low-oxygen space loses consciousness
    in seconds, without warning, without feeling short of breath.

    If someone collapses in an enclosed space:
    Do not follow them in.
    Throw a rope and try to pull them out from outside.
    Ventilate the space: open all available openings.
    Wait until the candle test passes before entering.

  RULE: Never enter an unknown enclosed space without testing
        the air first. No exception.
```

*These are not the only dangers. But they are the ones that look safe.*

*The ones that catch careful people.*

*Now you know them.*
