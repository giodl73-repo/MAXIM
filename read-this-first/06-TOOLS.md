## Knots

Learn these five knots. Practice until you can tie them without
looking. They will serve you for the rest of your life.

---

### Square knot — joining two ropes

Used to tie two ends of the same cord together: closing a bundle,
joining two ropes of the same thickness.

```
  START with two rope ends:

    right over left, cross, pull tight   →  makes a half knot
    left over right, cross, pull tight   →  completes the square knot

  RESULT:

     ──────\  /──────
            \/
            /\
     ──────/  \──────

  TEST: if it lies flat and symmetric, it is correct.
        If it looks like a lump on one side: try again.

  MEMORY: "right over left, left over right,
           makes a knot both tidy and tight."
```

---

### Bowline — a fixed loop that will not tighten or slip

This is the most important knot. Use it anywhere a loop is needed:
lowering someone, securing a boat, a rescue line.

```
  MEMORY: "the rabbit comes out of the hole, goes around the tree,
           and goes back into the hole."

  STEPS:
    Make a small loop in the standing rope. ← "the hole"
    Bring the end up through the hole.       ← "rabbit comes out"
    Take the end around behind the standing line.  ← "around the tree"
    Bring the end back down through the hole.      ← "back into the hole"
    Hold the standing line and pull the loop to tighten.

  RESULT:  fixed loop that stays open under load

        ○ ← fixed loop (does not change size)
        |
       /|\
      ( | ) ← goes around standing line and back through loop
        |
        ─── standing line
```

---

### Two half-hitches — securing rope to a post or tree

Fast, adjustable, reliable.

```
  STEPS:
    Pass rope around the post or bar.
    Bring the end over and under the standing line.  ← first half hitch
    Do it again.  ← second half hitch
    Pull tight.

  RESULT:

    POST
     │
     │← rope
    ─┼─ first half hitch
    ─┼─ second half hitch
     │
     ──── rope continues
```

---

### Clove hitch — fast temporary attachment

Good for quickly securing a rope to a post at the right height.
Adjustable — can be slid up and down.

```
  STEPS:
    Make two loops in the same direction.
    Pass the second loop behind the first.
    Slide both loops over the post.

  RESULT:

    POST
     │
    ═╪═  ← crossed wraps hold by friction
     │
```

---

### Timber hitch — dragging logs

Wraps around a log and grips harder as you pull.

```
  STEPS:
    Pass rope around the log.
    Bring the end around the standing part.
    Twist the end around itself 3–4 times.
    Pull. The more you pull, the tighter it grips.
```

## Making rope from plants

You do not need tools to make strong rope. You need plants and patience.

**Best plants for cordage:**
```
  Stinging nettle (dried stems)
  Dogbane / Indian hemp (dried stems)
  Cattail leaves (dried)
  Inner bark of basswood, cedar, willow, or elm
  Dried grasses twisted together (weaker, but available everywhere)
```

**The reverse-twist method:**

```
  STEP 1: Prepare your fibers.
    Dry the plant completely. Split or strip into long thin fibers.
    Soak in water briefly to make flexible if needed.

  STEP 2: Bundle into two groups.
    Hold a bundle of fibers in each hand.
    Or: fold a single bundle in half and hold the fold.

  STEP 3: Twist each bundle away from you (toward the right).
    While one bundle is twisted right, wrap it around the other.

  STEP 4: Repeat.
    Twist right, wrap around. Twist right, wrap around.
    The opposing twists lock each other in place.

  VISUAL:

    start:    /  /   ← two bundles of fiber
    twist:   /──/    ← twist each bundle clockwise
    wrap:    \  \    ← wrap one over the other
    result:   ╳  ╳  ← twisted rope structure

  STEP 5: Add more fiber to extend length.
    Overlap new fibers with the end of the bundle.
    Twist the new fibers in as you go.

  30 minutes of work = a hand-length of strong cord.
  Strong enough to hang your weight if made thick enough.
```

## Pottery

Clay pots hold water, cook food, and store seeds.
They can be made anywhere clay exists.

**Finding clay:**
```
  Clay is found near rivers, lakes, and in low-lying areas.
  TEST: Pick up a handful of moist soil. Squeeze it into a ball.
        Stretch it slowly.
        If it stretches without crumbling: clay.
        If it falls apart: not enough clay.

  Good clay should:
  ✓ Hold finger impressions clearly
  ✓ Roll into a thin rope without cracking
  ✗ If it crumbles: too sandy — find better clay
```

**Preparing clay:**
```
  1. Remove all stones and roots (they cause cracking in the fire).
  2. Knead the clay like bread until completely smooth and uniform.
     Work out all air bubbles.
  3. If cracking when you work it: add a little water.
     If sticky and wet: let it dry briefly in shade.
```

**The coil method:**
```
  1. Make a flat base: press a thick disc of clay onto a flat surface.
     About a hand-span across for a small pot.

  2. Roll a rope of clay between your hands.
     About as thick as your finger.

  3. Coil the rope around the top edge of the base.
     Press it firmly to the base — no gaps.

  4. Add another coil on top of the first.
     Press each coil firmly to the one below — both inside and outside.
     Smooth the joins with your thumb.

  5. Continue coiling upward. Angle inward for a narrower top.

  6. Smooth the outside with your fingers or a flat stone.
     The smoother, the stronger.

         ──────   ← coil 4
        ────────  ← coil 3
       ──────────  ← coil 2
      ────────────  ← coil 1
     ──────────────  ← base
```

**Drying:**
```
  CRITICAL: Do not rush this step.
  Dry in shade, slowly, for several weeks.
  If dried too fast: cracks form that will break in the fire.
  If there are small cracks: smooth them with wet clay and continue drying.
  The pot is ready to fire when it sounds hollow when tapped and
  feels completely dry — no cool areas when pressed to your cheek.
```

**Firing:**
```
  1. Build a fire and let it burn down to a deep coal bed.
  2. Set the pot next to the fire — not in it yet. Warm it slowly.
  3. After 30 minutes: push the pot onto the coal bed.
  4. Cover with more hot coals and small burning wood.
  5. Maintain for 2–3 hours. A successful fired pot glows red.
  6. Let it cool slowly inside the ash. Do not remove while hot.
     Rapid cooling causes cracking.
  7. A successful pot sounds like a bell when tapped.
     A cracked pot sounds dull.

  First pots often crack. This is normal. Keep practicing.
  Each pot teaches you something.
```

## A cutting tool from stone

You can make a cutting edge from certain types of stone with no metal.

**Find the right stone:**
```
  Look for: flint (grey or dark, often with white outer layer)
            obsidian (black, glassy — very sharp when broken)
            chert (like flint, various colors)
            quartzite (fine-grained, grey to white)

  TEST: drop the stone on a hard surface.
        Does it ring? Fine-grained and workable.
        Does it thud? Too coarse — find another.

  These stones are often found in riverbeds and hillside outcrops.
```

**Basic knapping:**
```
  Strike with a round hammerstone at about 70° to the flat face.
  Do not strike straight on — strike at an angle.

  FACE               ← flat surface of flint
     \
      \ ← 70° angle of strike
       \
        ○ ← hammerstone strikes here

  Each strike removes a flake and leaves a sharper edge.
  The flakes themselves are sharp — handle them carefully.
  Work around the edge, removing flakes alternately from each side.
```

**Handle:**
```
  Wrap the blunt end in rawhide or twisted plant fiber.
  Wet rawhide shrinks as it dries, gripping tightly.
  Lash with cord if needed.
```

## Bone tools

**Needle:**
```
  1. Find a bone from a bird or small mammal (leg bone works well).
  2. Split it lengthwise with a stone blade.
  3. Grind a thin strip of bone to a point on rough stone.
  4. Drill the eye (hole for thread): use a sharp flint point,
     twist it back and forth while pressing firmly.
  5. Smooth the whole needle on fine stone.
  A bone needle is strong enough to sew leather and heavy cloth.
```

**Awl:**
```
  1. Take a straight bone fragment. Grind one end to a sharp point.
  2. Used for: punching holes in leather, starting holes in wood,
     weaving, and basket-making.
```

## Wooden tools

**Digging stick:**
```
  Take a straight hardwood branch, wrist-thick, arm-length long.
  Sharpen one end with a stone blade.
  Harden the tip: hold it near hot coals (not in direct flame).
  Rotate slowly. The wood darkens and hardens.
  This is how humans farmed for most of history.
```

**Wooden mallet:**
```
  Find a branch with a thick node or branch junction at the end.
  The thick part becomes the head.
  Shape with a stone blade.
  Used for: driving stakes, knapping, pounding clay.
```

## Simple summary

```
  Knots to know:
    Square knot — joining two rope ends
    Bowline — fixed loop, will not slip, for safety
    Two half-hitches — securing to a post
    Clove hitch — quick temporary attachment
    Timber hitch — dragging heavy logs

  Making things:
    Rope: reverse-twist dried plant fibers. 30 min = strong cord.
    Pot: clay coils, slow dry (weeks), coal-bed fire.
    Blade: fine-grained stone struck at 70° angle.
    Needle: ground bone, twisted hole for eye.
    Digging stick: hardwood, shaped, fire-hardened tip.

  The most important tool is the one you know how to make.
```
