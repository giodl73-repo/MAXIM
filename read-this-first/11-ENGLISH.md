## How English is written on the page

Before reading words, know how the page works.

```
  ○ English reads LEFT to RIGHT.
    Start at the left edge of each line.
    Move your eye to the right.

  ○ English reads TOP to BOTTOM.
    When you finish one line, drop to the line below it.
    Start again from the left.

  ○ Spaces between words show where one word ends and another begins.
    water fire food   =  three separate words

  ○ A period ( . ) means the sentence is finished.
    A comma ( , ) means a short pause — the sentence continues.
    A question mark ( ? ) means it is a question.
    An exclamation mark ( ! ) means something urgent or strong.

  ○ Capital letters (A B C) are used at the start of a sentence,
    and for names of people and places.
    Small letters (a b c) are used everywhere else.
```

## How English sounds work

English has 26 letters but more than 40 distinct sounds.
Many letters make more than one sound.
Many sounds can be spelled different ways.

This is the hardest part of English. Be patient with it.
Start by learning the most common sound for each letter.

### Silent letters

Some letters are written but not said. This surprises many learners.

```
  Silent E at the end:  "fire" = "fyr"  (the E makes the I long)
                        "stone" = "ston"
                        "make" = "mayk"
  Silent K before N:    "knife" = "nyf"
                        "know"  = "noh"
                        "knot"  = "not"
  Silent B after M:     "climb" = "klym"
                        "thumb" = "thum"
                        "comb"  = "kohm"
  Silent G before N:    "sign"  = "syn"
                        "gnaw"  = "naw"
```

## How English sentences work

English sentences follow a pattern. Once you see it, everything opens up.

---

### The basic pattern: Who does What

```
  [WHO]        [DOES]     [WHAT]
  Water        boils      at 100 degrees.
  Fire         needs      air.
  The plant    grows      toward the sun.
  You          must       drink water.
  Wound        becomes    infected.

  The WHO is called the subject.
  The DOES is the verb.
  The WHAT is the object.
```

---

### Adding when, where, and how

```
  [WHO]   [DOES]    [WHAT]      [WHEN / WHERE / HOW]
  Boil    the water            before you drink it.
  Build   a shelter            before night.
  Press   the wound            until the bleeding stops.
  Plant   seeds                after the last frost.
  Store   food                 in a cool dry place.
```

---

### Giving instructions (commands)

In this book, many sentences are instructions.
The WHO is missing — it is understood to be "you."

```
  [DOES]     [WHAT]
  Boil       the water.
  Press      firmly.
  Cover      the wound.
  Let        it cool.
  Watch      for infection.
  Do not     drink seawater.
```

"Do not" before a verb means: stop, avoid, refuse to do that thing.
"Always" before a verb means: do this every time, without exception.
"Never" before a verb means: do not do this, not even once.

---

### Questions

Questions often start with: what · where · when · who · how · why

```
  What  is this?          (asking about a thing)
  Where is the water?     (asking about a place)
  When  should I plant?   (asking about time)
  Who   can help me?      (asking about a person)
  How   do I make fire?   (asking about method)
  Why   does this matter? (asking about reason)

  Questions with yes/no answers:
  Is this safe?     → Yes. / No.
  Does it hurt?     → Yes. / No.
  Can you walk?     → Yes. / No.
```

---

### Past, present, and future

```
  PRESENT — happening now:
    The water boils.    Fire burns.    The wound heals.

  PAST — already happened:
    The water boiled.   Fire burned.   The wound healed.
    (add -ed to most verbs to make them past)

  FUTURE — will happen:
    The water will boil.    Fire will burn.
    (add "will" before the verb)

  IRREGULAR PAST FORMS (common ones):
  is / are  →  was / were
  have      →  had
  do        →  did
  go        →  went
  come      →  came
  make      →  made
  find      →  found
  give      →  gave
  get       →  got
  see       →  saw
  eat       →  ate
  drink     →  drank
  run       →  ran
  take      →  took
  build     →  built
  grow      →  grew
  know      →  knew
```

## How to keep learning English

The fastest way to learn English is to read English every day —
starting with things you almost understand, and slowly moving up.

```
  WEEK 1–2:
    Read this appendix through once.
    Learn the structure words (page 3 of this chapter).
    Practice: point at things around you and say their English word.

  WEEK 3–4:
    Read chapter 01 (Water) again.
    Look up every word you do not know in this appendix.
    Write new words in a list. Review your list daily.

  MONTH 2:
    Read one chapter of this book each week.
    For each sentence you do not understand: break it into parts.
    Find the WHO. Find the DOES. Find the WHAT.
    The sentence will open up.

  MONTH 3 onward:
    Begin reading Natural World, volume I.
    The overview (00-OVERVIEW) is written to introduce vocabulary.
    Read slowly. Use a finger to track the line.
    When you finish a paragraph: say what it meant in your own language.
    That confirms you understood it.

  PERMANENT PRACTICE:
    When you learn a new word: use it three times that day.
    When you teach someone else a word: you remember it twice as long.
    The more you read, the faster it becomes natural.
    One year of daily reading: you will be fluent enough to teach others.
```

## A note on this

No language is better than another.
Every language on earth is complete — capable of expressing any idea,
any feeling, any nuance of the human experience.

English happens to be the language these books are written in.
That is a fact of history, not a judgment.

Your language carries things English does not.
Every person who speaks a second language knows: some thoughts
can only be said right in the first one.

But the knowledge in these books can be yours.
These words are learnable.
The ideas in them belong to everyone.

They were written for you.
