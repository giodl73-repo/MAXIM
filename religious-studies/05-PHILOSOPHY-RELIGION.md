# Philosophy of Religion

## The Big Picture

Philosophy of religion is the application of philosophical method to religious claims —
treating them as propositions to be assessed, arguments to be evaluated, concepts to be
clarified. It sits at the intersection of metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, and logic.
The central questions: Does God exist? If so, what is God like? Is religious experience
evidence of anything? How should we reason about God given limited evidence?

```
+---------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              PHILOSOPHY OF RELIGION — PROBLEM MAP                   |
|                                                                     |
|  GOD'S EXISTENCE         GOD'S NATURE           RELIGIOUS BELIEF    |
|  ---------------         -----------            ----------------    |
|  Cosmological arg:       Omnipotence:           Evidentialism:      |
|  First cause / KCA       paradoxes, coherence   evidence required   |
|                                                                     |
|  Ontological arg:        Omniscience:           Proper basicality:  |
|  Greatest conceivable    foreknowledge vs.      belief can be       |
|  being                   free will              basic (Plantinga)   |
|                                                                     |
|  Teleological arg:       Omnibenevolence:       Fideism:            |
|  Design inference;       Problem of evil,       faith beyond/       |
|  fine-tuning             theodicy               against reason      |
|                                                                     |
|  Ontological dis-        Immutability:          Wittgensteinian:    |
|  proof: atheological     eternal vs.            religion as form    |
|  arguments (evil,        everlasting            of life             |
|  hiddenness)                                                        |
+---------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

Note: this is the analytic philosophy of religion tradition (Anglo-American, 20th-21st c).
Continental philosophy of religion (Heidegger, Ricoeur, Levinas, Derrida on religion)
is a different tradition with different questions and methods.

## Arguments for God's Existence

### The Cosmological Argument

**Aristotle/Aquinas Version** (the "five ways"):
Aquinas's First Way: things are moved; nothing moves itself; there cannot be an
infinite regress of movers; therefore there is an unmoved mover. Similar for
causation (Second Way) and contingent existence (Third Way, the "argument from
contingency").

The logical form:
```
  1. Everything that exists has a cause.
  2. The universe exists.
  3. Therefore the universe has a cause.
  4. That cause is God.
```

Immediate problems: (1) is questioned (does everything need a cause, or just
contingent things?). The move from "cause of the universe" to "God" requires
substantial additional argument. Russell's response (BBC debate with Copleston,
1948): "The universe is just there, and that's all." Why does the universe need
a cause if God doesn't?

**Leibniz's Principle of Sufficient Reason Version**:
For every fact, there must be a sufficient reason why it is so rather than otherwise.
The fact of the universe's existence requires a reason outside itself.
Only a necessary being (one whose essence includes existence — cannot NOT exist)
provides sufficient reason. That necessary being is God.

Critique: Is the Principle of Sufficient Reason itself true? It seems to require
that there be explanations all the way down — but this may be an assumption, not
an evident principle.

**The Kalam Cosmological Argument** (Islamic tradition, revived by William Lane Craig):

```
  1. Whatever begins to exist has a cause.
  2. The universe began to exist.
  3. Therefore the universe has a cause.
```

Premise 2 support: (a) actual infinities cannot exist in reality (Hilbert's Hotel
arguments — infinite sets have counterintuitive properties); (b) the Big Bang
cosmology suggests the universe had a beginning.

Premise 1 support: causal principle seems self-evident; quantum mechanical
"spontaneous" events are not truly uncaused (just randomly caused, not causally
unconstrained).

From "cause of the universe" to "God": Craig argues the cause must be timeless,
spaceless, immensely powerful, and personal (to explain why it acted to create —
only a personal agent can explain why a timeless cause produced a temporal effect).

Critiques: Premise 2 is disputed — quantum cosmology allows models without a
beginning (Hawking-Hartle "no boundary" proposal). Premise 1 may not apply to
the universe's origin (the causal principle itself might be a feature of the
universe, not prior to it). The "personal agent" inference is not obviously
supported by premises 1-3.

### The Ontological Argument

The most philosophically fascinating of the theistic arguments — and the most
disputed. The argument tries to show that God's existence follows from the concept
of God alone, without empirical investigation.

**Anselm's Version** ("Proslogion," c.1078):

```
  1. God is defined as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived."
  2. Suppose God exists only in the understanding (not in reality).
  3. A being that exists in reality is greater than one that exists only
     in the understanding.
  4. Therefore we can conceive of something greater than God (namely,
     the same being but actually existing).
  5. This contradicts step 1.
  6. Therefore God exists in reality.
```

**Gaunilo's Objection** (contemporary of Anselm): Apply the same logic to
an island. Define "the greatest conceivable island." By the same argument,
it must exist. This reductio ad absurdum shows the argument form is invalid.

Anselm's response: the argument only works for a "maximally great being" —
an island is contingently great (it could be greater with more palm trees).
God's greatness is necessary greatness — a different kind of perfection.

**Kant's Refutation**: "Existence is not a predicate." When you say "God exists,"
you are not adding a property to the concept of God — you are saying the concept
is instantiated. Adding "exists" to a concept doesn't enrich the concept.
Therefore the move from conceptual greatness to real existence is illegitimate.

**Plantinga's Modal Ontological Argument** (S5 modal logic):

```
  1. It is possible that a maximally great being exists.
     (There is a possible world in which God exists.)
  2. If a maximally great being exists in any possible world,
     it exists in all possible worlds. (Maximal greatness requires
     necessary existence — existence in all possible worlds.)
  3. Therefore a maximally great being exists in the actual world.
```

The argument is valid in S5 modal logic (where ◇□P → □P — if it's possibly
necessary, it's necessary). The controversial premise is (1): is it actually
possible (in the modal-logical sense) that a maximally great being exists?
An atheist can simply deny that maximal greatness is metaphysically possible —
perhaps omniscience + omnipotence is incoherent.

Assessment: The ontological argument is philosophically fascinating as a study
in modal logic and the logic of necessity. Most philosophers who have studied it
find it unpersuasive as a proof of God, because premise (1) simply relocates
the dispute (why think it's possible?).

### The Teleological Argument (Argument from Design)

**Aquinas's Fifth Way**: Things without intelligence act for ends — the regularity
of nature implies a directing intelligence. An arrow shot by an archer requires
an archer even if the arrow has no intention.

**Paley's Watchmaker** (1802): If you found a watch on a heath, you would infer
a watchmaker. The complexity and functionality of the watch implies design. The
eye, the hand, the ecosystem — all exhibit the same complexity-serving-function.
Therefore they were designed.

**Darwin's Dissolution**: Natural selection produces apparent design without a
designer. Variation + differential reproduction + heredity → adaptation over
time. The appearance of design is a predictable output of the mechanism.
The watch analogy fails: watches don't reproduce; organisms do. Paley's argument
was persuasive before 1859; the evidential situation changed completely.

**The Fine-Tuning Argument** (modern version, Swinburne, Collins, Craig):
The fundamental constants of physics appear extraordinarily fine-tuned for life.
The cosmological constant, the strong nuclear force, the ratio of electromagnetic
to gravitational force — change any by tiny amounts and no stars, no chemistry,
no life. The probability of hitting these values by chance seems vanishingly small.
Design is a better explanation than luck.

```
  Responses to the fine-tuning argument:

  1. Anthropic Principle (Weak Anthropic):
     We can only observe a universe compatible with our existence.
     No mystery: we're in a life-permitting universe because we exist
     to observe it. (The question is whether this dissolves the
     improbability or just relocates it.)

  2. Multiverse:
     If there are vastly many universes with different constants,
     it's unsurprising that some (ours) have life-permitting values.
     Objections: multiverse is not empirically confirmed; importing an
     unobservable hypothesis raises its own evidential questions.

  3. God hypothesis:
     An intelligent designer who wanted life would select life-permitting
     constants. This is a simpler explanation than chance or multiverse.
     Objections: What explains God? God is not simpler than the multiverse.
```

## The Problem of Evil (Theodicy)

If God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, why does evil exist?
This is the strongest argument against theism.

### The Logical Problem of Evil (Mackie)

J.L. Mackie ("Evil and Omnipotence," 1955): The existence of an omnipotent,
omniscient, omnibenevolent God is logically inconsistent with the existence of
any evil whatsoever. God could eliminate evil; God knows all evil exists; God wants
to eliminate evil. Evil exists. Contradiction.

```
  1. God is omnipotent (can do anything).
  2. God is omniscient (knows everything, including all evil).
  3. God is omnibenevolent (wants to eliminate all evil).
  4. Evil exists.
  5. 1+2+3 → there should be no evil (God would know, could act, and wants to).
  6. 4 contradicts 5. Therefore: a God with properties 1+2+3 does not exist.
```

### The Free Will Defense (Plantinga)

Alvin Plantinga's response: possibly God could not create free creatures who never
choose evil. A world with free creatures who sometimes sin may be more valuable than
a world with morally determined creatures who always do good. God's omnipotence
doesn't extend to logical impossibilities — and it may be logically impossible to
create free beings guaranteed not to sin.

```
  Plantinga's technical claim:
  Possibly, every creaturely essence suffers from "transworld depravity" —
  in every possible world where God creates that creature, the creature
  freely commits at least one sin. If so, God cannot create any free
  morally perfect creatures. God had a choice: no free creatures,
  or free creatures who may sin. A world with free creatures is better.
```

This shows logical consistency, not truth. Mackie's logical problem is deflected:
there is no strict contradiction in theism + evil. But the evidential problem remains.

**The Evidential Problem of Evil** (Rowe):
Even if it's logically consistent, the amount and distribution of evil is strong
evidence against theism. "Gratuitous evil" — suffering that serves no greater
purpose — seems to exist. A fawn burning slowly in a forest fire, with no human
present, serves no apparent moral or pedagogical purpose.

**Soul-Making Theodicy** (Hick):
Moral evil and natural evil are necessary conditions for moral development.
A world without challenge, risk, and the possibility of genuine failure cannot
produce genuine virtue. God's goal is soul-making (theosis — becoming fully human
and therefore divine-like). Evil is the necessary conditions for that development.

**Skeptical Theism** (Wykstra, van Inwagen):
We are not in a position to assess whether a given instance of evil is truly
gratuitous (serves no justifying purpose). A parent's reasons for allowing a
child's suffering (vaccination, surgery) are opaque to the child. Our cognitive
limitations prevent us from seeing what God's reasons might be.
Critique: if we can't know whether evil is gratuitous, we also can't know whether
God's actions are good — skeptical theism risks undermining all moral theology.

### The Argument from Divine Hiddenness (Schellenberg)

If God is perfectly loving, God would ensure all seekers have the opportunity for
a personal relationship with God. Some sincere seekers find no evidence of God's
existence and experience God's absence. Therefore a perfectly loving God does not
exist.

```
  1. If a perfectly loving God existed, no reasonable non-belief would exist.
     (A perfectly loving God would ensure all seeking persons find God.)
  2. Reasonable non-belief exists.
  3. Therefore a perfectly loving God does not exist.
```

Responses: Divine hiddenness may itself serve purposes (soul-making, protecting
human freedom, epistemic distance for authentic response). These responses must
show why hiddenness is consistent with perfect love, which is not easy.

## Religious Epistemology

### Evidentialism

W.K. Clifford: "It is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything
upon insufficient evidence." Believing without evidence is epistemic irresponsibility.
Applied to religion: religious belief without sufficient evidence is unjustified.

William James's response ("The Will to Believe," 1896): Some questions cannot wait for
evidence. When the decision is live (genuinely possible for you), forced (you must
choose), and momentous (significant consequences) — and the evidence is insufficient —
you have the right to choose. Religious belief meets these conditions.

### Plantinga's Reformed Epistemology

Alvin Plantinga's "Warranted Christian Belief" (2000): Religious belief can be
"properly basic" — warranted without being inferred from other beliefs.

```
  Classical foundationalism:
  Beliefs are justified only if they are:
  (a) self-evident (2+2=4), incorrigible (I am in pain), or
      evident to the senses (the wall is white)
  OR
  (b) inferred from (a)

  Problem: Classical foundationalism is self-refuting.
  "Beliefs are justified only if..." is itself not self-evident,
  incorrigible, or evident to the senses — and can't be inferred
  from (a). Therefore classical foundationalism fails its own test.

  Plantinga's alternative: Belief in God can be properly basic —
  directly formed in response to experience (beauty, moral obligation,
  sense of divine presence) without inference from other beliefs.
  Like perceptual beliefs: "I see a tree" is not inferred from
  sense data — it is directly formed in response to experience.
  Similarly: "God is present" can be directly formed.
```

The crucial epistemological question: what makes a belief "properly basic"?
Plantinga's answer: a belief is warranted if it is produced by cognitive faculties
functioning properly in the appropriate environment according to a design plan
aimed at truth. If the sensus divinitatis (sense of divinity) is a properly
functioning cognitive faculty, theistic beliefs formed by it are warranted.

### Wittgensteinian Fideism (D.Z. Phillips)

Religion is not a theory about the world competing with science. It is a "form of
life" (Wittgenstein's term) — a practice with its own internal criteria of correctness.
Religious language does not make factual claims that can be assessed by scientific or
historical standards. "God exists" is not a hypothesis to be confirmed or disconfirmed;
it is an expression of a form of life.

Critique: this "non-realism" about religious language abandons the ordinary believer's
conviction that religious claims are true of reality. If "God exists" makes no factual
claim, what do believers think they are doing? Wittgensteinian fideism may save religion
from criticism at the cost of emptying it of the claims that matter to practitioners.

## Mystical Experience

### William James — The Varieties of Religious Experience (1902)

James identified four marks of mystical experience:
1. **Ineffability**: cannot be adequately described in language
2. **Noetic quality**: conveys knowledge — illumination, insight, not just feeling
3. **Transiency**: does not persist in full intensity
4. **Passivity**: the subject feels acted upon, not in control

James argued mystical experiences constitute evidence for the reality of an unseen
order — not proof, but the kind of evidence appropriate to religious inquiry.

### The Constructivist-Perennialist Debate

**Steven Katz's constructivism** ("Mysticism and Philosophical Analysis," 1978):
There is no unmediated mystical experience. Experience is always "shaped, molded,
and altered" by the conceptual framework, expectations, and prior training of the
experiencer. A Christian mystic's experience of union with God, a Sufi's fana,
and a Zen monk's satori are not the same experience described in different languages —
they are genuinely different experiences.

Evidence: Theravada meditators achieve nirodha samapatti (cessation of consciousness).
Christian mystics achieve union with a personal God. Advaita practitioners achieve
the identity of Atman and Brahman. These are not variants of the same thing.

**Robert Forman's perennialist response**: Some meditation traditions report
"pure consciousness events" — states of awareness with no intentional content
(no object, no thought, just awareness itself). This "pure consciousness event"
appears across traditions and cannot be explained as a product of expectation
(you can't expect a state with no content). It is a genuine cross-cultural datum.

### Alston — Religious Experience as Perception

William Alston ("Perceiving God," 1991): religious experience has the structure
of perception — it presents itself as perceiving an object (God). If sense perception
gives warranted beliefs about the physical world (despite underdetermination and
fallibility), then "mystical perception" can give warranted beliefs about the divine,
by the same structural argument.

Critique: the "doxastic practices" parallel breaks down because we have cross-checks
for sense perception (multiple senses, intersubjective agreement, predictive success)
that we lack for religious perception. Religious "perceptions" are not publicly
checkable the same way.

## Divine Attributes

### Omnipotence

Can God create a stone too heavy for God to lift? If yes, then God cannot lift
it (not omnipotent). If no, then God cannot create it (not omnipotent). The
stone paradox shows that "omnipotence" cannot mean "the ability to do absolutely
anything including logical contradictions." Standard responses:
- Omnipotence = maximal coherent power (can do anything logically possible)
- The paradox asks God to create something that defeats God's power — this is
  self-contradictory, not a limitation on real power

### Omniscience and Free Will

If God timelessly knows what I will do tomorrow, can I do otherwise? Apparent conflict
between foreknowledge and free will.

**Molinist response** (Luis de Molina, 1588): Middle knowledge — God knows not only
what will happen but what every free creature would do in any possible circumstance
(counterfactuals of creaturely freedom). God can use middle knowledge to providentially
arrange circumstances without determining actions.
Critique: counterfactuals of creaturely freedom may not be "knowable" — there may
be no fact of the matter about what a free creature would do in unrealized circumstances.

**Open Theism** (Clark Pinnock, Greg Boyd): God does not foreknow free future
contingents — the future does not yet exist to be known. God's omniscience covers
everything that can be known; the open future is not knowable even by God.
God is perfectly responsive rather than deterministically controlling.
Critique: requires revising the classical divine attribute set; seems to reduce
God's sovereignty.

**Boethian/Thomistic solution**: God is eternal (outside time), not merely everlasting
(temporally infinite). God's "foreknowledge" is not temporal precedence but eternal
presence — God sees all times as simultaneous from an eternal vantage point.
Not prediction but present perception.

### Process Theology (Whitehead/Hartshorne)

Alfred North Whitehead ("Process and Reality," 1929) and Charles Hartshorne:
God is dipolar — primordial nature (the totality of abstract eternal possibilities)
and consequent nature (God as affected by and growing with the world). God is not
omnipotent in the classical sense — God persuades but does not coerce.
The world co-creates with God. Evil is not God's responsibility because God cannot
unilaterally override creature freedom.

This radically revises classical theism: God suffers with creation, is genuinely
affected by events, and does not have the power to prevent evil unilaterally.
Theodicy becomes much easier. But: is this God worth worshipping? Is a God who
cannot prevent evil a God who can be trusted?

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Argument | Strongest form | Strongest objection | Current consensus |
|----------|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Cosmological (Kalam) | KCA: universe began, requires cause | What if universe had no beginning? Why does cause = God? | Defended by Craig; contested by cosmologists and philosophers |

## Common Confusion Points

**Philosophy of religion ≠ apologetics**: Apologetics is the project of defending
religious faith. Philosophy of religion is neutral — it applies philosophical
analysis to religious claims. It can result in arguments against religion as easily
as for it. Plantinga does reformed epistemology; Mackie argues against theism.
Both are doing philosophy of religion.

**The ontological argument is valid (in S5)**: Validity is not soundness. Plantinga's
modal ontological argument is valid — if the premises are true, the conclusion follows.
The dispute is over whether premise 1 is true (is it possible that a maximally great
being exists?). An atheist can consistently deny this.

**"Evil disproves God"**: Mackie's logical problem is widely considered deflected by
Plantinga's free will defense (or at least, a logical consistency is established).
The evidential problem (Rowe's gratuitous evil) is harder and more live. The two
problems require different responses.

**"Religious experience proves God"**: James's argument is that religious experience
is evidence, not proof. Alston's argument is that it can warrant belief, not that
it conclusively demonstrates. The key philosophical question is whether religious
experience has the structure of genuine perception or something else (cognitive
byproduct, cultural conditioning, psychosomatic process).
