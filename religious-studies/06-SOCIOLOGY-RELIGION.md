# Sociology of Religion

## The Big Picture

Sociology of religion treats religious phenomena as social facts — producible,
reproducible, and analyzable through the same methods applied to any other social
phenomenon. The three founding theorists (Durkheim, Weber, Marx) each gave religion
a different social function and generated different research programs.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              SOCIOLOGY OF RELIGION — THEORETICAL MAP                |
|                                                                      |
|  DURKHEIM                WEBER                  MARX                |
|  --------                -----                  ----                |
|  Religion = society      Religion = meaning      Religion = ideology |
|  worshipping itself.     system.                 mystifying class    |
|  Function: solidarity.   Function: theodicy,     relations.         |
|  Sacred/profane.         legitimation,           Opiate of the       |
|  Collective effervescence rationalization.        masses.            |
|  Ritual = solidarity     Protestant Ethic.       False consciousness.|
|  production engine.      Three authority types.  Gramsci: hegemony. |
|                                                                      |
|  CONTEMPORARY BRANCHES:                                              |
|  Secularization studies   Religious markets     NRM / cult studies   |
|  (Berger, Bruce, Wilson)  theory (Stark/Finke)  (Stark, Lofland)    |
|                                                                      |
|  Ritual theory (Collins)  Civil religion (Bellah) Fundamentalism    |
|  Social neuroscience      (Almond/Appleby)        studies           |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Durkheim — The Elementary Forms

"Les Formes Elementaires de la Vie Religieuse" (1912) is the foundational text
of the sociology of religion.

### Method and Data

Durkheim's strategy: to understand the essential nature of religion, study the
simplest form — totemism among the Arunta (also spelled Aranda/Arrernte) of
central Australia. His sources: Baldwin Spencer and F.J. Gillen's ethnography
(1899, 1904). The methodological assumption: complex phenomena can be understood
by analyzing their simplest form, as chemists reduce compounds to elements.

Problems with this method: the Arunta were not "primitive" — they had a complex
social organization. The simplest ethnographically known form is not necessarily
the historically earliest form. And Spencer/Gillen's data was later found to have
errors.

### Sacred and Profane

The sacred/profane distinction is Durkheim's central analytical category — and
notably not "belief in gods." The sacred is not defined by its content but by its
formal character: it is set apart, treated with respect and awe, protected by
prohibition. Anything can be sacred: a flag, a text, a place, a body.

```
  Sacred:  Set apart from ordinary use. Approached with awe,
           reverence, care. Surrounded by taboos.
           Examples: consecrated host, the Constitution, a grave.

  Profane: Ordinary, everyday, instrumental, approachable,
           available for use without special protocol.

  The distinction is formal, not substantive.
  The sacred is not God — a secular society can have a
  sacred flag as surely as a religious one has a sacred altar.
```

This analytical move has enormous consequences: it allows Durkheim to study
religion without presupposing theism, and it reveals secular phenomena (nation,
science, politics) as having structures analogous to religion.

### Collective Effervescence and Solidarity

The explanatory mechanism: intense group rituals (corroborees, collective ceremonies)
generate heightened emotional energy — what Durkheim calls "collective effervescence."
This energy is experienced as coming from outside the individual (from the totem,
from the sacred). It is then symbolically condensed into the totem emblem.

```
  Group assembles for intense ritual
       |
       v
  Collective effervescence — emotional contagion, heightened affect,
  feeling of being caught up in something larger
       |
       v
  This feeling is attributed to the totem (the sacred object that is
  present) — experienced as coming from outside
       |
       v
  The totem "has power" — religious belief formed
       |
       v
  Group disperses, but the belief (and thus solidarity) persists
```

The god is real: the energy generated in collective ritual is real social energy.
What religious people attribute to God is actually the force of the social group.
Religion is society worshipping itself without knowing it.

Collins's extension (Interaction Ritual Chains, 2004): ritual generates "emotional
energy" (EE) — a micro-level analytic. Face-to-face interaction with mutual focus
and shared emotional mood charges participants with EE. People seek high-EE
interactions; low-EE interactions drain. The sacred IS the high-EE focus object.
Implication: virtual meetings cannot fully replicate in-person ritual because the
feedback loops of physical co-presence are absent.

### Church and Sect (Troeltsch)

Ernst Troeltsch's extension of Weberian ideal types:

| Type | Membership | Attitude to world | Theology | Social correlate |
|------|-----------|------------------|----------|-----------------|
| Church | Born into; inclusive | Accommodates, transforms | Established, sacramental | Dominant social class |
| Sect | Voluntary; exclusive; adult conversion | Rejects, withdraws | Strict, literalist | Subordinate or marginalized class |
| Mysticism | Individual; no institution | Indifferent | Interior, experiential | Intellectual elite |

Sects tend to churchify over generations (routinization of charisma): the
founder's intense movement becomes institutionalized, doctrine systematized,
membership broadened by birth. The intensity that defined the sect becomes
socialized away. This explains why new sects continuously emerge (to recover
the intensity).

## Weber — The Protestant Ethic

### The Argument

"The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism" (1904-05) is one of sociology's
most famous and most disputed works.

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
|              WEBER'S ARGUMENT CHAIN                       |
|                                                           |
|  Calvinist theology:                                      |
|  Double predestination — God has already decided          |
|  who is saved and damned. No works can change this.       |
|  This creates a distinctive psychological problem:        |
|  Am I among the elect? How can I know?                    |
|       |                                                   |
|       v                                                   |
|  Resolution: Success in one's calling (Beruf) is          |
|  taken as a sign of election. Not a cause — a sign.       |
|  The elect will be systematically disciplined;            |
|  success in worldly work demonstrates election.           |
|       |                                                   |
|       v                                                   |
|  Behavioral consequence: Systematic, disciplined          |
|  work; no idleness; no spending on luxury (asceticism);   |
|  therefore: accumulation.                                 |
|       |                                                   |
|       v                                                   |
|  Capital formation: Accumulated wealth reinvested,        |
|  not consumed. The "spirit" of capitalism — not           |
|  greed but systematic rational accumulation as duty.      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
```

Weber's precise claim is not that Protestantism caused capitalism (capitalism existed
before Luther; Catholicism produced commercial innovation). The claim is "elective
affinity" (Wahlverwandtschaft) — a selective fit between a particular religious
ethic and a particular economic ethos. The Protestant ethic reinforced and accelerated
a development that had other causes.

Empirical critiques: Are Calvinist regions historically more capitalistic? The
evidence is mixed. Dutch Calvinism and Puritan England support Weber; French
Calvinist regions less so. Trevor-Roper argues Weber inverted the causal direction.
MacKinnon argues Weber misread Calvinist theology — Calvinists did NOT interpret
worldly success as a sign of election; that was a Puritan development.

### Rationalization and Disenchantment

Weber's broader thesis about modernity: the progressive rationalization of all
domains of life — replacing tradition and magic with calculation, systematization,
formal rules.

```
  "The fate of our times is characterized by rationalization and
  intellectualization and, above all, by the disenchantment
  (Entzauberung) of the world." — Weber, "Science as a Vocation" (1919)

  Disenchantment:  Magic, mystery, and transcendence are driven out.
                   The world becomes calculable, predictable, manageable.
                   No mysterious incalculable forces. Science and
                   bureaucracy replace priest and shaman.

  Bureaucratization: "The iron cage" — rational-legal authority produces
                   efficient organizations that constrain human freedom.
                   We are trapped in the structures we created.
```

Religion in rationalized modernity: either survives as a private meaning system
(subjective, not publicly authoritative) or gets absorbed into ethics and social
service while losing its transcendent dimension. Weber himself was not sure whether
disenchantment was permanent or whether charisma would break through.

### Three Types of Authority

| Type | Basis | Legitimation | Example |
|------|-------|-------------|---------|
| Charismatic | Personal qualities of leader | Leader's gifts, followers' recognition | Prophets, demagogues, revolutionary leaders |
| Traditional | Custom and precedent | "This is how it has always been done" | Monarchy, tribal elders, hereditary priesthood |
| Legal-rational | Rules and offices | Written rules, procedural correctness | Bureaucracy, representative government, corporations |

**Routinization of charisma**: Every charismatic movement, if it survives its founder's
death, must solve the succession problem. The solution always involves transferring
the charisma from person to office (priest as office-holder, not as personal saint).
This institutionalizes the movement and reduces intensity. The Catholic priesthood
represents extreme routinization; Pentecostalism attempts to maintain charismatic
intensity but also routinizes over time.

## Secularization — Rise, Fall, and Revision

### The Classic Thesis

The secularization thesis: modernization → social differentiation (religion loses
its role in politics, education, welfare) → pluralism (no one religious tradition
can claim public authority) → privatization (religion retreats to private sphere)
→ decline.

Strong versions (Bryan Wilson, Steve Bruce): religion loses social significance,
belief declines, churches empty. Weak version (Parsons): religion differentiates
and specializes, losing some functions but retaining others.

```
  Evidence FOR the thesis:
  European church attendance: UK, Germany, Netherlands, Scandinavia
  — dramatic declines in the second half of the 20th century.
  Elite secularity: scientists, academics, intellectuals are
  disproportionately secular.
  Institutional religion losing political authority in most
  Western democracies.
  Young cohort replacing old cohort at lower religiosity levels.
```

### The Collapse of the Classic Thesis

```
  Counter-evidence that accumulated from the 1980s onward:

  1. The United States: highly modern, highly religious.
     The American case directly refuted the simplest form
     of the thesis (modernization → secularization).

  2. Global South: Pentecostalism growing at ~35,000 new
     members per day (Barrett/Johnson estimates). Islamic
     renewal across the Middle East, Africa, Southeast Asia.
     The majority of the world's population lives in places
     where religion is not declining.

  3. "Believing without belonging" (Grace Davie, 1994):
     UK study showed institutional decline but persistent
     private belief. People leave churches but don't
     become atheists.

  4. "Spiritual but not religious": New Age, alternative
     spirituality, individualized spiritual practice growing
     alongside decline of institutional religion.

  5. Peter Berger's 2000 recantation:
     "The assumption that we live in a secularized world
     is false. The world today is as furiously religious
     as it ever was, and in some places more so than ever."
     He revised his position to: modernity produces pluralism,
     which produces uncertainty, which ironically can increase
     rather than decrease religious seeking.
```

### The Revised Picture

Norris and Inglehart ("Sacred and Secular," 2004): existential security thesis.
Secularization happens where material security is high (Scandinavia). Where
existential uncertainty is high (disease, poverty, political instability),
religion remains high. The US exception: high inequality creates insecurity
even in a wealthy nation, sustaining religiosity.

Steve Bruce defends the core thesis: institutional decline in the West is real
and continuing. The global South's religiosity will decline as it modernizes.
The thesis is about modernization, not about Europe specifically.

Current consensus (such as it is): Institutional religion is declining in
Western Europe, Australia, Canada, and among educated elites globally. Belief
and practice are more robust than institutional membership. The "Global South"
trajectory is uncertain. The secularization thesis in its strong form is refuted;
some weaker version may be defensible for specific contexts.

## Religious Markets Theory

Rodney Stark, Roger Finke, Laurence Iannaccone: supply-side theory of religion.

```
  Religious economies model:
  - Religious "consumers" have stable demand for religious goods
    (meaning, community, salvation assurance).
  - The supply side (number and quality of religious organizations)
    determines participation rates.
  - Monopoly → lazy church, poor product, low participation.
  - Competition → innovation, quality improvement, high participation.

  This explains the US-Europe gap:
  US: state-church separation from founding → competitive pluralism
  → vigorous, innovative religious organizations → high participation
  Europe: established state churches (CofE, Lutheran, Catholic)
  → monopoly or near-monopoly → low quality → low participation
```

The "strict church" thesis (Iannaccone): high-demand religious groups that
require costly commitment (no drinking, dress codes, tithing, dietary laws)
outcompete low-demand churches. Cost screens out free-riders and creates
high-commitment membership. Orthodox Judaism, Jehovah's Witnesses, and
Mormons outgrow mainline Protestants by being stricter, not more accommodating.

Critiques: Religious markets theory assumes consumer sovereignty that may not
apply (religious choice is heavily shaped by birth, culture, family). It may
describe American religion well and explain little about the rest of the world.
The "strict = successful" thesis has counterexamples.

## New Religious Movements (NRMs)

### Classification

Roy Wallis's typology (1984):
- World-rejecting NRMs: comprehensive rejection of conventional society
  (Peoples Temple, Children of God/The Family, ISKCON early phase)
- World-affirming NRMs: accept mainstream values, offer enhanced worldly success
  (Scientology, est/Landmark Forum, Silva Mind Control)
- World-accommodating NRMs: neither reject nor affirm the world, focus on
  religious experience (Charismatic Renewal, some Neo-Pagan groups)

### Conversion

Lofland and Stark's model (1965, based on Moonies): preconditions (background
factors, value conflicts) + predisposing factors (turning points, religious seeking)
+ situational factors (encounter with the group) + social bonds (affective ties
to members) + intensive interaction (full participation) → conversion.

Key finding: social bonds precede doctrinal acceptance. People join because
they like the people, not because they are convinced of the theology.
The theology comes after the social integration.

### The "Cult" Problem

"Cult" is a polemical term in common usage; scholars prefer "new religious movement."
The definitional issues:

```
  Folk usage of "cult":
  - Small, high-demand group
  - Charismatic leader
  - Thought control, exit barriers
  - Financial exploitation

  Sociological definition:
  - "Cult" = movement that is innovative (creates new doctrine)
    vs. "sect" (schism from existing tradition)
  - OR: "cult" is just the pejorative term for an NRM
    the speaker dislikes

  The problem: "cult" characteristics appear on a spectrum
  and are not unique to new movements. The Catholic Church
  has charismatic leaders, high exit barriers (excommunication,
  family pressure), required financial contributions.
  The difference is scale and normalization.
```

High-demand groups (Singer, Lifton's "thought reform" concept): Robert Lifton's
"Thought Reform and the Psychology of Totalism" (1961, based on Korean War
POW studies) identified eight criteria for thought-reform environments:
milieu control, mystical manipulation, demand for purity, confession,
sacred science, loading the language, doctrine over person, dispensing of existence.
Applied to NRMs (Lifton himself applied it to Unificationism and Scientology).
Critique: these criteria apply in degree to many institutional settings.

### Scientology as Case Study

L. Ron Hubbard founded Dianetics (1950) as a self-help psychology, then
reframed as a religion (Church of Scientology, 1953). Features making it
analytically interesting:
- Explicit "scripture" development by a living founder (unusual — most religions
  don't watch their scriptures being written)
- Graduated revelation structure (higher operating thetan levels reveal cosmological
  claims kept secret from lower members — Xenu narrative)
- Sophisticated use of tax exemption (IRS settlement, 1993) and intellectual property
  law to protect doctrine
- World-affirming theology: this-worldly success, not otherworldly salvation

## Fundamentalism

Martin Marty and Scott Appleby's "Fundamentalism Project" (5 vols, 1991-1995):
the most comprehensive comparative study. Key finding: fundamentalism is not
a primitive survival but a modern reaction to modernity.

```
  Fundamentalism as modern phenomenon:
  - Requires modernity as target to react against
  - Uses modern media, organization, political processes
  - Is not the same as "traditional religion"
    (traditional Amish are not fundamentalists; they withdrew
     rather than fighting back)
  - Is typically led by educated people, not the uneducated

  Five family resemblances across traditions:
  1. Selectivity: picks specific elements of tradition to assert absolutely
  2. Dualism: world sharply divided into good/evil, saved/damned
  3. Absolutism: chosen doctrines are non-negotiable
  4. Millennialism/messianism: expectation of eschatological transformation
  5. Chosen people: group identity as elect/remnant against corrupt world
```

Major varieties:

| Variety | Tradition | Key features | Key groups |
|---------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Protestant fundamentalism | Christianity | Biblical inerrancy, premillennialism, culture war | Jerry Falwell's Moral Majority, Christian nationalism |
| Jewish fundamentalism | Judaism | Land of Israel as sacred territory, Halakha as political law | Gush Emunim, Haredi anti-Zionist varieties |
| Islamic fundamentalism | Islam | Sharia as complete social order, rejection of Western influence | Muslim Brotherhood, Salafi movements, political Islam |
| Hindu fundamentalism | Hinduism | Hindu nation (Hindutva), history of Muslim oppression, cultural purity | RSS, BJP-affiliated organizations, VHP |
| Sikh fundamentalism | Sikhism | Khalistan as independent Sikh state | Babbar Khalsa, KCF |

## Civil Religion

Robert Bellah's "Civil Religion in America" (1967): America has a transcendent
self-understanding separate from any particular religious denomination — a
"civil religion" with its own sacred symbols, myths, rituals, and prophets.

```
  Sacred texts: Declaration of Independence, Constitution
  Sacred narratives: Revolution as founding act, Civil War as sacrifice
                     and rebirth, WWII as struggle against evil
  Sacred spaces: Arlington Cemetery, Gettysburg, Washington Mall
  Sacred calendar: Thanksgiving, Memorial Day, Independence Day
  Prophets: Washington (Moses), Lincoln (Christ-figure), MLK
  Creed: "all men are created equal... endowed by their Creator"
```

Civil religion is not Christianity but draws heavily on biblical typology.
It provides a shared framework for public life in a pluralistic society —
shared enough to be invoked, vague enough not to exclude.

Critique: Civil religion can legitimate state power and war as much as critique
them. Bellah himself later argued it had declined into "broken covenant" —
the transcendent values had been abandoned for raw nationalism.

Parallel in corporate culture: large organizations develop "culture" that
functions as civil religion — origin myths (the founder's garage), sacred
texts (Jeff Bezos's shareholder letters, the HP Way), ritual practices
(all-hands meetings, onboarding), heroes and villains, heresy (violating
core values). This is not metaphorical — the functional structure is identical.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is Durkheim's explanation of religion? | Society worshipping itself. Religious beliefs are symbolically condensed representations of the social group's power. Collective effervescence generates the sense of the sacred. |
| What is Weber's Protestant Ethic argument? | Calvinist anxiety about election → work as sign of election → systematic discipline → capital accumulation. Elective affinity with capitalism's spirit, not causal claim. |
| What is secularization? Has it happened? | Modernization leading to religion's decline. Classic thesis: partly supported for Western Europe, refuted globally. The world is not secularizing uniformly. |
| What is the religious markets theory? | Supply-side: religious pluralism and competition produce high participation; monopoly produces low participation. Explains US vs. Europe gap. |
| What is a "cult" sociologically? | A new religious movement that creates genuinely new doctrine (vs. a sect, which schisms from existing tradition). "Cult" in common usage is pejorative and analytically imprecise. |
| What is civil religion? | A society's transcendent self-understanding — sacred symbols, myths, and rituals that transcend any particular denomination and provide shared public framework. |
| What makes fundamentalism modern? | It requires modernity as target, uses modern media and organization, and is driven by educated people reacting to perceived threats, not by uneducated backwardness. |

## Common Confusion Points

**Weber didn't say Protestants invented capitalism**: The argument is about a
selective affinity between Calvinist theology and the ethos of rational accumulation.
Capitalism existed in Catholic Italy, Jewish communities, and Chinese networks.
The Protestant Ethic accelerated and shaped a development with multiple causes.

**Secularization ≠ atheism**: People leaving institutional religion are not mostly
becoming atheists. In the UK, the growth category is "spiritual but not religious,"
not "atheist." Belief in something supernatural remains high even where church
attendance has collapsed.

**"NRM" is not a compliment**: The shift from "cult" to "NRM" in academic
language was a deliberate attempt at neutral terminology. It does not mean
scholars have concluded that NRMs are harmless. Lifton's thought-reform criteria
can still apply; high-demand groups can still cause harm. The terminology is
about analytical precision, not endorsement.

**Collective effervescence is not mysticism**: Durkheim's concept is empirical:
crowd ritual generates real emotional energy that people experience as coming
from outside themselves. This doesn't require any metaphysical commitment.
Collins's extension to interaction ritual chains makes it fully sociological.
