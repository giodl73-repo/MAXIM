# Dharmic Religions — Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, Sikhism

## The Big Picture

"Dharmic religions" is a modern scholarly grouping for traditions originating in the
Indian subcontinent that share family resemblance concepts: karma (action and its
consequences), samsara (cycle of rebirth), dharma (cosmic/moral order, duty), and
some form of liberation as the soteriological goal. They are NOT a unified tradition
and have significant metaphysical disagreements — Buddhism explicitly rejected the
Hindu Atman concept; Jainism rejected the Vedic tradition entirely.

## Hinduism

### What Is Hinduism?

"Hinduism" is a 19th-century European category for an enormously diverse family
of Indian religious traditions. There is no single founder, no single creed, no
single scripture, no single authority structure. It is better understood as a
family of traditions united by shared textual heritage, ritual forms, concepts,
and broad geographic origin.

### The Upanishadic Turn — Core Metaphysics

The Upanishads are a philosophical revolution within the Vedic tradition: away from
ritual sacrifice toward interior knowledge and metaphysical inquiry.

Key equation: **Brahman = Atman**

```
  Brahman:   The ultimate reality, ground of all being.
             Not a personal god (though personal gods exist as
             manifestations). Impersonal, infinite, beyond predication.

  Atman:     The innermost self, the witnessing consciousness within.
             Not the ego, not the personality, not the body.
             The pure awareness behind all experience.

  The equation "tat tvam asi" (that thou art):
  Your deepest self IS the ultimate reality.
  Liberation (moksha) is realizing this identity —
  not achieving it, but recognizing what already is.
```

### Six Orthodox Darshanas (Philosophical Schools)

"Orthodox" here means accepting Vedic authority (sruti as valid pramana — source of knowledge).
All six are considered valid Hindu philosophical systems despite radical disagreements.

| School | Founder text | Metaphysics | Key claim |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Samkhya** | Samkhya Karika | Dualist: Purusha (pure consciousness) + Prakriti (matter/nature, 3 gunas) | No creator God; liberation = Purusha recognizing it is not Prakriti |
| **Yoga** | Patanjali's Yoga Sutras (~2nd c BCE) | Accepts Samkhya metaphysics; adds Ishvara (God) as special Purusha | Eight limbs (ashtanga): yama, niyama, asana, pranayama, pratyahara, dharana, dhyana, samadhi |
| **Nyaya** | Gautama's Nyaya Sutras | Realist; sophisticated epistemology | Four pramanas (valid knowledge sources): perception, inference, comparison, testimony |
| **Vaisheshika** | Kanada's Vaisheshika Sutras | Atomistic pluralism — reality composed of eternal atoms | Comparable to ancient Greek atomism — independently arrived at |
| **Mimamsa** | Jaimini's Mimamsa Sutras | Realist; primary interest in Vedic ritual interpretation | Vedic injunctions are eternal, self-sufficient; dharma is Vedic duty |
| **Vedanta** | Brahma Sutras (Badarayana) | Interpretation of Upanishads; several schools | Central dispute: nature of Brahman-Atman relationship |

Vedanta sub-schools (most important):
- **Advaita** (Shankara, 8th c CE): Non-dualism. Brahman alone is real; the individual
  self (jiva) and the world are appearances (maya). Liberation = knowledge that Atman = Brahman.
  Arguably the most philosophically influential. Analogous to absolute idealism.
- **Vishishtadvaita** (Ramanuja, 11th c): Qualified non-dualism. God (Vishnu/Brahman) is
  real; souls and world are real but are the body of God. Bhakti (devotion) as path.
- **Dvaita** (Madhva, 13th c): Dualism. God (Vishnu) is categorically different from souls
  and world. Five eternal distinctions (panchabheda). No identity possible.

### Popular Religion: Bhakti and the Sectarian Traditions

The philosophical schools are elite traditions. Popular Hinduism is dominated by
bhakti (devotion to a personal deity) and structured around three major sectarian traditions:

The bhakti movement (6th-17th centuries): devotional poetry in vernacular languages,
accessible to all castes including women and Dalits. The Alvars (Vaishnava) and
Nayanmars (Shaiva) in Tamil Nadu. Tulsidas, Kabir, Mirabai. Bhakti democratized
access to the sacred, circumventing Brahmin mediation.

### Caste System

Varna system (four categories, theoretically based on function):
- Brahmin (priests, scholars)
- Kshatriya (warriors, rulers)
- Vaishya (merchants, farmers)
- Shudra (servants)
- Outside the system: "Untouchables" (now: Dalits) — performing ritually polluting work

Jati (actual social group by birth): thousands of sub-castes defined by hereditary
occupation, endogamy (marry within jati), and rules of commensality (who you can
eat with). This is the lived reality of caste — far more complex than the four varnas.

Textual justification: Manu Smriti and the Purusha Sukta (Rig Veda 10.90 — cosmos
created from the body of the primordial being, castes from different body parts).
Reform: Bhimrao Ambedkar (1891-1956) — Dalit leader, principal drafter of India's
constitution, converted to Buddhism in 1956 with half a million followers. Gandhi
(advocated for "Harijans" but did not oppose the varna system itself) vs. Ambedkar
(opposed the entire system) — a live political and theological dispute.

## Buddhism

### Siddhartha's Context and Departure

Siddhartha Gautama (~480-400 BCE, dates contested): born into Kshatriya family in
Lumbini (present-day Nepal). The founding narrative is a teaching device: sheltered
upbringing → encounters with old age, sickness, death, and a wandering ascetic →
departure from palace → six years of asceticism (rejecting it as unproductive) →
the Middle Way → enlightenment at Bodh Gaya under the Bodhi tree → teaching for
45 years → death (parinirvana) at Kushinagar.

The Buddha is not a god in early Buddhism. He is one who has woken up (buddha = awakened).
Anyone can become enlightened.

### The Core Teaching

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|               FOUR NOBLE TRUTHS (Ariya Sacca)                    |
|                                                                  |
|  1. DUKKHA (suffering/unsatisfactoriness):                       |
|     All conditioned existence involves dukkha.                   |
|     Not just pain — the fundamental unsatisfactoriness           |
|     of impermanent, dependent phenomena.                         |
|                                                                  |
|  2. SAMUDAYA (arising/origin):                                   |
|     Dukkha arises from tanha (craving/thirst) —                  |
|     craving for sensual pleasure, existence, non-existence.      |
|     The Second Noble Truth is a causal claim.                    |
|                                                                  |
|  3. NIRODHA (cessation):                                         |
|     Dukkha can cease. Nirvana is the cessation of craving.       |
|     Not annihilation — cessation of the fires of greed,          |
|     hatred, delusion.                                            |
|                                                                  |
|  4. MAGGA (path):                                                |
|     The Noble Eightfold Path leads to that cessation.            |
|     Right View, Right Intention, Right Speech, Right             |
|     Action, Right Livelihood, Right Effort, Right                |
|     Mindfulness, Right Concentration.                            |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

### Three Marks of Existence (Tilakkhana)

- **Anicca** (impermanence): All conditioned phenomena are impermanent. This includes
  mental states, physical objects, and the self.
- **Dukkha** (unsatisfactoriness): Impermanent things cannot provide lasting satisfaction.
- **Anatta** (no-self): There is no permanent, unchanging self or soul. The "self" is
  a conventional designation for a stream of interdependent processes.

Anatta is the decisive break from Upanishadic Hinduism. The Hindu Atman (permanent,
unchanging self = Brahman) is precisely what the Buddha denied. The philosophical
dispute between Buddhist anatta and Hindu Atman persisted for centuries.

### Dependent Origination (Pratityasamutpada)

The 12-link chain of dependent origination: ignorance → mental formations → consciousness
→ name-and-form → six sense bases → contact → feeling → craving → clinging →
becoming → birth → aging-and-death (and suffering). Each link conditions the next.
Break any link — particularly ignorance or craving — and the chain stops.

This is both a description of how suffering arises and a formal causal theory
of rebirth. It is also the middle path between "everything exists" (eternalism,
which would require a permanent self) and "nothing exists" (nihilism).

## Jainism

Mahavira (Vardhamana, ~599-527 BCE) is the last of 24 tirthankaras (ford-makers —
those who have crossed samsara and show the way). Contemporary of the Buddha;
similar ascetic context. But radically different conclusions.

### Core Metaphysics

- **Jiva** (soul/living substance): each individual soul is eternal, omniscient in its
  liberated state. There is no cosmic soul (no Brahman); each jiva is independent.
- **Ajiva** (non-living substances): matter, space, time, motion, rest.
- **Karma** (in Jainism): physical matter that adheres to the soul as a result of
  actions. Not just causal consequence (as in Hindu/Buddhist karma) — literally
  a substance that weighs down the soul and keeps it in samsara.
- Liberation (moksha): shed all karma through right knowledge, right faith, right
  conduct. The liberated soul (siddha) rises to the top of the universe and remains
  there in omniscient bliss. No rebirth, no interaction with world.

### Key Doctrines

- **Ahimsa** (non-violence): the central ethical principle. Extends to all life —
  monks filter water, sweep the ground before walking, cover mouth (muhapatti)
  to avoid inhaling insects. Extreme ascetic monks (Digambara) go naked.
  Gandhi's philosophy of nonviolence drew directly from Jain influences.
- **Anekantavada** (many-sidedness of truth / non-one-sidedness): any complex
  reality cannot be fully described from a single standpoint. Every proposition
  is true in some respect and false in others. Epistemological pluralism and
  humility. The syadvada (doctrine of maybe/conditional predication): every
  assertion should be prefaced with "in some respects" (syat).
  Compare: in formal terms, this is similar to multi-valued logic or the
  recognition that all models are incomplete.

### Two Sects

- **Digambara** ("sky-clad"): monks wear no clothing; liberation possible only for
  male monks. More austere.
- **Shvetambara** ("white-clad"): monks wear white; women can achieve liberation.
  Hold that Mahavira was married before renunciation.

Small community (~5 million, concentrated in Gujarat/Rajasthan) with disproportionate
influence: high literacy, mercantile success, and philosophical sophistication.

## Sikhism

Founded by Guru Nanak (1469-1539 CE) in Punjab, northwestern India. Sikhism is a
distinct tradition — not a "synthesis" of Hinduism and Islam (a common
misconception it explicitly rejects).

### The Ten Gurus and Guru Granth Sahib

```
  Guru Nanak (1469-1539) -- first Guru, founder
  Guru Angad (1504-1552)
  Guru Amar Das (1479-1574)
  Guru Ram Das (1534-1581) -- founded Amritsar
  Guru Arjan (1563-1606) -- compiled Adi Granth; martyred by Mughal emperor
  Guru Hargobind (1595-1644) -- began militarization (miri-piri: temporal/spiritual)
  Guru Har Rai (1630-1661)
  Guru Har Krishan (1656-1664)
  Guru Tegh Bahadur (1621-1675) -- martyred defending Hindu rights
  Guru Gobind Singh (1666-1708) -- tenth and last human Guru
       |
       v
  Guru Granth Sahib -- the eternal, living Guru
  The scripture itself became the Guru in 1708.
  Treated as a living presence: installed on a throne (palki),
  put to "sleep" at night, read continuously (akhand path).
```

### Core Theology

- **Ik Onkar** ("One Creator"): radical monotheism. God (Waheguru) is One, formless,
  beyond the gods of Hindu polytheism, creator of all.
- **Naam simran** (remembrance of the Name): continuous meditation on God's name
  as the path to liberation. No pilgrimages, no rituals, no caste as necessary.
- **Sewa** (selfless service): service to others as worship.
- **Langar**: community kitchen serving free meals to all regardless of caste, religion,
  gender, or status. Institutional critique of caste discrimination.
- **Khalsa**: the order of initiated Sikhs founded by Guru Gobind Singh (1699).
  Five K's (panj kakars): kesh (uncut hair), kara (steel bracelet), kachha (short
  breeches), kanga (wooden comb), kirpan (steel sword).

## Shared Concepts — Comparison Across Traditions

| Concept | Hinduism | Buddhism | Jainism | Sikhism |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Karma | Moral law, shapes future rebirth; cosmic | Intentional action shaping rebirth; mental | Physical substance adhering to jiva | Action creating bondage; transcended through grace |
| Rebirth | Yes (Atman transmigrates) | Yes (no permanent self — stream of consciousness) | Yes (jiva transmigrates) | Yes, but liberation through Guru's grace |
| Liberation goal | Moksha: merging with Brahman / returning to God | Nirvana: cessation of craving; no more rebirth | Moksha: liberated jiva at universe's top | Mukti: union with God |
| Self | Atman (eternal, identical to Brahman) | Anatta (no permanent self) | Jiva (eternal individual soul) | Atma (part of God) |
| Creator God | Varies (Advaita: Brahman not personal; Vaishnavism: Vishnu creates) | No creator God (dependent origination) | No creator God (each jiva independent) | Yes — Waheguru, the one formless Creator |
| Authority | Vedas (for orthodox); varies | Buddha's teachings (Dhamma) | Mahavira's teaching; Agamas | Guru Granth Sahib |

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is Brahman? | Ultimate reality — impersonal ground of all being. Not a personal god per se, though it can be approached as one. |
| What is the Atman? | The deepest self, pure witnessing consciousness. In Advaita Vedanta, identical to Brahman. |
| Why is anatta a big deal? | Because it contradicts the Hindu Atman. No permanent self = no soul to be reborn = karma has to work through causal continuity, not soul-transfer. |
| What is sunyata (emptiness)? | Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka: all phenomena are empty of inherent (svabhava) existence. They exist only in dependence on other things. Not nihilism — the relative level remains. |
| What is the Bhagavad Gita about? | Krishna's teaching to Arjuna on the battlefield: act according to your dharma without attachment to results (karma yoga), recognize Krishna as the ultimate (bhakti yoga), pursue knowledge (jnana yoga). |
| How many types of yoga? | The word means "union" or "discipline." Main paths: karma yoga (action), jnana yoga (knowledge), bhakti yoga (devotion), raja yoga (meditation, Patanjali's system). Modern Western "yoga" is asana practice — the third of Patanjali's eight limbs. |
| What is the Dalai Lama? | The Tibetan tradition recognizes him as the reincarnation of Avalokiteshvara (bodhisattva of compassion). Temporal and spiritual leader of Tibetan Buddhism. The current (14th) Dalai Lama is Tenzin Gyatso, born 1935. |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Hinduism is polytheist"**: Sophisticated Hindu philosophy is not simply polytheist.
Advaita Vedanta holds that all the gods (devas) are manifestations of the one Brahman.
The plurality of gods is compatible with ultimate non-dualism. Correct description:
Hinduism includes polytheistic practice (temple worship of multiple deities), but its
philosophical traditions are often monist or qualified monist.

**"Buddhism is atheist"**: Early Buddhism neither affirmed nor denied gods — gods exist
in the cosmology, but they are also trapped in samsara. The relevant question is not
"does God exist?" but "what causes suffering and how do you end it?" Treating it as
atheism applies the wrong question.

**"Meditation = Buddhism"**: Meditation practices exist across Hinduism (yoga), Jainism,
Sikhism, and Daoism. The secular "mindfulness" tradition derives primarily from
Theravada Buddhist vipassana, but meditation is not unique to Buddhism.

**"Karma = fate"**: Karma is not predestination. In all these traditions, karma is
something you actively generate through present choices. The point of the teaching
is that present action shapes future outcomes — it is a doctrine of responsibility,
not fatalism.

**Theravada vs. Mahayana as lesser/greater**: "Hinayana" (lesser vehicle) was a
polemical Mahayana label for Theravada. Theravada scholars reject it. The traditions
differ in doctrine and emphasis but neither is superior; they preserved different
aspects of early Buddhism.
