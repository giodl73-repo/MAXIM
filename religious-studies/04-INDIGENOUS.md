# Indigenous and Folk Religions

## The Big Picture

"Indigenous religion" and "animism" are colonial-era scholarly categories that flatten
enormous diversity. What gets grouped here: oral-tradition-based, place-specific, non-
institutional religious practices that exist without separation of "religion" from
medicine, law, ecology, and social order. The category itself is contested.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|              THE PROBLEM WITH THE CATEGORY                           |
|                                                                      |
|  Who gets called "indigenous religion"?                              |
|  - Australian Aboriginal traditions (~500 distinct language groups)  |
|  - North and South American indigenous traditions (~1000+ groups)   |
|  - African traditional religions (~1000+ ethnic traditions)          |
|  - Pacific traditions (Melanesia, Polynesia, Micronesia)             |
|  - Siberian and Central Asian shamanic traditions                    |
|  - What these have in common is what was done TO them                |
|    (colonialism, documentation by outsiders, marginalization)        |
|    not necessarily what they share in structure                      |
|                                                                      |
|  The category problem:                                               |
|  "Religion" as a separate domain of life is itself a                 |
|  Western, Protestant-inflected concept. Most of the                  |
|  traditions grouped here do not separate "religion"                  |
|  from ecology, medicine, law, kinship, or politics.                  |
|  Calling them "religions" already distorts.                          |
|                                                                      |
|  What to do with this:                                               |
|  Study specific traditions in their specificity.                     |
|  Use comparative categories as analytical tools,                     |
|  not as descriptions of what practitioners believe.                  |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

Key scholars: Edward Tylor (animism theory, 1871), Mircea Eliade (shamanism theory,
1951), Claude Lévi-Strauss (totemism critique, 1962), Victor Turner (ritual process),
Clifford Geertz (thick description), Graham Harvey (new animism, 2005),
Eduardo Viveiros de Castro (Amazonian perspectivism, 1998),
Alice Kehoe (shamanism critique, 1990).

## Animism

### Tylor's Original Formulation

Edward Burnett Tylor defined animism as "belief in spiritual beings" and proposed
it as the minimum definition of religion — the irreducible core present in all
religious traditions.

His evolutionary claim: animism is the earliest, most primitive form of religion.
The reasoning: early humans, observing dreams and death, developed the concept
of a separable soul (the dream-self that travels; the animating principle that
leaves at death). This soul-concept extended to animals, plants, and natural phenomena
generally: everything has a spirit. Later evolution consolidated these spirits
into named gods (polytheism) and eventually into one God (monotheism).

Problems:
- Teleological: implies monotheism is the goal of religious evolution
- Psychological: the "primitive reasoning" story is speculative and ethnocentric
- Empirical: "animism" covers radically different beliefs (the Inuit relationship
  to animal spirits is nothing like Shinto kami, which is nothing like
  Amazonian perspectivism)

### Harvey's New Animism

Graham Harvey's "Animism: Respecting the Living World" (2005) reframes the concept
entirely: not "belief in spirits" (a psychological claim about what primitives
mistakenly think) but a relational ontology — a way of engaging with the world
in which other-than-human entities are treated as persons.

```
  Old animism (Tylor):
  "Primitive people believe rocks/trees have spirits."
  -> Animism as false belief needing explanation

  New animism (Harvey):
  "Some cultures engage with rocks/trees as persons."
  -> Animism as relational practice, not belief
  -> The relevant question is not: do rocks have spirits?
     but: what kind of relationships are appropriate
          with the entities in your world?
```

This shift has significant philosophical implications. It moves from epistemology
(what do they believe?) to ontology (what kinds of things are persons?). Compare:
Viveiros de Castro's point that Amazonian perspectivism is not a system of beliefs
about nature but a different description of what nature is.

### Amazonian Perspectivism (Viveiros de Castro)

Eduardo Viveiros de Castro's research on Araweté, Tupinamba, and other Amazonian
peoples: they hold a perspectivism that is not metaphor.

```
  Western assumption (multiculturalism):
  One nature, multiple cultures.
  All humans see the same world differently.

  Amazonian perspectivism (multinaturalism):
  Multiple natures, one culture.
  All beings (human, jaguar, river spirit) share the same
  "culture" (social relations, moral world, intentionality)
  but see different worlds.

  A jaguar sees itself as a human going to hunt.
  It sees the blood it drinks as manioc beer.
  Humans see it as a jaguar drinking blood.

  Neither perspective is the "real" one — both are real,
  from their respective standpoint.
```

This is not "symbolic" or "metaphorical" thinking — it is a different ontological
commitment. The category "nature" (a uniform, objective world) is a Western assumption
that this framework denies.

## Shamanism

### Eliade's Universalist Model

Mircea Eliade's "Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy" (1951) proposed a
universal shamanic complex across Siberia, Central Asia, the Americas, and beyond.

```
  ELIADE'S SHAMANIC COMPLEX:
  - Calling/initiation: chosen by spirits, often through illness
  - Ecstatic techniques: drumming, chanting, plant medicines, fasting
  - Soul flight: the shaman's spirit travels to other worlds
  - Vertical cosmos: three worlds (upper/middle/lower), axis mundi
  - Retrieval: finding lost souls, negotiating with spirits
  - Healing function: bringing back what was taken
```

Eliade's method: phenomenological synthesis — find the common pattern across cultures.
His result: shamanism as a universal "archaic technique of ecstasy."

### The Critique of Eliade's Shamanism

Alice Kehoe (1990, "Primal Gaia: Primitivists and Plastic Medicine Men"):
and Roberte Hamayon (1990, 1993) argue that Eliade's shamanism is a scholarly
construction, not a real cross-cultural category.

Problems:
- Siberian "shamanism" (the model for Eliade) is itself diverse — the Evenki
  shaman, the Buryat shaman, the Korean mudang are not interchangeable
- "Shaman" is a specific Tungusic term (saman) that Eliade universalized to cover
  every spiritual specialist in every tradition
- Eliade's sources were largely secondary: travelers' accounts, colonial reports
- The "ecstasy" model (soul-flight) doesn't characterize all shamanic traditions —
  some involve spirit possession (spirit enters practitioner, not practitioner
  traveling out), which is different
- The model was built partly to support Eliade's broader thesis (axis mundi,
  primordial sacred time) rather than from direct fieldwork

What is defensible: specialist practitioners who mediate between ordinary and
extraordinary worlds (spirits, disease-causing agents, ancestors) exist in many
cultures. They use altered states. They serve healing functions. This is
real cross-culturally. The question is how much the similarities reveal shared
structure vs. how much they are artifacts of the analytical category.

### Neurological Substrate

Michael Winkelman's work (shamanism and the evolution of consciousness):
altered states of consciousness used in shamanic practice have identifiable
neurological correlates.

```
  Mechanisms that produce altered states in shamanic practice:
  - Rhythmic drumming (~4-7 Hz, theta wave induction)
  - Psychoactive plants (psilocybin, ayahuasca, peyote)
  - Sensory deprivation (fasting, darkness)
  - Sleep deprivation
  - Intense physical movement/dance
  - Extreme cold, hyperventilation

  Common neurological result: serotonergic disruption,
  default mode network suppression, increased connectivity
  between normally separate neural systems.
  Phenomenologically: dissolution of self-boundaries,
  visual/auditory hallucinations, sense of presence/encounter.

  Winkelman's argument: these states are adaptive for
  social bonding, creative problem-solving, and healing
  (which has significant psychosomatic components).
  "Shamanistic states of consciousness" are a real category
  at the neural level, even if the cultural frameworks
  interpreting them vary enormously.
```

## Totemism

### Durkheim's Totemism

Durkheim proposed totemic religion as the most elementary form of religion. The clan
totem (an animal or plant emblem) simultaneously represented the clan and the sacred
force (mana) — therefore god = society. This was supposed to explain the origin of
both religion and social organization.

### Lévi-Strauss's Demolition

Claude Lévi-Strauss, "Totemism" (1962): totemism is not a unified phenomenon.
The diverse practices grouped under "totemism" share no essential common structure —
they are similar only because they have been extracted from their contexts and
grouped together by 19th-century scholars.

Lévi-Strauss's positive claim: animals are chosen as totems not because of any
intrinsic sacred quality but because they are "good to think" (bons à penser)
rather than merely "good to eat" (bons à manger). Totemic classification uses
natural species differences as a code for expressing social differences.
The logic is structural: the difference between Eagle clan and Bear clan
mirrors the difference between these two groups.

```
  The structural logic:
  Eagle : Bear :: Clan A : Clan B

  Animals are not symbols of clans — they are a system of differences
  used to code a system of social differences.
  The natural world provides a rich vocabulary of differences
  for thinking social distinctions.
```

This is Lévi-Strauss's broader structuralist method applied to religion:
myths and religious classifications are intellectual operations,
systems of thought using natural categories to work out social and cosmological
problems.

## Sacred Geography

Place-based spirituality is one of the most consistent features across indigenous
traditions: specific places are held to be sacred, powerful, and religiously
significant in ways that cannot be transferred.

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
|              FORMS OF SACRED GEOGRAPHY                    |
|                                                           |
|  Sacred mountains:                                        |
|  Mt. Fuji (Japan), Black Hills (Lakota), Uluru           |
|  (Anangu, Australia), Mt. Kailash (Hindu/Buddhist/Jain), |
|  Mt. Olympus (Greek). Sacred not because of height       |
|  but because of presence — what happened there,          |
|  what dwells there.                                       |
|                                                           |
|  Sacred rivers:                                           |
|  Ganges (India) — the goddess Ganga, purification.       |
|  Missouri/Mississippi (various nations) — relationships, |
|  not just geography.                                      |
|                                                           |
|  Songlines (Australian Aboriginal):                       |
|  Invisible pathways across the landscape corresponding    |
|  to routes traveled by Ancestors during the Dreamtime.   |
|  Singing (chanting) the song of a songline while          |
|  traveling it re-enacts and maintains the creation.       |
|  Not historical past — ontological foundation.            |
|  The Dreamtime is always happening.                       |
|  Bruce Chatwin's "The Songlines" (1987) is a            |
|  useful if imperfect introduction.                        |
|                                                           |
|  Land rights as religious rights:                         |
|  For traditions with place-specific sacred geographies,  |
|  the loss of ancestral territory is not just political   |
|  dispossession — it destroys the conditions for          |
|  religious practice. This is why UNDRIP (UN Declaration  |
|  on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, 2007) frames       |
|  land rights and religious rights together.              |
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Cargo Cults

Cargo cults are Melanesian religious movements arising in the late 19th through
20th centuries, typically in response to contact with Western material wealth.

The structural pattern: colonial contact revealed enormous disparity in material
wealth. Melanesians observed that Europeans received vast quantities of goods
("cargo") — but could not understand the actual mechanisms of production and
distribution. The religious response: if the correct rituals were performed,
the cargo (material goods) would arrive for the indigenous people as well.

Examples:
- John Frum movement (Tanna, Vanuatu): John Frum (possibly a pidgin corruption
  of "John from America") will return with abundance. Build airstrips to attract
  cargo planes. Still active.
- Prince Philip Movement (Tanna): Prince Philip of Britain was a divine being.
  After Philip's death (2021), movement continues with his son.
- "Vailala Madness" (Papua New Guinea, 1919): mass trance states, abandonment
  of traditional ceremonies, construction of "radio masts" to receive messages
  from the dead.

Worsley's analysis ("The Trumpet Shall Sound," 1957): cargo cults are not
irrational. They are structurally rational responses to a situation of colonial
inequality where the actual mechanisms of wealth production are hidden. They use
available religious resources to explain and respond to a new, bewildering situation.

The broader point (Festinger's cognitive dissonance, prophecy failure):
When cargo doesn't arrive, most movements don't collapse — they reinterpret
the failure (wrong ritual details, not enough faith, next year, etc.).
This is a general feature of failed prophecy, not specific to cargo cults.

## Revitalization Movements

Anthony F.C. Wallace's theory of revitalization movements (1956): a deliberate,
organized effort to construct a more satisfying culture when the existing culture
fails to meet people's needs under conditions of stress.

General pattern:
```
  Cultural stress (colonialism, disease, dispossession)
       |
       v
  Mazeway resynthesis: a visionary reformulates the world picture
  (often through vision, dream, or altered state)
       |
       v
  New synthesis: elements of traditional culture + new elements
  (sometimes including borrowed elements from the colonizing culture)
       |
       v
  Movement: prophet gathers followers, creates rituals, community
       |
       v
  Either: routinization (becomes stable institution)
       or: failure/collapse
```

Major examples:

**The Ghost Dance (1890)**: Wovoka (Jack Wilson), a Paiute holy man in Nevada,
received a vision: if Indians performed the Ghost Dance (a round dance with songs),
the whites would disappear, the buffalo would return, and the dead ancestors
would rise. The dance spread rapidly across the Plains nations during a period
of catastrophic dispossession and reservation confinement. The U.S. government,
alarmed by the movement, sent troops to suppress it. The massacre at Wounded Knee
(December 29, 1890, ~250-300 Lakota killed) ended the movement's active phase.

**Handsome Lake religion (Gaiwi'io, "Good Message")**: Seneca prophet Handsome Lake
(1735-1815) received visions after a period of alcoholism and cultural collapse
following the American Revolution. His synthesis: traditional Iroquois spiritual
practices + selective adoption of Quaker farming practices + temperance + adapted
moral code. The Longhouse Religion remains a living tradition among Haudenosaunee
(Iroquois) people today.

**Rastafarianism**: Arising in 1930s Jamaica in response to poverty and the
dispossession of African diaspora peoples. Key elements: Haile Selassie I (Emperor
of Ethiopia, 1930-1974) as divine figure/returned messiah; repatriation to Africa
(Zion); rejection of "Babylon" (colonial/capitalist order); use of cannabis as
sacrament; Afrocentric reinterpretation of the Bible; reggae music as propagation.
Not a uniform movement — multiple "mansions" (houses) with different practices.

## Ancestor Veneration

One of the most widespread practices across indigenous, folk, and even world religions.
The dead remain morally and socially present; the relationship between living and dead
must be maintained.

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
|              FORMS OF ANCESTOR VENERATION                 |
|                                                           |
|  Chinese: Home ancestor altars, incense offerings,        |
|  ancestral tablets with the deceased's name.              |
|  Qingming: annual grave sweeping. Ghost Month            |
|  (7th lunar month): the hungry dead need feeding.        |
|  Paper money and goods burned for the dead to use.        |
|                                                           |
|  Japanese: Butsudan (Buddhist altar) in the home,         |
|  tablets for the dead. Obon festival (mid-August):        |
|  the dead return, are welcomed and seen off.              |
|  Bon Odori dance. Lanterns on water to guide the dead.    |
|                                                           |
|  African traditional: Varies enormously.                  |
|  Common: the dead maintain relationships with kin;        |
|  can influence living outcomes. Offerings, libations,     |
|  consultation through divination or specialists.          |
|                                                           |
|  Mesoamerican: Day of the Dead (Dia de los Muertos)       |
|  in contemporary Mexico — blend of pre-Columbian and      |
|  Catholic practice. The dead return to visit; elaborate   |
|  ofrendas (altars with food, photos, marigolds).          |
+-----------------------------------------------------------+
```

Not the same as "worship" of ancestors — the distinction between veneration (showing
respect, maintaining relationship) and worship (treating as divine) matters, though
it can be blurry in practice.

## Legal and Rights Framework

The legal status of indigenous religious practice has been fought across multiple
dimensions:

- **Peyote exemptions**: The Native American Church uses peyote (mescaline-containing
  cactus) as a sacrament. Employment Division v. Smith (1990, U.S. Supreme Court)
  — Oregon could deny unemployment benefits to workers fired for peyote use in
  religious ceremony. American Indian Religious Freedom Act Amendments (1994)
  created federal exemption for NAC members.

- **NAGPRA (Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990)**: Requires
  U.S. federal agencies and institutions receiving federal funds to return Native
  American cultural items (human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects) to
  lineal descendants and tribal organizations. Landmark for recognizing
  that museum collections contained sacred and human materials requiring repatriation.
  Kennewick Man/Ancient One: 9,000-year-old skeleton — decade-long legal dispute
  between scientists wanting to study it and tribes claiming it for burial.

- **Sacred site protection**: No comprehensive federal protection in the U.S.
  Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association (1988): the government
  can build a road through a sacred site even if it destroys the religion practiced
  there. The First Amendment protects belief but not practice that requires specific
  land. Dakota Access Pipeline controversy: Standing Rock Sioux's sacred site/water
  protection claims vs. pipeline construction.

- **UNDRIP (UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples, 2007)**: Article 12
  recognizes the right to manifest, practice, develop, and teach spiritual and
  religious traditions, customs, and ceremonies; the right to maintain, protect,
  and have access in privacy to their religious and cultural sites. Not legally
  binding — a declaration, not a treaty. U.S., Canada, Australia, New Zealand
  initially voted against (later endorsed).

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| What is animism? | Tylor: belief in spiritual beings (minimum definition of religion). Harvey's new animism: relational ontology treating other-than-human entities as persons. Very different claims. |
| What is shamanism really? | Specialist practitioners mediating between ordinary and spirit worlds exist cross-culturally. Eliade's universal "archaic techniques of ecstasy" model over-generalizes from Siberian specifics. |
| Why are cargo cults not irrational? | They are structurally rational responses to colonial inequality: when you observe enormous wealth arriving by ship/plane, and the mechanism is hidden, ritual to attract it is not unreasonable. |
| What is the Dreamtime? | Australian Aboriginal concept: not historical past but ontological foundation. The creative acts of the Ancestors are always occurring. Songlines are paths through that living creation. |
| What is totemism, really? | Lévi-Strauss: not a unified phenomenon. Animals are "good to think" — differences in the natural world provide a code for expressing social differences. Not primarily about sacred power. |
| What is a revitalization movement? | Wallace: organized effort to construct a more satisfying culture under conditions of stress, typically involving a visionary prophet and synthesis of old and new elements. |

## Common Confusion Points

**"Animism is primitive"**: This is Tylor's 19th-century evolutionary assumption.
Animist ontologies (especially Amazonian perspectivism) are not failed attempts at
science — they are coherent ontological frameworks for a relational world. The
assumption that "nature" is an objective, uniform entity observed from the outside
is itself a historically specific philosophical position.

**Shamanism vs. possession**: In shamanic traditions (classic Siberian model), the
practitioner's soul leaves the body and travels. In possession traditions (Vodou,
Candomblé, Zar), the spirit enters and displaces the practitioner's personality.
These are structurally distinct practices often conflated. Most African traditions
are possession-based, not shamanic in Eliade's sense.

**Cargo cults as absurdity**: The reflex to find cargo cults absurd misses the analysis.
From inside the situation: you observe planes delivering goods; you don't know about
factories, supply chains, global trade. Building an airstrip to attract planes is
not categorically different from praying to make things happen.

**"These traditions are dying"**: Many indigenous religious traditions are living,
adapting, and actively revitalized. The Ghost Dance was suppressed but not ended.
The Longhouse Religion is active. Aboriginal Australians maintain songline
traditions. The narrative of inevitable disappearance is itself a colonial projection.
