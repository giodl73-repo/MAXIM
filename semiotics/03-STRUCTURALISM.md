# Structuralism and the Prague School

## What Structuralism Is

Structuralism is the application of Saussure's linguistic methodology — the study of signs as relational systems of differences — to domains beyond language: myth, kinship, unconscious, literature, culture. Its core claim is that beneath the surface of phenomena lies an underlying structure (a system of oppositions and rules) that generates the surface forms.

The Prague School (Prague Linguistic Circle, 1926–1948) was the first major institutional application of Saussurean ideas. It made fundamental contributions to phonology, the theory of functions of language, and the poetics of language.

## The Prague School: Phonology as Structure

### Trubetzkoy and the Phoneme

The Prague School's first great achievement was modern phonology — the study of the sound system of language as a structured system of functional distinctions.

```
  PHONETICS (pre-Prague):            PHONOLOGY (Prague):
  Study of physical sounds.          Study of sound FUNCTIONS.
  All physical variation recorded.   Only distinctive contrasts matter.
  /p/ in "pin" vs "spin" are         /p/ and /b/ are PHONEMES:
  phonetically different (aspirated  they distinguish meaning.
  vs unaspirated).                   "pat" ≠ "bat"

  PHONEME: The minimal unit of sound that distinguishes meaning.
  The abstract identity — not the physical sound token.

  /p/ is a phoneme in English because:
  "pat" / "bat" / "mat" / "fat" / "sat" — minimal pairs.
  Substitute /p/ → /b/ and meaning changes.

  [p] vs [pʰ] in English: NOT phonemes.
  They are allophones: variants of the same phoneme.
  Never contrast in the same environment.
  English speakers do not perceive them as different.
```

### The Phoneme as Opposition

```
  TRUBETZKOY's key insight (Grundzüge der Phonologie, 1939):
  A phoneme is defined by its OPPOSITIONS to other phonemes.
  It has no positive content — only relational value.

  (This is pure Saussure applied to phonological level.)

  /p/ in English is defined by:
  - Not /b/ (same place, voiced vs voiceless)
  - Not /t/ (same voiceless stop, different place)
  - Not /k/ (same voiceless stop, different place)
  - Not /f/ (same voiceless labial, fricative vs stop)
  - etc.

  DISTINCTIVE FEATURES (Jakobson & Halle):
  Phonemes are bundles of binary features.
  [+voice] / [-voice]
  [+nasal] / [-nasal]
  [+labial] / [-labial]
  [+strident] / [-strident]

  /p/ = [-voice, -nasal, +labial, -strident, +stop]
  /b/ = [+voice, -nasal, +labial, -strident, +stop]

  All phonemes in all languages from a small set of binary features.
  This is the prototypical application of binary opposition to structure.
```

## Marked and Unmarked: The Asymmetry Principle

A fundamental structuralist concept from Prague phonology, extending throughout semiotics.

```
  MARKED TERM: the specialized, restricted member of an opposition.
  UNMARKED TERM: the general, default, less restricted member.

  LINGUISTIC EXAMPLES:
  lion    / lioness    (lioness is marked: female-lion)
  actor   / actress    (actress is marked)
  dog     / bitch      (bitch is marked: female)
  red     / reddish    (reddish is marked: somewhat red)
  come    / came       (came is marked: past tense)

  PROPERTIES OF UNMARKED TERMS:
  - More frequent in texts
  - Broader meaning range (can sometimes stand for both)
  - Appears in more contexts
  - Acquired earlier in language development
  - Often the form used in questions ("How old is he?" vs
    "How young is he?" — only one is neutral)
  - Simpler morphologically (came is the exception)

  CULTURAL EXTENSION (Jakobson):
  Marked/unmarked extends to cultural categories:
  Male/female (male often unmarked in many cultures)
  Right/left (right is often culturally unmarked)
  White/black (in some cultural codes, white = unmarked/default)

  CS CONNECTION: Defaults in type systems, optional parameters,
  default values — the "zero value" in Go, default constructors
  in C# — all exploit the marked/unmarked logic.
  The absence of a value (null, 0, false) is often the "unmarked" state.
```

## Jakobson's Six Functions of Language

Roman Jakobson (1896–1982) moved from Prague to the US and became the most influential linguist of the 20th century. His model of communicative functions is used across semiotics, communication theory, and poetics.

```
  Every communicative act involves six factors:

                      CONTEXT
                    (Referential ─ orientation toward world/topic)
                          │
  ADDRESSER ────── MESSAGE ────── ADDRESSEE
  (Emotive         (Poetic          (Conative
   function:        function:        function:
   expresses        oriented         oriented
   sender's         toward the       toward
   inner state,     form of the      addressee;
   attitude)        message          "Come here!",
                    itself;          imperatives,
                    foregrounding    appeals,
                    of form)         "you" forms)
                          │
                      CONTACT
                    (Phatic ─ maintaining the channel:
                     "Hello?", "you know what I mean?",
                     "uh-huh", "are you still there?")
                          │
                        CODE
                    (Metalingual ─ about the code itself:
                     "What do you mean by X?",
                     "Define your terms",
                     a grammar is metalingual about language)

  PRIMARY FUNCTION = the one that dominates in a given text.
  A message may have all six simultaneously.
```

### The Six Functions Applied

```
+---------------------------------------------------------------------+
| FUNCTION      | ORIENTATION   | EXAMPLES                          |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Referential   | Context/topic | News report, encyclopedia article |
|               |               | "Water boils at 100°C"            |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Emotive       | Sender        | "Oh! That's disgusting!"          |
|               |               | Interjections, diary              |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conative      | Addressee     | Commands, ads, rhetoric           |
|               |               | "Buy now!" "Vote Smith!"          |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Phatic        | Contact       | "Hello?" "Nice weather, isn't it?"|
|               |               | Small talk, channel-checking      |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Metalingual   | Code          | Definitions, grammar books        |
|               |               | "What does 'irony' mean?"         |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| Poetic        | Message form  | Poetry, puns, alliteration        |
|               |               | "I like Ike" (sound pattern)      |
+---------------------------------------------------------------------+

  JAKOBSON ON THE POETIC FUNCTION:
  "The poetic function projects the principle of equivalence
  from the axis of selection into the axis of combination."
  = Paradigmatic choices (what COULD be here) become
    manifest in the syntagmatic sequence.
  Rhyme: two words share phonological equivalence at
  specific positions in the syntagmatic chain.
```

## Lévi-Strauss: Structuralism Applied to Myth and Kinship

Claude Lévi-Strauss (1908–2009) extended the Prague structural method to anthropology — the most ambitious application of Saussurean linguistics beyond language.

### Myth as Binary Opposition

```
  LÉVI-STRAUSS'S CLAIM: Myths are not random stories.
  They work through the mediation of fundamental binary oppositions:

  Nature / Culture            Raw / Cooked
  Life / Death                Human / Animal
  Male / Female               High / Low (social)

  THE OEDIPUS MYTH (Lévi-Strauss 1955):

  Column 1: Overvalued kin    Column 2: Undervalued kin
  relations                   relations
  Cadmus seeks sister         Cadmus kills dragon
  Oedipus marries mother      Eteocles kills brother
  Antigone buries brother     Oedipus kills father

  Column 3: Denial of         Column 4: Autochthony
  autochthonous origin        (born from earth)
  Oedipus = "swollen-foot"    Labdacos = "lame"
  Oedipus solves riddle       Laius = "left-sided"
  (human = walks upright)     Oedipus = "swollen-foot"

  The myth MEDIATES between the contradiction:
  (Are humans autochthonous? Earth-born or normally reproduced?)
  Column 1 asserts overvalued family ties (as if autochthonous).
  Column 2 denies normal origin.
  Column 3 denies autochthony (we walk upright, ≠ earth-beings).
  Column 4 asserts autochthony (origin in earth = lame gait).

  You can't resolve "born from earth or from parents" by logic.
  Myth works through it — not solving but repeatedly working through
  the contradiction in narrative form.
```

## Foregrounding and Defamiliarization

Prague School aesthetics, foundational to literary semiotics:

```
  AUTOMATIZATION: Language becomes automatic, transparent, habitual.
  We read without noticing the words — only attending to meaning.
  Ordinary prose aims at automatization.

  FOREGROUNDING (aktualizace):
  The device of making language itself VISIBLE and FELT.
  Breaking automatization.
  Calling attention to the sign vehicle (form) rather than
  purely to the signified (content).

  HOW FOREGROUNDING WORKS:
  - Unusual word order (inversion, ellipsis)
  - Unexpected collocations ("the sky is made of glass")
  - Sound patterns that exceed communicative necessity (rhyme, alliteration)
  - Rhythm that the reader notices
  - Metaphors that create a perceptible semantic distance

  This is Jakobson's POETIC FUNCTION in use.
  Poetry foregrounds form. News prose automatizes.

  RUSSIAN FORMALISTS' related concept: DEFAMILIARIZATION
  (ostranenie — Shklovsky): presenting familiar things as strange,
  forcing new perception. "Make the stone stony again."

  ART = defamiliarization of perception.
  REALISM ≠ mirroring reality; = making things strange
  enough to perceive again.
```

## Structural Analysis of Narrative: Propp Precursor

Vladimir Propp (1928, Morphology of the Folktale) anticipated structuralism by analyzing 100 Russian folk tales and finding:

```
  31 FUNCTIONS (narrative elements that may or may not appear,
  but always in the same ORDER if they do appear):

  1. Absentation: family member leaves
  2. Interdiction: hero is warned
  3. Violation: hero violates interdiction
  4. Villainy: villain causes harm
  ...
  31. Wedding: hero marries, ascends throne

  7 DRAMATIS PERSONAE (roles, not characters):
  Villain / Donor / Helper / Princess / Dispatcher /
  Hero / False Hero

  KEY: Character (who) is separated from FUNCTION (what role).
  The same character can play different functions.
  Different characters can play the same function.

  This is the direct ancestor of Greimas's actantial model.
  (See 06-NARRATIVE-SEMIOTICS.md)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Structuralism ≠ linguistics**: Lévi-Strauss applied linguistic methods to kinship and myth — he was not doing linguistics. The METHODOLOGY transferred; the object of study changed. This is why "structuralism" spans literature, anthropology, psychoanalysis, and philosophy.

**The Prague School ≠ Saussure's sociology**: Saussure emphasized the social/historical determination of langue. The Prague School focused on the formal/functional structure of the system. Jakobson was more interested in the internal logic of phonological systems than in their social embedding.

**Poetic function ≠ poetry**: The poetic function (orientation toward the message form) appears in advertising slogans, political speeches, children's songs — anywhere where the SOUND or STRUCTURE of the utterance is foregrounded. "Ike" in "I like Ike" was chosen for its phonological similarity to "I" — that's the poetic function in political rhetoric.

**Lévi-Strauss's binary oppositions are not claims about psychology**: He is not saying that humans think in binary oppositions (though he sometimes implies it). He is saying that the STRUCTURE of myths encodes binary oppositions — this is a formal claim about textual/cultural organization, not a psychological claim.

**Automatization ≠ bad writing**: Automatization is appropriate in texts whose purpose is transparent communication (instruction manuals, news reports, scientific prose). Foregrounding is appropriate in poetry and literature. Neither is superior — they are different functions.
