# Barthes: Myth, Connotation, and Cultural Signs

## Roland Barthes and His Project

Roland Barthes (1915–1980) is the most readable and influential figure in French semiotics. His project across several decades: apply Saussure's structural linguistics to cultural phenomena — mass media, fashion, food, wrestling, photography — to show how ideology works through sign systems, and later to show how texts exceed any single interpretation.

His work can be divided into two phases:
1. **Structuralist phase** (Mythologies, 1957; Fashion System, 1967): ideology as naturalized sign systems
2. **Post-structuralist phase** (S/Z, 1970; "Death of the Author," 1967): the plural, open text; the reader as producer

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                       BARTHES'S ARC                                   |
|                                                                       |
|  STRUCTURALIST (1950s–60s)       POST-STRUCTURALIST (1967+)           |
|  ----------------------------    ----------------------------         |
|  Mythologies (1957)             "Death of the Author" (1967)          |
|  Fashion System (1967)          S/Z (1970)                            |
|  Elements of Semiology          The Pleasure of the Text (1973)       |
|                                 Camera Lucida (1980)                  |
|                                                                       |
|  Ideology = naturalized         Author is "dead" — text is            |
|  second-order signs             not controlled by origin              |
|  (myth as mechanism)            Writerly vs readerly texts            |
|                                 Codes produce polysemy                |
|                                                                       |
|  SAUSSUREAN FRAMEWORK           DERRIDEAN INFLUENCE                   |
|  Binary: signifier/signified    Open-ended semiosis                   |
|  Two orders of signification    Plurality, dissemination              |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Myth: The Mechanism of Ideology

The central analysis of Mythologies. Barthes takes Saussure's two-level sign and adds a third level.

### The Semiological Chain

```
  FIRST ORDER SYSTEM (Language / Denotation):
  +------------------------------------------+
  |  Signifier    |    Signified             |
  |  (form)       |    (concept)             |
  |  [arbr]       |    tree-concept          |
  +------------------------------------------+
  This whole sign: [arbr / tree-concept] = SIGN₁

  SECOND ORDER SYSTEM (Myth / Connotation):
  Sign₁ becomes a new SIGNIFIER in a second system:
  +------------------------------------------+
  |  Signifier₂   |    Signified₂            |
  |  (Sign₁)      |    (mythical concept)    |
  |  [arbr/tree]  |    FRENCHNESS, NATURE,   |
  |               |    NATURAL ORDER         |
  +------------------------------------------+

  The first-order sign is PLUNDERED by myth.
  It retains its first-order meaning (a tree is still a tree)
  but is simultaneously HOLLOWED OUT and filled with
  ideological/mythical content.
```

### The Magazine Cover Example (Barthes's Own)

```
  A French magazine cover: a young Black soldier in French uniform
  saluting, eyes upturned toward the tricolor.

  FIRST ORDER:
  Signifier: the photograph
  Signified: a Black French soldier saluting the flag
  This is what the photograph "says" — a factual record

  SECOND ORDER (MYTH):
  This whole first-order sign becomes Signifier₂:
  Signified₂: "France is a great empire, her sons of
  whatever race faithfully serve under her flag,
  and there is no better answer to the detractors
  of colonialism than the zeal shown by this Negro
  in serving his so-called oppressors."

  The myth: French imperialism is NATURAL, legitimate,
  even desired by the colonized.
  The first-order image MOTIVATES the myth —
  it is presented as evidence, as natural fact.
```

### Why Myth Works: Naturalization

```
  MYTH'S MECHANISM:
  Myth takes a historical/political/contingent fact
  and presents it as NATURAL.

  "It has always been this way."
  "This is just how things are."
  "This is nature, not ideology."

  THE SLEIGHT OF HAND:
  Myth does not HIDE ideology — it NATURALIZES it.
  The content is present; it is the historical contingency
  that is concealed.

  EXAMPLES FROM MYTHOLOGIES:
  - Wrestling: theatrical performance of moral categories
    (the villain, the suffering good man) presented as sport
  - Toys: little-girl dolls that naturalize the "future mother"
    role; the toy as miniaturized adult reality
  - The face of Garbo: perfect divine face → the idea of
    Woman as Platonic form
  - Steak and chips: Frenchness mythologized through food
  - The new Citroën: technological mythology, car as cathedral

  IDEOLOGY'S CHARACTERISTIC: presents the cultural as natural,
  the historical as eternal, the partial as universal.
```

## Denotation and Connotation

```
  Barthes systematizes the two levels:

  DENOTATION:           CONNOTATION:
  First-order meaning.  Second-order meaning.
  Literal.              Cultural, ideological, evaluative.
  Relatively stable.    Variable across cultures/contexts.
  Dictionary meaning.   Dictionary doesn't capture it.

  ROSE:
  Denotation: a plant, genus Rosa, with petals and thorns
  Connotation: love, romance, passion, Englishness,
               desire, death (depends on context/culture)

  PHOTOGRAPH OF EMPTY CHAIR:
  Denotation: a chair, unoccupied
  Connotation: absence, death, loneliness, waiting

  KEY: Barthes insists connotation is as SYSTEMATIC as denotation.
  It is not random — it is a code shared by a community.
  The code can be analyzed semiotically.

  BUT: Myth tries to make connotation APPEAR as denotation.
  The message seems to be simply describing facts,
  while actually deploying ideological connotations.
```

## The Fashion System

Barthes's most systematic application of semiotics to culture.

```
  Three levels of fashion:
  1. CLOTHING AS REAL: the actual garment (material, technology)
  2. CLOTHING AS IMAGE: the photograph in the fashion magazine
  3. CLOTHING AS WRITTEN: the linguistic description
     ("A cardigan for autumn evenings")

  Barthes focuses on WRITTEN FASHION — the language of fashion journalism.

  FASHION AS SIGN SYSTEM:
  Signifier: garment feature (a belt, a collar, an open-neck shirt)
  Signified: a social situation / identity (sports, evening, formal)

  "Florals for spring" —
  Signifier: florals worn in spring
  Signified: the appropriate, the seasonal, the coded feminine

  FASHION CODE:
  Like language, it is arbitrary (nothing forces florals to mean spring)
  Like language, it is systematic (the whole system defines each term)
  Unlike language, it changes RAPIDLY (deliberate obsolescence)

  Fashion as MYTH: each season's "new look" is presented as
  natural, inevitable, the expression of true femininity/the age.
  The historical fact that fashion changes is concealed.
```

## S/Z: The Writerly and Readerly Text

Barthes's 1970 analysis of Balzac's novella "Sarrasine" — a close reading using five codes. This is his post-structuralist turn.

```
  READERLY TEXT (lisible):
  Classic realist narrative.
  Positioned to be READ — passively consumed.
  The reader follows; the text leads.
  Meaning flows from author → text → reader.
  Balzac's Sarrasine as traditionally read.

  WRITERLY TEXT (scriptible):
  Open, plural, without definitive meaning.
  Invites the reader to PRODUCE, not consume.
  "A text's unity lies not in its origin but in its destination."
  (anticipates "Death of the Author")

  BARTHES'S METHOD IN S/Z:
  Divide the text into LEXIAS (small units)
  Assign each to one of FIVE CODES:

  CODE           MEANING
  ─────          ───────
  Hermeneutic    Codes of enigma and truth — the riddles
  (HER)          that drive narrative forward
  Proairetic     Codes of action — sequences with predictable
  (PRO)          consequences (entering a room → action)
  Semantic       Connotation — excess of meaning beyond
  (SEM)          denotation; theme
  Symbolic       Symbolic structures — antitheses, oppositions
  (SYM)
  Cultural/REF   Reference codes — cultural knowledge invoked
                 (science, history, common sense)

  The point: NO text is purely "readerly."
  Every text is traversed by multiple codes simultaneously.
  Meaning is the play of codes, not the expression of an author.
```

## Death of the Author

Barthes's 1967 essay — perhaps the most influential short text in literary theory.

```
  TRADITIONAL VIEW: "What did Balzac mean when he wrote...?"
  The author is the source, origin, and arbiter of meaning.
  To interpret = to recover the author's intention.
  The text is a message from author to reader.

  BARTHES'S CLAIM:
  Writing is "the destruction of every voice, every point of origin."
  Once the text is produced, the author's intentions are IRRELEVANT.
  The text becomes independent.

  THE BIRTH OF THE READER:
  "...the birth of the reader must be ransomed by the death of the Author."
  Meaning is PRODUCED by the reader, not recovered from the author.
  Different readers, different contexts → different meanings.
  All equally legitimate.

  INTERTEXTUALITY:
  Every text is a "tissue of quotations" drawn from the codes of culture.
  Nothing is original; everything is citation of prior codes.
  The author is not a genius creating from nothing but a SCRIPTOR
  who collects and redistributes available cultural codes.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Literary criticism need not recover intention.
  The text is polysemic — has many meanings simultaneously.
  Criticism produces meaning, not discovers it.

  CONTRAST WITH E.D. HIRSCH:
  Hirsch (Validity in Interpretation, 1967) argues intention IS the
  legitimate anchor of meaning. Without it, interpretation is arbitrary.
  Barthes: the text is not controlled by origin; all readings are
  produced by the reader's encounter with codes.
```

## Camera Lucida: Photography and Death

Barthes's late phenomenological work on photography (1980).

```
  PUNCTUM vs STUDIUM:

  STUDIUM: The cultural, general interest in a photograph.
  What you can analyze, categorize, discuss.
  The photographer's intention is communicated.
  It is received with ordinary cultural competence.
  Example: the documentary interest in a war photograph.

  PUNCTUM: The detail that "pricks" or "wounds" you.
  Not general — specific to THIS viewer.
  Cannot be fully analyzed or verbalized.
  It exceeds the code.
  Example: the worn shoes of a subject; an unexpected gesture.
  It is what makes the photograph personal, touching.

  BARTHES ON PHOTOGRAPHY AND DEATH:
  Every photograph is a certificate of presence.
  "It has been."
  The referent is always past — the subject has been there.
  Photography is haunted by death: the subject was alive then,
  may be dead now.
  The photograph is a "return of the dead."

  The photograph of his dead mother triggers the reflection
  that drives Camera Lucida — grief as phenomenological method.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I'm asking... | Use this concept |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Why does this cultural practice, image, or text feel "natural" or "obvious"? | Myth analysis — what ideology is being naturalized? |
| What does this text/image say literally vs. what cultural values does it activate? | Denotation (first order) vs. connotation (second order) |
| Why do I keep analyzing this image / why does it hold me? | Punctum — the detail that personally catches you |
| What is my general, educated interest in this image? | Studium — the cultural competence that lets you read it |
| Does this text position me as a passive consumer or an active producer? | Readerly (scriptible=passive) vs. writerly (scriptible=active) |
| What codes is this narrative running simultaneously? | Five codes (S/Z): hermeneutic, proairetic, semantic, symbolic, cultural |
| Who controls the meaning of this text? | Death of the Author — the author's intention does not; the reader produces meaning in the reading |
| How does this text present ideology as fact? | Myth mechanism: ideology dressed as evidence / empirical description |

## Formal Systems Bridge

```
BARTHES'S MYTH MECHANISM ↔ TECHNICAL CONVENTION NATURALIZATION

Myth's operation: transform historical/contingent choices into
apparent nature. The sign at the first level (denotation) becomes
the SIGNIFIER at the second level — its history is emptied out
and replaced by apparent necessity.

TECHNICAL NATURALIZATION — THE SAME MECHANISM:

"Of course we use REST":
  First-order: REST is an architectural style formalized by Fielding (2000).
  Second-order (myth): REST is "the right way to build APIs."
  The history is erased: CORBA, SOAP, XML-RPC, GraphQL, gRPC
  all existed and made different tradeoffs. REST won partially
  on technical merits, partially through ecosystem network effects,
  partially through coincidence (JSON vs. XML adoption).
  The myth "REST is obvious/natural" suppresses this contingency.

"Obviously microservices":
  First-order: microservices is an architectural pattern popularized
  ~2014-2016 for specific scalability and team autonomy problems.
  Second-order (myth): microservices is "modern architecture."
  The history erased: monoliths served Google, Amazon, and every
  successful tech company before 2010. The pattern has real costs
  (distributed tracing, service mesh, eventual consistency).
  The myth converts a contingent tradeoff into "what serious
  engineers do."

"The cloud is just the way things work now":
  Platform defaults (AWS default regions, Azure resource groups,
  Kubernetes as "the" container platform) become naturalized:
  their contingency, their vendor dependencies, their design choices
  become invisible. Using them "just makes sense."

BARTHES'S DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION FOR ANY TECHNICAL CONVENTION:
  What history was emptied out to make this feel inevitable?
  What alternatives were suppressed?
  What interests does this naturalization serve?
  (Not a reason to reject the convention —
   a reason to choose it consciously.)
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Barthes ≠ pure ideology critique**: His early work is ideological critique (Mythologies). His later work moves away from Marxist semiotics toward a pleasure of the text that is personal, sensory, and anti-systematic. Camera Lucida is phenomenological. He did not stay a structuralist.

**"Death of the Author" does not mean biographies are useless**: Barthes's claim is specifically about interpretive authority — the author's intention does not fix or limit the valid readings of a text. Biographical information can still be relevant as context. The claim is normative (for interpretation), not descriptive (about what's interesting).

**Myth operates through evidence, not through denial**: Myth does not say "this is natural." It SHOWS evidence (the photograph, the anecdote, the statistic) that presents ideology as empirical fact. The mechanism is evidentiary, not assertoric. This is why it is harder to resist than an explicit ideological claim.

**Connotation is not just "what it makes you think of"**: For Barthes, connotation is systematic — it is a code shared by a cultural community. Different cultures have different connotation codes. Analyzing connotation means decoding the shared conventions, not just listing personal associations.

**Writerly is not a genre**: Writerly (scriptible) is not a characteristic of avant-garde texts. Barthes argues that ANY text, read carefully enough, reveals its plurality of codes and exceeds readerly closure. S/Z is a demonstration that "Sarrasine" — a seemingly readerly realistic story — is actually scriptible on careful analysis.
