# Derrida and Deconstruction

## What Post-Structuralism Is

Post-structuralism is a French intellectual movement of the late 1960s that grew out of structuralism by radicalizing and undermining its assumptions. Structuralism had assumed: (1) structures are stable and identifiable; (2) the sign is a stable unit (signifier plus signified); (3) the observer can step outside the system to analyze it. Post-structuralism questions all three.

Jacques Derrida (1930–2004) is the central figure. His project: close reading of philosophical and literary texts to show how they destabilize their own stated positions, particularly through their dependence on binary oppositions that they cannot sustain.

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
|               STRUCTURALISM → POST-STRUCTURALISM                      |
|                                                                       |
|  STRUCTURALISM assumes:           POST-STRUCTURALISM shows:          |
|                                                                       |
|  Signs are stable (Saussure)      Signs are unstable — meaning       |
|  Signifier ──────→ Signified      deferred and differed (différance) |
|                                                                       |
|  Structures can be identified     The observer is always inside       |
|  from outside                     the structure (no outside)          |
|                                                                       |
|  Binary oppositions are clear:    Oppositions are hierarchical and    |
|  speech/writing, presence/absence self-undermining — the excluded     |
|  male/female, culture/nature      term contaminates the privileged one|
|                                                                       |
|  The system has a center          Any "center" is itself a construct  |
|  (a ground, a foundation)         — there is no transcendental        |
|                                   signified                           |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Derrida's Critique of the Sign

### Saussure's Instability

```
  SAUSSURE claimed: signs have value through DIFFERENCE.
  No sign has positive content — meaning is relational.

  DERRIDA'S RADICALIZATION:
  If meaning is always differential (A is not B, not C, not D...),
  then meaning is always DEFERRED.
  To know what A means, you must know what B, C, D mean.
  But those meanings also refer to further differences.
  The chain of reference never arrives at a final, present meaning.

  DIFFÉRANCE (Derrida's neologism):
  "différance" blends two French meanings of "différer":
  1. To DIFFER (spatial difference — signs differ from each other)
  2. To DEFER (temporal deferral — meaning is always postponed)

  The word "différance" is ONLY distinguishable from "différence"
  in WRITING (the 'a'), not in speech (both pronounced the same).
  This enacts Derrida's argument: writing is not a secondary
  notation for speech; it reveals something about all language
  that phonocentrism conceals.

  RESULT:
  Meaning is never fully present.
  It is constituted by a play of differences.
  "There is nothing outside the text" (il n'y a pas de hors-texte).
```

## Logocentrism and the Metaphysics of Presence

Derrida's broadest critical target.

```
  LOGOCENTRISM:
  The tendency in Western philosophy to privilege SPEECH over writing
  and to assume that words carry the presence of meaning.
  The spoken word: the speaker is present, meaning is "alive,"
  intention is present, truth is accessible.
  Writing: speaker absent, meaning can be perverted,
  intention cannot be verified.

  THE PLATONIC ROOT:
  Plato's Phaedrus: Socrates argues against writing.
  Writing cannot answer questions. It is "dead" speech.
  The living philosopher carries truth in his voice.

  THE CHRISTIAN/METAPHYSICAL VERSION:
  Logos = the divine Word, self-present, foundation of meaning.
  Truth is what is "before" words, fully present to itself.
  Words TRY to represent this presence.
  Meaning flows from: inner thought → spoken word → written word
  (Aristotle: written words are symbols of spoken words,
  which are symbols of mental impressions)

  DERRIDA'S INVERSION:
  Writing is not a secondary, derivative form.
  The features attributed to writing (absence of speaker,
  deferral, citation, iterability) are features of ALL language.
  Speech is itself a form of writing in the extended sense.
  Derrida calls this "arche-writing" — writing in the general sense
  = the differential structure underlying all meaning.
```

## Deconstruction

The method (or rather, the reading practice) for which Derrida is known.

### What Deconstruction Is Not

```
  NOT: Destruction or demolition.
      Deconstruction does not show texts are meaningless.
  NOT: A hermeneutic method that can be applied algorithmically.
      Deconstruction is not a technique you can "apply."
  NOT: Relativism or nihilism.
      Derrida is not saying "anything goes" or "all readings
      are equally valid."
  NOT: A theory that DERRIDA developed in his head.
      Deconstruction, Derrida insists, is what TEXTS DO.
      He merely reads it in them.
```

### What Deconstruction Is

```
  DECONSTRUCTION as close reading:
  1. Find a BINARY OPPOSITION in the text:
     presence/absence, speech/writing, nature/culture,
     literal/figurative, inside/outside, serious/non-serious

  2. IDENTIFY THE HIERARCHY:
     One term is privileged (positive, primary, foundational)
     The other is marginal (derivative, secondary, parasitic)

  3. SHOW THE REVERSAL:
     The supposedly primary term depends on the secondary.
     The hierarchy is not natural — it is constructed.

  4. DISPLACE THE OPPOSITION:
     Neither term is simply primary.
     The opposition itself is unstable.

  EXAMPLE: SPEECH/WRITING (in Plato, Saussure, Rousseau)
  Privileged: SPEECH (present, authentic, alive, true)
  Marginalized: WRITING (absent, artificial, dead, supplementary)

  Derrida's reversal:
  Saussure says writing is a supplement to speech.
  But supplement means BOTH: addition AND replacement.
  What is added indicates a lack in the original.
  If speech needed no supplement, no supplement would be added.
  The supplement retroactively reveals that the "original"
  (speech) was never self-sufficient.
  Writing reveals what was always true of speech.
```

## Différance in Detail

```
  The key term — must be understood precisely.

  LEVEL 1: DIFFERING (synchronic)
  Signs differ from each other.
  "Cat" ≠ "bat", "car", "can"...
  Meaning is relational — from the Saussurean grid.

  LEVEL 2: DEFERRAL (diachronic)
  Meaning is deferred — always yet to come.
  To understand "cat," consult the dictionary:
  "cat: a small carnivorous mammal."
  "Carnivorous": "flesh-eating."
  "Flesh": "the soft tissue..."
  You never arrive at a pure, unmediated, present meaning.
  Meaning is always another sign, another deferral.

  THE TRACE:
  Every sign carries the "trace" of other signs —
  their presence-in-absence.
  "Cat" is not pure presence; it bears the trace of
  "bat", "can", "mat" as the marks of its difference.
  And the trace of past uses, contexts, other languages.

  THE SUPPLEMENT:
  The trace of what is absent makes presence possible.
  This is the "logic of supplementarity":
  The apparently external (writing, absence, difference)
  turns out to be constitutive of the apparently internal
  (speech, presence, identity).

  RESULT:
  No "transcendental signified" — no final meaning
  external to the sign-chain that grounds it.
  No center that is not itself part of the play.
```

## Iterability and Context

Another major contribution: against the assumption that meaning is controlled by intention.

```
  AUSTIN'S CLAIM: A performative only succeeds in the right context.
  SEARLE'S VERSION: Communication requires shared background, intention.

  DERRIDA'S CRITIQUE (in "Signature Event Context," 1972):
  For a mark to communicate, it must be ITERABLE —
  able to be repeated in different contexts.
  If a sign only worked in one specific context, it would
  not be a sign at all.

  A WRITTEN SIGN CAN FUNCTION:
  - Absent its sender (author may be dead)
  - Absent its addressee (may never arrive)
  - Cut off from its original context

  THIS IS NOT A FAILURE OF THE SIGN.
  It is what MAKES IT A SIGN.
  Iterability — citability, repeatability — is constitutive of the sign,
  not an accidental feature.

  CONSEQUENCE:
  Context cannot fully constrain meaning.
  Every use of a sign is a citation of prior uses.
  Meaning always escapes the context that was supposed to fix it.

  SEARLE'S REJOINDER (1977):
  Ordinary language communication succeeds.
  Derrida is pointing to degenerate cases (parasitic uses,
  quotation, fiction) and generalizing illegitimately.
  Iterability is consistent with communicative norms.

  THE DISPUTE continues in professional philosophy.
  Derrida (1988): Searle misread him; the argument is about
  the structure of any writing, not a claim that communication fails.
```

## Post-Structuralism Beyond Derrida

```
  FOUCAULT (1926–1984):
  Discourse analysis — power/knowledge relations.
  Discourses do not describe pre-given objects;
  they PRODUCE the objects they appear to describe.
  The prison, the clinic, sexuality — all produced by
  specific discursive formations in specific historical periods.
  The subject itself is a historical construct.

  LACAN (1901–1981):
  "The unconscious is structured like a language."
  Desire is generated by lack (the missing signified).
  The subject is split by language — the subject of the
  utterance ≠ the subject of the enunciation.
  (Psychoanalysis meets Saussure.)

  DELEUZE & GUATTARI (Anti-Oedipus, 1972; A Thousand Plateaus, 1980):
  Against the arborescent (tree-like, hierarchical) model of thought.
  For the rhizome — network without center, no beginning, no end.
  The rhizome as model of text, culture, desire.

  KRISTEVA (b. 1941):
  Intertextuality: every text is a mosaic of quotations,
  absorbing and transforming other texts.
  Semiotic vs Symbolic: the pre-linguistic drive-based
  semiotic dimension of language vs the rule-governed symbolic.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I'm asking... | Use this concept |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Does this text have a single correct reading? | Différance and iterability say no — meaning is constituted by difference and deferred; each reading context produces a new trace |
| Why does this binary always need its excluded term? | Supplement logic — the "external" term that seems added turns out to be constitutive of the primary term |
| Why does this text privilege speech over writing? | Logocentrism — Western metaphysics of presence; Derrida's deconstruction targets this |
| What does this sign carry of other signs? | Trace — every sign carries the mark of what it is not; pure presence is impossible |
| What is this text working against, what it's trying to exclude? | Deconstruction — find the hierarchy; invert it; show the dependency |
| Can this sign mean the same thing in every context? | Iterability — yes it can be repeated; no it cannot mean exactly the same; context constitutively transforms the sign |
| Is there a final ground of meaning outside the sign chain? | Transcendental signified — Derrida says no; there is no outside; no meta-language that escapes signification |

## Formal Systems Bridge

```
DERRIDA'S KEY CONCEPTS ↔ COMPUTATION AND TYPE THEORY

DIFFÉRANCE ↔ LAZY EVALUATION / DEFERRED COMPUTATION:
  Différance encodes two things simultaneously:
    differing: meaning constituted by DIFFERENCE (Saussure's heritage)
    deferring: meaning never fully present; always deferred
  The deferral is not a failure — it is the mechanism of signification.
  Computation parallel: lazy evaluation (Haskell thunks, C# IEnumerable/yield).
    A thunk is a deferred computation — the value is not present;
    it will be produced when forced.
    The "meaning" of a thunk is deferred until evaluation.
    But: a thunk forced in different contexts produces different values
    if it depends on context — exactly Derrida's iterability.
  IEnumerable<T> with yield: the sequence is not computed;
    it is deferred as a generator. The "meaning" (the values)
    is produced incrementally as demanded — never fully present
    in advance.
  The transcendental signified = eager evaluation of the full sequence.
  Derrida says: you can never force the full sequence;
  the evaluation is always partial, always deferred.

THE SUPPLEMENT ↔ DEPENDENCY INJECTION:
  The supplement is what appears to be merely added, external,
  inessential — but turns out to be constitutive of what it supplements.
  Writing is the supplement to speech: apparently merely added
  (you can speak without writing), but Derrida shows that the
  properties of writing (iterability, deferral, absence of speaker)
  are actually constitutive of language as such.
  Computation parallel: dependency injection.
    The injected dependency appears "external" — passed in, not intrinsic.
    But the component cannot function without it.
    The external dependency is constitutive of the component's behavior.
    IoC container = the supplement made explicit: what appeared optional
    is revealed as the structural condition of possibility.
  More precisely: any API that takes a callback or strategy pattern
  parameter is acknowledging that the "external" behavior is
  constitutive of what the function does. The supplement was always
  inside, waiting to be made visible.

ITERABILITY ↔ REFERENTIAL TRANSPARENCY AND CONTEXT DEPENDENCE:
  Iterability: a sign can be repeated in different contexts;
  but repetition in a new context is not pure repetition —
  the context constitutively transforms the meaning.
  Type theory parallel: a function value is referentially
  transparent (the same function when referenced anywhere)
  but its application in different call sites may produce
  different results if the arguments differ.
  More precisely: a symbol f is iterable (can appear anywhere
  a term is expected); but f(x) in context C1 ≠ f(x) in C2
  if the free variables captured by f's closure differ.
  Context is constitutive of application meaning.
  Derrida's insight: this is not a failure of language;
  it is the condition that makes signification possible.

DECONSTRUCTION ↔ FINDING THE HIDDEN DEPENDENCY:
  Deconstruction finds the binary hierarchy in a text
  (speech/writing, presence/absence, literal/metaphorical),
  shows that the devalued term is actually constitutive,
  and inverts or complicates the hierarchy.
  Computation parallel: finding a hidden dependency in a
  "decoupled" system. You think module A is independent of
  module B (B is external, B is the supplement). Deconstruction:
  trace the actual call graph; find that A calls B via an
  apparently external interface; the "decoupling" was nominal.
  The dependency was always there; it needed to be made explicit.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Nothing outside the text" ≠ "reality doesn't exist"**: Derrida is not making an idealist metaphysical claim. He is saying there is no position outside systems of interpretation from which to access "brute reality" unmediated by language, context, and difference. This is a claim about interpretation and understanding, not about ontology.

**Deconstruction ≠ "prove it has no meaning"**: Deconstruction shows texts are more COMPLEX than they appear — that they contain unresolved tensions and dependencies on what they claim to exclude. The text MEANS; it over-determines.

**Différance is not the same as indeterminacy**: Meaning is not random or arbitrary for Derrida. It is structured by its specific differences and traces. But it is never fully present, never exhausted, always carrying excesses.

**Post-structuralism is not anti-philosophy**: Derrida spent his career writing very detailed philosophical analyses of Husserl, Hegel, Plato, Kant, Freud, Saussure. He does philosophy; he is not opposed to careful reading. The "anti-foundationalism" is about the impossibility of a foundationalist grounding, not hostility to careful thought.

**Foucault and Derrida are not the same**: They are often lumped together as "post-structuralists" but their projects are quite different. Foucault is primarily a historian of discourse and power. Derrida is primarily a close reader of texts doing deconstructive analysis. They had a famous public argument and were not allies.
