# Narrative Semiotics: Greimas and Propp

## The Project of Narrative Semiotics

Narrative semiotics asks: is there a deep structure underlying all narratives? If signs function through relational systems of differences (Saussure), do narratives have an analogous underlying grammar that generates surface stories?

This field draws on: Vladimir Propp's morphology of folk tales (1928), Algirdas Julien Greimas's structural semantics and actantial model (1966–1987), the structuralist tradition in anthropology (Lévi-Strauss), and later extensions toward discourse and social semiotics.

## Propp: Morphology of the Folktale

Vladimir Propp (1895–1970) analyzed 100 Russian folk tales and discovered that they share a common structure.

### The 31 Narrative Functions

```
  Propp's KEY FINDINGS:
  1. The NUMBER of FUNCTIONS is limited and constant across all tales.
  2. The SEQUENCE of functions is always the same (never reordered).
  3. Not all functions appear in every tale; when present, order is fixed.
  4. Many tales share the same functions while having entirely
     different characters, settings, and surface content.

  INITIAL SITUATION:
  α. Introduction of family members, hero described.

  PREPARATORY SECTION:
  β.  Absentation: member of family absents themselves.
  γ.  Interdiction: hero addressed with an interdiction/command.
  δ.  Violation: interdiction is violated.
  ε.  Reconnaissance: villain seeks information.
  ζ.  Delivery: villain receives information.
  η.  Trickery: villain tries to deceive victim.
  θ.  Complicity: victim submits to trickery.

  COMPLICATION:
  A.  Villainy: villain causes harm to family member.
  a.  (or A alternative) Lack: family member lacks something.
  B.  Mediation: misfortune made known; hero dispatched.
  C.  Beginning counteraction: hero agrees to act.
  ↑.  Departure: hero leaves home.

  DONOR SEQUENCE:
  D.  First function of donor: hero tested, interrogated.
  E.  Hero's reaction: hero responds to donor.
  F.  Provision: hero receives magical agent.
  G.  Spatial transference: hero led to object of search.
  H.  Struggle: hero and villain join direct combat.
  I.  Branding: hero is branded/wounded/marked.
  J.  Victory: villain is defeated.
  K.  Liquidation: initial misfortune or lack resolved.
  ↓.  Return: hero returns.

  RESOLUTION:
  Pr. Pursuit: hero is pursued.
  Rs. Rescue: hero is rescued.
  O.  Unrecognized arrival: hero arrives home unrecognized.
  L.  Unfounded claims: false hero presents unfounded claims.
  M.  Difficult task: difficult task proposed to hero.
  N.  Solution: task is resolved.
  Q.  Recognition: hero is recognized.
  Ex. Exposure: false hero exposed.
  T.  Transfiguration: hero given a new appearance.
  U.  Punishment: villain is punished.
  W.  Wedding: hero is married and ascends throne.
```

### The Seven Spheres of Action

```
  Propp identified 7 CHARACTER ROLES (not characters):

  1. VILLAIN: struggles against hero (functions A, H, Pr)
  2. DONOR: provides hero with magical agent (functions D, E, F)
  3. HELPER: aids hero (functions G, K, Rs, N, T)
  4. PRINCESS (sought-for person): assigned to hero (functions M, J, Ex, N, Q, W)
     + her father (assigns tasks, marks, condemns, recognizes)
  5. DISPATCHER: sends hero on quest (function B)
  6. HERO: seeks (reacts to donor, weds) (functions C, ↑, E, W)
  7. FALSE HERO: unfounded claims (functions C, ↑, E, L)

  KEY INSIGHT: One person can play multiple roles.
  Multiple persons can share one role.
  What matters is the FUNCTION (what they do), not who they are.

  GREIMAS's EXTENSION: These 7 roles reduce to 3 OPPOSITIONS:
  Subject vs Object (Hero vs Princess/Quest)
  Sender vs Receiver (Dispatcher → Hero)
  Helper vs Opponent (Donor-Helper vs Villain)
```

## Greimas: The Actantial Model

Algirdas Julien Greimas (1917–1992) developed Propp's insights into a general model applicable to any narrative — myths, novels, company profiles, political speeches.

### The Six Actants

```
  ACTANTS (not characters):
  Abstract roles in the narrative grammar.
  A single character can fill multiple actant slots.
  A single actant can be filled by multiple characters.

  +-------------------------------------------------------+
  |                   ACTANTIAL MODEL                     |
  |                                                       |
  |   SENDER ────────────────────────────→ RECEIVER       |
  |   (motivates the                      (benefits from  |
  |    quest; gives                        the object     |
  |    value to object)                    being obtained)|
  |                       ↑                              |
  |                    OBJECT                            |
  |                 (what is sought)                      |
  |                       ↑                              |
  |   HELPER ────────────→ SUBJECT ←──────── OPPONENT    |
  |   (aids subject        (protagonist;    (opposes     |
  |    in quest)           hero)            subject)     |
  +-------------------------------------------------------+

  EXAMPLE (Cinderella):
  Subject:   Cinderella
  Object:    Marriage to the Prince / happiness
  Sender:    The Fairy Godmother / social convention
  Receiver:  Cinderella herself (also society in general)
  Helper:    Fairy Godmother, birds, magic
  Opponent:  Stepmother, stepsisters, time (midnight)

  EXAMPLE (Commercial — Greimas's extension):
  Subject:   The company
  Object:    Product value / solution
  Sender:    Customer needs / social conditions
  Receiver:  Customer
  Helper:    Technology, partners, employees
  Opponent:  Competition, constraints, obstacles

  This is how PR narratives, brand stories, and corporate
  messaging have underlying actantial structure.
```

### Narrative Levels

```
  Greimas proposes THREE levels of narrative structure:

  1. SURFACE LEVEL (discourse)
     The actual words, images, sequence of events.
     What you read/see.

  2. NARRATIVE LEVEL (structures)
     Narrative programs: Subject acquires Object.
     Qualifications, missions, performances, recompenses.
     The underlying trajectory of competence → performance.

  3. DEEP LEVEL (axiology)
     The fundamental VALUE OPPOSITIONS driving the narrative.
     life/death, culture/nature, freedom/constraint
     These are the semantic "deep grammar."
```

### The Narrative Program

```
  NARRATIVE PROGRAM (NP): the basic unit of narrative action.
  Subject (S) undergoes a state transformation:
  NP = F[S1 → (S2 ∩ O)] ∨ F[S1 → (S2 ∪ O)]

  where:
  F = function (transformation)
  S1 = subject performing the transformation
  S2 = subject being transformed
  O  = object
  ∩  = conjunction (acquiring the object)
  ∪  = disjunction (losing the object)

  DOING vs BEING:
  Narrative programs of DOING: transformations of state
    Hero acquires sword, defeats dragon, wins princess
  Narrative programs of STATE: descriptions of condition
    The kingdom is under the dragon's power

  MANIPULATION → COMPETENCE → PERFORMANCE → SANCTION:
  A canonical narrative follows these phases:
  1. MANIPULATION: Sender contracts with Subject (gives value)
  2. COMPETENCE: Subject acquires the means (helper, skills, objects)
  3. PERFORMANCE: Subject performs the transforming action
  4. SANCTION: Sender recognizes and rewards (or punishes)

  This is the "canonical narrative schema" —
  applicable to commercials, political speeches, HR processes...
```

## The Semiotic Square

Greimas's most abstract contribution — a tool for mapping conceptual oppositions.

```
  THE SEMIOTIC SQUARE (Carré sémiotique):
  Maps the logical relations among four terms generated
  by a primary opposition.

  Start with a binary opposition:
  LIFE ───────────────── DEATH
    │  (contradiction)     │
    │                      │
  NOT-DEATH               NOT-LIFE
    └────── (implication) ──┘

  Four positions:
  S1:  Life (positive term)
  S2:  Death (negative term)
  -S1: Not-Life (negation of positive)
  -S2: Not-Death (negation of negative)

  Relations:
  S1 ↔ S2:      Contrariety (life and death are contrary)
  S1 ↔ -S1:     Contradiction (life and not-life contradict)
  S2 ↔ -S2:     Contradiction (death and not-death)
  -S2 ↔ S1:     Implication (not-death implies life?)
  -S1 ↔ S2:     Implication (not-life implies death?)

  The MIDDLE TERMS complicate the binary:
  S1+S2: Life AND death = the complex term (e.g., "undead", zombie)
  -S1+-S2: Neither life nor death = the neutral term
           (e.g., sleep, coma, suspension?)

  EXAMPLE (politics):
  Freedom ─── Constraint
  Not-constraint  Not-freedom
  Complex: controlled freedom (compromise, regulated liberty)
  Neutral: neither free nor constrained (nature, animality)

  Used by Greimas to analyze ideological systems, plot structures,
  and the cultural logic of particular domains.
```

## Narrative Modalities

```
  Greimas identifies MODALITIES — the values attached to subject-object
  relations that determine the subject's competence.

  THE FOUR MODALITIES OF COMPETENCE:
  WANTING-TO-DO (vouloir):  Subject wants the object / to act
  HAVING-TO-DO (devoir):    Subject is obligated / must act
  BEING-ABLE-TO (pouvoir):  Subject has the capacity / power
  KNOWING-HOW-TO (savoir):  Subject has the skill / knowledge

  A hero's NARRATIVE TRAJECTORY involves acquiring these modalities:
  Stage 1: Subject wants (motivation established)
  Stage 2: Subject has to (obligation, contract with sender)
  Stage 3: Subject acquires ability (training, magical gift, tool)
  Stage 4: Subject acts with skill (the performance)

  MODAL CONFLICTS generate plot:
  "I want to do X BUT I am unable" → quest for power
  "I have to do X BUT I don't want to" → inner conflict
  "I am able to BUT I don't know how" → training narrative
  "I know how BUT I am not permitted" → transgression narrative

  THIS IS A DEEP GRAMMAR OF MOTIVATION:
  Every narrative is about a subject being equipped with
  or lacking one or more of these modalities.
  Their acquisition = the hero's journey.
```

## Extensions and Applications

```
  GENDER AND NARRATIVE:
  Feminist narratology (Teresa de Lauretis): the actantial model
  naturalizes a male Subject pursuing a female Object.
  The model is not neutral — it encodes a cultural default.
  Deconstructing narratives means asking whose actantial roles
  are fixed by convention.

  COGNITIVE NARRATOLOGY:
  How do actual readers/viewers process narrative?
  Event models, mental representations of character goals.
  Marie-Laure Ryan: immersion, interactivity, narrative in digital media.

  INTERACTIVE NARRATIVE / GAMES:
  Propp's functions map onto game mechanics:
  Quest, item acquisition, boss battle, reward.
  "Hero's Journey" (Campbell → Christopher Vogler)
  = Propp/Greimas adapted for Hollywood screenwriting.

  DISCOURSE ANALYSIS:
  AJ Greimas was applied to organizational communication,
  legal narratives, political discourse.
  "How does the company tell its story?"
  = Actantial analysis of the corporate narrative.

  ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE:
  Propp's morphology was one of the first story grammars.
  Roger Schank: scripts and plans (1977) — structured understanding
  of narrative events. Direct descendant.
  Modern: narrative generation systems often use Propp/Greimas
  as underlying story grammar.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Actant ≠ character**: This is the most important distinction. An actant is a structural role. One character can fill multiple actant roles simultaneously (the mentor who is also the dispatcher and donor). Multiple characters can share one actant role (all the people helping the hero). The model is functional, not persona-based.

**Propp's 31 functions are specifically for folk tales**: Propp analyzed a specific corpus (100 Russian folk tales). His functions describe that corpus. Greimas generalized into a theory applicable to all narrative. Applying Propp's 31 functions directly to a 20th-century novel often feels forced.

**The semiotic square is not just a 2×2 matrix**: The relations between the four terms (contrariety, contradiction, implication) are the content, not just the four cells. Reading the square means understanding which moves between positions are logically and culturally available.

**Narrative grammar ≠ literary criticism**: Greimas is doing something structuralist — he wants to describe the deep grammar that generates possible narratives, not to evaluate whether specific narratives are good or interesting. Narrative semiotics is generative and descriptive, not evaluative.

**The hero's journey (Campbell) ≠ Propp or Greimas**: Joseph Campbell's "monomyth" is a popular synthesis drawing on Jung and myth scholarship, not on Propp. Christopher Vogler's Hollywood adaptation ("The Writer's Journey") is even further from structural semiotics. The similarity to Propp is real but the theoretical bases are very different.
