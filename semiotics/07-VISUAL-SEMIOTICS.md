# Visual and Film Semiotics

## The Problem of Visual Signs

Verbal language is linear and arbitrary. Visual signs are spatial, simultaneous, and often motivated (iconic). Extending semiotic theory to images, film, and visual design requires rethinking the basic categories — the sign, the code, the opposition system.

## Barthes: "Rhetoric of the Image" (1964)

Barthes's analysis of a Panzani pasta advertisement — the foundational text of visual semiotics.

### Three Messages in the Image

```
  THE IMAGE (Panzani ad):
  Shopping bag with tomatoes, pepper, mushrooms, and Panzani pasta.

  MESSAGE 1: LINGUISTIC
  The brand name "Panzani" + captions.
  Function: ANCHORAGE
  Language ANCHORS the meaning of the image — narrows interpretation.
  "Anchors the floating chain of signifieds."
  Without text, images are ambiguous (polysemous).
  Text selects and fixes the preferred reading.

  MESSAGE 2: CODED ICONIC (Connotation)
  The signs are:
  - Freshness (the fresh vegetables)
  - Italianicity (the colors: green/white/red of Italian flag)
  - Still-life/domestic scene (the composition)
  These require CULTURAL DECODING — knowing the code (Italian cuisine
  associations, domestic bourgeois aesthetics, etc.).
  This is CONNOTATION — second-order signification.
  These meanings are LEARNED, not natural.

  MESSAGE 3: NON-CODED ICONIC (Denotation)
  The image is of actual objects. It is a photograph of things.
  The "photographic analogue" — the image seems to record
  reality without transformation.
  But: Barthes notes even denotation is coded.
  The photograph's own technique (framing, focus, lighting)
  constitutes choices — nothing is purely "natural."

  KEY CONCEPT: RELAY vs ANCHORAGE
  ANCHORAGE: text fixes the image's ambiguous signification
  RELAY: text and image are complementary — each contributes
         what the other lacks (common in comics, film dialogue)
```

## Metz and Film Semiotics

Christian Metz (1931–1993) applied structural linguistics to film, asking: is film a language?

### Film as Language (and Its Limits)

```
  IS FILM A LANGUAGE?
  ANSWER (Metz): Film is a language system (langage), not a language
  (langue) in Saussure's sense.

  WHY NOT LANGUE:
  No double articulation (phonemes + morphemes).
  Film has no second articulation — no meaningless units that
  combine to form meaningful units.
  No arbitrary signs — film images are iconic (motivated).
  No paradigmatic system of discrete elements in the way
  phonology has.

  WHY IT IS LANGUAGE-LIKE (langage):
  It has CODES — shared conventions for interpretation.
  The shot/counter-shot convention (editing for conversation).
  The close-up = emphasis, emotional significance.
  Fading to black = time passes.
  These are LEARNED conventions, not natural perception.

  METZ'S KEY DISTINCTION:
  First cinema (early 1900s): each shot = a scene. No grammar.
  Classical cinema (1915+): editing conventions developed.
  = A grammar emerged historically.
  Griffith invented many of these conventions; they became codes.
```

### The Grande Syntagmatique

```
  Metz's most systematic contribution: a taxonomy of how
  sequences of shots are organized into larger units.

  8 SYNTAGMATIC TYPES in classical narrative film:

  1. AUTONOMOUS SHOT: a single uninterrupted shot
     = one shot, one event (rare; an insert, a subjective shot)

  2. PARALLEL SYNTAGM: alternation of two series of events
     that are not contiguous in diegetic space or time
     (images of poverty alternating with images of wealth)

  3. BRACKET SYNTAGM: single shot or series summing up a
     type of event (without temporal/spatial continuity)
     (montage sequence: "life went on for years...")

  4. DESCRIPTIVE SYNTAGM: spatially contiguous elements
     shown in succession (panning/cutting around a location)
     Time is suspended; it's a spatial description.

  5. ALTERNATING SYNTAGM (cross-cutting): alternation of
     two or more events shown as SIMULTANEOUS (temporal continuity)
     (rescue cross-cut with danger; Griffith's classic form)

  6. SCENE: classical narrative sequence — appears to show
     continuous action in single time and space.
     Cuts are subordinated to continuous action.

  7. EPISODIC SEQUENCE: series of brief scenes connected by
     dissolves/fades implying temporal ellipsis.
     A "montage sequence" in Hollywood convention.

  8. ORDINARY SEQUENCE: narrative sequence showing selected
     instants from a continuous action; some moments are
     implied between shown shots. Most common in narrative film.

  THESE ARE CODES: Hollywood conventions that audiences learn.
  Audiences from cultures without these conventions
  initially misread the temporal/spatial logic.
```

## Kress and van Leeuwen: Grammar of Visual Design

Gunther Kress (1940–2019) and Theo van Leeuwen developed social semiotics for visual communication, extending Halliday's social semiotic linguistics to image analysis.

### Three Metafunctions (from Halliday)

```
  Halliday's metafunctions adapted to visual:

  1. REPRESENTATIONAL MEANING:
     What is the image about? What is represented?
     Narrative processes: action, events, vectors
     Conceptual processes: classification, analysis, symbol

     NARRATIVE: images showing action/events
     Vectors (diagonal lines, gestures, gaze) = action
     Actors → action → goals
     "The man hits the ball" — the ball is the GOAL

     CONCEPTUAL: images classifying or symbolizing
     Taxonomic: tree diagrams, organizational charts
     Symbolic: attributes defining identity (crown = royalty)

  2. INTERACTIVE MEANING:
     Who is looking at whom? What relationship?

     GAZE:
     DEMAND: depicted person looks DIRECTLY at viewer.
     = demands something from viewer; creates relationship.
     OFFER: depicted person does not look at viewer.
     = viewer is unseen observer.

     SOCIAL DISTANCE:
     Close-up = intimate (can see pores)
     Medium shot = social distance
     Long shot = public distance

     POWER:
     Camera above subject → viewer has power (they look down)
     Camera below subject → subject has power (they loom)
     Eye-level → equal relationship

  3. COMPOSITIONAL MEANING:
     How are elements arranged? What is foregrounded?

     INFORMATION VALUE:
     Given (left) / New (right) — LEFT = familiar, RIGHT = unfamiliar
     Ideal (top) / Real (bottom) — TOP = aspiration/promise, BOTTOM = fact
     Centre / Margin — central = nucleus of information

     SALIENCE: Size, focus, color, position determining what draws eye.
     FRAMING: Degree of connection/disconnection between elements.
```

## Psychoanalytic Film Theory: The Gaze

Laura Mulvey's "Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema" (1975) introduced psychoanalytic categories into film semiotics.

```
  MULVEY'S ARGUMENT:
  Classical Hollywood cinema is structured around a
  MALE GAZE — the spectator position constructed by
  mainstream narrative film is implicitly male.

  THREE GAZES IN CINEMA:
  1. Camera's gaze (as it records)
  2. Audience's gaze (as it watches)
  3. Characters' gazes at each other within the film

  These are organized to produce PLEASURE for the male spectator:
  Scopophilia (pleasure in looking) + narcissistic identification.

  THE WOMAN:
  Displayed as spectacle — the "to-be-looked-at-ness" of women.
  She connotes the visual; he drives the narrative.
  The male protagonist controls the look (active).
  The female character is the object of the look (passive).

  SCREEN THEORY CONTEXT:
  This applies Lacan (mirror stage, gaze, the symbolic order)
  + Freud (scopophilia, castration anxiety as visual pleasure motivation)
  to cinema.

  LEGACY:
  Enormously influential in film studies and cultural theory.
  Later critiques: the spectator position is more complex;
  female viewers can take up any position; queer theory
  further complicates who sees whom how.
```

## Codes in Visual Communication

```
  VISUAL CODES (Kress & van Leeuwen, Hall, Eco):

  PERCEPTUAL CODES:
  Depth cues, perspective, color, figure/ground —
  largely universal (rooted in perception).
  But: perspective in Western art = learned convention.
  African, Japanese, medieval European art use different
  conventions that seem "wrong" to trained Western eyes.

  CULTURAL/CONVENTIONAL CODES:
  Flags, logos, religious iconography.
  Red cross = medical care (Western convention).
  Color symbolism: white = purity (West); mourning (East Asia).
  Highly culturally specific; no universality.

  MODALITY:
  The degree to which an image claims to be "real."
  High modality: color photograph, realistic detail.
  Low modality: sketch, diagram, cartoon, abstraction.
  Context-dependent: in a scientific diagram, low modality
  (abstract) = HIGH information value; NOT low reliability.
  Modality is assessed relative to the code's standards.

  TYPOGRAPHY AS VISUAL SIGN:
  Font = visual form that connotes:
  Serif → authority, tradition, print history
  Sans-serif → modernity, clarity, screen
  Handwriting → personal, informal, authentic
  Grotesque → brutalism, shock, aggression
  The visual form of text is itself a semiotic resource.
```

## Eco's Critique: The Icon Problem

```
  UMBERTO ECO (A Theory of Semiotics, 1976):
  "Icons are not natural signs."
  The apparent naturalness of pictures is itself coded.

  EVIDENCE:
  Young children must LEARN to read photographs.
  3D perspective drawing is a European convention.
  X-rays require training to interpret correctly.
  Medical imaging: experts see disease; novices see blur.

  ECO'S CLAIM:
  What makes an image seem "natural" or "realistic" is
  a set of perceptual conventions we have internalized.
  These conventions vary across cultures and historical periods.

  IMPLICATIONS:
  Peirce's icon category does not escape convention.
  Even the most "direct" resemblance relationship is
  structured by codes we bring to perception.
  There is no "raw," unmediated visual experience.
  (This is Derrida's point extended to images.)

  BUT: This doesn't make all images equally conventional.
  A photograph does have a causal connection to its referent.
  The indexical dimension (light → sensor → pixel) is real.
  Eco is critiquing the naïve account, not denying
  that photographs are different from arbitrary symbols.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| Anchorage (Barthes) | Text fixing the ambiguous meaning of an image to a preferred reading |
| Relay (Barthes) | Text and image are complementary — each contributes what the other lacks |
| Demand vs Offer (gaze) | Demand: subject looks at viewer; Offer: subject does not look at viewer |
| Grande Syntagmatique | Metz's 8 types of shot sequence organization in classical film |
| Modality (Kress) | The degree to which a visual claim presents itself as "real" — context-dependent |
| Given/New (composition) | Left = given (familiar); Right = new (information value in composition) |
| Ideal/Real (composition) | Top = promise/aspiration; Bottom = factual/grounded |
| Double articulation | Meaningful units (morphemes) composed of meaningless units (phonemes) — film lacks this |

## Common Confusion Points

**Images are NOT naturally transparent**: Even photographs require learned perceptual codes to interpret. Young children, people from cultures without photography, and viewers of unusual imaging modalities (MRI, X-ray) must LEARN to read images. The apparent naturalness is itself a learned convention that has been internalized.

**Anchorage ≠ caption**: While captions often anchor, anchorage is a semiotic function, not a physical format. Any text in or around an image can anchor. Even brand names anchor (Panzani + the image → Italianicity anchored).

**Metz's grande syntagmatique is for classical Hollywood, not for all film**: These editing conventions are historical — they emerged in a specific tradition. Avant-garde film, documentary film, and non-Western cinema use different conventions. Applying the grande syntagmatique to Dziga Vertov or Ozu would be inappropriate.

**"Gaze" in film theory ≠ "gaze" in everyday usage**: In Lacanian film theory, "the gaze" is specifically a psychoanalytic concept involving desire, power, and the construction of the viewing subject. It is not simply "what someone looks at."

**Kress and van Leeuwen's social semiotics ≠ structuralist semiotics**: Where Saussure saw fixed codes, Kress and van Leeuwen see "semiotic resources" — potentials for meaning-making that are actualized differently in different contexts. Signs are not fixed — they are made in use, by sign-makers, in response to social contexts. This is a significant departure from structuralist semiotics.
