# 01 — Prehistoric & Earth Pigments

## Ochre to Umber — The First Palette, 100,000 Years of Iron Oxides

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Ochre** (yellow, red, brown): iron oxide/hydroxide minerals; yellow ochre = goethite (α-FeOOH); red ochre = hematite (α-Fe₂O₃); converting yellow to red by heating (dehydration: FeOOH → Fe₂O₃ at ~200°C) — prehistoric peoples understood this transformation; wide geographic availability (iron is 4th most abundant element in Earth's crust)
- Blombos Cave (South Africa): ~75,000–100,000 BCE ochre processing workshop; ochre cakes with engraved geometric patterns; abalone shells used as mixing containers with ochre + charcoal + fat + bone powder — first identified paint recipe; cross-cutting (mixing) evidence shows intentional craft, not accidental
- Why ochre first: abundant, non-toxic, stable, easily applied, adhesive with fat binder; red associated with blood, life, fire — symbolic value across nearly all known ancient cultures independently
- **Burnt sienna and raw sienna**: named after Siena, Italy but found globally; raw sienna = yellow-brown (limonite + manganese oxides + clay); burnt sienna = deep reddish-brown (heat transforms limonite goethite → hematite); the subtle warm transparency makes them still valued in oil painting
- **Raw and burnt umber**: umber = Italian *terra d'ombra* (shadow earth) from Umbria, Italy; manganese dioxide (MnO₂) + iron oxides + clay; fast-drying in oil (manganese is a siccative/drying accelerant); burnt umber darker, cooler; Rembrandt's brown underpaintings; "dead layer" technique (grisaille) → glazing
- **Manganese dioxide** (black): natural pyrolusite; used alongside ochre in cave paintings for dark areas; Lascaux black outlines often manganese dioxide; alternative: charcoal (carbon black) and bone black
- **Cave painting technology**: Altamira (~36,000 BCE), Chauvet (~35,000 BCE), Lascaux (~17,000 BCE); pigment application — brushes (moss/hair/feather), blowing through tubes (spray painting!), finger painting; lamps burning animal fat for illumination; scaffolding evidence at some sites; pigment durability across tens of thousands of years explains the record
- **Charcoal and bone black**: carbon-based blacks; charcoal = incompletely burned wood/vine; bone black (ivory black) = charred bone — calcium phosphate + carbon, bluish undertone; most ancient black available; vine black (burned grape vines) = slightly different undertone
- **Green earth** (terre verte): celadonite and glauconite (iron-magnesium silicates); muted, cool gray-green; used in Byzantine icon painting as flesh underpainting (complementary undertone approach — green under warm flesh tones); Verona, Cyprus, Bohemia sources
- **Red lead / minium**: Pb₃O₄; occurs naturally but also manufactured by roasting lead white; vermilion-orange-red; used in manuscript illumination ("miniature" derives from *minium*, not from "mini"); toxic; confused with cinnabar historically
- Durability of earth pigments: iron oxides chemically stable across millennia; explains why prehistoric cave paintings survive; contrast with organic pigments that fade; modern artists still use earth pigments for permanence (ASTM Lightfastness I rating)
