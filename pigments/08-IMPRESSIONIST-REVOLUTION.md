# 08 — The Impressionist Revolution

## Tube Paint, Synthetic Ultramarine, Cadmiums — How Chemistry Enabled a Movement

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Tube paint** (1841): John G. Rand, American portrait painter in London; collapsible tin tube with screw cap; before this, painters ground pigments themselves or bought from colormen in bladders (pig bladders — messy, short shelf life, inconvenient in field); tubes enabled plein air painting (*en plein air*) — painting outdoors in natural light; Windsor & Newton manufactured Rand's tubes; direct causal link between tube paint and Impressionism
- Pre-tube field painting: watercolor already field-portable (William Reeves portable watercolor cakes, 1780); but oil painting required wet grinding, immediate use; portable paint chest with color grinding slab; Constable's cloud studies (1820s) — proto-plein air but laborious
- **Synthetic ultramarine** (1826): Jean-Baptiste Guimet (France) and Christian Gmelin (Germany) independently solved the 3,000-year-old problem; process: calcine kaolin, sulfur, sodium sulfate/carbonate, and carbon; reproduces natural lazurite formation conditions in kiln; immediately patented and commercialized; price drop from ~$2,000/oz (natural lapis) to ~$0.25/oz within a decade; transformed painting — previously only affordable by nobility/church for special commissions
- Synthetic vs natural ultramarine: chemically identical (same lazurite structure); slight particle size and shape differences affect handling; synthetic slightly more greenish-blue; natural lapis has warm, slightly violet undertone due to calcite particles; both still available; natural lapis ultra-premium (Williamsburg Paints, etc.)
- **Cadmium yellow** (CdS): Henri Stromeyer discovered cadmium 1817; cadmium sulfide pigments commercialized ~1840s; cadmium yellow (CdS, yellow), cadmium orange (CdS/CdSe mix), cadmium red (CdSe, cadmium selenide); brilliant, opaque, excellent lightfastness; replaced chrome yellow (unstable) and Naples yellow (toxic lead); cadmium pigments themselves somewhat toxic (heavy metal) but fixed in oil/acrylic matrix have low bioavailability; EU regulations on cadmium restricting use (artist-grade still permitted)
- Cadmium band gap engineering: CdS has band gap 2.42 eV (yellow); CdSe has band gap 1.74 eV (red); mixed CdS₁₋ₓSeₓ → band gap varies continuously with composition → smooth range from yellow to red; classic semiconductor band gap color tuning; same principle as modern LED phosphors and quantum dots
- **Zinc white** (ZnO): discovered ~1782, produced commercially ~1840s; alternative to lead white; non-toxic; cooler, slightly blue-tinted white; slower drying; more brittle film (less flexible than lead white → prone to cracking in thick applications); used in watercolor (Chinese white); useful in combination with other whites
- **Cerulean and cobalt**: cerulean blue adopted by Impressionists for sky painting; Turner's cloud studies; Monet's sky blues
- **Viridian** adoption: Impressionist green landscapes; Cézanne as heavy viridian user; replacement for toxic emerald green
- Impressionist palette implications: Monet's lily pond blues (cobalt, ultramarine, cerulean, Prussian blue); Renoir's flesh tints (new range of subtle pinks from lakes + cadmiums); Seurat's pointillist color theory (simultaneous contrast, optical mixing); systematic application of Chevreul's and Maxwell's color theory to painting enabled by stable, controllable new pigments
- **Organic synthetic dyes** meeting painting: William Perkin's mauveine (1856, accidental first synthetic dye); rapid proliferation of aniline dyes; some adopted as pigments, mostly fugitive; alizarin (1869) replaced natural madder; still used as alizarin crimson lake (ASTM II — slightly fugitive but acceptable)
