# 10 — Conservation & Authentication

## Fugitive Colors, Lightfastness, Forensic Pigment Analysis

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Why pigments degrade**: photodegradation (UV breaks conjugated π systems in organic chromophores); oxidation (metals change valence state — chrome yellow Cr⁶⁺ → Cr³⁺); hydrolysis (moisture attacks certain bonds); sulfidation (atmospheric H₂S + lead/silver → dark sulfides); migration (pigment particles move in binder); cross-reactions between incompatible pigments (lead + sulfur pigments darken each other)
- **Fugitive colors**: definition — pigments that fade/change significantly on light exposure; most lake pigments (organic dye on substrate) are fugitive; historical paint books explicitly warned about fugitive colors; watercolor especially vulnerable (no protective varnish, thinner pigment layer); Constable's early sketches now much paler than originally painted; Van Gogh wrote about permanent vs fugitive colors in letters
- **Lightfastness rating systems**:
  - ASTM D4303 (USA): I (excellent, ≥100 yr in museum), II (very good, ≥25 yr), III (not sufficient), IV (poor), V (very poor)
  - Blue Wool Scale (ISO 105-B02, textile industry): 1–8; wool samples with known fugitive-to-stable range; comparison standard
  - Rating determined by accelerated fade testing (xenon arc lamp, controlled humidity/temperature) vs reference standards
- **Notable color changes in famous works**:
  - Van Gogh's *Sunflowers*: chrome yellow → darkened chrome green/brown (photoreduction of PbCrO₄); reconstruction shows originally brighter yellows
  - Vermeer's *Woman in Blue Reading a Letter*: azurite sky degraded to blue-gray; malachite/azurite conversion
  - Raphael's *Madonna of the Goldfinch*: lead white darkened in pollution; cleaned and restored
  - Renoir's lake-based pinks: faded in many works; original saturation lost
  - Smalt gray in Rembrandt: cobalt smalt lost blue in oil medium → gray-brown areas
  - El Greco flesh tones: lead white + smalt mixtures
- **Varnish yellowing**: traditional copal and mastic varnishes yellow significantly with age (oxidized polyterpenes); obscures colors; cleaning varnish from paintings = major conservation intervention; Maclaren (1952) and subsequent scholarship on varnish chemistry; modern synthetic varnishes (Paraloid B-72, Laropal K80) more stable and reversible
- **X-ray fluorescence (XRF)**: non-destructive elemental analysis; identify lead (lead white, red lead, Naples yellow, chrome yellow), cadmium (cadmium yellow/red), mercury (vermilion), arsenic (orpiment, emerald green), cobalt, chromium; maps pigment distribution; standard in conservation labs
- **Raman spectroscopy**: non-destructive molecular fingerprinting; identifies specific pigment compounds even in complex mixtures; distinguishes malachite from verdigris, lapis from azurite, Egyptian blue from Han blue; portable Raman devices enable in-gallery analysis without moving works
- **Infrared reflectography (IRR)**: carbon-based drawing materials (black chalk, charcoal, carbon-based inks) absorb IR; underdrawing revealed under paint layers; artist's original drawing vs final painting; preparatory grid lines, pentimenti, changes of mind
- **Forensic authentication — pigment anachronism**: most powerful tool against forgery; if a painting supposedly from 1600 contains titanium white (commercial 1916) or phthalocyanine blue (1936), it's a forgery; Elmyr de Hory forgeries (detected partly by pigment analysis); Han van Meegeren Vermeer forgeries (detected by bakelite in paint — pre-war synthetic resin); Mark Landis (prolific art donator/forger) — detected via pigment analysis
- **Original polychrome**: ancient Greek and Roman marble sculpture was painted (not white); Elgin Marbles had paint; UV fluorescence and microsampling reveal pigment traces; "archeological color" (archäologische Farbe) reconstruction projects; the white marble aesthetic of Neoclassicism was built on misunderstanding
- **Decision cheat sheet**: pigment identification methods, degradation mechanisms, lightfastness categories, authentication red flags by era
