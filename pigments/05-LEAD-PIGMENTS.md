# 05 — Lead Pigments

## Lead White, Red Lead, Naples Yellow — 2,000 Years of Toxic Dominance

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Lead white** (basic lead carbonate, 2PbCO₃·Pb(OH)₂ — hydrocerussite + cerussite): the dominant white pigment for ~2,000 years until titanium white (1916); opaque, dense, warm-toned, excellent handling in oil; still used by some artists for unique properties
- Stack process (Dutch stack process): lead sheets suspended over vinegar in clay pots, stacked in horse manure; CO₂ from manure + acetic acid vapor convert lead to basic carbonate over several weeks; scaled to industrial production in Netherlands; "flake white" = traditional name; Krems white (Austrian variant); "silver white" (German)
- Lead white in painting: excellent oil absorption; forms hard, durable film; fast drying (lead is a siccative); "cremnitz white" = highest quality; oil-and-lead interaction → lead soap formation over centuries → certain transparency (pentimento effects); warms and becomes slightly transparent with age — important for flesh rendering
- Lead poisoning of painters: chronic lead poisoning (plumbism) a known occupational hazard; symptoms — abdominal colic, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive effects; Goya's late black paintings and health decline attributed partly to lead exposure; Francisco de Goya, Caravaggio, and many others worked with lead-based pigments daily; "painters' colic" historical diagnosis
- **Red lead** (minium, Pb₃O₄): lead(II,IV) oxide; orange-red; made by heating lead white or litharge in air; used as primer/undercoat (rust-inhibiting properties — still used industrially for metal priming under modern names); "minium" gave the word "miniature" (from its use in manuscript illumination); toxic
- **Naples yellow** (lead antimonate, Pb₂Sb₂O₇): opaque, pale yellow; ancient origin (found at Pompeii); manufactured by fusing lead and antimony oxides; the warm pale yellow of many Renaissance/Baroque portraits' flesh tones; incompatible with sulfide pigments (darkens); modern "Naples yellow hue" = cadmium yellow + white (non-toxic substitute)
- Lead tin yellow (Pb₂SnO₄ and PbSn₄O₄): warm, brilliant yellow used extensively in 15th–17th century European painting; identified in many Old Master works via X-ray fluorescence; manufacture and use largely forgotten by 18th century, rediscovered by art historians in 20th century via analytical chemistry
- **Degradation chemistry**: PbS formation — lead white + hydrogen sulfide (from air pollution) → lead sulfide (black); explains darkening of lead-white areas in polluted urban environments; reversal possible via hydrogen peroxide (oxidizes PbS back to PbSO₄ white) — used in conservation; also: lead white becomes more transparent in oil over time via lead soap formation
- Lead chromate (chrome yellow): 19th century pigment, see Prussian Blue Era file; Van Gogh's Sunflowers darkening = chrome yellow photoreduction
- Replacement: titanium white (1916) and zinc white (19th century) both non-toxic alternatives; zinc white (ZnO) — cooler, slightly less opaque, slower drying; still used; titanium white (TiO₂, rutile or anatase) — highest refractive index of any white pigment (2.7, vs lead white 2.0), maximum opacity, non-toxic; now >95% of white paint globally
