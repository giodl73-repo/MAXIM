# 09 — Modern Synthetic Pigments

## Titanium White, Phthalocyanines, Quinacridones — 20th-Century Color Chemistry

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Titanium white** (TiO₂): discovered 1821 (Gregor/Klaproth), commercial production begins 1916; rutile polymorph preferred for pigment (higher refractive index than anatase); refractive index 2.70–2.73 (rutile) = highest of any white pigment; compare lead white 2.0, ZnO 2.0; high RI → high scattering power → maximum opacity; non-toxic (food-safe — E171); photocatalytic activity (anatase degrades organic molecules under UV — photocatalytic bleaching/yellowing in some applications)
- Titanium white replacing lead white: by ~1940, titanium white dominant; lead white still used by some artists (different handling, still available) but no longer in house paint; lead paint ban in USA ~1978; titanium now >95% of white pigment globally (~5 million tons/year); applications: paint, plastic, paper (brightener), food, sunscreen (nano-TiO₂ physical UV blocker)
- **Phthalocyanine blue** (PB15, copper phthalocyanine): discovered 1928 (Scottish Dyes Ltd), accidentally; developed by Linstead/Dandridge (ICI, 1934); most lightfast blue organic pigment available; intense, transparent, cool greenish-blue; extended aromatic porphyrin-like structure (18-π-electron system); copper central atom; ASTM Lightfastness I (will not fade); now ubiquitous — most commercial blues in paint, plastics, ink
- Phthalocyanine green (PG7): copper phthalocyanine with 14–16 chlorine atoms substituted; shifting chlorination shifts color from blue-green to yellow-green; PG36 = partial bromine substitution → more yellow-green; both extremely stable; replaced viridian and emerald green in commercial applications
- **Quinacridone** (PV19, PR122 etc.): synthesized 1935 (Liebermann), pigment form 1958 (DuPont); linear acene structure, quinacridone core; range from orange-red to violet depending on crystal polymorph (polymorphism key to color variety — same molecule, different packing → different color); excellent lightfastness; transparent, high tinting strength; replaced alizarin crimson and many fugitive magentas in modern painting
- **Dioxazine violet** (PV23): large aromatic molecule; deep, strong violet; so strong it must be used sparingly; the most common violet in modern paint
- **Carbon black**: long history (lamp black, vine black); modern: furnace black (petroleum feedstock); the blackest blacks; high surface area → absorbent; used in rubber (tire production consumes ~70% of carbon black production); inkjet printing, toner; different from historical bone black (see prehistoric file)
- **Fluorescent pigments**: daylight fluorescent — absorb UV and convert to visible light → appear to glow; impossible effect before synthetic fluorescent organic molecules (naphthalimides, rhodamines, coumarins); not lightfast (fluorophore degrades); used in safety clothing, highlighters, signs; some "DayGlo" colors only possible with fluorescent pigments
- **Interference pigments**: mica flakes coated with thin transparent oxide layers (TiO₂, Fe₂O₃); thin-film interference → iridescent/pearlescent effects; angle-dependent color (structural color); automotive "pearl" and "flip-flop" paints; cosmetics (lip gloss shimmer); not traditional chromophore-based
- **Quantum dot pigments**: semiconductor nanocrystals (CdSe, InP, perovskites); color tuned by particle size (quantum confinement — smaller = higher energy = bluer, larger = redder); same material, all colors possible; narrow emission bands → highly saturated colors; used in display technology (QLED TVs), photovoltaics, biomedical imaging; some reaching paint/ink applications
- **Carbon nanotubes and graphene**: deep, near-perfect blacks (Vantablack, 99.965% light absorption — carbon nanotube forest); controversial in art world (Stuart Semple vs Anish Kapoor exclusivity dispute); structural absorption, not chromophore-based
