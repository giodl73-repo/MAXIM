# 03 — Purple, Red & Luxury Colorants

## Tyrian Purple, Cochineal, and the Politics of Expensive Color

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Tyrian purple** (6,6′-dibromoindigo): from *Murex brandaris*, *Murex trunculus*, and *Stramonita haemastoma* sea snails; Phoenician monopoly based in Tyre (hence Tyrian); produced by crushing snails' hypobranchial gland — precursor dibromoindigotin forms on exposure to air and sunlight; ~10,000 snails per gram of dye; Tyre's harbor archaeological evidence — mountain of crushed Murex shells
- Tyrian purple chemistry: 6,6′-dibromoindigo (same core structure as indigo but with bromine substitution at 6-position of each indole ring); bromine atoms shift absorption from ~610nm (indigo, blue) to ~580nm → reddish-purple; exceptional lightfastness (brominates indigo more stable than unsubstituted)
- Purple as power: Roman emperors' purple toga (*toga picta*); "born to the purple" (*porphyrogennetos*) — Byzantine princes born in purple chamber; Sumptuary laws restricting purple use to royalty; Emperor Aurelian refused to buy his wife a purple robe (too expensive even for him); fall of Constantinople (1453) ends Byzantine purple monopoly; purple = power, unaffordability
- *Murex trunculus* blue: when the same snail's gland exposed to sunlight WITHOUT air contact → produces regular indigo (not brominated) → blue; archaeologists now believe "tekhelet" (sacred blue of Hebrew priestly garments) was from Murex, not a plant; Hillazon Tachti cave evidence; modern revival of tekhelet production
- **Cochineal** (*Dactylopius coccus*): scale insect living on prickly pear cactus (*Opuntia*); cultivated by Aztecs in Mexico (Oaxaca and Tlaxcala centers of production); carminic acid (C₂₂H₂₀O₁₃) = anthraquinone glycoside; ~17–24% of dried insect weight; females collected, dried, ground; ~70,000 insects per pound of dye
- Cochineal's New World monopoly: Spanish brought cochineal from Mexico ~1523; second most valuable export from New Spain after silver for two centuries; Spain guarded source identity jealously — Europeans knew only it was called "grana" and came from Spain; naturalists debated whether it was mineral, vegetable, or animal until Antoni van Leeuwenhoek (1703) confirmed with microscope; English and Dutch attempted to establish competing sources but failed
- Cochineal chemistry: carminic acid produces crimson in acid conditions, purple in alkaline; mordant-dependent (aluminum mordant → scarlet, iron mordant → gray, tin mordant → brilliant scarlet); scarlet of British redcoats; cardinals' red robes; replaced by synthetic alizarin after 1869; still used as E120 in food/cosmetics — one of the most common red food colorings
- **Kermes** (*Kermes vermilio*, *K. ilicis*): scale insect on kermes oak (*Quercus coccifera*) in Mediterranean; kermesic acid (similar anthraquinone structure to carminic acid); the European red before cochineal arrived; far weaker than cochineal (~1.5% dye content vs 17-24%); word origin: Sanskrit *kṛmi* (worm) → Arabic *qirmizī* → crimson; replaced by cochineal within decades of cochineal's introduction — price and intensity no contest
- **Lac dye** (*Kerria lacca*): Asian scale insect; laccaic acid (anthraquinone); primary red dye of Asia before cochineal; shellac (resin same insect) is the unrelated product; India, Thailand, Myanmar production
- **Madder lake** (alizarin + purpurin from *Rubia tinctorum*): the red dye from madder root; not a natural pigment — must be precipitated onto aluminum hydroxide substrate to make "lake" pigment; alizarin (1,2-dihydroxyanthraquinone); Turkey red (alum mordant process); alizarin synthesized synthetically by Perkin (England) and Graebe/Liebermann (Germany) in 1869 — simultaneously — destroying madder agriculture overnight; Vermeer's lake pigments now fugitive → faded pink areas in his paintings
