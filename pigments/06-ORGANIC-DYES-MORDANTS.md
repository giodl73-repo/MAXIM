# 06 — Organic Dyes & Mordants

## Indigo, Madder, Weld — Plant Chemistry and the Fabric of Trade

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Organic colorants: carbon-based; mostly from plants (and some insects — see purple/red file); key challenge: most organic molecules photodegrade (UV breaks conjugated π systems → loss of chromophore); hence "fugitive" colors; trade-off between organic (complex, varied) vs inorganic (simpler, more stable)
- **Indigo** (indigotin, C₁₆H₁₀N₂O₂): the dominant blue textile dye for most of human history; one molecule found in two completely different plants: *Isatis tinctoria* (woad, European) and *Indigofera tinctoria* (true indigo, tropical Asian/American)
- Woad vs indigo: same molecule (indigotin), dramatically different concentration; woad leaves ~0.1–0.4% indigotin; *Indigofera* leaves ~1–4%; tropical *Indigofera* plants yield 10× more dye; when Indian/Caribbean indigo reached Europe, woad industry (major in France, Germany, England) collapsed within decades; French and German woad growers lobbied for indigo bans ("devil's dye") unsuccessfully
- Indigo chemistry: indigotin itself insoluble in water; reduction to leucoindigo (yellow-green, soluble) in alkaline vat → fabric absorbs leucoindigo → re-oxidized on exposure to air → insoluble indigotin locked in fiber; traditional vats used stale urine (ammonia + fermentation) or plant-based fermentation; the chemistry of "vatting" is microbiological reduction
- Indigo and colonialism: Portuguese Vasco da Gama's route made Indian indigo accessible; slave plantation indigo in South Carolina and Caribbean; Elizabeth Lucas Pinckney (South Carolina, 1740) established indigo as major cash crop; Bengal indigo plantation system and the Indigo Revolt (1859–1860, Neel Bidraha) — indigo farmers' uprising against British planters, precursor to Indian independence movement
- Synthetic indigo: Adolf von Baeyer synthesized indigo (1865, 19th attempt); BASF industrial process (1897); killed the natural indigo industry within a decade; still the dye in blue jeans (denim — per denim = "from Nîmes", serge de Nîmes); synthetic indigo ~22,000 tons/year, overwhelmingly for denim
- **Madder** (*Rubia tinctorum*): root contains alizarin (1,2-dihydroxyanthraquinone) and purpurin; the primary red dye of Europe and Asia for millennia; Turkey red (alizarin + aluminum mordant + turkey oil — a complex process developed in India, brought to Europe ~1770); alizarin synthesized by Perkin/Graebe-Liebermann (1869) → madder agriculture collapses; alizarin crimson (synthetic alizarin lake) still used by artists (slightly fugitive — ASTM Lightfastness II)
- **Weld** (*Reseda luteola*): primary yellow dye plant in Europe; luteolin + apigenin (flavones); with alum mordant → clear, bright yellow; used with woad to make "Lincoln green" (Robin Hood's green — woad blue × weld yellow); moderately fugitive; largely replaced by quercitron (North American black oak bark) and later synthetic flavone dyes
- **Mordant chemistry**: most organic dyes cannot bond directly to fiber without a mordant (from Latin *mordere*, to bite); mordant = metallic salt that bonds to both fiber and dye simultaneously; alum (potassium aluminum sulfate) → warm, bright colors; ferrous sulfate (iron mordant) → darker, sadder tones, "saddening"; copper sulfate → greener tones; tin mordant → bright, fluorescent-like scarlet (with cochineal); mordant choice shifts color dramatically with the same dye
- **Lake pigments**: organic dye precipitated onto inorganic substrate (usually aluminum hydroxide, Al(OH)₃) to make an insoluble particle; converts soluble dye to usable pigment; carmine lake (cochineal), crimson lake, rose madder, yellow lake (weld); fugitivity of organic chromophore means many lake pigments fade; "lake" etymology disputed (Arabic *lakk* or Sanskrit *lākshā* — both referring to lac resin)
- **Quercitron** (black oak bark, *Quercus velutina*): North American; quercetin + quercitrin; briefly major yellow dye before synthetic alternatives; discovered by Edward Bancroft (1775); efficient yellow without weld's geographic limitations
