# 07 — Prussian Blue Era

## Chrome Yellow, Cobalt, Emerald Green — The 18th-Century Synthetic Revolution

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Prussian blue** (Fe₄[Fe(CN)₆]₃ — iron(III) hexacyanoferrate(II)): discovered 1704 in Berlin by colormaker Johann Jacob Diesbach — accidentally contaminated a red lake experiment with potash that had been used to process animal blood (providing cyanide); Diesbach's partner Johann Conrad Dippel; the first modern synthetic pigment produced by intentional (if accidental) chemistry rather than processing natural materials
- Prussian blue chemistry: iron(III) hexacyanoferrate — mixed-valence iron complex; intense blue from metal-to-metal charge transfer (Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺) through cyanide bridges; not *d-d* transition — explains intense color and high extinction coefficient; open framework crystal structure that can absorb heavy metal cations (hence its use as antidote for thallium and cesium-137 poisoning — FDA approved)
- Prussian blue in art: immediately adopted by European painters; Watteau, Canaletto, Hogarth; cheaper and more stable than smalt and azurite; Hokusai's *The Great Wave off Kanagawa* (1831) — blue is Prussian blue, introduced to Japan via Dutch traders; Van Gogh's night scenes; turns green in some binders over time
- **Chrome yellow** (lead chromate, PbCrO₄): discovered by Louis Nicolas Vauquelin (discoverer of chromium, 1798); range from pale yellow to deep orange depending on crystal form; Van Gogh's *Sunflowers* and many other post-1800 yellow areas; photochemical darkening — chrome yellow reduces to chromium(III) oxide (green) on UV exposure in presence of sulfur; explains brown-green areas of Van Gogh's sunflowers that X-ray fluorescence shows were originally bright yellow
- Chrome green: mixture of Prussian blue + chrome yellow; became "chrome green" when chemists produced chromium oxide pigments; Chrome oxide green (Cr₂O₃) — extremely stable, opaque, dull; viridian (see Impressionist file) is hydrated version
- **Scheele's green** (copper arsenite, CuHAsO₃): Carl Wilhelm Scheele (1778, same chemist who discovered chlorine and oxygen but died young of chemical exposure); brilliant, vivid green; immediately popular in wallpapers, textiles, confections; highly toxic (arsenic); "Paris green" = emerald green (see below); Scheele's green possibly responsible for Napoleon's death on St. Helena (green wallpaper in damp conditions → Gosio gas, trimethylarsine, produced by mold)
- **Emerald green** (Paris green, copper acetoarsenite, Cu(C₂H₃O₂)₂·3Cu(AsO₂)₂): even more brilliant green than Scheele's; adopted by Impressionists for landscape painting; also an effective insecticide (used against Colorado potato beetles and as a mosquito larvicide in malaria control); highly toxic; Cézanne's neuropathy, Monet's cataracts, and health problems of multiple 19th century painters may relate to arsenic exposure; eventually banned/abandoned
- **Viridian** (hydrated chromium oxide, Cr₂O₃·2H₂O): transparent, cool, stable green; synthesized 1838 (Pannetier, Paris); adopted by Impressionists as safe alternative to arsenic greens; still widely used (ASTM Lightfastness I); Sargent, Monet, Pissarro
- **Cobalt blue** (CoAl₂O₄): Thénard (1802); spinel structure; stable, intense, slightly warm blue; replaced smalt; modern uses in ceramics (Delftware blue was cobalt-based), glass, artist paint
- **Cerulean blue** (cobaltous stannate, CoO·nSnO₂): Hoppfner (1805), commercialized 1860; sky blue, greenish; the sky color — originally called "coeruleum" (Latin for sky-blue); still used
- Industrial chemistry's impact: from 1800–1900, systematic chemistry produced more new pigments than the previous 3,000 years combined; each required understanding of inorganic synthesis; colormaking transformed from craft/trade secret to chemical industry; BASF and Bayer (Germany) emerge as pigment/dye giants
