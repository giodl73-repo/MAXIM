# 00 — Pigments: Overview

## Color, Chemistry, and the History of Paint — From Cave Walls to Quantum Dots

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Pigment vs dye distinction: pigment = insoluble colored particle, dispersed in medium (binder), stays as a particle; dye = soluble colored molecule, forms solution, bonds to substrate; mordant dyes = dye made into pigment by precipitating onto inert substrate (lake pigments — aluminum hydroxide + mordant dye = "lake"); practical implications for permanence, working properties, application
- How pigments produce color: two main mechanisms:
  - Selective absorption (most pigments): molecule/crystal absorbs specific wavelengths, reflects others; absorbed = color's complement; red iron oxide absorbs blue/green, reflects red
  - Structural color (not pigment-based but important contrast): iridescence from thin-film interference or photonic crystals (butterfly wings, opal) — no absorption, color from wavelength-selective constructive interference
- Chromophore mechanisms by chemical class:
  - Transition metal complexes: *d-d* electron transitions (crystal field theory); iron oxides (ochre/sienna), copper carbonates (malachite/azurite), cobalt blue, chromium oxide green; color tuned by ligand field strength (spectrochemical series)
  - Charge transfer: lapis lazuli — electron transfer between sulfur radical anions in zeolite framework (not simple *d-d*); intensely blue, high extinction coefficient
  - Organic π→π* transitions: organic pigments (phthalocyanines, quinacridones, indigo); extended conjugated π systems; HOMO-LUMO gap in visible range; larger conjugation → longer wavelength absorption (redder)
  - Band gap absorption: inorganic semiconductors (cadmium sulfide/yellow, cadmium selenide/red, lead chromate/yellow); color determined by band gap energy; explains why cadmium series spans yellow → orange → red as selenium substitutes for sulfur
- History arc (full timeline):
  - ~100,000 BCE: ochre processing at Blombos Cave (earliest evidence of deliberate pigment use)
  - ~40,000 BCE: cave paintings (Chauvet, Altamira, Lascaux) — ochre, manganese dioxide, charcoal
  - ~3,100 BCE: Egyptian blue — first synthetic pigment intentionally manufactured
  - ~1500 BCE: Tyrian purple — from Murex sea snails; most expensive colorant in antiquity
  - ~1200–600 BCE: lapis lazuli trade from Badakhshan to Mediterranean
  - ~100 CE: Roman lead white (stack process), vermilion (ground cinnabar)
  - ~1704: Prussian blue — first modern synthetic pigment (accidental)
  - ~1826: synthetic ultramarine — democratized the rarest color
  - ~1841: tube paint — enabled Impressionism
  - ~1850–1900: industrial chemistry revolution — cadmiums, cobalts, chrome, synthetic organics
  - ~1916: titanium white — ends 2000 years of lead white dominance
  - ~1936: phthalocyanine blue — most lightfast blue available
  - ~2000s: quantum dot pigments, fluorescent pigments, metameric inks
- Binder and medium: pigment must be suspended; linseed oil (oxidative polymerization dries to solid film); egg tempera (protein matrix); gum arabic (watercolor); acrylic polymer (modern); fresco (pigment in wet lime plaster — CO₂ carbonation locks pigment); encaustic (wax); each binder changes optical properties (oil increases saturation/transparency, changes drying)
- Decision cheat sheet: pigment families, chemistry type, historical period, lightfastness, toxicity, replacement pigments
