# Hydrothermal Systems and Ore Formation

## The Big Picture

Hydrothermal systems are hot, chemically reactive fluid circulation systems driven by magmatic heat or geothermal gradients. They are responsible for a large fraction of the world's metal ore deposits and are active on the modern ocean floor.

## Mid-Ocean Ridge Hydrothermal Systems

```
BLACK SMOKER ANATOMY
=====================

  SEAWATER INFILTRATION
    Cold seawater (~2°C) percolates down faults/fractures
    → Heated by underlying magma/hot rock
    → Chemical reactions with basalt:
      Mg²⁺, SO₄²⁻ removed from seawater (precipitation)
      Ca²⁺, Si, Fe, Mn, H₂S, ³He, H₂ added to fluid
      pH drops from 8.1 to 3-4 (acidic)
      Temperature rises to 350-400°C

  VENT GEOMETRY:
                        Seawater
                           |
                    Black smoker plume
                     (particle-laden)
                    /                \
              vent orifice         Diffuse
              (350-400°C)         venting
              /                   (2-50°C)
        Sulfide chimney
        (anhydrite + sulfides)
                    |
         Stockwork zone (veins)
                    |
         Reaction zone (basalt-fluid reaction)
                    |
         Magma heat source

  BLACK SMOKER FLUID CHEMISTRY:
    pH:      3-4
    T:       300-400°C (some up to ~400°C)
    Fe:      100-1000s mmol/kg (from basalt)
    Mn:      10-100 mmol/kg
    H₂S:     1-100 mmol/kg
    Si:      15-20 mmol/kg (from quartz)
    Cl:      typically ~700 mmol/kg (seawater-like + phase separation)
    H₂:      sometimes elevated (serpentinization)

  PRECIPITATION AT MIXING:
    Hot vent fluid mixes with cold seawater
    Fe²⁺ + H₂S → FeS₂ (pyrite), FeS (pyrrhotite) → BLACK particles
    Zn²⁺ + H₂S → ZnS (sphalerite)
    Cu²⁺ + H₂S → CuS, Cu₂S (covellite, chalcocite)
    → Sulfide chimney builds up → Volcanogenic Massive Sulfide (VMS) deposit
```

## VMS (Volcanogenic Massive Sulfide) Deposits

```
VMS DEPOSIT CHARACTERISTICS
=============================

  METALS: Cu, Zn, Pb, Au, Ag, Co
  ORE MINERALS: Pyrite, chalcopyrite, sphalerite, galena, tetrahedrite
  FORM: Mound-shaped lenses at seafloor; stockwork zones below

  CLASSIFICATION BY TECTONIC SETTING:
    Cyprus-type:    MOR-hosted; Cu-Fe (Cu, Zn, some Au)
    Kuroko-type:    Bimodal (basalt + rhyolite); Zn-Pb-Cu
                    Japan; felsic magmas provide more Zn, Pb
    Besshi-type:    Mafic; deep marine; Cu-Zn-Co
    SEDEX (Sed-Exhalative): Zn-Pb-Ag; stratiform; in fine sediments

  ALTERATION ZONES:
    Ore lens (massive sulfides) at top
    Stockwork (sulfide veins in silicified rock)
    Chlorite alteration zone (below stockwork)
    Sericite alteration (silica + sericite)
    Distal Mn-rich sediments (halo)

  MODERN EXAMPLES:
    TAG hydrothermal field (Mid-Atlantic Ridge): ~3500 m depth; 5 Mt sulfides
    Rainbow field (MAR): ultramafic-hosted; high H₂, high Fe, Au
    Juan de Fuca Ridge (Pacific): multiple VMS-forming systems

  ANCIENT (mined):
    Noranda/Abitibi (Canada): Archean; 100+ VMS deposits
    Iberian Pyrite Belt (Portugal/Spain): Paleozoic; Rio Tinto (Zn-Cu-Pb-Ag)
    Kuroko deposits (Japan): Miocene; Zn-Pb-Cu
```

## Porphyry Copper Systems

```
PORPHYRY COPPER-MOLYBDENUM-GOLD SYSTEM
========================================

  SETTING: Subduction-related arc magmatism
  SCALE: Largest ore deposits in the world (billions of tonnes)
  EXAMPLES: Chuquicamata (Chile, 10 Gt ore), Bingham Canyon (Utah, 10 Gt)

  FORMATION SEQUENCE:
  1. PORPHYRY INTRUSION:
     Large felsic to intermediate intrusion (granodiorite porphyry)
     Crystallizes at 3-5 km depth
     Magmatic fluids exsolve as pressure drops (second boiling)
     Fluid is hot (400-600°C), saline, metal-rich (Cu, Mo, Au)

  2. POTASSIC ALTERATION CORE:
     Highest T (400-600°C); K-feldspar + biotite alteration
     Cu-Mo mineralization in veins and stockwork
     Chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂) + molybdenite (MoS₂) + pyrite + gold

  3. PHYLLIC ALTERATION (SERICITIC):
     Intermediate T (250-400°C)
     Sericite (muscovite) + quartz + pyrite
     Grade transitions outward

  4. ARGILLIC ALTERATION:
     Lower T (100-250°C)
     Kaolinite, smectite, alunite alteration
     Advanced argillic: alunite-rich (epithermal transition)

  5. PROPYLITIC HALO:
     Outermost; distal (chlorite + epidote + carbonate)
     Essentially unaltered appearance

  ZONATION MAP (plan view):
    +-----------+
    |  Potassic |  ← Cu-Mo core
    | +-------+ |
    | |Phyllic| |  ← transitional
    | +-------+ |
    |  Argillic |  ← lower grade
    +-----------+
    | Propylitic|  ← background
    +-----------+

  FLUID SOURCE:
    Initially: magmatic (exsolved from crystallizing magma)
    Later: meteoric water drawn in from overlying rock (convective)
    Isotopic evidence: δD and δ¹⁸O mixing trajectories in fluid inclusions

  GEOCHEMICAL SIGNATURE:
    High Mo/Cu ratio → Mo-porphyry; High Au/Cu → Au-porphyry
    Re content of molybdenite → Re-Os age of mineralization
    Halogen ratios (Cl/Br) → magmatic vs seawater fluid
```

## Epithermal Gold Deposits

```
EPITHERMAL GOLD-SILVER SYSTEM
==============================

  SETTING: Shallow crustal level (< 2 km depth)
  T RANGE: 150-300°C
  TWO TYPES:

  LOW-SULFIDATION (LS) EPITHERMAL:
    Neutral pH fluid; meteoric water dominant
    Classic: Creede, Comstock Lode (Nevada), Waihi (NZ)
    Ore minerals: electrum (Au-Ag alloy), argentite, native Au
    Alteration: quartz-adularia-illite; silicification
    Control: boiling (CO₂ loss; pH rise; Au precipitation)
    Au deposits: veins, stockwork, disseminations
    Au+Ag grade: typically 3-30 g/t Au

  HIGH-SULFIDATION (HS) EPITHERMAL:
    Low pH (magmatic acid sulfate) fluid
    Condensation of SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ + H₂S
    Classic: El Indio (Chile), Lepanto (Philippines)
    Ore minerals: enargite (Cu₃AsS₄), luzonite, native Au
    Alteration: alunite-kaolinite-quartz; vuggy silica
    More Cu-rich than LS

  INTERMEDIATE-SULFIDATION:
    Overlapping characteristics; transitional fluid chemistry
    Mostly Ag-rich; base metal-bearing veins

  BOILING AS ORE-PRECIPITATING MECHANISM:
    Rising fluid → decompression → boiling → CO₂ loss
    CO₂ loss → pH rise: HS⁻ + H⁺ → H₂S
    pH rise → Au(HS)₂⁻ → Au(s) (gold precipitation)
    Evidence: "bladed calcite" = boiling indicator; vapor-rich inclusions
```

## Fluid Inclusions: Reading Hydrothermal Conditions

```
FLUID INCLUSION ANALYSIS
==========================

  WHAT THEY ARE:
    Tiny volumes of ancient fluid trapped in mineral during growth
    Most common in quartz, calcite, fluorite
    Sizes: 1-100 μm; preserved for millions of years

  TYPES:
    Type I: Liquid-only (trapped below boiling T)
    Type II: Liquid + vapor (2-phase; trapped at or near boiling T)
    Type III: Liquid + vapor + daughter crystals (saline brines)
    Type IV: Vapor-rich (trapped from vapor phase)

  MEASUREMENT:
    MICROTHERMOMETRY: Heat/cool on stage; measure:
      T_m (ice): melting T of ice → salinity (wt% NaCl equiv.)
        Tm_ice = 0°C → pure water
        Tm_ice = -21.2°C → NaCl saturation (~23 wt%)
      T_h (homogenization): T at which vapor bubble disappears
        → minimum trapping T (need P correction for true T)
    RAMAN SPECTROSCOPY: gas composition (CO₂, CH₄, H₂S, N₂)
    LA-ICP-MS: trace element + major element chemistry

  INTERPRETATION:
    High salinity + high T: magmatic brine (porphyry)
    Low salinity + T<300°C: meteoric water (epithermal)
    CO₂-bearing: deep crustal fluids; higher P trapping
    P-T PATH: suite of inclusions can reconstruct pressure-temperature history

  BOILING ASSEMBLAGE:
    Coexisting vapor-rich + liquid-rich inclusions trapped at same T
    → Both formed from a boiling fluid
    → T = true trapping T (no pressure correction needed)
    → Key evidence for fluid boiling in epithermal systems
```

## Stable Isotopes in Hydrothermal Systems

```
ISOTOPE TOOLS IN ORE GEOLOGY
==============================

  SULFUR ISOTOPES (³⁴S/³²S):
    δ³⁴S_mantle: ~0±2‰ (magmatic sulfur baseline)
    δ³⁴S_seawater sulfate: +21‰ (modern); varies over time
    δ³⁴S_bacterial sulfide: very negative (-20 to -40‰)

    APPLICATIONS:
    Porphyry: δ³⁴S near 0‰ → magmatic sulfur source
    VMS: variable; seawater SO₄ reduction by bacteria → very negative δ³⁴S
    MVT: large variation → bacterial reduction involved

    Mass-Independent Fractionation (MIF-S):
    Archean rocks show Δ³³S ≠ 0 (deviation from mass-dependent line)
    Only possible if SO₂ photolysis by UV in O₂-free atmosphere
    → Proves O₂ < 10⁻⁵ PAL before Great Oxidation Event (2.4 Ga)

  OXYGEN ISOTOPES (δ¹⁸O fluid):
    Meteoric water: -20 to -4‰ (latitude-dependent)
    Seawater: ~0‰
    Magmatic water: +6 to +10‰ (high due to mantle + fractionation)

    Mixed fluid:
    Porphyry Cu: starts magmatic (+8‰) → mixed meteoric (-3‰) outward
    Epithermal: mostly meteoric; small magmatic component

  LEAD ISOTOPES (²⁰⁶Pb, ²⁰⁷Pb, ²⁰⁸Pb):
    All non-radiogenic in ore (no parent U/Th in galena)
    → Record Pb isotope composition of SOURCE
    → Fingerprints ore district to basement terrane
    → Correlate ore districts over large regions
```

## Mississippi Valley-Type (MVT) Deposits

```
MVT DEPOSIT FORMATION
======================

  METALS: Zn, Pb (with some Cu, Ba, F)
  HOST: Carbonate platform sequences (limestone, dolostone)
  CLASSIC EXAMPLES: Tri-State district (Missouri, Kansas, Oklahoma)
                    Viburnum Trend (Missouri); Pine Point (Canada)

  FORMATION MODEL:
    Basin brine (100-200°C; high salinity, 15-25 wt% NaCl)
    → Expelled from basin during orogeny (compaction, tectonics)
    → Migrates outward to platform carbonate margins
    → Mixing with H₂S-bearing water (from TSR or bacterial reduction)
    → Zn²⁺ + S²⁻ → ZnS (sphalerite); Pb²⁺ + S²⁻ → PbS (galena)

  THERMOCHEMICAL SULFATE REDUCTION (TSR):
    CaSO₄ + CH₄ → CaCO₃ + H₂S + H₂O
    Anhydrite + organic matter → calcite + H₂S
    → H₂S is the sulfur source for sulfide precipitation
    → δ³⁴S of ore sulfides near that of evaporite sulfate (+17 to +28‰)

  DISTINGUISHING MVT vs VMS:
    MVT: carbonate host; no associated volcanism; late diagenetic
    VMS: oceanic setting; associated volcanics; synsedimentary
    Fluid inclusions: MVT has saline brines at 100-200°C
                      VMS: seawater-like fluids at 200-400°C
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| What makes a porphyry Cu deposit form? | Exsolution of magmatic fluid from crystallizing subduction-related magma; Cu and Mo partitioned into the fluid; deposition at depth ~3-5 km via pressure drop |
| What is the zonation sequence in a porphyry system? | Core: potassic (K-feldspar+biotite+Cu-Mo veins); Phyllic (sericite+pyrite); Argillic (kaolinite); Propylitic (chlorite+epidote) — T decreases outward |
| Why does gold precipitate in epithermal systems? | Boiling: CO₂ loss raises pH → gold bisulfide complex Au(HS)₂⁻ destabilizes → native gold precipitates |
| What is a VMS deposit? | Volcanogenic Massive Sulfide — seafloor deposit from hydrothermal venting; Cu-Zn-Fe-Au-Ag in sulfide minerals (pyrite, chalcopyrite, sphalerite) |
| How do fluid inclusions constrain ore formation conditions? | Trapping temperature (homogenization) and salinity (ice melting point) reveal fluid T, P, and composition at time of mineral growth |
| What does Δ³³S (sulfur MIF) tell you? | Mass-independent sulfur fractionation; non-zero Δ³³S in Archean rocks proves O₂-free atmosphere (UV photolysis of SO₂); records Great Oxidation Event timing |
| How do Pb isotopes fingerprint ore deposits? | Galena has no U or Th → Pb isotopes record the source of Pb at time of ore formation; correlates ore districts to basement rock type/age |
| What drives Mississippi Valley-Type deposit formation? | Basin-expelled hot saline brine mixing with H₂S (from sulfate reduction or bacterial) in carbonate platform rocks; no volcanic association |

## Common Confusion Points

**Black smokers are not uniform**: They vary from ~200°C diffuse vents to 407°C supercritical fluids. The famous black color comes from metal sulfide particles precipitating as vent fluid mixes with cold seawater. "White smokers" produce anhydrite, barite, silica at lower temperatures.

**Porphyry refers to the intrusion texture, not the ore**: "Porphyry" describes the intrusion with phenocrysts in a fine-grained groundmass. Porphyry Cu deposits are found in rocks with this texture, but the deposit itself is copper mineralization dispersed through the rock — not a porphyry texture ore.

**Epithermal and porphyry are not the same system**: They can be spatially and genetically linked (epithermal forms above a deeper porphyry), but they are different in temperature, depth, and fluid chemistry. Not every porphyry has an epithermal top, and not every epithermal deposit is connected to a porphyry below.

**Re-Os dating of molybdenite is direct, not isochron**: Molybdenite excludes Os from its structure but accepts Re. Therefore all Os in molybdenite is radiogenic ¹⁸⁷Os from ¹⁸⁷Re decay. The age is calculated from a single sample, not from a suite of co-genetic samples with varying Re/Os ratios (as in the isochron method).
