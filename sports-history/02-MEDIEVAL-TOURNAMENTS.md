## Tournament Origins and Evolution

**The tournament** (torneamentum) emerged in 11th-century France as organized military exercise that had developed into public spectacle. Geoffrey de Preuilly (died 1066) is credited in Orderic Vitalis with establishing tournament rules, though the exact timing and extent of his contribution is disputed.

```
TOURNAMENT EVOLUTION:

PHASE 1: EARLY TOURNAMENT (11th-12th c.)
  +------------------------------------------+
  | MASS MELEE (tournoi)                     |
  | -- Two teams of knights, any size         |
  | -- Actual weapons, lethal force allowed  |
  | -- No defined "arena" -- wide geography  |
  |    (fields, forests, even villages)      |
  | -- Victory: capture opponents for ransom |
  | -- Prize: horse, armor, the man himself  |
  | -- Casualties: common, deaths: frequent  |
  | -- Church banned repeatedly (Council     |
  |    of Clermont 1130, later bans)          |
  +------------------------------------------+

PHASE 2: REGULATED TOURNAMENT (13th c.)
  +------------------------------------------+
  | RULES IMPOSED                            |
  | -- Defined lists (enclosed field)        |
  | -- Blunted weapons introduced (a         |
  |    plaisance = for pleasure/display)     |
  | -- Sharp weapons still in a outrance     |
  |    (to the utmost = serious combat)      |
  | -- Royal licensing required (England,   |
  |    Richard I licensed 5 sites 1194)      |
  | -- Formal heraldic display developed    |
  | -- Structured program (opening parade,  |
  |    jousts, final melee)                  |
  +------------------------------------------+

PHASE 3: JOUST-DOMINATED TOURNAMENT (14th-16th c.)
  +------------------------------------------+
  | INDIVIDUAL JOUST TAKES OVER              |
  | -- Tilt barrier introduced ~13th c.      |
  | -- Separates horses, reduces collision   |
  | -- Pageantry dominates content           |
  | -- Elaborate costumes, theatrical themes |
  | -- "pas d'armes" (passage of arms):      |
  |    challenger holds a "passage" vs all   |
  | -- Declining military relevance          |
  +------------------------------------------+
```

## William Marshal (1147-1219): The Tournament Professional

William Marshal is the clearest historical exemplar of professional tournament competition in the High Medieval period. The Histoire de Guillaume le Marechal (verse biography commissioned by his son) provides detailed tournament records.

**Career**: Born landless fourth son of a minor English nobleman. Made his reputation and fortune entirely through tournament. Served under Henry II, Richard I, John, and Henry III (regent during minority). Rose to become 1st Earl of Pembroke.

**Tournament economics** (William Marshal's career model):
```
TOURNAMENT INCOME MODEL:
  Knight captured = captive can be ransomed
    -- Ransom value: horse + armor + additional sum
    -- Horse value: 2-3 years of peasant income
    -- Armor (full harness): 5-10 years peasant income
    -- Skilled knight could earn modern equivalent of
       hundreds of thousands per year in active tourney years

  William Marshal's recorded captures (1166-1183):
    -- ~500 knights captured over 17-year period
    -- ~10 years of active touring circuit
    -- Philippe d'Alsace, Count of Flanders: among patrons

  Career trajectory:
    Tourney champion -> Royal household -> Marriage to
    Isabel de Clare (heiress of Striguil) -> Earl of Pembroke
    -> Regent of England 1216-1219
```

William Marshal demonstrates that tournament was a viable professional career path for a skilled, ambitious knight with no inherited wealth. The melee was his business; ransoms were his product.

## The Joust: Technical Details

The joust evolved from a component of the melee into the dominant individual event. Key technical innovations:

```
JOUST ANATOMY:
                    Barrier (tilt)
  Knight A ======= | ======= Knight B
  direction <--    |    --> direction
  approach at trot/canter

  Objective: Break lance on opponent's body/shield
  -- "Courses" (passes): typically 3 per match
  -- Points awarded for:
     a) Breaking lance on body (>helmet = extra)
     b) Unhorsing opponent (maximum)
     c) Striking in defined target zones

  LANCE (tilt lance, from ~15th c.):
  -- Hollow wood (pine/ash) to shatter on impact
  -- Coronel tip: three-pointed crown, not point
    (for a plaisance -- reduces penetration)
  -- Length: ~3.6-4.8m (12-16 feet)
  -- Held in couched position (under arm) vs
     earlier overhand grip

  ARMOR EVOLUTION:
  -- 11th c. tournament: mail hauberk + conical helm
  -- 13th c.: transitional plate over mail
  -- 14th-15th c.: full plate armor (Milanese/German)
  -- Jousting armor specialized: heavier reinforcement
    on left side (toward opponent), fixed helm visor
  -- "Frog-mouth" helm: no vision directly forward
    (tilt of head = vision; posture protection)
```

**The tilt barrier** (separator wall running down the center): Introduced gradually through the 13th century. Forces horses to run parallel rather than converging, eliminating horse-on-horse collision that was a major source of deaths and injuries. Transformed jousting from more genuinely dangerous to more controlled spectacle.

**à plaisance vs. à outrance**:
- *à plaisance* (for pleasure): blunted lances (coronel), often lighter armor, points-based judging, display and pageantry emphasized
- *à outrance* (to the utmost): sharp weapons, real combat intent, judicial duels fell in this category

## Archery: The Longbow Tradition

**Origins**: The English longbow's development is Welsh, not English. Welsh archers using long bows of yew were observed by English forces during 12th-century campaigns in Wales. The Welsh longbow tradition was absorbed into English military infrastructure.

```
LONGBOW SPECIFICATIONS:
  Material: Primarily yew (Taxus baccata)
            Ideal: sapwood (tension side) + heartwood
            (compression side) naturally in same stave
  Length:   ~1.8-2.1m (6-7 feet)
  Draw weight: 100-185 pounds (modern reproductions)
               Skeletal evidence: asymmetric development
               of left arm (draw bow arm), spine
               deformities -- serious lifelong training
  Arrow:    Cloth-yard shaft (~0.75m), goose feather
            fletching, bodkin point (armor-piercing) or
            broadhead (anti-cavalry, anti-horse)
  Rate of fire: 10-12 aimed arrows/minute trained archer
                6 effective aimed shots/min sustained
  Effective range: 200m against personnel
                   100-150m vs. armored targets

LONGBOW VS. CROSSBOW:
  +------------------+------------------+
  | Longbow          | Crossbow         |
  +------------------+------------------+
  | Rate: 10-12/min  | Rate: 1-2/min    |
  | Training: years  | Training: hours  |
  | Range: ~200m     | Range: ~300m     |
  | Penetration:     | Penetration:     |
  |  bodkin at 100m  |  superior at     |
  |  vs. mail        |  range, armor    |
  | Cheap weapon     | Expensive mech.  |
  | Physically hard  | Physical easy    |
  | Social: yeoman   | Social: any rank |
  +------------------+------------------+
```

**English mandatory archery practice**: The Assize of Arms (1252, Henry III) and subsequent statutes required men to practice archery and maintain bows. Sunday practice at butts (earthen targets) was legally required. The "shooting at the butts" was both training and public spectacle. English yeomen developed through this systematic training program.

---

The longbow's advantage is a throughput story: 7,000 archers at 10-12 shots/min = ~70,000 rounds/min aggregate vs. 6,000 crossbowmen at 1-2/min = ~8,000 rounds/min. The crossbow has higher per-unit lethality (higher penetration) but lower throughput. The training cost asymmetry is the bottleneck: a crossbow requires days of training (low skill barrier, easy to replicate), but a longbow requires years of continuous practice from childhood (high skill barrier, replication-limited). England's mandatory archery practice laws were a multi-decade investment in a human capital pipeline.

## Battle of Crécy (1346): Archery as Military Paradigm Shift

```
CRÉCY, AUGUST 26, 1346:

  ENGLISH (Edward III)          FRENCH (Philip VI)
  ~12,000 total                ~35,000 total
  ~7,000 longbowmen            ~6,000 Genoese crossbowmen
  Dismounted knights           Mounted French knights
  Prepared defensive position  Disordered attack

  SEQUENCE:
  1. Genoese crossbowmen advance -- strings wet (no protection)
     Rate: ~1-2 bolts/min, effective range ~250m
  2. English longbowmen (estimated 7,000):
     Rate: ~10/min = 70,000 arrows/minute volley
     Arrow storm at range 100-200m
  3. Genoese routed -- French knights rode them down
     (own troops, disordered, frustrated)
  4. French knights charged repeatedly up slope
     -- ~15 separate charges during the day
     -- Each broken by arrow fire
     -- Horses not knights primary targets
     -- Unhorsed knight in full plate = helpless
  5. English loss: ~200 men; French: ~10,000+ dead
     (including King of Bohemia, Count of Flanders)

  LESSON: The massed longbow volleys did not penetrate
  full plate armor at range -- they targeted horses
  and opened gaps for dismounted English knights.
  The revolution was tactical combined arms, not
  pure archery penetration.
```

**Agincourt (1415, Henry V)**: Outnumbered English (~9,000 vs ~25,000+ French), muddy field after rain, narrow front, French men-at-arms advancing through churned mud in full plate. English longbowmen on flanks. French advance bogged down, arrows at close range found gaps in armor. ~6,000-10,000 French dead vs ~100-400 English. Henry V's murder of French prisoners: tactical decision when feared second attack, controversial.

**The longbow's end**: The matchlock firearm superseded the longbow in English military service by ~1590-1600. Firearms required minimal training (weeks vs. years), could be manufactured without the specific yew timber, and penetrated armor more reliably at range. The strategic advantage of training time and cost eventually favored firearms.

## Folk Football

Before the Victorian codification of association football, "football" in England meant a variety of mass participation games with minimal rules, typically played on festival days.

```
FOLK FOOTBALL CHARACTERISTICS:
  -- Played on Shrove Tuesday (day before Ash Wednesday)
     or other festival occasions
  -- Teams: entire parishes, villages, guilds vs. each other
     (hundreds of players per side)
  -- Goals: defined points (parish churches, markers miles apart)
  -- Rules: minimal (must use only feet? often ignored)
  -- Violence: normalized, injuries common, deaths occasional
  -- Geography: fields, streets, rivers -- all fair ground
  -- Duration: from noon to sunset or longer
  -- Spectators: few formal boundaries

SURVIVING EXAMPLES:
  Ashbourne Royal Shrovetide Football (Derbyshire, England):
  -- Still played annually (Shrove Tuesday + Ash Wednesday)
  -- "Upwards" vs "Downwards" (town divided by river)
  -- "Goals" are millstones 3 miles apart
  -- Ball scored by tapping against goal 3 times
  -- Entire town is the pitch
  -- Violence limitations exist (not in churches, cemeteries,
     memorial garden) but contact is robust
  -- Modern: ~1,000+ players per side

  Ba' game (Kirkwall, Orkney, Scotland):
  -- Similar structure, Christmas/New Year tradition
  -- "Uppies" vs "Doonies"
```

**Calcio Storico (Florence)**: First documented in Giovanni de' Bardi's *Discorso sopra il giuoco del calcio fiorentino* (1580), which codified rules for a game already established in Florentine culture. The most famous historical match: February 17, 1530, played in Piazza Santa Croce during the Imperial siege of Florence as an act of defiance against Charles V's forces. The game was played continuously through the Renaissance and Baroque periods, declined in the 18th century, and was revived in 1930 under Mussolini's cultural nationalism program. The continuity question is real: the modern game claims unbroken tradition but the 1930 revival involved significant reconstruction of rules. Still, the 1580 codification provides a documented baseline that the modern game broadly follows. Played in Piazza Santa Croce, Florence. Four teams representing historical city quarters: Azzurri (Santa Croce), Bianchi (Santo Spirito), Rossi (Santa Maria Novella), Verdi (San Giovanni). 27 players per side. 50-minute match. Combination of football and wrestling -- punching, choking, and kicking allowed. No substitutions, no stoppage time for injuries. Often described as the world's most violent sport still played. Annual festival (June, patron saint John the Baptist's feast).

## Blood Sports: The Pre-Modern Mainstream

The modern reader assumes blood sports involving animals were always fringe or lower-class entertainment. This is historically wrong.

```
BLOOD SPORTS LANDSCAPE (England, 16th-18th c.):

  BEAR-BAITING:
  -- Bear chained to post
  -- Multiple dogs attack sequentially
  -- Bear fights back, mauls dogs
  -- Crowd bets on bear vs. dogs
  -- Venue: "bear garden" (Bankside, London,
     immediately adjacent to the Globe Theatre)
  -- Patrons: working class to royalty
  -- Queen Elizabeth I's court entertainment
  -- Henry VIII kept a royal bear-garden at
     Whitehall
  -- Survival: bear gardens outlasted some churches

  BULL-BAITING:
  -- Bull tethered by nose ring (short tether)
  -- Dogs set at bull
  -- Bulldogs specifically bred for this:
     short snout (breathe while biting), low
     center of gravity, muscular, tenacious
     Modern English Bulldog: vestigial breed
     from this function

  COCKFIGHTING:
  -- Roosters fitted with steel spurs
  -- Weight-matched bouts
  -- Wagering integral
  -- "Cockpits" throughout England
  -- Aristocratic pastime: Oxford University
     had a cockpit within its grounds
  -- International spread: Philippines, Caribbean,
     Latin America (still legal in many nations)

BANNED: Cruelty to Animals Act 1835 (England)
  -- Bear-baiting and bull-baiting prohibited
  -- Cockfighting prohibited (England)
  -- Timeline: these bans preceded many
    modern humanitarian reforms
```

**Why banned**: The 1835 Act reflected a confluence of evangelical Christian animal welfare sentiment (Wilberforce connections), public order concerns (blood sport venues attracted disorderly crowds and gambling), and emerging middle-class sensibilities about cruelty. The ban was not primarily driven by welfare of animals in the abstract -- horses continued to be worked to death, fox hunting survived, racing continued.

**Fox hunting** (post-enclosure): The Inclosure Acts (18th-19th c.) created large estates perfect for mounted fox hunting. Became the defining aristocratic English country sport. Master of Foxhounds, the hunt, the field. Survived the 1835 Act. Eventually banned in England/Wales under Hunting Act 2004 -- politically contentious, enforcement spotty.

## Falconry: Aristocratic Pursuit

Falconry -- training birds of prey to hunt -- was the prestige sport of the medieval aristocracy. Not just leisure but marker of social status, political alliance, and technical mastery.

```
FALCONRY STATUS HIERARCHY (medieval):
  Emperor/King:       Golden eagle, gyrfalcon (most prestigious)
  Earl/Duke:          Peregrine falcon
  Baron:              Bastard hawk (immature peregrine)
  Knight:             Saker falcon
  Squire:             Lanner falcon
  Lady:               Merlin
  Priest:             Sparrowhawk
  Yeoman:             Goshawk
  Knave:              Kestrel

(Boke of St. Albans, ~1486 -- classification may be prescriptive
 not descriptive, but shows the symbolic hierarchy)
```

**Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor (1194-1250)**: Author of De Arte Venandi cum Avibus (The Art of Hunting with Birds, ~1244). One of the most sophisticated scientific texts of the Middle Ages. Frederick conducted systematic experiments (covered falcon eggs to see if sight or hearing triggered hatching; tested which birds could fly without eyesight). Represents empirical proto-scientific methodology applied to sport.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Tournaments were about chivalric love, not economics"** -- The Romantic tradition (19th-century) overlaid medieval tournaments with courtly love ideology. The primary economic mechanism was ransom. William Marshal's career demonstrates that tournaments were serious commercial enterprises. The pageantry co-existed with mercenary incentive.

**"The longbow was a purely English invention"** -- The longbow is Welsh in origin. English armies adopted it from Welsh enemies/allies during 12th-13th century Welsh campaigns. The English then systematized and scaled it through mandatory practice laws.

**"Bear-baiting was lower-class entertainment"** -- Elizabeth I's Privy Council blocked Sabbath football matches to protect attendance at bear-baiting. The bear garden on Bankside was immediately adjacent to the Globe Theatre and was equally well-attended by all social classes. Henry VIII personally maintained the royal bears.

**"The joust was to the death"** -- à plaisance jousting (the vast majority) used blunted coronel-tipped lances designed to shatter on impact. Deaths occurred but were exceptional and considered failures of the event. The theatrical function was display of skill, not combat outcome.

**"Blood sports bans were about animal welfare"** -- The 1835 Cruelty to Animals Act was partly evangelical humanitarian motivation, but primarily about public order, middle-class sensibility about "brutal" spectacle, and the disorderly gambling crowds these events attracted. Fox hunting -- exclusively aristocratic -- was exempt from most of the legislation and survived for another 169 years.

**"Medieval football was the direct ancestor of soccer"** -- Folk football and association football share the concept of a ball and goals but essentially nothing else. The codification of 1863 was not a simplification of folk football -- it was a near-complete reconstruction with specific rules, field dimensions, time limits, referees. Folk football was the cultural background, not the technical ancestor.
