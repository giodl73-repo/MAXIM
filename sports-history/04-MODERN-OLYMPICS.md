## Pierre de Coubertin: The Architect

**Background**: Pierre de Fredy, Baron de Coubertin (1863-1937), French aristocrat, educator, and sports organizer. Not primarily an athlete -- a policy thinker. His project was educational reform, not sport for its own sake.

**Intellectual formation**:
```
COUBERTIN'S INFLUENCES:

  Thomas Arnold (Rugby School headmaster) ->
    Muscular Christianity model:
    sport as character formation,
    sport as preparation for citizenship
    (Coubertin visited Rugby, deeply impressed)

  German excavations at Olympia (1875-1881) ->
    Archaeological romantic revival:
    Ernst Curtius, German expedition
    Revealed stadium, hippodrome, temples
    Created Victorian fascination with ancient Games

  France's 1870 military defeat to Prussia ->
    Coubertin's political project:
    France needed physically vigorous youth
    Sport as national regeneration program

  English public school sport model ->
    Template: house matches, house pride,
    inter-school competition as socializing force
    Scale this internationally = world peace?
```

**The internationalist thesis**: Coubertin believed that international competition under sportsmanlike rules would build mutual respect between nations. "The important thing in the Olympic Games is not winning but taking part; the essential thing in life is not conquering but fighting well." This Coubertin quote is widely cited but the historical attribution and precise wording is contested -- some version of it appears in his writings.

**Coubertin's blind spots**: He was explicitly opposed to women's Olympic competition. He considered women's sport aesthetically unpleasant and contrary to the nature of the Games. Women's events were included over his objection, beginning with tennis and golf in 1900. His class assumptions were embedded in the amateurism rules he designed.

## Athens 1896: The Revival

```
ATHENS 1896 — FACTS:
  Host city: Athens, Greece
  Dates: April 6-15, 1896
  Nations: 14 (plus individual entrants)
  Athletes: ~241 (all male)
  Events: 43 (athletics, cycling, fencing,
           gymnastics, shooting, swimming,
           tennis, weightlifting, wrestling)
  Venue: Panathenaic Stadium (rebuilt on
          ancient site, Herodes Atticus financed
          marble restoration)
  Spectators: ~80,000 for major events

  NOTABLE:
  -- USA sent team despite no government support
     (private fundraising, Princeton/Harvard athletes)
  -- James Brendan Connolly (USA): first modern
     Olympic champion (triple jump, April 6, 1896)
  -- Marathon won by Spyridon Louis (Greek shepherd)
     -- Nationalist myth constructed immediately
     -- 25-year-old postal courier from Maroussi
     -- Won by significant margin; crowd ecstatic
  -- Cycling: 87km road race Athens to Marathon
     and back (Leon Flameng, France)
  -- Swimming: held in open Bay of Zea,
     water temperature ~13°C
  -- No women (Coubertin's exclusion, Greek tradition)
```

**The Marathon**: The 1896 marathon (40km roughly) was run along the road from Marathon to Athens (approximating the mythological run of Pheidippides after the 490 BCE battle). The event was invented for 1896 -- there was no ancient Olympic marathon. It was a 19th-century invention to honor the ancient battle. Spyridon Louis's victory was instrumentalized into Greek nationalist narrative almost instantly.

## The Amateurism Regime and Its Hypocrisies

The amateurism rules governing Olympic eligibility were written by and for upper-class Englishmen and Americans. Their effect was to exclude working-class athletes regardless of national origin.

```
AMATEURISM AS CLASS MECHANISM:

  RULE (approximate): An amateur is one who has not
  competed for money, wagered, taught for payment,
  or competed against a professional.

  INTENDED EFFECT:
  -- Working-class athletes needed payment to
     train, travel, miss work
  -- Payment = "professional" = barred
  -- Upper-class athletes had leisure, family money,
     no income need -> automatically "amateur"

  EUROPEAN EXCEPTION ("shamateurs"):
  -- Soviet bloc athletes from 1952 Olympics onward:
     "state employees" who trained full-time
     -> officially "amateurs" under rules
  -- Western European state-funded athletes:
     similar arrangements tolerated
  -- Reality: full-time athletes with state stipends
     classified as amateurs while US part-timers
     who took $2/game were professionals

  BRUNDAGE ENFORCEMENT:
  Avery Brundage (IOC President 1952-1972)
  -- Obsessive amateurism enforcer
  -- Tolerated Soviet system as long as no
     formal payment was admitted
  -- Pursued American athletes aggressively
  -- His own skiing business benefited from
     connection to alpine ski manufacturers
     while he banned ski racers for boot deals
```

**Jim Thorpe (1912 Stockholm)**: Won the pentathlon and decathlon -- considered the greatest athlete of the early Olympics. Stripped of medals in 1913 after it was revealed he had played semi-professional baseball in 1909-10 for $25/week in North Carolina. He stopped when told his Olympic eligibility was at risk, returned to amateur athletics, and believed the matter resolved. The IOC restored his medals posthumously in 1983 -- after his death in 1953. The enforcement against Thorpe was selective: many White American athletes had similar minor professional connections that went uninvestigated.

## Olympic Timing and Officiating Technology

The Olympics drove the evolution from human-eye to sensor-based adjudication:

```
OLYMPIC TIMING EVOLUTION
══════════════════════════

1896 Athens:      Hand-held stopwatch (±0.2s accuracy)
1912 Stockholm:   Semi-automatic electrical timing
1932 Los Angeles: First photo-finish camera (Kirby camera)
                  Resolved a dead heat in 100m
1948 London:      Omega becomes official Olympic timekeeper
                  Photo-finish standard, timing to 1/10s
1964 Tokyo:       Electronic timing to 1/100s
                  Touch pads in swimming (Omega swim)
1972 Munich:      Timing to 1/1000s (displayed to 1/100s)
                  9.95 vs 9.96: Borzov 100m
2008 Beijing:     Starting blocks with force sensors
                  False start = reaction time < 0.100s
2012 London:      Hawk-Eye deployed for tennis
                  Goal-line technology approved by FIFA
                  (used at 2014 World Cup, not Olympics)
2020 Tokyo:       3D athlete tracking (Intel/Alibaba)
                  Pose estimation via multi-camera CV
```

Omega's timing infrastructure at a modern Olympics: ~450 tons of equipment, 350 trained timekeepers, sensors on every track lane, pool wall, and finish line. The transition from stopwatch to sensor eliminated an entire class of adjudication disputes — but introduced new ones (false start threshold of 0.100s is a convention, not a physiological constant; some athletes react faster).

### 1968 Mexico City

**Altitude**: Mexico City at 2,240m elevation caused:
- Short-burst events (sprints, jumps): favored (less air resistance, explosive power unaffected)
- Endurance events: suffered acutely (sea-level athletes, reduced O2)
- Records in sprints/jumps: many broken or approached
- Distance events: times significantly slower than world records
- Bob Beamon's long jump: 8.90m (+55cm beyond world record) -- still Olympic record

**Fosbury Flop**: Dick Fosbury (USA) won high jump gold with his backward curved technique. Every high jumper before used straddle or Western Roll. Every high jumper since uses the Fosbury Flop. It was legal because rules required "athlete must clear bar" -- no specification of orientation.

**The Black Power Salute**:

### 1976 Montreal

**African boycott (28 nations)**: New Zealand's rugby team had toured apartheid South Africa. African nations demanded NZ expulsion from Montreal. IOC refused (rugby was not an Olympic sport). 28 African nations withdrew, plus Guyana and Iraq. Tanzania's Julius Nyerere: "We are against apartheid and all forms of racial discrimination."

**Cost**: Montreal's Olympic facilities ran massively over budget. Montreal taxpayers repaid the debt over 30 years (final payment 2006). Triggered redesign of Olympic financial models.

```
1980 MOSCOW BOYCOTT:
  Trigger: Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (December 1979)

  Participating boycotting nations: ~65 nations
  (including W. Germany, Japan, Canada, China, Kenya)
  Nations that participated: 80 nations
  -- Britain, France, Australia partially participated
     (athletes competed under IOC flag, not national flag)
  -- British athletes who competed later said UK

  had already won 5 speed skating golds at Lake Placid
  1980 -- those Games not affected) lost their only
  possible Olympic moment.

1984 LOS ANGELES BOYCOTT:
  Soviet-led retaliation for 1980
  ~14 nations boycotted (Soviet bloc minus Romania)
  Romania broke with bloc and competed

1984 LA OLYMPICS -- THE TURNING POINT:
  Organizer: Peter Ueberroth
  Model: No public funding. Corporate sponsorship.

  Innovations:
  -- Official sponsor categories sold exclusively

  -- TOP Program (The Olympic Partners) 1985:

  -- Host city burden increased as IOC captured
     revenue while cities carried infrastructure cost
  -- Los Angeles 2028 return: same city, same model
```

## Doping History: The Technical Arc

```
DOPING TIMELINE:

1904 St. Louis: Thomas Hicks (marathon winner) given
               strychnine + brandy + raw egg by coach mid-race
               (strychnine = stimulant at low doses, legal)

1950s:         Amphetamines widespread in cycling, field events
               Danish cyclist Knud Enemark Jensen dies,
               Rome 1960 (amphetamine + Roniacol)
               British cyclist Tommy Simpson dies,
               Tour de France 1967 (amphetamines)

1960s-70s:     Anabolic steroids enter sport
               Shot putters, weightlifters, field athletes
               No test yet -- doctors prescribed openly

1970s-80s:     EAST GERMAN STATE DOPING PROGRAM
               Staatssicherheit (Stasi) secret program
               Systematic oral Turinabol (chlorodehydromethyl-
               testosterone, "OT") to all East German athletes
               Athletes often not told what they were taking
               Documented via Stasi files after 1989 reunification
               ~10,000 athletes given the program
               Results: massive medal haul; significant health
               damage to athletes (gender-based side effects)

1988 Seoul:    Ben Johnson (Canada)
               100m final: 9.79 (WR) -- extraordinary race
               Carl Lewis 9.92, Linford Christie 9.97
               Johnson: stripped, stanozolol positive
               9.79 removed from record books
               BUT: investigation revealed multiple
               other runners in that race had doped
               at various points

1990s:         EPO (erythropoietin) revolution
               Synthetic version of natural hormone
               Increases red blood cells -> endurance boost
               No test until 2000
               Cycling: 1990s known as "EPO era"
               1998 Tour de France Festina affair:
               Festina team car searched at border
               Found massive drug cache (EPO, HGH,
               testosterone, amphetamines)
               Tour scandal; Willy Voet (masseur) arrested
               Multiple teams withdrew; Tour continued

1999:          WADA established
               World Anti-Doping Agency
               Montreal headquarters
               Response to Festina and growing concern

2000 Sydney:   First EPO blood test (indirect markers)
               Gene doping concerns begin

2008 Beijing:  Biological Passport introduced
               Method: Bayesian longitudinal anomaly detection —
               establish per-athlete baseline distribution of
               blood values, flag deviations beyond credible
               interval. This is statistical process control
               applied to biomarkers: the "normal range" is
               athlete-specific (individual prior), not
               population-level. Detects doping by profile
               change, not by identifying the substance.
               (hematological module -- tracks blood values)
               Steroidal module added 2014

2012:          WADA begins retesting stored samples
               from previous Games (10-year storage)
               2012-2016: multiple 2008/2012 medalists
               stripped on retest

2014-2022:     RUSSIA STATE DOPING PROGRAM
               McLaren Report (2016): FSB (intelligence
               service) coordinated sample-swapping
               at Sochi 2014
               "Mouse hole" in anti-doping lab wall
               Urine samples swapped through hole at night
               Russia excluded from Rio 2016 flag/anthem
               (athletes competed as "OAR")
               Russia excluded from Tokyo/Beijing/Paris
               flag/anthem (AIN = Individual Neutral Athletes)
```

**Lance Armstrong**: 7 Tour de France titles (1999-2005), all vacated after USADA investigation (2012). Most methodical doping program in sport history: EPO, testosterone, blood transfusions, human growth hormone, cortisone. Armstrong's aggression toward accusers (Greg LeMond, Betsy Andreu, Floyd Landis, others) was itself a documented program of intimidation. USADA report: 1,000+ pages.

**Marion Jones**: 5 medals (3 gold) Sydney 2000, admitted steroid use in 2007, all medals stripped. Served 6 months federal prison for lying to federal investigators (perjury charge -- not doping charge).

## The Commercial Olympics: Post-1984 Model

```
IOC REVENUE STRUCTURE (typical quadrennium):

  Broadcast rights:       ~75% of revenue
  TOP sponsorship:        ~18% of revenue
  Other (licensing etc.): ~7%

  TOP PROGRAM PARTNERS (~2024):
  Alibaba (digital), Airbnb, Bridgestone, Coca-Cola,
  Intel, Omega, Panasonic, Procter & Gamble, Samsung,
  Toyota, Visa

  BROADCAST DEALS (USA alone):
  NBC deal through 2032: $7.65 billion
  NBC/Peacock: streaming + linear split

  HOST CITY ECONOMICS (reality):
  IOC collects: broadcast + TOP revenue
  Host city pays: infrastructure, security, operations
  -- Athens 2004: ~$11B spent, severe overruns
  -- Beijing 2008: ~$40B total (state-funded, deliberately)
  -- London 2012: ~$18B total
  -- Rio 2016: ~$13.2B, massive corruption scandal
  -- Tokyo 2020/2021: ~$20B+ (pandemic costs added)
  "Olympic effect" on economy: most independent
  studies show negative or near-zero net economic benefit
  for host cities. Venue white elephants are common.
```

**The Dream Team (Barcelona 1992)**: For the first time, NBA professionals were allowed to compete for the US Olympic basketball team. The roster (Jordan, Magic, Bird, Barkley, Ewing, Pippen, Drexler, Stockton, Malone, Mullin, Robinson, Laettner) destroyed every opponent by an average of 44 points. The significance: it marked the explicit abandonment of the amateur ideal in basketball. All other sports subsequently liberalized.

## IOC Governance Scandals

**Salt Lake City bribery scandal (1998-1999)**: Investigation revealed that several IOC members had accepted cash, gifts, medical care, university tuition for children, and other benefits from Salt Lake City's bid committee for the 2002 Winter Olympics. 10 IOC members expelled, 10 others sanctioned. Juan Antonio Samaranch (IOC President 1980-2001) was criticized for a culture of patronage and corruption that allowed this to develop over decades. The scandal resulted in new ethics rules, but the structural incentives (wealthy cities competing for the Games with enormous commercial interests) remained.

**Thomas Bach era (2013-present)**: Continued IOC confrontations with Russia, Qatar, China over human rights. Bach's approach: "political neutrality" framing that often appeared indistinguishable from deference to authoritarian hosts. Tokyo 2020 corruption probe: organizing committee executives arrested for bribery related to corporate sponsorship contracts.

## Common Confusion Points

**"Amateurism was a fair system"** -- It was structurally a class filter. A Soviet state-paid athlete who trained 8 hours a day was "amateur." An American who played 3 semi-pro baseball games for gas money was "professional." Avery Brundage called Soviet state sport "amateur" while calling out American athletes for minor payments.

**"Hitler publicly snubbed Jesse Owens"** -- This didn't happen as commonly described. Hitler stopped congratulating medal winners publicly after the IOC told him to congratulate all or none. Owens was not singled out. Owens himself was more upset that FDR sent no telegram. The real American scandal was the last-minute removal of Jewish sprinters Glickman and Stoller from the relay team.

**"The Munich massacre ended the Games"** -- The Games were suspended for one day (memorial service) and then resumed. Avery Brundage's "The Games must go on" speech at the memorial mixed Munich with his amateurism concerns in a way that was widely criticized. The IOC resuming the Games was controversial but deliberate.

**"Ben Johnson's 9.79 was the real record"** -- The 100m final in Seoul 1988 was later called the "dirtiest race in history." Six of the eight finalists had been connected to doping at some point. Johnson was caught; others were not at that time. The race was not uniquely dirty -- it was uniquely investigated.

**"East German athletes knew they were doping"** -- The Stasi documents reveal that many athletes, particularly younger ones, were not told what they were being given ("vitamins," "supplements"). Coaches and doctors knew; athletes varied. The subsequent health damage (particularly to female athletes) was documented but not compensated for decades.

**"The Olympic Games provide economic benefit to host cities"** -- The academic consensus is that hosting the Olympics rarely produces the forecast economic benefits. Cost overruns are systematic. Venue white elephants (Athens's empty velodrome, Rio's green diving pool) are standard. The primary beneficiary is the IOC's revenue structure, not the host city.
