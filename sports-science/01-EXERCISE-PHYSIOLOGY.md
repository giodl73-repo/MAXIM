# Exercise Physiology: Energy Systems

## The Big Picture

The body has three energy systems for regenerating ATP. They operate in parallel but at different rates and predominate at different exercise durations. Understanding them is the foundation for designing any training program.

```
ENERGY SYSTEM OVERVIEW:

  Time     0               10s            2min            1hr+
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  System   |  PCr/ALACTIC  |   GLYCOLYTIC   |    OXIDATIVE              |
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Fuel     Phosphocreatine  Muscle glycogen  Glycogen + fat
  O₂?      No (anaerobic)   No (anaerobic)   Yes (aerobic)
  Lactate? No               Yes (H⁺ + La⁻)  No (at moderate intensity)
  Rate     Fastest          Fast             Slow
  Capacity Very limited     Limited          Very large
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ALL THREE SYSTEMS OPERATE SIMULTANEOUSLY.
The "dominant" system at any intensity is the one contributing most ATP.
At maximal 100m sprint: ~50% PCr, ~45% glycolytic, ~5% aerobic.
At marathon pace: ~2% PCr, ~3% glycolytic, ~95% aerobic.
```

## The Phosphocreatine (PCr) System

### Mechanism

```
ATP → ADP + Pi + Energy (for muscle contraction)
↑
PCr + ADP → Cr + ATP  (creatine kinase reaction, very fast)

THE RELAY:
  PCr is pre-stored in muscle. When ATP is split, creatine kinase
  uses PCr to regenerate ATP almost instantaneously.
  No O₂ required. No lactate produced.

CAPACITY:
  Resting PCr stores: ~15–20 mmol/kg dry muscle weight.
  At max effort: depleted in ~5–10 seconds.
  Recovery: PCr resynthesis ~50% in 30 seconds; ~95% in 3–5 minutes.
  This is why sprint training uses 30s rest between 5–10s efforts.
  Less rest → less PCr restoration → lower power output in next sprint.

ATP CONCENTRATION:
  Resting ATP: ~4–5 mmol/kg dry muscle weight.
  Without PCr buffering: would be depleted in ~1–2 seconds.
  PCr extends the high-power, anaerobic window by ~5–8 seconds.

SPORTS APPLICATION:
  Weightlifting, 100m sprint, shot put, high jump:
  All rely primarily on the PCr system.
  Training: high-intensity, short-duration efforts (3–8 sec);
            full recovery between sets (3–5 minutes).
  Creatine supplementation: increases PCr stores by 10–40%.
  This directly extends the PCr energy window and improves
  performance in this time domain.
```

## The Glycolytic System

### Mechanism

```
GLYCOLYSIS (Embden-Meyerhof pathway):
  Glucose → 2 Pyruvate + 2 ATP (net)

  FAST GLYCOLYSIS:
    When pyruvate production exceeds mitochondrial processing capacity:
    Pyruvate → Lactate (via lactate dehydrogenase)
    + NAD⁺ regenerated (allows glycolysis to continue)
    Produces: ~3 ATP per glucose (or ~3.3 from glycogen)

  SLOW GLYCOLYSIS:
    Pyruvate enters mitochondria → acetyl-CoA → TCA cycle.
    This is the bridge to oxidative metabolism.
```

### The Lactate Misconception

```
THE MISCONCEPTION: "Lactic acid causes the burn and fatigue."
This is wrong. Two separate errors:

ERROR 1: "Lactic acid" is produced.
  At physiological pH, lactic acid immediately dissociates:
  Lactic acid → Lactate⁻ + H⁺
  The compound in muscle is lactate (the ion), not lactic acid.
  This matters because:

ERROR 2: Lactate causes fatigue.
  Lactate is NOT the cause of the burning sensation or fatigue.
  Evidence: lactate is actually a FUEL — muscles, heart, and brain
            preferentially use lactate as fuel during exercise.
            Infusing lactate into resting muscles does not cause fatigue.

WHAT ACTUALLY CAUSES FATIGUE (current evidence):
  H⁺ (proton accumulation): acidosis impairs cross-bridge cycling.
    ATP hydrolysis releases H⁺: ATP → ADP + Pi + H⁺.
    Glycolysis does not produce H⁺ net — the ATP hydrolysis does.
  Pi (inorganic phosphate): impairs Ca²⁺ sensitivity of troponin.
    Also impairs cross-bridge power stroke.
  These two (H⁺ and Pi) explain most acute muscular fatigue.

WHY THE MISCONCEPTION PERSISTS:
  Lactate and H⁺ rise together (because glycolysis → H⁺ from ATP hydrolysis).
  Blood lactate is easy to measure; H⁺ is harder.
  Therefore: lactate became the proxy measure for acidosis.
  The correlation was mistaken for causation.
  Teach: "lactate threshold" is a real and important concept,
          but it is a marker, not the cause of the problem.

LACTATE AS FUEL:
  George Brooks' "lactate shuttle" (1984, confirmed many times):
  Lactate produced in fast-twitch glycolytic fibers is exported
  to slow-twitch oxidative fibers, heart, and liver for oxidation.
  Also shuttled between muscles → liver → blood glucose.
  At rest: the heart prefers lactate over glucose.
  During exercise: liver takes up lactate → converts to glucose (Cori cycle).
```

## The Oxidative System

### Mechanism

```
FULL AEROBIC PATHWAY:
  Glucose/Glycogen → Pyruvate (glycolysis)
                          ↓ (if O₂ available)
                    Acetyl-CoA
                          ↓
                     TCA Cycle (Krebs cycle)
                          |
             NADH, FADH₂ produced →
                   Electron Transport Chain (ETC)
                          |
             O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O    (terminal electron acceptor)
                          |
                         ATP synthesis (ATP synthase, chemiosmosis)

TOTAL ATP YIELD:
  Glucose → oxidative: ~30–32 ATP per glucose molecule
  Compare glycolysis alone: ~2–3 ATP
  The ETC provides ~90% of aerobic ATP.

FAT OXIDATION (beta-oxidation):
  Fat stored as triglycerides → free fatty acids → acetyl-CoA → TCA.
  ATP yield: ~130 ATP per 18-carbon fatty acid (vs. 30 for glucose).
  MUCH more total energy per gram, but:
  Rate-limiting: fat oxidation is slower than glycogen (less ATP/second).
  Requires more O₂ per ATP produced.
  KEY: At low-moderate intensity (below LT1): fat is the primary fuel.
        At high intensity: carbohydrate dominates (rate requirement).
        "Fat burns in a carbohydrate flame" — TCA cycle requires OAA
        (from carbohydrate) to accept acetyl-CoA.
```

## Muscle Fiber Types

```
FIBER TYPE COMPARISON:

                    Type I          Type IIa        Type IIx
                  (Slow-twitch)   (Fast-twitch,   (Fast-twitch,
                                  oxidative)       glycolytic)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Twitch speed       Slow            Fast             Very fast
Force production   Low             High             Very high
Fatigue           Resistant        Moderate          Fast
Mitochondria      High density     Moderate          Low
Myoglobin          High (red)      Moderate (red)    Low (pale)
Capillary density  High            Moderate          Low
Primary fuel       Fat             Mixed             Glycogen/PCr
Fiber diameter     Small           Medium            Large
ATPase activity    Slow myosin     Fast myosin       Fast myosin
                   heavy chain     heavy chain       heavy chain
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DISTRIBUTION:
  Humans: ~50:50 Type I:II average (highly variable; range ~20–80% Type I).
  Elite endurance athletes: ~80%+ Type I (or training-induced Type IIa↑).
  Elite sprinters: ~80%+ Type II.
  Note: Type IIb/IIx is the same fiber (IIx is modern nomenclature; IIb is older).
        Humans have very little pure IIx; most "fast" fibers are IIa.

TRAINABILITY:
  Type I ↔ Type IIa: these fiber types shift toward each other with training.
  Type IIx: can shift toward IIa with endurance training; rarely to Type I.
  Fundamental fiber type (I vs. II): highly heritable; resistant to change.
  This is why elite endurance athletes are born with high Type I proportions;
  training improves all athletes but cannot fully overcome this genetic factor.
```

## Metabolic Adaptations to Training

### Endurance Adaptations

```
MITOCHONDRIAL BIOGENESIS:
  Endurance training → mitochondria multiply.
  Signal pathway: PGC-1α (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor
                   gamma coactivator 1-alpha) is the master regulator.
  Signals that activate PGC-1α:
    AMPK: activated by low energy state (high AMP:ATP ratio)
    Ca²⁺: from repeated muscle contractions
    Reactive oxygen species (ROS): from exercise
  PGC-1α → upregulates mitochondrial DNA transcription → more mitochondria.

  TIME COURSE: 4–8 weeks of endurance training → detectable mitochondria increase.
               Full adaptation: months to years.

  CONSEQUENCE: More mitochondria → higher oxidative capacity →
               more fat oxidation at given intensity →
               carbohydrate spared for high-intensity work →
               improved endurance performance.

FAT OXIDATION IMPROVEMENT:
  Trained athletes: oxidize fat at higher intensities than untrained.
  Also: higher absolute rate of fat oxidation at any given intensity.
  Training mechanism: more mitochondria + more fat oxidation enzymes
                       (beta-oxidation enzymes upregulated by PGC-1α).
  Practical: athletes who "train low" (low glycogen) may further enhance fat adaptation.

GLYCOGEN STORAGE:
  Training → muscle glycogen stores increase by 20–50%.
  Trained athlete: ~500–600g total body glycogen vs. ~300g untrained.
  This is important: glycogen is the critical fuel at race intensities.
  More storage → longer before depletion.

CAPILLARY DENSITY:
  Endurance training → more capillaries per muscle fiber (angiogenesis).
  More capillaries → shorter O₂ diffusion distance → better O₂ delivery.
  Also: more time for blood-muscle O₂ exchange (slower RBC transit past fiber).
```

### Strength Adaptations

```
EARLY STRENGTH GAINS (weeks 1–8): neural adaptations.
  Increased motor unit recruitment (more fibers firing).
  Improved motor unit synchronization.
  Disinhibition: removal of inhibitory reflexes (Golgi tendon organ).
  No measurable hypertrophy yet; strength increases rapidly.

LATER STRENGTH GAINS (months): hypertrophy.
  Myofibrillar protein synthesis: more actin and myosin in each fiber.
  Satellite cells: muscle stem cells; fuse with fibers to add myonuclei.
  Connective tissue adaptations: tendon, bone (slower than muscle).
```

## Oxygen Debt and EPOC

```
EPOC (Excess Post-Exercise Oxygen Consumption):
  After exercise, O₂ consumption remains elevated above resting levels.
  Previously called "oxygen debt" (old term; mechanistically imprecise).

  CAUSES:
  Fast component (minutes):
    PCr resynthesis: requires ATP → aerobic metabolism.
    Lactate clearance: conversion to glucose (Cori cycle) + oxidation.
    Elevated core temperature: higher T → faster metabolism.

  Slow component (hours):
    Hormone normalization (catecholamines, cortisol).
    Restoration of glycogen (glucose→glycogen = energy cost).
    Protein synthesis (post-exercise).
    Mitochondrial O₂ consumption (leak).

  MAGNITUDE:
  After moderate exercise: ~3–10 L EPOC over 20–30 minutes.
  After maximal exercise: up to ~15+ L over longer period.

  MARKETING CLAIM: "EPOC burns fat for 48 hours after exercise."
  REALITY: EPOC is real; the magnitude is small.
           After 45 minutes of vigorous cycling: EPOC ~100–200 extra calories over 24 hours.
           This is not zero; it is also not transformative.
           The dominant calorie burn is during the exercise, not after.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Energy System | Dominant Time Domain | Sport Example | Training Method |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| PCr | 0–10 seconds | 100m sprint, powerlifting | 3–6 sec efforts; 3–5 min rest |
| Glycolytic | 10 sec – 2 min | 400m, 800m, hockey shift | 15–120 sec efforts; 1:3–5 work:rest ratio |
| Oxidative | 2 min+ | Marathon, triathlon, road cycling | Long slow + interval training |

| Adaptation | Training Stimulus | Time Course |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| Mitochondrial biogenesis | Volume endurance training | 4–12 weeks |
| Fat oxidation improvement | Endurance + train-low protocol | 4–12 weeks |
| Glycogen stores increase | Endurance + CHO-rich diet | 2–4 weeks |
| Neural strength gains | Heavy resistance training | 1–4 weeks |
| Myofibrillar hypertrophy | Volume + intensity resistance | 6–12+ weeks |
| PCr store increase | Creatine + sprint training | 1–4 weeks (creatine loading) |

## Common Confusion Points

**The three energy systems are always simultaneously active**: the "dominant system" concept is a teaching simplification. All three contribute at every intensity; the ratios shift with intensity and duration.

**Aerobic means with oxygen; anaerobic means without**: these describe metabolic pathways, not exercise. You breathe hard during glycolytic exercise, but the glycolysis itself does not use O₂. You are breathing to process the products (lactate, H⁺) and to restore PCr, not because the glycolysis needs O₂.

**Lactate is a fuel, not a waste product**: this is the most important conceptual update in the last 40 years of exercise physiology. Lactate produced in glycolytic fibers fuels oxidative fibers, the heart, and the liver. "Removing" lactate is removing a fuel source, not detoxifying. Lactate clearance in trained athletes is via oxidation, not via conversion to glucose (though both occur).

**Type IIb fibers in humans**: most textbooks still say humans have Type I, IIa, and IIb fibers. The modern nomenclature replaces IIb with IIx, based on the myosin heavy chain isoform. More important: humans have very little pure IIx — most "fast" human fibers are IIa (fast oxidative glycolytic), not pure glycolytic like IIx in rodents.

**EPOC is real but small**: the claim that high-intensity training "keeps burning calories for 24–48 hours" has a real basis in EPOC but is dramatically overstated in commercial fitness contexts. The excess oxygen consumption after hard exercise is measurable but small relative to the exercise itself.
