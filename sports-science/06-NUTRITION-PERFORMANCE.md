# Sports Nutrition and Ergogenic Aids

## The Big Picture

Sports nutrition is the application of nutritional science to optimize training adaptation and competitive performance. The key insight: fueling strategy is as trainable as any physical quality.

```
SPORTS NUTRITION FRAMEWORK:

  ENERGY AVAILABILITY (EA)
  +------------------------------------------+
  | EA = Energy intake - Exercise expenditure |
  | EA target: >45 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day |
  | EA <30 kcal/kg FFM/day → RED-S risk       |
  +------------------------------------------+
             |
    +---------+----------+
    |                    |
    v                    v
  MACRONUTRIENT         MICRONUTRIENT
  PERIODIZATION         STATUS
  +------------------+  +------------------+
  | CHO availability |  | Iron, Vitamin D, |
  | Protein timing   |  | Calcium, B12...  |
  | Fat adaptation   |  | Deficiency =     |
  |                  |  | performance loss |
  +------------------+  +------------------+
             |

  ERGOGENIC AIDS
  +------------------------------------------+
  | Evidence-based: creatine, caffeine, beta- |
  | alanine, sodium bicarbonate, nitrate      |
  +------------------------------------------+
```

## Energy Availability

```
DEFINITION:
  EA = Energy intake (kcal/day) - Exercise energy expenditure (kcal/day)
       ÷ Fat-free mass (kg)
  Units: kcal/kg FFM/day

  LOW EA THRESHOLD: <30 kcal/kg FFM/day → metabolic disruption.
  OPTIMAL EA: 45+ kcal/kg FFM/day.

RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport):
  Named: Mountjoy et al. (British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2014).
  Replaced older "female athlete triad" (disordered eating, amenorrhea, osteoporosis).
  RED-S affects both males and females.

  CONSEQUENCES OF LOW EA:
  Bone: reduced bone mineral density (stress fracture risk).
  Endocrine: suppressed testosterone/estrogen; LH pulse suppression.
  Metabolic rate: decreases → weight stable despite very low intake.
  Cardiovascular: cardiac function changes.
  Immune: impaired function.
  Muscle: protein synthesis impaired → poor training adaptation.
  Psychology: mood, cognition, concentration.
  Growth (adolescents): delayed puberty, stunted growth.

  DIAGNOSIS:
  Questionnaire tools (LEAF-Q for females, LEAF-M for males).
  Clinical: menstrual history, bone density, hormonal panel.
  Energy audit: detailed dietary records + energy expenditure assessment.

  INCIDENCE:
  Female endurance athletes: 30–50% estimated low EA.
  Male endurance athletes: 15–25%.
  Weight-class sports (judo, wrestling, boxing): high risk around competition.
```

## Macronutrient Periodization

### Carbohydrate Availability

```
CARBOHYDRATE AVAILABILITY CONCEPT:
  "Train low, compete high" — strategic manipulation of muscle glycogen.

TRAIN HIGH (high CHO availability):
  Glycogen stores full; high-quality training possible.
  Recommended for: hard training sessions; technical sessions;
                   high-intensity interval sessions; competition preparation.

TRAIN LOW (low CHO availability):
  Reduced glycogen before/during session.
  Methods:
    Fasted morning training (overnight fast depletes liver glycogen).
    Two-a-day training: second session before first session's glycogen is fully restored.
    Sleep low: carbohydrate restricted after evening session.
  Proposed benefit: amplified AMPK and PGC-1α signaling → stronger adaptation.
  Evidence: short-term acute adaptation markers improved.
            Long-term performance benefits: less clear.
            Training quality reduced: hard sessions suffer on low CHO.

CARBOHYDRATE RECOMMENDATIONS:
  Low-intensity days: 3–5 g/kg body weight.
  Moderate training: 5–7 g/kg.
  High-volume/intensity days: 7–10 g/kg.
  Endurance events >90 minutes: 10–12 g/kg in the 24–36h before.

THE PRACTICAL RULE: high CHO for important sessions; flexible CHO for easy sessions.
                     The science of periodizing within this is evolving.
```

### Pre/During/Post Exercise Nutrition

```
PRE-EXERCISE:
  Main meal: 3–4 hours before.
    Mixed composition (CHO + protein + moderate fat); ~700–1,200 kcal.
    Purpose: top up glycogen; stable blood glucose; gut settled.

  Optional top-up: 30–60 minutes before.
    Simple CHO: 30–60g; low fiber, low fat.
    Bananas, white toast, sports drink, gel.
    High-GI foods: fast gastric emptying; glucose available within 30 min.

DURING EXERCISE:
  Duration <60 min: water only (glycogen will not limit performance).
  Duration 60–90 min: optional 30g CHO (benefit marginal but not harmful).
  Duration >90 min: 30–60g CHO/hour (single-transporter: glucose or fructose).
  Duration >2.5h+: 60–90g CHO/hour requires MULTIPLE TRANSPORTABLE CHOs.

  MULTIPLE TRANSPORTABLE CARBOHYDRATES (MTC):
  Glucose uses SGLT1 transporter (saturable at ~60g/hr).
  Fructose uses GLUT5 transporter (parallel, not competing).
  Combined glucose + fructose (2:1 ratio): up to 90g CHO/hr absorbed.
  Products: most modern "isotonic" sports drinks; maltodextrin + fructose gels.
  Evidence: 2:1 glucose:fructose improves performance vs. glucose alone at >60g/hr.

POST-EXERCISE:
  Window: most important within 30 minutes for glycogen resynthesis.
           Protein: most important within 2 hours for MPS.
  CHO: 1.0–1.2 g/kg immediately post; repeat every 30 min for 4h.
        High-GI CHO: fastest glycogen repletion.
  Protein: 0.3 g/kg within 2 hours; emphasize leucine-rich sources.
  Combined CHO + protein post: synergistic for glycogen AND protein synthesis.
```

## Protein Requirements

```
PROTEIN FOR ATHLETES:
  Sedentary adults: 0.8 g/kg/day (RDA).
  Endurance athletes: 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day.
  Strength/power athletes: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day.
  Hypertrophy goal: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (upper end of range gives marginal additional benefit).
  Evidence: meta-analysis (Morton et al. 2018): >1.62 g/kg provides no further benefit
            for hypertrophy in resistance-trained athletes.

  LEUCINE THRESHOLD:
  Leucine is the key amino acid signaling mTOR (protein synthesis).
  ~2–3g leucine per meal appears to maximally stimulate MPS.
  Achievable with: 25–40g of most protein sources (dairy, eggs, meat).
  Whey: ~11% leucine → 25g whey = ~2.75g leucine.
  Plant proteins: lower leucine per gram → need more total protein.
    Soy: ~8% leucine; rice protein: ~8%; pea: ~8%.
    Combine plant proteins or increase total intake.

PROTEIN DISTRIBUTION:
  3–4 meals per day, each containing ~25–40g protein.
  Evidence: equal distribution across 3 meals vs. skewed distribution
            (most at one meal) → equal distribution produces more MPS
            over 24h. Consistent with "leucine threshold" concept.
  Pre-sleep protein: 40g casein before sleep → MPS during overnight fast.
    Casein: slow-digesting; maintains elevated amino acids for hours.
    Beneficial for athletes in heavy training phases.
```

## Creatine Monohydrate

The most evidence-supported legal ergogenic aid:

```
MECHANISM:
  Supplementation increases muscle PCr stores by 10–40%.
  More PCr → more rapid ATP regeneration → more repetitions at high intensity.
  Also: increased cell hydration (creatine is osmotically active → water follows).
  Also: evidence of satellite cell activation → enhanced hypertrophy.

PROTOCOL:
  Loading (optional):
    20g/day × 5 days (divided into 4 × 5g doses with meals).
    Achieves muscle saturation in 5 days.

  Maintenance:
    3–5g/day (saturation maintained).
    Without loading: saturation in ~3–4 weeks.
    Loading is optional — same endpoint, different timeline.

  POST-LOAD:
    Continue 3–5g/day indefinitely if training continues.
    Stop supplementation: PCr stores normalize in 4–6 weeks.

EVIDENCE:
  >500 published studies. Consistent effects for short-duration,
  high-intensity performance (sprint repeat, resistance training).
  Meta-analysis data: +8% performance in high-intensity exercise;
                       +14% resistance training volume compared to placebo.
  Hypertrophy: +1–2 kg more lean mass than placebo over 8–12 weeks
               of resistance training.
  Evidence rating: Grade A (multiple high-quality RCTs, consistent findings).

WHO RESPONDS:
  ~25% are "non-responders" (already high baseline PCr stores, typically
  high meat diet). Vegetarians: typically larger response (lower baseline).

SAFETY:
  Extensively studied; no adverse effects in healthy adults.
  Common concern: kidney damage — not supported in healthy individuals.
    Creatinine (a creatine metabolite) increases; this is NOT kidney damage.
    It is a false positive for kidney stress markers.
  Avoid: in individuals with pre-existing kidney disease (precautionary).
  Weight gain: 0.5–1.5 kg in first week = water (creatine osmotic effect).
               This is not fat; it reverses on stopping.
```

## Caffeine

```
MECHANISM:
  Adenosine receptor antagonist.
  Adenosine: inhibitory neurotransmitter; accumulates with fatigue.
  Caffeine: blocks adenosine receptors → delays perception of fatigue.
  Also: enhances calcium release from sarcoplasmic reticulum →
        improved muscle contraction force.
  Central AND peripheral effects.

DOSE:
  Optimal: 3–6 mg/kg body weight, 45–60 minutes before exercise.
  70 kg athlete: 210–420 mg (~2–4 espressos; standard cup of filter coffee ~80–150 mg).
  Higher doses (>6 mg/kg): no additional benefit; more side effects.
  Low doses (1–3 mg/kg): may still be effective; fewer side effects.

EFFECTS:
  Endurance: delay to fatigue; reduced RPE.
  Team sports: sprint performance, reaction time, accuracy.
  Strength: mixed evidence; modest effect on 1RM.
  Evidence: grade A for endurance and team sports.

HABITUAL USE:
  Regular caffeine consumers develop tolerance.
  Performance benefit is NOT abolished by habituation (meta-analysis).
  But: optimal to minimize use in days before competition (acute sensitivity).
  Caffeine withdrawal (tapering 3–7 days before): small additional benefit.
  This is not required for benefit; the effect is marginal.

GUT TOLERANCE:
  Some athletes: GI distress at higher doses; during long events.
  GI-sensitive athletes: test dose and timing in training, not competition.
  Alternative delivery: gum (faster absorption); gel.
```

## Other Evidence-Based Ergogenics

```
BETA-ALANINE:
  Precursor to carnosine (a muscle buffer).
  Carnosine buffers H⁺ → delays acidosis in glycolytic exercise.
  Loading: 3.2–6.4 g/day over 4–6 weeks → elevated muscle carnosine.
  Evidence: benefits 1–4 minute high-intensity events (800m, 400m, rowing 2km).
  Side effect: paresthesia (tingling) — harmless; reduced by slow-release forms.
  Evidence grade: A for 1–4 min high-intensity events; B elsewhere.

SODIUM BICARBONATE:
  Extracellular buffer → offloads H⁺ from muscle to blood.
  Dose: 0.3 g/kg body weight, 60–90 min before event.
  Evidence: benefits high-intensity exercise 1–8 min duration.
  Side effect: significant GI distress risk (nausea, vomiting) in many athletes.
  Test tolerance in training before competition use.
  Evidence grade: A for 1–8 min high-intensity performance.

DIETARY NITRATE (beetroot juice):
  Nitrate → Nitrite → Nitric oxide (NO).
  NO: vasodilator; also reduces O₂ cost of exercise (improved mitochondrial efficiency).
  Source: 300–600 mg nitrate (~500 mL beetroot juice or concentrate shot).
  Timing: 2–3 hours before exercise.
  Evidence: improves exercise efficiency (same power for less O₂);
            benefits endurance and team sport performance.
  Evidence grade: A for recreation to sub-elite; weaker effect in elite athletes
                  (who may already have highly efficient vasodilation).
```

## Iron Status in Endurance Athletes

```
IRON PHYSIOLOGY:
  Iron is the center of the hemoglobin molecule.
  Hemoglobin carries O₂ in blood.
  Iron deficiency → iron deficiency anemia → reduced O₂ delivery → impaired VO2max.

ENDURANCE ATHLETE RISK FACTORS:
  Foot-strike hemolysis: running impact destroys red blood cells in foot capillaries.
    More common with high mileage, hard surfaces, thin insoles.
  Hepcidin response: exercise increases hepcidin (iron absorption regulator);
    reduces iron absorption in hours after exercise.
    Avoid iron supplements and high-iron foods immediately post-exercise.
  Menstrual losses: significant iron loss monthly.
  Inadequate dietary iron: especially on plant-based diets.
  Sweat losses: minor but cumulative.

MONITORING:
  Serum ferritin: iron stores. Target >30 ng/mL for athletes (vs. >12 for general population).
    Low-normal ferritin (12–30): "iron-depleted without anemia" — still causes fatigue and
    impaired adaptation without measurable anemia.
  Hemoglobin: <12 g/dL female, <13 g/dL male = anemia.
  Regular monitoring: female endurance athletes every 6 months; male every year.

TREATMENT:
  Dietary: red meat (heme iron, high bioavailability), plus plant iron (non-heme) with Vit C.
  Supplement: ferrous sulfate 100–200mg/day, between meals, with Vit C, away from exercise.
  IV iron: faster repletion; reserved for severely depleted athletes or race preparation.
```

## Heat and Altitude Nutrition

```
HEAT ACCLIMATIZATION:
  Increased sweat volume → higher electrolyte losses.
  Sodium: most important; lost in sweat (1–3g/hr at high sweat rate).
  Inadequate sodium replacement during long events in heat → hyponatremia
  (low blood sodium) — more dangerous than dehydration.
  1–2g sodium/hour for events >3 hours in heat.

ALTITUDE NUTRITION:
  High altitude (>2,000m): increased EPO production (hypoxia stimulus).
  Enhanced RBC production requires: iron (ferritin >35 recommended before altitude camp),
                                     B12, folate.
  Iron supplementation: consider supplementing if ferritin <35 before altitude exposure.
  Energy needs increase at altitude (respiratory work + cold + possible appetite suppression).
  Carbohydrate: altitude increases reliance on CHO (fat oxidation impaired).
                Higher CHO intake recommended at altitude.
```

## Common Confusion Points

**RED-S is not a female condition**: originally described as the "female athlete triad" (disordered eating, amenorrhea, osteoporosis). The 2014 reclassification to RED-S recognizes that low energy availability in male athletes causes equivalent consequences (testosterone suppression, bone density reduction, hormonal disruption, impaired performance). Male endurance athletes under-report this.

**Multiple transportable carbohydrates are not a marketing gimmick**: the saturation of the SGLT1 glucose transporter at ~60g/hr is real gut physiology. Adding fructose (different transporter) genuinely increases total carbohydrate absorption. This has been replicated in numerous exercise science studies. The products designed around this (2:1 glucose:fructose ratios) have meaningful evidence behind them for events >2.5 hours.

**Protein timing is less critical than protein total**: the "anabolic window" concept was exaggerated. While there is some evidence for post-exercise protein benefit, meeting total daily protein intake (1.6–2.2 g/kg) distributed reasonably across meals is more important than hitting a 30-minute window. The window may matter more in fasted training states.

**Creatine does not directly cause hypertrophy**: creatine increases training volume (more reps per set) because of PCr store enhancement. The hypertrophy comes from the extra training stimulus, not from creatine itself. Plus water weight (osmotic) is immediately added. The long-term lean mass difference in training studies likely reflects the greater cumulative training work done, not a direct anabolic effect.

**Hydration is not just water**: during events >1–2 hours, electrolyte (especially sodium) replacement is essential alongside water. Drinking only water in a marathon creates hyponatremia risk. Pre-loading with sodium-containing fluids is better than just consuming water. Sports drinks that contain electrolytes are not just marketing.
