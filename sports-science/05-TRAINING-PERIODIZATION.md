# Training Theory and Periodization

## The Big Picture

Periodization is the systematic organization of training over time to optimize performance at specific target events while minimizing injury and overtraining. It is fundamentally a load management problem with biological system dynamics.

```
PERIODIZATION HIERARCHY:

  MACROCYCLE (entire season or Olympic cycle: 1–4 years)
  ├── MESOCYCLE (training block: 2–6 weeks)
  │   ├── MICROCYCLE (week: 7 days typically)
  │   │   ├── Training session (individual workout)
  │   │   │   ├── Warm-up
  │   │   │   ├── Main work
  │   │   │   └── Cool-down
  │   │   ├── Training session
  │   │   └── Recovery day
  │   └── Deload microcycle
  └── Competition phase

THE PLANNING QUESTION:
  What training at what intensity and volume, in what sequence,
  produces peak performance at competition time?
  Answer: depends on sport, athlete, training age, injury history.
  But the biological principles are universal.
```

## Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

The theoretical foundation of all periodization:

```
SELYE'S GAS MODEL (Hans Selye, 1936):
  Originally described for disease stress; applied to exercise:

  ALARM PHASE:
    Body encounters stressor (training load).
    Performance temporarily decreases.
    Acute fatigue; muscle soreness; reduced strength.
    The body recognizes: "this is more than I can handle currently."

  RESISTANCE PHASE:
    Body adapts to the stressor.
    Performance returns to baseline and improves.
    Specific adaptations to imposed demands (SAID principle).

  EXHAUSTION:
    If stress too high or too prolonged:
    Adaptation fails; performance degrades.
    Structural and hormonal damage.
    This is overtraining syndrome.

  FOR TRAINING APPLICATION:
  Goal: stay in the resistance phase, not the exhaustion phase.
  Method: progressive overload (increase stress gradually);
          regular deload (prevent exhaustion phase accumulation).
```

## Supercompensation Model

```
SUPERCOMPENSATION:

  Performance
       |
       |                    /\
       |                   /  \
base --|------------------/----\---->
       |           ↑     /      \
       |          deload        \→ next training
       |         /             (decline without training)
       |        /
       |    load↓
       |   /
       |  /
       | / <-- fatigue/performance dip
       |/
       +-----------------------------------------> Time
            Training      Recovery    Peak      Next load

  TIMING THE SUPERCOMPENSATION PEAK:
  Apply next training load at the peak → accumulate fitness.
  Apply training too early (still in fatigue) → no adaptation gain.
  Apply training too late (after decline) → fitness returns to baseline.

  COMPLICATION: different qualities have different supercompensation timing.
    Speed/power: peak at 24–48 hours after session.
    Strength: 48–72 hours.
    Endurance: 3–7 days.
    This is why complex programming is needed — multiple qualities
    peak at different times within the same week.
```

## Historical Periodization Models

### Matveyev's Linear Model

```
MATVEYEV'S PERIODIZATION (Lev Matveyev, Soviet, 1964/1965 published):
  The original systematic periodization model.
  Based on analysis of Soviet Olympic athletes' training data.

  PHASES:
  +-------------------+--------------------+--------------------+
  | PREPARATORY       | COMPETITIVE        | TRANSITION         |
  | (General + Spec.) | (peaking + comps)  | (active rest)      |
  +-------------------+--------------------+--------------------+
  High volume →       Volume decreasing→  Low volume
  Low intensity       Intensity increasing Low intensity

  WITHIN PREPARATORY:
  General Preparation: high volume, general fitness, all energy systems.
  Specific Preparation: increasing specificity, competition-like intensity.
  Pre-Competitive: volume drops; intensity peaks; skills refined.
  Competition: performance peaks; maintenance volume only.

  MESOCYCLE STRUCTURE (within Matveyev):
  Accumulation → Transmutation → Realization
    (volume)     (intensity)     (competition)

  LIMITATION: designed for individual sport with single peak.
              Does not work well for team sports with season of games.
              Does not work well for multiple peaks (track & field with many meets).
```

### Non-Linear (Undulating) Periodization

```
NON-LINEAR PERIODIZATION:
  Variation in training stimulus within shorter time frames
  (daily or weekly) rather than long linear progression.

  DAILY UNDULATING PERIODIZATION (DUP):
  Monday: Heavy (3–5 reps, 85–90% 1RM) — neural/strength
  Wednesday: Moderate (8–12 reps, 65–75% 1RM) — hypertrophy
  Friday: Light-power (12–20 reps + speed, 40–60% 1RM) — endurance/power

  EVIDENCE: DUP consistently matches or exceeds linear periodization
  for strength and hypertrophy gains in the short to medium term.
  Mechanism: more frequent variation → more varied stimuli → multiple
  adaptive pathways stimulated simultaneously.

  BEST FOR:
    Athletes with multiple physical qualities to develop.
    Intermediate to advanced athletes where variety is needed.
    Year-round athletes without single annual competition peak.
```

### Block Periodization

```
BLOCK PERIODIZATION (Vladimir Issurin, 1980s/90s):
  Concentrated loading of a single physical quality per block.
  Blocks last 2–4 weeks; each has a highly specific objective.

  BLOCK TYPES:
  ACCUMULATION: high volume, low intensity.
    Builds general conditioning, aerobic base, hypertrophy.
    Minimizes competing stimuli.

  TRANSMUTATION: moderate volume, high intensity.
    Converts general fitness into sport-specific fitness.
    Reduces competing qualities to focus on specific needs.

  REALIZATION: low volume, very high intensity.
    Express the developed qualities in competition-specific form.
    Taper → peak performance.

  ADVANTAGE: concentrated load creates stronger stimulus for
             the targeted quality without interference.
  DISADVANTAGE: qualities not trained in a block temporarily decline.
               Requires careful sequencing to have all qualities
               peaking simultaneously at competition.

  EVIDENCE: Better than traditional periodization for some sports
             (swimming research: Mujika et al.) but not all contexts.
```

### Conjugate / Westside Method

```
CONJUGATE PERIODIZATION (Westside Barbell / Louie Simmons):
  Develops multiple qualities simultaneously at all times.
  Named after Soviet "conjugate sequence" concept but implemented differently.

  TWO PRIMARY TRAINING METHODS (per week):
  Max Effort (ME): 1–3 rep max; weekly variation of exercise selection.
    Develops: maximal strength (neural → structural).
    Key: change the ME exercise each week to prevent accommodation.
    (Accommodation = adaptation plateau — stimulus loses effect.)

  Dynamic Effort (DE): 45–60% 1RM, maximal intent, 8–12 sets × 2–3 reps.
    Develops: rate of force development and power.
    Bars loaded with 20–30% additional elastic band tension.

  RATIONALE:
    Heavy lifting maximizes force potential.
    Fast lifting converts force potential to power output.
    Training both simultaneously maintains both qualities.

  CONTEXT: originally developed for powerlifting geared (suits, bands).
            Widely adapted but original protocols are specifically powerlifting.
```

## Load Monitoring

### RPE and Session RPE

```
RATING OF PERCEIVED EXERTION (RPE):
  Borg CR10 scale (0–10):
    0 = nothing at all
    2 = easy
    3 = moderate
    4 = somewhat hard
    5 = hard
    7 = very hard
    10 = maximal

  SESSION RPE (Foster et al., 2001):
  Session RPE × Session duration = Training Load (arbitrary units)
  Example: 60-minute session at RPE 7 = 420 AU.
  Validated against more expensive measures (HR-based TRIMP, GPS load).
  Requires: asking athlete 30 minutes after session (rating recent session,
            not current state).

  EVIDENCE: Session RPE × duration correlates well with physiological
             load measures (r = 0.85+ in multiple studies).
             Advantages: simple, free, applicable to any sport.
             Disadvantages: subjective; response varies between athletes;
             does not capture within-session intensity variation.
```

### HRV as Recovery Indicator

```
HEART RATE VARIABILITY (HRV):
  Time-series analysis of the variation between heartbeats.
  High HRV: parasympathetic dominance → recovered, ready for training.
  Low HRV: sympathetic dominance → fatigued, stressed, potentially ill.

  MEASUREMENT:
  Chest strap (gold standard) or optical HR (PPG) in wristband.
  Measure in standardized conditions: 5 min lying, morning after waking.
  Consistent timing critical (HRV varies throughout day).

  METRIC: RMSSD (root mean square of successive differences).
    Most responsive to acute changes (parasympathetic influence).
    Ln(RMSSD) often used (more normally distributed).

  HRV-GUIDED TRAINING:
  If HRV is trending down from individual's baseline:
    Reduce training intensity or volume.
    Focus on recovery (sleep, nutrition).
  If HRV is high or trending up:
    Body is recovered; hard training can proceed.

  EVIDENCE: HRV-guided periodization vs. preset periodization:
    RCTs (Kiviniemi et al., 2007; Vesterinen et al., 2016) show:
    HRV-guided training produces similar or better VO2max improvements
    with less total training time in recreational endurance athletes.
    Effect in team sports and elite athletes: less clear.

  LIMITATIONS:
    Highly variable day-to-day (stress, alcohol, illness, poor sleep all lower HRV).
    Needs 4–8 weeks of baseline before meaningful interpretation.
    Does not replace subjective athlete monitoring.
```

### GPS Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio

```
ACUTE:CHRONIC WORKLOAD RATIO (ACWR) (Hulin, Gabbett et al., 2016):
  Acute load: current week's training load.
  Chronic load: rolling 4-week average of weekly load.
  ACWR = Acute / Chronic

  INTERPRETATION:
  ACWR 0.8–1.3: "sweet spot" — appropriate load progression.
  ACWR >1.5: "danger zone" — injury risk approximately doubled.
  ACWR <0.8: undertraining — fitness declines.

  LOGIC:
  Chronic load = athlete's fitness/preparation level.
  Acute load = current week's fatigue.
  ACWR = how much this week's stress exceeds what the athlete is prepared for.
  Spike (ACWR >1.5) = body not prepared for this load → injury.

  THE "1.5 SPIKE RULE":
  Widely adopted in professional sports (NRL rugby, AFL, soccer).
  Training management: do not allow any player's ACWR to exceed 1.5.
  Also: avoid allowing ACWR to stay <0.8 for >3 weeks (detraining + injury risk when load returns).

  CRITICISMS (important):
  Subsequent meta-analyses (Windt & Gabbett, 2017 onwards):
    ACWR explains ~5% of injury risk variability.
    Many confounders (age, history, sleep, nutrition) not captured.
    The "1.5 threshold" may not be universally valid.
    Model does not account for type of load (different injury risks).
  CURRENT STATUS: a useful but imperfect heuristic.
                   Part of a monitoring toolbox, not a deterministic rule.
```

## Taper

```
TAPER (pre-competition performance optimization):
  Reduce training volume while maintaining or slightly reducing intensity.
  Purpose: allow accumulated fatigue to dissipate; residual fitness is high.
           The taper reveals the "hidden" fitness that was masked by fatigue.

  OPTIMAL TAPER PARAMETERS:
  Duration: 2–3 weeks for endurance; 1 week for short-preparation sports.
  Volume reduction: 40–60% volume decrease.
  Intensity: maintain high-intensity sessions (reduces less or not at all).
  Frequency: can be reduced slightly (2–3 sessions/week vs. usual 4–6).

  PERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENT FROM TAPER:
  Endurance athletes: 2–3% improvement on average (meta-analysis, Bosquet et al. 2007).
  Range: 0–8% depending on athlete and taper structure.
  Mechanisms: glycogen supercompensation, neural recovery, reduced chronic fatigue.

  EXPONENTIAL TAPER vs. LINEAR:
  Exponential: volume decreases more rapidly in first week, slowly thereafter.
               Better than linear in multiple controlled studies.
  Linear: even reduction; less evidence of superiority.
  Step: abrupt reductions at set time points; less sophisticated.

SUPER-COMPENSATION TIMING:
  Athlete's peak performance: depends on when supercompensation is realized.
  Competition on day 14 of taper vs. day 7: different for different athletes.
  Individual testing: time trial performance tracking during taper reveals
  individual timing. Not all athletes peak at same taper duration.
```

## Overtraining vs. Overreaching

```
SPECTRUM:
  Normal training → Functional Overreaching → Non-Functional Overreaching → Overtraining Syndrome

FUNCTIONAL OVERREACHING (FOR):
  Brief period (days–weeks) of overload, intentionally applied.
  Performance temporarily decreases.
  Recovery with 1–2 weeks of reduced load → supercompensation.
  This is normal and intentional in block periodization.
  A "hard week" followed by a deload is deliberately functional overreaching.

NON-FUNCTIONAL OVERREACHING (NFOR):
  Overload accumulated without adequate recovery.
  Performance decreases persist for weeks–months.
  Mood disturbances; sleep problems; resting HR elevation.
  Recovery requires weeks to months of reduced training.
  NOT intentional; results from poor programming or life stress accumulation.

OVERTRAINING SYNDROME (OTS):
  Severe, prolonged NFOR.
  Recovery: months to years.
  Hormonal abnormalities: altered cortisol/testosterone ratio;
                           reduced sympathetic sensitivity.
  Psychological: major mood disorder-like symptoms; loss of motivation.
  Diagnosis of exclusion: no other medical cause for fatigue.
  Prevalence in elite athletes: ~10–65% lifetime (survey data; varies by sport).

DISTINCTION IS CLINICAL:
  FOR vs. NFOR vs. OTS: primarily distinguished by recovery time.
  Cannot be diagnosed until the athlete has had recovery time and
  results are evaluated.
  No single biomarker reliably distinguishes them in advance.
  HRV, hormonal panels, and athlete monitoring reduce (not eliminate) the risk.
```

## Team Sport Periodization

```
TEAM SPORT CHALLENGE:
  Traditional periodization assumes: 1 competition at season end.
  Team sports reality: 30–60+ competitive games over 6–9 months.
  No long off-season preparation; preparation happens during competition season.

  +---------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+ ... +-----+-----+
  | PreSeas |  W1 |  W2 |  W3 |  W4 |  W5 | ... |W30  | PO  |
  |  (4-8w) |  G  |  G  |  G  |  G  |  G  |     |  G  |  G  |
  +---------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+ ... +-----+-----+
  G = game; 1–3 games per week in congested fixture period.

  APPROACH: Weekly microcycle structured around game(s).
  MD (Match Day) → MD-1 (recovery) → MD-2 (activation) → MD+1 (reload) → ...

  LOAD MANAGEMENT WITHIN SEASON:
  Reduce volume 24–48 hours before game (activation, not fatigue).
  Increase load 72+ hours before game (sufficient recovery window).
  Congested fixtures: very limited time for high loads → tactical and
  technical sessions replace intensity.

INDIVIDUALIZATION CHALLENGE:
  A team of 25 players: each player may be at different fitness,
  fatigue, and injury risk levels simultaneously.
  Sports science role: identify who needs more recovery, who needs more load.
  Decision tools: GPS load data, HRV, subjective wellness scores.
  The head coach still makes the final decision; science informs it.
```

Periodization hierarchies (macrocycle -> mesocycle -> microcycle) parallel software iteration hierarchies (roadmap -> sprint cycle -> daily build). Block periodization (accumulation -> transmutation -> realization) maps to diverge -> converge -> release. Both are systematic iterative improvement patterns that manage competing priorities across timescales.

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Situation | Periodization Approach | Key Parameter |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| Single-sport athlete, annual peak | Matveyev linear | Volume down, intensity up toward competition |
| Strength + size goal, gym only | Daily undulating (DUP) | Vary rep ranges 3x/week |
| Elite powerlifter | Conjugate (ME + DE) | Weekly exercise variation; accommodation prevention |
| Team sport in-season | Weekly microcycle around match | MD-1 recovery; 72h pre-game load |
| Pre-competition taper | 40–60% volume reduction, 2–3 weeks | Maintain intensity; exponential volume reduction |
| HRV trending down for 3+ days | Reactive reduction | Reduce to 60–70% normal load until HRV recovers |
| ACWR approaching 1.5 | Reduce upcoming week load | Keep ACWR within 0.8–1.3 sweet spot |

## Common Confusion Points

**Periodization is not a schedule template to copy**: every periodization program is a hypothesis about what will work for a specific athlete. Copy someone else's program and you're using a hypothesis calibrated for someone else. Use the principles; individualize the prescription.

**Taper improves performance through fatigue removal, not fitness gain**: the athlete's fitness at the start of taper is the fitness peak. Taper does not create new fitness — it reveals the fitness that was masked by accumulated fatigue. There is no "fitness improvement" during taper; the improvement in performance is from fatigue reduction.

**HRV-guided training does not replace programming**: HRV provides a daily readiness signal. It does not tell you what training to do; it tells you whether to proceed as planned or modify intensity/volume. The program still needs to be written in advance.

**"Overtraining" is often overdiagnosed**: athletes frequently underperform due to temporary fatigue, illness, inadequate nutrition, or poor sleep — none of which are OTS. True OTS requires months of recovery and is relatively rare. Most cases of "overtraining" are functional or non-functional overreaching that resolves in weeks.

**The acute:chronic ratio is a model, not a law**: the 1.5 threshold originated from specific rugby league data. Its generalizability to other sports, athletes, and load metrics is imperfect. It is a useful starting heuristic, but elevated ACWR does not guarantee injury, and injuries occur at normal ACWR values. Use it as one input among several.
