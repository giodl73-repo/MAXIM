# Sports Psychology: Flow, Motivation, and Anxiety

## The Big Picture

Sports psychology is applied cognitive and social psychology constrained by extreme performance conditions. The core problems: managing arousal so it optimizes rather than impairs performance, sustaining motivation over years of training, maintaining attention under pressure, and building the mental resilience to recover from failure. The science has both strong theoretical foundations (flow, self-efficacy, goal-setting) and practical intervention repertoires used with elite athletes.

## Flow State

### Csikszentmihalyi's Model

```
FLOW STATE (Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, 1975/1990):
  "The state in which people are so involved in an activity that
  nothing else seems to matter; the experience itself is so
  enjoyable that people will do it even at great cost, for the
  sheer sake of doing it."

  DEFINING CHARACTERISTICS (Csikszentmihalyi's 9 dimensions):
  1. Challenge-skill balance: task difficulty ≈ current ability.
  2. Action-awareness merging: no separation between doing and thinking.
  3. Clear goals: moment-to-moment purpose is unambiguous.
  4. Unambiguous feedback: immediate feedback about success.
  5. Concentration on task at hand: total absorption; no irrelevant thought.
  6. Paradox of control: sense of effortless control; no fear of failure.
  7. Loss of self-consciousness: the observing self disappears.
  8. Time transformation: time slows or accelerates differently.
  9. Autotelic experience: intrinsically rewarding; the activity is its own reward.

  THE CHANNEL DIAGRAM:
  Skill Level
       |
  HIGH |               /  Arousal
       |         Flow /
       |        /----/
       |       /
       | Boredom
  LOW  |_______________________  Challenge Level
              LOW            HIGH

  Flow occurs in the channel where challenge ≈ skill.
  Too much challenge, too little skill: anxiety.
  Too little challenge: boredom and disengagement.
  The channel moves right and up as skill develops — to maintain
  flow, challenges must increase with competence.

SPORT-SPECIFIC RESEARCH:
  Jackson's flow in sport (1992–2000): structured interview approach.
  Flow State Scale (FSS): validated questionnaire, 36 items, 9 subscales.
  Replicated dimensions in sport; consistent across swimming, golf, running.
  Precursors: optimal preparation, confidence, focus before performance.
  Disruptions: concern about performance/crowd, physical problems, injury.

FLOW IS NOT PEAK PERFORMANCE:
  Some peak performances occur outside flow (grinding through fatigue).
  Some flow states do not produce peak objective performance.
  They correlate but are not identical. Flow is a subjective state;
  peak performance is an objective outcome.
```

## Arousal and Performance

### Inverted-U Hypothesis

```
YERKES-DODSON LAW (Yerkes & Dodson, 1908):
  Original: maze-learning in mice; intensity of punishment vs. performance.
  Applied to human performance: arousal vs. performance inverted-U curve.

  Performance
       |          *
       |        *   *
       |       *     *
       |     *         *
       |   *             *
       |_*_________________*____> Arousal (activation)
         LOW             HIGH

  OPTIMAL AROUSAL: the peak of the inverted-U.
  Below optimal: underaroused (bored, sluggish, insufficient activation).
  Above optimal: overaroused (anxious, attentional narrowing, errors).

  TASK DEPENDENCY:
  Fine motor tasks (golf putting, archery): low optimal arousal.
  Gross motor tasks (sprinting, weightlifting): higher optimal arousal.
  Complex cognitive tasks: lower optimal arousal than simple tasks.
  This is the original Yerkes-Dodson finding: task difficulty modulates
  the optimal arousal level.

CRITICISM OF INVERTED-U:
  Assumes single optimal point; smooth curve.
  Does not account for cognitive vs. somatic anxiety distinction.
  Does not explain catastrophic performance failure (cliff drop, not smooth decline).
```

### IZOF — Individual Zones of Optimal Functioning

```
INDIVIDUAL ZONES OF OPTIMAL FUNCTIONING (IZOF)
(Yuri Hanin, 1978; published in English 1980s–1990s):

  Key innovation: the optimal arousal/anxiety level is INDIVIDUAL,
  not a fixed point on a population curve.

  METHODOLOGY:
  Athlete recalls their best performances; rates anxiety/affect experienced.
  Athlete recalls worst performances; rates anxiety/affect.
  Construct individual "zones" (ranges) for facilitating and debilitating
  anxiety levels.

  ZONE CONCEPT:
  Zone of optimal functioning: anxiety level associated with best performance.
  Some athletes perform best at high anxiety (interpreting it as facilitative).
  Others perform best at lower anxiety.
  The zone is ±2.5 units around the athlete's personal optimal level.

  EXPANDED IZOF:
  Hanin expanded from anxiety to all emotional states (positive/negative ×
  optimal/dysfunctional dimensions).
  An athlete may need some anger, some tension, AND some joy simultaneously
  for their best performance. Profiles are idiosyncratic.

  PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
  Identify individual zone through performance history logs.
  Pre-competition monitoring: where is the athlete today?
  Is today's state inside or outside their zone?
  Intervention: arousal up (activation strategies) or down (calming strategies)
  based on where they are relative to their personal zone.
```

### Cognitive and Somatic Anxiety

```
MULTIDIMENSIONAL ANXIETY THEORY (Martens et al., 1990):
  Competitive anxiety has two components:

  COGNITIVE ANXIETY:
  Negative expectations and mental worries.
  Thoughts: "I won't perform well," "They're better than me."
  Linear negative relationship with performance: higher = worse.
  Does not fluctuate much during competition day.

  SOMATIC ANXIETY:
  Physiological symptoms: heart rate, sweating, muscle tension, butterflies.
  Inverted-U relationship with performance (moderate = useful).
  Rises sharply in the hours before competition; drops after competition starts.

  SELF-CONFIDENCE:
  Third component of the Competitive State Anxiety Inventory-2 (CSAI-2).
  Positive relationship with performance: higher confidence = better performance.
  Acts as a buffer against cognitive anxiety effects.

ANXIETY INTERPRETATION MODEL (Jones, 1995):
  Whether anxiety helps or hurts depends on the INTERPRETATION:
  "Facilitative" interpretation: "I'm pumped up, ready to perform well."
  "Debilitative" interpretation: "I'm anxious, I'm going to perform badly."
  The same physiological arousal → different performance outcomes
  based on attribution.
  Intervention: reappraisal (reframe anxiety as excitement — "I'm excited").
  Evidence: Alison Wood Brooks (2014) — "Get Excited" framing improves
  performance on singing, math, and speech tasks vs. "Calm down" framing.
```

## Self-Efficacy

```
SELF-EFFICACY (Albert Bandura, 1977/1986):
  "Beliefs in one's capabilities to organize and execute the
  courses of action required to manage prospective situations."

  NOT the same as self-esteem (global sense of worth).
  NOT the same as self-confidence (general across domains).
  SELF-EFFICACY IS TASK-SPECIFIC: "I can make this free throw
  when the game is tied in the fourth quarter" is a self-efficacy belief.

  SOURCES OF SELF-EFFICACY (Bandura's 4 sources):
  1. MASTERY EXPERIENCES: past success is the most powerful source.
     Actual accomplishment — having done the task well before.
     Why skills progression in training builds performance confidence.

  2. VICARIOUS EXPERIENCE (modeling):
     Watching someone similar perform successfully.
     "If they can do it, I can do it."
     Social comparison: most effective when the model is perceived as
     similar (not a world-record holder watching when you're a beginner).

  3. VERBAL PERSUASION:
     Coach/peer feedback: "You can do this."
     Credibility of source matters greatly.
     Less powerful than mastery experiences; can be undermined by failure.

  4. PHYSIOLOGICAL / AFFECTIVE STATES:
     Interpreting arousal as confidence ("I feel ready") vs. fear ("I'm nervous").
     Physical fatigue interpreted as weakness reduces efficacy.
     Influence of mood on efficacy appraisal.

  COLLECTIVE EFFICACY:
  Team-level analog: "We can win this game."
  Predicts team performance across multiple sports (Feltz et al., 2008).
  Built through: shared mastery, team goal achievement, communication,
  leadership modeling.

SELF-EFFICACY → PERFORMANCE PATHWAY:
  High efficacy → choice of challenging activities.
  High efficacy → effort and persistence under difficulty.
  High efficacy → strategic thinking under adversity.
  High efficacy → lower emotional reactivity to failure.
  Each performance then feeds back to update efficacy beliefs.
```

## Motivation Frameworks

### Self-Determination Theory

```
SELF-DETERMINATION THEORY (Deci & Ryan, 1985–2000):
  A macro-theory of human motivation; best-studied in sport and education.

  THREE BASIC PSYCHOLOGICAL NEEDS:
  AUTONOMY: sense of volition; behavior is self-chosen and self-endorsed.
  COMPETENCE: sense of effectiveness; ability to meet challenges.
  RELATEDNESS: sense of belonging; meaningful connections with others.

  MOTIVATION CONTINUUM:
  AMOTIVATION → EXTERNAL → INTROJECTED → IDENTIFIED → INTEGRATED → INTRINSIC

  External: "I train because my coach will drop me if I don't."
  Introjected: "I train because I'd feel guilty if I didn't."
  Identified: "I train because I understand it's important for my goals."
  Integrated: "Training is part of who I am."
  Intrinsic: "I train because I love it."

  OUTCOMES:
  Moving right along the continuum:
    Greater persistence, well-being, performance, satisfaction.
  External regulation: compliant but fragile; stops when reward stops.
  Intrinsic + Integrated: most durable; survives adversity.

  COACHING IMPLICATION:
  Autonomy-supportive coaching: rationale for rules, choice where possible,
  athlete input into training decisions.
  Controlling coaching: rigid prescription, surveillance, conditional regard.
  SDT meta-analysis (Ntoumanis et al., 2020): autonomy support →
  better motivation quality, less burnout, better performance.
```

### Goal Orientation Theory

```
ACHIEVEMENT GOAL THEORY (Nicholls, 1984; Dweck, 1986; Ames, 1992):
  How athletes define "success" shapes motivation and behavior.

  TASK ORIENTATION (mastery goal orientation):
  Success = personal improvement, skill mastery, effort applied.
  "I was better today than yesterday."
  Reference: self-comparison (intrapersonal).

  EGO ORIENTATION:
  Success = outperforming others, demonstrating superiority.
  "I was better than everyone else."
  Reference: normative comparison (interpersonal).

  MOTIVATIONAL CLIMATE:
  Mastery climate (created by coach): emphasizes improvement, learning,
  effort. Mistakes are learning opportunities.
  Performance/ego climate: emphasizes winning, ranking, social comparison.
  Mistakes are failures.

  OUTCOMES:
  Mastery orientation + mastery climate: intrinsic motivation, persistence,
  enjoyment, willingness to take on challenges.
  Ego orientation in performance climate: fragile — good when winning;
  drops dramatically when losing. More likely to cheat.
  EXCEPTION: high ego + high task orientation (both) is often functional.
  Problems: high ego + low task orientation alone.

  IMPLICATIONS FOR YOUTH SPORT:
  Early specialization + performance climate → burnout and dropout.
  Mastery climate especially critical during development years.
  The best youth coaches create environments where effort is celebrated
  and errors are expected components of learning.
```

## Attentional Focus and Quiet Eye

### Attentional Focus Effects

```
ATTENTION FOCUS (Gabriele Wulf, 1998–):
  INTERNAL FOCUS: attention directed to body movements.
    "Keep your elbow extended through the follow-through."
    "Think about your foot placement."
  EXTERNAL FOCUS: attention directed to the effect of movement on the environment.
    "Keep the clubface aimed at the target."
    "Push the floor away."

  CONSTRAINED ACTION HYPOTHESIS (Wulf):
  Internal focus → conscious control of movement → disrupts automaticity
    (the movement is too constrained by conscious monitoring).
  External focus → subcortical/automatic processing → more efficient,
    more accurate, better retention.

  EVIDENCE:
  Wulf et al. (1998): balance board task; external cue > internal cue.
  Dozens of replications across:
    Golf, basketball shooting, dart throwing, soccer, balance tasks.
  External > internal for: accuracy, retention, transfer, efficiency.
  Meta-analysis (Wulf & Lewthwaite, 2016): 64 studies; external focus advantage.
  Especially strong for: experts performing under pressure; complex skills.

  PRACTICAL INSTRUCTION:
  Avoid: "Don't bend your arm" (internal) or "Keep your weight on your back foot."
  Prefer: "Aim for the target" or "Push the ground away as you accelerate."
  External focus language is unnatural for some coaches — requires deliberate shift.
```

### Quiet Eye

```
QUIET EYE (Joan Vickers, 1996):
  DEFINITION: the final fixation or tracking gaze of the eye directed at
  the critical location of the target before and during execution of
  the final movement of a motor action.

  MEASUREMENT: eye-tracking equipment (now available in glasses form).
  The "quiet" period: when the gaze has settled and before/during
  the key moment of action.

  FINDINGS:
  Basketball free throws: experts have longer quiet eye on rim before release.
  Golf putting: experts fixate on ball longer before stroke.
  Ice hockey goaltending: longer quiet eye on the puck.
  Surgery (laparoscopy): surgeons' gaze behavior differentiates expert from novice.
  Under pressure: expert QE maintained; novice QE shortened under pressure.

  QUIET EYE TRAINING:
  Athletes can be trained to maintain longer QE with performance feedback.
  Vickers et al. (2007, 2009): QE training improved basketball free throw %
  and soccer penalty shots vs. technical instruction alone.
  Effect under pressure: QE-trained athletes more robust under pressure.

  MECHANISM HYPOTHESIS:
  Long QE → more time for preprogramming the motor action.
  QE onset correlates with motor program finalization in the brain.
  Disrupted QE (short, multiple fixations) → incomplete motor planning →
  higher execution error.
```

## Mental Skills Toolkit

### Self-Talk

```
SELF-TALK:
  Internal statements athletes say to themselves; verbalized or subvocal.
  Types:
    Instructional: "Elbow up" — technical reminder.
    Motivational: "Come on, push!" — arousal/confidence boost.
    Negative: "I can't do this" — maladaptive.

  EVIDENCE (Hatzigeorgiadis et al., 2011 meta-analysis):
  Self-talk interventions improve performance across sports.
  Effect size: moderate (d = 0.48 average).
  Motivational self-talk: better for endurance/strength tasks.
  Instructional self-talk: better for accuracy/fine motor tasks.

  MECHANISMS:
  Attentional: directs focus to task-relevant cues.
  Cognitive-behavioral: changes interpretation of events.
  Motivational: affects perceived effort and persistence.

  CUE WORDS:
  Short, specific, personally meaningful.
  Example: "Smooth" for a swimmer's stroke; "Explode" for a sprinter's start.
  Must be trained in practice to be available under pressure.
  Paired with physical practice (not just taught as a concept).
```

### Imagery

```
MENTAL IMAGERY (mental rehearsal):
  Mentally simulating sport performance — kinesthetic + visual.
  Not day-dreaming: structured, deliberate simulation of specific movements.

  BIOINFORMATIONAL THEORY (Lang, 1977):
  Images have propositional structure: stimulus propositions (what you see)
  + response propositions (physiological responses during imagery).
  Effective imagery: include response propositions (feel the muscle activation,
  the breathing, the stress response).
  Imagery with response content activates motor cortex (fMRI evidence).
  Physiological similarity: HR slightly elevates during intense imagery.

  TYPES:
  Internal: first-person perspective (looking out from eyes).
  External: third-person perspective (watching yourself).
  Evidence for superiority: mixed; depends on skill type.
  Internal better for: skills requiring feel/proprioception.
  External better for: skills where full body form matters.

  EFFECTIVENESS EVIDENCE:
  Driskell et al. (1994) meta-analysis: imagery improves performance.
  Effect strongest when: combined with physical practice (not instead of).
  Mental practice alone: ~2/3 of physical practice benefit.
  Mental + physical: exceeds physical alone in controlled studies.

  APPLIED PROTOCOL (PETTLEP model: Holmes & Collins, 2001):
  Physical: adopt relevant physical position/posture.
  Environment: imagery in or simulated to actual performance environment.
  Task: match content to the specific task.
  Timing: real-time speed (not slow motion for most skills).
  Learning: match imagery content to current learning stage.
  Emotion: include emotional content of performance.
  Perspective: choose based on the skill.
```

### Pre-Performance Routines

```
PRE-PERFORMANCE ROUTINES (PPR):
  Standardized sequences of thoughts and behaviors before performance.
  Common in: golf, tennis serving, basketball free throws, gymnastics.

  FUNCTIONS:
  Attentional: directs focus to relevant cues; fills time with task-focused
  activity (prevents distraction wandering).
  Arousal: brings athlete to their IZOF; the routine signals "start."
  Consistency: same emotional/physiological state entering execution.

  EVIDENCE:
  Cotterill (2010) review: PPR associated with improved performance,
  especially for self-paced closed skills.
  Tennis serve routine: players with longer consistent routines → better
  second serve performance under pressure.
  Golf pre-shot routine: correlation between routine consistency and
  performance consistency.

  CHOKING UNDER PRESSURE:
  Pressure → increased self-consciousness → internal focus → disrupts
  automaticity (see attentional focus, above).
  PPR mechanism: routine re-establishes external focus and primes
  the automatic motor program execution.
  Reinvesting in explicit processing of automated skills = choking.
  Explicit monitoring theory (Masters, 1992): choking = reverting to
  step-by-step execution of skills that should be automatic.
```

## Burnout and Overtraining Psychology

```
ATHLETE BURNOUT (Raedeke, 1997; Maslach model applied to sport):
  Three dimensions:
  1. Emotional and physical exhaustion: depleted by sport demands.
  2. Depersonalization / sport devaluation: cynical about sport participation.
  3. Reduced sense of accomplishment: no longer feels competent or improving.

  DISTINGUISHED FROM OVERTRAINING:
  Overtraining: physiological maladaptation to training load.
  Burnout: psychological disengagement from sport.
  They co-occur but are distinct:
  Overtrained athlete who loves the sport = not burned out (yet).
  Burned out athlete with normal training load = possible.

  RISK FACTORS:
  Perfectionism + self-critical attributions.
  External pressure (parental, financial, scholarship pressure).
  Low autonomy in training decisions (controlling coach).
  High ego orientation (social comparison threatens self-worth).
  Early specialization and year-round single-sport training.
  Unidimensional identity (athlete identity = entire self-concept).

  IDENTITY FORECLOSURE:
  Athlete who has only an "athlete" identity (no other roles).
  When injury or poor performance threatens this → crisis.
  Healthy development: athletic identity is one identity among several.
  This is a particular challenge for elite youth athletes.

RESILIENCE FRAMEWORK:
  Resilience: positive adaptation following adversity.
  Not a trait — a dynamic process that involves:
  Protective factors: social support, coping skills, self-efficacy.
  Recovery from setbacks correlates with mastery motivation climate
  (errors are normal) and psychological skills training.
```

## Team Cohesion

```
TEAM COHESION (Carron et al., 1985):
  DEFINITION: "a dynamic process that is reflected in the tendency for
  a group to stick together and remain united in the pursuit of its goals."

  CONCEPTUAL MODEL:
  GROUP ENVIRONMENT QUESTIONNAIRE (GEQ): 4-factor model.

  TASK COHESION: degree to which members unite for group tasks.
    Attraction to Group Task (ATG-T): individual attraction to group work.
    Group Integration Task (GI-T): group perception of unity.

  SOCIAL COHESION: degree to which members like each other.
    Attraction to Group Social (ATG-S): individual attraction to group social.
    Group Integration Social (GI-S): group perception of social unity.

  PERFORMANCE RELATIONSHIP:
  Meta-analysis (Carron et al., 2002): cohesion → performance relationship.
  Effect size: d = 0.65 (moderate-strong).
  Causality: bidirectional — cohesion → performance, and winning → cohesion.
  Task cohesion predicts performance more strongly than social cohesion.
  Team sports: both matter; individual sport teams: task only matters.

  BUILDING COHESION:
  Clear roles: every member understands their contribution.
  Team goal setting: shared goals vs. only individual goals.
  Norm development: team sets own standards.
  Shared adversity: teams that suffer together bond (training camps, preseason).
  Communication: open + regular.
  Collective efficacy is both cause and consequence of cohesion.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Problem | Framework | Intervention | Evidence Level |
|---------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Pre-competition anxiety too high | IZOF; reappraisal | Progressive relaxation, centering breath; reframe as "excitement" | A (multiple RCTs) |
| Skill breaking down under pressure | Explicit monitoring theory | External attentional focus; PPR; dual-task practice | A |
| Gaze instability under pressure (aiming tasks) | Quiet Eye | Quiet eye training with feedback | A |
| Poor motivation quality (external only) | SDT | Autonomy-supportive coaching; internalization interventions | A |
| Youth athlete burnout risk | Burnout model + SDT | Diversification, autonomy, mastery climate, identity breadth | B |
| Negative self-talk spiral | Self-talk theory | Cue word substitution; thought stopping | A |
| Pre-performance inconsistency | Pre-performance routine | Routine design + deliberate practice under simulated pressure | B-A |
| Team not gelling | Cohesion model | Role clarity; team goal-setting; team building activities | B |
| Post-injury confidence loss | Self-efficacy theory | Mastery experiences; modeling; progressive loading | B |

## Common Confusion Points

**Flow is not automatically triggered by expertise**: a world-class athlete can perform without being in flow; a novice can occasionally experience flow (very easy challenges can produce it via the channel model). Flow requires challenge-skill balance, which means the challenge must match — not simply that skill is high.

**IZOF and Yerkes-Dodson are not the same theory**: Yerkes-Dodson posits a universal inverted-U with one optimal point. IZOF posits that the optimal anxiety level is individual and variable — some athletes perform best at high arousal levels, others at low. IZOF is empirically derived from the individual's performance history rather than assumed from population data.

**Self-efficacy is not self-esteem**: self-esteem is a global evaluation of one's worth. Self-efficacy is task-specific confidence in capability. An athlete can have low self-esteem globally but high self-efficacy on a specific task (or vice versa). Interventions targeting self-esteem do not necessarily improve self-efficacy, and vice versa.

**External attentional focus is counterintuitive for coaches**: many coaches have been trained to give internal focus cues ("keep your elbow up," "push from your heel"). The evidence clearly favors external cues ("push the ground away," "aim for the target"). This requires deliberate re-tooling of coaching language — the internal cue is natural but suboptimal.

**Mental imagery is not visualization**: "visualization" implies only visual content. Effective imagery includes kinesthetic, auditory, and emotional content. The bioinformational theory is clear that response propositions (physiological responses during imagery) are required for imagery to activate relevant neural pathways. "See and feel" the performance, not just observe it.

**Burnout is not just overtraining**: overtraining is a physiological state (high training load, insufficient recovery). Burnout is psychological exhaustion and devaluation of sport. They often co-occur (high-load + no recovery + no autonomy → both). But an athlete can overtrain without being burned out if their motivation remains high. And athletes burn out at moderate training loads if psychological factors (perfectionism, external control, identity foreclosure) are present.
