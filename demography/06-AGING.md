## Dependency Ratios

```
OLD-AGE DEPENDENCY RATIO (OADR):
  OADR = (Population 65+) / (Population 15-64) × 100
  Interpretation: elderly per 100 working-age adults

  OADR VALUES (2023, approximate):
  Japan:        51  (highest globally)
  Italy:        38
  Germany:      36
  UK:           30
  US:           27
  China:        21
  Brazil:       14
  India:        11
  Nigeria:       6

  Trajectory:
  US 1960: 17 → 2000: 21 → 2023: 27 → 2050 (projected): 39

YOUTH DEPENDENCY RATIO (YDR):
  YDR = (Population 0-14) / (Population 15-64) × 100

  High-fertility countries: YDR ≈ 80-90 (Sub-Saharan Africa)
  Low-fertility countries: YDR ≈ 20-25

TOTAL DEPENDENCY RATIO (TDR):
  TDR = OADR + YDR = (Population 0-14 + 65+) / (Population 15-64) × 100

  Countries early in transition: TDR dominated by YDR
  Countries post-transition: TDR increasingly dominated by OADR

  DEMOGRAPHIC WINDOW: period when both OADR and YDR are low
  → minimum TDR → most favorable for savings and investment
  → This is the demographic dividend opportunity (see 09-DEMOGRAPHIC-DIVIDEND)

SUPPORT RATIO:
  Inverse of TDR: working-age per dependent.
  More economically interpretable than dependency ratio.
  Support ratio = (Working-age population) / (Total dependents)
```

## Limitations of Dependency Ratios

The conventional OADR uses crude chronological age cutoffs. These are increasingly misleading:

```
PROBLEMS WITH CONVENTIONAL OADR:

1. AGE 65 IS ARBITRARY:
   Bismarck set pension age at 70 (later 65) in 1889 when German LE was ~40.
   Today, a 65-year-old in a rich country has ~19 more years of life expectancy
   and often full cognitive and physical capacity.

2. LABOR FORCE PARTICIPATION VARIES:
   Many people "retire" before 65 (early retirement incentives).
   Many work past 65 (self-employed professionals, poor health insurance).
   Actual economic contributions matter more than age.

3. HEALTH STATUS CHANGING:
   Today's 75-year-old is healthier than 75-year-old was in 1990.
   Rowe & Kahn "successful aging": active, engaged, cognitively intact.

ALTERNATIVE MEASURES:
  Prospective age: "prospective age 65" = age at which remaining life expectancy
    equals 15 years. This ages with mortality improvements.
    If LE improves by 5 years, prospective age 65 shifts to chronological 70.

  Economic support ratio: actual hours worked / actual consumption by age
    (National Transfer Accounts methodology — Mason & Lee)
    Captures both consumption behavior and labor force behavior.
    More accurate than demographic ratios.

  EXAMPLE: US economic support ratio falling slower than OADR suggests
  because older workers working longer and being more productive.
```

## Social Security Sustainability

```
PAY-AS-YOU-GO (PAYG) PENSION SYSTEM:
  Current workers pay taxes → fund current retirees' benefits.
  No actual investment fund; an inter-generational transfer.
  Sustainability equation:
  Total benefits = Total contributions
  b × R = τ × W × E
  where:
    b = average benefit per retiree
    R = number of retirees
    τ = payroll tax rate
    W = average wage per worker
    E = number of employed workers

  Rearranged:
  τ = (b/W) × (R/E)
    = (benefit replacement rate) × (dependency ratio in workers)

  To maintain solvency with rising R/E:
  → Raise τ (higher taxes)
  → Lower b/W (lower replacement rate → benefit cuts)
  → Raise retirement age (reduce R, increase E)
  → Allow immigration (increase E)
  → Raise employment rate (increase E)
  → Allow higher wages (increase W → more taxes same rate)

SOCIAL SECURITY IN THE US:
  Old-Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance (OASDI)
  Funded by 12.4% payroll tax (split employer/employee)
  Trust Fund: surplus accumulated when OADR was low
  Depletion projected: ~2033 (OASDI trustees 2023 report)
  At depletion: incoming taxes fund ~77% of benefits (not zero)
  Fix: 25% benefit cut OR 25% payroll tax increase (or combination)

FUNDED (CAPITALIZED) SYSTEMS:
  Workers save into individual accounts.
  Benefits based on accumulated assets and returns.
  Examples: Chile (privatized 1981), Singapore (CPF), Netherlands (occupational)
  Pros: less exposed to demographic change; builds national savings
  Cons: market risk, transition cost (funding old pay-as-you-go while funding new),
    intergenerational equity issues, management fees
  Hybrid systems (NDC — Notional Defined Contribution): most reforms moving here
```

## Healthy Aging and Active Aging

```
HEALTHY AGING FRAMEWORK (WHO 2015 World Report on Ageing):
  Core concept: functional ability (capacity to do what one has reason to value)
  = intrinsic capacity × environment
  Intrinsic capacity: physical, cognitive, psychological dimensions
  Environment: built, social, policy environment that can compensate decline

  COMPRESSION OF MORBIDITY (Fries 1980 — see also 03-MORTALITY):
  Goal: compress the period of disability/morbidity near end of life
  Healthy behaviors (exercise, diet, no smoking, moderate alcohol, social engagement)
  → delay onset of chronic disease → shorter disability period per life

  EVIDENCE ON AGING INTERVENTIONS:
  Physical activity: most evidence-based intervention for healthy aging
    → prevents CVD, diabetes, osteoporosis, falls, cognitive decline, depression
    → even modest activity (150 min/wk moderate) significantly beneficial
  Cognitive engagement: higher cognitive reserve → delayed dementia onset
    Education, lifelong learning, social engagement all protective
  Falls prevention: multifactorial interventions reduce falls by 20-30%
    (exercise programs, medication review, home hazard assessment, vitamin D)

DEMENTIA BURDEN:
  55M people globally with dementia (2020); 10M new cases/year
  Alzheimer's: ~60-70% of dementia
  Cost: ~$1.3T/year globally (2019; higher than cancer)
  No disease-modifying treatment available (as of 2024, lecanemab approved
    for early Alzheimer's — modest effect, very high cost)
  Primary prevention: address modifiable risk factors (hearing loss, depression,
    social isolation, physical inactivity, diabetes, hypertension)
  Lancet Commission (2020): 40% of dementia potentially preventable
    through modifiable risk factors
```

## Fiscal Implications of Aging

```
AGING AND GOVERNMENT SPENDING:
  Age-related spending as % GDP (OECD average):
    Pensions:    ~8% GDP
    Health care: ~7% GDP
    Long-term care: ~1.5% GDP
  Combined: ~16.5% GDP; projected to rise 3-4% GDP by 2060

  JAPAN CASE STUDY:
    National debt: ~260% of GDP (highest among major economies)
    Social security spending: ~23% of government expenditure
    Rising long-term care costs as super-aging society (>30% aged 65+)
    Strategies: extend working age, increase female labor force participation,
    immigration (limited historically), productivity growth

  GENERATIONAL ACCOUNTING:
    Method: compute lifetime taxes - benefits for each birth cohort
    Compare to assess generational equity
    Finding in aging societies: younger cohorts face larger net lifetime fiscal burden
    (pay into system that funds large elderly cohort; won't receive same benefits)

LONG-TERM CARE:
  Services for people unable to perform basic activities of daily living (ADLs)
  ADLs: bathing, dressing, eating, transferring, continence, toileting
  IADLs: managing finances, housework, medication management

  Care options:
    Home-based (preferred by most elderly): paid caregivers, family caregivers
    Community day programs
    Assisted living facilities
    Nursing home/residential care (highest care needs)

  GENDER ASYMMETRY:
    Women constitute 65% of caregivers for older adults
    Women also constitute 70% of long-term care recipients (live longer)
    Long-term care "crisis" is fundamentally a women's labor issue
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Aging question | Key metric / action |
|---|---|
| Measure aging burden on working-age population | OADR = population 65+ / population 15-64 |
| Project OADR impact on pension sustainability | τ = (benefit replacement rate) × (R/E) |
| Identify most sustainable pension reform options | Raise retirement age + immigration + higher employment rates |
| Improve healthy life expectancy | Physical activity programs; fall prevention; CVD risk factor control |
| Quantify dementia future burden | Prevalence × cost; ~10M new cases/yr globally; doubles every 20 years |
| Compare national aging trajectories | % 65+; old-age support ratios; projected pension spending as % GDP |
| Evaluate "demographic window" for a country | Minimum total dependency ratio period = dividend opportunity |

## Common Confusion Points

**Aging is primarily caused by low fertility, not improved longevity**: Doubling survival in old age has a much smaller effect on age structure than halving fertility. Intuition: a smaller cohort of young people created by low birth rates shifts the mean upward more than longer survival. Keyfitz (1968) showed this formally: fertility decline is by far the dominant driver of population aging.

**OADR and actual fiscal burden are weakly correlated**: Actual pension/healthcare costs depend on retirement age (not age 65), health status, coverage rates, benefit levels, and productivity growth. Two countries with the same OADR can have very different fiscal pressures. Japan's per-capita healthcare spending is ~$4,700 (much lower than US $12,000) despite far older age structure.

**Conventional OADR is increasingly misleading**: Age 65 was meaningful in 1935; less so today. A prospective or economic support ratio corrected for actual labor force behavior and health status gives a more accurate picture of actual support burden.

**Immigration can slow aging but not reverse it**: Adding immigrants temporarily reduces OADR if migrants are working-age. But immigrants also age and their children may be smaller families. The aging effect is merely delayed, not eliminated. Only sustained high-fertility immigration with multigenerational below-replacement fertility can reverse aging — demographically implausible at required scale for most rich countries.
