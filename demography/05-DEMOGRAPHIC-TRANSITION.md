## Stage-by-Stage Analysis

```
STAGE 1: Pre-Transitional
  CBR: 40-50/1000, CDR: 35-45/1000
  NI: ~5-10/1000 (slow natural increase)
  Age structure: very young (high fertility barely replaces high mortality)
  No living population remains in Stage 1.
  Historical Europe: pre-1800; contemporary: no analogue
  Characterization: subsistence agriculture, high infant mortality,
    episodic famine/epidemic reversals, high fertility as "insurance"

STAGE 2: Early Transition (CDR falls, CBR stays high)
  CDR falls due to: sanitation, clean water, food production,
    basic vaccines, antibiotics → child survival improves dramatically
  CBR remains high: fertility behaviors unchanged; cultural lag;
    parents don't immediately reduce births in response to lower child mortality
  NI: maximum — can reach 3-4% annual growth
  Age structure: rapidly younging (large young cohort from improved child survival)
  Contemporary examples: much of Sub-Saharan Africa in late Stage 2 / early Stage 3
  Historical Europe: 1800-1870 approximately

STAGE 3: Late Transition (CBR falls)
  CBR begins declining as:
    Urbanization: children economically costly in cities
    Women's education/employment: opportunity cost of childbearing rises
    Effective contraception: ability to act on reduced fertility preference
    Secularization: weakened religious pronatalism
    Child survival improvement: "insurance" motive for high fertility weakens
  NI narrowing but still positive (large cohort of women in reproductive ages)
  Contemporary examples: South Asia, SE Asia, Latin America; much of MENA
  Historical Europe: 1870-1950 approximately

STAGE 4: Post-Transitional
  CBR and CDR both low (8-15/1000)
  NI ≈ 0 (or slightly positive/negative)
  TFR near or below replacement
  Age structure: aging, rectangular or inverted
  Contemporary examples: Western Europe, North America, Japan, Australia, Korea
  Historical Europe: post-1950

STAGE 5 (Second Demographic Transition):
  TFR persistently below replacement (<2.0)
  CDR begins rising (aging population)
  Natural decrease: CDR > CBR
  Contemporary examples: Japan, Germany, Italy, Eastern Europe
  Population sustained only by immigration (if at all)
```

## The Second Demographic Transition (SDT)

```
SDT THEORY (van de Kaa 1987, Lesthaeghe 1995):
  Conceptually distinct from classical transition.
  Classical DT: fertility falls to replacement as survival improves.
  SDT: fertility falls BELOW replacement, driven by value change.

  TWO PHASES:
  Phase 1 (1960s-70s): Fall in marital fertility (inside marriage)
    Contraceptive revolution + changing ideals of family size
  Phase 2 (1970s-): Change in family formation itself
    Rise of non-marital cohabitation
    Delayed marriage and first birth
    Rise in non-marital fertility (paradoxically)
    Increase in divorce
    Increase in childlessness
    Diverse family forms normalized

  VALUE CHANGE MECHANISM (Maslow/Inglehart):
  Post-WWII prosperity → material security achieved → shift toward
  post-material values: self-expression, individual autonomy, gender equality
  Rising educational attainment (especially women) → rejection of
  traditional gender roles → delayed reproduction, career prioritization

  LESTHAEGHE INDEX:
  Countries classified by extent of SDT adoption based on:
  Non-marital birth rate, cohabitation rate, divorce rate, age at first birth

SDT EVIDENCE:
  Non-marital birth share:
    Iceland: 71%, France: 62%, Norway: 57%, UK: 50%
    US: 40%, Germany: 35%, Japan: 2.5% (different trajectory)
  Mean age at first birth:
    Italy: 31.5, Spain: 31.2, Germany: 29.5, US: 27.0
  Childlessness:
    Finland, Germany: ~20% of women born 1970s → childless at 40

  SDT SPREADING: Korea, Japan, China, Taiwan, urban Latin America
  all show delayed marriage, delayed birth, rising childlessness
  despite very different cultural starting points.
  Globalization of post-industrial values and urban economies.
```

  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

SUB-SAHARAN AFRICA DIVERGENCE:
  Why is SSA transitioning more slowly than comparable-income regions 1970s?
  Proposed explanations:
  1. Higher desired family size (value of children, kinship systems,
     bride price → economic value of children, gender power dynamics)
  2. Lower women's education and autonomy
  3. Higher child mortality → more insurance fertility needed
  4. Weaker contraceptive access and supply systems
  5. Religious and cultural pronatalism
  6. Large subsistence agricultural populations (children = labor)

  Evidence: Bongaarts proximate determinants → marriage and contraception
  both contribute to high fertility in SSA.
  But desired fertility itself is higher in SSA than comparable-income LMICs.
  This is not just a supply-side (contraceptive access) problem.

CHINA'S EXCEPTIONAL CASE:
  One Child Policy (1980-2015):
  Compulsory fertility reduction via government mandate.
  Rapid decline from TFR ~3 (1979) to TFR ~1.5 (1995).
  Consequences:
    TFR now ~1.0 — possibly the lowest ever for a large country
    Sex-selective abortion: sex ratio at birth 117-120 (normal ~105)
    Aging "4-2-1 family" structure (4 grandparents, 2 parents, 1 child)
    Population projected to peak ~2022, then sustained decline
  Two-child (2015) and three-child (2021) policy reversals:
    TFR did not recover → preferences firmly set at 1-2 children
    Demonstrates: once low-fertility culture is established, policy reversal difficult
```

  Negative correlation: TFR = f(income) is the most robust empirical
  regularity in demography.

  BUT: correlation weakens at high income levels.
  At very high income, TFR can stabilize or even recover slightly.
  Nordic countries + France: TFR 1.6-1.9 at very high income.
  Japan/Korea: TFR < 1.3 at comparable income.
  What explains the variation at high income?
  → Family policy (childcare, parental leave), gender equality,
    housing costs, social norms, immigration.

THEORY vs. REALITY:
  Classic DT theory predicted fertility converges to replacement at high income.
  Reality: fertility has fallen well below replacement in many countries.
  Revised view: no universal equilibrium; countries can remain below
  replacement for decades with population consequences.
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Transition question | Key concept |
|---|---|
| What stage is a country in? | CDR trajectory vs. CBR trajectory; NI; TFR level |
| Why does population grow fastest during transition? | Stage 2: CDR falls, CBR still high; maximum NI |
| Why do East Asian countries have lowest fertility? | SDT + housing costs + gender inequality + Confucian family values → delayed marriage |
| Can pronatalist policy raise TFR? | Limited evidence; Nordic approach (egalitarian gender + family support): best results; ~1.7-1.9 |
| Is Sub-Saharan Africa following Asia's path? | Slower transition but following; different timing; desired family size remains higher |
| Will Africa's population growth slow? | Yes, eventually; UN medium projection assumes fertility transition; timing is key uncertainty |

## Common Confusion Points

**The transition is descriptive, not predictive**: The classic DTM describes a historical pattern, not a law of nature. It doesn't predict WHEN transitions occur or how fast. Some countries transitioned in 50 years (East Asia: rapid); others took 150 years (Europe: gradual). The mechanism is not guaranteed.

**Death rate falling before birth rate: mechanism matters**: CDR falls first not because of a mystery lag — birth rates respond to entirely different forces than death rates. Death rates respond to public health infrastructure, sanitation, vaccines (relatively fast, policy-driven, top-down). Birth rates respond to education, women's empowerment, urbanization, culture (slower, behavioral change).

**SDT is a separate process from classical DT**: Classical DT: fertility falls from very high to replacement. SDT: fertility falls further BELOW replacement. They may have different mechanisms. Classical DT driven by survival-insurance logic and economic modernization. SDT driven by value change and individualization. Both real; analytically distinct.

**Population won't decline automatically when TFR < 2.1**: Momentum can sustain growth for 50-70 years even at below-replacement fertility. Japan: TFR < 2.1 since 1974, but population continued to grow until 2008 (34 years!). The age structure effect is powerful and slow to reverse.
