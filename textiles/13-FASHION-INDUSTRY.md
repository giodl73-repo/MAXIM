## Haute Couture: The System

```
  LEGAL DEFINITION (France):
    Chambre Syndicale de la Haute Couture (founded 1868)
    Membership criteria (Chambre Syndicale / FHCM):
    → Garments made-to-order for private clients
    → At least 15 full-time employees in Parisian atelier
    → Present 2 collections per year (SS + AW)
       of at least 50 designs each
    → Must include both day and evening wear

  CURRENT OFFICIAL MEMBERS: ~15 (varies year to year)
    Chanel, Dior, Givenchy, Valentino, Jean Paul Gaultier,
    Schiaparelli, Elie Saab, Viktor & Rolf,
    Iris van Herpen, Alexis Mabille (among others)
    "Guest members": Armani, others on invitation basis

  THE ECONOMICS OF COUTURE:
    Revenue: ~$1.5-2B globally (tiny vs global fashion ~$2.5 trillion)
    Purpose: NOT primarily to make money on clothes
    → Press and editorial coverage (3-6 shows/year)
    → Brand elevation ("if you can afford a Dior dress,
      you might buy Dior perfume")
    → Customer relationship (2,000 clients = high-net-worth
      maintenance; repeat buyers of accessories/fragrance)
    Fragrance: Chanel No. 5 alone generates ~$600M/year
    The runway show is the most expensive advertising campaign
    in the world

  THE ATELIER SYSTEM:
    Première (head seamstress): leads the workroom
    Petite main: senior seamstress; executes complex work
    Flou atelier: soft, draped garments (dresses, gowns)
    Tailleur atelier: tailored garments (suits, structured)
    Broderie (Lesage): embroidery house
    Plumassier (Lemarié): feathers and flowers
    Bottier (Massaro): shoes
    Many "petites mains" ateliers are independent specialist
    houses contracted by multiple couture houses
```

## Historical Key Figures

```
  CHARLES FREDERICK WORTH (1825-1895):
    First couturier — invented the role of designer-as-artist
    English-born; moved to Paris; opened at 7 rue de la Paix (1858)
    Prior: dressmakers made what clients requested
    Worth: presented his own designs; clients chose from them
    → Dress design as creative work, not service
    Dressed Empress Eugénie and European aristocracy
    First to show clothes on live models
    First to put his name in garments (the label)

  PAUL POIRET (1879-1944):
    "King of fashion" before WWI
    Liberated women from the corset (dramatic shift, 1906-1910)
    Introduced harem pants, hobble skirt, kimono jacket
    Theatrical fashion presentations; influenced by Orientalism
    First to license his name commercially
    First to create brand-connected fragrance (Rosine, 1911)
    Financial collapse during WWI; never recovered

  COCO CHANEL (1883-1971):
    Jersey fabric for women's daywear — revolutionary (1913)
    The "little black dress" (1926) — democratized elegance
    Chanel No. 5 (1921) — abstracted floral + aldehydes;
      first modern designer fragrance; still world's bestseller
    Removed corset, shortened skirts, adopted male motifs
    Came out of retirement 1954 to compete with Dior's New Look

  CHRISTIAN DIOR: "NEW LOOK" (1947):
    Post-war return to femininity: full skirts, tiny waist,
    soft shoulders — the opposite of wartime utility
    Controversy: women's groups protested (fabric luxury;
    liberation rollback); others embraced escape from rationing
    Defined 1950s silhouette globally
    Dior house today: €6.7B revenue (2022)

  CRISTÓBAL BALENCIAGA (1895-1972):
    "The master of couture" — even Chanel called him the only
    true couturier (she was a dressmaker; he was an architect)
    Trained in Spain; opened in Paris 1937
    Engineered silhouette away from body: baby doll, cocoon coat,
    sack dress — clothes as sculpture
    Closed house 1968 (refused to go RTW); never reopened in his lifetime
    Current Balenciaga (Demna Gvasalia): radical reinterpretation

  YVES SAINT LAURENT (1936-2008):
    "Le Smoking" (1966): tuxedo suit for women
    Mondrian dress (1965): color-block Mondrian painting as clothing
    Pop art prints, African/Moroccan cultural references
    Ready-to-wear line (Rive Gauche, 1966): brought designer to masses
    Said: "Chanel freed the body; I armed it"

  REI KAWAKUBO / COMME DES GARÇONS (founded 1969, Tokyo):
    Deconstruction, asymmetry, raw edges, "poverty aesthetic"
    Paris debut 1981: called "anti-fashion" and "Hiroshima chic"
    by French press — response to Western beauty norms
    Never interviews; work is the statement
    Dover Street Market concept stores as retail philosophy

  VIRGIL ABLOH (1980-2021):
    Streetwear + luxury convergence
    Off-White brand (2013): quotation marks on clothes,
    industrial belt buckles — citing/commenting on fashion
    Louis Vuitton Men's Artistic Director (2018) —
    first Black American artistic director of a major French house
    Trained as architect (IIT); brought structural thinking to fashion
```

## The Fashion Calendar and Trend Cycle

```
  TRADITIONAL MODEL (two seasons):
    Spring/Summer (SS): February shows; hits stores June
    Autumn/Winter (AW): September shows; hits stores December/January
    Six-month lead time from design to store

  MODERN CALENDAR EXPANSION:
    Pre-Fall (Resort): November-January; between main seasons
    Resort/Cruise: May-June; show at travel-relevant venues
    Men's: separate fashion week (January London/Florence/Paris;
           June Milan/Paris)
    Result: 4-6 collections per year from major houses

  FASHION WEEKS ("The Big Four"):
    New York → London → Milan → Paris
    Order matters: NY/London = more commercial; Milan/Paris = more artistic
    Show order: emerging brands → established houses → final shows
    Shows are NOT primarily for public: buyers, press, editors
    Retail orders placed at showrooms (appointment-based)
    The show = brand image; the showroom = actual commerce

  SOCIAL MEDIA DISRUPTION:
    Pre-Instagram: fashion absorbed into public 6 months after show
    Post-Instagram: show images globally available in minutes
    → "Outdated" before stores carry it
    → Response: "see now, buy now" (Burberry 2016, briefly)
    → TikTok microtrends: "ballet core" "quiet luxury" live days
    → Traditional 6-month lead impossible for microtrend response
    → Only Zara/Shein speed can respond
```

## Fast Fashion Economics

```
  ZARA MODEL (Inditex, founded Amancio Ortega, 1975, La Coruña, Spain):

    VERTICAL INTEGRATION:
      Own factories in Spain, Portugal, Morocco for speed
      (not cheapest but fastest — 2-week concept to store)
      Asian sourcing for basic/commodity items (longer lead)
      Own logistics, own trucking to European distribution

    THE FEEDBACK LOOP:
      Store staff report weekly what's selling/not selling
      → Design adjusts in real time
      → Small initial orders → rapid reorder if selling
      → Never over-committed to trend that dies
      Result: 80%+ full-price sell-through (industry: ~60%)
      Low markdown rate = higher margin despite lower prices

    LEAD TIME: 2-3 weeks (Europe) vs 6 months (traditional)
    COLLECTIONS: ~20+ "drops" per year vs traditional 2-4

  H&M MODEL:
    Longer lead times (6-8 months for Asia production)
    Lower prices, higher volume than Zara
    Guest designer collaborations (Lagerfeld 2004, Balmain, Versace)
    More markdowns; higher inventory risk
    Struggling with overstock and ESG pressure

  SHEIN MODEL (founded 2008, Nanjing; now HQ disputed):
    Digital-native, no physical stores
    AI-driven trend identification from social media
    ~2,000-10,000 new styles PER DAY (not per season — per day)
    Tiny test batches (100-300 units) → AI-gauged response
    → Scale only what sells → extremely low markdown rate
    Direct from Chinese factory to consumer (cross-border ecommerce)
    US: de minimis exemption (packages <$800 duty-free)
    Regulatory scrutiny: labor practices, IP theft, data
    Valuation: ~$66B at 2023 attempted IPO; postponed

  COST STRUCTURE (representative $10 garment):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Fabric:         $1.20 (~12%)                           │
  │  Cut/sew labor:  $0.60 (~6%)                            │
  │  Trims/labels:   $0.20                                  │
  │  Factory margin: $0.30                                  │
  │  Freight:        $0.40                                  │
  │  TOTAL FOB:      $2.70                                  │
  │  Brand buys at:  $2.70 (FOB Bangladesh)                 │
  │  Retail price:   $10 (3.7× markup)                      │
  │  Labor content:  6% of retail price                     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Labor Economics and Rana Plaza

```
  GARMENT WORKER WAGES (2020s):
    Bangladesh: ~$95-100/month minimum wage (frequently violated)
    Cambodia: ~$190/month
    Vietnam: ~$180-200/month
    China (coastal): ~$500-600/month (pricing out of cheapest garments)
    Ethiopia (pre-conflict): ~$26-35/month (world's lowest, 2018-2021)

  THE RANA PLAZA COLLAPSE (April 24, 2013):
    Location: Savar, Dhaka, Bangladesh
    Building: 8-story commercial building containing 5 garment factories
    + bank + shops
    Day before: cracks noticed; bank and shops evacuated
    Workers told to return next morning (factory managers threatened
    pay cuts for missing work)
    8:57 AM: building collapsed
    DEATHS: 1,134 confirmed (worst industrial disaster in garment history)
    INJURED: 2,500+

    AFTERMATH:
      Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety (2013):
        Legally binding agreement between brands and unions
        (85 major brands signed — H&M, Primark, PVH, Mango)
        Third-party inspection of factories
        Workers can refuse unsafe work without losing job
        Notable absences: Walmart and Gap signed separate,
        weaker non-binding compact (Alliance for Bangladesh Safety)
      Fashion Revolution campaign (annual "Who Made My Clothes?"):
        Day of remembrance; brands pressured to disclose supply chain
      UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights:
        Due diligence requirements for supply chain

  SUPPLY CHAIN OPACITY:
    Tier 1: Brand's direct supplier (factory making the garment)
    Tier 2: Fabric mill supplying Tier 1
    Tier 3: Yarn spinner supplying Tier 2
    Tier 4: Fiber producer/chemical supplier
    Most brands don't know their Tier 2 and below
    Subcontracting without brand knowledge is common
    Rana Plaza buildings weren't even Tier 1 for most brands —
    subcontracted without brand authorization
```

    For luxury: scarcity + heritage + craftsmanship narrative

  HERMÈS BIRKIN BAG:

## Textile Recycling: The Bottleneck

```
  MECHANICAL RECYCLING:
    Shred fabric → shorter fibers → re-spin
    Quality degrades each cycle (shorter fiber = weaker yarn)
    Cotton → recycled cotton (50%+ quality loss)
    Can blend with virgin fiber to maintain strength
    Scalable now but produces lower-grade output

  CHEMICAL RECYCLING (fiber-to-fiber — the goal):
    Dissolve polymer → recover monomer → re-polymerize
    Polyester: depolymerize PET → monomers (TPA + EG)
      → re-polymerize to virgin-equivalent PET
      Companies: Eastman Renew, Worn Again, Carbios
      Scale: commercial but not yet large
    Cotton: dissolve cellulose → Lyocell process
      → equivalent to Tencel from wood pulp
      Companies: Evrnu, Renewlone, Re:newcell (Sweden)
      Scale: pilot to early commercial

  THE BLENDED FIBER PROBLEM:
    ~70% of garments are cotton/polyester blends
    Each fiber requires different chemistry to dissolve/recover
    Sorting and separating blended fiber: unsolved at scale
    This is the key bottleneck for the recycling economy

  COLLECTION AND SORTING:
    <1% of garments collected for fiber-to-fiber recycling
    Charitable collection (Goodwill, Salvation Army):
      ~80% sold secondhand; 20% to rag/wiping cloth grade;
      fraction to true recycling
    In-store brand take-back (H&M, Zara): mostly downcycle
    Automated NIR sorting (identifies fiber content): scaling
      but still limited capacity vs volume of waste
```

## Intellectual Property: Why Fashion Has So Little

```
  US LEGAL FRAMEWORK:
    Garment designs NOT copyrightable (general rule)
    Garments = "useful articles" doctrine (Copyright Act)
    Design is functional → not protected

    Star Athletica v. Varsity Brands (Supreme Court, 2017):
      Limited exception: separable artistic elements can be
      protected (surface graphic design on a cheerleading uniform)
      but not the garment's shape/cut/construction

  WHAT IS PROTECTED:
    Fabric pattern: can be copyrighted (surface graphic design)
    Brand name/logo: trademark (Chanel, Nike swoosh)
    Louboutin red sole: trademark (specific red on sole)
    Trade dress: distinctive overall look of store, not garment
    Design patents: possible but 15-year term + narrow

  PRACTICAL RESULT:
    Fast fashion legally copies runway designs directly
    "Inspired by" Chanel jacket = legal
    Making/selling knockoffs of Chanel jacket = legal
    USING Chanel logo on it = illegal (trademark infringement)
    SELLING it WITH Chanel label = illegal (counterfeiting)

  EU APPROACH:
    Unregistered Community Design: 3 years protection automatically
    Registered Community Design: up to 25 years
    More protection than US; still limited enforcement vs fast fashion

  COUNTERFEITING (using actual trademark) = ILLEGAL everywhere:
    $0.5T global market (estimates) in counterfeit goods
    IP enforcement: brand watches for trademark use, not design copy
```

## Common Confusion Points

**Haute couture is profitable in the accessories and fragrance businesses, not the clothing**: Chanel couture gowns lose money. Chanel No. 5 makes it up many times over. The shows exist to elevate the brand perception that sells the $300 scarves and $500 belts at volume.

**The cotton gin expanded slavery, not ended it**: (See 08-HISTORICAL-TRADE for full development.) The gin is relevant here because the same dynamics drive modern fast fashion: automating one bottleneck (ginning / Shein's AI design) expands the adjacent manual labor (picking / garment sewing) dramatically.

**"Organic cotton" is not a water solution**: Organic certification addresses pesticide use, not water consumption. An organic cotton T-shirt may still require 2,700 liters of water to produce. The environmental benefit is primarily reduced chemical input, not water use.

**Greenwashing is pervasive**: "Conscious collection" (H&M), "Sustainable" lines, recyclability claims — most lack independent verification and represent small fractions of total production. Third-party certification (GOTS, bluesign, Cradle to Cradle) provides more meaningful signals than brand self-description.

**Resale market is not automatically sustainable**: Resale (Depop, ThredUp) is lower impact than new production. However, resale markets have been documented to displace low-income consumers from thrift stores by raising prices, and "haul culture" applied to secondhand creates its own overconsumption dynamic.
