# 13 — Fashion Industry

## Haute Couture to Fast Fashion — The Economics and Ecology of Dress

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Industry structure**: fashion operates in tiers with very different economics, timelines, and labor structures:
  - **Haute couture**: ~15 official Paris houses (Chanel, Dior, Givenchy, Valentino, Jean Paul Gaultier...); FHCM membership requires meeting Chambre Syndicale criteria; ~2,000 private clients worldwide; pricing $10,000-$300,000+ per piece; primarily a loss leader for brand prestige; revenue comes from ready-to-wear, accessories, fragrance, licensing; 2× annual collections (SS/AW)
  - **Luxury ready-to-wear / designer**: Hermès, Louis Vuitton, Gucci, Prada; 2-4 collections/year; $500-$10,000+ price points; quality materials and construction; sold through own boutiques and luxury department stores; LVMH, Kering, Richemont as conglomerates owning multiple houses
  - **Contemporary / bridge**: between luxury and mass market; Calvin Klein, Ralph Lauren, Theory; $100-$800; department stores and specialty retail
  - **Fast fashion**: Zara (Inditex), H&M, Shein, ASOS; 52+ micro-seasons per year (Shein releases 2,000-10,000 new items daily); $5-$80; disposable quality; exploits trend cycles
  - **Value / mass market**: Target, Walmart, Primark; commodity clothing; extreme cost pressure → lowest labor cost countries
- **The fashion calendar**: historical 2-season model (Spring/Summer + Autumn/Winter); contemporary: 4-6 or more collections/year (Pre-Fall, Resort/Cruise added); fashion weeks (New York, London, Milan, Paris — "The Big Four"); Menswear fashion weeks (separate schedule); shows as marketing events more than trade events (B2B buyers' orders placed at showrooms, not runways); social media (Instagram, TikTok) accelerating trend cycles to days
- **Fast fashion economics**: Zara model (Inditex): vertical integration; own factories in Spain/Portugal/Morocco for speed; 2-week lead time from design to store (vs 6-month industry average); small initial orders, rapid reorder if selling; result = full-price sell-through, less markdown waste; H&M model: longer lead times, lower prices, more Asian sourcing; Shein model: pure digital-native, AI-driven trend identification, extremely short runs to test demand, direct-to-consumer from Chinese factories
- **Labor economics**: a $10 garment might contain $0.50-$1.00 of labor; garment workers in Bangladesh earning $95-$100/month (2020s minimum wage, frequently violated); Rana Plaza collapse (2013, Dhaka) — 1,134 deaths when eight-story factory building collapsed despite structural cracks; exposed subcontracting opacity; led to Bangladesh Accord (legally binding fire/building safety agreement between brands and unions); Fashion Revolution campaign (#WhoMadeMyClothes); supply chain opacity — brands often don't know sub-contractors; tier 1 (direct supplier), tier 2 (fabric), tier 3 (yarn), tier 4 (fiber)
- **Sustainability crisis**: fashion industry = 10% of global carbon emissions (widely cited, contested in methodology); ~85% of textiles go to landfill or incineration; polyester sheds microplastics (700,000 microplastic fibers per wash); water usage (2,700 liters to produce one cotton T-shirt — one person's 3-year drinking water need); Aral Sea desiccation partly from Uzbek cotton irrigation diversion; textile dyeing = 20% of industrial water pollution
  - Fiber impact hierarchy contested: organic cotton (lower pesticides, same water), conventional cotton (water-intensive), recycled polyester (lower energy than virgin, still microplastics), linen/hemp (lower water + inputs), wool (methane from sheep), conventional polyester (fossil fuel derived)
  - Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) legislation emerging (EU Textiles Strategy 2030); mandatory recycled content requirements; eco-design regulations
- **Trend forecasting**: WGSN, Trendalytics, Edited — professional forecasting services; color trend forecasting (Pantone Color of the Year as marketing exercise; Pantone Fashion Color report semi-annual); trend filtering from runway → media → mass market (trickle-down theory, Simmel 1904) vs street style → luxury adoption (bubble-up theory); currently social media flattening the traditional trickle-down
- **The luxury brand machine**: brand equity built on scarcity, heritage, craftsmanship narrative; Hermès Birkin bag waitlist as artificial scarcity mechanism; handbag as investment (Birkin outperformed S&P 500 2000-2015); logo elasticity (when logos too visible → brand cheapens); licensing model (Pierre Cardin licensing disaster — 800+ licenses by 1980s); brand rehabilitation (Gucci → Tom Ford 1994 turnaround); LVMH strategy (acquire, protect, elevate heritage brands while cross-subsidizing)
- **The intellectual property problem**: fashion is notoriously IP-poor; clothing designs not copyrightable in the US (garments = "useful articles" — Star Athletica v. Varsity Brands, 2017, granted protection only for separable artistic elements); fabric patterns can be copyrighted; Louboutin red sole trademark protected; Hermès stitching patterns; result: fast fashion legally copies runway directly; "inspired by" = legal copying; EU offers somewhat more protection via design rights (3 years unregistered, 25 years registered); counterfeiting (using trademark) = illegal; copying the design = legal
- **Vintage and resale market**: ThredUp, Poshmark, Depop, The RealReal; secondhand apparel market growing 15-20% annually; projected to exceed fast fashion by 2030 (ThredUp reports); authentication services for luxury resale; resale price tracking (StockX for streetwear/sneakers); vintage as sustainability but also as gentrification of thrift stores (displacement of low-income shoppers)
- **Textile recycling**: mechanical recycling (shredding → shorter fiber → lower quality, downgraded); chemical recycling (dissolving cellulose → lyocell process, or PET depolymerization → monomer recovery); fiber-to-fiber recycling very limited commercially (cotton-to-cotton: Renewlone, Evrnu; polyester-to-polyester: Eastman Renew); mixed fiber challenge — cotton/polyester blends extremely difficult to separate; collection and sorting bottleneck (less than 1% of global clothing collected for fiber recycling); Worn Again, Renewlone as emerging startups
- **Key historical figures**: Charles Frederick Worth (1825-1895) — first couturier, introduced concept of seasonal collections and branding; Paul Poiret (1879-1944) — liberated women from corsets, introduced harem pants, created theatrical fashion shows; Coco Chanel (1883-1971) — jersey fabric for women's clothing, little black dress, Chanel No. 5 (1921), masculine silhouettes; Christian Dior's "New Look" (1947) — post-war return to femininity; Yves Saint Laurent (1936-2008) — tuxedo suit for women (Le Smoking, 1966), Mondrian dress; Rei Kawakubo (Comme des Garçons) — deconstruction, anti-fashion from 1970s; Alexander McQueen — theatrical dark romanticism; Vivienne Westwood — punk + historical; Virgil Abloh — streetwear + luxury convergence (Off-White, LV Men's 2018)
