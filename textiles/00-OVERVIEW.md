# 00 — Textiles: Overview

## Fiber → Yarn → Fabric → Finish → Garment — The Full Supply Chain

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- The textile supply chain as a linear transformation: (1) raw fiber (natural or synthetic) → (2) yarn preparation (cleaning, carding/combing, spinning) → (3) fabric construction (weaving/knitting/felting) → (4) wet processing (dyeing, printing, finishing) → (5) cutting (marker making, cutting) → (6) sewing → (7) finishing (pressing, quality control, labeling) → (8) distribution; each step historically a separate trade with specialized workers
- **Fiber classification**:
  - Natural cellulosic: cotton, linen (flax), hemp, jute, ramie — plant cell wall material; cool, breathable, moisture-absorbing, dye well
  - Natural protein: wool, silk, cashmere, mohair, alpaca — animal-derived; warm, moisture-wicking, naturally crimp/luster, complex dyeing
  - Regenerated (cellulosic source, chemical process): rayon/viscose, modal, lyocell/Tencel, cupro — plant cellulose dissolved and re-extruded
  - Synthetic (petrochemical): nylon, polyester, acrylic, spandex, polypropylene — petroleum-derived monomers polymerized and extruded
  - Mineral: glass fiber, ceramic, carbon fiber, basalt — industrial/technical applications
- **Historical arc**: textiles among the oldest human technologies (flax fiber found in Dzudzuana Cave, Georgia, ~30,000 BCE); linen dominant in ancient Mediterranean; wool central to medieval European economy; silk the luxury good driving Silk Road trade; cotton democratized by Industrial Revolution (spinning jenny/water frame/power loom 1760s-1780s); synthetic fibers from 1890s-1950s transformed what "fabric" could be; fast fashion (1990s-present) making textiles disposable
- **The craft trades — historical roles**:
  - Spinner: converted raw fiber into yarn (predominantly female labor throughout history)
  - Weaver: constructed fabric on a loom (male-dominated guild trade in most of Europe)
  - Fuller/Tucker: cleaned and thickened (felted) woolen cloth by trampling in water + urine (ammonia)
  - Dyer: applied color (separate trade with jealously guarded secrets — "dyers' guilds")
  - Draper: wholesale cloth merchant
  - Tailor: measured, cut, and fitted men's garments (male-dominated)
  - Seamstress/mantua-maker: women's garments (female-dominated, lower status)
  - Hosier: knitted stockings and other hosiery
  - Embroiderer: decorative needlework (often female, but male guild in some European cities)
  - Cordwainer: shoemaker from new leather (vs cobbler who repaired)
  - Currier: processed tanned leather into usable material
  - Saddler: harness and saddlery leatherwork
- **Modern industry structure**: fiber producers (Invista for Lycra/Kevlar, Lenzing for lyocell) → yarn spinners → fabric mills (greige goods) → converters (dye, print, finish) → apparel manufacturers → brands/retailers; enormous geographic fragmentation (design in Paris/NYC/Milan, fabric in Italy/Korea/Japan, sewing in Bangladesh/Vietnam/Cambodia/Ethiopia); vertical integration rare but growing (Zara maintains more integrated supply chain than most)
- **Fiber properties that matter**: tensile strength (how strong under load), elongation (stretchiness), abrasion resistance, moisture absorbency (vs wicking), thermal insulation, UV resistance, flammability, dyeability, hand (tactile feel), drape (how fabric hangs), dimensional stability (does it shrink/stretch with washing?)
