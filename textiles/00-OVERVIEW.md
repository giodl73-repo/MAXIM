The textile chain is unusual: **every stage is a distinct industry**, each with its own capital equipment, geography, chemistry, and workforce. A shirt on your back passed through 6-10 distinct manufacturing operations, typically in 3-5 different countries.

## Historical Arc: Four Revolutions

```
REVOLUTION 1: AGRICULTURAL (10,000 BCE – 1750 CE)
  Hand cultivation → hand processing → hand spinning → hand weaving
  Speed: 1 weaver produces ~1 yard/hour
  Geography: distributed — cloth made where fiber grows
  Key: Silk Road (200 BCE–1450 CE) — silk as currency and diplomatic weapon

REVOLUTION 2: INDUSTRIAL (1750–1900)
  Spinning jenny (Hargreaves, 1764) — 8→80 threads simultaneously
  Water frame (Arkwright, 1769) — continuous water-powered spinning
  Power loom (Cartwright, 1785) — mechanized weaving
  Cotton gin (Whitney, 1793) — seed separation ×50 faster
  Speed: 1 weaver + power loom produces ~100× pre-industrial
  Geography: concentrated — Manchester, Lowell, Lyon

  Dark side: Lancashire mills run on slave-grown American cotton.
  British textile expansion REQUIRED American slavery expansion.

REVOLUTION 3: CHEMICAL (1856–1970s)
  Perkin's mauveine (1856) — first synthetic dye, entire color chemistry follows
  Rayon (1892) — first man-made fiber, from cellulose
  Nylon (Du Pont/Carothers, 1938) — first fully synthetic fiber
  Polyester (ICI, 1941) — becomes world's most-produced fiber by 1970s
  Spandex/Lycra (Du Pont, 1958) — elastomeric fiber

REVOLUTION 4: GLOBALIZATION + FAST FASHION (1990s–present)
  CAD pattern making (Gerber, Lectra) + cut-file transmission
  Just-in-time manufacturing in low-wage countries (Bangladesh, Vietnam)
  Zara model: concept→store in 15 days (vs. 6 months industry standard)
  Shein model: algorithmic micro-batches, 6,000 new SKUs/day
  Environmental cost: ~10% of global CO₂, largest water user after agriculture
```

Historical roles: **Spinner** (converted fiber to yarn — predominantly female throughout history), **Weaver** (constructed fabric — male-dominated guild in medieval Europe), **Fuller/Tucker** (cleaned and thickened woolen cloth by trampling in water + urine, for the ammonia), **Dyer** (applied color — separate guild with jealously guarded secrets), **Draper** (wholesale cloth merchant), **Tailor** (men's garments — male-dominated, higher status), **Seamstress/Mantua-maker** (women's garments — lower status), **Cordwainer** (shoemaker using new leather), **Currier** (processed tanned leather), **Saddler** (harness work).

## Fiber Classification: The Master Taxonomy

```
NATURAL FIBERS
├── Plant (cellulosic) — primary component: cellulose polymer
│   ├── Seed hair:  Cotton (Gossypium)
│   ├── Bast/stem:  Flax/Linen, Hemp, Jute, Ramie, Kenaf
│   └── Leaf/misc:  Sisal, Coir (coconut), Pineapple fiber
│
└── Animal (proteinaceous) — primary component: protein polymer
    ├── Fleece:  Wool (sheep), Cashmere (Capra hircus), Mohair (Angora goat),
    │            Alpaca, Llama, Vicuña, Qiviut (musk ox)
    ├── Filament: Silk (Bombyx mori, Tussah)
    └── Hair:    Angora (rabbit), Camel hair

MANUFACTURED FIBERS
├── Cellulosic regenerates — from natural cellulose, chemically processed
│   ├── Rayon/Viscose (1892) — cellulose from wood pulp, xanthate process
│   ├── Modal — higher-wet-strength viscose variant
│   ├── Lyocell/Tencel — N-methylmorpholine N-oxide (NMMO) closed-loop process
│   ├── Cupro — copper ammonium process (rare)
│   └── Acetate/Triacetate — partially esterified cellulose
│
└── Synthetic (fully petrochemical)
    ├── Polyamide:  Nylon 6, Nylon 6,6
    ├── Polyester:  PET (world's most-produced fiber by volume)
    ├── Acrylic:    Polyacrylonitrile
    ├── Polypropylene (olefin): lowest density, wicks moisture
    ├── Elastane:   Spandex/Lycra — segmented polyurethane
    └── Specialty:  Aramids (Kevlar/Nomex), Carbon fiber, PBO (Zylon)
```

## Key Measurement Systems

### Fiber Fineness
```
  Diameter (μm)     Used for wool and specialty animal fibers
  Ultrafine merino: 15–17 μm
  Fine merino:      17–19 μm
  Medium wool:      22–25 μm
  Coarse wool:      >30 μm (carpet grades)
  Human hair:       ~70 μm (reference)
```

### Yarn Count Systems

```
  FIXED WEIGHT SYSTEMS (indirect — higher number = finer yarn)
  ┌──────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ System       │ Definition                                     │
  ├──────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Ne (English) │ # of 840-yd hanks per pound. Cotton/spun.      │
  │ Nm (Metric)  │ # of 1000-m lengths per kg                     │
  │ Worsted count│ # of 560-yd hanks per pound. Wool.             │
  └──────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FIXED LENGTH SYSTEMS (direct — higher number = heavier/thicker yarn)
  ┌──────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ tex          │ mass (g) per 1000 m — SI system                │
  │ denier (dtex)│ mass (g) per 9000 m — hosiery/filament         │
  └──────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Conversion: tex = 590.5 / Ne = 1000 / Nm
  Denier = tex × 9
```

### Fabric Weight
```
  g/m² (GSM) — grams per square meter
  oz/yd²     — ounces per square yard (US, especially denim)

  Lightweight:   < 150 GSM  (voile, chiffon, lawn)
  Medium:        150–300 GSM (poplin, denim, jersey)
  Heavyweight:   > 300 GSM  (canvas, heavy denim, upholstery)
```

## The Sustainability Crisis: By the Numbers

```
WATER:
  1 cotton T-shirt = 2,700 liters to produce
  1 pair of jeans  = 7,000–10,000 liters
  Aral Sea disaster: Soviet cotton irrigation — sea lost 90% of volume

CHEMICALS:
  25% of global insecticide use goes to cotton crops
  Dyeing: ~200,000 tons of dyes discharged to water annually
  Finishing: PFAS ("forever chemicals") in DWR treatments under regulatory pressure

CARBON:
  ~10% of global greenhouse gas emissions from fashion industry
  Polyester washing: ~500,000 tons of microplastics to ocean/year

LABOR:
  Rana Plaza (2013): 1,134 garment workers killed, Bangladesh
  ~75 million garment workers globally, majority women
  Garment worker share of retail price: ~2–3%
```

## Common Confusion Points

**"Natural" ≠ sustainable**: Cotton is natural but uses 25% of global insecticides and massive water. Lyocell (Tencel) is semi-synthetic but uses a closed-loop solvent process and is more sustainable than conventional cotton by most metrics.

**Rayon is not synthetic**: It's a *regenerated cellulosic* — made from wood pulp cellulose, chemically dissolved and extruded. Not petroleum-based, but requires intensive chemical processing.

**Knit vs woven feel**: Knit fabrics (T-shirts, jerseys) have stretch because loops can elongate. Woven fabrics (denim, poplin) are dimensionally stable because warp and weft interlock at right angles — stretch requires added elastane.

**Thread count marketing**: Thread count matters up to ~400 in woven sheets. Beyond that, manufacturers count multi-ply threads as multiple threads — 800-thread-count claims are mostly marketing.

**Denier and tex are inverse to Ne**: In denier/tex, higher = thicker. In Ne (English cotton count), higher = finer. This trips up everyone who crosses between systems.
