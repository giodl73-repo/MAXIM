# 09 — Leatherworking

## Tanning, Grades, Craft Trades — Skin to Saddle to Shoe

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Hides and skins**: "hide" = large animal (cattle, horse, buffalo); "skin" = smaller animal (pig, lamb, goat, calf/veal); weight, thickness, and grain pattern vary by species, age, and part of animal; belly vs back vs shoulder — different densities, consistency; cattle hide (cowhide) = most common commercial leather (byproduct of beef industry); pigskin = suede and some gloves; lambskin = soft garment leather; goatskin = Morocco leather (traditional bookbinding), durable gloves; exotic = alligator, crocodile, ostrich, stingray, python (CITES-regulated)
- **Tanning — the core transformation**: raw hide = mostly collagen fibers + water + fats + proteins; without treatment, decomposes rapidly; tanning cross-links the collagen fibers → stabilized, preserved, flexible; three main tanning types with very different chemistry, timing, and results:
  1. **Vegetable tanning** (oldest, ~7,000 BCE): plant tannins (from oak bark, chestnut, mimosa, quebracho, sumac) bond to collagen; traditional tanning pits (progressive stronger solutions over months); slow (3-6 months for traditional pit tanning, faster with drum tanning); result: firm, stiff initially, softens and develops patina with use; warm natural colors (tan, brown); the classic "full-grain vegetable-tanned" leather for belts, soles, saddles, watch straps, quality goods; develops character over time; biodegradable; Italian Leather from Tuscany (Cuoio di Toscana PDO) = vegetable-tanned standard
  2. **Chrome tanning** (1858, Knapp; industrial process perfected 1893): chromium(III) sulfate penetrates collagen rapidly (8-24 hours in tumbling drum); produces "wet blue" (blue-gray hide after tanning); soft, pliable, more consistent than veg-tan; allows thinner leathers; dominant commercial process (~80-90% of world leather); disadvantages: chromium waste (environmental problem — Cr³⁺ relatively safe, but can oxidize to toxic Cr⁶⁺ in improper waste handling); Bangladesh tannery district (Hazaribagh, Dhaka) = major environmental disaster from chrome tanning waste
  3. **Aldehyde tanning**: glutaraldehyde or oxazolidine; "washable" leather; "chamois" leather (traditional from chamois fat, now synthetic aldehyde-tanned sheepskin); light-colored; automotive use
- **Leather grades** (from best to worst):
  - **Full-grain leather**: top surface of hide used, grain intact, natural markings visible; most durable (densest fibers at top), develops best patina; slight imperfections (brands, bug bites) accepted; the "real" leather; LV and Hermès use full-grain
  - **Top-grain leather**: top surface sanded lightly to remove imperfections, embossed with uniform grain; more uniform appearance than full-grain; slightly less durable (weakened fiber structure); most common in mid-range goods; "corrected grain" = heavier sanding + embossing
  - **Split leather**: the lower layers separated from the top grain; suede (flesh side — soft, napped), split suede, or bonded from splits; much weaker fiber structure; used for suede shoes/jackets, cheaper goods
  - **Bonded leather** ("bicast," "reconstituted"): shredded leather scraps + polyurethane binder pressed into sheets; peels and flakes; used in cheap furniture and accessories; marketing often obscures true nature
- **Finishing**: aniline leather (dyed with transparent aniline dyes, no pigment surface coat — natural grain fully visible, minimal processing, most natural feel but marks easily); semi-aniline (light pigment coat + aniline dye → some protection while retaining natural look); pigmented leather (opaque pigmented coat + embossed grain — most uniform, most durable, least natural feel → most mass-market furniture)
- **Craft trades and their historical roles**:
  - **Tanner**: converted raw hides to tanned leather (a separate trade from leather workers); tanneries historically located outside city walls (smell from fermenting hides and urine); "unclean" trade in many religious traditions; place names: Tanners' Court, Barkers Row (bark = tanning agent → "barker" = tanner)
  - **Currier**: finished the tanned leather — fat-liquoring (restoring oils), stretching, slicking, dyeing, finishing; essential step between tanner and leather user; currying = processing after tanning
  - **Cordwainer**: shoemaker using new leather (from Córdoba, Spain — where finest leather originated, hence "cordovan leather"); distinct from cobbler (who repaired shoes only — lower status); Worshipful Company of Cordwainers founded in London 1410
  - **Cobbler**: shoe repairer; the village cobbler (still viable trade); cobblers' bench and tools: lasting pincers, lasting iron, hammers, awls, lasting board
  - **Saddler**: harness and saddle maker; complex three-dimensional structures; combination of leatherwork, metal work (buckles, rings), stuffed seats; saddlery was a major skilled trade until automobile replaced the horse
  - **Glover**: glove maker; fine kid (young goat) or lambskin gloves; hand-measuring and cutting; split-finger construction; historically important (English royal gloves, theatrical gloves)
  - **Bookbinder**: leather-covered books; full leather, half leather, quarter leather bindings; Moroccan goatskin traditionally for finest bindings
- **Hand tools and techniques**: round knife, skiver (thinning leather at edges/joins), swivel knife (decorative carving), beveler, backgrounder (western-style carving), stamps, pricking iron (marking stitch spacing), stitching groover, edge slicker; saddle stitch (two needles, waxed linen/polyester thread — more durable than machine lockstitch); rivet setting, eyelet setting; edge finishing (burnishing, edge paint, wing divider lines)
