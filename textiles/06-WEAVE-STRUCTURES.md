## The Three Fundamental Weaves

Everything woven is a variation of three interlacement patterns:

```
PLAIN WEAVE:             TWILL WEAVE:             SATIN WEAVE:
warp: ─ over ─ under ─   warp floats over 2-3,     warp floats over 4-7,
weft: ─ under ─ over ─   offset 1 per row          offset 2+ per row

■□■□■□                   ■■□■■□                   ■■■■□■■■
□■□■□■                   □■■□■■                   ■□■■■■□■
■□■□■□    max crossings  ■□□■□□    diagonal rib   □■■■■■■□  long floats
□■□■□■                   ■■□■■□                   ■■□■■■■■
■□■□■□    most stable    □■■□■■    strong+supple  ■■■□■■■■  lustrous

■ = warp over weft       ■ = warp over weft        ■ = warp over weft
□ = weft over warp       □ = weft over warp         □ = weft over warp

PROPERTIES:              PROPERTIES:               PROPERTIES:
Equal face/back          Diagonal visible          Floats catch light
Maximum stability        Stronger per weight       Beautiful luster
Maximum intersections    Closer sett possible      Snags easily
Stiffer hand             Softer drape              Fragile face
```

## Twill Weave Fabrics

### Twill Notation

```
  TWILL NOTATION: (warp over)/(warp under) with diagonal direction

  2/1 twill: warp over 2, under 1 → 2/3 of face = warp
  3/1 twill: warp over 3, under 1 → 3/4 of face = warp (denim)
  2/2 twill: balanced twill (equal warp/weft on face)
             → used in herringbone, gabardine

  WARP-FACED vs WEFT-FACED:
    If more warp shows on face: "warp-faced twill"
    If more weft shows on face: "weft-faced twill"

  DIAGONAL DIRECTION:
    Z-twill: / diagonal (left-to-right rise) — most common
    S-twill: \ diagonal (right-to-left rise)
    Reversing produces: herringbone (V-zigzag), broken twill (denim variation)
```

### Key Twill Fabrics

```
DENIM:
  3/1 warp-faced Z-twill
  Warp: ring-dyed indigo blue cotton (only outer fiber layers dyed)
  Weft: undyed white cotton
  As garment wears: indigo abraded from warp float tips
  → white weft shows through → distinctive "fade/wear" pattern
  Selvedge denim: woven on narrow shuttle loom (30"-36" wide)
                  Self-finished edges (no need to cut and overlock)
                  Premium — vintage character, small batches
                  Made in Japan (Kojima), Italy, US (Cone Denim)
  Modern denim: wide air-jet loom, 60"+ wide, faster, cheaper
  Stretch denim: 2-4% elastane added to weft yarns

GABARDINE:
  Tightly woven 2/2 or 4/4 twill
  Fine tightly twisted yarns → smooth, durable face
  Thomas Burberry (1879): developed gabardine for weatherproofing
  Weatherproof mechanism: tightly woven → rain can't easily penetrate
                          (physical barrier, not DWR coating)
  Uses: coats, suits, military uniforms (durable + weather-resistant)

HERRINGBONE:
  Broken twill: Z-twill in one direction, S-twill reversed at intervals
  Creates V-shaped zigzag resembling fish skeleton
  Typically 2/2 twill
  Uses: traditional British suiting/outerwear, tweeds, sportswear

SERGE:
  Even 2/2 twill; smooth face; wool or synthetic
  "Serge de Nîmes" (France) → possibly "denim"?
  Classic military and suit cloth

TWEED:
  Rough, loosely woven wool twill
  Mixed natural-colored yarns → characteristic flecked appearance
  Harris Tweed PDO (Protected Designation of Origin, EU/UK):
    Must be woven by hand in Outer Hebrides (Harris, Lewis, etc.)
    Must be from pure virgin wool dyed + spun on islands
    Harris Tweed Authority certifies with orb stamp
  Non-Harris tweeds: machine woven elsewhere, same style

FLANNEL:
  Loosely woven 2/2 twill (typically)
  After weaving: napped/raised on one or both faces
  Napping: wire-covered drums pull fiber ends to surface
  → soft, fuzzy, insulating; hides weave structure
  Wool flannel (suiting, traditional) vs cotton flannel (shirts, PJs)
```

## Satin Weave Fabrics

```
SATIN vs SATEEN:
  Satin: warp-faced (warp floats on face)
         Silk or polyester
         Smooth warp runs over face, catches light
  Sateen: weft-faced (weft floats on face)
           Cotton weft floats
           "Sateen" sheets, cotton dress fabrics

FLOAT LENGTH NOTATION:
  5-harness satin: warp over 4, under 1 (most common)
  8-harness satin: warp over 7, under 1 (most luxurious face)
  Higher harness = longer floats = more luster + more snag risk

CHARMEUSE:
  Lightweight satin (4-harness) weave
  Silk or polyester; very soft drape; no body
  One face: satin (lustrous), other face: crêpe-like (dull)
  Bias cut → clings to body; lingerie, blouses, gowns

SILK SATIN:
  Genoa was historical capital of European silk satin (15th century)
  Float lengths allow silk's triangular cross-section prism effect
  to reflect light → characteristic depth and shimmer
  Care: fragile (floats snag), dry clean, handle gently
```

## Pile Weaves

```
PILE WEAVE PRINCIPLE:
  Additional yarn set forms loops above ground weave
  Loops left intact: terry cloth (uncut pile)
  Loops cut: velvet, corduroy (cut pile)

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CROSS-SECTION (cut pile):                      │
  │  ///\\\///\\\///\\\  ← cut pile ends sticking up│
  │  ─────────────────   ← ground fabric            │
  │  warp and weft of ground fabric                 │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Velvet

```
  WARP PILE velvet:
    Additional warp threads loop over wires inserted during weaving
    Wires withdrawn → loops can be cut (velvet) or left (uncut velvet)
    OR: two velvets woven face-to-face on same loom, cut apart in loom

  PILE DIRECTION (NAP):
    All cut pile leans slightly in one direction
    Light reflects differently with vs against nap
    → whole garments must be cut with pile running one direction
    → fashion convention: pile runs upward (darker, richer appearance)
    → cutting "nap" means: pile direction issue with fabric

  VARIETIES:
    Silk velvet: most luxurious; Genoa and Lyon historically
    Cotton velvet: more common; slightly matte
    Velveteen: weft pile (vs velvet's warp pile); cotton; shallower pile
    Crushed velvet: pile pushed in random directions → vintage effect
    Devore/burnout: pile selectively dissolved → transparent/pile contrast
    Velour: KNIT structure with cut pile — not a woven velvet

CORDUROY:
  Weft pile arranged in parallel ridges (wales)
  Wale count: ridges per inch
  Fine wale: 18–21 wpc (smooth, dressy)
  Standard:  11–14 wpc (classic casual trousers/skirts)
  Wide wale:  6–8 wpc (heavy casual)
  Elephant:   1–3 wpc (dramatic; 1970s revival)

TERRY CLOTH:
  Uncut warp pile loops on both faces
  Loops absorb water (surface area, wicking)
  Single terry: loops one side (bathrobes)
  Double terry: loops both sides (towels)
  Turkish terry vs Egyptian cotton terry: marketing terms for
  fiber origin/quality, not construction type
```

## Dobby and Jacquard

### Dobby Weave

```
  MECHANISM: dobby attachment controls harness lifting sequences
             Repeating geometric patterns (not individual thread control)
             More versatile than plain shaft weave, less than Jacquard

  FABRICS:
    Oxford cloth: 2×1 basket variation (2 warp ends together)
                  Soft, slightly rough texture; casual shirting
    Piqué: tuck stitch patterns create raised texture
           Polo shirts (Lacoste, Ralph Lauren "polo" fabric)
    Waffle cloth: square geometric raised pattern → looks like waffle
```

### Jacquard Weave

```
  INVENTION: Joseph-Marie Jacquard, Lyon, 1804
    Punch card system controls each warp thread individually
    Each hole → heddle raised or lowered → any interlacement possible
    → complex patterns limited only by card design

  THE COMPUTING LINEAGE:
    Jacquard punch cards (1804)
         ↓
    Babbage's Analytical Engine (Babbage explicitly noted the connection ~1840)
         ↓
    Herman Hollerith's census tabulator (1890) — punch cards for data
         ↓
    IBM 80-column punched card standard (1928)
         ↓
    Early computer input (1950s–1970s)

  This is not metaphorical — Jacquard's binary punch system
  (hole/no-hole) directly inspired computing's development

  JACQUARD FABRICS:
    Brocade: raised supplementary weft pattern in gold/silver/silk
             Florentine brocades (15th century), Lyonnaise silk
             Pattern floats on surface; often multi-colored ground

    Damask: single-color, self-pattern from satin vs sateen contrast
            Tablecloths, napkins, formal home textiles
            Both faces show same pattern in reverse

    Tapestry: hand-woven picture (weft-faced jacquard)
              Bayeux Tapestry (actually embroidery, not tapestry)
              Gobelins factory (Paris, 1662) → formal tapestry

    Matelassé: double-cloth with wadding → quilted appearance
               Jacquard-controlled compound weave structure
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want a fabric that... | Weave structure |
|---|---|
| Is most stable, firm, two-faced | Plain weave |
| Drapes softly, is strongest for weight | Twill |
| Is most lustrous/luxurious looking | Satin |
| Has texture on surface (velvet/soft) | Cut pile |
| Absorbs water | Terry (uncut pile) |
| Has complex pattern woven in | Jacquard |
| Has small geometric texture | Dobby |
| Is classic shirting | Poplin (plain) or Oxford (basket/dobby) |
| Is classic suiting | Gabardine (twill) or Serge (twill) |
| Is most prestigious historical luxury | Silk velvet or Damask brocade |

## Common Confusion Points

**Denim fades where it does because of the weave**: In 3/1 warp-faced twill denim, the indigo-dyed warp floats on the surface. The warp is ring-dyed (only outer layers have indigo — core is white). Abrasion removes outer dye layers from the float tips, revealing white core below. The pattern of fading reflects which parts of the float contact surfaces most.

**Velour is knit; velvet is woven**: Both have cut pile. Velvet = warp pile woven structure. Velour = sheared looped pile on a knit ground. Velour is softer and has stretch (knit base); velvet is structured and dimensionally stable (woven base).

**Jacquard is a mechanism, not a fabric**: "Jacquard" describes how the loom controls warp threads — individually, via punch cards or digital actuators. A "Jacquard shirt" is a shirt with a woven-in pattern produced using Jacquard-controlled interlacement. The pattern can look like anything.

**Jacquard's punch card is a physical binary automaton**: hole/no-hole encodes raise/lower for each warp thread. Reading 1,200 wires per pass across 1,200 threads per row is essentially a read head scanning a 1,200-bit binary tape — Babbage recognized this immediately and borrowed the concept directly for the Analytical Engine's program storage. The loom is a finite-state machine whose state transitions are encoded in the card deck; the fabric output is the computation trace.

**"Thread count" is gamed above 400**: Good single-ply fine cotton sheets at 300–400 TC are superior to 800TC sheets made from 2-ply yarn counted as 2 threads each. Higher thread count ≠ better beyond the point of diminishing returns in single-ply construction.
