# 08 — Historical Trade

## Silk Road, Cotton Slavery, Wool and Medieval Europe — Textiles as Economic Engine

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Textiles as the first global commodity: before coal, steel, or petroleum, cloth was the most valuable manufactured good in international trade; drove exploration, colonization, and war; financed states and enriched (and destroyed) empires
- **The Silk Road** (~200 BCE – 1450 CE): not a single road but a network of overland and maritime routes connecting China to the Mediterranean; silk the quintessential luxury good (so valuable it was used as currency — Chinese armies paid in silk, Roman senators wore silk); Chinese state monopoly on sericulture technology for ~3,000 years — exporting fabric but not knowledge; major nodes: Chang'an (Xi'an) → Dunhuang → Kashgar → Samarkand → Merv → Ctesiphon → Antioch/Constantinople; goods traveling in both directions (silk west, glassware/silver/horses east); also transmitted Buddhism, Islam, plague, printing; "Silk Road" coined by German geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen in 1877 — a 19th century term for an ancient network
- Secret of silk broken: Justinian I (Byzantine, ~550 CE) dispatched Nestorian monks to China who smuggled silkworm eggs hidden in hollow walking staffs; Byzantine sericulture established; later Arab expansion spread silk production to Spain (Al-Andalus), Sicily; Italy (Lucca, Florence, Venice) becoming dominant silk producers by 12th-14th centuries
- **Indian cotton trade**: India had the world's most sophisticated cotton textile industry for millennia; Indian cotton prints (chintz, calico, muslin) were so superior that European consumers preferred them over domestic linen/wool; East India Company initially a trading company importing Indian textiles; British weavers lobbied Parliament → Calico Acts (1690, 1720) — banned import/wear of Indian cotton in Britain; these acts ironically accelerated the Industrial Revolution by creating demand for domestic cotton that had to be fulfilled through mechanization; by 1800s British mills were producing cheap cotton and destroying Indian industry through political/tariff means
- Dhaka muslin: "woven air" — the finest cotton textile ever produced; Dhaka (Bangladesh) produced muslin so fine it was described as "running water" and "evening dew"; Emperor Aurangzeb reportedly reprimanded a daughter for impropriety — she was wearing seven layers; warp yarn count reaching 1,200-1,800 Nm; the weaving skill was lost under British colonial suppression; recent attempts to revive (2013-present Bangladeshi government project)
- **American slave cotton**: post-cotton gin (1793), short-staple cotton profitable throughout the US South; cotton production increased 10× between 1800 and 1820; enslaved population grew from 1M to 2M in same period; Manchester and other British mill towns dependent on slave-grown American cotton (~80% of British cotton supply); British abolitionists argued for boycotts; some northern US merchants avoided; but cotton economics won; American Civil War cotton shortage devastated British textile industry — the "Lancashire Cotton Famine" (1861-1865) causing mass unemployment in mill towns; Egypt and India rapidly expanded production to fill gap
- **Wool and medieval Europe**: wool the economic foundation of medieval English prosperity; English wool exports to Flemish weavers (Ghent, Bruges, Ypres) drove Flemish urban growth and culture; "the wool sack" = Lord Chancellor's seat in House of Lords (still present, reminding Parliament of wool's importance); Florentine merchants (Bardi, Peruzzi banking families) financed English wool exports to Italy; the Hundred Years' War partly fought over Flemish wool trade (French vs English control); English wool staple: only designated ports could export wool (controlled, taxed); eventually England developed its own weaving industry → exported finished cloth instead of raw wool → even more valuable; "Worsted" = village in Norfolk where wool worsted technique developed
- **The Hanseatic League** (1241-1669): merchant confederation of northern European cities; Baltic trade including flax (for linen), hemp (for rope), furs; Lübeck, Hamburg, Bruges, London Steelyard as nodes; textiles central to Hanse commerce
- **Flemish and Italian wool guilds**: craft guilds strictly controlled production quality, prices, and entry to the trade; Arte della Lana (wool guild, Florence) one of most powerful guilds in medieval Italy; guild systems maintained quality but also limited innovation; collapse of guild system (16th-18th centuries in England) enabled industrial disruption
- **The Manchester/Lancashire cotton industry**: from ~1770 (spinning jenny, water frame) to 1900, Lancashire transformed from agricultural county to world's leading cotton manufacturing region; Manchester as "Cottonopolis"; cotton exchange; mills powered by water then steam; child labor (piecing broken threads, cleaning under moving machinery — extreme hazard); 1833 Factory Act limited child labor; Friedrich Engels' *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) — the definitive account of mill-town conditions; basis for Marxist analysis
- **Indigo revolt** (1859-1860, Bengal): British planters forced Indian peasants to grow indigo instead of food crops under coercive contracts; peasants had to grow minimum portion of land as indigo regardless of market price; Indigo Revolt = mass peasant refusal; dramatized in Dinabandhu Mitra's *Nil Darpan* (1858); pre-cursor to Indian independence movements; synthetic indigo (BASF, 1897) ultimately solved the problem by destroying the natural indigo economy
