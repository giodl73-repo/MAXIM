# 10 — Pattern Making

## Flat Pattern, Draping, Grading — From Body to Blueprint

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **What a pattern is**: a set of flat templates (paper or card) representing each cut piece of a garment; when sewn together in correct order, create a 3D garment that fits a body; the pattern encodes all shaping (darts, seams, ease, grain lines) needed to transform flat fabric into 3D form; patterns include seam allowances (typically 5/8 inch in US home sewing, 1 cm in industrial/European), grain lines (must align with warp), notches (matching marks), and instructions
- **Ease**: critical concept — the difference between body measurement and garment measurement; wearing ease = minimum needed for movement (2-4" chest on a jacket); design ease = added for style/silhouette (10"+ on an oversized garment); pattern books list body measurements + their ease built in; negative ease = compression (stretch garments)
- **The three main approaches to pattern making**:
  1. **Flat pattern drafting**: mathematical construction from body measurements; draft on paper using rulers, curves, and calculation; start from a basic block (sloper); add design ease and style lines; French curve for armholes, hip curves; seam allowances added last; highly reproducible; suited for industrial production; requires knowledge of standard proportional relationships
  2. **Draping (moulage)**: fabric draped directly on a dress form/mannequin, pinned, marked, then transferred to paper; more intuitive approach; better for complex 3D shapes (cowl necklines, bias-cut gowns); used in couture; draping reveals how fabric wants to move and fall; transferred from form to flat paper for reproducibility
  3. **Pattern adjustment from commercial patterns**: modifying existing patterns to fit a specific body; the fitting process — basting toile, adjusting, re-cutting; most common for home sewers
- **The sloper (block)**: a basic fitted pattern with minimum ease for a given size; no design details, no seam allowances; the starting point for creating new designs; slopers for bodice (front/back), sleeve, skirt (front/back), trouser; fitted precisely to a standard size or to a specific individual; all design patterns derived from the sloper by adding ease, changing silhouette lines, combining pieces
- **Dart manipulation**: darts = wedge-shaped tucks that shape flat fabric to 3D curves; dart rotation principle — any dart can be moved to any position around the pivot point without changing the fit; all the dart intake can be distributed as gathers, pleats, tucks, or seams; classic bust dart can become French dart, shoulder dart, waist dart, underarm seam; this equivalence is fundamental to pattern making
- **Pattern grading**: scaling a pattern up/down through a size range; NOT simply enlarging — proportions change non-linearly between sizes; professional grading tables give precise measurement changes per size per pattern piece at specific grade points; computerized grading (CAD) standard in industry; grading rules differ between style categories (fitted vs loose)
- **CAD pattern making**: Gerber AccuMark, Lectra, Optitex, CLO — industry-standard software; digital patterns stored and shared; automatic grading; virtual 3D draping (CLO3D) simulates how pattern will look on digital avatar; fabric simulation (bending rigidity, weight, surface friction) increasingly realistic; reduces sampling cycles from months to days; digital fitting on avatars of varying body types
- **Made-to-measure vs bespoke**: made-to-measure = standard pattern graded/adjusted to individual measurements (semi-custom); bespoke = pattern drafted specifically for the individual, multiple fittings (hand-basted toile → first fitting → corrections → second fitting → final); Savile Row bespoke suit = 50-80 hours of work; bespoke price range $3,000-$15,000+; Neapolitan tailoring tradition vs English Savile Row tradition (soft vs structured construction)
- **The toile (muslin)**: a prototype garment made in cheap fabric (calico/muslin) before cutting the fashion fabric; essential for fitting; reveals issues before expensive material is cut; adjusted, re-cut, re-fitted until correct; transferred back to paper pattern; "making a toile" is standard design practice; "muslin" = the process (US term) or the fabric; toile = French term used in English fashion industry
- **Marker making (cutting lay)**: arranging pattern pieces on fabric width to minimize waste; fabric typically 44-45" or 58-60" wide; grain requirements, pattern matching (plaids, stripes), and nap direction constrain placement; computerized marker making (Gerber, Lectra) optimizes fabric yield; typical yield: 80-85% for well-managed markers; waste = "fallout"; marker making is an economic driver — 1% fabric waste reduction = significant cost on large volume
- **Standard sizing**: US sizes not standardized across manufacturers; "vanity sizing" drift over decades (a size 8 in 2020 fits a larger body than a size 8 in 1950); ASTM D5585 (women's sizes) provides measurement specifications that manufacturers may ignore; EU numeric sizing (34, 36, 38...) nominally based on bust measurement; UK sizes use different numbers; Japanese sizes use O, S, M, L or numeric; no true international standard; solution = measurements, not size numbers
