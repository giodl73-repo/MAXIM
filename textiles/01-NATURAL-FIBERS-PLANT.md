# Natural Fibers — Plant (Cellulosic)

## The Big Picture: Cellulose as the Common Thread

```
ALL PLANT FIBERS = CELLULOSE POLYMER
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|   β-D-glucose monomer    (C₆H₁₀O₅)                               |
|        ↓                                                         |
|   β-1,4-glycosidic linkage (not α-1,4 like starch)               |
|        ↓                                                         |
|   Linear cellulose chains (degree of polymerization 1,000–15,000)|
|        ↓                                                         |
|   Hydrogen bonding between chains                                |
|        ↓ ↓                                                       |
|  Crystalline regions     Amorphous regions                       |
|  (strong, rigid,         (flexible, accessible                   |
|   hydrophobic)            to water, dyeable)                     |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

Cotton:  ~90% cellulose, DP ~1,000–3,000 (shorter chain = weaker)
Linen:   ~80% cellulose, DP ~8,000 (longer chain = stronger)
Hemp:    ~70% cellulose, DP ~6,000
Jute:    ~65% cellulose (more lignin = stiffer, coarser)
```

Why cellulose feels good against skin: the hydroxyl (-OH) groups make it hydrophilic — it absorbs moisture from sweat, then releases it to air. Cotton at 7% moisture regain; linen at ~12%. This is why linen is cooler-feeling than cotton — better thermal conductivity when moist.

## Cotton (*Gossypium* spp.)

### Species and Grades
```
  G. hirsutum (Upland):    ~90% of world production
                            Staple: 7/8"–1 1/8"
                            Most versatile, used for everyday textiles

  G. barbadense (Long staple): Egyptian, Pima, Sea Island
                            Staple: >1 3/8" (extra-long)
                            Finer, stronger, silkier hand
                            Egypt, Peru, Arizona (Pima)

  G. arboreum, G. herbaceum: Old World species, South Asia
                            Shorter staple, now minor commercial use
```

### The Boll and Fiber Structure
```
COTTON BOLL CROSS-SECTION:
  ┌─────────────────┐
  │    Boll capsule │
  │  ┌───────────┐  │
  │  │  Seeds    │  │← 3-5 seeds per boll
  │  │ ●●●●●●●●● │  │
  │  └──│────│───┘  │
  │     │    │      │← fibers = unicellular seed hairs
  │  ~~~│~~~~│~~~   │   attached to seed coat
  └─────────────────┘

SINGLE COTTON FIBER:
  ─ Cuticle (waxy outer layer — removed by scouring)
  ─ Primary wall (cellulose fibrils, randomly oriented)
  ─ Secondary wall (concentric lamellae, spiral fibrils 20-30°)
  ─ Lumen (central canal, collapses on drying → kidney bean shape)

  IMMATURE fiber: thin-walled, hollow — dyes poorly, pills easily
  MATURE fiber:   thick-walled, collapsed lumen — strong, dyes well
  DEAD fiber:     no lumen contents — dull, weak
```

### Processing: From Field to Bale
```
HARVEST
  Mechanical: spindle pickers (strips seed cotton from boll)
              stripper harvesters (strips everything — more trash)
  Hand:       developing world — cleaner cotton, picker quality variable

GINNING (separating fiber from seed)
  Saw gin (post-Whitney, dominant): rotating saw teeth pull fiber
  through slots too narrow for seed; ~200 bales/day machine
  Roller gin (older, for long-staple): gentler, avoids fiber nep

  Whitney's 1793 patent: mechanized short-staple ginning ×50 faster
  Historical impact: made short-staple Upland cotton economically viable
  across American South, directly expanding and entrenching slavery
  (see module 08)

OPENING AND CLEANING
  Bale opener → hopper blendomat → fine cleaner
  Removes: leaf trash, seed coat fragments (motes), vegetable matter

CARDING
  Fiber individualized on wire-covered rotating cylinders
  Output: "sliver" — continuous soft rope of loosely aligned fiber
  Removes: neps (fiber tangles), short fibers (< 1/2 staple length)

COMBING (optional — for finer yarns)
  Additional combing step removes more short fibers
  "Combed cotton" has longer average fiber length → smoother, stronger yarn
  ~20% of fiber is removed as "noil" (shorter fibers, used for cheaper goods)

DRAWING
  Multiple slivers fed together and attenuated → more uniform sliver
  Typically 3 passes (1st, 2nd, 3rd draw)

ROVING
  Further attenuation + slight twist — reduces sliver to manageable size
  for spinning frame

SPINNING
  Final attenuation + twist → yarn (see module 04)
```

### Cotton and Slavery: The Indivisible Link

This is not tangential history — it is the reason cotton industrialized when it did.

```
TIMELINE:
  1793  Whitney's cotton gin — short-staple ginning industrialized
  1800  US cotton exports: 18 million pounds
  1860  US cotton exports: 1,768 million pounds (×98 in 60 years)
  1860  3.84 million enslaved people in US (up from ~700,000 in 1790)

  MECHANISM:
  1. Gin makes short-staple cotton profitable → expand cotton cultivation
  2. Cotton cultivation required intensive hand labor for weeding, thinning,
     picking (gin solved ginning, not growing/picking)
  3. Slavery was the labor source → slavery expansion, not contraction,
     was the economic logic after the gin

  BRITISH CONNECTION:
  ~75% of British cotton mills' raw material came from American slave cotton
  Manchester "Cottonopolis" processed slave cotton into global trade goods
  Lancashire textile workers paradoxically supported Union in Civil War
  despite cotton embargo destroying their livelihoods (1862 Lancashire
  Cotton Famine) — moral opposition to slavery
```

### India and the Destruction of Its Textile Industry

```
  Pre-British: India had most sophisticated cotton textile industry globally
               Dhaka muslin so fine it was called "woven air"
               Indian calicoes exported to Rome, Arab world, Europe

  East India Company strategy:
  1. Import duties on Indian finished textiles into Britain
  2. Reverse duties: raw cotton from India exported cheaply to Britain
  3. Finished British cloth re-imported to India cheaply
  4. Result: India forced into raw material exporter, finished goods importer

  Gandhi's charkha (spinning wheel): symbol of resistance to economic
  subjugation — "buy Indian-made, spin your own" campaign
```

## Linen (Flax: *Linum usitatissimum*)

### The Plant and Fiber Anatomy
```
FLAX CROSS-SECTION:
  ┌──────────────────────────────┐
  │  Epidermis (outer skin)      │
  │  ┌────────────────────────┐  │
  │  │  Cortex/Bast fibers    │← ← linen fibers are here
  │  │  (10–40 long bundles,  │     in the stem cortex
  │  │   20–30 fibers/bundle) │
  │  ├────────────────────────┤  │
  │  │  Woody core (shive)    │← ← what must be removed
  │  │  (xylem/phloem)        │
  │  └────────────────────────┘  │
  └──────────────────────────────┘

  Fiber bundle length: 25–35 mm (individual technical fibers)
  Long-line fiber: 50–90 cm (the valuable stuff)
  Flax fiber diameter: 12–16 μm
```

### Retting: Controlled Biological Degradation
```
  PURPOSE: dissolve the pectin that bonds fiber bundles to the
           woody stem — without damaging the cellulose fiber itself

  WATER RETTING (pool/river/tank):
    Bundles submerged 5–14 days
    Anaerobic bacteria secrete pectinases
    Produces: softer, more uniform fiber
    Problem: effluent (high BOD) pollutes water — regulated or banned
             in many countries
    Historically: Belgian/Dutch linen superiority from clean river water

  DEW RETTING (field retting):
    Pulled flax spread in fields for 3–8 weeks
    Morning dew + fungi + alternating wet/dry
    Slower, more variable, but no water pollution
    Most common today (cost/environment)

  CHEMICAL RETTING:
    Sodium hydroxide or oxalic acid accelerates process
    Faster but can damage fiber if overcooked

  TEST: "Over-retted" = weak, disintegrating fiber
        "Under-retted" = shive still attached, coarse fabric
```

### Processing After Retting
```
  BREAKING:    Wooden fluted rollers crush the stalks
               Breaks woody core (shive) without cutting bast fiber

  SCUTCHING:   Rotating wooden paddles beat the broken stalks
               Removes shive fragments
               By-product: tow (short tangled fibers), used for rope/paper

  HECKLING (HACKLING):
    The fiber drawn through progressively finer metal combs
    Removes short fibers (creates tow fraction)
    Aligns long-line fibers into parallel strands
    Multiple passes: rough hackle → fine hackle
    "Heckler" etymology: the heckling operation required constant
    adjustment and questioning the work — vocal critics at public
    meetings came to be called hecklers

  OUTPUT:
    Long-line (line flax): fine, lustrous, expensive
    Tow flax: shorter, used in coarser linens
```

### Linen Properties
```
  STRONGEST natural fiber when WET (unlike cotton, which also weakens)
  Cool to touch: higher thermal conductivity than cotton
  Wrinkles easily: poor elasticity (low crimp, rigid cellulose)
  Low elongation: 2.7–3.5% at break (cotton: 3–7%)
  Luster: smooth fiber surface → characteristic sheen
  Biodegradable, anti-allergenic, pH-neutral
  Difficult to dye: lower dye affinity than cotton — often left natural
  Price: 2–5× cotton (labor-intensive processing)
```

## Hemp (*Cannabis sativa*)

```
  Bast fiber from stem, similar to flax but coarser
  Fiber length: 1–3 m (longest natural bast fiber)
  Diameter: 17–23 μm (coarser than linen)

  Processing identical to flax (retting → breaking → scutching → heckling)
  but hemp's higher lignin content makes processing harder

  PROPERTIES VS LINEN:
  Stronger than linen (higher tensile strength)
  Coarser hand — not next-to-skin comfort
  More UV-resistant
  Excellent for rope, canvas, sacking, industrial uses

  ETYMOLOGY: "canvas" from "cannabis" — hemp was the original canvas
  "denim" possibly from "de Nîmes" (serge de Nîmes, France), originally
  hemp-warp fabric

  HISTORY: major fiber until 20th century
  US Marihuana Tax Act (1937): effectively banned all Cannabis cultivation,
  including industrial hemp → US industry collapsed
  2018 Farm Bill: hemp (< 0.3% THC) removed from Controlled Substances Act
  → current revival as sustainable crop

  SUSTAINABILITY vs COTTON:
  Hemp needs ~50% less water than cotton
  No pesticide requirement (natural pest resistance)
  Higher yield per acre
  Sequesters carbon in roots (soil carbon)
```

## Jute (*Corchorus* spp.) and Ramie (*Boehmeria nivea*)

```
JUTE:
  "Golden fiber" — Bangladesh and India, 95% of world production
  Bast fiber: coarser than flax/hemp, significant lignin (~13%)
  Cheap, biodegradable, strong in bulk
  Uses: burlap/hessian (sacking), carpet backing, rope, geotextiles
  Softening: degumming removes some lignin → softer jute blends
  Not suited for fine apparel (lignin causes brittleness)

RAMIE (China grass):
  Very long, strong, lustrous fiber (longest natural fiber by some measures)
  High gum content (up to 30%) — difficult, expensive to degum
  Degumming: NaOH treatment required
  Properties post-processing: strong, lustrous, resembles linen
  Uses: filter fabrics, canvas, blended with linen/cotton
  Never became mainstream: processing cost vs alternatives
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want a fabric that... | Use |
|---|---|
| Is soft, affordable, easy to dye | Cotton |
| Is cool, strong, has linen texture | Linen |
| Is strongest, most UV-resistant | Hemp |
| Is cheapest, for industrial use | Jute |
| Is longest-staple, finest hand | Pima or Egyptian cotton |
| Is most sustainable (per lifecycle) | Hemp or Lyocell (not a plant fiber, but beats all) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Cotton gin didn't reduce the need for enslaved labor**: Whitney's gin mechanized the seed separation (1 person = 50-person output). But it made cotton so profitable that cultivation expanded massively — and cultivation (planting, tending, picking) remained entirely hand labor. More cotton land = more enslaved labor, not less.

**Linen vs flax**: Linen is the *fabric/fiber*; flax is the *plant*. The same plant also produces linseed oil from the seeds. A flax plant is both an oilseed crop and a fiber crop depending on how it's cultivated — fiber flax is grown tall and densely; oilseed flax is grown with wider spacing.

**Hemp ≠ marijuana**: Industrial hemp is *Cannabis sativa* bred for <0.3% THC. The same species, different cultivar. Processing hemp fiber doesn't produce anything psychoactive. The 1937 US ban was partly motivated by lumber/cotton interests lobbying against hemp competition.

**Combed vs carded cotton**: Both are from the same fiber. Combed cotton removes more short fibers → longer average staple → smoother, stronger yarn → better fabric. Combed is a subset of carded (carding always precedes combing).

**Linen wrinkles are inherent**: Linen wrinkles because cellulose is stiff and the fiber lacks the crimp/elasticity that would allow it to spring back. No finish permanently prevents this. Polyester-linen blends trade linen's hand for reduced wrinkling.
