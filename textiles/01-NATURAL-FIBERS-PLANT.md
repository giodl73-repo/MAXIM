# 01 — Natural Plant Fibers

## Cotton, Linen, Hemp — Cellulose, Cultivation, and Colonial History

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Cellulose chemistry**: all plant fibers are cellulose (β-1,4-glucan polymer); long chains of glucose units; hydrogen bonding between chains → crystalline regions (strong, stiff) + amorphous regions (flexible, dyeable); cotton ~90% cellulose, linen ~80%; cellulose absorbs moisture (hygroscopic), comfortable to wear, biodegradable, dyes well with reactive and vat dyes; burns cleanly (no toxic fumes unlike wool or synthetics)
- **Cotton** (*Gossypium* spp.): seed hair fiber; primarily *G. hirsutum* (Upland, ~90% of world production), *G. barbadense* (Egyptian/Pima/Sea Island — longer staple, finer), *G. arboreum* and *G. herbaceum* (Old World, shorter, coarser); boll = protective capsule opening to reveal fiber; fiber length (staple): short (<7/8"), medium (7/8"-1 1/8"), long (1 1/8"-1 3/8"), extra-long (>1 3/8" — Egyptian/Pima)
- Cotton fiber processing: harvest (mechanical or hand) → ginning (separating fiber from seed — Eli Whitney's cotton gin 1793, patented — but actually similar devices existed earlier; the gin's importance was enabling short-staple cotton cultivation across the American South, making slavery dramatically more economically valuable and entrenching it) → baling → opening/cleaning → carding (aligning fibers, removing remaining trash) → drawing → roving → spinning
- **Cotton and slavery**: American South dominated world cotton production 1800-1860 using enslaved labor; "King Cotton" supplied ~75% of British cotton mills' raw material; Manchester and other mill towns depended on slave-grown cotton; 1860 — 3.84 million enslaved people in US, mostly in cotton production; Civil War cotton embargo devastated British textile industry; Egypt and India rushed to fill gap; cotton was the most important commodity in 19th century global trade
- **Cotton and colonialism in India**: India had sophisticated cotton textile industry for millennia (Indian muslins, calicoes, and chintzes exported globally, famous in Roman era); British East India Company systematically dismantled Indian textile industry in 18th-19th century to protect British mill interests — import duties on Indian cloth, forcing India to export raw cotton and import British finished goods; Gandhi's spinning wheel (*charkha*) as symbol of resistance to this economic subjugation
- **Linen (flax)** (*Linum usitatissimum*): bast fiber from the stem (not the seed — linseed oil from the seeds); processing: retting (soaking in water to decompose the pectin binding fibers to woody core — water retting produces softer fiber, dew retting slower but less water pollution) → breaking → scutching (removing woody stem pieces) → heckling/hackling (combing through finer and finer metal teeth to align and separate fibers) → spinning; each step historically a skilled trade; "heckler" etymologically from heckling (heckling was the operation that required the most intervention and adjustment — a heckling audience)
- Linen properties: strongest natural fiber, becomes stronger when wet; cool, smooth, low friction; poor elasticity (wrinkles easily — linen texture inseparable from its wrinkle); excellent moisture absorption; difficult to dye (lower dye affinity than cotton); expensive to produce (labor-intensive processing); historically dominant in Europe for undergarments, table linen, sailcloth
- **Hemp** (*Cannabis sativa*): bast fiber, similar processing to flax; stronger and coarser than linen; "canvas" from "cannabis" (hemp cloth was the original canvas material); rope, sailcloth, ship rigging historically; marijuana content bred out of industrial hemp cultivars; US cultivation banned 1937-2018 (Marihuana Tax Act) — disrupted American hemp industry; global revival as sustainable fiber (lower water/pesticide needs than cotton)
- **Jute**: bast fiber from *Corchorus* spp.; Bangladesh and India dominant producers; burlap/hessian cloth; grain sacks, carpet backing, rope; coarse, biodegradable, cheap; "golden fiber" — major export crop of Bengal
- **Ramie**: from *Boehmeria nivea*; very long, strong, lustrous fiber; difficult to process (high gum content requires chemical degumming); blended with cotton/linen; China dominant producer; used in filter fabrics, fire-resistant applications
