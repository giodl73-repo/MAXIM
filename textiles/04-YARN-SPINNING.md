# 04 — Yarn & Spinning

## Drop Spindle to Ring Frame — Twist, Count, and the Spinning Revolution

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **What spinning does**: drafts (attenuates) a roving of fibers into a thinner bundle, then twists that bundle → friction between fibers locks them together into a continuous yarn; twist is essential — fibers alone pull apart easily; twist direction: S-twist (spirals like middle of S when vertical) vs Z-twist (like middle of Z); plied yarns typically twist in opposite direction to singles (Z-spun singles plied S-wise → balanced, stable yarn)
- **Drop spindle**: oldest spinning technology (~20,000 BCE, likely older); weighted disk (whorl) on a stick (spindle shaft); fiber attached to spindle, spindle dropped and spun → twist travels up into drafted fiber; worldwide tradition; still used in Andean communities, hand-spinners; all ancient textiles — Egyptian linen, Roman wool, Chinese silk — made with spindle-spun yarn
- **Spinning wheel**: spindle mounted horizontally, turned by drive band from large wheel; great wheel (walking wheel) — hand-driven, spinner walks back to draft; treadle wheel (Saxony wheel, ~15th century Europe) — foot-operated treadle, both hands free for drafting; continuous spinning (flyer-and-bobbin mechanism); spinning wheel = transformative labor productivity gain; ~3x faster than drop spindle; still required significant skill
- **The Industrial Revolution spinning machinery**:
  - **Spinning jenny** (James Hargreaves, ~1764): multiple spindles operated simultaneously (8, then up to 120); spinning only (no drawing); manually operated; enabled home-based cottage industry expansion; Hargreaves' home attacked by textile workers fearing job loss
  - **Water frame** (Richard Arkwright, 1769): rollers drawing fiber instead of fingers → "drafted" yarn; water-powered; produced stronger, harder-twisted yarn (suitable for warp); required a factory (too heavy/complex for home); created the factory system
  - **Spinning mule** (Samuel Crompton, 1779): combined jenny's multiple spindles with water frame's roller drafting; produced finer, stronger yarn than either alone; mule yarn = fine muslin; Crompton made little money from invention; industrialized spinning completed the displacement of hand-spinners by ~1820
  - **Ring spinning** (1828): continuous spinning (vs mule's reciprocating motion); ring and traveler (small C-shaped clip running around ring) adds twist as yarn is wound onto bobbin; dominant spinning technology for most of 20th century; still widely used
  - **Open-end/rotor spinning** (1960s): fibers separated to individual fibers, fed into rotating rotor, twisted by vacuum → yarn formed at much higher speed than ring spinning; coarser yarns; denim yarns typically rotor-spun; 5-10× faster than ring spinning; replaced ring spinning for many applications
  - **Air-jet spinning**: high-velocity air jets twist fiber bundle; very fast; limited fiber types; Murata Jets; used for blended and cotton yarns
- **Yarn count systems** (measuring yarn thickness/weight — confusingly, multiple systems in use):
  - **Denier** (filament yarns, silk, synthetics): weight in grams per 9,000 meters; lower denier = finer yarn; 75D = thicker than 30D; standard for nylon and polyester
  - **Tex**: weight in grams per 1,000 meters; SI system; universal but not universally used
  - **Ne (English cotton count)**: number of 840-yard hanks per pound; higher number = finer yarn; 30s cotton = medium, 100s = very fine; counterintuitive direction vs denier
  - **Nm (metric count)**: meters per gram; higher number = finer
  - Conversion: Denier × Ne ≈ 5315 (approximate conversion)
- **Ply**: number of singles twisted together; 2-ply, 4-ply (in hand-knitting yarn), 12-ply (bulky); plying improves strength, reduces hairiness, balances twist; fingering weight, DK, worsted, bulky (knitting yarn weight systems); thread count in woven fabric = how many yarns per inch in warp and weft directions (higher ≠ always better — very high thread counts sometimes use 2-ply counted as 2 threads)
- **Specialty yarns**: textured (false-twist textured polyester — bulk and stretch), bouclé (looped), chenille (velvet-like), slub (irregular thickness — "linen-look" in cotton), core-spun (spandex core + cotton or polyester wrapping → stretch fabric), novelty yarns for hand-knitting
