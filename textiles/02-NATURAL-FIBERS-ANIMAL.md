# 02 — Natural Animal Fibers

## Wool, Silk, Cashmere — Protein Chemistry, Sericulture, Craft Traditions

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Protein fiber chemistry**: wool and silk are proteins (long-chain polypeptides); wool = α-keratin (same protein as hair/nails, coiled α-helix structure → springy, elastic); silk = fibroin (β-sheet crystal structure → strong, relatively inelastic, smooth); both contain sulfur-bearing amino acids (wool especially — disulfide bonds between cysteine residues → cross-links → structural stability); protein fibers dye well with acid dyes (the amino groups provide bonding sites); burn with distinctive smell (burning hair); biodegradable
- **Wool** (*Ovis aries* sheep): world's dominant protein fiber; fleece = annual shearing of the whole fleece; grades by fiber diameter (microns) — finest 14-18 μm (Merino superfine), medium 20-28 μm, coarse >30 μm; staple length; crimp (wave frequency); lanolin (grease) content — raw fleece is ~30-50% non-fiber material
- Wool processing: shearing → skirting (removing heavily contaminated edges) → sorting/classing (grading) → scouring (washing in hot alkaline solution to remove lanolin, dirt, sweat) → carbonizing (acid treatment dissolves vegetable matter — "burrs") → carding (opening, cleaning, aligning) → two paths: woolen spun (shorter fibers, carded only → bulky, fuzzy, warm — think Shetland sweater) vs worsted spun (long fibers, combed → align parallel, spin smooth → fine suiting cloth, gabardine)
- **Merino wool**: from Merino breed (Iberian Peninsula origin, jealously guarded — Spain banned export of Merino sheep under pain of death until late 18th century); finest, softest wool (16-22 μm); next-to-skin comfort (no itch); Australia dominant producer; New Zealand, Argentina; high-value: Merino activewear, base layers
- **Wool's functional properties**: natural crimp traps air → excellent thermal insulation; moisture-wicking (absorbs up to 30% weight in moisture without feeling wet, releases slowly → regulates temperature); natural flame resistance (keratin self-extinguishes); antimicrobial (lanolin + protein structure → bacterial growth inhibited → "no-wash" Merino claims); felting when agitated in hot water (scales on fiber surface interlock → irreversible shrinkage)
- **Specialty wools**: Shetland (coarser, from Scottish island sheep, traditional Fair Isle knitting), Icelandic (outer coat/inner coat distinction — "lopi"), Gotland (lustrous, for outerwear), tweed (coarse, water-resistant, Harris Tweed from Outer Hebrides — legally protected Geographic Indication)
- **Silk**: produced by silkworm (*Bombyx mori*) in pupal cocoon; each cocoon = single continuous filament 1,000-4,000 feet long; sericulture (silk farming) involved: mulberry cultivation → egg hatching → feeding larvae exclusively mulberry leaves → cocoon formation → killing pupae (with heat, before moth emerges and breaks filament) → reeling (several cocoons' filaments wound together off boiling water bath) → throwing (twisting multiple reeled threads)
- Silk history: invented in China ~2700 BCE (legend: Empress Leizu); silk production techniques guarded as state secret for ~3,000 years — export of silkworms punishable by death; Nestorian monks smuggled silkworm eggs to Byzantine Empire ~550 CE (Procopius account); silk drove the Silk Road trade (see textiles/08); varieties: mulberry silk (standard), tussah/wild silk (from several Antheraea moths — coarser, irregular, "raw silk" appearance), spider silk (impossibly difficult to farm — dragline silk stronger than Kevlar per unit weight, current research in transgenic goats/bacteria/yeast for mass production)
- Silk properties: triangular cross-section prism → reflects light → characteristic luster; smooth, low friction; strong for weight; poor elasticity; degrades in UV (yellows); perspiration degrades; dry clean recommended; excellent dye affinity (vibrant colors); temperature regulating
- **Cashmere**: undercoat of Cashmere goat (*Capra hircus laniger*); combed (not shorn) during spring molt; fiber 14-18 μm (similar to finest Merino but without scales → softer); China (~70%), Mongolia, Iran as producers; scarcity → desertification in Mongolia (goat overpopulation destroying grasslands); "cashmere" mislabeling common (must be ≤19 μm by standard); pashmina = Persian/Urdu word for cashmere (often misused for any soft shawl)
- **Alpaca**: from *Vicugna pacos* (South America — Peru, Bolivia); two varieties: Huacaya (fluffy, most common), Suri (silky, lustrous); very fine, no lanolin (hypoallergenic), hollow fiber (light + warm); Inca nobility fabric; current revival in luxury market; *Vicuña* (wild relative, uncamelid) — world's finest fiber (~12 μm), can only be shorn every 3 years, extremely expensive, once reserved for Inca royalty
- **Mohair** (Angora goat *Capra hircus*) and **Angora** (Angora rabbit *Oryctolagus cuniculus*): mohair = lustrous, strong, takes dye brilliantly (characteristic "sparkle"); Turkey/South Africa production; Angora rabbit fiber = very soft, fluffy, felts easily
