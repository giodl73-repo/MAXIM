# Natural Fibers — Animal (Protein)

## The Big Picture: Protein Chemistry

```
ALL ANIMAL FIBERS = PROTEIN POLYMER
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                                  |
|  Amino acid monomers (20 types; all have -NH₂, -COOH, R-group)   |
|        ↓                                                         |
|  Peptide bonds: -CO-NH- (condensation of -COOH + -NH₂)           |
|        ↓                                                         |
|  Polypeptide chain (protein)                                     |
|        ↓ ↓                                                       |
|  WOOL (α-keratin):          SILK (fibroin):                      |
|  α-helix secondary struct.  β-sheet secondary struct.            |
|  Coiled coils → crimped     Flat sheets → smooth, lustrous       |
|  Disulfide bonds (Cys-Cys)  Glycine/Alanine repeat — small R     |
|  Elastic, springy, warm     Inelastic, strong, slippery          |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+

WHY PROTEIN FIBERS BEHAVE DIFFERENTLY FROM CELLULOSIC:
  - Dye affinity: acid dyes bond to amino groups → vibrant color
  - Fire: char and self-extinguish (keratin crosslinks; not melt/burn)
  - Wet strength: wool weakens less when wet; silk loses strength
  - Alkali sensitivity: NaOH attacks peptide bonds → always use
    acidic or neutral wool wash products, never sodium carbonate
  - Insects: dermestid beetles, clothes moths eat keratin proteins
    (wool and silk storage requires cedar/lavender or sealed bags)
```

## Wool

### Fleece Anatomy and Grading

```
SHEEP FLEECE STRUCTURE:
  Individual fiber anatomy:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Cuticle: overlapping scales (like roof     │
  │  shingles) pointing from root to tip        │
  │  → interlocking in hot/wet agitation        │
  │  → felting (irreversible)                   │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Cortex: two cell types (ortho/para)         │
  │  offset arrangement → bilateral structure    │
  │  → CRIMP (natural waviness of wool fiber)   │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Medulla: hollow core (some fibers only)    │
  │  Present in coarse fibers, carpet wools     │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘

GRADING SYSTEM:
  Diameter (μm)    Bradford count    Grade/use
  < 15             120s              Ultra-fine (rare, Vicuña-level)
  15–17            100s–110s         Ultrafine Merino
  17–19            80s               Fine Merino (base layer quality)
  19–22            70s               Fine Merino/Superfine
  22–25            60s               Medium (suiting, knitwear)
  25–30            50s–56s           Medium-coarse
  30–35            46s–48s           Coarse (outerwear, tweeds)
  > 35             40s and below     Carpet grades

  Prickle threshold: ~22–25 μm — fibers above this diameter
  deflect (not bend) against skin nerve endings → itch sensation
  Merino (<22 μm) is genuinely next-to-skin comfortable for most people
```

### Scouring: Removing the Lanolin

```
RAW FLEECE COMPOSITION:
  ~40–70% fiber (by weight)
  ~20–25% lanolin (grease, wax esters from sebaceous glands)
  ~10–15% suint (dried sweat — potassium salts)
  ~10–15% vegetable matter (grass, seeds, burrs)
  ~5–10% sand, soil

SCOURING PROCESS:
  Hot water (60°C) + surfactant (alkyl sulfate or soap)
  Runs of progressively cleaner water (5-10 bowls)
  Wringing/squeezing removes liquor without agitation
  (agitation would felt the wet fiber)
  Recovered lanolin → commercial lanolin (cosmetics, waterproofing)

CARBONIZING (vegetable matter removal):
  Dilute H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid, ~5%) treatment
  Acid degrades cellulose (vegetable matter) without attacking wool keratin
  Drying + crushing → vegetable matter crumbles away
  Neutralization → ready for processing
```

```

=============                    ==============
```

### Wool's Functional Properties

```
MOISTURE MANAGEMENT (most important for activewear):
  Wool absorbs up to 30% of its weight in moisture
  without feeling wet — absorbed into fiber core
  (vs polyester: stays on surface → clammy feel)
  Releases moisture slowly via evaporative cooling
  → temperature regulation: warm when dry, less cold when wet
  Merino next-to-skin layer principle: same as synthetic
  wicking but with better comfort + odor control

NATURAL FLAME RESISTANCE:
  LOI (limiting oxygen index): wool ~25%, cotton ~18%
  Cotton burns in normal air (21% O₂); wool self-extinguishes
  Mechanism: high moisture regain + cross-linked keratin
  → wool required in aircraft/public transport seating
  → Nomex wool blends for military uniforms

ANTIMICROBIAL / ODOR RESISTANCE:
  Lanolin trace amounts + protein structure inhibit bacteria
  Merino marketers claim "wear multiple days without washing"
  — credible for base layers in field conditions; debatable for
  gym use

FELTING (the failure mode):
  Scale surface on wet, warm, agitated wool → ratchets
  in one direction → fibers migrate toward root end
  → irreversible entanglement → shrinkage 20-40%
  Prevention: Superwash treatment — scale removal by
  chlorine oxidation + polymer coating (Hercosett resin)
```

### Merino and Specialty Wools

```
MERINO:
  Origin: Iberian Peninsula; Spain banned export under death penalty
          until late 18th century (flock smuggled to Prussia, later
          to Australia/New Zealand)
  Current: Australia (~80% of ultrafine Merino world supply)
  Range: 15.5–22 μm; 17.5 μm is typical "fine" Merino knitwear
  Price premium: 2–5× standard wool

SHETLAND (Scotland):
  From North Sea island sheep; native hardened breed
  22–28 μm; traditional Fair Isle stranded colorwork
  Authentic Harris Tweed: Geographic Indication (EU/UK)
  Hand-woven in Outer Hebrides, 100% virgin wool

ICELANDIC:
  Dual-coat fleece: outer tog (water/weather resistant) +
  inner þel (soft insulating layer)
  Lopi yarn spun from combined coats — bulky, light, warm

CASHMERE (Cashmere goat, Capra hircus laniger):
  Combed (not shorn) from undercoat during spring molt
  China: ~70% world production; Mongolia, Iran, Afghanistan
  14–16 μm — very fine, soft; no scales (vs wool) → no itch
  2–8 oz per goat per year → scarcity → price
  Sustainability crisis: overgrazing for cashmere demand
  → Mongolian grassland desertification
  Standard: ≤ 19 μm by fiber analysis (common mislabeling)

ALPACA (Vicugna pacos):
  Two types: Huacaya (fluffy, crimped — 90% of market)
             Suri (silky, lustrous, uncommon)
  No lanolin → hypoallergenic; hollow fiber → light + warm
  South America (Peru ~80%, Bolivia); Inca nobility fabric
  12–30 μm range; finest = "baby alpaca" (first shearing)

VICUÑA (Vicugna vicugna):
  Wild camelid, Andes; ~12 μm — world's finest textile fiber
  Traditionally: Inca "royal fiber", captured for shearing then
  released (chakku ceremony); hunting banned → extinction risk
  Today: licensed sustainable harvesting under Peruvian law
  3 oz per animal per 3-year interval → extreme scarcity
  Price: $300–600+/oz raw fiber; $5,000–30,000 for a coat
```

## Silk

### Sericulture: The Biology of Silk Production

```
SILK LIFECYCLE:
  Mulberry tree     Egg        Larva (silkworm)   Pupa
  cultivation   → hatching → feeding 35 days  → cocoon
                                  ↓                ↓
                           1,000 mulberry      continuous silk
                           leaves per larva    filament
                           until pupation      600–1,500 m

THE COCOON:
  Single continuous filament of fibroin protein
  Covered in sericin (silk gum — water-soluble binding protein)
  Two fibroin filaments (baves) side by side, coated by sericin
  Filament diameter: ~10–13 μm
  Filament tensile strength: 4.5 cN/dtex (comparable to nylon)

REELING:
  Cocoons in hot water (50–60°C) → sericin softens
  4–8 cocoon filaments combined onto reel
  Fibers twist together as they dry → "raw silk" thread
  (reeled filament, before sericin removed)

THROWING:
  Multiple reeled threads twisted together → stronger thread
  Organzine (S-twist singles then Z-twist plied) = warp thread
  Tram (slight twist) = weft thread
  Different twist levels → different fabric properties

DEGUMMING (removing sericin):
  Boiled in soap solution → sericin dissolves
  ~25% weight loss (sericin ~25% of cocoon weight)
  Reveals: lustrous, soft fibroin fiber
  "Weighted silk" (historical practice): replaced lost weight
  by impregnating with metallic salts → heavier feel but
  degrades rapidly (shattering); now largely banned
```

### China's 3,000-Year Monopoly

```
  ~2700 BCE   Legend: Empress Leizu discovered silk (cocoon fell
              in tea → thread unspooled)
  ~3000 BCE   Archaeological silk fragments in China
  -200 BCE    Han Dynasty: silk as diplomatic currency and tribute
              Silk road trade begins (silk going west, glass coming east)
  ~550 CE     Nestorian monks smuggle silkworm eggs to Byzantine Empire
              (Procopius account) — ending Chinese monopoly
              Hidden in hollow walking staves
  1200s CE    Italy (Lucca, Florence, Venice) develops European silk industry
  1700s CE    Lyon (France) becomes European silk capital
              Jacquard loom invented 1801 in Lyon for patterned silk

  EXPORT PROHIBITION:
  China: exporting live silkworms or eggs = death penalty
  Yet the technology spread through industrial espionage
  parallel to later theft of various national manufacturing secrets
```

### Silk Properties and Varieties

```
FIBER CROSS-SECTION:
  Triangular prism shape → reflects light from multiple angles
  → characteristic shimmering luster

FIBROIN β-SHEET STRUCTURE:
  Gly-Ala-Gly-Ala-Gly-Ser repeat → flat β-sheets stack
  → crystalline, inextensible fiber
  Elongation at break: 15–25% (vs cotton 3–7%)
  No crimp → smooth, slippery hand

PROPERTIES:
  Tensile strength: 3.5–5.0 cN/dtex (strong for weight)
  Poor UV stability: yellows with sunlight exposure
  Degrades with perspiration (sweat acids attack fibroin)
  Dry clean recommendation (water + friction → damage)
  Excellent thermal regulation (cool in summer, warm in winter)
  Excellent moisture absorption (11% regain)

VARIETIES:
  Mulberry silk: Bombyx mori; fed exclusively mulberry;
                 smooth, uniform, lustrous; 90%+ of commercial silk
  Tussah/Wild silk: Antheraea moths; oak/other leaves;
                    coarser, irregular, off-white/tan; "natural" texture
                    "Raw silk" appearance in fabric
  Spider silk: dragline = 1,000+ MPa tensile (vs steel ~400 MPa);
               toughness exceeds Kevlar; cannot farm spiders
               (territorial, cannibalistic); bioengineered spider silk
               from transgenic goats/bacteria/yeast = active research
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| I want a fiber that... | Choose |
|---|---|
| Is soft next-to-skin, odor-resistant | Merino wool |
| Provides most warmth-per-weight | Vicuña > Qiviut > Cashmere > Alpaca |
| Is most durable for wear | Silk (or wool for abrasion) |
| Is machine-washable wool | Superwash Merino |
| Has the most luster | Silk > Mulberry silk > Mohair |
| Is hypoallergenic | Cashmere or Alpaca (no lanolin) |
| Is cheapest protein fiber | Standard wool |

## Common Confusion Points

**Wool doesn't burn — it chars**: Wool's LOI is ~25% — requires an oxygen-enriched atmosphere to sustain combustion. Normal air is 21% O₂ → wool self-extinguishes. This is structural (crosslinked keratin + high moisture regain), not a finish that wears off.

**Felting is irreversible**: Wool scales ratchet in one direction when wet + hot + agitated. Once felted, you cannot unfelt. Superwash removes/smooths scales — but Superwash wool loses some natural properties (less elastic, may pill more).

**"Cashmere" is often mislabeled**: Blends with rabbit hair, yak fiber, or standard wool labeled as "cashmere." The EC regulation requires fiber testing. Authentic cashmere is ≤19 μm by testing.

**Spider silk is not available commercially**: Every "spider silk" product on the market is either conventional silk, or synthetic/bioengineered silk protein. Wild spider silk cannot be harvested at scale. Bioengineered spider silk protein (Bolt Threads, AMSilk) is real but still niche and expensive.

**Sericin vs fibroin**: Sericin is the water-soluble gum binder; fibroin is the structural protein. "Raw silk" (unreeled thread before degumming) contains both. Degummed silk is pure fibroin. Some products sold as "raw silk" fabric are actually not raw — the term is often used loosely for tussah silk.
