## Waterproof/Breathable Membranes

### Gore-Tex and ePTFE

```
  ORIGIN:
    PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene) = Teflon
    1969: Bob Gore (son of W.L. Gore) rapidly stretched PTFE
    → discovered expanded PTFE (ePTFE): white, porous, strong
    → nodes connected by fine fibrils → microporous structure

  THE PHYSICS:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ePTFE MEMBRANE PORE SIZE: ~0.2 microns (200 nm)        │
  │                                                          │
  │  COMPARISON:                                             │
  │  Water droplet (rain/mist): ~100 microns (100,000 nm)   │
  │  Water vapor molecule: ~0.0003 microns (0.3 nm)         │
  │                                                          │
  │  Liquid water droplet: 500,000× too large → BLOCKED     │
  │  Water vapor molecule: 667× too small → PASSES THROUGH  │
  │                                                          │
  │  RESULT: Waterproof + Breathable                        │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PORE DENSITY: ~9 billion pores per square inch

  LAMINATE CONSTRUCTION:
    Face fabric (outer: nylon, polyester)
    ePTFE membrane
    Backer fabric (lining or mesh)
    All layers adhesive-laminated together

  2-LAYER vs 3-LAYER vs 2.5-LAYER:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  2-layer:   Face + membrane; separate lining            │
  │             Softer, drapier; separate lining can bunch  │
  │  3-layer:   Face + membrane + backer ALL laminated      │
  │             Stiffer, more durable; used in hard shells  │
  │  2.5-layer: Face + membrane + printed texture (not lining)│
  │             Compromise; common in packable shells        │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PERFORMANCE METRICS:
    MVTR (Moisture Vapor Transmission Rate): g/m²/24h
      Gore-Tex Pro: ~25,000+ g/m²/24h
      Budget WPB membranes: ~5,000-10,000 g/m²/24h
    Waterproofness: hydrostatic head test (mm of water column)
      Gore-Tex: typically 28,000+ mm (well above rain pressure)

  COMPETITORS (post-1997 patent expiration):
    eVent: directly venting ePTFE (similar principle)
    Pertex Shield: polyurethane membrane
    Polartec Neoshell: mechanically perforated PU membrane
    All function on same principle: microporous barrier

  THE DWR PROBLEM (see also 07-DYEING-FINISHING):
    Face fabric must shed water, not absorb it
    If face fabric wets out: membrane still works but body
    feels cold (evaporative cooling through wet fabric)
    DWR (Durable Water Repellency) on face fabric prevents
    this. As DWR wears, performance degrades even though
    membrane is intact.
    Restore DWR: tumble dry on low heat or iron through cloth
    Re-treat: spray-on DWR products (Nikwax, Grangers)
```

## High-Performance Structural Fibers

### Kevlar (Para-Aramid)

```
  INVENTOR: Stephanie Kwolek, Du Pont, 1965
  Full name: poly-para-phenylene terephthalamide (PPTA)

  MOLECULAR STRUCTURE:
    Para = benzene rings connected at para positions (180°)
    → Rigid, highly linear polymer chain
    → Rigid chains align into ordered liquid crystal in solution
    → Wet spinning → orientation locked in → crystal alignment

  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Kevlar structure (schematic):                           │
  │  –[NH-C₆H₄-NH-CO-C₆H₄-CO]ₙ–                           │
  │  Aromatic rings connected at para positions              │
  │  → all-aromatic backbone → extremely rigid              │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MECHANICAL PROPERTIES:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Property         │ Kevlar 29      │ Steel     │ Nylon     │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Tensile strength │ 3.6 GPa        │ 0.4 GPa   │ 0.08 GPa  │
  │  by weight        │ (5× steel/wt)  │           │           │
  │  Density          │ 1.44 g/cm³     │ 7.85      │ 1.14      │
  │  Specific strength│ 2.5 MN·m/kg   │ 0.05      │ 0.07      │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Body armor: NIJ IIIA soft armor (multiple woven layers)
      → stops 9mm, .44 Magnum; not rifle rounds
    Cut-resistant gloves: kitchen, industrial
    Helmets (PASGT, ACH, ECH military helmets)
    Tires: bead and belt reinforcement
    Brake pads: replacing asbestos
    Cables: marine, fiber optic cable jacket

  LIMITATIONS:
    Poor UV resistance: degrades in sunlight → body armor
      must be stored covered; replaced on schedule
    Low compressive strength: strong in tension, weak in compression
    Not flame resistant (chars above 400°C but does ignite)

### Nomex (Meta-Aramid)

  Same chemistry as Kevlar but META linkage (120° vs 180°)
  → Non-linear, non-crystalline structure
  → NOT high tensile strength
  → But: inherently flame resistant (does not melt, drip, or ignite)

  KEY PROPERTY: when exposed to flame, Nomex chars and
  swells → creates insulating char barrier

  APPLICATIONS:
    Firefighter turnout gear (outer shell)
    F1/motorsport race suits
    Military flight suits
    Industrial FR garments (arc flash, flash fire)
    Honeycomb core material (aircraft interior panels)

  NOMEX vs KEVLAR IN PROTECTION:
    Nomex: use for flame/thermal protection (suits, gloves)
    Kevlar: use for cut/ballistic/impact resistance
    Often combined: Kevlar/Nomex blends for multi-hazard
```

### UHMWPE (Ultra-High Molecular Weight Polyethylene)

```
  BRANDS: Dyneema (DSM, Netherlands), Spectra (Honeywell, US)

  STRUCTURE:
    Same polymer as ordinary polyethylene
    Molecular weight: 3.5-7.5 million g/mol
    (vs commodity PE: 50,000-300,000 g/mol)
    Gel spinning aligns molecular chains → crystalline structure

  PROPERTIES:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Highest specific strength of any fiber (surpasses     │
  │  Kevlar on a weight basis — up to 15× stronger than    │
  │  steel by weight)                                       │
  │  Floats on water (density < 1 g/cm³)                   │
  │  Excellent cut resistance                               │
  │  Excellent abrasion resistance                          │
  │  Very low friction surface                              │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  APPLICATIONS:
    Body armor (NIJ III/IV rigid plates — UHMWPE + ceramic)
    Cut-resistant gloves (kitchen, industrial)
    Yacht racing rigging: replacing wire rope and stainless
    Marine mooring lines: Dyneema rope at 1/7 weight of steel
    Medical implants: hip and knee joint implants (bearing surfaces)
    Drone/parachute lines

  CRITICAL LIMITATION:
    Melts at ~130-150°C (much lower than aramids ~400+°C)
    → NOT flame resistant; will melt near heat sources
    → Cannot be used where thermal exposure is a risk
```

## Flame-Resistant Textiles

```
  TWO CATEGORIES: INHERENT vs TREATED FR

  INHERENTLY FR (flame resistance in polymer chemistry):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Fiber          │ Mechanism                │ Temp limit    │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Nomex          │ Chars, swells, no drip   │ ~370°C        │
  │  Kevlar         │ Chars, high LOI          │ ~400°C        │
  │  PBI            │ Polybenzimidazole; ultra │ ~480°C        │
  │  (Celazole)     │ high protection; expensive│               │
  │  Carbon fiber   │ Doesn't burn; conducts   │ Varies        │
  │  Modacrylic     │ Self-extinguishing        │ ~150°C        │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  TREATED FR (coating or reactive finish):
    PROBAN process: tetrakis-hydroxymethyl-phosphonium salt
      precondensed and cured in cotton fiber
      → phosphorus-based chemistry interrupts combustion
      Durable to 50+ washes; not permanent
    Reactive FR finishes: reacted into cellulose fiber
    Limitations: wash durability; not inherent

  LAYERING SYSTEM (industrial protection):
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  OUTER SHELL: arc/flash fire protection (FR cotton,      │
  │               Nomex blend, Kevlar/Nomex)                  │
  │                                                          │
  │  MID LAYER: FR insulation (if needed for thermal         │
  │             protection in firefighting applications)      │
  │                                                          │
  │  BASE LAYER: moisture management; NO non-FR synthetics   │
  │              (polyester melts to skin in flash fire →    │
  │              catastrophic burns; always FR base layer)   │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  STANDARDS:
    NFPA 2112: industrial flash fire garments
    ASTM F1506: electrical arc flash garments
    EN ISO 11612: heat and flame protection (EU)
    NFPA 1977: wildland fire (USFS specification)
```

## Medical Textiles

```
  WOUND CARE MATERIALS:
    Woven gauze (cotton): absorptive; wound packing
    Non-woven dressings: polyester, PP — wound contact layers
    Alginate dressings:
      From seaweed (Laminaria, Ascophyllum) — polysaccharide gel
      Absorbs up to 15× own weight in wound exudate
      Hemostatic (calcium alginate promotes clotting)
      Biodegradable; conforms to wound shape
    Hydrocolloid: CMC/gelatin/pectin matrix — absorbs, forms gel
    Silver dressings: Ag ions, nanoparticles, or coatings
      Antimicrobial; prevent infection in chronic wounds

  IMPLANTABLE TEXTILES:
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SURGICAL SUTURES:                                       │
  │  Absorbable:                                             │
  │    PGA (polyglycolic acid) — Dexon: absorbs 3-4 months  │
  │    PGLA (glycolide/lactide) — Vicryl: absorbs 2-3 months│
  │    Chromic gut: collagen + chromium; absorbs 2-3 weeks   │
  │  Non-absorbable:                                         │
  │    Nylon (monofilament or braided)                       │
  │    Polypropylene: least tissue reactivity               │
  │    Silk: braided; traditional but absorbs slowly         │
  │    Stainless steel wire: bone closure, sternal wires    │
  │                                                          │
  │  HERNIA MESH:                                            │
  │    Polypropylene knitted mesh — most common              │
  │    Lightweight vs heavyweight: tissue ingrowth tradeoffs │
  │    Controversy: PP mesh complications; FDA warnings       │
  │    Biologic meshes (porcine/bovine collagen): alternative│
  │                                                          │
  │  VASCULAR GRAFTS:                                        │
  │    ePTFE (Gore-Tex) or PET/Dacron (woven or knitted)    │
  │    For replacing/bypassing blood vessels                 │
  │    Porosity allows tissue ingrowth over time             │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ELECTROSPUN NANOFIBERS:
    High-voltage electrostatic field draws polymer into
    fibers 100-1000 nm diameter (vs normal ~10 microns)
    Scaffold for tissue engineering — cells grow on 3D matrix
    Wound dressings with biomimetic ECM structure
    Drug-releasing membranes (drug in polymer → controlled release)
```

## Geotextiles

```
  USE: engineered into ground → interact with soil and water

  FUNCTIONS (one structure may serve several):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SEPARATION: prevent mixing of soil layers              │
  │    Road base over soft soil; railroad ballast over subgrade│
  │                                                          │
  │  FILTRATION: allow water through, retain soil           │
  │    Drain behind retaining wall; around drainage pipe    │
  │                                                          │
  │  DRAINAGE: transmit water within geotextile plane       │
  │    Wrapped around perforated pipe; chimney drain        │
  │                                                          │
  │  REINFORCEMENT: add tensile strength to soil mass       │
  │    Slope stabilization; mechanically stabilized earth   │
  │    retaining walls (layers of geogrid + compacted fill) │
  │                                                          │
  │  CONTAINMENT: erosion control; pond liner reinforcement │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  MATERIALS:
    Woven: PP or PET; high strength; good drainage through plane
    Non-woven needle-punched: PP; filtration + drainage
    Geogrid: polymer grid (not textile per se); soil reinforcement

  DESIGN SPECIFICATIONS:
    AOS (Apparent Opening Size): pore size in geotextile
      Must be fine enough to retain soil particles
      (AOS < D₈₅ of soil being retained)
    Permittivity: flow perpendicular to plane
    Transmissivity: flow in plane of geotextile
    Survivability: strength to survive installation
      (tear, puncture, burst — construction loads)
```

## Smart Textiles and E-Textiles

```
  CONDUCTIVE YARNS:
    Stainless steel fibers blended into core yarn
    Silver-coated polyester or nylon fibers
    Carbon-coated fibers
    Metal wire wrapped around fiber core (embroidery thread)

  RESISTIVITY RANGE:
    Copper wire: 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m (excellent conductor)
    Silver-coated fiber: ~10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ Ω·m
    Stainless steel fiber: ~10⁻⁷ Ω·m
    Carbon fiber: ~10⁻⁵ Ω·m
    (Standard polyester: ~10¹² Ω·m — insulator)

  APPLICATIONS:

  EMF SHIELDING:
    Silver/stainless fabrics: attenuate electromagnetic radiation
    Used for RF shielding, hospital equipment, Faraday cages
    Also marketed in consumer "anti-tracking" products (limited basis)

  HEATED GARMENTS:
    Resistive heating elements (carbon fiber, stainless wire panels)
    Battery pack → current → heat dissipated in resistance
    Ski gloves, jackets, insoles, motorcycle gear, therapy garments
    Typical: 3-12W heating per zone, 3-8 hour battery life

  BIOMETRIC SENSING:
    ECG electrodes woven at chest position (heart rate monitoring)
    Strain sensors (piezoelectric or resistive change on stretch)
    → Detect body posture, breathing rate, muscle activation
    Textile-based EMG electrodes for prosthetic control research

  PHASE-CHANGE MATERIALS (PCMs):
    Microencapsulated paraffin wax with specific melt point
    (e.g., 30°C = skin temperature range)
    Embedded in fiber coating or applied to fabric surface
    When temperature rises → wax melts → absorbs heat
    When temperature drops → wax solidifies → releases heat
    → Thermal buffering across temperature range
    Outlast technology: developed for NASA spacesuits,
      now in consumer bedding, athletic wear, gloves

  PHOTOVOLTAIC TEXTILES:
    Flexible thin-film or CIGS solar cells on fiber
    Wearable energy generation concept
    Research stage; low efficiency; washability unsolved

  SHAPE MEMORY TEXTILES (research):
    NiTi (nitinol) wires woven into fabric
    Garment changes shape/openings with temperature
    Ventilation openings open when hot, close when cold
    Primarily research; limited commercial deployment
```

## Ballistic and Stab Protection

```
  BALLISTIC PROTECTION (soft armor):
    NIJ 0101.06 standard:
    Level IIA: 9mm, .40 S&W
    Level II:  9mm, .357 Magnum
    Level IIIA: 9mm, .44 Magnum (most common soft armor)
    Level III:  rifle rounds (requires hard plates)
    Level IV:   armor-piercing rifle (hard plates)

  HOW SOFT ARMOR WORKS:
    Multiple layers woven Kevlar/Dyneema
    Ballistic impact → fiber layers catch projectile
    → Deform and distribute energy over area
    → Fiber absorbs energy in stretch/fracture
    More layers = more protection (and weight)

  STAB RESISTANCE (separate standard):
    NIJ 0115.00 standard
    DIFFERENT MECHANISM than ballistic:
      Ballistic: projectile stopped by tensile fabric strength
      Stab: sharp pointed object penetrates between fibers
      Soft Kevlar vest provides minimal stab protection
      (knife slips between yarn openings)
    Stab-resistant solutions:
      Chainmail (metal links prevent knife passage)
      PE hardened plate/panel inserts
      Tightly woven aramid (small openings)
    Combined ballistic + stab protection requires different design

  HARD ARMOR PLATES:
    Steel: heavy, affordable
    Ceramic + UHMWPE composite: lighter; ceramic fractures
      bullet while UHMWPE backing catches fragments
    Full UHMWPE: lightest; rifle protection at reduced weight
    Multi-hit capability depends on plate material
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Application | Technical textile solution |
|---|---|
| Waterproof rain jacket | Gore-Tex or ePTFE membrane laminate (3-layer for hard shell) |
| Cut-resistant gloves (kitchen/industrial) | Dyneema/Spectra UHMWPE blend |
| Firefighter outer shell | Nomex/Kevlar blend (inherent FR + strength) |
| Industrial flash fire garment | NFPA 2112 rated FR cotton or Nomex |

| Road subgrade stabilization | Non-woven PP geotextile (separation + filtration) |
| Retaining wall reinforcement | Geogrid + woven geotextile |
| Heated wearable | Resistive carbon fiber panel + battery |

## Common Confusion Points

**Gore-Tex doesn't fail when wet — DWR does**: The ePTFE membrane continues to block liquid water even when wet. What degrades is the DWR on the face fabric. When the face fabric wets out, it feels cold and clammy — the membrane still functions but the experience is poor. Restore DWR with heat (dryer or iron) before buying new gear.

**Kevlar doesn't stop knives**: A vest rated NIJ IIIA provides excellent protection against handgun rounds but minimal stab resistance. The knife slides between the woven fibers. Stab-resistant armor requires different construction (chainmail, hardened panels). Combined threats require multi-layer systems.

**Meta-aramid (Nomex) ≠ Para-aramid (Kevlar)**: Same chemical family, completely different properties. Nomex = flame resistance. Kevlar = high tensile strength. The meta vs para linkage angle creates completely different molecular geometry and fiber behavior.

**UHMWPE cannot be used near heat**: Dyneema and Spectra melt at ~130°C. This makes them inappropriate near open flame, welding, or any thermal hazard where Nomex or Kevlar would be required. This is why you don't see UHMWPE in fire protection applications despite its high specific strength.

**Smart textiles are mostly R&D, not commodity products**: The concept of fully integrated wearable electronics is mostly research stage. Commercial applications (heated gloves, heart rate chest straps) use relatively simple resistive elements. True seamless bioelectronic garments are not yet commercially mainstream.
