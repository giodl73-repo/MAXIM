Wet processing is the most chemistry-intensive part of textile manufacturing and the largest source of wastewater pollution. The ~200 kilometers of rivers around industrial textile districts in China, Bangladesh, and India are often so heavily contaminated that satellite imagery shows dye releases.

## Dye Chemistry: Fiber-Specific Classes

### The Bonding Problem

```
  DYE MOLECULE must:
  1. Travel from dye bath into fiber
  2. Bond to fiber through some mechanism
  3. Stay bonded during washing, light exposure, abrasion

  FIBER CHEMISTRY DETERMINES DYE CLASS:
  ┌──────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Fiber            │ Reactive sites                             │
  ├──────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Cotton/linen     │ Hydroxyl groups (-OH) on cellulose        │
  │ Wool/silk/nylon  │ Amino groups (-NH₂) on protein            │
  │ Polyester        │ No ionic groups — only hydrophobic voids  │
  │ Acrylic          │ Nitrile groups; ionic modification         │
  └──────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Reactive Dyes (Cotton, Linen, Rayon)

```
  MECHANISM: form covalent bonds with cellulose -OH groups

  REACTIVE GROUP:  -SO₂-CH=CH₂  (vinyl sulfone) or
                   -NH-CO-CH₂Cl  (monochlorotriazine)

  CHEMISTRY:
    Cellulose-OH + Dye-Cl → Cellulose-O-Dye + HCl
    (nucleophilic substitution, alkaline conditions)

  PROCESS:
    1. Pad or exhaust dye bath with dye + salt (NaCl/Na₂SO₄)
    2. Add alkali (Na₂CO₃ or NaOH) → reaction begins
    3. Fix at temperature (varies by reactive group type)
    4. Wash thoroughly (unfixed dye = wash-off problem)
       ~50% of reactive dye is typically hydrolyzed
       (reacts with water instead of fiber) → rinse waste

  PROPERTIES:
    Excellent wash-fastness (covalent bond to fiber)
    Wide color range; bright colors
    Standard for all cotton T-shirts, denim weft yarns
    Environmental issue: heavy NaCl use + unfixed dye in effluent
```

### Vat Dyes (Cotton, Especially Denim/Indigo)

```
  MECHANISM: insoluble dye → soluble leuco form → re-oxidize in fiber

  INDIGO (the paradigm):
    Indigo (insoluble, blue)
         ↓ reduction: sodium hydrosulfite + NaOH
    Leuco indigo (soluble, yellow-green)
         ↓ fiber absorbs leuco form
    Re-oxidation in air
    Indigo (insoluble, blue; trapped in fiber)

  WHY INDIGO FADES:
    Covalent bond is NOT formed — indigo is physically trapped
    Surface of fiber has more indigo than core
    (Cotton fiber adsorbs leuco form from surface inward)
    Denim is "ring-dyed" — color concentrated at yarn surface
    Mechanical abrasion removes surface → fading

  DENIM PRODUCTION:
    Rope dyeing: warp yarn bundles passed through vat 6-10× times
    Slasher dyeing: warp sheet passed through vats
    Each pass: thin layer of indigo deposited
    Multiple passes → build up color (deeper blue)
    Still only ~0.1 mm penetration → core stays white

  OTHER VAT DYES:
    Indanthrone blue (synthetic, brighter), Caledon varieties
    Used for industrial workwear, uniforms — excellent colorfastness
```

### Acid Dyes (Wool, Silk, Nylon)

```
  MECHANISM: electrostatic attraction between dye anion and
             protonated amino groups in protein fiber

  CHEMISTRY:
    In acidic bath (pH 3-6): -NH₂ + H⁺ → -NH₃⁺ (positive charge)
    Dye molecule: sulfonate groups -SO₃⁻ (negative charge)
    → Electrostatic attraction + van der Waals + some H-bonding

  CLASSES:
    Level acid dyes: excellent leveling, moderate fastness
    Fast acid dyes: better fastness, less level
    Milling dyes: best wet fastness, tend to unlevel

  METALLIC COMPLEX (Chrome) dyes:
    Dye + chromium forms metal complex
    Better lightfastness than plain acid dyes
    Chromium effluent environmental concern

  WOOL DYEING CHALLENGE:
    Scales on wool fiber create diffusion barrier
    High-temperature dyeing (95–100°C) opens scales
    Pressurized dyeing vessel for polyester/nylon
    Fiber-reactive dyes for wool: newer technology
```

### Disperse Dyes (Polyester, Nylon, Acetate)

```
  MECHANISM: diffuse into amorphous regions of hydrophobic polymer

  NO IONIC SITES on polyester → cannot use ionic dye chemistry
  Disperse dyes: small, nonionic molecules dispersed as particles

  PROCESS:
    High temperature (130°C under pressure, in pressurized jet dyeing)
    Heat: polymer chains move, create temporary voids
    Dye molecules diffuse into voids
    Cooling: chains freeze with dye trapped inside
    (Solid solution in polymer — physical entrapment)

  POLYESTER DYED AT 130°C:
    Jet dyeing machine: fabric rope jetted through dye liquor
    under pressure at elevated temperature
    Atmospheric-pressure dyeing only possible with carrier chemicals
    (toxic, mostly phased out)

  HEAT TRANSFER PRINTING (Dye Sublimation):
    Disperse dyes printed on paper → press paper to polyester
    Heat (190–210°C): dye sublimes (solid → gas) → enters polyester
    → photographic quality print; brilliant colors
    Only for polyester and nylon (protein/cellulose reject disperse dyes)
    Used for: sportswear, synthetic fabric printing, promotional items
```

## Natural Dyes: Chemistry and History

### Mordanting

```
  NATURAL DYES AND MORDANTS:

  PROBLEM: most natural dye molecules bond weakly to fiber alone
           wash out quickly without mordant

  MORDANT: metallic salt that binds to both fiber and dye
           creates coordination complex bridging fiber to dye

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  COMMON MORDANTS                                             │
  │  Alum (KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O):  Most common; bright colors;      │
  │                             safe; "mineral alum"             │
  │  Iron (FeSO₄, copperas):   Darkens, saddens colors;          │
  │                             "iron mordanting" shifts hue     │
  │  Copper (CuSO₄):           Greens; some health concern       │
  │  Tin (SnCl₂):              Brightens, "moons";               │
  │                             can damage protein fibers        │
  │  Tannin (plant-based):     For cellulosic fibers —           │
  │                             required pre-mordant step        │
  │                             (cotton needs tannin to bind     │
  │                             metallic mordants)               │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PROCESS:
  1. Mordant fiber in mordant solution (water, heat)
  2. Mordant binds to fiber (coordination to protein groups)
  3. Dye fiber in dye bath
  4. Dye binds to mordant (coordination complex)
  5. Fix and wash

  COTTON requires extra step (tannin pre-mordant) because no
  protein groups — tannin provides metal coordination sites
```

### Key Historical Dyes

```
  WOAD (Isatis tinctoria):
    European blue, pre-indigo import
    Same colorant (indigotin) as indigo, lower concentration
    Major crop in France (Toulouse region), England, Germany
    Trade collapse when Indian indigo arrived in quantity (17th century)
    Woad growers successfully lobbied for trade bans temporarily

  MADDER (Rubia tinctorum, alizarin):
    Red/orange/brown depending on mordant
    Alizarin + alum mordant = Turkey Red (brilliant red)
    Turkey Red process: complex multiple-mordant process
    using sheep dung, olive oil, alum, gall nuts → months
    Most sought-after red in Europe 16th–18th century
    Source: Turkey, Egypt, India
    1869: Graebe and Liebermann synthesize alizarin from coal tar
          → natural madder industry destroyed

  WELD (Reseda luteola):
    Yellow dye; lightfast with alum mordant
    Combined with woad → green (before synthetics)

  COCHINEAL (Dactylopius coccus):
    Red from dried female scale insects on Opuntia cactus
    Aztec/Mexican tradition; Spanish brought to Europe ~1523
    Brilliant scarlet-red with alum mordant; carmine crimson
    70,000 insects = 1 pound of dye
    Displaced kermes (European scale insect red)
    Still used in food (E120 carmine) and cosmetics
    Carmine in strawberry Activia yogurt controversy

  INDIGO (Indigofera tinctoria):
    Plant-source vat dye; blue
    India → Silk Road trade to Europe
    "India" in the word: European source was Indian indigo trade
    Banned in some German states to protect woad industry
    1883: von Baeyer determines structure
    1897: BASF industrial synthesis — destroys Indian indigo industry
    Today: almost all denim indigo is synthetic
```

### Perkin's Revolution (1856)

```
  WILLIAM HENRY PERKIN, 18 years old, Easter holiday 1856:
    Attempting to synthesize quinine (anti-malarial) from coal tar
    Got brown sludge → added ethanol → purple color
    Recognized commercial potential → tested on silk
    → Mauveine (first synthetic dye): "Tyrian purple" of the middle class

  IMPACT:
    Mauveine patented; Perkin built a factory at 18
    German chemical industry (BASF, Bayer, Hoechst):
      Synthesized mauveine by 1859, improved it
      Developed systematic dye chemistry research
      By 1880s: Germany controlled ~90% of synthetic dye trade
      Including to Britain (which had started it)
    1869: alizarin synthesized → madder industry ends
    1897: indigo synthesized → Indian indigo industry devastated

  SYNTHETIC DYES FOLLOWED:
    Azo dyes (1864, Griess): diazonium coupling reaction
    → hundreds of yellow, orange, red, black dyes
    Phthalocyanine blue/green (1928 — ICI, accidental discovery)
    Disperse dyes, reactive dyes, fiber-reactive dyes
    → the entire spectrum of textile color
```

### Discharge Printing

```
  TECHNIQUE: print ONTO already-dyed fabric with reducing agent
             Reducing agent destroys vat/reactive dye in printed area
             → lighter or white pattern on colored ground

  USE: vintage-look printing; sophisticated colorways
  Reducing agents: sodium formaldehyde sulfoxylate (Rongalit)
  Can add opaque white pigment in discharge paste for solid white
  Some dyes resist discharge (indigo resists reducing agents partially)
```

### Resist Printing

```
  BATIK (Indonesia, Java):
    Wax applied by tjanting (copper cup tool) or tjap (stamp block)
    Fabric dyed → wax prevents dye in waxed areas
    Wax removed (boiling or cracking)
    Multiple wax/dye steps → complex multi-color designs
    "Batik" = dropped water (Javanese) → dot/wax technique

  TIE-DYE:
    Fabric bound, folded, tied with string
    Dye doesn't penetrate tied/bound areas
    Shibori (Japanese): sophisticated folding/binding/clamping
    → refined geometric effects vs Western "hippie" tie-dye

  IKAT:
    Resist applied to YARN before weaving, not to fabric
    Single ikat: warp or weft pre-dyed in pattern
    Double ikat: both warp AND weft pre-dyed to align
    → Patola silk (Gujarat, India): double-ikat silk saris,
       months of labor per piece, extremely prestigious
    Uzbek ikat: silk warp ikat, bold geometric
```

## Chemical Finishing

### Mercerization (1844)

```
  JOHN MERCER, 1844 (UK); commercialized with tension ~1890

  PROCESS:
    Cotton immersed in 18-25% NaOH solution under tension
    Temperature: room temperature to 15°C

  WHAT HAPPENS:
    NaOH swells cotton fiber (osmotic + chemical)
    Kidney-bean-shaped collapsed lumen → ROUND cross-section
    Cellulose crystal structure: Cellulose I → Cellulose II
    (mercerized cotton has different crystal structure)
    Fiber becomes rounder → reflects light more uniformly → LUSTER
    Held under tension → no shrinkage → longer fiber

  EFFECTS:
    Luster increase: ~20-30%
    Dye uptake: ~25% better (more accessible hydroxyl groups)
    Tensile strength: +10-15%
    Moisture absorption: improved
    Dimensional stability: better

  "MERCERIZED COTTON" label = quality premium
  Dress shirts: almost all high-quality shirting cotton is mercerized
```

### Sanforizing (1930)

```
  SANFORD LOCKWOOD CLUETT, 1930, Troy Collar Company

  PROBLEM: cotton shrinks up to 10% first wash
           → garments made to size shrink to smaller on first wear

  PROCESS:
    Fabric fed onto rubber blanket
    Blanket compressed by guide roller → blanket surface
    stretches on the curve
    Fabric forced to "pre-shrink" by compressive action
    Residual shrinkage after treatment: <1% (ASTM/ISO standard)

  LICENSING: Sanforized Co. licensed process; manufacturers
             pay license fee → use "Sanforized®" trademark
             Non-licensed pre-shrunk processes exist but
             "Sanforized" is specific trademarked guarantee

  CONSEQUENCE: raw selvedge denim is NOT sanforized
    "Unsanforized" or "shrink-to-fit" denim expected to
    shrink ~8-10% on first soak → buy 1-2 sizes larger
    "Shrink-to-fit" Levi's 501 (historical) → soak in bathtub
    wearing jeans → dry on body for custom fit
```

### DWR (Durable Water Repellency)

```
  PURPOSE: cause water to bead and roll off fabric surface
           without wetting the fiber → maintaining breathability

  HOW IT WORKS:
    Extremely low-surface-energy coating on fiber surfaces
    Water contact angle > 90° → droplet beads up, rolls off
    Fabric feels dry; pores remain open → breathable

  TRADITIONAL: C8 PFAS (perfluorooctane sulfonates and carboxylic acids)
    Perfluorinated 8-carbon chain gives ~18-19 mN/m surface energy
    (PTFE/Teflon is ~18 mN/m — extremely hydrophobic)
    PROBLEM: C8 PFAS bioaccumulates, persistent environmental pollutant
             Found in polar bears, Arctic sediment, human blood
             EPA and EU: phasing out C8 PFAS

  C6 PFAS: shorter chain, less bioaccumulative, but still PFAS
            Regulatory pressure continuing

  ALTERNATIVES:
    C0 DWR: wax/hydrocarbon (Polygiene C0, Nikwax TX.Direct)
    Silicone-based DWR
    Dendrimer/polymer DWR
    Lower initial performance; faster wash-out; lower environmental risk

  DWR MAINTENANCE:
    Washing with detergent loads up DWR sites → beads less
    Tumble dry or iron on low → reactivates DWR
    Eventually wash-out requires re-treatment (Nikwax, Grangers)
    DWR is NOT waterproofing — it's surface treatment only
    True waterproofing requires membrane (Gore-Tex, eVent) + DWR outer
```

## Common Confusion Points

**DWR ≠ waterproof**: DWR is a surface treatment that makes water bead. Under sustained pressure (rain, sitting on wet surface, face fabric compressed) water still penetrates. Waterproofing requires a membrane (laminated Gore-Tex, etc.) with DWR as the outer treatment. Both are needed for functional rainwear.

**Indigo is a vat dye, not a reactive dye**: Indigo doesn't covalently bond to cotton. It's trapped physically via reduction-reoxidation cycle. This is why it fades — no covalent bond holding it. Reactive dyes do form covalent bonds and are far more wash-fast.

**Batik is not tie-dye**: Batik uses wax applied precisely with a tjanting (drawing tool) to create crisp-edged patterns. Tie-dye uses binding/tying to create blurred, irregular patterns. Both are resist methods but different techniques, aesthetics, and origins (Indonesia vs American 1960s counterculture derived from shibori).

**Mercerized and pre-shrunk are different processes**: Mercerization (NaOH treatment under tension) adds luster and dye affinity. Sanforizing (rubber blanket compression) removes shrinkage. A fabric can be mercerized but not sanforized, or both. Dress shirt cotton is typically both.
