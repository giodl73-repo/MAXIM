# 07 — Dyeing & Finishing

## Mordants, Synthetic Dyes, Mercerization, Sanforizing — Color and Performance

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **Dye classification by fiber chemistry**: dyes bond to fibers by different mechanisms depending on fiber type; natural dyes bridged this with mordants; synthetic dye chemistry differentiated into fiber-specific classes
  - Acid dyes: bond to amino groups (positive charges) in protein fibers (wool, silk, nylon); acid (pH 4-6) conditions required; brilliant colors; wool and silk dyeing
  - Reactive dyes: form covalent bonds with hydroxyl groups in cellulosic fibers (cotton, linen, viscose); most common for cotton; bright, wash-fast; requires fixing at high pH then washing out unfixed dye
  - Vat dyes: insoluble, reduced to soluble "leuco" form in alkaline solution, fabric absorbs, oxidized back to insoluble state in fiber; excellent wash-fastness; indigo is a vat dye; used for denim
  - Disperse dyes: no ionic groups; dispersed as fine particles in dye bath; diffuse into hydrophobic synthetic fibers (polyester, nylon, acetate) at high temperature under pressure; the only practical way to dye polyester
  - Direct dyes: bond to cellulose without mordant; acceptable wash-fastness; mostly used for cheaper applications
  - Mordant/chrome dyes: metal complex dyes; chromium salt mordant used with wool; excellent lightfastness; environmental concerns about chromium
- **Natural dyes** (see pigments/ file for fuller history): woad, madder, weld, indigo, cochineal, weld — all required mordants for most fibers; alum (potassium aluminum sulfate) most common mordant; iron mordant (copperas) darkens and saddens colors; tin mordant (stannous chloride) brightens and makes brilliant; tannin mordant for cotton (plant fibers don't have protein-like affinity for mordant metallic salts — require tannin pre-treatment)
- **William Perkin's mauveine** (1856): coal tar dye synthesis (aniline → mauveine purple by accident); launched synthetic dye industry; German chemical industry (BASF, Bayer, Hoechst) dominated by 1880s via superior chemistry R&D; synthetic dyes: alizarin (1869), azo dyes (hundreds of yellows, oranges, reds, blacks via diazonium coupling), phthalocyanines (see pigments/09)
- **Printing methods**:
  - Block printing: carved wooden block, hand-applied dye/pigment; Woodblock printing originated India/China; slow but beautiful irregular quality; traditional Indian block-print cotton (Ajrakh, Bagru, Sanganeri)
  - Engraved roller printing (1783, Thomas Bell): copper roller engraved with pattern, applies dye at high speed; dominant 19th century; precise repeat registration
  - Screen printing (flat bed and rotary): stencil stretched on frame; paste pushed through open areas; flat screen slow but versatile (discharge printing, specialty effects); rotary screen (cylindrical) — high speed industrial
  - Digital printing: inkjet with textile inks; no screens; short runs; complex photographic patterns; heat transfer (dye sublimation) for polyester — dye transfers from paper to fabric under heat; reactive inkjet for cotton; growing share of production
  - Discharge printing: remove dye from pre-dyed fabric chemically → lighter pattern on darker ground; reducing agent (sodium formaldehyde sulfoxylate) destroys reactive dyes; vintage-look printing
  - Resist printing: wax (batik — Javanese tradition), paste (mud cloth/Malian bogolan), tied areas (tie-dye, ikat — pre-woven resist) prevent dye penetration
- **Ikat**: dyeing yarn in pattern before weaving; single ikat (warp or weft), double ikat (both — Patola silk from Gujarat, India — extremely difficult and prestigious); pattern created by binding sections of yarn before dye bath
- **Finishing processes**:
  - **Mercerization** (John Mercer, 1844): cotton immersed in concentrated NaOH under tension; cellulose crystal structure rearranges from cellulose I → cellulose II; fiber swells and flattens → rounder cross-section → better light reflection → increased luster; also improves dye uptake (~25% more) and tensile strength; "mercerized cotton" = standard for quality shirting
  - **Sanforizing** (Sanford Cluett, 1930): mechanical pre-shrinking of cotton fabric; rubber blanket compresses fabric longitudinally → residual shrinkage <1% after washing; pre-sanforizing cotton must be washed before cutting to pattern; "Sanforized" label = pre-shrunk
  - **Calendering**: fabric passed between heavy rollers under heat and pressure → smooth, glazed surface; Schreiner calendar (fine-engraved roller) → pearlescent effect; embossing calendar → pressed-in pattern
  - **Raising/napping**: fabric passed over rotating drums covered with wire card clothing → fiber ends pulled to surface → fluffy, raised surface; flannel, fleece, peachskin; can be done to woven or knitted fabric
  - **Shearing**: cutting raised nap to uniform height → smooth velour, velvet; shorn fleece (for garments) vs unshorn (for stuffed animals/blankets)
  - **Resin finishing** (durable press/wrinkle-resistant): DMDHEU (dimethylol dihydroxyethylene urea) or other resins cross-link cellulose chains → fabric resists wrinkle; permanent press shirts; formaldehyde release concern (free formaldehyde in fabric — regulations limit levels); reduces abrasion resistance slightly
  - **DWR (Durable Water Repellency)**: hydrophobic chemical applied to fabric surface; water beads up, doesn't wet fabric; traditional: perfluorinated compounds (C8 PFAS — persistent environmental pollutants, being phased out); replacement C6 PFAS → bio-based DWR (wax, silicone-based) alternatives; applied to outerwear, umbrellas; NOT waterproofing (water can still penetrate seams, pores under pressure) — combined with membrane (Gore-Tex) for true waterproofing
