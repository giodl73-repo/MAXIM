# 03 — Synthetic Fibers

## Rayon to Spandex — Polymer Chemistry and the Petrochemical Wardrobe

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- The synthetic fiber revolution: textiles untethered from agriculture and animals; properties engineered to specification; mass production at low cost; environmental costs deferred for decades; currently ~65% of global fiber consumption is synthetic (polyester alone ~54%)
- **Rayon/viscose** (1892, Cross/Bevan/Beadle UK): first "manufactured" fiber; not truly synthetic — derived from cellulose (wood pulp or cotton linters); cellulose dissolved in sodium hydroxide + carbon disulfide (xanthate process) → viscose solution → forced through spinneret into acid bath → cellulose regenerates as fiber; "artificial silk" — similar feel and drape to silk; name: "rayon" (ray of light) because of sheen; properties: absorbent, comfortable, drapes well, cheap; problems: weak when wet, environmentally problematic (CS₂ toxic, chemical effluent from traditional viscose process)
- **Modal** (1951, Lenzing): refined rayon process, longer chain cellulose, higher strength especially when wet; typically beechwood cellulose; softer than standard rayon; widely used in underwear/t-shirts
- **Lyocell/Tencel** (1972, Courtaulds; TENCEL brand owned by Lenzing): cellulose dissolved in NMMO (N-methylmorpholine N-oxide) instead of toxic CS₂; closed-loop solvent recovery → much more sustainable process; stronger than rayon, softer, biodegradable; Tencel as luxury sustainable fiber; forms characteristic micro-fibril surface → prone to "fuzzing" but produces soft hand
- **Nylon** (polyamide 6,6; Du Pont, 1938): Wallace Carothers' research at Du Pont (condensation polymerization of hexamethylenediamine + adipic acid); nylon 6,6 announced at 1939 World's Fair as "stronger than steel, finer than silk"; first nylon stockings sold 1940 (sold out in days — women had been wearing rayon or silk); WWII redirected all nylon to parachutes, glider tow ropes, ropes; post-war: nylon stockings return, nylon in toothbrush bristles, gear, rope; nylon 6 vs nylon 6,6 (different monomer, slightly different properties, both common); properties: strong, abrasion resistant, elastic, dries quickly, melts rather than burns; not UV stable (yellows); absorbs some moisture
- **Polyester** (1941, British Calico Printers/ICI as Terylene; 1951 Du Pont as Dacron): PET (polyethylene terephthalate) most common; condensation polymer of ethylene glycol + terephthalic acid (from petroleum); largest-volume textile fiber globally; strong, wrinkle-resistant, dimensionally stable, dries quickly, UV resistant; low moisture absorption (stays damp on skin — "clammy"); microplastic shedding during washing (50,000-200,000 fibers per wash → ocean pollution); recycled polyester (rPET from bottles — Patagonia pioneered) improves environmental profile somewhat
- **Acrylic**: polyacrylonitrile; wool-like appearance and warmth; cheap; used in knitwear, fake fur; weakest common synthetic; pills badly; Orlon (Du Pont brand), Acrilan; declining use as wool alternatives improve
- **Spandex/Lycra/Elastane** (1958, Joseph Shivers, Du Pont): polyurethane-polyurea copolymer; trade name Lycra (still owned by Lycra Company, formerly Invista/Du Pont); stretches 500-600% and recovers fully; revolutionary for fitted clothing, swimwear, activewear; typically used as 2-30% blend (100% spandex garments rare — unpleasant feel); synthetic rubber (chloroprene neoprene) as predecessor for some applications; Spandex = anagram of "expands"
- **Polypropylene (PP)**: lightest synthetic fiber; does not absorb moisture at all (hydrophobic); excellent moisture-wicking (transports moisture by capillary action along fiber surface); used in thermal base layers, industrial applications; cannot be dyed with standard dyes (solution-dyeing = color added to polymer melt before extrusion); low melting point (cannot be laundered hot, iron)
- **Microfibers**: fibers with diameter <1 denier (per-unit-weight measurement); polyester and nylon microfibers; ultra-soft hand; high surface area → excellent moisture management; "microfiber cloth" for cleaning → capillary absorption in the split fiber structure; fashion fabrics: suede-like "Alcantara/Ultrasuede," peachskin, crepe
- **Environmental overview**: synthetic fiber production uses ~342 million barrels of oil per year; polyester not biodegradable (100-500 year breakdown); microplastic shedding; textile production = ~10% of global carbon emissions and ~20% of global industrial water pollution; recycling rates very low (<1% of clothing recycled into new clothing); chemical recycling (depolymerization back to monomers) promising but not yet at scale
