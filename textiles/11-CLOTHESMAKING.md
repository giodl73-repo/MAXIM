# Clothesmaking

## The Trade Hierarchy — From Cutter to Seamstress to Tailor

## The Big Picture

```
CLOTHESMAKING SUPPLY CHAIN:

  Design/Pattern Making
          ↓
  Grading (size scaling)
          ↓
  Marker Making (fabric layout)
          ↓
  CUTTING (most skilled: cutter)
          ↓
  Bundling (pieces grouped for assembly)
          ↓
  SEWING (multiple specialized operators in industrial;
          one person in bespoke/home sewing)
          ↓
  PRESSING (at each stage and final)
          ↓
  Finishing (hand finishing, buttons, trims)
          ↓
  Quality Control + Packing

  KEY INSIGHT: In industrial production, NO ONE person makes the whole
  garment. Each operator does ONE operation (collar attach, sleeve attach,
  pocket, hemming) all day. SAM (Standard Allowed Minutes) = time each
  operation should take per unit. Factory efficiency measured in % of SAM.
```

## Historical Trade Hierarchy

```
THE CLOTHESMAKING TRADES (historical, mostly collapsed now):

  CUTTER:
    Most senior skilled trade in a tailoring house
    Responsibility: interpret pattern, select grain, cut fabric
    A mis-cut is unrecoverable (fabric destroyed)
    Separated from sewing rooms (cutting = planning/precision)
    In Savile Row: head cutter = effective master of shop
    Earned significantly more than sewers
    Industrial cutting today: band knife, die cutting, laser CNC

  TAILOR:
    Historical definition: MEN'S outerwear (suits, coats, doublets)
    Worshipful Company of Merchant Taylors (London, chartered 1327)
    — one of the Great Twelve Livery Companies
    Skills: padding, interlining, hand-canvassing, steam shaping
    The suit jacket: 60+ pieces, structured shoulder, shaped chest,
    one of the most technically complex garments in existence
    Bespoke tailoring: multiple fittings, hand-made buttonholes,
    hand-picked stitching on lapel edge

  SEAMSTRESS / DRESSMAKER:
    Historical definition: WOMEN'S clothing, lighter garments
    Often piece-work from home (sweated labor; minimal wages)
    Lower status than tailor; frequently exploited
    The distinction tailor/seamstress has largely collapsed in
    modern usage — "tailor" now used for any skilled garment maker

  MILLINER:
    Hat maker; separately trained from clothesmakers
    Hats built on wooden hat blocks (crown shapes)
    Straw work, felt hood blocking, trimming
    "Mad as a hatter": mercury (mercurous nitrate) used in felt-
    making caused tremors/neurological symptoms in hat makers
    Historically important trade (each fashion season = new hat)

  COUTURIÈRE / COUTURIER:
    French tradition; HIGH FASHION DRESSMAKER
    Haute couture = custom-fitted, hand-made by member houses
    Chambre Syndicale de la Haute Couture criteria:
      → Custom-made for individual client
      → At least 15 people employed full-time in atelier
      → Present 2 collections/year of 50+ designs
    Currently ~15 official houses
    Distinct from prêt-à-porter (ready-to-wear, mass production)
```

## Machine Sewing: The Mechanics

```
LOCKSTITCH (domestic and industrial sewing machine):

  Upper thread (needle) + Lower thread (bobbin)
  Needle carries thread DOWN through fabric
  Hook catches upper thread loop around bobbin thread
  Needle rises → threads tighten → "lock" forms at middle of fabric

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Fabric top surface:  ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ (even stitch line)     │
  │  Fabric interior:         X X X X (interlocked threads)  │
  │  Fabric underside:   ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ (even stitch line)      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SPEED:
    Home machine: 800-1,200 stitches per minute
    Industrial lockstitch: 5,000-6,000+ SPM

OVERLOCK / SERGER (3, 4, or 5 thread):

  Simultaneously:
    1. Stitches seam (lockstitch component)
    2. Trims seam allowance
    3. Overcasts raw edge (looper threads wrap edge)
  Single pass = stitch + finish in one operation
  Industrial: used for knit garments and seam finishing
  Cannot be used alone for all seams (no lockstitch on interior)

COVERSTITCH (hemming knit fabrics):
  2 or 3 needles on face → 2-3 parallel rows
  Looper on back → chain stitch holding loops
  Looks like twin-needle hem on face, stretchy on back
  Standard hem for T-shirts, activewear (not a rolled hem)

BLIND STITCH MACHINE:
  Needle catches only a few threads of face fabric
  Hem not visible on right side
  Standard for trouser hems, dress hems in industrial production
```

## Seam Types

```
PLAIN SEAM (most common):
  Two pieces, right sides together
  Stitch at seam allowance width (5/8" US, 1 cm industrial)
  Press open or to one side
  Raw edges finished (serge, zigzag, Hong Kong)

  Used for: virtually everything

FRENCH SEAM (clean finish on both sides):
  Step 1: Wrong sides together, stitch narrow seam (3/8")
  Step 2: Trim seam allowance, press
  Step 3: Fold right sides together, stitch again (5/8")
  Result: raw edges fully enclosed inside seam

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Inside view: smooth, no raw edges    │
  │  Used for: sheers, lingerie, silk     │
  └────────────────────────────────────────┘

FLAT-FELLED SEAM (extremely strong, visible topstitching):
  Step 1: Stitch plain seam, press to one side
  Step 2: Trim one seam allowance narrower
  Step 3: Wrap wide SA over narrow, press flat
  Step 4: Topstitch through all layers
  Result: two visible rows of topstitching on face side

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Jeans inseam and side seam           │
  │  Work shirts (western shirts)         │
  │  Extremely durable; won't ravel       │
  └────────────────────────────────────────┘

LAPPED SEAM:
  One piece overlaps another; top edge topstitched down
  Used for yokes, back seams on work wear

SERGED / OVERLOCKED SEAM:
  Overlock machine stitches + finishes in one pass
  Standard for knit garments (T-shirts, jersey)
  Slight stretch in the seam (chain stitch component)

HONG KONG FINISH:
  Bias tape folded around raw edge, stitched
  Clean finish inside tailored garments
  Mimics bound seam allowance of couture construction
```

## Hand Sewing Stitches

```
RUNNING STITCH:    ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─
  Basic in-out forward stitch; gathering; basting
  Weakest stitch; gaps between thread

BACKSTITCH:        ──────────────────
  Needle back to prior hole; overlap each stitch
  STRONGEST hand stitch (no gaps); for seams

BASTING:           ─   ─   ─   ─   ─
  Long running stitch; temporary; removed after fitting
  Hold layers together for trying on; mark positions

SLIP STITCH:
  Catches just a few threads on face; travels in fold
  Invisible hem joining; closing a lining
  Hand-join lining to shell invisibly

BLIND HEM:
  Catch ONLY 1-2 threads of face fabric with needle
  Hem invisible on right side
  Less invisible than slip stitch but faster on thick fabrics

CATCH STITCH (herringbone):   X X X X
  Zigzag motion; crosses between hem and fashion fabric
  Flexible — good for stretch fabrics and trouser hems
  More catching area = better hold; less visible = better look

PAD STITCHING (tailoring):
  Rows of diagonal stitches attaching canvas to fabric layers
  Builds permanent "roll" in lapel and chest piece
  Hundreds of individual stitches; 1-2 hours per jacket front
  Creates memory in cloth — jacket molds to wearer's body
```

## Pressing

```
  "SEWING IS 50% PRESSING"
  (Professional clothesmakers agree; most beginners skip this)

  PRESSING vs IRONING:
    Ironing: sliding motion → stretches bias, distorts
    Pressing: UP-DOWN motion → shapes without distortion

  PRESSING EQUIPMENT (professional):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Tailor's Ham:   Stuffed egg-shaped cushion             │
  │  (ham cushion)   Press curved seams (bust seam,         │
  │                  armhole, sleeve head)                   │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Sleeve Board:   Narrow board; fits inside sleeve       │
  │                  Press sleeve seams without creasing      │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Seam Roll:      Cylindrical; press seams open on       │
  │                  curved areas without impressions        │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Point Presser:  Narrow board; press points of collars  │
  │                  and cuffs before turning right side out │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  Pressing Cloth: Damp or dry cloth between iron + fabric │
  │                  Protects delicate surfaces from shine   │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WOOL AND STEAM:
    Wool responds dramatically to steam
    Steam can shape, ease, shrink ease in sleeve head
    "London shrunk" = wool pre-shrunk with damp pressing
    Preshrink all fabrics before cutting (prevents post-construction
    shrinkage, especially cotton, linen, wool)

  PRESSING SEQUENCE IN TAILORING:
    Press each seam before crossing another seam
    Press darts before pressing the seam they live in
    Press inward curves (neckline) on the ham
    Press sleeve head ease in before setting
```

## Interfacing and Interlining

```
  INTERFACING: adds body and stability to fashion fabric
  Applied to specific areas: collars, cuffs, waistbands,
  plackets, jacket fronts, button zones

  FUSIBLE INTERFACING:
    Heat-bond adhesive on one side
    Iron onto wrong side of fabric
    Problem: if misapplied, creates bubbles (de-laminates)
    Fast, industrial standard for most construction
    Test before using: some fabrics don't take fusible well

  SEW-IN INTERFACING:
    Stitched to fabric; no glue
    Used for: delicate fabrics (won't fuse), tailoring,
    any application needing softer hand
    Hair canvas: woven fabric with horsehair → structured,
    used in suit fronts (the "chest piece")

  WOVEN vs NON-WOVEN INTERFACING:
    Woven: has grain; must align with fashion fabric grain;
           drapes better; used in structured garments
    Non-woven: no grain; cut any direction; stiffer;
               lower cost; used in craft and mass production

  UNDERLINING vs INTERLINING:
    Underlining: full layer of fabric under fashion fabric
                 (entire garment back-lined with another fabric)
                 Adds body, supports delicate fabric, hides seam
                 allowances in sheer fabric
    Interlining: layer between fabric and lining for WARMTH
                 Fleece, felt, or insulating batting

  CANVAS AND PAD STITCHING (tailoring):
    Traditional jacket front has "chest canvas" — hair canvas
    + linen — pad-stitched to create jacket's permanent shape
    "Floating canvas" moves with body; "fused jacket" is stiff
    How to detect: pinch jacket fabric between thumb and forefinger
      Two layers sliding independently = floating canvas ✅
      One stiff unit = fused interfacing
    Fused jacket can bubble when subjected to dry cleaning
    or humidity; good canvas jacket improves over years of wear
```

## Closures

```
BUTTONS AND BUTTONHOLES:
  Machine buttonhole: bar-tack at each end; industrial standard
  Worked buttonhole (hand-finished): tailor's buttonhole;
    continuous blanket stitch around cut opening; Savile Row
  Bound buttonhole: rectangular opening with fabric lips
    Couture and high-end tailoring; most laborious; flat, elegant
  Shank buttons: loop on back; for thick fabric (the shank
    allows button to clear the extra fabric thickness)
  Flat buttons: 2-hole or 4-hole; sewn directly to fabric

ZIPPERS:
  Coil zipper: nylon/polyester spiral; flexible, lightest;
    most common for garments
  Metal zipper: brass or aluminum teeth; jeans, heavy bags
  Lapped zipper: one side covers the zipper
  Centered zipper: both sides equal; standard for center-back,
    center-front, skirts
  Invisible zipper: coils hidden when closed; waistband,
    couture
  Fly-front zipper: extension placket hides zipper;
    trousers; the placket is the "fly"
  Separating zipper: opens fully at bottom; jacket

VELCRO (hook-and-loop fastener):
  Invented by George de Mestral (Swiss engineer), 1941
  After examining burdock burr hooks under microscope
  Patented 1955; brand name Velcro
  Hook side (rough) + loop side (soft) = re-attachable bond
  Used in: footwear, medical, aerospace (NASA adopted early),
  children's garments (easy on/off)
  Weakness: loops collect lint and fiber, reducing grip over time

OTHER CLOSURES:
  Hooks and eyes: bra, waistband
  Snaps (press studs): babies, casual garments, foul-weather gear
  Frog closures: loop + decorative knot; Asian garment tradition
    (Chinese jackets, mandarin collar coats)
  Toggles: toggle + loop; duffle coats; sporting garments
  Lacing: corsets, shoes; adjustable fit
```

## Industrial Production Flow

```
FAST FASHION PRODUCTION MODEL:

  DESIGN → PATTERN → GRADE → MARKER
       ↓
  FABRIC ordered (months ahead in traditional)
       ↓
  CUTTING (computerized cutter, stacked layers, laser or knife)
       ↓
  BUNDLING (pieces for one garment bundled together,
             tagged with production ticket)
       ↓
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SEWING LINE (sequential operations):                   │
  │  Machine 1: collar pieces sewn together                 │
  │  Machine 2: collar attached to body                     │
  │  Machine 3: sleeves hemmed                              │
  │  Machine 4: sleeves attached to body                    │
  │  Machine 5: side and underarm seams                     │
  │  Machine 6: buttons sewn                                │
  │  Machine 7: buttonholes worked                          │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
       ↓
  PRESSING and FINISHING (trim threads, labels)
       ↓
  QC INSPECTION (random pull inspection, defect rate)
       ↓
  PACKING and SHIPPING

  SAM (Standard Allowed Minutes):
    Each operation has a target time
    Collar attach: ~3 SAM; button attach: ~1 SAM
    Factory efficiency = actual SAM / available SAM × 100%
    70-80% efficiency = well-run factory
    Low efficiency = operator fatigue, machine downtime, material delay

  BANGLADESH/VIETNAM/CAMBODIA:
    Labor cost: ~$0.50-1.00 per garment in labor
    A $10 T-shirt: $1 fabric, $0.60 labor, $0.20 trims,
      $0.30 overhead, $0.40 freight → $2.50 FOB (factory)
    Brand/retail: buy at $2.50, sell at $10+ (4× markup minimum)
```

## Embroidery and Surface Decoration

```
HAND EMBROIDERY:
  Satin stitch: parallel stitches filling an area; smooth surface
  Crewelwork: wool thread on linen; Jacobean style
  Goldwork: metal thread couching; ecclesiastical, military dress
  Whitework: white thread on white ground; cut and drawn work
  Hardanger (Norwegian): counted thread on even-weave fabric

MACHINE EMBROIDERY:
  Schiffli machine (Swiss, late 19th century):
    Wide machine (up to 15 meters) with hundreds of needles
    Simultaneous embroidery in same pattern across full width
    Used for mass lace, mass embroidery on collars, cuffs, trim
  Modern domestic/industrial embroidery machines:
    Digital design imported (DST, PES formats)
    Digitizing: converting artwork to stitch paths

ZARDOZI (Indian metalwork embroidery):
  Gold/silver wire, sequins, beads
  Mughal court tradition; currently Lucknow, Agra
  Bridal wear, court dress, royal regalia
  Extremely labor-intensive (weeks per garment)

SMOCKING:
  Gathered fabric sewn with decorative stitches on gathered pleats
  Allows stretch while maintaining decoration
  Traditional on children's garments (bishop dresses)
  North American Liberty bodice tradition

TRAPUNTO (stuffed quilting):
  Two layers; batting stuffed through backing into design areas
  Creates raised bas-relief patterns
  Common in quilting and bridal wear

APPLIQUÉ:
  Separate fabric pieces sewn onto ground fabric
  Cut shapes, pressed under SA, stitched down (or fused + stitched)
  Contrast colors and textures; quilts, formal wear
```

## Decision Cheat Sheet

| Scenario | Tool/Technique |
|---|---|
| Strongest seam for stress point | Flat-felled (jeans) or locked plain seam |
| Sheer/delicate fabric seam | French seam (hides raw edges) |
| Stretch knit seam | Overlock/serger stitch |
| Curved seam that lies flat | Clip/notch seam allowance + press on ham |
| Structured collar/cuff | Fuse with interfacing (woven preferred for quality) |
| Traditional suit jacket | Floating canvas + pad stitching |
| Fast fashion suit jacket | Fused interfacing (watch for bubbling long-term) |
| Hem on Jersey T-shirt | Coverstitch (2-needle, stretchy) |
| Invisible hem on trousers | Blind hem machine or catch stitch by hand |
| Multi-season garment closure | Buttonholes + buttons (durable, replaceable) |

## Common Confusion Points

**Industrial sewing is specialized operations, not whole-garment construction**: A factory worker may spend 8 hours sewing only pockets — nothing else. One person who can make an entire garment is unusual in industrial production and is a genuinely high-level skill.

**Pressing is construction, not afterthought**: Professional garments are pressed after every single seam. Skipping pressing and pressing only at the end cannot undo un-set fabric. The iron is a shaping tool, not just a smoothing tool.

**Fused vs canvassed jacket tells you quality**: Fused jacket = fast, cheap, eventually bubbles. Canvas = labor-intensive, molds to body, lasts decades. The two-layer slide test (pinch jacket front) is a real and reliable detection method.

**Seamstress and tailor are not the same skill set**: Tailoring involves structural construction (padding, canvassing, steam shaping, complex fitting of hard-fabric suits). Dressmaking involves different skills (drape, softer construction, fitting of female form curves). Both are highly skilled; they are not interchangeable.

**An overlocker does NOT replace a sewing machine**: A serger/overlocker finishes edges and sews seams in knits, but it cannot do flat seams, topstitching, buttonholes, or most construction steps. Both machines are needed in a complete sewing setup.
