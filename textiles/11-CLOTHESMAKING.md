# 11 — Clothesmaking

## The Trade Hierarchy — From Cutter to Seamstress to Tailor

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- **The clothesmaking trade hierarchy** (historically and in modern industry):
  - **Cutter**: the most skilled and senior trade in a tailoring house; responsible for interpreting the pattern, selecting the correct grain, and cutting the fabric; a mis-cut cannot be repaired; in Savile Row tradition, the head cutter was effectively the master of the shop; cutting rooms separated from sewing rooms; cutter earned significantly more than sewers; industrial cutting: band knife (straight cuts), die cutting, laser cutting (CNC)
  - **Tailor**: historically a man's trade, making outerwear (suits, coats, doublets); Worshipful Company of Merchant Taylors (London, 1327) — one of the Great Twelve Livery Companies; tailoring involves padding, interlining, hand-canvassing, shaping with steam; the suit jacket is one of the most technically complex garments (60+ pieces, structured shoulder, shaped chest); bespoke tailoring = multiple fittings, hand-made buttonholes, hand-picked stitching
  - **Seamstress / dressmaker**: historically woman's trade; making women's clothing, lighter garments, household linens; often worked from home (piece-work, sweated labor); less status than tailor; the distinction has mostly collapsed in modern usage
  - **Milliner**: hat maker; separately trained from clothesmakers; hats built on wooden hat blocks (crown shapes); straw work, felt hood blocking, trimming; millinery historically important (changing fashion required new hat each season); "Mad as a hatter" — mercury used in felt-making (mercurous nitrate process, hat-shaking felting) caused tremors and neurological symptoms
  - **Couturière / couturier**: high fashion dressmaker; French tradition; haute couture = custom-fitted, hand-made garments by member houses of the Chambre Syndicale de la Haute Couture (Paris); criteria: custom-made for client, at least 15 people employed in atelier, present two collections per year of 50+ designs; currently ~15 official haute couture houses; distinction from prêt-à-porter (ready-to-wear)
- **Hand sewing stitches**: running stitch (basic forward stitch), backstitch (strongest hand stitch), slip stitch (invisible hem joining), blind hem stitch, whip stitch (finishing raw edges), overcast stitch, catch stitch (flexible hem on knits), basting stitch (long, temporary — removed after fitting), pad stitching (tailoring — attaches canvas to lapel, builds shape)
- **Machine sewing basics**: straight stitch (plain seam), zigzag stitch (raw edge finishing, stretch), overlock/serger (simultaneous stitch + trim + overcast at seam edge — 4-thread or 5-thread); coverstitch (hemming jerseys — looks like twin needle on face, looper on back); blind hem stitch (machine version); industrial machines run 5,000+ stitches per minute vs home machine 800-1,200
- **Seam types**: plain seam (most common — two pieces right sides together, pressed open or to one side); French seam (encloses raw edges — seam sewn twice, raw edges hidden inside; used on sheer fabrics); flat-felled seam (both allowances folded and topstitched — jeans inseam — very strong); lapped seam; serged/overlocked seam; Hong Kong finish (bias tape around raw edge)
- **Pressing**: "sewing is 50% pressing" — pressing at each construction stage shapes the garment; tailor's ham (stuffed cushion for pressing curves — sleeve heads, bust seams); sleeve board; point presser; seam roll; pressing cloth (protective layer for delicate fabrics); steam is the key (wool especially — responds to steam); pressing sets seams, shrinks ease, shapes structure; never iron synthetic without pressing cloth
- **Interfacing**: non-woven or woven fabric fused or sewn to fashion fabric to add body and stability; woven interfacing (more drape, used in structured garments), non-woven (stiffer, cheaper), fusible (heat-bond adhesive on one side — iron on), sew-in; used in collars, cuffs, waistbands, plackets, jacket fronts; weight must match fashion fabric; interlinings (warmth), underlinings (support full garment layer)
- **Canvas and pad stitching** (tailoring construction): traditional tailored jacket front has a chest canvas (hair canvas + linen) that gives the jacket its shape; canvas is pad-stitched to the lapel and front — hundreds of tiny stitches that build a permanent roll; "floating canvas" vs "fused" jacket — canvassed jacket molds to the wearer's body over time; fused jacket uses iron-on interfacing (cheaper, faster, but can bubble and age poorly); how to tell the difference: pinch the fabric and feel for two layers sliding independently (canvassed) vs one stiff layer (fused)
- **Closures**: buttons and buttonholes (worked buttonhole = hand-finished, bound buttonhole = fabric-faced — both haute couture; machine buttonhole = industrial); zippers (coil vs metal, lapped vs centered vs invisible vs fly front); hooks and eyes; snaps/press studs; Velcro (hook-and-loop fastener, George de Mestral, 1941, after examining burdock burrs under microscope); toggles; frog closures (Asian garment tradition)
- **Industrial garment manufacturing**: global supply chain — design in NYC/London/Paris, pattern grading and marker making digitally, cutting in factory (often Bangladesh/Vietnam/Cambodia), sewing by piece-rate workers, finishing/QC/packing, shipping; SAM (Standard Allowed Minutes) = time each operation should take; factories organized by operations — not one worker makes whole garment, but specialized operators each doing one operation (pocket, collar, sleeve attach); efficiency metrics; fast fashion vs slow fashion; fast fashion timeline: from runway to H&M floor in 2 weeks vs traditional 6-month lead time
- **Alterations**: the cobbler equivalent for clothing; taking in/letting out seams, raising/lowering hems, replacing zippers, tapering trouser legs; a crucial trade sustaining quality clothing; the distinction between alterations tailor (reactive, fixes fit) and custom tailor (proactive, makes to measure)
- **Embroidery and surface decoration**: hand embroidery (satin stitch, crewelwork, goldwork, whitework — Hardanger); machine embroidery (Schiffli embroidery machine — Swiss, used for lace and mass embroidery); Zardozi embroidery (Indian metalwork); smocking (decorative gathered fabric); beading and sequin attachment; appliqué; trapunto (quilted relief)
