# 05 — Fabric Construction

## Weaving, Knitting, Felting, Nonwovens — How Fibers Become Cloth

> **STUB** — outline only, content to be authored

**Planned coverage:**
- Four fundamentally different ways to make fabric from fibers/yarns: (1) weaving (interlacing two sets of yarns at right angles); (2) knitting (interlocking loops of yarn); (3) felting (entangling fibers through mechanical agitation); (4) nonwovens (bonding fibers chemically, thermally, or mechanically without forming yarn or interlacing)
- **Weaving**: warp threads run lengthwise (parallel to selvage), under tension on the loom; weft threads interlace crosswise through the warp; shed = the opening created between warp threads through which the weft shuttle passes; heddles and harnesses lift alternating warp threads to create the shed; beater presses each weft pick into previous picks
- Loom types: backstrap loom (earliest, portable — warp attached to fixed point, tension by weaver's body), warp-weighted loom (ancient Greek), ground loom (horizontal), rigid heddle, treadle floor loom, Jacquard loom (see weave structures), power loom (Cartwright 1784 — mechanized weaving, displaced handweavers); rapier loom, air jet loom, water jet loom (modern industrial)
- **Weft knitting**: single yarn looped to form interconnected loops (stitches); all loops in a horizontal row (course) formed simultaneously on needles; a dropped stitch can run (ladder) in the course direction; more elastic than woven (loops can shift); hand knitting vs circular knitting machine vs flat knitting machine; jersey fabric (t-shirt fabric), rib (cuffs, waistbands), purl, interlock
- **Warp knitting**: multiple yarns run vertically, each yarn forming its own chain of loops while also interlinking with adjacent yarns; produces stable (doesn't ladder) fabric; Raschel and Tricot machines; lace, lingerie, swimwear, net curtains, geotextiles; high production speeds; less elastic than weft knitting
- **Knit fabric terminology**: jersey = single knit, one face smooth, one face looped; rib = alternating knit and purl columns visible on both faces → stretchy vertically and horizontally; interlock = two ribbed fabrics knitted together → smooth both faces, stable, slightly heavier; piqué = raised textural effect via tuck stitches; French terry = looped on one face, smooth on other (sweatshirt fabric); velour = sheared terry
- **Felting**: uses wool's scale structure; scales interlock when agitated in hot water + pressure; fibers entangle irreversibly → dense, matted fabric; no yarn, no weaving needed; pre-loom technology; cut without fraying (no yarn to unravel); excellent insulation; limited stretch; two types:
  - Wet felting: water, heat, and mechanical agitation (hand-rubbing, rolling, fulling mill)
  - Needle felting: barbed needles punch repeatedly through fiber batt → mechanically entangle; no water; industrial process (car interiors, industrial filters) and craft technique
- **Industrial wool finishing — fulling**: woven wool cloth shrunk and felted slightly under controlled conditions → denser, softer, more durable; historical fulling mills (water-powered hammers); "walker" or "tucker" was the trade (treading cloth in vats of water + urine); place names: Walker, Tucker, Fuller (English surnames from the trade)
- **Nonwoven fabrics**: fibers laid in web, bonded without yarn formation; dominant for disposable applications (diapers, wet wipes, surgical masks/gowns, air filters, geotextiles, carpet backing); three bonding methods:
  - Thermal bonding: heat melts binder fibers (often BiCo — bicomponent core/sheath fibers)
  - Chemical bonding: latex or adhesive sprayed onto web
  - Hydroentanglement (spunlace): high-pressure water jets entangle fibers; soft, cloth-like; used for wet wipes, medical fabrics
  - Spunbond: polymer extruded directly into filaments, laid as web, thermally bonded; very strong; PP spunbond = backbone of disposable medical protective apparel (N95 mask layers)
  - Meltblown: molten polymer blown with high-velocity hot air → extremely fine microfibers; excellent filtration (N95 filter layer); weak mechanically — sandwiched between spunbond layers in SMS construction
- **Bias cut**: cutting fabric at 45° to warp/weft grain; woven fabric has maximum stretch on the bias (diagonal); Madeleine Vionnet (1910s-1920s) revolutionized fashion by cutting on bias → garments that drape and cling to body; fundamental technique in couture; requires more fabric (diagonal waste), more skill to sew (stretches and distorts during construction)
